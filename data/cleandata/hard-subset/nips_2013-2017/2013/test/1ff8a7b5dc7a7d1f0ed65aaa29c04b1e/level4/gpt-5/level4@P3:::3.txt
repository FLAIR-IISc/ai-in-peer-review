Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) formed by stacking recurrent layers, where each layer is a tanh RNN receiving the previous layer’s hidden state. Two output configurations are considered: top-layer-only (DRNN-1O) and all-layers-to-output (DRNN-AO). The authors train with plain SGD (using gradient norm normalization and a decaying learning rate), and for DRNN-1O employ incremental layer-wise training. On character-level language modeling (Wikipedia), the models achieve competitive bits-per-character (BPC) relative to prior recurrent models, including multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free (HF) optimization. Beyond performance, the paper analyzes emergent time scales via perturbation tests, ablations of layer outputs during generation, and a case study on parentheses closure.

The work is well-situated relative to hierarchical RNNs (El Hihi & Bengio; Fernández et al.; Graves et al.), deep bidirectional LSTMs (e.g., TIMIT), and character-level RNNs (e.g., Sutskever et al.). The emphasis on simple training, stacked recurrent architectures, and empirical analysis of temporal hierarchies provides a useful complement to optimization-focused studies (e.g., Martens; Martens & Sutskever) and word-level RNN LMs (Mikolov et al.). It would also be helpful to discuss recent ICML 2013 work on gradient issues in RNNs (e.g., Pascanu, Mikolov, Bengio, “On the difficulty of training RNNs”) given the role of gradient normalization here.

Strengths
- Clear architectural proposal and analysis: Stacked RNNs are a natural way to induce temporal hierarchies; the paper backs this up with multiple probes (typo perturbations, context switches, and output ablations) showing layer-dependent time scales.
- Competitive performance with simple SGD: Achieving state-of-the-art results for recurrent models of comparable size on the Wikipedia dataset without HF is notable.
- Insightful qualitative evaluation: The generative ablations (Table 2) are compelling; the parentheses experiment nicely demonstrates the model’s ability to sustain long-term dependencies beyond the BPTT window.
- Careful training procedure: The incremental training for DRNN-1O is sensible and seems crucial.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Baseline fairness and parameter accounting: The paper states all models have ~4.9M parameters, yet the single-layer RNN has many fewer units than the stacked models. Please clarify how recurrent and inter-layer matrices were counted. A stronger case would compare DRNNs to a single-layer RNN with the same number of units and to DRNNs of varying depth trained from scratch while controlling either parameter count or unit count.
- Depth vs width tradeoff: Rather than only ablating layer outputs at test time (Figure 2), I would like to see performance as a function of depth (e.g., 1–5 layers) with width adjusted to match parameter budgets. This would directly test the value of “deep-in-time” processing.
- Gradient normalization: The paper claims normalization helps avoid “bifurcations.” Please quantify its impact (with/without normalization; thresholded clipping vs full normalization), relate it to exploding/vanishing gradients (Pascanu et al., ICML 2013), and discuss whether normalization distorts the averaging behavior of SGD or effective step size. An ablation here would strengthen the training story.
- Interpretation of layer ablations: As the authors note, output contributions from different layers are interdependent. The “brain surgery” tests are useful but not definitive. Consider complementary probes (e.g., layerwise receptive-field estimation via controlled context masking; linear probes/CCA on hidden states; or using only one layer’s state to drive output while freezing others) to corroborate the time-scale claims.
- Missing baselines: Since “deeper transition” within a time step is a nearby alternative (i.e., extra nonlinearities between t and t+1), a control with a single-layer RNN that has a multi-layer transition function would help isolate whether depth across layers or depth within transitions is key. A gated baseline (e.g., LSTM) with comparable parameters would also contextualize the gains.
- Comparisons to prior compression/LM work: In addition to MRNN and PAQ, consider referencing Mikolov et al.’s RNN language models (word-level), noting differences in setup while situating the reported BPC. Also clarify the impact of using 96 vs 86 characters; a short sensitivity analysis would be helpful.

Quality
The methodology is generally sound, and the training setup is described in good detail. The analysis is thoughtful. The main gaps are in baseline depth/width controls, ablations for gradient normalization, and clearer parameter accounting.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. Please define DRNN-AO and DRNN-1O unambiguously at first mention in the text (not only in a figure), and maintain consistent notation (e.g., 1O vs IO). It would also help to make explicit the initialization of output layers (set to zero) and why this does not create harmful symmetries.

Originality
Stacking RNN layers is not entirely new, but the combination of: (i) a simple DRNN architecture trained with SGD, (ii) competitive character-level results, and (iii) systematic analysis of emergent time scales, provides a useful and original contribution.

Significance
Character-level modeling remains a challenging testbed for long-term dependencies. Demonstrating that simple stacked RNNs can match more sophisticated training methods and revealing the temporal hierarchy they learn should be of interest to the NIPS community. With stronger baseline controls, the case for DRNNs’ advantages would be even more persuasive.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add controlled comparisons across depths (1–5 layers) with matched parameter budgets and, if possible, matched unit counts.
- Include an ablation on gradient normalization/clipping (importance, thresholds) and relate to ICML 2013 work on exploding/vanishing gradients.
- Consider a deep-transition single-layer RNN baseline and, resources permitting, a gated baseline (e.g., LSTM) of similar size.
- Clarify parameter counts and training cost across methods; report how many effective passes through data each model sees.
- Expand discussion of related work (Mikolov et al., Pascanu et al.) and briefly justify the 96-character choice with a sensitivity note.

Pros
- Clear idea; solid analysis of time scales and qualitative behavior
- Competitive results with simple training
- Well-written and honest about limitations

Cons
- Limited depth/width controls; fairness of comparisons not fully convincing
- No ablation for gradient normalization
- Interpretability of layer ablations is limited by interdependencies

Overall assessment
A strong and informative paper that advances our understanding of deep recurrent architectures and provides competitive results with simple training. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying parameter accounting and, if possible, adding depth/width controls or gradient normalization ablations.
