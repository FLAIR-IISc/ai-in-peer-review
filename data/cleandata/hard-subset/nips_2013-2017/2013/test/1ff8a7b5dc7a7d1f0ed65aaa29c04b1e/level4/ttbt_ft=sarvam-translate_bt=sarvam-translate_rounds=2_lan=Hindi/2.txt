Response to the rebuttal:

- I mostly agree with the one-sentence comment.
- It would still be worth trying out the truncated backprop, for example, with 30 steps. It's unlikely that it will learn to balance parentheses (but it would be nice to verify), but it could still achieve competitive entropy at a fraction of the cost.
The usefulness of deep recurrent neural networks has been investigated.
The paper reaches interesting conclusions: when the number of parameters is fixed, deep recurrent neural networks perform better than standard RNNs. Other interesting conclusions include an analysis that shows that deep networks have more "long-term information," and the result is that plain BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, where previously it was only thought possible with HF.
But it claims to present deep RNNs, but they have been used before; for example, in http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf, and possibly even before that. Therefore, the paper should not claim to present this architecture. It is fine not to present new architectures and to focus on the analysis.
The analysis, although meaningful, would be even more interesting if it were shown on several problems, perhaps on some speech tasks. Otherwise, the conclusions could be an artifact of the problem.
Finally, the HF experiments were probably too expensive, because the truncated backprop approach (which was introduced in 90) was successfully used by Mikolov to train RNNs to become excellent language models (see his PhD thesis). Thus, it is likely that truncated BPTT will perform well here as well, and it would be nice to confirm this fact. This work presents an interesting analysis of deep RNNs. There are two main results: 1) deep RNNs perform better than standard RNNs on character-level language modeling when the number of parameters is fixed; 2) deep layers exhibit a long-range structure; and 3) truncated BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses. The results are interesting, but given that this is only an analysis, the paper would be much stronger if it had a similar analysis on some speech tasks (for example, to show that deep RNNs do better, and to show that their deep layers have more long-range information).
