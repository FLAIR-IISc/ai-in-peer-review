

The authors introduce a novel deep architecture that integrates deep learning hierarchies with time-series modeling techniques from HMMs or recurrent neural networks. The training algorithm constructs the network incrementally layer-by-layer through supervised (pre-)training using a next-letter prediction objective. Experiments demonstrate that after approximately 10 days of training on the Wikipedia dataset (Hinton et al.), the model achieves improved performance over prior work. The authors further analyze the network's operational mechanisms, revealing that long-term dependencies are captured in higher layers and that matching parentheses are represented as a "pseudo-stable attractor-like state."  

The paper is of high quality, with clear writing, well-designed experiments, and supporting figures. However, the primary concern lies with the experimental evaluation. The comparison is limited to a single dataset (Wikipedia), which has limited prior research, and the benchmarking focuses exclusively on RNN-based models without contrasting with established hierarchical HMMs or other standard approaches for similar tasks (Table 1 cites methods on different corpora, rendering them incomparable). While the paper’s contribution centers on the analysis of the network’s operational dynamics—well-executed as it is—the stated objective is to propose a new deep architecture. Consequently, the results remain somewhat inconclusive relative to the paper’s explicit focus.
