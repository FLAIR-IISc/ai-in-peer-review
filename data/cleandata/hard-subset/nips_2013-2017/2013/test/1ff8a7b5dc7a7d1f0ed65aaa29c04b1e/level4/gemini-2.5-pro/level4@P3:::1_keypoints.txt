- Proposes Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN) that stacks RNN layers into a temporal hierarchy where lower layers capture short-term and higher layers capture long-term dependencies; introduces DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO variants.
- Evaluated on large-scale character-level Wikipedia language modeling and achieves state-of-the-art performance, matching/exceeding models trained with Hessian-free optimization while using simple SGD with gradient clipping.
- Provides a detailed "typo perturbation" experiment that visualizes distinct memory time scales emerging in different layers, supporting the temporal hierarchy claim.
- Demonstrates long-range dependency learning via a parenthesis-matching analysis, describing learned dynamics as a "pseudo-stable attractor-like state."
- Uses generative text examples and layer-ablation studies to qualitatively show different roles of lower vs. higher layers in generation.
- Strong empirical result suggests architectural changes can rival advances from complex optimization methods.
- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, and includes informative figures and detailed experimental setup.
- Original contribution is the systematic investigation and analysis of emergent properties in stacked RNNs, extending beyond prior work.
- Main weakness: limited experimental scope—results are confined to a single dataset/task, leaving generalization to other sequential domains unclear.
- Comparison set is narrow, focusing mainly on other RNNs and lacking discussion or empirical comparison with other hierarchical models (e.g., HHMMs).
- Best performance relies on an incremental, layer-wise training procedure (especially for DRNN-1O), indicating difficulty training very deep RNNs end-to-end and potential practical limitations.
- Overall assessment: high-quality, insightful paper with strengths outweighing weaknesses; recommendation for acceptance.
