REPLY TO REBUTTAL:

I mostly agree with the comment regarding the focus on a single task. While the analysis is valuable, it would be beneficial to verify the findings across multiple tasks. One potential direction is to investigate the performance of truncated backpropagation with a smaller number of steps (e.g., 30 steps). Although it may be less likely to learn to balance parentheses, it could still achieve competitive entropies at a lower computational cost. It would be worthwhile to confirm this hypothesis.

The paper explores the effectiveness of deep recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and presents several interesting conclusions. Notably, when the number of parameters is held constant, deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs. The analysis also reveals that deeper networks tend to capture more "long-term information." Furthermore, the results show that plain backpropagation through time (BPTT) can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, a task previously thought to be achievable only with Hessian-free optimization.

However, the paper's claim to introduce deep RNNs is not accurate, as this architecture has been used in prior work (e.g., http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf). While it is acceptable to focus on analysis rather than introducing new architectures, the paper should not make this claim.

The analysis, although meaningful, would be more compelling if it were demonstrated on multiple problems, including speech tasks. The current findings may be an artifact of the specific problem being addressed. A more comprehensive evaluation would strengthen the paper's conclusions.

The Hessian-free (HF) experiments may have been unnecessarily expensive, as truncated backpropagation, introduced in the 1990s, has been successfully used by Mikolov (see his PhD thesis) to train RNNs as excellent language models. It is likely that truncated BPTT would yield similar results in this context, and confirming this would be a valuable addition to the paper.

This work presents an interesting analysis of deep RNNs, with two primary results: (1) deep RNNs outperform standard RNNs on character-level language modeling when the number of parameters is fixed, and (2) deeper layers exhibit more long-range structure. While the results are intriguing, the paper would be significantly stronger if it included a similar analysis on a speech task, demonstrating the effectiveness of deeper RNNs and the presence of long-range information in their deeper layers.
