This paper presents an analysis of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) and their application to character-level language modeling. The authors investigate the effect of a hierarchy of recurrent neural networks on processing time series, where each layer is a recurrent network that receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input. This architecture allows for hierarchical processing on difficult temporal tasks and more naturally captures the structure of time series.

The paper relates to previous work on DRNNs, including [8], which employed stacked bi-directional LSTM networks and achieved state-of-the-art performance on the TIMIT phoneme dataset. However, the authors note that their paper is focused on analyzing the contribution of the network architecture, rather than solely on achieving good performance.

The authors study the DRNN architecture on a Wikipedia-based text corpus and demonstrate that it reaches state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks on character-level language modeling when trained with simple stochastic gradient descent (SGD). They also offer an analysis of the different emergent time scales in the network.

The paper's main contributions are: (1) deep RNNs outperform standard RNNs when the number of parameters is fixed on character-level language modeling; (2) the deeper layers exhibit more long-range structure; and (3) plain backpropagation through time (BPTT) can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, which was previously thought to be possible only with Hessian-free (HF) optimization.

The strengths of the paper include its thorough analysis of the DRNN architecture and its application to a real-world task. The authors also provide a detailed comparison with previous work and demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. Firstly, the authors claim to introduce the DRNN architecture, but it has been used before in other works, such as [http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf]. While it is okay to not introduce new architectures and focus on analysis, the authors should not claim to have introduced this architecture.

Secondly, the analysis is only performed on a single task, character-level language modeling. To make the findings more generalizable, it would be beneficial to perform a similar analysis on other tasks, such as speech recognition.

Lastly, the HF experiments were likely too expensive, and the authors could have used truncated backpropagation through time (TBPTT) instead, which has been successfully used in other works, such as [Mikolov's PhD thesis]. It would be interesting to see how TBPTT performs on this task.

In conclusion, this paper presents an interesting analysis of DRNNs and their application to character-level language modeling. While it has some weaknesses, the paper provides valuable insights into the effectiveness of DRNNs and their potential for modeling long-range dependencies.
