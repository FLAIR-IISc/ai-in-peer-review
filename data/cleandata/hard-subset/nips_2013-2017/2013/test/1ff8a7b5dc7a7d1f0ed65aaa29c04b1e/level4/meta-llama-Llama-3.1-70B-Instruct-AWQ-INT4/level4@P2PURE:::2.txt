REPLY TO REBUTTAL: 
I largely concur with the rebuttal's focus on a single task. Nevertheless, it would be valuable to explore the efficacy of truncated backpropagation with, for instance, 30 steps. Although it may be less likely to master balancing parentheses (which should be empirically verified), it could still yield competitive entropies at a lower computational cost.

This paper examines the effectiveness of deep recurrent neural networks (RNNs). The authors arrive at several intriguing conclusions, including the finding that, when the number of parameters is held constant, deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs. Notably, their analysis reveals that deeper networks capture more "long-term information," and they demonstrate that plain backpropagation through time (BPTT) can successfully train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, a task previously thought to be feasible only with Hessian-free (HF) optimization.

However, the paper's claim to introduce deep RNNs is inaccurate, as this architecture has been employed in prior research, such as in Graves' ICASSP 2013 paper, and likely in earlier work as well. The authors should not assert that they have introduced this architecture. Instead, they can focus on the analysis and acknowledge that their contribution lies in the examination of deep RNNs, rather than their invention.

While the analysis is meaningful, its significance would be enhanced by demonstrating its applicability to multiple problems, potentially including speech-related tasks. This would help to establish the generality of the findings and mitigate concerns that they might be an artifact of the specific problem under consideration.

Furthermore, the HF experiments may have been unnecessarily computationally expensive, given the success of truncated BPTT in training RNNs, as demonstrated by Mikolov in his PhD thesis. It is likely that truncated BPTT would yield similar results in this context, and confirming this would be a valuable addition to the paper.

This work presents an interesting analysis of deep RNNs, yielding three primary results: (1) deep RNNs outperform standard RNNs on character-level language modeling when the number of parameters is fixed, (2) deeper layers exhibit more long-range structure, and (3) plain BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses. Although the results are intriguing, the paper's impact would be strengthened by including a similar analysis on a speech-related task, such as demonstrating the superiority of deeper RNNs and the presence of long-range information in their deeper layers.
