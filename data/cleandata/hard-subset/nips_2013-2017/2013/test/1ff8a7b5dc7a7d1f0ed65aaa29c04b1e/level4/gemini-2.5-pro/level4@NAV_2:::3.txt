This paper introduces a Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN) architecture, which is constructed by stacking multiple RNN layers. The core motivation is that while standard RNNs introduce memory over time, they lack an explicit mechanism for hierarchical processing of information at a single time step, a feature to which the success of deep feed-forward networks is often attributed. The authors argue that in a standard RNN, newer data receives only one layer of processing, while older data is processed many more times, which may not be optimal. The proposed DRNN addresses this by allowing information to flow through multiple layers at each time step, creating a temporal hierarchy where different layers can potentially operate on different time scales. The authors evaluate this architecture on a character-level Wikipedia language modeling task, demonstrating that a 5-layer DRNN trained with simple SGD can achieve state-of-the-art performance comparable to more complex models trained with advanced second-order methods. A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to analyzing the trained models, providing evidence that the stacked layers learn a hierarchy of time scales and can capture very long-term dependencies, such as closing parentheses over long distances.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The work is well-motivated, addressing a clear and plausible limitation of standard RNNs. While the idea of stacked RNNs is not entirely new (as the authors acknowledge), this paper provides a thorough and insightful application to a challenging, large-scale task. The strong empirical results, achieving state-of-the-art performance with a relatively simple training algorithm, underscore the significance of the architectural contribution.
*   **Quality of Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting performance metrics and includes a series of thoughtful experiments designed to understand the model's behavior. The analyses of emergent time scales (Figure 3), long-term dependencies (parentheses analysis), and the qualitative effects of ablating layer outputs during text generation (Table 2) are particularly compelling and add significant value to the paper.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, easy to follow, and the core ideas are communicated effectively. The authors are also commendably transparent about the potential limitations of their analyses, for example, noting the difficulty in interpreting the "brain surgery" experiments due to complex interdependencies between layers.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**

1.  **Strength of the Baseline Comparison:** The central claim is that the hierarchical structure of the DRNN is beneficial. To make this claim fully convincing, the baseline comparisons could be strengthened. While the total parameter counts are matched, the distribution of these parameters is very different. A single-layer RNN has a very large recurrent weight matrix, whereas the DRNN distributes its parameters across smaller matrices in depth. A crucial missing experiment is whether the observed benefits truly come from depth, or from this particular distribution of parameters.
    *   Could the authors compare the DRNN against a standard (single-layer) RNN with a similar number of *units*, even if it has more parameters?
    *   Alternatively, would a 2-layer DRNN outperform a single-layer RNN with the same total number of units? This could more directly test the benefit of adding a second layer of hierarchical processing.

2.  **Evaluating the Contribution of Depth:** The experiment in Figure 2, which ablates the output of single layers, is interesting but, as the authors note, hard to interpret. A more direct way to evaluate the benefit of depth would be to train DRNNs of varying depths (e.g., 2, 3, 4, and 5 layers) from scratch. To ensure a fair comparison, the layer widths could be adjusted to maintain a constant parameter budget across these different depths. This would provide clearer evidence on how performance scales with depth for this task.

3.  **Training Procedure Details:** The gradient normalization heuristic is critical to the training success.
    *   Could the authors provide more intuition or analysis on why this helps avoid "bifurcations"? A discussion in the context of recent work on gradient explosion and truncation in RNNs (e.g., Pascanu et al., ICML 2013) would be valuable.
    *   Does normalizing the gradient magnitude at each step interfere with the implicit averaging properties of SGD that are important for convergence?

4.  **Alternative Architectures:** The paper explores adding depth by stacking recurrent layers. Another approach to add processing depth per time step is to insert additional feed-forward non-linear layers *within* the recurrent update (i.e., between `a(t-1)` and `a(t)`). Have the authors considered such an alternative, and how might it compare to the proposed DRNN?

5.  **Related Work:** For the Wikipedia compression task, it would be beneficial to also compare against or discuss the results from other relevant work in language modeling, for example, the work by Mikolov et al., to provide a broader context for the reported performance.

**Minor Point:**

*   The terms DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO are used before they are formally defined in the text (they are introduced in a figure caption). Defining them in the main body of the text upon their first use would improve clarity for the reader.
