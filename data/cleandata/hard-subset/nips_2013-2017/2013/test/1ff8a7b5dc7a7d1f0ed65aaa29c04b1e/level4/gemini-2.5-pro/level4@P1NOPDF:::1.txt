The authors propose a novel deep architecture that combines the hierarchical feature representation of deep learning with the time-series modeling capabilities of models like HMMs and Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). The network is constructed using a layer-by-layer supervised pre-training algorithm on a next-letter prediction task. The experiments, conducted on a large Wikipedia dataset previously used by Hinton et al., demonstrate that the proposed model outperforms previous work after extensive training (approximately 10 days). The paper follows this with a detailed analysis of the network's internal mechanisms, showing for instance that higher layers learn to model long-term dependencies, while phenomena like matching parentheses are handled via what the authors describe as "pseudo-stable attractor-like states."

This is a high-quality, well-written paper. The experiments are thoughtfully designed, and the figures clearly illustrate and support the authors' findings.

However, my main concern lies with the scope of the experimental evaluation. The empirical results are based on a single dataset, one which has not been widely used as a benchmark in the literature. Furthermore, the direct performance comparisons are limited to other RNN-based models. While other relevant approaches capable of solving similar tasks, such as hierarchical HMMs, are mentioned, they are not directly compared against. Table 1, for instance, lists results for other models, but these were obtained on a different corpus, which prevents a direct and fair comparison.

Ultimately, the paper's primary contribution appears to be its insightful analysis of *how* the deep network operates, rather than the performance it achieves. This analysis is quite thorough and valuable. However, this emphasis seems somewhat at odds with the paper's framing, which presents a new, high-performing architecture as its main contribution. In summary, while the paper proposes an interesting model and provides a compelling analysis of its behavior, the conclusiveness of the results is undermined by the narrow experimental evaluation.
