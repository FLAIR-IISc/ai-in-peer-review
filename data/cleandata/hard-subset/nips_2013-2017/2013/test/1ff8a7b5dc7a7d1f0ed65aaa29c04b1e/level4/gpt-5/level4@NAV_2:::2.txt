REPLY TO REBUTTAL:
- I largely concur with the “single-task” concern.
- It would still be valuable to test truncated backpropagation through time (e.g., 30 steps). While it may be less adept at learning to balance parentheses (worth verifying), it could plausibly achieve comparable bits-per-character at substantially lower computational cost.

This paper examines the utility of deep recurrent neural networks. The main empirical takeaways are: with a fixed parameter budget, deeper recurrent architectures outperform standard RNNs on character-level language modeling; deeper layers appear to carry more long-range information; and plain BPTT suffices to train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses—a capability previously thought to require HF.

However, the paper presents the deep RNN architecture as novel, whereas similar stacked recurrent models have appeared before (e.g., http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf, and likely earlier). The manuscript should not claim architectural novelty; it is perfectly fine to focus on analysis and empirical insights.

The analysis is meaningful but would be more compelling if demonstrated on multiple domains, such as a speech task. Otherwise, some findings might be idiosyncratic to this dataset/problem.

Finally, the HF experiments were probably overkill: truncated BPTT (from the 1990s) was successfully used by Mikolov (see his PhD thesis) to train strong RNN language models. It is therefore plausible that truncated BPTT would also perform well here, and a confirmation would be useful.

Overall, this is an interesting analysis of deep RNNs. The principal results are: 1) deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs under a fixed parameter budget on character-level language modeling; 2) higher layers exhibit longer-range structure; and 3) truncated BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses. The work would be considerably stronger with a parallel analysis on a speech benchmark (e.g., showing both improved performance for deeper RNNs and evidence that deeper layers encode longer-range information).
