

This paper introduces Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), a hierarchical architecture where each layer is a recurrent network receiving the hidden state of the layer below. The authors argue that this structure naturally captures temporal hierarchies in time series—such as phonemes (short-scale) and sentences (long-scale)—which standard RNNs implicitly embed in dynamics. They demonstrate DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks on character-level language modeling (Wikipedia corpus) using only stochastic gradient descent (SGD), previously requiring Hessian-free optimization. Crucially, they analyze how different layers encode distinct time scales (e.g., bottom layers handle short-term character statistics, top layers model long-term dependencies like parentheses), and show DRNNs learn to treat opening/closing parentheses as "pseudo-stable attractor-like states."  

The work builds on prior hierarchical RNN proposals (e.g., [5, 8]), but distinguishes itself by analyzing time-scale emergence rather than merely achieving high accuracy. While [8] used stacked LSTMs for speech recognition with minimal architectural analysis, this paper provides a detailed empirical study of DRNNs’ temporal dynamics. The comparison to Hinton et al.’s [19] (the prior state-of-the-art for RNNs on this corpus) is well-motivated, though the authors note their DRNN-1O (output only at top layer) outperforms [19]’s Hessian-free MRNN while using simpler training.  

**Strengths:**  
- **Quality & Significance:** The technical analysis is rigorous. The time-scale experiments (typo perturbation, context-switching, parentheses closure) convincingly demonstrate how DRNNs develop hierarchical temporal processing. The parentheses test is particularly insightful: DRNN-AO closes brackets correctly in 88% of cases (vs. 72% for RNNs), showing explicit long-term memory beyond standard RNNs. The results advance the field by revealing *how* hierarchical RNNs operate—addressing a gap in prior work.  
- **Clarity & Originality:** The architecture (Fig. 1) and experiments are clearly described. The distinction between DRNN-1O (output at top layer) and DRNN-AO (output at all layers) is well-justified, and the analysis of layer contributions (Fig. 2, Table 2) effectively supports the core claim about time-scale hierarchy. The work is novel in its focus on *emergent* time scales in stacked RNNs, not just performance gains.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- **Scope of Comparison:** The evaluation is limited to RNN-based baselines (e.g., [19], [11], [16]), but the paper’s claim about "hierarchical processing" could be strengthened by comparing to non-RNN hierarchical models (e.g., hierarchical HMMs [21] or deep HMMs [22]). While Table 1 includes PAQ compression and Shannon’s entropy as rough benchmarks, these are not directly comparable (different datasets/criteria), reducing their utility.  
- **Results Ambiguity:** DRNN-AO slightly underperforms DRNN-1O (Table 1), but the paper does not clearly explain why output at all layers (theoretically beneficial) leads to worse results. The training strategy (layer-wise for DRNN-1O, full-network for DRNN-AO) is described, yet the trade-off between architectural design and optimization remains under-analyzed.  
- **Reproducibility:** Training for ~10 days on a single GPU (with 1.4B-character corpus) is resource-intensive. While the authors note parameter counts are comparable to [19], the lack of ablation studies (e.g., varying layer count, neuron count) limits understanding of the architecture’s robustness.  

**Recommendation:**  
This paper makes a valuable contribution by empirically validating that stacking RNNs *creates* a natural temporal hierarchy, not just a deeper network. The analysis of time-scale emergence is compelling and addresses a key gap in RNN literature. While the scope of comparison could be broadened, the core technical insights are significant for understanding how deep RNNs process time series. The results are sound, the writing is clear, and the work aligns with NIPS’ focus on principled advances in machine learning.  

**Pro:** Novel architecture revealing emergent time-scale hierarchy; rigorous analysis of layer-specific roles; state-of-the-art RNN results with simple SGD.  
**Con:** Limited comparison to non-RNN hierarchical models; DRNN-AO underperforms DRNN-1O without clear explanation; training cost high for large corpus.  

**Verdict:** Accept. The paper’s strength lies in its insightful analysis of *how* DRNNs process temporal hierarchies—a contribution beyond mere performance gains. This advances the understanding of recurrent architectures and provides a foundation for future work on hierarchical time-series modeling. The minor weaknesses do not undermine the core novelty or technical quality.
