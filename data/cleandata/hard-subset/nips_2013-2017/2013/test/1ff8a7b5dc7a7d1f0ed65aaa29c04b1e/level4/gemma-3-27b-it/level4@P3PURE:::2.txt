This paper investigates the usefulness of deep recurrent neural networks and reaches interesting conclusions: when holding the number of parameters constant, deeper recurrent neural networks outperform standard RNNs. Further interesting findings include an analysis demonstrating that the deeper networks contain more “long-term information,” and the result that plain backpropagation through time (BPTT) can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses – a task previously thought to require Hessian-Free optimization.

However, the paper’s claim to introduce the “deep RNN” is misleading, as such architectures have been used previously; for example, in Graves et al. (2013) [http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf], and likely earlier. The paper would be strengthened by acknowledging this prior work and focusing on its analysis rather than presenting the architecture as novel. 

The analysis, while meaningful, would be significantly more compelling if extended to other problems, such as a speech task. Otherwise, the observed findings may be an artifact of the specific character-level language modelling problem used. 

Regarding the Hessian-Free experiments, a more computationally efficient approach may have yielded comparable results. Truncated backpropagation through time (TBPTT), introduced in the 1990s, was successfully employed by Mikolov (as described in his PhD thesis) to train RNNs to be excellent language models. It is therefore likely that TBPTT would also perform well in this context, and it would be valuable to confirm this.

In summary, this work presents an interesting analysis of deep RNNs. The key results are: 1) deep RNNs outperform standard RNNs with a fixed number of parameters on character-level language modelling; 2) deeper layers exhibit more long-range structure; and 3) truncated BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses. While the results are interesting, the paper would be considerably stronger if it included a similar analysis on a speech task to demonstrate the generalizability of the findings and the benefits of deeper layers in capturing long-range dependencies.

**Reply to Rebuttal:**

I largely agree with the authors’ comment regarding the single task. It would still be worthwhile to explore truncated backpropagation with, for example, 30 steps. While it may be less effective at learning to balance parentheses, verifying this would be beneficial. Furthermore, even if unsuccessful in that specific task, it may still achieve competitive entropies at a significantly reduced computational cost.
