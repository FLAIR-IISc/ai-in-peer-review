This paper presents a hybrid deep and recurrent neural network architecture (DRNN) and demonstrates its strong performance on text prediction and compression tasks compared to existing approaches. A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to a series of experiments designed to argue that the higher-level layers learn to represent more abstract, longer-timescale structures in the data. While these experiments are interesting, I have a few reservations and suggestions for additional experiments that could strengthen the paper's claims.

Overall, this is a well-written and intellectually honest paper that investigates an important direction for temporal models. The authors are commendably thorough in discussing the potential shortcomings of their own analysis.

**Detailed Comments**

The paper's core motivation is compelling: in a standard RNN, older inputs arbitrarily receive more non-linear processing than more recent ones. Proposing a deep architecture where each time step's input is processed through multiple layers is a sensible and elegant way to address this. An alternative approach would be to add layers of non-linearities between the hidden state updates at each timestep; have the authors considered or experimented with this?

The analysis of the roles of different layers is one of the most interesting parts of the paper. Of the various experiments designed to probe these roles, the text generation results in Table 2 are the most persuasive. However, as the authors correctly point out, interpreting the effects of this kind of "lesion study" can be problematic due to the complex interdependencies that may develop between layers during training.

This leads to what I see as the most significant empirical question left unanswered by the paper: is the hierarchical structure genuinely responsible for the model's ability to capture longer-term, abstract properties? While the experiments show that higher layers *do* take on these roles, they don't conclusively demonstrate that this is a non-trivial consequence of the deep architecture. A crucial control experiment would be to compare the DRNN against a standard (single-layer) RNN with a comparable number of parameters. This would help disentangle the effects of depth from the effects of simply having a larger model.

On that note, the comparison to the standard RNN may be unfair. By my calculation, the DRNNs used in the experiments appear to have significantly more parameters than the baseline RNN (e.g., a 5-layer DRNN with 2119 hidden units per layer has far more parameters than a standard RNN with 2767 units). A more convincing comparison would involve a DRNN and a standard RNN with a matched parameter count. If the DRNN still outperforms a wider, shallower RNN, the paper's claims about the benefits of depth would be substantially strengthened.

Along these same lines, Figure 2 could be more informative. Instead of showing the effects of removing layers from a pre-trained model, it would be valuable to see a performance comparison of DRNNs of various depths (e.g., 1 to 5 layers), each trained from scratch. To ensure a fair comparison, the shallower models could be made wider to control for the total number of parameters or units.

Finally, a few smaller points:

*   Could the authors elaborate on the importance of gradient normalization and clarify how it "avoids the problem of bifurcations"? The discussion could be strengthened by relating this technique to recent work on gradient clipping in RNNs (e.g., the ICML 2013 paper by Pascanu et al.). Additionally, does this normalization interfere with the implicit averaging behavior of SGD?
*   For the Wikipedia compression task, a comparison to other relevant results, such as those from Mikolov et al., would provide valuable context for the reported performance.
*   Minor: The terms DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO are used in Figure 1 but should be explicitly defined in the main text for clarity.
