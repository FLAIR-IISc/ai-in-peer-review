- Introduces Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs) as a hybrid deep/recurrent architecture for text prediction and compression.  
- Core idea: older data may benefit from deeper processing than newer data; using deep RNN structure to capture short-term dependencies and broader effects is reasonable.  
- Suggest exploring adding extra nonlinearities between timesteps as a straightforward extension.  
- Gradient normalization is crucial but the explanation of how it avoids bifurcations is unclear; authors should clarify the mechanism.  
- Recommend citing related ICML 2013 work (University of Montreal) on gradient truncation/optimization in RNNs for relevant insights.  
- Consider whether gradient normalization affects the averaging behavior typically achieved with SGD.  
- Compare results to Mikolov et al.’s work on Wikipedia compression for better contextualization.  
- Text generation results (Table 2) are the most convincing experimental evidence.  
- Interpreting layer-manipulation effects is difficult due to complex interdependencies between layer outputs; authors correctly acknowledge this.  
- Open question: do higher layers truly process more abstract/long-term properties, or would those units behave similarly if placed in the first layer?  
- Need to demonstrate a non-trivial causal benefit from preceding layers rather than just observing emergent roles.  
- Experimental comparison may be unfair: the baseline RNN has fewer parameters than DRNNs; compare to standard RNNs with equivalent parameter/unit counts.  
- Suggest plotting performance of DRNNs with varying depths trained from scratch and possibly using wider layers in deeper models to account for parameter differences.  
- Minor: define acronyms DRNN-AO and IO in the text, not only in Figure 1.  
- Overall assessment: worthwhile, clearly written exploration with intellectual honesty and merit, but requires stronger empirical comparisons and clarifications.
