The paper "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks" explores the effectiveness of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) in character-level language modeling. The authors demonstrate that DRNNs outperform standard recurrent neural networks (RNNs) when the number of parameters is fixed, and provide an analysis of the emergent time scales in the deeper layers.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of standard RNNs and the potential benefits of using DRNNs. The experimental results are impressive, with the DRNNs achieving state-of-the-art performance on a Wikipedia-based text corpus. The analysis of the emergent time scales is also interesting, and provides insight into how the deeper layers of the DRNNs are able to capture longer-range dependencies in the data.

However, the paper has some limitations. The authors claim to have introduced the deep RNN architecture, but this is not accurate. Deep RNNs have been used before, for example in Graves et al. (2013). It is okay to not introduce new architectures and to focus on an analysis, but the authors should not claim to have introduced this architecture.

The analysis, while meaningful, would be even more interesting if it were shown on several problems, perhaps something on speech. Otherwise, the findings may be an artifact of the problem. Additionally, the authors do not provide a comparison with other architectures, such as long short-term memory (LSTM) networks, which are commonly used in language modeling tasks.

The authors also mention that they used plain backpropagation through time (BPTT) to train the DRNNs, but they do not discuss the potential limitations of this approach. In particular, they do not mention the problem of vanishing gradients, which can make it difficult to train deep RNNs using BPTT.

In terms of originality, the paper does not introduce new architectures or algorithms, but it does provide a novel analysis of the emergent time scales in DRNNs. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, but it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach and the potential avenues for future research.

In terms of significance, the paper demonstrates the potential benefits of using DRNNs in language modeling tasks, and provides insight into how these models are able to capture longer-range dependencies in the data. However, the results are limited to a single task, and it is not clear how well the approach will generalize to other tasks.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, but with some revisions to address the limitations mentioned above. The authors should also consider providing a more detailed discussion of the potential avenues for future research, and how their approach can be extended to other tasks.
