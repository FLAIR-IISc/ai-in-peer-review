The authors introduce a novel deep architecture that integrates the hierarchical aspects of deep learning with time-series modeling, reminiscent of HMMs and recurrent neural networks. The proposed training algorithm constructs the network in a layer-by-layer fashion, utilizing supervised (pre-)training with a next-letter prediction objective. The experimental results show that, after training large networks for approximately 10 days, the network's performance on the Wikipedia dataset published by Hinton et al. surpasses that of previous work. The authors then delve into an analysis of the network's task-solving approach, revealing that higher layers model long-term dependencies, and the correspondence between opening and closing parentheses is represented as a "pseudo-stable attractor-like state".

This paper is of good quality, featuring clear writing, well-designed experiments, and figures that effectively support the findings. However, my primary concern lies in the experimental setup. The comparison is limited to a single dataset, which appears to have received relatively little attention in the literature. Moreover, the comparison is restricted to RNN-related models, neglecting other hierarchical HMMs that can tackle similar tasks (although Table 1 lists alternative approaches, they are evaluated on a different corpus, rendering them incomparable).

The paper's primary contribution lies in its analysis of how the network operates, which is conducted thoroughly. Nevertheless, this focus diverges from the paper's original objective, which aimed to introduce a new deep architecture. While the paper is well-written, and the model is analyzed effectively, the results are somewhat inconclusive.
