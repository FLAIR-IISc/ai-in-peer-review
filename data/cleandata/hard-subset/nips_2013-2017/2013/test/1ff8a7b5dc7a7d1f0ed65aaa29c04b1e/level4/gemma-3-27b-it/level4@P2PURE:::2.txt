REPLY TO REBUTTAL:
I largely agree with the author's comment regarding the single task. It would still be valuable to explore truncated backpropagation, for example, with a length of 30 steps. While it might be less effective at learning to balance parentheses (this should be verified), it could potentially achieve competitive entropies with significantly reduced computational cost.

This paper investigates the usefulness of deep recurrent neural networks and reaches several interesting conclusions. Specifically, the authors demonstrate that deeper recurrent neural networks outperform standard RNNs when the number of parameters is held constant. Further noteworthy findings include an analysis revealing that deeper networks capture more “long-term information” and the result that plain backpropagation through time (BPTT) can successfully train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses – a task previously thought to require hierarchical reinforcement learning (HF). 

However, the claim of introducing the “deep RNN” is inaccurate, as this architecture has been used previously, for instance, in Graves et al. (2013) [http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf], and likely earlier. The paper should therefore refrain from asserting novelty in the architecture itself and instead focus on the presented analysis.

The analysis, while meaningful, would be strengthened by its application to a wider range of problems, such as a speech task. Without this broader evaluation, the findings risk being specific to the character-level language modelling problem and may not generalize. 

Finally, the HF experiments appear computationally expensive. Given the success of truncated backpropagation – introduced in the 1990s and effectively used by Mikolov (as detailed in his PhD thesis) to train high-performing RNN language models – it is plausible that truncated BPTT would also yield good results here. Confirmation of this hypothesis would be beneficial.

In summary, this work presents an interesting analysis of deep RNNs. The main results are: 1) deep RNNs outperform standard RNNs with a fixed parameter count on character-level language modelling; 2) deeper layers exhibit greater long-range structural information; and 3) truncated BPTT can effectively train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses. The results are interesting, but the paper would be considerably stronger if the analysis were extended to a speech task, demonstrating similar benefits of increased depth and long-range information capture in a different domain.
