This paper introduces the Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN), an architecture that stacks multiple RNN layers to create a hierarchy for processing time-series data. The core idea is that each layer operates on a different time scale, with lower layers capturing short-term dependencies and higher layers modeling longer-term structures. The authors propose two variants: one where only the top layer produces an output (DRNN-1O) and another where all layers contribute to the output (DRNN-AO). The DRNN is evaluated on a large-scale, character-level Wikipedia language modeling task. The paper demonstrates that this architecture, trained with a relatively simple stochastic gradient descent (SGD) method, achieves state-of-the-art performance, matching or exceeding results from more complex models trained with second-order methods like Hessian-free optimization. A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to analyzing the network's internal dynamics, providing evidence that the stacked architecture indeed learns a temporal hierarchy and can capture very long-term dependencies, such as matching opening and closing parentheses over hundreds of characters.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Quality and Significance of Analysis:** The paper's primary strength is not just in achieving a strong result, but in the detailed analysis of *how* the model works. The authors conduct several insightful experiments to probe the network's behavior:
    *   The "typo perturbation" experiment effectively visualizes the different memory time scales that emerge in each layer, confirming the central hypothesis of a learned temporal hierarchy.
    *   The analysis of parenthesis matching is a compelling case study demonstrating the model's ability to learn long-range, non-trivial structural dependencies. Describing this as learning a "pseudo-stable attractor-like state" provides a valuable conceptual model.
    *   The generative text examples, particularly the ablations where different layers' contributions are removed, offer clear qualitative insights into the roles of the lower and higher layers in the hierarchy.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark using a simpler and more widely accessible training algorithm (SGD with gradient clipping) than the previously best-performing model (which used Hessian-free optimization). This is a significant result, suggesting that architectural improvements can be as, or more, impactful than advances in optimization algorithms for this class of problems.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The architecture is explained concisely, the experimental setup is detailed, and the results are presented with well-designed figures that effectively support the paper's claims. The authors provide sufficient detail to understand their methodology.

4.  **Originality:** While the concept of stacked or hierarchical RNNs has been explored before (as the authors acknowledge, citing Graves et al., 2013), this work's contribution is novel in its systematic investigation and analysis of the emergent properties of this architecture. It shifts the focus from purely performance-driven engineering to a deeper understanding of the model's internal representations, which is a valuable scientific contribution.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection/Revision):**

1.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** The primary weakness is that the empirical validation is confined to a single task and dataset: character-level language modeling on one specific Wikipedia corpus. While this is a challenging task, it is unclear how well the architecture and its observed properties generalize to other sequential data domains like speech recognition, music generation, or video analysis. The paper would be much stronger with results on at least one other distinct task.

2.  **Narrow Comparison to Other Models:** The comparison is largely restricted to other RNN-based models. Given the paper's focus on hierarchical temporal modeling, a discussion or comparison with other classes of hierarchical models, such as Hierarchical Hidden Markov Models (HHMMs) or other structured-prediction approaches, would have provided valuable context and strengthened the claims of the DRNN's advantages.

3.  **Ad-hoc Training Procedure:** The best-performing model (DRNN-1O) requires a specific, incremental layer-wise training procedure to achieve good performance. This suggests that training a very deep RNN end-to-end from a random initialization is difficult, which may limit the practical applicability of the architecture. While this finding is itself interesting, it is presented as a necessary trick rather than being fully analyzed as a property of the model.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a significant contribution to the understanding of deep learning models for sequential data. Its main strengths are the impressive state-of-the-art results achieved with a simple training method and, most importantly, the insightful and detailed analysis of the model's emergent temporal hierarchy. This analysis provides strong evidence for the central claims and offers valuable insights that are likely to influence future research in recurrent architectures.

The main weakness is the narrow experimental scope, focusing on a single dataset and task. However, the depth and quality of the analysis on this task partially compensate for the lack of breadth. The findings regarding long-term dependencies and hierarchical time scales are compelling and of broad interest to the NIPS community.

Overall, the strengths of the paper convincingly outweigh its weaknesses. It is a well-executed piece of research that is both technically sound and provides novel insights.

**I recommend acceptance.**
