I appreciate the observation that older inputs can pass through many more layers of computation than more recent inputs. Constructing a deep RNN seems like a reasonable way to improve short-term modeling, among other possible benefits. Another straightforward variation would be to insert additional nonlinear layers between successive timesteps—have you tried this?

How critical was gradient normalization, and in what sense does it “avoid the problem of bifurcations”? There is a recent ICML 2013 paper from the University of Montreal on gradient truncation and RNN optimization that may be relevant. Also, could this style of gradient normalization disrupt the averaging behavior of SGD?

For prior work on Wikipedia compression benchmarks, you might also compare against results by Mikolov et al.

Among the experiments dissecting the roles of different layers in terms of time scale and potential “abstraction,” the text generation study (Table 2) is the most convincing to me. However, as you note in the paragraph around line 241, interpreting this kind of “brain surgery” on RNNs is tricky due to complex interdependencies that can form among the outputs of different layers.

The biggest empirical gap, in my view, is whether higher layers actually handle more abstract, longer-term properties better than if the same units were placed in the first layer—i.e., whether they benefit nontrivially from the extra preceding processing stages. The fact that they appear to adopt more abstract and longer-range roles after training is encouraging but not definitive evidence.

I also notice that the standard RNN appears to use fewer parameters in these experiments, since 2767^2 × 5 > 2119^2 (I’m counting both recurrent and inter-layer weight matrices, hence the factor of 2), so the comparison may be somewhat unfair. It would be more compelling if the deeper RNNs outperformed a standard RNN with the same number of parameters, or better yet, the same number of units. For example, compare a 2-layer DRNN to a single-layer RNN with the same number of units, in a setup where parameter-count differences do not bias the results in favor of the RNN so strongly as to undermine the fairness of the comparison. This would substantially strengthen the paper’s claims, in my opinion.

Also, instead of Figure 2, it would be more informative to show how different DRNN depths perform on the benchmarks when trained from scratch, possibly making shallower models wider to equalize the number of parameters and/or units.

This paper investigates a hybrid of deep and recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), which resemble deep networks but include per-layer recurrent connections operating through time. The authors demonstrate that this architecture performs very well for text prediction/compression relative to existing approaches. A large part of the paper presents experiments arguing that higher layers capture more abstract and longer-term structure. These studies are quite persuasive, though I have a few reservations (detailed above) and would welcome a couple more experiments. Overall, it is valuable to explore such deep temporal architectures and to analyze their post-training behavior. The paper is also clearly written and admirably candid about potential issues and limitations, which I especially appreciate.
