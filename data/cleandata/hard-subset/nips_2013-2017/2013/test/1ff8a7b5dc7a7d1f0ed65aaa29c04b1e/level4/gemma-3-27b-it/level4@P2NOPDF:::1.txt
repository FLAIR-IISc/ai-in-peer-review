This paper introduces a novel deep learning architecture that integrates the hierarchical representation learning of deep networks with time-series modeling techniques commonly found in Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs). The authors employ a layer-by-layer supervised training procedure, pre-training each layer with a next-letter prediction objective. They demonstrate that, after an extensive training period of approximately ten days on substantial computational resources, their model achieves improved performance on a Wikipedia text dataset previously used by Hinton et al., surpassing results reported in prior work. A significant portion of the paper is then dedicated to analyzing the internal workings of the trained network, revealing insights into how it tackles the task. Notably, the authors observe that long-term dependencies are modeled in deeper layers, and that the network appears to represent matching parentheses through “pseudo-stable attractor-like states.”

Overall, this is a well-written and technically sound paper. The experiments are clearly presented and the accompanying figures effectively illustrate the reported findings. However, my primary concern lies with the experimental evaluation. The model is evaluated solely on a single dataset, one that doesn't appear to have attracted a large body of existing research. The direct performance comparisons are limited to other RNN-based approaches; a comparison against more traditional hierarchical HMMs, or other models capable of addressing similar sequential tasks, would strengthen the evaluation considerably. While Table 1 acknowledges related work, the use of a different corpus renders these comparisons difficult to interpret.

The paper’s primary contribution resides in its insightful analysis of *how* the network functions—an analysis that is, indeed, performed thoroughly—rather than in a dramatic improvement in state-of-the-art performance. While the authors explicitly aim to propose a new architecture combining the strengths of deep learning and time-series modeling, the results, while positive, feel somewhat incremental given the computational cost and lengthy training time. 

In summary, the paper presents a well-articulated model and a compelling analysis of its behavior. However, the limited scope of the experimental evaluation and the resulting inconclusive nature of the performance gains temper the overall impact.
