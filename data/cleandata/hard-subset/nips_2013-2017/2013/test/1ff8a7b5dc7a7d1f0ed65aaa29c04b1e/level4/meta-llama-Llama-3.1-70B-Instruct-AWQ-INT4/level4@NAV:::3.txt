I appreciate the authors' observation about older data benefiting from more extensive processing compared to newer data, which can be addressed by employing a deep RNN. A potential extension could be introducing additional layers of nonlinearities between each timestep. It would be interesting to know if the authors have explored this approach.

The role of gradient normalization in avoiding the "problem of bifurcations" is not entirely clear to me. I would like to see a more detailed explanation of its importance. Additionally, a recent ICML 2013 paper on gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs may be relevant to this discussion. It is also worth considering whether normalizing the gradient in this way could interfere with the "averaging" behavior of SGD.

In the context of Wikipedia compression tasks, the authors may want to compare their results with those obtained by Mikolov et al. The experiment that I found most compelling was the text generation task (Table 2). However, as the authors themselves note, interpreting the effects of "brain surgery" on RNNs can be problematic due to the complex interdependencies between layer outputs.

A critical aspect of the empirical analysis that is not fully addressed is whether the higher layers are truly better at processing abstract and long-term properties of the text, or if they are simply benefiting from the additional processing steps. The fact that they take on more abstract roles after training is encouraging but not conclusive evidence.

I noticed that the regular RNN has fewer parameters in these experiments, which may make the comparison unfair. A more convincing demonstration would be to show that deeper RNNs outperform a standard RNN with the same number of parameters or units. For example, comparing a 2-layer DRNN with a standard RNN having the same number of units would strengthen the paper's claims.

Instead of Figure 2, it would be more informative to see the performance of various DRNN depths on the benchmarks when trained from scratch, possibly with wider layers to compensate for the difference in parameters and/or units.

Minor suggestions:

- The authors should define DRNN-AO and IO in the text, not just in Figure 1.
This paper explores a hybrid deep and recurrent neural network architecture called DRNNs, which combines the strengths of both deep networks and recurrent connections. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this architecture for text prediction and compression tasks. While the experiments are convincing, I have some reservations and would like to see additional experiments to further support the claims.

I think it is valuable to investigate these deep temporal networks and gain insight into their behavior after training. The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and commendably transparent about its potential limitations and challenges.
