Summary
This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) formed by stacking recurrent layers, where each layer receives the hidden state of the layer below. Two output variants are considered: DRNN-1O, with outputs only from the top layer, and DRNN-AO, with outputs from all layers. The authors train these models with stochastic gradient descent (SGD) using gradient normalization and a linear learning-rate schedule. For DRNN-1O, they employ incremental layer-wise supervised training while always optimizing the full network. The primary evaluation is on character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus. Both DRNNs achieve bits-per-character (BPC) comparable to the best reported RNN-based results with similar parameter counts, previously obtained with multiplicative RNNs (MRNNs) trained by Hessian-free methods. Beyond accuracy, the paper offers a thoughtful analysis: ablations of layer contributions to output, perturbation tests showing emergent layer-dependent time scales, and qualitative and quantitative evidence that higher layers capture longer-term structure (e.g., closing parentheses over long spans). These analyses suggest the stacked recurrence induces a temporal hierarchy.

Relation to prior work
The idea of hierarchical recurrent architectures has a significant history (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio, 1996; Fernández et al., 2007), and stacked LSTMs (e.g., Graves et al., 2013) are closely related. Sutskever et al. (2011) demonstrated strong character-level modeling with MRNNs and Hessian-free optimization. The current paper differs in two ways: (1) it uses simple SGD and shows competitive RNN performance at similar parameter budgets, and (2) it provides systematic analyses of emergent time scales and layer roles. Compared to earlier work focused on accuracy (e.g., bi-directional deep LSTMs on TIMIT), this paper contributes more interpretability.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated architecture and training setup, with unambiguous equations and careful hyperparameter reporting (sequence length, BPTT window, parameter counts).
- Strong empirical analysis beyond headline performance: layer-output ablations; typo perturbation to measure memory decay; context-switch tests to quantify effective context length; generative experiments; and a targeted test of long-distance dependencies via parentheses.
- Evidence for emergent hierarchical time scales is convincing, including differences between DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO tied to their training procedure.
- Demonstrates that SGD with gradient normalization can train large stacked RNNs to competitive performance without second-order methods; this is practically useful.
- The paper is well written and figures support the claims.

Weaknesses
- Limited experimental scope: one dataset and a small set of model variants due to computational constraints. No variance estimates across random seeds.
- Baseline coverage is narrow. Comparisons to MRNNs are indirect and complicated by different character vocabularies (95 vs. 86). There is no LSTM baseline on the same corpus, and non-neural baselines (e.g., strong n-gram LMs or compression-based models like PAQ) are not evaluated on the same data, limiting interpretability of Table 1.
- Novelty is more incremental on the architectural side; stacked recurrent layers and multi-output connections have close precedents. The most distinctive contribution is the analysis.
- Some training choices merit further justification: zero initialization of output weights; the effect of gradient normalization vs. clipping; the necessity and benefits of the incremental supervised pretraining for DRNN-1O; and the decision to keep equal layer widths.
- Comparability concerns: using a different character set than prior work may affect BPC. The paper asserts minimal impact, but an empirical check would strengthen the claim.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The training methodology and analysis protocols are appropriate and clearly described. The perturbation-based analysis is careful and averaged over many instances. However, the limited baselines and dataset coverage prevent a definitive statement about state-of-the-art beyond “among RNNs with similar parameter counts on this corpus.”

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The motivation for stacked recurrence and for outputs at all layers is well articulated, and figures are informative. Minor suggestions:
- Clarify the relationship between “gradient normalization” and standard norm clipping, and report the typical gradient norms observed.
- Provide validation protocol details (e.g., how hyperparameters were chosen) and any early stopping or regularization used.
- Explicitly state the effect of using 95 vs. 86 characters by re-evaluating on the 86-character vocabulary, or provide a controlled estimate.

Originality
Moderate. Stacked recurrent architectures and hierarchical RNNs have prior art; similarly, outputs from multiple layers are common in deep nets. The originality lies primarily in the careful, empirically grounded analysis of time scales and long-term structure within stacked RNNs trained with SGD.

Significance
The analysis is valuable to the community and will likely inform design and training of deep recurrent architectures. Demonstrating competitive performance with simple SGD is practically relevant. However, the impact on state-of-the-art is limited by the constrained comparisons and single dataset.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you report results for an LSTM or stacked LSTM baseline on the same dataset and vocabulary? This would situate DRNNs among widely used recurrent architectures.
- How sensitive are results to the gradient normalization scheme? An ablation comparing no normalization, clipping at various thresholds, and full normalization would be useful.
- For DRNN-1O, is incremental training essential? Please show a comparison with/end-to-end training from scratch.
- Could you include error bars across multiple initializations for the main models, or at least report variance on a held-out subset?
- Please reconcile the different character vocabularies by re-running (or approximating) results on the 86-character set to enable direct comparison with Sutskever et al.
- Consider adding a second corpus (e.g., enwik8, text8, or Penn Treebank character-level) to test generality.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-executed analysis revealing emergent time scales and layer roles in DRNNs.
- Competitive RNN performance with simple training.
- Good writing and reproducible methodology.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited baselines and single dataset make it hard to assess true significance.
- Architectural novelty is incremental relative to prior hierarchical/stacked RNN work.
- Some training choices are ad hoc and insufficiently ablated.

Overall assessment
A solid and insightful paper whose primary contribution is the analysis of stacked recurrent architectures rather than a decisive advance in accuracy. With stronger baselines and tighter comparability, it would be a clear accept. As it stands, it is borderline to weak accept on the strength of its careful analyses and clarity.
