

The paper presents a compelling exploration of Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs) for text prediction and compression, with a strong emphasis on understanding how hierarchical processing layers interact with temporal dynamics. The core insight—that older data benefits from deeper processing due to accumulated temporal context—is well-motivated and aligns with recent work on hierarchical representations in sequential models. The DRNN architecture, which integrates recurrent connections across layers, demonstrates clear empirical advantages over standard RNNs, particularly for short-term dependencies. The text generation experiment (Table 2) provides the most persuasive evidence for the functional specialization of higher layers, though the authors rightly acknowledge limitations in interpreting "brain surgery" interventions due to complex layer interdependencies. Overall, the paper is well-written, intellectually rigorous, and transparent about its constraints—qualities that significantly enhance its credibility.

That said, several key concerns require clarification or additional experiments to strengthen the claims. First, the parameter count comparison between standard RNNs and DRNNs appears unfair. The standard RNN uses 2119² parameters (for recurrent weights), while the DRNN employs 2767² (for its recurrent weights alone), ignoring that DRNNs also have inter-layer weights. This disparity likely inflates the DRNN’s advantage. A more convincing comparison would use DRNNs with equivalent total parameters (e.g., a 2-layer DRNN vs. a standard RNN with matching weight count) or equivalent units (e.g., wider standard RNN layers). Without this, the results could merely reflect capacity differences rather than architectural superiority. The authors should address this in revisions.

Second, the role of gradient normalization (specifically "DRNN-AO") needs deeper justification. The claim that it "avoids the problem of bifurcations" is unclear—bifurcations in what context? The recent ICML 2013 work by Bengio et al. on gradient truncation in RNNs (which addresses similar stability issues) should be discussed, as it may offer relevant insights or alternative approaches. Crucially, normalizing gradients could disrupt SGD’s averaging behavior, potentially altering convergence or generalization. The authors should explicitly test whether gradient normalization alone (without the DRNN structure) yields comparable gains, or whether the effect is uniquely tied to the deep architecture.

Third, the paper’s strongest claim—that higher DRNN layers process *abstract* and *long-term* properties—lacks causal evidence. While higher layers exhibit longer-term sensitivity (e.g., in Table 2), this could simply reflect that lower layers first extract low-level features, which higher layers then process. The authors must demonstrate that *repositioning* units from higher to lower layers *reduces* performance on long-term tasks (e.g., by "surgery" experiments where high-layer units are moved to the bottom). Without this, the observed role specialization may be correlative rather than functional. The current analysis (line 241) correctly notes the interdependency challenge, but the paper stops short of addressing it empirically.

Additional minor points:  
- Define DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO in the main text (not just Figure 1).  
- Compare results against Mikolov et al.’s Wikipedia compression work to contextualize performance.  
- Figure 2 should show depth-scaling results (e.g., 1–5 layer DRNNs) trained from scratch, with width-adjusted baselines to control for parameter count. A single "best" DRNN model is insufficient to prove depth benefits.

The paper’s greatest strength is its focus on *mechanistic interpretation* of deep temporal models, which is underexplored in the literature. The proposed experiments (e.g., layer-wise ablation, text generation) are well-designed to probe functional specialization. However, the parameter fairness issue is critical—it risks undermining the primary contribution. Addressing this, alongside the abstraction evidence gap, would transform this from a strong empirical study into a definitive analysis of hierarchical RNNs. The intellectual honesty in acknowledging limitations (e.g., the "brain surgery" caveat) is commendable and should be preserved.

In summary, the paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding deep temporal architectures. With revisions to the experimental design (parameter-matched comparisons, causal abstraction tests) and clearer technical explanations (gradient normalization, bifurcation context), it would be a significant advance. As it stands, the evidence for the *functional necessity* of deep processing—beyond mere capacity—remains incomplete. The authors have the opportunity to strengthen this substantially with targeted experiments. I recommend acceptance pending these revisions.
