REPLY TO REBUTTAL: 

I largely concur with the suggestion to focus on a single task. Nevertheless, it would be worthwhile to experiment with truncated backpropagation using approximately 30 steps. Although this approach may be less likely to learn to balance parentheses, it could still achieve competitive entropies at a reduced computational cost, making it a valuable verification to conduct.

The paper explores the effectiveness of deep recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and presents intriguing conclusions. Specifically, when the number of parameters is held constant, deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs. Two notable findings are the analysis demonstrating that deeper networks contain more "long-term information" and the result that plain backpropagation through time (BPTT) can successfully train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, a task previously thought to be achievable only with Hessian-free (HF) optimization.

However, the paper's claim to introduce deep RNNs is inaccurate, as this architecture has been employed in previous studies, such as the work presented in http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf. It is acceptable for the paper to focus on analysis rather than introducing novel architectures, but it should not claim to have pioneered this concept.

While the analysis is meaningful, its significance would be enhanced by demonstrating its applicability to multiple problems, including speech tasks. This would help to verify that the findings are not specific to the problem at hand. Furthermore, the HF experiments may have been computationally expensive, whereas the truncated backpropagation approach, introduced in the 1990s and successfully employed by Mikolov in his PhD thesis to train RNNs as excellent language models, could potentially yield comparable results. It would be beneficial to confirm this hypothesis.

This work presents an interesting analysis of deep RNNs, yielding two primary results: (1) deep RNNs outperform standard RNNs when the number of parameters is fixed in character-level language modeling, and (2) the deeper layers exhibit more long-range structure. Although the results are intriguing, the paper would be strengthened by a similar analysis on a speech task, demonstrating the superiority of deeper RNNs and the presence of long-range information in their deeper layers.
