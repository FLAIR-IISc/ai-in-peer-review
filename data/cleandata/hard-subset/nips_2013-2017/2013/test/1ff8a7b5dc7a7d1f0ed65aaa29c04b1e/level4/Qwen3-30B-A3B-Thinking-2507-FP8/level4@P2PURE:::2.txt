

REPLY TO REBUTTAL:  
- I largely agree with the comment regarding the single-task focus.  
- It would still be worthwhile to test truncated backpropagation with, e.g., 30 steps. While this approach is less likely to learn to balance parentheses (though it would be valuable to confirm this), it may achieve competitive entropies at a significantly reduced computational cost.  

The paper investigates the usefulness of deep recurrent neural networks. It reports several interesting findings: (1) with a fixed number of parameters, deeper recurrent neural networks outperform standard RNNs on character-level language modeling; (2) deeper networks retain more "long-term information"; and (3) plain backpropagation through time (BPTT) can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses—a capability previously thought achievable only with high-frequency (HF) methods.  

However, the paper claims to introduce the "deep RNN" architecture, which has been used prior to this work (e.g., Graves et al., 2013, and likely earlier). The paper should not assert that it introduces this architecture, though it is acceptable to focus on analysis without proposing novel architectures.  

The analysis, while meaningful, would be significantly strengthened by extending it to multiple tasks—such as speech recognition—to confirm whether the findings generalize beyond character-level language modeling. Without this, the results could be specific to the problem studied.  

Additionally, the HF experiments appear unnecessarily costly. Truncated BPTT (introduced in the 1990s) was successfully employed by Mikolov (in his PhD thesis) to train highly effective language models. Thus, truncated BPTT likely suffices for the parenthesis-balancing task and would be worthwhile to test, providing a more efficient validation of the approach.  

This work presents an interesting analysis of deep RNNs. The two main results are: (1) deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs under fixed parameter count on character-level language modeling, and (2) deeper layers exhibit greater long-range structure. The result that truncated BPTT trains character-level RNNs to balance parentheses is noteworthy. However, given the analysis is confined to a single task, the paper would be substantially stronger with a parallel analysis on a speech task (e.g., demonstrating improved performance and greater long-range information in deeper layers).
