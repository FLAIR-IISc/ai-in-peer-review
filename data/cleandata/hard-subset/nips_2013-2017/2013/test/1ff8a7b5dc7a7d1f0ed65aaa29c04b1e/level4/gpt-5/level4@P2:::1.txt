Summary
The paper proposes a deep recurrent neural network (DRNN) architecture that stacks recurrent layers to induce a temporal hierarchy, with each layer receiving the hidden state of the layer below. Two output configurations are studied: connecting only the top layer to the output (DRNN-1O) and connecting all layers (DRNN-AO). Training is done with stochastic gradient descent and gradient normalization; for DRNN-1O, the authors use a supervised, incremental layer-wise procedure to mitigate vanishing gradients. The main task is character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus, with models constrained to roughly 4.9M parameters to match prior work. The DRNNs achieve bits-per-character comparable to the best reported RNN results of similar size, previously obtained with multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization. Beyond accuracy, the paper analyzes emergent time scales per layer via perturbation tests, measures effective context length for prediction, evaluates generative behavior, and presents qualitative evidence that upper layers capture longer dependencies (e.g., consistently modeling closing parentheses across long spans). The work relates to earlier hierarchical RNNs and stacked LSTMs (e.g., on TIMIT), but focuses more on analysis of temporal hierarchy and training with simple SGD.

Quality
- The paper is technically sound and clearly describes the architectures, training setup, and evaluation metric. The analysis is careful, with informative diagnostics (layer ablations, perturbation studies, and qualitative generation).
- The experimental design is coherent (matched parameter counts, controlled training schedules), though some choices (e.g., zero-initialized output layers, specific learning-rate schedules, and layer-wise pretraining only for DRNN-1O) could benefit from more justification or ablation.
- Claims about emergent time scales are supported by multiple measurements, and the qualitative parentheses study is compelling. However, broader empirical validation is limited.

Clarity
- The manuscript is well organized, readable, and figures support the conclusions. The presentation of the DRNN variants and training procedures is clear.
- Minor suggestions: clarify the gradient “normalization” procedure (is it clipping to unit norm or scaling by a cap?), report variability across runs (if negligible, state that), and more explicitly discuss computational budget and total data passes relative to compared methods.

Originality
- Architecturally, DRNNs are essentially stacked RNNs, a concept present in prior work (e.g., hierarchical/stacked RNNs and stacked LSTMs). The novelty lies mainly in the systematic analysis of layer-wise time scales and the demonstration that simple SGD can reach strong character-level performance with this architecture.
- The incremental supervised layer-wise training for DRNN-1O is a practical contribution, though similar ideas have appeared in deep network pretraining.

Significance
- The empirical results match state-of-the-art RNN performance of similar size on this dataset using a simpler optimizer, which is valuable from a practical standpoint.
- The analyses provide insights into how depth induces temporal hierarchy and how different layers contribute to short- vs. long-range structure. These observations may inform future architecture and training design.
- That said, the empirical scope is narrow (one dataset) and baselines are limited, which makes it difficult to assess broader impact on sequence modeling.

Strengths
- Clear, well-written paper with thoughtful analyses.
- Demonstrates that stacked RNNs trained with SGD can be competitive with more complex training methods.
- Compelling evidence for emergent time scales and long-term structure (e.g., parentheses handling).
- Useful diagnostics (layer ablations, typo/context perturbations) that others can adopt.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation on a single corpus; limited comparators beyond MRNN and a compression baseline. Missing strong baselines such as LSTM/stacked LSTM, high-order n-grams, and hierarchical HMMs on the same data.
- Architectural novelty is modest; much of the contribution is analytical rather than introducing a fundamentally new model.
- Limited exploration of design space (depth, width, output-connection patterns) and lack of statistical variability across runs.
- Fairness of comparisons is hard to judge without clearer accounting of compute/data passes relative to prior work.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments on additional standard character-level benchmarks (e.g., enwik8/text8, Penn Treebank chars) and include LSTM/stacked LSTM and strong n-gram/compression baselines trained/evaluated under the same protocol.
- Provide ablations on depth, per-layer sizes, and output-connection strategies; compare the incremental layer-wise schedule against full joint training for DRNN-1O.
- Report run-to-run variance and training cost (GPU-days, effective epochs) for fairer comparison to Hessian-free results.
- Clarify the gradient normalization method and motivate zero initialization of output layers.
- Consider releasing code/configurations to aid reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid, carefully executed analysis that advances understanding of temporal hierarchy in deep RNNs.
- Practical takeaway that simple SGD with stacked RNNs can achieve strong performance.
- Clear writing and instructive experiments likely to be useful to the community.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical validation (single dataset, few baselines) weakens claims of general advantage.
- Architectural novelty is incremental relative to prior stacked/hierarchical RNN and LSTM work.
- Conclusions about superiority over alternatives are not fully supported without broader comparisons.

Overall
A well-written and insightful paper that contributes meaningful analysis of deep recurrent architectures, with competitive results using simple training. However, the empirical validation is narrow and the novelty is more in the analysis than in the architecture itself. I view this as borderline; strengthening the experimental comparisons and breadth would substantially improve its case.
