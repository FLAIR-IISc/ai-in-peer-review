The authors present a novel deep learning architecture that integrates the hierarchical structure of deep networks with time-series modelling techniques common in Hidden Markov Models and recurrent neural networks. Their training methodology involves a layer-wise construction of the network, utilizing supervised pre-training with a next-letter prediction objective. Extensive experiments, requiring approximately ten days of training for large networks, demonstrate performance gains on a Wikipedia dataset (as introduced by Hinton et al.) compared to existing approaches. The paper further provides insightful analysis into the network’s operational characteristics; for instance, it observes that higher layers capture long-term dependencies and that balanced structures, such as parenthesis, are represented as “pseudo-stable attractor-like states.”

This is a well-written and high-quality paper with well-designed experiments and figures that effectively illustrate the findings. However, the experimental evaluation is a primary concern. The assessment relies solely on a single dataset that appears relatively underexplored, and the direct performance comparisons are limited to recurrent neural network-based models. While Table 1 acknowledges alternative approaches like hierarchical HMMs capable of tackling similar tasks, these are evaluated on a different corpus, rendering a direct comparison difficult. 

The paper’s principal contribution lies in its detailed analysis of *how* the network functions, a component executed to a high standard, rather than demonstrating a substantial performance advancement – although this wasn’t necessarily the paper’s explicit goal. Ultimately, a novel deep architecture blending hierarchical feature learning with time-series modelling is proposed and well-analyzed, but the results, while promising, remain somewhat inconclusive due to the limited scope of experimental validation.
