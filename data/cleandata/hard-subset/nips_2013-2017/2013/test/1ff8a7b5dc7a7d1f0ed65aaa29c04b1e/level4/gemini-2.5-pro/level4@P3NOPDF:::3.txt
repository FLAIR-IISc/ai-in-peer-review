This paper introduces the Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN), an architecture that extends standard RNNs by incorporating multiple recurrent layers. The authors demonstrate its effectiveness on text prediction and compression tasks. A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to a series of experiments designed to argue that higher layers in the DRNN learn to represent more abstract and longer-term structures in the data. The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and laudably transparent about its own potential shortcomings. The investigation into deep temporal models is a valuable contribution, and the analysis of the network's internal functions is insightful. While the experiments are promising, I have a few reservations and suggestions for additional experiments that could further strengthen the paper's claims.

**Strengths and Contributions**

The central motivation for the architecture is compelling: in a deep RNN, inputs from the distant past are processed through more non-linear layers than recent inputs, naturally creating a hierarchy of timescales. The proposal to build a deep RNN is a reasonable approach for better modeling short-term dependencies while retaining long-term memory.

The series of experiments designed to examine the roles of different layers is particularly interesting. The text generation experiment (Table 2), which analyzes the effect of disabling specific layers, is the most persuasive in demonstrating a functional hierarchy.

**Major Concerns and Questions**

My primary concern revolves around the empirical evaluation and the need for more controlled comparisons to firmly establish the benefits of depth.

1.  **Fairness of Baselines and the Benefit of Depth:** The paper's central claim is that depth allows the network to learn a hierarchy of representations. However, the most critical piece of evidence—that higher layers are more effective at this task *than if their units were simply moved to the first layer*—is not fully established. The fact that deeper layers happen to learn longer-term roles is interesting but incomplete evidence.

    To make a stronger case, the DRNN should be compared against a standard (shallow) RNN with a comparable number of parameters or, ideally, units. In the current experiments, the standard RNN baseline appears to have fewer parameters than the DRNN models (e.g., a 5-layer DRNN with 2119 units per layer has significantly more parameters than a standard RNN with 2767 units). This makes the comparison potentially unfair, as the performance gains could be attributed to the larger model capacity rather than the architectural depth. A more convincing experiment would compare, for instance, a 2-layer DRNN against a standard RNN with enough hidden units to match the parameter count. If the DRNN still performs better, it would provide much stronger evidence for the benefits of depth.

2.  **Gradient Normalization Details:** The paper mentions gradient normalization as an important factor in its success. Could the authors elaborate on how critical this was for training? Specifically, how does this technique "avoid the problem of bifurcations"? A more detailed explanation or reference would be beneficial. Additionally, does normalizing the gradient in this manner have any unintended consequences for the averaging behavior of SGD? It would be useful to connect this method to recent literature on training RNNs, such as the work on gradient clipping by Pascanu et al. at ICML 2013, which also addresses optimization difficulties.

3.  **Interpretation of Ablation Experiments:** While the "brain surgery" experiments are insightful, the authors correctly point out the difficulty of interpreting their effects due to the complex interdependencies that may develop between layers during training. This is an important caveat that somewhat limits the conclusiveness of these findings.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Alternative Architectures:** A deep RNN is one way to add more processing steps. Another straightforward approach would be to add more layers of non-linearities *between* timesteps within a single recurrent layer. Have the authors considered or experimented with this alternative? Comparing against such a model could provide further insight into the best way to leverage depth in temporal models.

2.  **Comparison to Prior Work:** For the Wikipedia compression tasks, a comparison to other state-of-the-art results, such as those from Mikolov et al., would help to contextualize the performance of the DRNN and better establish its contributions.

3.  **Experimental Visualization:** Instead of Figure 2, it would be more informative to see a plot showing the final benchmark performance of DRNNs with varying depths (e.g., 1 to 5 layers), each trained from scratch. To ensure a fair comparison across depths, shallower models could be made wider to maintain a similar parameter budget to their deeper counterparts. This would directly address the question of how performance scales with depth under a fixed parameter count.

**Minor Issues**

*   The terms DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO are used in Figure 1 but are not defined in the main text. Please add explicit definitions for clarity.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a thought-provoking paper that explores an important direction for temporal modeling. The core ideas are interesting, and the analysis is thorough. Addressing the concerns about experimental baselines, particularly by comparing against a parameter-matched shallow RNN, would substantially strengthen the paper's claims and overall impact.
