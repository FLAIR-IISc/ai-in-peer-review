

The authors present an interesting exploration of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) for text prediction, particularly highlighting the role of hierarchical processing in capturing long-term dependencies. I appreciate the observation that older data benefits from deeper processing due to its greater temporal context, and the DRNN architecture indeed offers a promising approach for handling shorter-term dynamics alongside this. A natural extension would be to insert additional nonlinear layers between timesteps; have the authors explored this?

Regarding gradient normalization, I'd like clarification on its necessity and the claim that it "avoids the problem of bifurcations." A relevant recent work is the ICML 2013 paper by researchers from U of Montreal on gradient truncation in RNNs, which may provide context. Additionally, normalizing gradients could potentially disrupt SGD's natural averaging behavior—would this affect convergence or generalization?

For empirical grounding, the comparison to Mikolov et al.'s work on Wikipedia compression tasks would strengthen the paper's positioning. Among the experiments examining layer roles, the text generation results (Table 2) are the most compelling. However, as noted on line 241, interpreting "brain surgery" interventions is complicated by the intricate interdependencies between layers, limiting causal claims about their specific contributions.

The most significant gap lies in demonstrating that higher layers *require* the preceding hierarchical processing to capture abstract, long-term features. While it's observed that higher layers develop these roles post-training, this alone doesn't prove they benefit nontrivially from the deeper architecture. A critical experiment would directly test: if we relocate units from higher layers to the first layer (while preserving the same total parameters), does performance degrade? If so, this would confirm that the hierarchical structure itself is essential for processing abstract features, rather than merely coinciding with them.

The parameter count discrepancy raises a fairness concern. The standard RNN uses fewer parameters (2767² × 5 vs. 2119²), as both recurrent and inter-layer weight matrices are counted. This imbalance likely favors the RNN, making the comparison potentially misleading. A more convincing test would compare a 2-layer DRNN against a standard RNN with identical total parameters or units—e.g., a single-layer RNN with the same number of units as the DRNN's hidden layers. This would isolate the architectural advantage of depth from the confounding effect of parameter count.

Further, Figure 2's comparison of DRNN depths would be more informative if it included results from training deeper DRNNs from scratch on benchmarks, with wider layers (to compensate for parameter differences relative to shallower models). This would clarify whether depth *itself* drives performance gains, independent of model size.

Minor points: Define DRNN-AO and IO in the main text (not solely in Figure 1). The paper's description of DRNNs as "deep networks with recurrent connections at each layer" is clear, but the definition of these specific variants should be explicit earlier.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding deep temporal architectures. The experimental design is largely thorough, and the authors' self-awareness about limitations (e.g., layer interdependencies) is commendable. The writing is accessible, and the intellectual honesty about the work's boundaries is particularly praiseworthy. With the suggested refinements—particularly the parameter-equivalent comparison and the direct test of hierarchical necessity—the paper's claims would be significantly strengthened. The core idea of leveraging depth for hierarchical temporal processing is compelling and warrants further investigation.
