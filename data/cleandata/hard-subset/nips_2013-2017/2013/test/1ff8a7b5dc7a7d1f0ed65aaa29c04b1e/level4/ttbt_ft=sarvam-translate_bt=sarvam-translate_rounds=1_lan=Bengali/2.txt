The usefulness of deep recurrent neural networks has been investigated.
The paper reaches interesting conclusions: when the number of parameters is fixed, deep recurrent neural networks outperform standard RNNs. Other interesting results include an analysis showing that deep networks contain more "long-term information," and the result is that general BPT can train character-level RNNs to balance the brackets, where it was previously thought only possible with HF.
However, it claims to introduce deep RNNs, but they have been used before; for example, http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf, and probably much earlier. So the paper should not claim to introduce this architecture. It is okay not to introduce a new architecture and focus on the analysis.
Although the analysis is meaningful, it would have been more interesting if it had been shown in a few problems, perhaps some lecture-related ones. Otherwise, the results could be an artifact of the problem.
Finally, HF experiments were probably very expensive, since the truncated backprop method (which was introduced in the 90s) was successfully used by Mikolov (see his PhD thesis) to train RNNs to be excellent language models. So it is probably that truncated BPT will do well here too, and it would be good to get a confirmation of this fact. This work presents an interesting analysis of deep RNNs. The two main results are: 1) deep RNNs outperform standard RNNs when the number of parameters is fixed in character-level language modeling; 2) deep layers exhibit more long-range structures; and 3) truncated BPT can train character-level RNNs to balance the brackets. The results are interesting, but since it is only an analysis, the paper would have been stronger if it had similar analysis on some lecture work (for example, to show that deep RNNs do well, and have more long-range information in their deep layers).
