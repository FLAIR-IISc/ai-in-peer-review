- Paper proposes Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs) where each RNN layer receives the previous layer’s hidden state to build hierarchical temporal processing for character-level language modeling.
- Core idea: question indefinite memory depth of standard RNNs and use layer hierarchy to capture multiple time scales.
- Key insight: stacking RNNs can create different emergent time scales at different levels, analogous to hierarchical feature extraction in deep networks.
- Empirical result: achieves state-of-the-art performance on a Wikipedia corpus using simple SGD rather than advanced optimizers.
- Provides detailed analysis of emergent time scales and experiments on text generation (notably Table 2).
- Writing is generally clear and organized, with candid discussion of limitations and experimental subtleties.
- Weakness: connection between the initial motivation (older data having more processing layers) and the specific architectural choices is not fully explored.
- Suggestion: consider testing additional non-linearities between timesteps and report whether these were tried.
- Weakness: effect of gradient normalization and its relation to bifurcations needs deeper explanation.
- Suggestion: discuss and cite the ICML 2013 paper on gradient truncation to strengthen analysis.
- Missing comparison: relevant prior work by Mikolov et al. (Wikipedia compression) should be included and compared.
- Major methodological gap: lacks a direct causal demonstration that higher layers process more abstract/long-term properties versus simply being positioned higher.
- Fairness concern: comparisons to standard RNNs are skewed by differing parameter counts; recommend equal-parameter or equal-unit comparisons.
- Suggested experiments: performance vs. depth graphs for DRNNs trained from scratch, and depth/width tradeoffs with adjusted layer widths to keep parameter counts consistent.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong benchmark performance with simple training, compelling hierarchical motivation and architecture, detailed emergent-timescale analysis, and honest discussion of limitations.
- Arguments against acceptance: motivation-to-design link needs strengthening, unfair baseline comparisons, lack of definitive evidence for abstraction in higher layers, and missing related-work comparisons.
- Overall recommendation: valuable contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
