This paper proposes a novel architecture that combines the strengths of deep and recurrent neural networks, called Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this architecture on a text prediction/compression task, achieving impressive results compared to existing approaches.

One of the key insights of this paper is the arbitrariness of older data benefiting from potentially many more layers of processing compared to newer data. To address this issue, the authors propose building a deep RNN, which seems like a reasonable approach to improve performance on shorter-term tasks, in addition to other potential benefits. Another possible extension could be to add extra layers of nonlinearities between each time step. It would be interesting to know if the authors have explored this idea.

The use of gradient normalization in the DRNN architecture is also noteworthy. The authors claim that this approach avoids the problem of bifurcations, but it would be helpful to elaborate on this point. Additionally, a recent paper on gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs (ICML 2013) may be relevant to this discussion. Furthermore, normalizing the gradient in this way may potentially disrupt the "averaging" behavior of stochastic gradient descent (SGD). It would be beneficial to discuss this potential issue.

In terms of related work, the authors may want to compare their results to those of Mikolov et al. on similar Wikipedia compression tasks. This comparison could provide additional context and insights into the effectiveness of the DRNN architecture.

The experiments designed to examine the roles played by each layer in terms of time-scale and abstraction are well-conceived. However, as the authors themselves point out, interpreting the results of these experiments can be problematic due to the complex interdependencies between the outputs of different layers. The text generation experiment (Table 2) is particularly persuasive, but it is essential to consider the limitations of this type of analysis.

A significant missing piece of the empirical puzzle in this paper is the question of whether the higher layers are genuinely better at processing more abstract and long-term properties of the text than if the units were moved to the first layer. The fact that the higher layers take on these roles after training is encouraging but incomplete evidence. It would be beneficial to design experiments that more directly address this question.

The comparison between the DRNN and the regular RNN may be unfair due to the difference in the number of parameters. The regular RNN seems to have fewer parameters (2767^2 < 2119^2, considering both recurrent and inter-layer weight matrices). A more convincing comparison would be to pit the DRNN against a standard RNN with the same number of parameters or units. For example, a 2-layer DRNN versus a standard RNN with the same number of units would provide a more meaningful comparison.

Instead of Figure 2, it would be more informative to show the performance of DRNNs with varying depths on the benchmarks when trained from scratch. This could include wider layers to compensate for the difference in the number of parameters and/or units.

Minor suggestions:

* Define DRNN-AO and IO in the text, not just in Figure 1.
* Consider adding more experiments to strengthen the paper's claims.

Overall, this paper is well-written, easy to follow, and intellectually honest about its potential problems and shortcomings. The authors' willingness to explore the limitations of their approach is commendable. The idea of examining deep temporal networks is worthwhile, and this paper provides valuable insights into how these networks function after training.
