Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies stacked recurrent architectures (“deep recurrent neural networks,” DRNNs) for character-level language modeling. Each layer is a recurrent network that receives the hidden state of the previous layer, with outputs either from the top layer (DRNN-1O) or from all layers (DRNN-AO). With parameter counts held roughly constant (~4.9M), deeper RNNs outperform a single-layer RNN in bits-per-character on Wikipedia text. The authors analyze emergent time scales across layers and show that higher layers encode longer-range information. They also demonstrate that plain SGD/BPTT suffices to learn long-range structure such as balancing parentheses, a result previously associated with Hessian-free (HF) or specialized architectures (e.g., multiplicative RNNs, LSTMs).

The architecture and idea of stacking recurrent layers are not new; related models have appeared in prior work, e.g., stacked (bi)directional LSTMs for speech recognition (Graves, Mohamed, Hinton, ICASSP 2013) and hierarchical RNNs (El Hihi & Bengio; Fernández, Graves & Schmidhuber). The contribution here is a careful empirical and analytic study on a modern large-scale character-level task, using plain SGD, and comparisons under a fixed parameter budget.

Strengths
- Clear empirical finding: For a fixed parameter budget, stacked RNNs improve character-level cross-entropy over single-layer RNNs.
- Insightful analysis: Layer-wise ablations and perturbation tests convincingly show that deeper layers carry longer-range context, and the DRNN-1O training schedule induces long time scales in upper layers.
- Practical takeaway: Plain BPTT with gradient normalization trains models to handle long dependencies (parentheses), challenging the notion that HF is necessary for such behavior.
- Careful parameter-matched comparisons and qualitative generative examples.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scope: Results are shown on a single task/dataset. I mostly agree with the “one task” criticism raised in the rebuttal; the conclusions may be task-specific. Replication on at least one other domain with well-known temporal hierarchies (e.g., TIMIT/WSJ character or phoneme modeling, or another large text corpus) would substantially strengthen the claims.
- Novelty/positioning: The paper risks overstating novelty of the architecture. While the text cites prior work using similar stacked recurrent models, certain phrasing suggests introduction of the DRNN concept. The contribution should be framed as an analysis of stacked RNNs rather than a new architecture.
- Training cost: End-to-end BPTT over ~200 steps makes runs very expensive (~10 days/GPU), limiting seeds and ablations. HF comparisons are informative historically, but are not necessary here; more relevant would be to evaluate truncated BPTT.
- Baselines: A stronger baseline would include an LSTM (possibly stacked) with truncated BPTT, which was already competitive in sequence tasks by 2013. Also, more than one random seed (with error bars) would help quantify variability.
- Parentheses focus: While instructive, this phenomenon may be sensitive to BPTT horizon; it is unclear how much it contributes to cross-entropy.

Quality
- Technically sound and carefully executed. The time-scale analyses, ablations, and parentheses experiments support the main claims on this task.
- Some experimental choices (long BPTT, small number of runs) limit the breadth of evidence. Conclusions about generality should be tempered.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The training schedules and architectures are clearly described. Minor suggestions: explicitly detail sequence lengths used for BPTT and emphasize parameter counts per layer in the main text. Clarify which claims are novel versus confirmations of prior observations.

Originality
- Architectural novelty is limited; stacked RNNs predate this work. The original contribution lies in the systematic, parameter-matched analysis on character-level modeling and the demonstration that plain SGD/BPTT is sufficient for long-range effects.

Significance
- Moderate. The empirical finding that depth helps under fixed parameter budgets and that higher layers capture longer time scales is useful and has likely impact on practitioners. The significance would increase with validation on additional tasks and stronger baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Evaluate truncated BPTT: Try horizons around 30 steps (as in Mikolov’s work). This may miss some parentheses closures but could match cross-entropy at much lower computational cost. At minimum, report cross-entropy and training time versus horizon to quantify the trade-off.
- Add an LSTM baseline (single-layer and/or stacked) under comparable parameter budgets with truncated BPTT.
- Extend to at least one non-text task with clear temporal hierarchy (e.g., speech) to test whether the learned time-scale stratification and performance gains carry over.
- Report results over multiple random initializations, even if fewer updates, to assess variance.
- Clarify related work and novelty: explicitly acknowledge stacked recurrent architectures in Graves (2013) and others, and frame the paper as analysis-focused.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Solid, parameter-controlled evidence that stacked RNNs outperform shallow RNNs on character-level modeling.
- Careful analysis revealing emergent time-scale hierarchy and layer roles.
- Demonstrates that plain BPTT can learn long-range dependencies (parentheses).

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Single-task evaluation; generality uncertain.
- Architectural novelty limited; prior work used similar stacks.
- Expensive training; lack of truncated BPTT and stronger baselines (e.g., LSTM).

Overall recommendation
Leaning accept for its clear empirical result under a fixed parameter budget and insightful analyses, provided the paper is positioned as an analysis of deep RNNs rather than a new architecture. If space permits, adding a truncated BPTT comparison (e.g., 30-step horizon) and one additional task would significantly strengthen the submission.
