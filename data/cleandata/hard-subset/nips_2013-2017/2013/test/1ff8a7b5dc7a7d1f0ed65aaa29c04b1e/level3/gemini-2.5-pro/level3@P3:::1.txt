This paper proposes and investigates a Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN) architecture for modeling time-series data with hierarchical temporal structures. The core idea is to stack multiple RNN layers, where each layer receives the hidden state sequence from the layer below it as input. This contrasts with a standard RNN, which can be seen as deep unfolded in time but is architecturally shallow at each time step. The authors hypothesize that this spatial depth allows the model to explicitly learn a hierarchy of temporal features, with higher layers capturing longer-term dependencies.

The proposed DRNNs are evaluated on a large-scale character-level language modeling task using a Wikipedia dataset previously explored by Sutskever et al. (2011). The authors introduce two variants: one with output connections from all layers (DRNN-AO) and another with an output only from the top layer (DRNN-1O). For the latter, they employ a layer-by-layer supervised training scheme. The paper demonstrates that these large DRNNs, trained with simple stochastic gradient descent, achieve performance comparable to the state-of-the-art for RNNs on this dataset, which was previously set using a more complex Hessian-free optimization method. A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to a detailed analysis of the trained models, providing compelling evidence that different layers learn to operate at different time scales and that the model can capture complex, long-range dependencies like parenthesis matching.

**Strengths**

*   **Excellent Analysis and Insight:** The paper's primary strength lies in its thorough analysis of the DRNN's internal dynamics. Rather than just reporting a final performance metric, the authors conduct a series of well-designed experiments to understand *how* the model works. The typo-perturbation analysis (Figure 3) provides clear evidence for the emergence of a temporal hierarchy, showing that higher layers maintain memory of a perturbation for longer periods than lower layers. The investigation into parenthesis closing (Figure 4) is particularly insightful, demonstrating that the model learns a robust, pseudo-stable attractor-like state for this long-range dependency, rather than simply memorizing sequences. This level of analysis is a significant contribution and provides valuable intuition about the function of depth in recurrent models.
*   **Sound Methodology and Training:** The paper successfully trains very large networks (nearly 5 million parameters) to achieve strong performance using a relatively simple SGD-based approach. The proposed layer-wise pre-training procedure for the DRNN-1O model is a practical and effective contribution to training very deep sequential architectures, helping to mitigate the vanishing gradient problem during initialization. The experimental details are clearly described, enhancing the work's reproducibility.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation for DRNNs is clearly articulated, and the model architecture is explained precisely. The figures are clean, informative, and strongly support the paper's central claims.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Limited Empirical Scope:** The main limitation of this work is its evaluation on a single, non-standard dataset. While the Wikipedia corpus is large and challenging, its use restricts the comparability and generality of the results. It would have been more convincing to see performance evaluated on more established language modeling benchmarks (e.g., Penn Treebank) to better contextualize the performance gains.
*   **Narrow Comparison to Alternative Models:** The performance comparison is restricted almost entirely to other RNN-based models on the same dataset. The paper's motivation centers on modeling temporal hierarchies, yet there is no comparison to other families of models designed for this purpose, such as Hierarchical HMMs or other structured temporal models. This makes it difficult to assess the DRNN's advantages relative to the broader field of hierarchical time-series modeling.
*   **Inconclusive Performance Gains:** While the DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art results for RNNs on this dataset, the improvement over the prior work of Sutskever et al. (2011) is marginal. The stated goal is to show that the architecture is beneficial, but from a purely performance-based perspective, the evidence is not overwhelming. The paper's contribution is therefore stronger in its analysis and the demonstration that simpler optimization can be effective for this architecture, rather than in showing a definitive leap in modeling capability.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

The authors mention that an untrained DRNN shows far smaller differences in time-scales between layers. It would be interesting to see this result visualized (perhaps in an appendix) to provide a clearer baseline for the learned temporal hierarchy shown in Figure 3.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to our understanding of deep learning for sequential data. Its primary strength is not in shattering performance records but in providing a careful, insightful analysis of how a stacked recurrent architecture learns to model hierarchical temporal structures. The weaknesses related to the limited scope of evaluation are present but do not detract from the importance of the analysis provided. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and is likely to inspire further work on both the architecture and analysis of deep recurrent models.
