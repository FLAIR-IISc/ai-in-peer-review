This paper proposes a novel deep architecture that combines hierarchical deep learning features with time-series modeling, specifically designed for processing time series data with a temporal hierarchy. The architecture, called Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN), consists of multiple layers of Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), where each layer receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this architecture on character-level language modeling, achieving state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks on a Wikipedia text corpus.

The paper relates to previous work on hierarchical architectures employing RNNs, such as [3, 5, 6], but differs in its focus on analyzing the contribution of the network architecture to the task. The authors also draw inspiration from the success of deep neural networks (DNNs) in tasks such as speech recognition and handwritten digit recognition.

The quality of the paper is high, with well-written text, clear experiments, and supportive figures. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the network's behavior, showing that higher layers capture long-term dependencies and that parentheses matching appears as a pseudo-stable attractor-like state.

The originality of the paper lies in its proposal of a new deep architecture that combines hierarchical deep learning features with time-series modeling. The authors also introduce a layer-by-layer supervised pre-training procedure using a next-letter prediction objective, which is a novel contribution.

The significance of the paper is high, as it provides a new perspective on processing time series data with a temporal hierarchy. The results demonstrate the potential of DRNNs in capturing long-term dependencies and modeling complex temporal relationships. The paper also contributes to the ongoing debate on training algorithms, particularly the use of stochastic gradient descent (SGD) versus second-order methods.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The evaluation is restricted to a single dataset, the Hinton et al. Wikipedia dataset, which is not widely used. The comparisons are also limited to RNN-related models, excluding hierarchical HMMs and other approaches that may be relevant. Additionally, the results are somewhat inconclusive relative to the paper's stated goals, as the performance gains are not consistently demonstrated across all experiments.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and original deep architecture that combines hierarchical deep learning features with time-series modeling.
* The authors provide a detailed analysis of the network's behavior, showing that higher layers capture long-term dependencies and that parentheses matching appears as a pseudo-stable attractor-like state.
* The paper contributes to the ongoing debate on training algorithms, particularly the use of SGD versus second-order methods.
* The results demonstrate the potential of DRNNs in capturing long-term dependencies and modeling complex temporal relationships.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The comparisons are limited to RNN-related models, excluding hierarchical HMMs and other approaches that may be relevant.
* The results are somewhat inconclusive relative to the paper's stated goals, as the performance gains are not consistently demonstrated across all experiments.

While there are some limitations to the paper, the authors provide a detailed analysis of the network's behavior and demonstrate the potential of DRNNs in capturing long-term dependencies and modeling complex temporal relationships.
