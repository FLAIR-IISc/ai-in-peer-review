Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) formed by stacking recurrent layers so that each layer receives the previous layer’s hidden state, with two output variants: DRNN-1O (only the top layer connects to the output) and DRNN-AO (all layers connect to the output). Using simple SGD with gradient normalization and a layer-wise incremental training schedule for DRNN-1O, the authors report strong results on character-level language modeling on Wikipedia, comparable to earlier multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization. Beyond accuracy, they analyze emergent time scales via perturbation studies and context switching, and illustrate qualitative behavior with text generation and a bracket-closing analysis.

The work connects to hierarchical RNNs (El Hihi & Bengio, 1996), hierarchical/stacked LSTMs (e.g., Graves et al., 2013), and MRNNs/Hessian-free training (Sutskever, Martens, Hinton, 2011). The emphasis on architectural depth to capture temporal hierarchies complements contemporaneous work from U. Montreal on training pathologies and gradient clipping (Pascanu, Mikolov, Bengio, ICML 2013).

Strengths
- Clear, appealing idea: stacking recurrent layers yields a temporal hierarchy where older information is processed by more layers. The hypothesis that deep RNNs can improve short-term processing at lower layers while higher layers handle longer contexts is well-motivated and supported by analyses.
- Simple training and strong performance: state-of-the-art among RNNs of similar size using plain SGD, without second-order methods.
- Thoughtful analyses: perturbation experiments, context-length quantification, and the parenthesis case study convincingly demonstrate emergent time scales and nontrivial long-range behavior.
- Readability: the paper is well-structured and accessible.

Weaknesses and questions
- Depth vs. width confounds. While models are approximately parameter-matched overall, some conclusions about the value of depth could be strengthened by controls that reallocate parameters. In particular, it remains unclear whether higher layers genuinely benefit from upstream processing or whether equivalent gains could be achieved by moving capacity to the first layer.
- Ablations on gradient normalization are missing. The training relies on normalizing the gradient vector at each update (Eq. 3) to “avoid bifurcations,” but the empirical importance of this choice is not quantified.
- Limited architectural comparisons. The paper explores stacked depth across time, but another axis is depth within a time step (extra nonlinear layers in the recurrent transition). This “deep transition RNN” variant is not investigated.
- Comparisons could be broader. Results are compared to MRNN/HF and PAQ, but Mikolov et al.’s Wikipedia compression/language modeling benchmarks (e.g., enwik8/enwik9) are not directly addressed.
- Interpretation of layer “surgery” is confounded by interdependencies among layers’ contributions to the readout; conclusions about each layer’s role should be drawn cautiously.

Detailed comments and requests
- Gradient normalization and “bifurcations”:
  - How critical is normalization for final BPC and training stability? Please include an ablation: no normalization; norm clipping (||g|| <- min(||g||, c)), as in Pascanu et al. (ICML 2013); and the current full renormalization. Report learning curves, gradient norm statistics, and final BPC.
  - Clarify “avoid the problem of bifurcations”: do you refer to abrupt qualitative changes in hidden-state dynamics under parameter updates? Provide evidence (e.g., trajectories, Lyapunov/activation statistics) or a precise definition. 
  - Discuss whether renormalizing the gradient at every step disrupts SGD’s averaging/noise-scaling behavior (Bottou’s large-scale learning). Since step sizes become independent of gradient magnitude, small and large gradients are treated uniformly; why is this preferable to clipping with threshold c that preserves scale below c?
  - Please position your approach relative to Pascanu, Mikolov, and Bengio (ICML 2013, U. Montreal) on exploding/vanishing gradients, gradient clipping, leaky units, and truncated BPTT. You already use truncated BPTT; a discussion would help.

- Architecture:
  - Extra nonlinear layers between time steps: Have you tried deepening the recurrent transition (e.g., ai(t) = tanh(W2 φ(W1[ai(t−1), a_{i−1}(t)]))) or residual/MLP transitions within a time step? This directly targets short-term processing while keeping the number of layers across time fixed. A comparison under matched parameter budgets would probe whether hierarchical temporal processing (stacked layers) is specifically beneficial beyond increasing transition capacity.
  - Key missing control: Test whether higher layers truly gain from upstream processing versus relocating the same number of units to layer 1. Please add baselines:
    - Single-layer RNN with width increased to match parameter count of 5-layer DRNNs.
    - A family sweeping depth = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5} with widths chosen so total parameters are constant; train all from scratch with identical optimization.
    - A “capacity relocation” experiment where units are progressively moved from top layers to the bottom while keeping total parameters fixed.

- Fairness and plots:
  - Ensure comparisons control for parameter-count differences introduced by DRNN-AO’s multiple output connections versus DRNN-1O. Report exact parameter counts per component (recurrent, inter-layer, output).
  - Replace/augment current Figure 2 with a plot of test BPC versus DRNN depth for several parameter budgets. Include wider shallow baselines to disentangle depth benefits from capacity.
  - Please compare on standard compression tasks (Mikolov et al.) such as enwik8/enwik9 with canonical data splits and BPC. If possible, report compression (bits) using arithmetic coding driven by your model to enable apples-to-apples with PAQ and RNN baselines.

- Interpretation cautions:
  - The text-generation ablation (Table 2) is persuasive. However, the “layer surgery” experiments (zeroing a layer’s readout) can be misleading because of complex interdependencies and cancellations among layers in DRNN-AO. Consider complementary diagnostics (e.g., CKA/CCA similarity across layers, probing classifiers fed from each layer with a frozen model).

- Minor presentation:
  - Define DRNN-AO and DRNN-1O explicitly in the main text upon first mention (and ensure consistent notation; “1O” vs “IO”). Briefly explain “bifurcation” in the training section. Clarify how many full passes through the data each model sees, and specify batching/seeding details for reproducibility.

Quality: The methodology and analyses are sound overall, with clear experimental protocols. The main claims are supported, but the importance of gradient normalization and the specific advantage of stacked depth over width require additional ablations.

Clarity: Well-written and organized; adding the requested definitions and more precise descriptions of training heuristics would further improve clarity.

Originality: Combining stacked RNNs with a careful analysis of emergent time scales is a timely contribution beyond prior stacked LSTMs focused on performance.

Significance: Results are competitive and the analyses are insightful. With the suggested controls and broader comparisons, this work could be influential for understanding and training deep RNNs.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear problem motivation; simple, effective architecture and training.
- Strong empirical results with SGD.
- Compelling analyses of hierarchical time scales and long-range behavior.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing depth-vs-width and parameter relocation controls.
- No ablation of gradient normalization; unclear theoretical/empirical role of “bifurcations.”
- Limited comparisons to standard Wikipedia compression benchmarks.

Overall recommendation
Worthwhile, readable, and largely convincing. I lean to acceptance contingent on adding the key ablations (gradient normalization, depth/width sweeps with matched parameters) and broader comparisons (Mikolov/PAQ on enwik8/enwik9).
