The paper "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks" explores the use of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) for processing time series data. The authors propose a hierarchical architecture where each layer is a recurrent network that receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input, allowing for hierarchical processing of temporal tasks. This architecture is applied to character-level language modeling, where the authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks using simple stochastic gradient descent.

The main contribution of the paper is the analysis of the effectiveness of DRNNs in capturing temporal hierarchies in time series data. The authors show that deeper layers in the DRNN encode more long-range information, and that the network is able to model long-term dependencies in the data. The results are promising, with the DRNN outperforming standard RNNs when the number of parameters is held constant.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The experimental setup is thorough, and the results are presented in a clear and concise manner. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the time scales present in the layers of the DRNN, which is an interesting contribution to the understanding of these models.

One potential weakness of the paper is the claim that the authors have introduced the deep RNN architecture, when in fact similar models have been proposed in prior work (e.g., Graves 2013). However, it is acceptable to focus on analysis rather than proposing a new architecture, and the authors' contributions to the understanding of DRNNs are still significant.

A concern is that the results may be task-specific, and that the analysis should be replicated on additional tasks (e.g., speech) to strengthen the claims. Additionally, the authors' use of Hessian-free (HF) experiments may be unnecessarily expensive, and truncated backpropagation through time (BPTT) (as used by Mikolov) may be sufficient and should be tested to confirm.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a thorough analysis of the effectiveness of DRNNs in capturing temporal hierarchies in time series data.
* The results are promising, with the DRNN outperforming standard RNNs when the number of parameters is held constant.
* The authors provide a detailed analysis of the time scales present in the layers of the DRNN, which is an interesting contribution to the understanding of these models.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper does not propose a new architecture, but rather builds on existing work.
* The results may be task-specific, and the analysis should be replicated on additional tasks to strengthen the claims.
* The use of HF experiments may be unnecessarily expensive, and truncated BPTT may be sufficient.
