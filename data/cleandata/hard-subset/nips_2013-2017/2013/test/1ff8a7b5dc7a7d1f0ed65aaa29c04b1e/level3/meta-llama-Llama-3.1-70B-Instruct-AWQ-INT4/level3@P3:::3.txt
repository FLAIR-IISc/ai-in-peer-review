This paper introduces the concept of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) and demonstrates their effectiveness in character-level language modeling. The authors argue that DRNNs can capture temporal hierarchies in data, which is a common phenomenon in many real-world tasks. They propose a hierarchical architecture where each layer is a recurrent neural network that receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the architecture and training procedure of DRNNs. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the results, including the performance of DRNNs on a Wikipedia text corpus and the emergence of different time scales in the layers of the DRNNs.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to demonstrate the effectiveness of DRNNs in capturing long-term dependencies in data. The authors show that DRNNs can learn to model parentheses as a pseudo-stable attractor-like state, rather than memorizing parenthesized phrases of different lengths. This is a significant improvement over common RNNs, which struggle to capture long-term dependencies.

However, there are some potential weaknesses in the paper. One concern is that the authors do not provide a clear explanation of why DRNNs are more effective than common RNNs in capturing temporal hierarchies. While they argue that the hierarchical architecture of DRNNs allows for more effective processing of temporal information, they do not provide a detailed analysis of how this architecture contributes to the improved performance.

Another concern is that the authors do not provide a fair comparison between DRNNs and common RNNs. The DRNNs used in the paper have a significantly larger number of parameters than the common RNNs, which could contribute to their improved performance. The authors should provide a comparison between DRNNs and common RNNs with the same number of parameters to demonstrate that the improved performance is due to the architecture rather than the number of parameters.

The paper also raises some interesting questions about the role of each layer in the DRNN. The authors show that the top layer is the most important for generating output, while the intermediate layers are more important for preprocessing the data for the top layer. However, they do not provide a clear explanation of how the layers interact with each other and how the information is propagated through the network.

In terms of originality, the paper builds on previous work on hierarchical recurrent neural networks, but the authors provide a new perspective on how to design and train these networks.
