## Review of "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks"

This paper investigates the effect of depth in recurrent neural networks (RNNs), proposing a Deep RNN (DRNN) architecture where each layer is a recurrent network receiving the hidden state of the previous layer. The authors motivate this by arguing that standard RNNs don’t explicitly capture the hierarchical temporal structure often present in time series data. They demonstrate competitive performance on character-level language modeling, achieving state-of-the-art results for recurrent networks with a comparable parameter count, and provide some analysis of emergent time scales within the DRNN.

The core idea – that deeper RNNs can be beneficial, particularly for data with hierarchical temporal dependencies – is a valuable one, building on the success of deep learning in other domains. The observation that older data *may* benefit from more processing layers is well-taken and aligns with intuition about hierarchical representations. The experimental results, particularly the text generation example in Table 2, are persuasive in demonstrating the DRNN’s capabilities. 

However, several points require further investigation. The authors mention the potential for adding extra nonlinear layers *between* timesteps, and it’s crucial to know if this was explored. The importance of the gradient normalization technique is not fully explained; the paper states it avoids “bifurcations” but doesn’t elaborate. This is a key mechanism, and a more detailed explanation, perhaps referencing the ICML 2013 Montreal paper on gradient truncation/optimization (as suggested by my understanding of the supervisor’s notes), is needed. Furthermore, the potential disruption of SGD’s averaging behavior due to gradient normalization should be addressed.

The comparison to Mikolov et al. on Wikipedia compression tasks would provide a stronger benchmark. The claim of state-of-the-art performance needs careful consideration given parameter count differences. Baselines with comparable parameters or units are essential. Plotting performance for various DRNN depths (trained from scratch, including wider layers to match parameters/units) instead of Figure 2 would offer a clearer picture of the benefits of depth. Minor points include explicitly defining DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO in the text, not just in Figure 1.

A critical missing experiment is to determine if the higher layers genuinely benefit from upstream processing or if their performance could be achieved by simply relocating units to the first layer. This would isolate the value of the hierarchical structure. Finally, the interpretation of “layer surgery” in the text generation experiment is likely confounded by complex interdependencies between layers.

Overall, this is a worthwhile and largely convincing paper. The writing is clear and the ideas are well-motivated. However, addressing the points above – particularly the additional experiments and more rigorous comparisons – would significantly strengthen the contribution. I lean towards acceptance, contingent on revisions addressing these concerns.
