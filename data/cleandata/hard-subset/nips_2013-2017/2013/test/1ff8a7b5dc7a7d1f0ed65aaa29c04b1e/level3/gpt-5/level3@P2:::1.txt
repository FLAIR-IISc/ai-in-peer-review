Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies “deep recurrent neural networks” (DRNNs) formed by stacking multiple recurrent layers, where each layer is a simple tanh RNN receiving the hidden state of the layer below. The authors argue that such stacking naturally induces a hierarchy of temporal scales, akin to deep feedforward hierarchies but for time series. Two output variants are considered: output only at the top layer (DRNN-1O) and output at all layers (DRNN-AO). Training is done with SGD and BPTT, using gradient normalization and an incremental, supervised layer-wise scheme for DRNN-1O: layers are added one at a time, each temporarily directly connected to the output via a next-character prediction objective.

The main empirical task is character-level language modeling on the Wikipedia corpus introduced by Sutskever et al. (2011), holding model size fixed (~4.9M parameters) to compare DRNNs to a plain RNN and to prior reported results (notably MRNNs trained with Hessian-free methods). The authors report improved or competitive bits-per-character (BPC) for the DRNNs relative to plain RNNs and comparable to the best reported recurrent models at this scale with more sophisticated optimization. Beyond accuracy, the paper offers detailed analyses: (i) layer-wise ablations of output contributions, (ii) perturbation experiments showing emergent layer-specific time scales and effective context length, and (iii) evidence that the top layers capture long-range dependencies such as parentheses matching, behaving like a pseudo-stable attractor during bracketed spans.

This work is closely related to stacked RNN/LSTM architectures used in speech recognition (e.g., Graves et al. 2013) and earlier hierarchical recurrent formulations (El Hihi & Bengio 1996; Fernández et al. 2005). It differs by using simple tanh RNNs, emphasizing an incremental supervised pretraining scheme, and providing thorough analyses of the temporal hierarchy. It also speaks to training-method debates (SGD vs. second-order) by achieving strong results without HF. Connections to hierarchical HMMs (e.g., Fine et al., 1998) are conceptually relevant, though not empirically explored here.

Strengths
- Clear, well-written paper; figures and experimental design are easy to follow.
- Solid empirical performance: DRNNs outperform a plain RNN at fixed parameter budget and approach state-of-the-art RNN results using only SGD.
- Careful, informative analyses: perturbation and layer-ablation studies convincingly demonstrate emergent, distinct time scales and roles of different layers; the parentheses experiment is compelling and intuitive.
- Practical training insights: gradient normalization and incremental supervised layer-wise training for DRNN-1O are clearly described and appear effective.
- Contributes understanding of how deep recurrent stacks operate, not just improved numbers.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation is limited to a single dataset/preprocessing; cross-corpus generality (e.g., text8/enwik8/PTB-Char) is not established.
- Comparative baselines are narrow: largely RNN variants; no comparison to stacked LSTM/GRU (which were already prevalent) or to non-RNN hierarchical models (e.g., HHMMs, n-gram/KN language models at character level).
- Some training choices (zero-initialized output layers, aggressive layer-wise schedule, truncation length) could be ablated more thoroughly to disentangle where gains come from (architecture vs. training protocol).
- Limited reporting of variability: large models trained once per condition, no confidence intervals.
- Claims about pseudo-attractor dynamics are suggestive but not quantified (e.g., Jacobian spectra or stability analysis).

Quality
Technically sound overall. The architecture is straightforward, optimization choices are reasonable, and analyses are thoughtfully designed. Claims are supported by experiments, though broader baselines and multi-corpus evaluation would strengthen conclusions. The interpretation of long-term “attractor-like” behavior is plausible but would benefit from more formal diagnostics.

Clarity
Very clearly written and well-organized. Methods and training schedules are adequately described to enable reproduction (given computational resources). Figures effectively support the narrative.

Originality
Stacking recurrent layers is not new per se, but the paper’s contributions include: (i) a simple supervised incremental training scheme for DRNN-1O; (ii) a careful empirical demonstration that stacked tanh RNNs trained with SGD can match HF-optimized results at similar scale; and (iii) detailed, layer-wise temporal analyses. The emphasis on understanding and diagnosing emergent time scales is a valuable addition.

Significance
Moderate. The empirical gains over a plain RNN at fixed parameter count are meaningful, and the analyses provide insights likely to guide future model design and diagnostics. However, without broader baselines (e.g., stacked LSTM/GRU) and multi-corpus validation, it is harder to conclude that DRNNs advance the state of the art beyond specific settings. The training insights and analyses are likely to be used by other researchers.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to gated architectures (LSTM/GRU) with similar parameter counts and depth, and to n-gram/compression baselines on the same preprocessing to calibrate BPC.
- Evaluate on additional standard corpora (e.g., text8/enwik8, PTB-Char) to establish robustness.
- Provide ablations: effect of number of layers, AO vs. 1O without incremental pretraining, different truncation lengths, gradient clipping vs. normalization, and nonzero output initialization.
- Report variance across seeds for at least one setting.
- Quantify the “attractor-like” dynamics (e.g., measure local Jacobian eigenvalues along bracketed spans) to support the interpretation.
- Discuss computational trade-offs more explicitly (updates seen, wall-clock) to contextualize the SGD vs. HF comparison.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear demonstration that stacked simple RNNs with careful training yield competitive results.
- Strong, insightful analyses of emergent time scales and long-range dependency handling.
- High clarity and reproducibility; contributions are of interest to the NIPS community.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited breadth of empirical validation and baselines; unclear advantage over contemporary gated deep RNNs.
- Single dataset with nonstandard preprocessing; cross-dataset generality unproven.
- Some claims (e.g., attractor dynamics) are not rigorously quantified.

Overall assessment
A well-executed and clearly presented study that combines competitive results with valuable analyses of deep recurrent behavior. While the empirical scope could be broader, the insights into temporal hierarchies and the practical training recipe make this a worthwhile contribution. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying comparisons and, if possible, adding a gated baseline or additional corpus experiments.
