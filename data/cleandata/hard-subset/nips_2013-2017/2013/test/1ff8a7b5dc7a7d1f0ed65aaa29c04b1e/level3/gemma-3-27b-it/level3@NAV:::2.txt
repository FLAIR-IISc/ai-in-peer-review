## Review of “Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks”

This paper investigates the impact of depth in recurrent neural networks (RNNs) for character-level language modeling. The core idea is to stack RNNs to create a deep architecture capable of capturing hierarchical temporal dependencies, a feature often lacking in standard RNNs. The authors demonstrate that deeper RNNs, with a comparable number of parameters to shallower counterparts, outperform the latter on a Wikipedia-based text corpus, achieving state-of-the-art results using simple stochastic gradient descent (SGD). They further present an analysis of emergent timescales within these deep RNNs, showing that different layers appear to encode information over varying temporal ranges, and surprisingly, plain BPTT can balance parentheses.

The paper’s strengths lie in its empirical results and the subsequent analysis. The demonstration of competitive performance with a relatively straightforward training method (SGD) is notable, especially compared to prior work relying on more complex techniques like Hessian-free optimization. The analysis of time scales and the surprising ability to handle parentheses with basic BPTT offers valuable insights into the network's learned representations. However, the claim of introducing a “deep RNN” architecture is inaccurate, as similar models were proposed by Graves (2013) and others. While the authors acknowledge this and pivot to framing the work as an analysis of existing architectures, this weakens the originality aspect. 

A major concern is the task-specificity of the results. The findings are demonstrated solely on character-level language modeling. While a robust initial demonstration, it limits the generalizability of the conclusions. Replication of the analysis on other sequential tasks – such as speech recognition as suggested in the paper itself, or even more complex time-series data – would substantially strengthen the claims about the benefits of deep RNNs and the emergence of hierarchical temporal representations. 

Furthermore, the authors expend considerable computational resources on full BPTT. As highlighted in the rebuttal, exploring truncated BPTT (e.g., with ~30 steps) is crucial. It’s likely that a much lower computational cost can achieve comparable performance, particularly given the demonstrated ability to handle parentheses without Hessian-Free training. Testing this is a critical next step. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** Solid empirical results, insightful analysis of emergent time scales, demonstration of surprising capabilities (parentheses balancing with BPTT).
**Arguments against acceptance:** Lack of architectural novelty, task-specific results, potentially unnecessarily expensive training (BPTT vs. truncated BPTT).

**Overall:** I lean towards acceptance. The paper, despite its limitations in originality, presents a valuable contribution to understanding deep RNNs. The analysis is thoughtful and the results are encouraging. Addressing the concerns regarding task specificity and exploring truncated BPTT would significantly enhance the paper's impact.
