This paper introduces a Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN), a novel architecture that stacks multiple RNN layers to form a temporal hierarchy. The core idea is to combine the hierarchical feature processing of deep networks with the sequential modeling capabilities of RNNs, where each layer receives the hidden state of the layer below it as input. This work builds upon previous efforts to improve RNNs for complex sequences, such as the work by Sutskever et al. (2011) on character-level language modeling. However, it distinguishes itself by focusing on architectural innovation and a deep analysis of the model’s learned behavior, rather than solely on advanced optimization methods. The authors also propose an effective layer-by-layer supervised pre-training scheme to manage the difficulty of training such a deep architecture.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its thorough and insightful analysis of the DRNN’s internal dynamics. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that the hierarchical structure encourages layers to operate on different time scales, with higher layers capturing longer-term dependencies. The investigation into parenthesis matching is particularly compelling, suggesting the model learns pseudo-stable, attractor-like states to handle long-range syntactic structures. This analytical contribution provides valuable insights into how deep temporal models operate. Furthermore, the paper is exceptionally well-written and clear, with supportive figures that effectively illustrate the model’s behavior. The reported performance on the Wikipedia dataset is competitive, achieving results comparable to more complex Hessian-free optimization methods with a simpler SGD-based approach.

However, the experimental validation has some notable limitations that temper the significance of the performance claims. The evaluation is conducted on a single, not widely-used dataset, making it difficult to assess the generalizability of the results. Moreover, the comparisons are limited to other RNN-based models, omitting potentially relevant baselines like hierarchical Hidden Markov Models. This narrow scope makes the claims of state-of-the-art performance somewhat inconclusive.

In summary, while the empirical evidence for performance superiority is not definitive, the paper makes a strong contribution through its novel architecture and, more importantly, its detailed analysis. The insights into how hierarchical temporal models learn are significant and will likely be valuable to the research community.
