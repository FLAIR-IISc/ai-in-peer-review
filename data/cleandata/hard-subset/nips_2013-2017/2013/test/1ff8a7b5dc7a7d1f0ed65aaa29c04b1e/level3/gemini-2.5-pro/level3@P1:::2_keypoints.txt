- Paper shows deep recurrent neural nets (DRNNs) outperform shallow RNNs at character-level language modeling when parameter count is fixed.
- Deeper layers in DRNNs empirically learn longer time scales and capture more abstract, long-range dependencies.
- DRNN trained with simple SGD/BPTT (with gradient clipping) achieves performance comparable to more complex HF-trained models.
- Strong empirical results: significant BPC improvements on a challenging Wikipedia benchmark and competitive state-of-the-art performance.
- Thorough analysis: quantitative "memory" experiments (typo perturbation), qualitative text generation, and long-range dependency tests (parentheses balancing).
- Practical finding: first-order optimization (BPTT) is sufficient for learning very long-range structure, countering the necessity of second-order methods.
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and presents experiments and figures effectively.
- Novelty is limited: stacked/deep RNN architectures have prior work (e.g., Graves ICASSP 2013); framing should emphasize analysis over architectural novelty.
- Generalizability is uncertain: experiments are limited to one task (character-level language modeling); validation on other domains (speech, music) would strengthen claims.
- Efficiency concern: training uses very long BPTT (250 steps); comparison to truncated BPTT (shorter horizons) is needed to assess computational trade-offs versus BPC loss.
- Overall: technically sound, significant and insightful analysis, but would benefit from clearer positioning, broader validation, and efficiency comparisons.
