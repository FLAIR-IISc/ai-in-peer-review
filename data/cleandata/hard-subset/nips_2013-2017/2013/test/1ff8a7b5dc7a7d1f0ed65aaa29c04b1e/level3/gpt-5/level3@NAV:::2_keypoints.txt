- Paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (stacked RNN layers with per-layer recurrency) for character-level language modeling and compares depth at fixed parameter budget.  
- Main findings: deeper RNNs outperform single-layer RNNs at fixed parameter counts and higher layers encode longer time scales.  
- Shows plain BPTT/SGD can learn bracket-balancing behavior often attributed to advanced training (HF).  
- Empirical methodology is generally sound: parameter-controlled comparisons, per-layer output ablations, and time-scale probes (typo decay, context switch).  
- Experimental weakness: evaluation on a single task (Wikipedia CLM) with only three model instantiations and one random seed per setting.  
- HF comparison is indirect (via prior literature) rather than from controlled re-runs in this paper.  
- Training details, hyperparameters, and ablation protocols are clearly described; gradient normalization and incremental layer-wise training are explained.  
- Paper is well written and reproducible at a high level, but should report variance across seeds and normalize compute by tokens seen for cost claims.  
- Originality is overstated: stacked/deep RNN architectures (including stacked LSTMs) predate this work (e.g., Graves et al., 2013) and should be more explicitly credited.  
- Significance: useful finding that depth helps at fixed parameter budgets for CLM and evidence that deeper layers capture longer-range dependencies; plain BPTT result is noteworthy.  
- Recommendation to corroborate hierarchical temporal representation claims on additional domains (e.g., speech, word-level LM).  
- Pros: clear parameter-controlled evidence of depth benefits, insightful analyses of time scales and layer roles, and strong results with simple SGD/BPTT.  
- Cons: novelty overstated/insufficiently credited prior work, single-task evaluation with limited seeds, and computationally heavy training setup.  
- Suggestions: evaluate truncated BPTT (~30 steps) to compare cost vs. performance, replicate key analyses on a second task (e.g., TIMIT/LibriSpeech), and report multi-seed means/variances plus compute/data-pass budgets.
