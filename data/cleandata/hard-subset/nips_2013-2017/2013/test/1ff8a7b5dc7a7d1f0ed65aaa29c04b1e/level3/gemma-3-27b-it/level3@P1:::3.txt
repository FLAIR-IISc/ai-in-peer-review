## Review of Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks

This paper investigates the effect of a hierarchical structure on recurrent neural networks (RNNs) for processing time series data, specifically focusing on character-level language modeling. The core idea – a deep RNN (DRNN) where each layer is a recurrent network receiving the hidden state of the previous layer – is a natural extension of deep neural networks to sequential data and a compelling approach to capturing temporal hierarchies. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on a Wikipedia text corpus using simple stochastic gradient descent (SGD), outperforming previous recurrent network results that relied on more advanced optimization techniques. The paper also includes an initial analysis of emergent time scales within the DRNN architecture.

The motivation for this work is well-placed. While deep learning has achieved significant success in many areas, the application of depth to RNNs has largely been limited to training algorithms rather than architectural innovation. The authors correctly identify that standard RNNs don’t explicitly handle the hierarchical nature of many time series, and their proposed DRNN addresses this limitation by allowing different layers to operate at different timescales. This work builds upon previous hierarchical RNN approaches [3, 5, 6], but extends them to a real-world task and offers a more in-depth analysis of the resulting temporal dynamics. The connection to [8] is also appropriately acknowledged.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Soundness:** The DRNN architecture is a reasonable and well-motivated approach to incorporating hierarchical processing into RNNs. The empirical results, achieving state-of-the-art performance with a relatively simple training procedure (SGD), are compelling.
* **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental question in recurrent neural network design - how to effectively leverage depth. Successful demonstration of improved performance on a challenging task like character-level language modeling suggests potential impact for applications requiring the capture of long-term dependencies.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the DRNN architecture and its motivation. Figure 1 is helpful in visualizing the network structure.
* **Analysis:** The initial analysis of emergent time scales is a valuable contribution, providing insights into how the DRNN processes information at different levels of abstraction. The text generation experiments and layer “surgery” analysis, while cautious, are insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Gradient Normalization:** The role of gradient normalization is not fully explored. The authors mention it avoids “bifurcations” but lack detail on *how* it achieves this. A more detailed explanation, alongside a discussion of potential downsides (e.g., disruption of SGD’s averaging behavior), is needed.  Related work, specifically the ICML 2013 paper from the University of Montreal exploring gradient truncation and optimization, should be considered and discussed.
* **Baselines & Comparisons:** The comparison to existing work is somewhat limited by differences in parameter counts. While the authors attempt to match the number of trainable parameters, direct comparisons with baselines employing the *same* number of parameters or units would strengthen the conclusions. Furthermore, a comparison to Mikolov et al.’s work on Wikipedia compression tasks would be valuable, providing a benchmark beyond language modeling perplexity.
* **Architectural Investigation:** The paper would benefit from a more systematic exploration of DRNN depth and width. Figure 2, while illustrative, doesn’t provide a comprehensive picture of performance as a function of network depth. Plotting performance for DRNNs of varying depths, trained from scratch (and potentially with wider layers to maintain comparable parameter counts), would be informative.
* **Layer Interpretation:** The interpretation of layer contributions based on "surgery" experiments (removing output connections) is potentially confounded by complex interdependencies between layers. A more definitive test is needed to determine whether higher layers genuinely benefit from upstream processing, versus simply achieving similar performance by relocating the same number of units to the first layer.
* **Minor Clarity Issue:** The definitions of DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO are provided in the figure caption but not explicitly in the text, making the paper slightly harder to follow initially.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The DRNN architecture is a novel and well-motivated approach to improving RNN performance.
* The empirical results are strong, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on a challenging task.
* The analysis of emergent time scales provides valuable insights into the network's internal dynamics.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the concepts.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of a more thorough investigation of gradient normalization and its potential drawbacks.
* Limited direct comparison to baselines with comparable parameter counts.
* Absence of a more systematic exploration of DRNN architecture (depth, width).
* Potential for confounding factors in the interpretation of layer contributions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a worthwhile and largely convincing paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of recurrent neural networks. While some additional experiments and comparisons would strengthen the conclusions, the core idea is sound, the results are compelling, and the paper is well-written. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly concerning gradient normalization and baseline comparisons. The key experiment of determining whether higher layers truly benefit from the hierarchy, or simply replicate performance with relocated units, is critical.
