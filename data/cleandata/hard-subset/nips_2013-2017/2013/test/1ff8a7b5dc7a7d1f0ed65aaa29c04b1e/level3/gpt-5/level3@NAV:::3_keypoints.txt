- Paper studies stacked recurrent networks (DRNNs) with top-only (DRNN-1O) or all-layers outputs (DRNN-AO) and shows strong character-level language modeling with simple SGD + gradient normalization.  
- Provides analyses of emergent time scales and long-term behaviors (e.g., matching parentheses) linking depth to temporal hierarchy.  
- Situates work relative to hierarchical RNNs, stacked LSTMs, and MRNNs/HF-based models; complements optimization-focused studies.  
- Experiments are careful and time-scale analyses are insightful and readable.  
- Training heuristic (global gradient normalization) is inadequately ablated—need quantification of its necessity, how it “avoids bifurcations,” and effects on SGD averaging/statistical efficiency.  
- Contrast between gradient normalization and gradient clipping (Pascanu et al., ICML 2013) is missing and should be included.  
- “Layer surgery” results in Table 2 are persuasive qualitatively but causal claims are confounded by inter-layer dependencies.  
- Clarity is good overall; define DRNN-AO and DRNN-1O at first mention in main text, not only in Fig. 1.  
- Originality: framing depth-as-temporal-hierarchy and analyses are useful despite stacked RNNs being established.  
- Suggestion to test “deep transition” variants (MLP inside recurrent transition) to probe depth-in-time vs depth-in-state.  
- Significance: competitive results for pure SGD and likely-citable analyses, but impact depends on stronger experiments.  
- Key missing experiments: baselines matched by parameter count and by unit count to rule out width vs depth advantages.  
- Recommend performance curves over DRNN depth trained from scratch (including wider layers to match params/units) to replace/augment Fig. 2.  
- Recommend comparison to Mikolov et al.’s Wikipedia compression/language modeling results to contextualize BPC and compression baselines.  
- Pros: clear motivation, strong qualitative analyses, compelling text generation, and simple training achieving competitive performance.  
- Cons: insufficient ablations of gradient normalization and depth vs width; some comparisons and plots are unfair or uninformative.  
- Overall: Worthwhile and largely convincing; with the additional experiments/comparisons recommended, it would be a strong NeurIPS paper.
