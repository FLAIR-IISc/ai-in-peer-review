- Proposes Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs) that stack RNN layers so each layer processes the hidden sequence of the layer below to build a temporal hierarchy.  
- Core claim: deeper processing helps older data be represented at longer time scales, making DRNNs suited for hierarchical temporal tasks.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art character-level language modeling on a real-world task using simple SGD, matching more complex Hessian-free methods.  
- Strong, clear motivation and well-written analysis demonstrating emergent time scales across layers.  
- Experiments on long-term dependencies (e.g., parenthesis closing) and text generation convincingly show distinct roles for different layers.  
- Weakness: experimental comparison to a standard RNN is confounded by differences in units per layer; need baselines with equal unit counts, not just equal total parameters.  
- Missing experiment: test whether higher-layer functions can be replicated by relocating units to a wider, shallower network; replot Figure 2 with various depths and wider shallow baselines.  
- Suggest adding comparison to Mikolov et al.'s Wikipedia compression results for context.  
- Training details need clarification: precisely how gradient normalization is done, how it prevents bifurcations, and whether it disrupts SGD averaging.  
- Suggest discussing related work on gradient truncation (e.g., Pascanu et al. ICML 2013).  
- Architectural question: consider variants that deepen the transition function (additional non-linear layers between timesteps) as an alternative form of depth.  
- Overall judgment: worthwhile, convincing paper needing stronger baseline comparisons; reviewer recommends acceptance.
