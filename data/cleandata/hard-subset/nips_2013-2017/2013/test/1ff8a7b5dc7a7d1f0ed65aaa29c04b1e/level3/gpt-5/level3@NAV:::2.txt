This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs)—stacked RNN layers with per-layer recurrency—for character-level language modeling (CLM). Holding parameter count fixed, the authors report that deeper RNNs outperform a single-layer RNN and provide analyses suggesting that higher layers encode longer time scales. They also show that plain BPTT (without Hessian-free, HF) suffices to learn bracket balancing behavior often attributed to more advanced training. The work connects to prior stacked/“deep” RNNs used in speech (e.g., Graves et al., 2013) and to CLM results with HF/MRNNs (Sutskever et al., 2011) and truncated BPTT in RNNLMs (Mikolov, 2010–2012).

Quality: The empirical methodology is generally sound: parameter-controlled comparisons, ablations on per-layer outputs, and informative time-scale probes (typo decay, context switch). However, conclusions rest on a single application (Wikipedia CLM) with only three model instantiations and one random seed per setting. The HF comparison is mainly via literature rather than controlled re-runs. The gradient normalization and incremental layer-wise training are clearly described.

Clarity: The paper is well written and reproducible at a high level; training details, hyperparameters, and ablation protocols are explicit. Please report variance across seeds and normalize compute by tokens seen to make cost claims more comparable.

Originality: The architectural idea (stacked RNNs with outputs from multiple layers) is not new; similar stacked LSTMs (and bidirectional variants) appeared earlier in speech. The paper should soften novelty claims and more clearly credit Graves et al. (2013) and related work. That said, focusing on analysis rather than proposing a new architecture is acceptable and valuable.

Significance: The finding that depth helps at fixed parameter budget for CLM, and the evidence that deeper layers capture longer-range dependencies, are useful and likely to influence practice. The observation that plain BPTT can acquire parenthesis tracking is noteworthy.

I mostly agree with the “one task” concern discussed in the rebuttal: claims about hierarchical temporal representations should be corroborated on additional domains (e.g., speech, word-level LM).

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear parameter-controlled evidence that depth helps in CLM.
- Insightful analyses of emergent time scales and layer roles.
- Demonstrates strong results with simple SGD/BPTT.

Cons for acceptance:
- Overstated novelty; insufficient credit to prior stacked RNNs.
- Single-task evaluation; limited seeds.
- Computationally heavy training setup.

Suggestions:
- Evaluate truncated BPTT (~30 steps à la Mikolov). It may miss some long parentheses but could match entropies at far lower cost—please test to confirm.
- Replicate key analyses on a second task (e.g., TIMIT/LibriSpeech) to assess generality.
- Report multi-seed means/variances and clarify compute/data-pass budgets.
