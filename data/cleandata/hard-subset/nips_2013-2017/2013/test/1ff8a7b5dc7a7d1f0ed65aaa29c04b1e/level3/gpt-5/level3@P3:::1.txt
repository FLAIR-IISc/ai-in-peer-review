Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) obtained by stacking multiple recurrent layers, each receiving the hidden state of the layer below. The architecture aims to combine hierarchical feature processing—central to deep feedforward models—with time-series modeling. Two output coupling schemes are considered: DRNN-1O (only the top layer connects to the softmax) and DRNN-AO (all layers connect to the softmax), the latter also serving as a form of deep supervision that improves gradient flow. Training uses SGD with gradient-norm normalization and backpropagation through time. For DRNN-1O, the paper introduces an incremental layer-wise supervised pre-training schedule: layers are added one at a time, each temporarily serving as the top layer trained on next-character prediction before the next layer is added.

Experiments are conducted on the Wikipedia character-level dataset popularized by Sutskever, Martens, and Hinton, with roughly 4.9M parameters per model and about 10 days of training on a single GPU. DRNNs match or modestly improve upon prior RNN results obtained with second-order optimization (multiplicative RNN, Hessian-free). The paper devotes substantial effort to analyzing network behavior: layer-wise contributions to prediction, emergent time scales, and long-term phenomena such as parentheses matching, for which the top layer exhibits an attractor-like state that persists until the closing parenthesis.

The work relates to earlier hierarchical RNNs (El Hihi & Bengio; Fernández/Graves/Schmidhuber) and stacked LSTMs for speech recognition (Graves et al.), but differs in: (i) a simple stacked tanh-RNN architecture trained purely with SGD; (ii) explicit deep supervision via all-layer outputs; and (iii) a careful empirical analysis of emergent temporal hierarchies on real text. It also sits alongside hierarchical HMMs/HHMMs as an approach to multi-timescale sequence structure, though no direct comparison is made.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architecture and training procedures are clearly specified and standard; the gradient normalization heuristic is reasonable. The incremental supervised layer-wise training is well-justified empirically.
- Empirical support: Results on a large-scale text corpus are solid for RNNs with comparable parameter counts. The analyses (typo sensitivity, context switch experiments, layer-wise contributions, and parentheses dynamics) are carefully designed and convincingly interpreted.
- Limitations: Evaluation is confined to a single, not widely standardized corpus, and comparisons are limited to RNN-like baselines. Important alternatives (e.g., hierarchical/explicit multi-timescale HMMs or HRNNs with subsampling) are not evaluated on the same data. Only three model instantiations are tried due to training cost; no variance across random seeds or hyperparameter sweeps is reported, leaving uncertainty about robustness and the absolute significance of the reported gains.

Clarity
- The paper is well-written and organized; the figures and experimental descriptions are clear. Model definitions, training details (BPTT length, minibatch setup, initialization), and evaluation metric (BPC) are provided with enough detail to enable reproduction. The analysis sections are particularly well-explained and insightful.
- Minor suggestions: Report variance across runs; include training curves; clarify the precise data pass/epoch count to ease comparison to HF training.

Originality
- Stacking recurrent layers is not entirely new, and prior work has explored hierarchical RNNs and stacked LSTMs. The incremental supervised pre-training for DRNN-1O and the all-layer output connections constitute a novel and pragmatic combination in this context. The main originality lies in the depth of the analysis linking layers to emergent timescales and long-term structures (e.g., parentheses as attractor-like states) in a real-world corpus.

Significance
- The work shows that large, simple stacked RNNs trained with SGD can be competitive with HF training on character-level language modeling, which is of practical interest. The analysis advances our understanding of how depth in recurrent architectures translates into multi-scale temporal processing—likely to influence future modeling and diagnostics.
- However, because evaluation is restricted to one dataset and a narrow set of baselines, the strength of the performance claims is limited. The paper’s main impact is the analysis and training insights rather than a conclusive advance in state-of-the-art results.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Broaden comparisons: include hierarchical HMMs/HHMMs and HRNNs with explicit multi-timescale mechanisms; report on standard char-level benchmarks (e.g., enwik8/text8, Penn Treebank char) for broader relevance.
- Ablations: quantify the effects of (i) all-layer outputs vs top-only outputs, (ii) the incremental supervised pre-training schedule, (iii) gradient-norm normalization, and (iv) BPTT truncation length.
- Model variants: compare tanh DRNNs to stacked LSTMs/GRUs under comparable parameter budgets; explore layer widths that vary with depth.
- Robustness: report multiple random initializations and hyperparameter sweeps (learning rate schedules, depth) to establish stability and significance.
- Analysis extensions: relate measured timescales to linearized dynamics (eigenvalue spectra) or gating statistics (if using LSTMs); probe nested parentheses and other hierarchical structures (quotes, brackets) and quantify failure modes.
- Reproducibility: release code and trained models if possible; detail data preprocessing choices and character vocabulary mapping.

Reasons to accept
- Clear, careful paper with high-quality figures and analyses.
- Simple stacked RNN trained with SGD achieves strong results on a large corpus, comparable to HF-trained MRNNs.
- Convincing evidence that depth induces multi-timescale processing; insightful diagnostics (typo/context tests, layer contributions).
- Novel and practical supervised layer-wise training schedule; deep supervision via all-layer outputs.

Reasons to reject
- Evaluation limited to one dataset; some comparisons not on the same corpus, reducing the credibility of performance claims.
- Baselines exclude hierarchical HMMs and some relevant hierarchical RNN variants; no LSTM/GRU comparisons under controlled settings.
- Limited exploration of hyperparameters and seeds; results could be sensitive given large models and long training.
- The contribution leans more toward analysis than demonstrating unequivocal performance gains.

Overall evaluation
A strong and well-executed paper whose primary contribution is a clear demonstration and analysis of emergent temporal hierarchies in deep recurrent networks, supported by solid large-scale experiments. While the performance evidence is promising, it is not definitive due to the narrow empirical scope. I recommend acceptance if the committee values analytical insight and clarity about how depth shapes recurrent computation; otherwise, this is borderline given the limited benchmarking.
