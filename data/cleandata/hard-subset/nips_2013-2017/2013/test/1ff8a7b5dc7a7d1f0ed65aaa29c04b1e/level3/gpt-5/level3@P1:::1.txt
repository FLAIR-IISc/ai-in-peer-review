This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) formed by stacking recurrent layers, where each layer receives the hidden state of the layer below. Two output couplings are considered: a single output from the top layer (DRNN-1O) and outputs from all layers summed before the softmax (DRNN-AO). Using simple SGD with gradient normalization, the authors train large models for character-level language modeling on a Wikipedia corpus, and analyze how temporal hierarchies emerge across layers. They report competitive bits-per-character (BPC) relative to prior recurrent baselines, and present probing experiments indicating that higher layers capture longer time scales (e.g., sustained evidence for closing parentheses).

Relation to prior work: The architecture is a straightforward stacked RNN, closely related to earlier hierarchical RNN constructions (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio; Fernández, Graves & Schmidhuber) and the stacked bi-directional LSTM systems used for speech (Graves et al.). The comparison to multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization (Sutskever, Martens & Hinton) is appropriate for the chosen dataset/metric. Unlike previous performance-focused work, this paper provides careful analysis of learned time scales and layer contributions, and shows that plain SGD (with gradient normalization) suffices to reach competitive results for large recurrent models.

Main contributions:
- An explicit formulation of stacked recurrent layers with two output coupling strategies (DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO), and a simple, supervised incremental training schedule (layer-wise addition with persistent end-to-end BPTT) that serves as pre-training for DRNN-1O.
- Empirical results on large-scale character modeling showing improved BPC over a shallow RNN and performance competitive with Hessian-free-trained MRNNs at similar parameter counts.
- Systematic analysis of layer roles and time scales via ablations of layer outputs, typo perturbations, context switches, and a parentheses-closing probe; evidence that upper layers encode longer-term structure and that DRNNs can sustain attractor-like states corresponding to open parentheses over hundreds of characters.

Quality:
- Technical approach is sound and clearly specified (update equations, training schedule, initialization, truncation length, gradient normalization). The analysis suite is thoughtful and well-executed; figures and diagnostics convincingly show emergent temporal hierarchies and long-term interactions.
- Limitations: experiments are confined to a single (not widely standardized) Wikipedia character dataset, with slight differences in character vocabulary from prior work; only three model instantiations were trained due to compute; no variance across random seeds; and baselines are limited to closely related RNN variants. Comparisons to LSTM/stacked LSTM, hierarchical HMMs/multi-scale temporal models, or more standard corpora (e.g., text8/enwik8, Penn Treebank char) would strengthen claims. The choice of 5 layers is motivated by “results not shown,” and there is little exploration of depth/width trade-offs or the effect of training schedule and truncation length.

Clarity:
- The manuscript is well written, organized, and accessible. Architectural definitions, training details, and evaluation metrics are sufficiently specified to allow reproduction by an expert reader. The rationale for AO vs. 1O is clear, and the incremental supervised pre-training is described transparently.
- Suggestions: (i) provide pseudo-code for the incremental training schedule; (ii) report compute budget more precisely (epochs-through-data, wall-clock per update) to enable fairer cost comparisons; (iii) quantify sensitivity to truncation length and gradient normalization versus standard clipping; (iv) clarify the impact of the character set differences on BPC.

Originality:
- The core architecture—stacking RNN layers—is not novel. The originality lies in the training strategy (a pragmatic supervised layer-wise schedule for 1O), the use of outputs at all layers (AO) for both optimization and interpretability, and the analysis methodology that quantifies time scales and long-range dependencies. These analyses provide new insights beyond typical performance reports of the era.

Significance:
- Moderate. The empirical results show that simple SGD on stacked RNNs can match prior RNN state-of-the-art at comparable parameter counts, which is practically useful. The analysis convincingly demonstrates an emergent temporal hierarchy and offers tools/intuition that may influence subsequent architecture and probing work. However, the limited breadth of datasets and baselines constrains the strength of performance claims, and the architecture itself is incremental relative to contemporary stacked LSTMs and multi-scale RNNs.

Pros:
- Clear, well-motivated study of stacked RNNs with thorough analysis of emergent time scales and layer roles.
- Simple training procedure (SGD with gradient normalization; supervised incremental schedule) that achieves competitive BPC.
- Insightful probes (typo perturbations, context switches, parentheses attractor) that concretely illustrate long-term behavior.

Cons:
- Empirical scope limited to a single dataset and few model variants; no comparisons to LSTM/stacked LSTM or hierarchical HMMs on standardized corpora.
- Limited ablations (depth, width, truncation length, training schedule) and no reporting of variance across runs.
- “State-of-the-art” claims are hard to assess given dataset/version differences and lack of broader baselines.

Recommendations for improvement:
- Evaluate on at least one additional standard character-level corpus (e.g., text8/enwik8) and include strong baselines (LSTM/stacked LSTM, gated RNNs, and, if feasible, hierarchical HMMs or multi-scale clockwork RNNs).
- Provide ablations over number of layers, units per layer, AO vs. 1O with/without incremental pre-training, truncation length, and gradient normalization versus clipping.
- Report multiple seeds to assess stability; include parameter-efficiency plots (BPC vs. parameters/depth).
- Explore second-order training on DRNNs (as proposed in future work) to directly compare with Hessian-free MRNNs under equal compute/data budgets.

Overall, this is a careful and informative paper whose primary impact is the analysis of deep recurrent architectures rather than a decisive advance in empirical performance. I lean toward acceptance if space allows for analytical contributions, but the authors should temper claims and broaden the empirical evaluation where possible.
