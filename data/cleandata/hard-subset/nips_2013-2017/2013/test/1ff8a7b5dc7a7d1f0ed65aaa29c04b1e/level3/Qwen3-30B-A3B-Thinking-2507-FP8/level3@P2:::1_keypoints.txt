- Introduces Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs) that stack recurrent layers to create a temporal hierarchy where different layers process different time scales.
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art character-level language modeling on Wikipedia using only SGD, avoiding more complex Hessian-free optimization.
- Provides detailed analysis of emergent time scales across layers, with perturbation experiments showing higher layers capture longer-term dependencies.
- Shows DRNNs can handle long-range dependencies (e.g., parentheses matching) and provides qualitative results like text generation.
- Builds on prior hierarchical RNN work but contributes a systematic analysis of how temporal scales naturally emerge in the architecture.
- Strength: technically sound architecture with robust experiments and insightful perturbation-based analysis of time scales.
- Strength: exceptionally clear writing, figures, and intuitive demonstrations of long-term memory capabilities.
- Strength: original contribution in analyzing temporal scales and showing competitive performance with simple SGD.
- Strength: significant implications for temporal hierarchies beyond language modeling and for understanding recurrent processing without specialized memory units.
- Weakness: evaluation limited to a single dataset (Wikipedia) and comparisons confined mainly to RNN-related models.
- Weakness: performance gains are modest and somewhat inconclusive (e.g., DRNN-AO underperforms DRNN-1O; small BPC improvements).
- Weakness: the layer-wise incremental training procedure for DRNN-1O is complex and not clearly justified.
- Pro: offers important theoretical and empirical insights into hierarchical temporal processing and challenges assumptions about complex training needs.
- Con: lacks broader comparisons (e.g., hierarchical HMMs), has limited experimental scope, modest gains, and an underexplained training methodology.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address limited evaluation scope and to better justify the DRNN-1O training procedure.
