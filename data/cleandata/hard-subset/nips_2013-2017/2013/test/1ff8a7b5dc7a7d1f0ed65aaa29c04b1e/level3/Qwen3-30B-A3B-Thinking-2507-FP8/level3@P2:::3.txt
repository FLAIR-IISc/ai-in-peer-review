

# Review of "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks"

This paper introduces Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), a novel architecture where multiple recurrent layers are stacked such that each layer receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input. The authors argue that this architecture naturally creates a temporal hierarchy, allowing different layers to process information at different time scales. They demonstrate that DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks in character-level language modeling using simple SGD, and provide an analysis of emergent time scales across layers.

The paper builds on previous work such as hierarchical RNNs in [3,5,6] and stacked bidirectional LSTMs in [8], but provides a more thorough analysis of the architecture's temporal properties. The authors' main contribution is showing that DRNNs automatically create a hierarchy of time scales through their architecture, rather than relying on explicit subsampling as in [5], and that this leads to better performance on character-level language modeling.

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and presents a compelling architecture for processing time series with hierarchical temporal structures. The text-generation experiments (Table 2) are particularly convincing, demonstrating that the DRNN-AO model produces coherent text with meaningful long-term dependencies. The analysis of time scales through the typo-perturbation test (Figure 3) provides strong evidence that different layers indeed operate at different time scales, which is a key claim of the paper. The paper also makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of how DRNNs process information at multiple time scales, which is relevant to the broader question of how neural networks handle temporal hierarchies.

**Weaknesses and Recommendations:**

1. **Comparison fairness:** The paper compares DRNNs with a standard RNN that has 2,119 units, while the DRNNs have approximately 4.9 million parameters. This is an unfair comparison since the DRNNs have vastly more capacity. The authors should provide baselines with the same number of parameters or units to properly assess the architectural contribution versus raw parameter count.

2. **Missing key experiment:** The paper claims that higher layers benefit from processing by lower layers, but this needs empirical validation. A critical missing experiment would be to test whether the same units placed in the first layer (rather than higher layers) would achieve similar performance. The current experiments (Figure 2) don't distinguish between the benefits of architectural hierarchy versus simply having more units.

3. **Gradient normalization explanation:** The authors use gradient normalization to prevent "bifurcations" but provide insufficient explanation. How does this technique specifically avoid the problems of gradient explosion and instability? The paper should clarify how this differs from standard gradient clipping and whether it disrupts SGD's averaging behavior.

4. **Missing comparisons:** The paper should compare results to Mikolov et al.'s work on Wikipedia compression tasks, which is the standard benchmark for character-level language modeling. Additionally, the paper should cite and discuss relevant ICML 2013 work on gradient optimization (e.g., the U. of Montreal paper on gradient truncation) that could inform their training approach.

5. **Terminology clarity:** DRNN-AO and DRNN-1O should be defined in the text, not just in Figure 1, to improve accessibility for readers.

6. **Layer surgery interpretation:** The paper's interpretation of layer removal experiments (Figure 2) is confounded by complex interdependencies between layers. The authors should temper their claims about individual layer importance and acknowledge this limitation.

7. **Parameter count vs. depth:** The paper should plot performance for various DRNN depths trained from scratch (including wider layers to match parameters/units), rather than the current Figure 2, to better isolate the effect of depth from parameter count.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable architecture for processing time series with hierarchical temporal structures. The core idea that stacking RNNs creates natural time-scale hierarchies is well-motivated and supported by the experiments, though the evidence would be stronger with the suggested additional experiments. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to understanding how RNN architectures process temporal information.

The most significant concerns are the unfair comparison with parameter counts and the missing experiment to validate whether higher layers genuinely benefit from upstream processing. Addressing these would substantially strengthen the paper's claims. As it stands, the paper is largely convincing but requires these additional experiments and comparisons before it can be considered for publication.

**Recommendation:** Major Revision. The paper has strong potential but needs to address the key experimental and comparative issues identified above. With these revisions, the paper would make a significant contribution to the field of recurrent neural networks.
