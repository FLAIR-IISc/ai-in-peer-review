This paper proposes a novel architecture, called Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), which combines the strengths of deep neural networks and recurrent neural networks. The authors argue that traditional RNNs do not explicitly accommodate temporal hierarchies, and most research has focused on training algorithms rather than the basic architecture. In contrast, DRNNs introduce a hierarchy of recurrent neural networks, where each layer is a recurrent network that receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input. This architecture allows for hierarchical processing of time series data and more naturally captures the structure of time series.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of DRNNs on a character-level language modeling task, achieving state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks on a Wikipedia text corpus. They also provide an analysis of the different emergent time scales in the DRNNs, showing that the layers develop distinct time scales during training.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their architecture and experiments. However, there are a few areas that could be improved.

One potential weakness of the paper is the lack of comparison to other architectures with the same number of parameters. The authors compare their results to a common RNN and a multiplicative RNN (MRNN) trained with Hessian-free optimization, but these models have different numbers of parameters. To make a fair comparison, the authors should provide baselines with the same number of parameters or units.

Another area for improvement is the interpretation of the layer "surgery" experiments. While these experiments are persuasive, the authors acknowledge that the results are confounded by complex interdependencies between layers. To address this, the authors could provide additional experiments that test whether higher layers genuinely gain from upstream processing versus simply relocating units to the first layer.

The authors also mention that they used gradient normalization to avoid the problem of bifurcations, but they do not provide a detailed explanation of how this works. They could benefit from a more detailed discussion of this technique and its potential impact on the performance of the DRNNs.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel architecture that combines the strengths of deep neural networks and recurrent neural networks. While the idea of stacking RNNs is not new, the authors provide a clear explanation of how this architecture can be used to capture temporal hierarchies in time series data.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to improve the performance of recurrent neural networks on tasks that require hierarchical processing of time series data. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their architecture on a character-level language modeling task, and their results suggest that DRNNs could be used to improve performance on other tasks that require similar processing.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their architecture and experiments. While there are a few areas that could be improved, the paper is worthwhile and largely convincing.
