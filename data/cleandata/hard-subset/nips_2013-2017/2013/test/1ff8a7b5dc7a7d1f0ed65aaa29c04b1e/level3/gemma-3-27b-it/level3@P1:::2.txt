This paper investigates the benefits of depth in recurrent neural networks (RNNs), specifically focusing on character-level language modeling. The core contribution is demonstrating that deeper RNNs, with a comparable number of parameters to shallower models, achieve state-of-the-art performance. The authors also present an analysis suggesting deeper layers encode longer-range dependencies and exhibit distinct time scales. While the paper doesn't introduce a fundamentally *new* architecture – similar stacked RNNs have appeared previously (e.g., Graves 2013) – it provides a valuable analysis of the emergent properties of such networks and demonstrates strong performance using a relatively simple training procedure (SGD) on a challenging task.

The work builds upon the established successes of deep neural networks (DNNs) in areas like speech and image recognition, questioning why similar hierarchical structures haven't been fully explored in the recurrent domain. The authors rightly point out that traditional RNNs primarily focus on memory, not explicit hierarchical processing. By stacking RNNs, the resulting Deep RNN (DRNN) naturally introduces multiple timescales, allowing for processing of time series with inherent hierarchical structure.  The experiments using character-level language modeling are a strong choice, as this task demands modeling both short and long-term dependencies, offering a good testbed for the proposed architecture.

The strengths of the paper are significant. The empirical results are compelling, showing a clear performance improvement with depth. The analysis of time scales and layer contributions is insightful. The finding that plain BPTT can achieve competitive results, previously thought to require more sophisticated methods like Hessian-free optimization, is noteworthy and potentially reduces the computational cost for training these models. The included examples of text generation and the analysis of parenthesis balancing demonstrate the network’s ability to learn long-range dependencies. 

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. The claim of introducing the deep RNN architecture is inaccurate, as similar architectures were explored in prior work, particularly in Graves (2013). While the authors acknowledge prior work, framing the contribution as solely an analysis rather than architectural novelty would be more accurate.  A more substantial concern is the potential task-specificity of the results.  Character-level language modeling, while challenging, may not fully capture the complexity of all time series data. Replicating the analysis on other tasks, such as speech recognition, would significantly strengthen the claims about the benefits of hierarchical processing and the emergence of different timescales. Finally, the computational cost of training is high. While demonstrating state-of-the-art performance is valuable, exploring more efficient training methods than full BPTT is important. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results demonstrating improved performance with depth in RNNs.
*   Insightful analysis of time scales and layer contributions.
*   Demonstration of competitive performance with a simpler training algorithm (SGD) than previously believed necessary.
*   Addresses an important question regarding the role of depth in recurrent architectures.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Inaccurate claim of introducing a novel architecture.
*   Potential task-specificity of the results; lack of generalization to other time series domains.
*   High computational cost of training.



I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Clarify the novelty:** Reframe the contribution to emphasize the analysis of emergent properties and performance gains rather than introducing a new architecture. Acknowledge prior work on stacked RNNs more explicitly in the introduction and related work sections.
2.  **Discuss limitations & future work:** Expand the discussion on the potential task-specificity of the findings and outline plans for future work to validate the results on other datasets and tasks.
3.  **Explore truncated BPTT:** As suggested in related rebuttals, the authors should explore truncated BPTT with a reasonable step size (e.g., 30 steps). This would likely significantly reduce computational cost while potentially maintaining comparable performance. While missing extremely long-range dependencies (like perfectly balancing deeply nested parentheses) might be a tradeoff, it would be a worthwhile compromise to achieve similar entropy at a lower cost.



Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field, offering valuable insights into the behavior of deep recurrent neural networks. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above will further strengthen the paper and its impact.
