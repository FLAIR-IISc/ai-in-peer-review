Summary and relation to prior work
This paper investigates deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) formed by stacking recurrent layers, where each layer receives the hidden state of the previous layer. Two output variants are considered: output from the top layer only (DRNN-1O) or from all layers (DRNN-AO). The authors study character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus and analyze emergent time scales across layers via perturbation experiments and a targeted “parentheses” probe. Main findings are: (i) for a fixed parameter budget, deeper RNNs outperform a single-layer RNN; (ii) higher layers tend to encode longer-range dependencies; and (iii) plain SGD/BPTT suffices to train models that capture long-term structure (e.g., parentheses), challenging the notion that Hessian-free (HF) optimization is necessary for such tasks.

The architectural idea of stacked/“deep” RNNs has appeared before, notably hierarchical/stacked RNNs for long-term dependencies (El Hihi & Bengio, 1996), sequence labeling (Fernández et al., 2005), and, very closely, deep/stacked LSTM RNNs for speech (Graves, Mohamed, Hinton, ICASSP 2013). Multiplicative RNNs and HF training (Sutskever, Martens & Hinton, ICML 2011) established strong character-level baselines; Mikolov’s RNN LMs used truncated BPTT. The present work’s novelty lies less in architecture and more in the empirical analysis of time scales and in showing that vanilla BPTT/SGD with gradient norm clipping (cf. Pascanu et al., 2013) can reach competitive bits-per-character (BPC) on enwik-style data under a fixed parameter budget.

Strengths
- Clear empirical result: with the same number of parameters, stacked RNNs outperform a single-layer RNN on character-level modeling.
- Careful, insightful analyses of emergent time scales via perturbation experiments, layer ablations, and a concrete “parentheses” probe. The qualitative generation examples are informative.
- Demonstrates that simple SGD with gradient clipping suffices to learn long dependencies in this domain, reducing reliance on HF and complex architectures.
- Nice discussion of output-from-all-layers vs top-only, and a practical incremental layer-wise training scheme for DRNN-1O.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty/positioning: the paper sometimes reads as if the DRNN architecture is introduced here; stacked recurrent architectures are not new. Please moderate claims and more prominently acknowledge closely related prior work (e.g., stacked LSTMs for speech).
- Scope: all results are on a single task/dataset. The “one task” criticism is valid; conclusions about time-scale hierarchies and training ease might be task-specific. Extending the analysis to a second domain (e.g., TIMIT/WSJ phoneme/character tasks, or Penn Treebank/enwik8) would strengthen claims.
- Training cost and design: unrolling 250 steps with long BPTT is expensive. Given Mikolov’s success with truncated BPTT (≈20–30), it would be very informative to compare against truncated BPTT (e.g., 30 steps). It may reduce cost substantially while preserving BPC; if it degrades the parentheses behavior, that would clarify what is gained by long truncation.
- Limited ablations: only three model configurations are reported; no sweep over depth vs width, unroll length, or gating (e.g., LSTM/GRU) under the same parameter budget. The AO vs 1O comparison is interesting but inconclusive without more runs or statistical confidence.
- Comparisons: MRNN/HF comparisons are informative but not fully normalized for data passes/compute. Please clarify total tokens seen and wall-clock vs algorithmic efficiency to enable fairer comparisons.

Quality
- Generally sound and well-executed. Training details are provided (parameter counts, initialization, learning rate schedule, BPTT length, batch construction). Analyses are thoughtful and go beyond headline numbers. However, the experimental scope is narrow (one dataset, three configs), and some claims about architecture novelty should be toned down. Including a truncated-BPTT baseline and an additional dataset would considerably strengthen the evidence.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. Figures effectively convey time-scale analyses. Suggestions:
  - Make the novelty claims precise; cite stacked LSTMs for speech more prominently in the introduction and abstract.
  - Report unroll length (effective BPTT truncation) more explicitly in the main text and discuss its computational implications.
  - Clarify the role of gradient norm clipping (and cite Pascanu et al., 2013 on exploding gradients).
  - Provide exact training budgets (updates, epochs, tokens) for all models and for the MRNN/HF baseline for fairer comparison.

Originality
- Architectural originality is limited; stacked RNNs and multi-timescale ideas are established. The main original contributions are the systematic time-scale analyses on a large natural dataset, the AO vs 1O comparison, and the empirical finding that SGD can reach strong BPC and long-range behaviors without HF.

Significance
- Moderate. The results will be useful to practitioners considering depth in RNNs and to researchers studying temporal hierarchies. If corroborated on additional tasks and with cheaper training (e.g., truncated BPTT), the conclusions would be more broadly impactful.

Suggestions for improvement and additional experiments
- Add a truncated BPTT baseline (e.g., 30 steps), ideally matched for parameter count and total tokens seen. This could match BPC at much lower cost; if it degrades the parentheses behavior, report that trade-off explicitly.
- Replicate core findings on at least one additional task (e.g., TIMIT phoneme recognition or PTB/enwik8) to address the “one task” concern.
- Include a depth/width trade-off study under a fixed parameter budget; report variance across random seeds for robustness.
- Clarify compute normalization in comparisons to HF/MRNN; consider a small HF experiment on your DRNN to separate optimizer vs architecture effects.
- Consider gating baselines (LSTM/GRU) at equal parameter budget to contextualize the benefit of depth vs gating.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear and practically relevant finding that depth helps RNNs at fixed parameter count.
- Insightful and carefully designed analyses of layer-wise time scales and long-range behaviors.
- Demonstrates strong performance with simple training, potentially lowering the barrier to entry.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty in architecture; related stacked RNN work predates this submission.
- Evidence is confined to one task/dataset with a small set of configurations.
- Missing truncated-BPTT baseline; training may be unnecessarily expensive relative to achievable BPC.

Overall recommendation
Leaning toward accept if the authors moderate novelty claims and add at least a truncated-BPTT comparison (and, ideally, one additional dataset). Otherwise, this is a solid, well-executed analysis paper whose insights on temporal hierarchies in deep RNNs would be of interest to the NIPS community.
