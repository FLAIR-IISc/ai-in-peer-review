

**Review of "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks"**

This paper introduces "Deep Recurrent Neural Networks" (DRNNs), a hierarchical architecture where each layer is a recurrent network that receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input. The authors argue this architecture better captures temporal hierarchies in time series data, particularly for character-level language modeling. They demonstrate that DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks on Wikipedia text using only stochastic gradient descent (SGD), whereas prior state-of-the-art results required Hessian-free optimization. The paper also analyzes how different layers operate at different time scales and demonstrates that DRNNs can learn to balance long sequences of parentheses.

The work builds on prior research: Graves (2013) used stacked bidirectional LSTMs for speech recognition, and El Hihi & Bengio (1995) proposed hierarchical RNNs for artificial tasks. However, the paper incorrectly claims to have "introduced" the deep RNN architecture, which is a significant oversight.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a compelling empirical case that deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs when the number of parameters is held constant on character-level language modeling—a significant contribution to understanding depth in RNNs. The analysis of time scales across layers provides valuable insights into how hierarchical processing manifests in RNNs. Most notably, the paper demonstrates that plain BPTT can train RNNs to balance long sequences of parentheses (contrary to prior belief that Hessian-free methods were necessary), which is a substantial contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
The most serious weakness is the incorrect claim that the authors "introduced" the deep RNN architecture. Graves (2013) already used stacked RNNs (with LSTMs), and El Hihi & Bengio (1995) proposed hierarchical RNNs. The authors should acknowledge this prior work and reframe their contribution as an analysis of an existing architecture rather than a novel proposal.

The claim that DRNNs "reach state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks" is misleading. The authors achieved comparable performance to Hessian-free trained networks with the same number of parameters, but this could be achieved with more parameters in standard RNNs. The real contribution is that they achieved this with SGD rather than Hessian-free optimization.

The results are limited to character-level language modeling. The paper would be significantly strengthened by replication on additional tasks (e.g., speech recognition) to confirm the generality of their findings. The authors should also test truncated BPTT (with ~30 steps) to confirm whether it could match the entropy results at much lower computational cost, as Mikolov et al. demonstrated for similar tasks.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-designed experiments that support its main claims. The analysis of time scales is particularly strong. However, the authors should be more careful in their claims about novelty and clarify that their contribution is primarily analytical rather than architectural.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with effective figures (Figures 1-4) that convey the architecture and experimental results. The explanation of the DRNN architecture and its time-scale properties is particularly clear.

**Originality:** The paper's main contribution is not a new architecture (as claimed), but rather an analysis of an existing architecture. While the analysis of time scales and long-range dependency learning is novel, the paper's claim of introducing a new architecture is problematic. The authors should reframe their contribution as "analyzing the properties of an existing deep RNN architecture."

**Significance:** The paper makes an important contribution to understanding how depth affects RNNs, with practical implications for training sequential models. The finding that plain SGD can achieve state-of-the-art results (previously requiring Hessian-free optimization) is significant for practitioners. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the narrow scope of experiments.

**Recommendation:** With revision to correct the novelty claim and clarify the contribution as analytical rather than architectural, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As written, the paper has a significant weakness in its claim of novelty, but the technical content is strong.

**Pro and Con Arguments:**
*Pro:* Demonstrates deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs with same parameters; provides valuable time-scale analysis; shows plain BPTT can handle long-range dependencies (contrary to prior belief); well-written with clear experimental design.
*Con:* Incorrectly claims to have introduced deep RNN architecture (Graves 2013, El Hihi & Bengio 1995 already used similar models); results limited to character-level language modeling; misleading "state-of-the-art" claim; HF experiments likely unnecessarily expensive.

**Additional Suggestions:**
1. Acknowledge prior work on stacked RNNs (Graves 2013, El Hihi & Bengio 1995) and reframe contribution as analytical.
2. Test truncated BPTT (~30 steps) to confirm whether it could achieve similar results at lower computational cost.
3. Replicate findings on additional tasks (e.g., speech recognition) to strengthen claims about generality.
4. Clarify that the "state-of-the-art" claim refers to achieving comparable performance with SGD rather than HF optimization.
