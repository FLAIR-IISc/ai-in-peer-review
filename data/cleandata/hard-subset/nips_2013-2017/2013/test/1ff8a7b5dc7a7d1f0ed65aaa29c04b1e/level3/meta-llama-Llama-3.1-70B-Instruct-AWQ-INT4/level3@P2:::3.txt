The paper "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks" presents a novel architecture that combines the strengths of deep neural networks (DNNs) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs). The authors propose a deep recurrent neural network (DRNN) that consists of multiple layers, each of which is a recurrent neural network that receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input. This architecture allows for hierarchical processing of time series data and captures the temporal hierarchy present in the data.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a character-level language modeling task, achieving state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks on a Wikipedia text corpus. They also provide an in-depth analysis of the network's behavior, including the emergence of different time scales at different layers and the ability to model long-term dependencies.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their architecture and experimental setup. The results are impressive, and the analysis of the network's behavior provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.

One of the key strengths of the paper is its ability to capture the temporal hierarchy present in the data. The authors demonstrate that their approach can learn to model long-term dependencies and capture the structure of the data at different time scales. This is a significant improvement over traditional RNNs, which often struggle to model long-term dependencies.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the main concerns is the lack of comparison to other architectures with the same number of parameters or units. The authors compare their approach to a traditional RNN with a different number of parameters, which makes it difficult to determine the true effectiveness of their approach. Additionally, the authors could have provided more analysis on the importance of the gradient normalizations and how they avoid the problem of bifurcations.

Furthermore, the paper could benefit from additional experiments and comparisons. For example, the authors could have tested whether higher layers genuinely gain from upstream processing versus simply relocating units to the first layer. This would provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of their approach and help to address concerns about the unfair comparison.

Despite these limitations, the paper is worthwhile and largely convincing. The authors provide a novel architecture that addresses some of the key limitations of traditional RNNs, and the results are impressive. With some additional experiments and comparisons, this paper could make a significant contribution to the field of deep learning.
