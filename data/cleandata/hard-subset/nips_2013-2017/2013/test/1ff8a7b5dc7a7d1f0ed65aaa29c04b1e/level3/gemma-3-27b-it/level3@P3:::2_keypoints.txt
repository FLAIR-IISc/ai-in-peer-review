- Core idea: stack RNN layers to form a hierarchy where each layer receives the previous layer’s hidden state to capture temporal hierarchies in sequences.  
- Main empirical claim: deeper RNNs with comparable parameter counts achieve state-of-the-art character-level language modeling.  
- Analysis finding: deeper layers encode longer time scales; experiments show different layers respond to perturbations at different rates.  
- Surprising result: plain BPTT can train the network to balance parentheses without specialized optimization (e.g., Hessian-free).  
- Relation to prior work: builds on existing deep and hierarchical RNN research; similar stacked architectures (e.g., Graves) reduce novelty of the architecture itself.  
- Strength — empirical: strong performance on a challenging task using simple SGD training.  
- Strength — analysis: insightful visualization and experiments on temporal scales and parenthesis balancing.  
- Strength — presentation: well-written, clear motivation, architecture, and experimental setup.  
- Strength — relevance: investigating depth in RNNs is important for sequential modeling research.  
- Weakness — novelty: claiming a novel “deep RNN” architecture is questionable given prior similar works.  
- Weakness — generality: results are shown only on character-level language modeling; unclear generalization to other sequential domains.  
- Weakness — computational cost: authors do not sufficiently investigate BPTT’s cost; truncated BPTT (e.g., ~30 steps) might be much cheaper with similar performance.  
- Weakness — architecture variations: limited exploration of output-connection strategies beyond the presented DRNN-1O vs DRNN-AO comparison.  
- Overall assessment: solid paper with valuable analysis and empirical evidence, but should frame contribution as analysis of existing architectures rather than a novel architecture.  
- Recommendation and revisions: accept with minor revisions — investigate truncated BPTT efficiency, clarify novelty claim, and extend/replicate analysis on other tasks (e.g., speech) to strengthen generality.
