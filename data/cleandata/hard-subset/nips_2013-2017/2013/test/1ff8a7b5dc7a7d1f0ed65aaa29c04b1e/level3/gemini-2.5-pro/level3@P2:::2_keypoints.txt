- Investigates deep recurrent neural networks (stacked RNN layers) hypothesized to learn a temporal hierarchy across layers.  
- Evaluates DRNNs on character-level language modeling (large Wikipedia corpus) against a parameter-matched single-layer RNN.  
- Main contributions: empirical demonstration that DRNNs outperform shallow RNNs and analysis showing higher layers capture longer time-scale dependencies.  
- Key finding: simple SGD with gradient clipping can train DRNNs to state-of-the-art performance and learn very long-range dependencies (e.g., balancing parentheses).  
- Experimental design is strong and fair, controlling total parameter count for depth comparisons.  
- Analysis is thorough and insightful (emergent time scales, long-term dependency experiments) beyond BPC reporting.  
- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides sufficient training-detail reproducibility.  
- Architectural novelty is overstated: similar stacked RNN/LSTM architectures exist (e.g., Graves et al. 2013); novelty lies primarily in analysis and training observations.  
- Significance: supports depth as beneficial for RNNs, offers insight into temporal hierarchies, and challenges the necessity of second-order optimizers for long-range tasks.  
- Limitation: results are shown on a single task, weakening claims of generality across sequential domains like speech or word-level modeling.  
- Concern: training cost is high (full BPTT over 250 steps); no comparison to cheaper approaches like truncated BPTT.  
- Arguments for acceptance: parameter-controlled depth benefit, novel layer-wise temporal analysis, challenge to pessimism about SGD, strong Wikipedia benchmark results.  
- Arguments for rejection: overstated novelty, single-task evidence limits generality, and lack of efficiency baselines (truncated BPTT).  
- Suggestions: reframe to emphasize analysis over architectural novelty, replicate experiments on another sequential task (e.g., speech), and include truncated BPTT baselines to assess cost-performance tradeoffs.  
- Overall verdict: significant and insightful work despite originality and scope issues; reviewer leans toward acceptance.
