- Proposes Deep RNN (DRNN) architecture where each layer is an RNN receiving previous layer hidden states to capture temporal hierarchies.  
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art character-level language modeling on Wikipedia using simple SGD.  
- Provides initial analysis of emergent time scales and layer-specific temporal processing.  
- Motivation: addresses lack of explicit hierarchical/depth structure in standard RNNs for time series.  
- Novelty & soundness: DRNN is a well-motivated extension of depth to RNNs with compelling empirical results.  
- Significance: shows improved ability to capture long-term dependencies, relevant for many sequential tasks.  
- Clarity: paper is generally well-written and Figure 1 effectively visualizes the architecture.  
- Analysis: text generation and layer “surgery” experiments offer useful but cautious insights into internal dynamics.  
- Gradient normalization: role and mechanism are under-explained; potential downsides and related work (e.g., Montreal ICML 2013) are not discussed.  
- Baselines & comparisons: comparisons are limited by mismatched parameter counts and lack of direct baselines with equal parameters; missing comparison to Mikolov et al. on Wikipedia compression.  
- Architectural investigation: lacks systematic exploration of DRNN depth and width—Figure 2 is illustrative but insufficient.  
- Layer interpretation: “surgery” results may be confounded by inter-layer dependencies; need experiments to test if higher layers truly benefit from hierarchy versus relocated units.  
- Minor clarity: DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO are defined only in a figure caption, not in the main text.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, well-motivated architecture with strong empirical results, insightful time-scale analysis, and clear writing.  
- Arguments for rejection: insufficient analysis of gradient normalization, limited baseline parity, no systematic architecture study, and potential confounds in layer contribution interpretation.  
- Overall assessment: worthwhile and convincing paper recommending acceptance with minor revisions to address gradient normalization, baseline comparisons, and experiments validating hierarchical benefit.
