## Review of "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks"

This paper investigates the impact of depth in recurrent neural networks (RNNs) for character-level language modeling. The core idea is to stack RNN layers, creating a deep RNN (DRNN) that can potentially capture hierarchical temporal structures within sequential data. The authors demonstrate that deeper RNNs, with a comparable number of parameters to standard RNNs, achieve state-of-the-art performance on a Wikipedia text corpus. They further present analysis suggesting that deeper layers encode more long-range information and, surprisingly, that plain backpropagation through time (BPTT) can successfully train these networks to balance parentheses – a task previously believed to require more sophisticated methods like Hessian-free training.

The paper is well-motivated, building on the success of deep neural networks in other domains and questioning why this depth hasn’t been more fully explored in the context of RNNs. The experimental results are compelling, showing a clear performance improvement with increasing depth. The analysis of time scales and parenthesis balancing is insightful and contributes to a better understanding of how these deeper networks function. However, the authors’ claim of *introducing* the deep RNN architecture is inaccurate; prior work, notably Graves (2013), already utilized stacked RNNs, including LSTMs. While the paper rightly pivots to analysis, this oversight should be acknowledged.

A significant weakness is the potential task-specificity of the findings. Character-level language modeling, while a good testbed, may not fully generalize to other sequential tasks. Replicating the analysis on tasks like speech recognition, as suggested in the paper itself, would significantly strengthen the claims. Furthermore, the authors’ use of full BPTT is computationally expensive. As highlighted in the rebuttal and in line with established practice (e.g., Mikolov’s work), truncated BPTT with a reasonable length (I suggest around 30 steps) should be tested. While it might miss some very long-range dependencies, it could achieve comparable performance at a much lower computational cost and provide a more practical training approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates a clear performance gain with depth in RNNs.
*   Provides insightful analysis of learned time scales and parenthesis balancing.
*   Addresses an important question about the role of depth in recurrent models.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Inaccurate claim of introducing the deep RNN architecture.
*   Potential task-specificity of results.
*   Computational cost of full BPTT; truncated BPTT should be explored.



**Overall:** Despite the minor historical inaccuracy and the need for broader task validation and more efficient training exploration, this paper represents a valuable contribution to understanding deep recurrent networks. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors acknowledging the prior work on stacked RNNs and including a discussion of truncated BPTT in the final version.
