- Proposes Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs): stack multiple RNN layers where each layer receives hidden sequences from the layer below to model temporal hierarchies.  
- Motivation: temporal data often has hierarchical time scales, so deeper per-timestep processing can better capture long-range dependencies.  
- Empirical result: a 5-layer DRNN trained with simple SGD and a gradient-normalization heuristic achieves state-of-the-art character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus.  
- Training insight: simple SGD plus a gradient normalization trick matched performance of more complex second-order methods (e.g., Hessian-free).  
- Model analysis: qualitative experiments suggest different layers operate at different time scales and can capture very long-term dependencies (e.g., matching parentheses hundreds of characters apart).  
- Contribution relative to prior work: focuses on analyzing architectural benefits of stacked RNNs rather than using stacking solely as an engineering tool.  
- Strength — conceptual clarity: presents a simple, intuitive idea shifting focus from complex training algorithms to architectural design.  
- Strength — strong empirical evidence: impressive large-scale results demonstrate architecture’s practical impact.  
- Strength — insightful analysis: layer ablation, text samples, and long-range dependency tests provide persuasive evidence of hierarchical behavior.  
- Weakness — baseline comparisons: lacks rigorous depth-vs-width controlled baselines (e.g., single-layer RNNs widened to match parameter counts of deeper models).  
- Weakness — layer ablation limitations: removing a layer post-training may mislead because layers develop interdependencies; need experiments training networks of varying depths from scratch.  
- Weakness — insufficient detail on gradient normalization: heuristic is under-explained, its necessity unquantified, and its relation to established clipping methods (Pascanu et al.) is not discussed.  
- Weakness — broader comparisons: should contextualize results against other contemporaneous language-modeling work (e.g., Mikolov et al.).  
- Questions/suggestions: consider testing alternative depth (deeper within-step transition functions) and clarify acronyms (DRNN-1O, DRNN-AO) in text.  
- Overall verdict: well-written, convincing paper with strong results; recommend acceptance but request additional controlled depth-vs-width experiments to strengthen the central claim.
