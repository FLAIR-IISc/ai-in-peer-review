

This paper proposes Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), where multiple RNN layers are stacked such that each layer receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input. The authors argue that this architecture naturally creates a temporal hierarchy, with different layers operating at different time scales, which is particularly beneficial for processing time series with complex temporal structures. The paper demonstrates DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks on character-level language modeling using simple SGD training, without requiring advanced optimization techniques like Hessian-free methods. The authors also provide analysis of emergent time scales through various experiments, including layer "surgery" tests and analysis of parenthesis closure capabilities.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it clearly establishes the conceptual link between deep architectures in feedforward networks and recurrent networks, arguing that conventional RNNs do not explicitly support hierarchical temporal processing. The proposed DRNN architecture elegantly combines these concepts. Second, the paper provides compelling experimental evidence that DRNNs develop distinct time scales across layers, with bottom layers handling short-term dependencies and top layers capturing longer-term patterns. The parenthesis closure experiment (Figure 4) particularly demonstrates the DRNN's ability to maintain long-term dependencies, a capability that is more pronounced than in standard RNNs.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a strong case for the proposed architecture. The results are technically sound, with thorough experiments on a Wikipedia text corpus and appropriate comparison to previous work, including the Hessian-free MRNN approach. The authors' analysis of different time scales across layers (Figure 3) provides valuable theoretical insight into how DRNNs process temporal information.

However, several concerns need addressing. The comparison to previous work is not entirely fair due to parameter count differences: the DRNN-AO (9.6M parameters) outperforms the RNN (2.1M parameters), but the authors do not provide a fair comparison with networks of comparable size. The paper should include baselines with matched parameter counts to ensure the performance gains are due to architecture rather than capacity. The authors should also clarify whether they've tried adding extra nonlinear layers between timesteps, as the paper suggests this might be beneficial.

The gradient normalization technique used (Equation 3) is not thoroughly justified. The authors claim it "avoids the problem of bifurcations," but do not explain this term or provide evidence that this technique is necessary for convergence. This could be a significant point of confusion for readers. The paper would benefit from a comparison to the gradient truncation/optimization method described in the ICML 2013 paper by Pascanu et al., which addresses similar concerns.

The layer "surgery" experiments (Figure 2) are interesting but potentially misleading. The authors acknowledge that layer contributions are likely not independent, yet they interpret the results as if each layer's contribution is additive. This confounds the interpretation of which layers are truly important. A more convincing experiment would test whether higher layers genuinely benefit from upstream processing versus simply relocating units to the first layer.

The paper would be strengthened by additional experiments showing performance across varying DRNN depths (not just the 5-layer version presented), with architectures designed to match parameter counts. This would better establish the depth-performance relationship. The authors should also define DRNN-AO and DRNN-1O earlier in the text, not just in Figure 1.

The text-generation examples (Table 2) are the most persuasive evidence of the DRNN's capabilities, particularly the clear degradation when bottom layer contributions are removed. However, the claim that DRNNs "reach state-of-the-art performance" is somewhat misleading, as the paper does not compare to the best-performing models on this specific dataset (e.g., Mikolov et al.'s work on Wikipedia compression).

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes an important conceptual contribution to understanding recurrent network architectures. The technical execution is sound, and the analysis of temporal hierarchies is novel and valuable. The paper would be significantly improved with the suggested additional experiments, fairer comparisons, and more careful interpretation of the layer surgery results. The authors have made a compelling case that hierarchical RNNs can naturally capture temporal structure, and this work should inspire further research into the relationship between network depth and temporal processing. The paper is well-suited for NIPS, offering both theoretical insight and empirical validation.
