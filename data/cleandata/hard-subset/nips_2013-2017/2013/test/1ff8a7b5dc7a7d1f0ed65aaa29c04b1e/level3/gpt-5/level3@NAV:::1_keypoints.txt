- Paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) stacked to induce temporal hierarchies, trained with SGD and supervised layer-wise pre-training.
- Evaluated on character-level language modeling (Hinton/Sutskever Wikipedia corpus) reporting competitive bits-per-character versus prior RNN work.
- Analyses show higher layers capture longer time scales and phenomena like parentheses matching act as pseudo-stable attractor-like states.
- Relates to prior hierarchical RNNs, stacked LSTMs, and complements second-order training approaches.
- Methodology is sound, carefully executed, and training details (architectures, schedules, clipping) are clearly described.
- Empirical analyses (perturbation tests, context switches, layer ablations, generative probing) substantiate claims about emergent time scales and long-term dependencies.
- Evaluation is weakened by use of a single, non-standard dataset and limited hyperparameter exploration (only three model instantiations).
- Comparisons are restricted to RNN variants; hierarchical HMMs and non-RNN neural/compression baselines are missing.
- Paper is very well written and reproducible in spirit with most key hyperparameters specified.
- Originality lies in the supervised layer-wise pre-training protocol and the depth of behavioral analyses, despite stacking RNNs not being new.
- Significance: strong analytical insights into how depth induces temporal hierarchies, but empirical gains are not yet conclusive for broad impact.
- Pros: insightful analyses, transparent training procedure, and a practical supervised pre-training protocol for deep RNNs.
- Cons: single dataset, narrow baselines, heavy compute limiting number of runs and ablations (depth vs width, pre-training vs none).
- Suggestions: add results on standard corpora (text8/enwik8, PTB), include hierarchical HMM and compression-model baselines on the same data, and ablate the pre-training scheme.
- Overall recommendation: weak accept based on valuable analytical insights into deep recurrent modeling.
