- Proposes Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs): a hierarchy of recurrent layers where each layer receives the previous layer’s hidden state to capture temporal hierarchies.  
- Argues traditional RNN work has focused on training algorithms rather than architectures that explicitly model temporal hierarchies.  
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance among recurrent networks on a character-level language modeling task (Wikipedia corpus).  
- Shows analysis that different DRNN layers develop distinct emergent time scales during training.  
- Paper is well-written with a clear explanation of architecture and experiments.  
- Lacks comparison to other architectures with the same number of parameters/units, making fairness of results unclear.  
- Layer “surgery” experiment interpretations are confounded by complex interdependencies between layers and need further validation.  
- Mentions using gradient normalization to avoid bifurcations but provides insufficient detail on the method and its impact.  
- Originality: stacking RNNs is not entirely new, but the paper clearly articulates how stacking can model temporal hierarchies.  
- Significance: DRNNs have potential to improve RNN performance on tasks requiring hierarchical time-series processing.  
- Suggest providing parameter-matched baselines (e.g., wider single-layer RNNs) and plotting performance across DRNN depths trained from scratch.  
- Suggest experiments to test whether higher layers truly benefit from upstream processing versus mere redistribution of units.  
- Suggest adding a detailed description of gradient normalization and defining DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO in the text.  
- Overall assessment: worthwhile and largely convincing paper with a few important experiments and clarifications needed.
