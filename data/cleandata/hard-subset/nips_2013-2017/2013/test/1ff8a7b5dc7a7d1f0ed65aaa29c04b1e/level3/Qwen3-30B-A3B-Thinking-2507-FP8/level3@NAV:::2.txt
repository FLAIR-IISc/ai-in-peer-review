

This paper investigates deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), where each layer is a recurrent network receiving the hidden state of the previous layer as input. The authors demonstrate that DRNNs outperform standard RNNs on character-level language modeling when parameter count is held constant, with deeper layers encoding more long-range information. A key finding is that plain BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, contradicting prior beliefs that Hessian-free (HF) optimization was necessary.

The paper's strengths include a clear demonstration of hierarchical time scales through extensive experiments and a valuable analysis of layer contributions to the task. The results on the Wikipedia text corpus are compelling, showing DRNNs reaching state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks using only stochastic gradient descent, without requiring HF optimization.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses. First, it improperly claims to have introduced the DRNN architecture, when Graves (2013) and others have already used similar models. While focusing on analysis rather than architecture novelty is acceptable, the paper fails to properly acknowledge prior work. Second, the results are narrowly focused on character-level language modeling, with no evidence that these findings generalize to other tasks like speech recognition. Third, the paper unnecessarily employs HF training for comparison when truncated BPTT (as used by Mikolov) would likely suffice and be much more efficient.

The paper would be strengthened by proper attribution of prior work, replication of key findings on additional tasks, and testing with truncated BPTT to confirm that HF training is not necessary. The current results, while interesting for language modeling, appear too task-specific to claim broader significance.

While the analysis of DRNNs' hierarchical time scales is valuable, the paper's narrow scope, improper attribution, and unnecessary computational expense limit its contribution. The work represents a modest advance in understanding DRNNs but fails to meet the significance and originality standards expected at NIPS. I recommend rejection.
