Summary and relation to prior work: The paper studies stacked recurrent architectures (DRNNs) in which each layer is a recurrent module receiving the previous layer’s hidden state, with either top-only outputs (DRNN-1O) or all-layers outputs (DRNN-AO). Using simple SGD with gradient normalization, the model achieves strong character-level language modeling performance on Wikipedia and provides analyses of emergent time scales and long-term behaviors (e.g., parentheses). This connects to hierarchical RNNs (El Hihi & Bengio), stacked LSTMs for speech (Graves et al.), and MRNNs trained with HF (Sutskever et al.). The exploration of depth as temporal hierarchy echoes the intuition that older data may benefit from more processing steps, and the paper offers a readable empirical analysis that complements optimization-focused work.

Quality: The experiments are careful and the time-scale analyses are insightful. However, the training heuristic (global gradient normalization) is not adequately ablated. Please quantify how crucial normalization is, how it “avoids bifurcations,” and whether normalization alters SGD’s averaging/statistical efficiency; contrast with clipping as in Pascanu, Mikolov, Bengio (ICML 2013). The “layer surgery” interpretation in Table 2 is persuasive qualitatively, but inter-layer dependencies confound causal claims.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well organized. Minor: define DRNN-AO and DRNN-1O in the main text at first mention, not only in Fig. 1.

Originality: While stacked RNNs are not new, the framing that deeper recurrence yields a hierarchy of time scales, and the analyses provided, are useful. Consider also “deep transition” variants: have you tried inserting extra nonlinear layers within the recurrent transition (i.e., MLP between timesteps) to test whether depth-in-time versus depth-in-state matters?

Significance: Results are competitive for pure SGD training and the analyses are likely to be cited. To strengthen impact:
- Provide baselines matched for parameter count and, separately, for unit count to rule out unfair comparisons; a key missing experiment is to test whether higher layers truly gain from upstream processing versus simply relocating those units to the first layer (widened single-layer RNN with identical parameters).
- Replace/augment Fig. 2 with performance curves over DRNN depth trained from scratch, including wider layers to match parameters/units.
- Compare to Mikolov et al.’s Wikipedia compression/language modeling results to contextualize BPC and compression-based baselines.

Pros:
- Clear motivation and strong qualitative analyses; text generation study is compelling.
- Simple training achieving competitive performance.

Cons:
- Insufficient ablations of gradient normalization and depth vs width.
- Some comparisons and plots could be fairer/more informative.

Overall: Worthwhile, readable, and largely convincing; with the additional experiments and comparisons above, this would be a strong NeurIPS paper.
