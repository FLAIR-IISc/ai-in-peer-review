This paper proposes and analyzes Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), an architecture where multiple RNN layers are stacked hierarchically. The core idea is that each layer processes the hidden state sequence of the layer below, allowing the network to build a temporal hierarchy and learn features at different time scales. This work builds upon prior efforts in hierarchical RNNs but provides a more thorough analysis on a challenging, real-world task—character-level language modeling—where it achieves state-of-the-art results using simple SGD.

**Strengths:**
The paper’s primary strength is its clear motivation and analysis. The central premise—that older data benefits from more layers of non-linear processing, making deep RNNs a natural fit for hierarchical temporal tasks—is both intuitive and compelling. The analysis of the emergent time scales in different layers and the experiments on long-term dependencies (e.g., parenthesis closing) provide convincing evidence for the model's capabilities. The text generation experiment (Table 2) is particularly persuasive in demonstrating the distinct roles of different layers. Furthermore, the paper is well-written, and the results are significant, matching more complex Hessian-free methods with a simpler training approach.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
While the paper is strong, its central claims could be better supported with additional experiments and clarifications.

1.  **Experimental Comparisons:** The comparison to a standard RNN is weakened by the large difference in the number of units per layer. To fairly assess the benefit of depth over raw capacity, the authors should compare their DRNN against a baseline RNN with the same (or a similar) number of *units*, not just total parameters. A key missing experiment is to test whether higher layers genuinely benefit from upstream processing or if their function could be replicated by simply relocating their units to the first layer of a wider, shallower network. I would suggest re-plotting Figure 2 to show performance for DRNNs of various depths trained from scratch, including wider shallow baselines. A comparison to Mikolov et al.'s results on Wikipedia compression would also be valuable context.

2.  **Training Details:** The paper should elaborate on the gradient normalization. How, precisely, does it "avoid the problem of bifurcations"? The authors should also consider whether normalizing the gradient magnitude might disrupt the averaging behavior of SGD. Related work on gradient truncation, such as Pascanu et al. (ICML 2013), should be discussed.

3.  **Architectural Questions:** Was a variant with additional non-linear layers *between* timesteps (i.e., making the transition function deeper) considered? This is an alternative way to introduce depth.

**Overall:**
This is a worthwhile, readable, and largely convincing paper. It presents an important architectural idea with strong results. The weaknesses primarily relate to the need for more rigorous baseline comparisons to fully substantiate the claims about depth. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the work. I recommend acceptance.
