- Paper studies stacked tanh RNNs (DRNNs) and argues stacking yields hierarchical temporal scales, comparing outputs at top-only (1O) vs all-layers (AO).
- Training uses SGD + BPTT with gradient normalization and an incremental supervised layer-wise scheme for DRNN-1O.
- Main evaluation is character-level language modeling on the Sutskever et al. Wikipedia corpus with fixed model size (~4.9M params).
- DRNNs outperform a plain RNN at equal parameter budget and approach state-of-the-art RNN results obtained with more complex optimization.
- Thorough analyses include layer-wise output ablations, perturbation experiments showing layer-specific time scales and effective context lengths, and a parentheses-matching experiment.
- Paper connects to prior work on stacked RNN/LSTM and hierarchical recurrent models but differs by using simple tanh RNNs, incremental pretraining, and detailed temporal analyses.
- Strength: clear, well-written presentation with easy-to-follow figures and experimental design.
- Strength: solid empirical performance using only SGD, showing competitive BPC.
- Strength: informative perturbation and ablation studies convincingly demonstrating emergent hierarchies.
- Strength: practical training insights (gradient normalization, layer-wise training) are clearly described and effective.
- Weakness: evaluation limited to a single dataset/preprocessing, so cross-corpus generality is untested.
- Weakness: narrow baselines—no comparisons to stacked LSTM/GRU or non-RNN hierarchical models and n-gram baselines.
- Weakness: some training choices (output init, layer-wise schedule, truncation) require ablation to separate architectural vs training effects.
- Weakness: limited reporting of variability—models trained once per condition, no confidence intervals.
- Weakness: claims about pseudo-attractor dynamics are suggestive but lack formal quantification (e.g., Jacobian spectra).
- Quality: technically sound; experiments support claims but broader baselines and diagnostics would strengthen conclusions.
- Clarity: methods and training schedules are well-described and reproducible given compute resources.
- Originality: stacking RNNs is not new, but incremental supervised pretraining, competitive SGD results, and detailed temporal analyses are novel contributions.
- Significance: moderate—useful insights and training recipes, but limited by breadth of baselines and datasets.
- Suggested improvements: add gated-architecture comparisons, evaluate on additional corpora, run ablations (layers/AO vs 1O, truncation, clipping), report seed variance, quantify attractor dynamics, and discuss computational trade-offs.
- Arguments for acceptance: clear demonstration of competitive stacked RNNs with insightful analyses and reproducibility.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical breadth, single dataset, and unquantified claims about attractor behavior.
- Overall: well-executed, informative study that merits acceptance if comparisons and/or additional validation are added.
