

This paper investigates deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), where each layer is a recurrent network that receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input, creating a temporal hierarchy for processing time series data. The authors demonstrate DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks on character-level language modeling (Wikipedia text corpus) using only stochastic gradient descent (SGD), challenging the prior belief that Hessian-free optimization was necessary for such tasks. They also provide an insightful analysis of how different layers encode distinct time scales, with bottom layers handling short-term dependencies and top layers capturing long-term context.

The paper builds on prior work by Graves et al. (2013), who used stacked bidirectional LSTMs for speech recognition, but the authors' claim of "introducing" the DRNN architecture is inaccurate. While Graves et al. focused on achieving high performance on speech tasks, this paper provides a more thorough architectural analysis, which is valuable in itself. The paper also relates to El Hihi & Bengio (1996) and Fernández et al. (2007) who proposed hierarchical RNNs for other tasks, though the current work is the first to analyze the temporal hierarchy aspect in detail.

The main strengths are the clear demonstration that deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs with the same number of parameters on language modeling, and the insightful analysis of how different layers encode different time scales. The authors convincingly show that DRNNs can balance parentheses (a long-term dependency task) without Hessian-free training, which is a significant contribution to understanding training dynamics. Their perturbation experiments revealing layer-specific time scales (bottom layers: short-term, top layers: long-term) are particularly well-executed and provide valuable theoretical insights.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. First, the claim of novelty regarding the DRNN architecture is problematic, as Graves et al. (2013) already used a similar stacked RNN approach. The authors should acknowledge this more thoroughly and clarify how their analysis differs from prior work. Second, the experiments are limited to character-level language modeling, making it difficult to generalize the findings. The authors should replicate their analysis on additional tasks (e.g., speech recognition) to strengthen their claims about the generality of the temporal hierarchy concept. Third, they don't test truncated BPTT (as used by Mikolov et al. for RNNs), which could be a more efficient training approach than Hessian-free optimization. The paper suggests that HF was necessary for previous state-of-the-art results, but Mikolov's work shows that truncated BPTT can achieve similar performance with much lower computational cost.

The paper's training costs are also concerning, requiring approximately 10 days per network on a single GPU. This high computational burden makes the approach less practical for many applications, especially compared to alternatives like truncated BPTT. The authors could have addressed this by comparing training costs more thoroughly.

The paper's significance is somewhat limited by its narrow scope. While the analysis of time scales is interesting, the work doesn't clearly advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way beyond the language modeling results. The claim that DRNNs "more naturally capture the structure of time series" is somewhat vague without broader empirical validation.

Pro arguments for acceptance: The paper provides a thorough analysis of temporal hierarchies in RNNs, demonstrates that SGD can achieve state-of-the-art results without Hessian-free training, and offers valuable insights about how different layers process information at different time scales.

Con arguments against acceptance: The architectural novelty claim is inaccurate, the findings are limited to one task, the paper doesn't test more efficient training approaches like truncated BPTT, and the computational cost is prohibitively high for practical applications.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding how hierarchical processing works in recurrent networks, but its claim of architectural novelty is problematic. The analysis of time scales is strong, but the narrow scope limits the significance. The paper would be significantly strengthened by replicating the analysis on additional tasks and testing more efficient training approaches. As it stands, the work is acceptable for NIPS but would be stronger with revisions addressing the novelty claim and expanding the experimental scope. The authors should acknowledge Graves et al. (2013) more thoroughly and clarify how their analysis differs from prior work on stacked RNNs.
