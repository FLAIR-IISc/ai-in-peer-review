This paper introduces the Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN), an architecture that stacks multiple RNN layers, where each layer takes the hidden state of the layer below as its input. The motivation is to create an explicit processing hierarchy, allowing the network to operate on multiple time scales simultaneously. This is contrasted with standard RNNs where hierarchical processing is only implicitly related to the passage of time. The authors test this architecture on the task of character-level language modeling using a large Wikipedia corpus. They demonstrate that DRNNs, trained with a simple stochastic gradient descent (SGD) method, can achieve state-of-the-art performance, comparable to more complex models trained with second-order methods like Hessian-free optimization. The paper also provides a valuable analysis of the trained models, investigating the emergent time scales in different layers and the network's ability to capture long-range dependencies.

**Quality & Significance**

The paper's main strength is its clear motivation and strong empirical results. The idea that deep architectures can benefit sequence modeling by providing hierarchical processing—rather than just memory—is compelling. The argument that complex processing may be needed for the *current* input, not just for older inputs, provides a solid rationale for stacking RNNs. Achieving state-of-the-art results on a difficult benchmark with a relatively simple training algorithm (SGD with gradient normalization) is a significant contribution and suggests that this architecture is a powerful and practical tool.

The analysis provided is also a major strength. The experiments visualizing the different time scales of each layer (Figure 3), the investigation of parenthesis balancing (Figure 4), and the generative examples (Table 2) offer persuasive evidence that the model is learning the intended temporal hierarchy. The text generation experiment, in particular, effectively demonstrates the different roles played by the lower and upper layers.

However, the experimental design has a significant weakness that tempers the main conclusions. The primary comparison is between a deep, multi-layer DRNN and a single-layer RNN with a similar total parameter count. While this is a reasonable starting point, it doesn't fully isolate the benefit of the hierarchical structure. A key missing baseline would be a single, very wide RNN layer with the same *total number of units* as the DRNN, or a standard RNN with additional non-linear feedforward layers inserted within each timestep. Such an experiment would help determine whether the performance gain comes from the specific temporal hierarchy or simply from increased computational capacity and non-linearity between time steps. Without this control, it is difficult to definitively conclude that the "deep" structure is the critical factor.

Furthermore, the layer-wise training procedure used for the DRNN-1O model, while pragmatic, complicates comparisons with the other models that were trained end-to-end. A more direct and informative experiment would be to train DRNNs of varying depths (1, 2, 3, etc.) from scratch, possibly making the layers wider for shallower networks to maintain a constant parameter or unit count. This would provide a clearer picture of the relationship between depth and performance.

The training procedure itself raises some questions. The authors use gradient normalization, stating it helps to "avoid extremely large gradients near bifurcations." This claim is not sufficiently explained. It would be helpful to clarify what bifurcations are being referred to and to quantify how critical this normalization step is for successful training. It is also worth considering how this normalization affects the SGD optimization dynamics, as it removes the magnitude information that is central to standard gradient descent. The authors should consider citing and discussing related work on gradient clipping/truncation, such as the recent work from the University of Montreal group at ICML 2013, which provides a more formal treatment of this issue.

**Originality**

While the idea of stacking recurrent layers is not entirely new (as acknowledged by the authors), this work is one of the first to successfully apply it to a large-scale, challenging task and to provide a detailed analysis of its properties. The paper effectively demonstrates the practical value of a simple architectural idea and provides new insights into its behavior. The analysis of emergent time scales and long-term dependencies in such a deep structure is novel and valuable.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The DRNN architecture is explained well with both equations and diagrams. The experiments are described in sufficient detail to understand the setup.

A minor suggestion for improvement: the terms DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO are used in the caption of Figure 1 before being defined in the main text (Section 2.2). Defining them in the text first would improve readability.

**Summary of Arguments**

**Pro-acceptance:**
*   Presents a simple, well-motivated, and effective architecture for sequence modeling.
*   Achieves state-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark using a simpler optimization algorithm than prior work.
*   Provides strong and insightful analysis of the model's internal dynamics, demonstrating the emergence of a temporal hierarchy.
*   The paper is clear, readable, and the findings are likely to be influential for practitioners and researchers working with RNNs.

**Con-acceptance:**
*   A key control experiment is missing: comparing the DRNN to a single-layer RNN with equivalent computational capacity (e.g., same total number of units or additional intra-timestep layers) to isolate the benefit of the hierarchical structure.
*   The training procedure and comparisons are not fully controlled (e.g., layer-wise pre-training for one model but not others).
*   Important technical details regarding the gradient normalization method are not fully explained or justified.
*   The comparison could be strengthened by including results from other strong language modeling baselines, such as the work of Mikolov et al. on Wikipedia data compression.

Overall, this is a worthwhile and largely convincing paper that presents an important architectural idea with strong empirical support. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened with additional baselines to make the claims more robust, the current results and analysis are a valuable contribution to the field.
