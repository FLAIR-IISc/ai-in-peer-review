- Paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) where each layer is a recurrent net receiving previous layer hidden state, applied to character-level language modeling.
- Main empirical result: DRNNs outperform standard (shallow) RNNs at character-level language modeling when parameter count is matched.
- Deeper layers in DRNNs encode longer-range information, demonstrating hierarchical time scales across layers.
- Key finding: plain truncated BPTT (stochastic gradient descent) can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, contradicting prior claims that Hessian-free (HF) optimization was required.
- Strength: clear, extensive experiments that convincingly demonstrate hierarchical time scales and layer contributions.
- Strength: strong results on the Wikipedia corpus—state-of-the-art for recurrent networks using only SGD, without HF.
- Weakness: incorrect claim of introducing the DRNN architecture—similar models (e.g., Graves 2013) exist and are not properly acknowledged.
- Weakness: narrow empirical focus on character-level language modeling with no evidence of generalization to other tasks (e.g., speech recognition).
- Weakness: unnecessary use of HF for comparisons when truncated BPTT (as used by Mikolov) would likely suffice and be more efficient.
- Suggested fixes: properly attribute prior work, replicate results on additional tasks, and test with truncated BPTT to confirm HF is unnecessary.
- Conclusion: modest advance in understanding DRNNs but limited significance/originality due to narrow scope, attribution issues, and extra computational expense—recommend rejection.
