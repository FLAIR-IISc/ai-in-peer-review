- Introduces Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), a hierarchical architecture of stacked recurrent layers using previous-layer hidden states as input.  
- Empirically shows deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs when parameter count is held constant on character-level language modeling.  
- Demonstrates plain SGD/BPTT can train RNNs to match prior Hessian-free results and learn long-range dependencies (e.g., balanced parentheses).  
- Analyzes layer-wise time scales, showing different layers operate at different temporal resolutions.  
- Incorrectly claims to have "introduced" deep RNNs despite prior work (Graves 2013; El Hihi & Bengio 1995).  
- The "state-of-the-art" claim is misleading—results match HF-trained networks with SGD but could be matched by larger standard RNNs.  
- Experimental scope is narrow, limited to character-level language modeling, reducing generality of conclusions.  
- Authors should test truncated BPTT (~30 steps) to evaluate computationally cheaper alternatives, per Mikolov et al.  
- Recommendation: accept after revision to correct novelty claims and reframe contribution as analysis rather than a new architecture.  
- Quality: experiments are technically sound and well-designed, supporting main claims.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written with effective figures explaining architecture and results.  
- Originality: novel analysis of time scales and long-range learning, but not a new architecture.  
- Significance: important for understanding depth in RNNs and showing SGD can suffice, but limited by task scope.  
- Pro: deeper RNNs outperform baselines with same parameters; strong time-scale analysis; plain BPTT handles long-range dependencies; clear presentation.  
- Con: false novelty claim; limited to character-level tasks; misleading SOTA wording; HF baselines may be unnecessarily expensive.  
- Additional suggestions: acknowledge prior work, test truncated BPTT, replicate on other tasks (e.g., speech), and clarify SOTA claim pertains to SGD vs HF.
