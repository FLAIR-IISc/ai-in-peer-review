This paper explores the effectiveness of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) in character-level language modeling tasks. The authors propose a hierarchical architecture, where each layer is a recurrent network that receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input. This architecture is shown to outperform standard recurrent neural networks (RNNs) when the number of parameters is held constant.

The paper's main contribution is the demonstration that deeper RNNs can capture longer-range information and improve performance on character-level language modeling tasks. The authors also provide an analysis of the emergent time scales in the different layers of the DRNNs. The results show that the DRNNs can learn to model long-term dependencies, such as parentheses, and that the top layer of the DRNNs captures text statistics longer than word-length sequences.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed architecture and the experiments. The results are presented in a clear and concise manner, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the performance of the DRNNs.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to demonstrate the effectiveness of DRNNs in a real-world task. The authors use a large-scale dataset, the Wikipedia text corpus, and show that the DRNNs can achieve state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks. The paper also provides a detailed analysis of the time scales of the different layers of the DRNNs, which is an important contribution to the understanding of how these networks work.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the main criticisms is that the authors do not properly acknowledge prior work on similar architectures. For example, Graves (2013) proposed a similar architecture, and the authors should have referenced this work and explained how their approach differs.

Another limitation is that the paper focuses on a single task, character-level language modeling, and it is not clear how well the DRNNs will perform on other tasks. The authors should have provided more experiments on different tasks to demonstrate the generalizability of the approach.

In terms of originality, the paper does not propose a completely new architecture, but rather a variation of existing architectures. However, the authors do provide a thorough analysis of the emergent time scales in the different layers of the DRNNs, which is an original contribution.

In terms of significance, the paper is important because it demonstrates the effectiveness of DRNNs in a real-world task. The results show that DRNNs can achieve state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks, and the analysis of the time scales provides important insights into how these networks work.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, but with some revisions to address the limitations mentioned above. The authors should provide more experiments on different tasks, and they should properly acknowledge prior work on similar architectures.
