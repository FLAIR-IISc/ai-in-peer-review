This paper explores the use of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) for time series processing, specifically focusing on character-level language modeling. The core idea – that stacking RNNs allows for hierarchical processing and capturing different timescales – is a logical extension of the success of deep learning in other domains and a natural consideration given the inherent temporal structure of sequential data. The authors compare DRNNs to a standard RNN and show competitive performance on a Wikipedia corpus, achieving state-of-the-art results for recurrent networks with a similar parameter count using only stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The analysis of emergent timescales within the layers of the DRNN is also a valuable contribution.

The work builds on previous research in hierarchical RNNs [3, 5, 6] and stacked LSTMs [8], but goes beyond simply demonstrating performance gains by attempting to analyze *how* the architecture influences processing. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results. It is largely convincing in its claims and offers several insightful observations, particularly regarding the differing timescales learned by different layers.

However, several areas could be strengthened with additional experiments and analysis. First, the authors mention adding extra nonlinear layers between timesteps as a potential avenue for improvement. It would be beneficial to know if this was explored and, if not, why.

A crucial question raised is the importance of the gradient normalizations and how they “avoid the problem of bifurcations.” The explanation provided is somewhat vague. A more detailed explanation of the observed bifurcations and how gradient normalization specifically addresses them would be valuable. Relatedly, the authors should consider the work of Sutskever et al. (ICML 2013, University of Montreal) on gradient truncation and optimization, which addresses similar instability issues in RNN training. This paper provides a relevant context and potentially alternative approaches worth discussing. A concern exists that normalizing the gradient might disrupt the averaging behavior inherent in SGD, and addressing this would further strengthen the justification for this technique.

The comparison to Mikolov et al. on Wikipedia compression tasks (as alluded to in the conclusion referencing Hutter) would be a useful addition. While the authors focus on language modeling, demonstrating performance on a compression task provides a different perspective on the model’s ability to capture the underlying structure of the data.

The text-generation experiment (Table 2) is the most persuasive aspect of the paper, vividly illustrating the network's capabilities. However, the authors correctly acknowledge that interpreting layer “surgery” results is confounded by complex interdependencies. It's difficult to isolate the contribution of a single layer in such a highly interconnected system.

A key missing experiment is a test to determine whether higher layers genuinely benefit from upstream processing versus simply relocating units to the first layer. If the advantage of depth stems solely from increased capacity, then a wider first layer with the same number of parameters might achieve similar performance. This would help clarify whether the hierarchical structure is truly exploited or merely a way to distribute parameters.

Furthermore, the parameter count comparison is potentially unfair. The authors state they chose network sizes to have approximately 4.9 million trainable parameters, but the RNN has significantly fewer. A fairer comparison would involve baselines with the same number of parameters or units. Plotting performance as a function of DRNN depth, trained *from scratch* (including wider layers to match parameters/units), instead of the current Figure 2, would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the benefits of depth. 

Minor points: The definitions of DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO should be explicitly stated in the main text, not just in Figure 1.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of deep learning principles to recurrent networks.
*   State-of-the-art performance on a challenging task.
*   Insightful analysis of emergent timescales.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of exploration of alternative techniques (e.g., additional nonlinear layers).
*   Insufficient explanation of gradient normalization.
*   Missing comparison to relevant work (e.g., Sutskever et al.).
*   Potentially unfair parameter count comparison.
*   Key missing experiment to validate the benefits of hierarchical processing.



Overall, this is a worthwhile paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The ideas are sound, the results are promising, and the writing is clear. However, addressing the suggested experiments and comparisons would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points above.
