

This paper introduces Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), a novel architecture where each layer is a recurrent network receiving the hidden state of the previous layer as input. The authors propose a hierarchical processing approach that explicitly models temporal hierarchies, contrasting with standard RNNs that implicitly embed time scales in network dynamics. The work builds on previous hierarchical RNN proposals [3,5,8] but offers a more systematic analysis of how temporal hierarchies emerge during training.

The paper demonstrates that DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks on character-level language modeling (Wikipedia dataset) using only SGD, eliminating the need for complex optimization techniques like Hessian-free methods [19]. The key innovation lies in the detailed analysis of emergent time scales across layers, showing higher layers capture long-term dependencies and that parentheses matching manifests as a pseudo-stable attractor state. The layer-wise pre-training procedure using next-letter prediction is a valuable contribution to training deep recurrent architectures.

Strengths include exceptional clarity in exposition, well-designed experiments, and insightful visualizations of network behavior. The paper is technically sound with comprehensive analysis, and the writing is exemplary for a machine learning paper.

However, the evaluation has significant limitations. The single-dataset focus (Wikipedia) restricts generalizability, and comparisons are limited to RNN-related models, excluding hierarchical HMMs which would be a natural comparison point. The performance gains over prior work are marginal (1.47 BPC vs. 1.46 for Hessian-free RNNs [19]), and the paper's claim of "state-of-the-art" performance is somewhat undermined by the lack of direct comparison with hierarchical HMMs on the same dataset.

While the analysis of emergent time scales is compelling and represents a strong theoretical contribution, the paper's main claim of superior performance is not conclusively demonstrated. The contribution is stronger in understanding model behavior than in establishing definitive performance improvements.

Arguments for acceptance: High-quality writing, novel analysis of temporal hierarchies, technically sound, valuable pre-training procedure. Arguments against: Limited evaluation scope, marginal performance gains, comparison limitations. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to understanding how deep architectures process temporal data, but the experimental validation is insufficient to fully support the claims.
