Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies stacked recurrent architectures (“deep recurrent neural networks,” DRNNs) in which each layer is itself a recurrent network taking the previous layer’s hidden state as input. Two output variants are considered: DRNN-1O (only the top layer connects to the softmax) and DRNN-AO (all layers contribute to output). On character-level language modeling for Wikipedia, the DRNNs trained with simple SGD achieve state-of-the-art performance among RNNs with comparable parameter budgets, rivaling multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization. The paper analyzes emergent time scales across layers using perturbation (typo) experiments, context-switch tests, and a focused study on closing parentheses.

The work relates to earlier hierarchical/stacked recurrent architectures (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio; Graves et al. on stacked LSTMs for TIMIT) and to multi-timescale ideas (subsampling-based hierarchies). It also connects to optimization work on training RNNs (Martens; Sutskever; Hessian-free) and compression/language modeling baselines (PAQ; MRNN). The analysis of learned time scales and long-range effects complements prior results on long-term memory in LSTMs and HF-trained RNNs.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architecture with strong empirical results using plain SGD, demonstrating that depth in recurrent stacks improves character-level modeling.
- Thoughtful analyses probing emergent temporal hierarchies, including typo perturbations, context-switch BPC degradation, and the parentheses case study. The text-generation examples are particularly persuasive.
- Useful training insights (layer-wise supervised pretraining for DRNN-1O; gradient normalization) and a concrete comparison between DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO.
- Relevance to a core NIPS topic (sequence modeling, deep architectures, optimization) and to widely used benchmarks (Wikipedia char modeling).

Weaknesses and questions
- Optimization details and claims: The paper uses gradient normalization (always stepping in the unit-norm gradient direction with decaying learning rate) to “avoid bifurcations.” This differs from standard gradient clipping and may affect SGD’s averaging properties. Please:
  - Quantify the importance of normalization: report training stability, convergence speed, and final BPC with/without normalization and with standard clipping (e.g., clip at c) as advocated in “On the difficulty of training RNNs” (Pascanu, Mikolov, Bengio, ICML 2013, U. Montreal). This paper should be cited and discussed.
  - Clarify what “avoiding bifurcations” means operationally; provide diagnostics (e.g., gradient norms, hidden-state statistics) and show that normalization, rather than other choices, is responsible.
- Depth vs width and the role of upstream processing: A key missing control is whether improvements arise from hierarchical processing or simply from allocating more units somewhere.
  - Provide baselines that keep the total parameter budget fixed while varying depth (1, 2, 3, 5 layers) and width, training all variants from scratch with the same optimizer and schedule. Include a “wider shallow RNN” that matches the deep model’s parameter count and a strong “deep-transition” RNN (adding extra nonlinear layers within a single time step) to test the hypothesis that extra short-term processing helps (as your introduction motivates). Did you try such deep-transition/MLP-between-recurrence variants?
  - Test whether higher layers genuinely benefit from upstream processing by moving the same number of units to the first layer (keeping parameters matched) and measuring BPC. This addresses whether the stack does more than relocate capacity.
- Comparisons and fairness:
  - Internal comparisons appear roughly parameter-matched (~4.9M), but DRNN-AO adds output heads per layer. Please tabulate exact parameter counts for each model (including all output matrices) and confirm matching within small tolerance. Also report results for models matched by units where informative.
  - External comparisons: In addition to PAQ and MRNN (Sutskever et al.), please compare or at least discuss results versus Mikolov et al.’s RNN-based compression/language modeling on Wikipedia/enwik8 where feasible, clarifying dataset and evaluation differences (e.g., your 96-character alphabet vs 86).
- Interpretability of layer-ablation “surgery”: Zeroing one layer’s output is confounded by interdependencies (cancellation, synergy). The current results are useful but should be framed cautiously. Consider stronger analyses:
  - Retrain models without the removed output head to assess the counterfactual fairly.
  - Use linear probes on individual layers, or measure contribution via leave-one-layer-out with shallow retraining of only the output layer.
  - Complement with representational analyses (e.g., CCA/SVCCA across layers; specificity to n-gram length).
- Training protocol nuances:
  - DRNN-1O requires incremental layer-wise supervised pretraining. Please report performance when trained end-to-end from scratch to quantify the necessity and benefit of the curriculum.
  - Provide variance across random initializations (you state differences are small) and training-time/computation comparisons to HF-trained MRNNs for a clearer cost-performance picture.

Quality
- Technically sound overall, with careful experiments and insightful analyses. The main concerns are about optimization ablations (gradient normalization vs clipping), depth/width controls, and stronger baselines to isolate architectural contributions.
- Claims about emergent time scales are supported; the parentheses study is convincing and a highlight.

Clarity
- The paper is well written and organized. Minor issues:
  - Define DRNN-AO and DRNN-1O explicitly in the main text when first introduced (not just in the figure caption).
  - Make evaluation comparability explicit (alphabet size, test split) when citing external BPCs.
  - Provide a concise parameter-count table.

Originality
- Stacking recurrent layers is not entirely new, but this work provides a clean formulation, a thorough empirical study on a challenging real-world task with SGD, and careful time-scale analyses. The specific training insights and diagnostics add novelty.

Significance
- Character-level language modeling remains a touchstone for sequence modeling. Demonstrating that simple SGD-trained DRNNs reach competitive performance and exhibit clear temporal hierarchies is valuable. With the requested controls and comparisons, the contribution would be impactful and informative for practitioners.

Recommended additional experiments/analyses
- Ablate gradient normalization vs clipping; cite and discuss Pascanu et al. (ICML 2013).
- Depth/width sweep under fixed parameter budgets; include deep-transition RNNs and wider shallow baselines trained from scratch.
- More rigorous ablation of layer outputs (retrain output layer; linear probes).
- Report variance over seeds and exact parameter counts; clarify external comparison setups.
- If possible, compare to Mikolov et al.’s Wikipedia/compression results or discuss gaps.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical results with a simple architecture and optimizer.
- Convincing analyses of emergent time scales and long-term dependencies.
- Relevance and clarity; likely to influence how practitioners think about depth in RNNs.

Cons against acceptance
- Missing critical baselines (depth vs width; deep-transition) that isolate the architectural advantage.
- Optimization choices (gradient normalization) insufficiently justified/ablate; could confound conclusions.
- Some interpretability claims rely on potentially confounded ablations.

Overall: A worthwhile and largely convincing paper. With the additional experiments and clarifications above, it would make a solid contribution to NIPS.
