This paper introduces the Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN), an architecture that stacks multiple RNN layers, where each layer receives the hidden state sequence from the layer below as its input. The motivation is to create a model capable of hierarchical temporal processing, combining the memory of RNNs with the hierarchical feature extraction of deep networks. The authors argue that this structure is more natural for tasks with multi-scale temporal dependencies, such as language. They test this architecture on character-level language modeling on a Wikipedia corpus, demonstrating that a 5-layer DRNN trained with simple Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) achieves performance competitive with state-of-the-art models that use more complex architectures (MRNN) and second-order optimization methods (Hessian-free). The paper also provides an analysis of the learned representations, suggesting that different layers operate at different time scales and that the model can capture very long-range dependencies.

The primary strength of the paper lies in its well-motivated and intuitive core idea. The argument that older data has passed through more recurrent iterations and thus benefits from more layers of processing is compelling. Similarly, the notion that deep RNNs can provide a more structured way to improve short-term processing (by adding depth within a timestep) than simply making a single RNN layer wider is reasonable. The paper is clearly written, readable, and the analysis provides persuasive evidence for the model's capabilities. The text generation experiment (Table 2) is particularly convincing; the qualitative difference between the full model and the models with "ablated" layers provides strong support for the idea of a functional hierarchy, with lower layers handling basic statistics and higher layers capturing longer-term structure.

Despite these strengths, the experimental validation of the key claims could be significantly strengthened. The main weakness is the comparison between the DRNN and the baseline RNN. While the models are matched for the total number of parameters, this comparison is potentially unfair. A deep, narrow model has a different computational structure than a shallow, wide one. A key missing experiment is to test whether the higher layers are genuinely gaining from the hierarchical processing of upstream layers, or if the performance gain is simply an artifact of a better parameter allocation. To disentangle this, the authors should compare their DRNN to a single-layer RNN with the same number of hidden units per layer (a much smaller model) and, more importantly, a wider single-layer RNN that has a similar or greater number of parameters. This would establish whether depth itself, rather than just a different parameterization, is the source of the improvement.

Following this, Figure 2, which analyzes layer contributions, is interesting but potentially misleading. The layer-wise training for DRNN-1O and the complex interdependencies in DRNN-AO make interpreting these "surgery" experiments difficult. A more informative experiment would be to replace Figure 2 with a plot showing test performance as a function of DRNN depth (e.g., for 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 layers), with each model trained from scratch. This plot should include wider single-layer baselines with matched parameter counts to provide a clear picture of the trade-off between depth and width.

The description of the training method also raises questions. The gradient normalization heuristic in Equation 3 is critical to the results, but its justification is brief. The authors state it is used "to avoid extremely large gradients near bifurcations." It would be helpful to expand on this connection and explain why this specific normalization (scaling by the L2 norm) is an appropriate solution. Furthermore, the paper omits a crucial piece of related work: Pascanu et al.'s ICML 2013 paper, "On the difficulty of training Recurrent Neural Networks," which provides a detailed analysis of the exploding/vanishing gradient problem and formally proposes gradient norm clipping as a solution. This work should be cited and discussed. There is also a theoretical concern: normalizing the gradient at every step alters the update direction. Could this disrupt the beneficial averaging behavior that SGD relies on, especially with a linearly decaying learning rate?

A few other points for consideration:
- The architecture passes the hidden state `a_i(t-1)` and the lower-layer state `a_{i-1}(t)` through a single `tanh` layer. Was any consideration given to adding extra nonlinear layers *between* timesteps, i.e., `a_i(t) = f(g(W*a_i(t-1)) + h(Z*a_{i-1}(t)))`, where f, g, h are nonlinearities? This might allow for more complex interactions.
- For a more direct comparison to the state of the art in language modeling at the time, results should be compared to Mikolov et al.'s work on the same Wikipedia compression tasks.
- The text generation samples in Table 2 are the most persuasive part of the analysis. However, the interpretation that the top layer captures "text statistics longer than word-length sequences" is confounded by the fact that it is the only layer receiving processed input from all other layers. Its role is inherently different.
- Minor clarity issue: The terms DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO are defined in the caption for Figure 1 but should also be explicitly defined in the main text for readability.

In summary, this is a worthwhile and largely convincing paper that introduces an important and influential architecture. The idea is sound, the results are strong, and the writing is clear. However, the central claim about the superiority of a deep architecture is not fully substantiated by the current set of experiments. The paper would be much stronger with more rigorously controlled baselines (especially a wider single-layer RNN) and a more thorough discussion of the gradient normalization technique in the context of prior work.
