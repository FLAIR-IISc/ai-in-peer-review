This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) formed by stacking recurrent layers whose hidden states feed upward in the hierarchy, with either a single output head at the top (DRNN-1O) or output heads at all layers (DRNN-AO). The central claim is that stacking induces emergent temporal hierarchies: lower layers capture short-range regularities while higher layers build longer-range context. On a large Wikipedia character-level language modeling task, DRNNs trained with simple SGD achieve bits-per-character (BPC) competitive with state-of-the-art RNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization. The paper further analyzes learned time scales via perturbation experiments and illustrates long-term structure with a parentheses-closing analysis and text generation.

Relation to prior work: The idea of hierarchical/stacked RNNs dates to El Hihi & Bengio (NIPS 1996) and more recent stacked LSTM architectures (e.g., Graves et al., ICASSP 2013) and MRNNs (Sutskever et al., ICML 2011). The novel contribution here is a systematic empirical study of stacked vanilla RNNs trained with SGD, with careful analysis of layer-wise time scales and qualitative generation. The results complement Hessian-free work (Martens; Martens & Sutskever) and the Montreal group’s analysis of exploding/vanishing gradients and gradient clipping (Pascanu, Mikolov, Bengio, ICML 2013).

Quality:
- Architecture and training are clearly specified, including update equations, layer-wise output variants, BPTT truncation length, initialization, and a simple gradient normalization heuristic.
- Empirical results are credible and include baselines (single-layer RNN), parameter matching (~4.9M params), and comparisons to MRNN and PAQ. The text-generation and parentheses analyses convincingly demonstrate learned structure beyond very short contexts.
- Some methodological choices need deeper justification or ablation: the gradient normalization scheme; the dependence on incremental layer-wise training for DRNN-1O; and the extent to which gains stem from depth vs. more units or effective transition depth.

Clarity:
- The paper is readable and well organized, and figures support the claims. Minor: explicitly define DRNN-AO and DRNN-1O in the main text at first mention (not only in Fig. 1); clarify “bifurcations” terminology in the training section; and report the BPTT truncation window more prominently.
- Consider adding a plot of performance as a function of depth when trained from scratch, alongside the current layer-contribution analyses.

Originality:
- Stacking RNNs is not new; the novelty lies in showing that even simple stacked tanh RNNs trained with SGD can match strong baselines on large-scale character modeling, and in the insightful diagnostics of emergent time scales and long-range interactions. The training schedule for DRNN-1O (incremental layer addition with temporary output head) is a practical twist meriting discussion.

Significance:
- The results suggest that architectural depth, even without gating or second-order methods, yields tangible gains and interpretable time-scale separation. This is relevant to the community’s understanding of when simple RNNs suffice versus when LSTMs/advanced optimization are necessary. The analysis techniques are likely to be adopted by others.

Strengths:
- Clear, simple architecture with strong performance using SGD.
- Thorough empirical analyses of time scales (perturbation, context switching), and qualitative generation demonstrating nontrivial structure (parentheses).
- Useful insight that output connections at intermediate layers aid optimization and interpretability.
- Fair attempt at parameter matching to prior work; hardware/training budget reported.

Weaknesses and requested clarifications:
- Gradient normalization: Please clarify how the unit-norm gradient update “avoids bifurcations,” and whether it acts effectively as global gradient clipping. Could such normalization disrupt SGD’s stochastic averaging properties and bias learning dynamics? A comparison to standard gradient clipping (Pascanu et al., ICML 2013) and to unnormalized SGD would be informative.
- Depth vs. width: It remains unclear whether higher layers genuinely benefit from upstream processing, or whether similar gains arise by reallocating those units into a single wider layer or deeper per-timestep transitions. A key missing experiment is a strong single-layer baseline with the same number of units as the total across layers (or matched parameter count), and a “deep-transition” RNN (extra nonlinear layers between timesteps) with comparable parameters.
- Training schedule confound: DRNN-1O required incremental layer-wise training; DRNN-AO did not. Please assess whether depth benefits persist when all depths are trained from scratch with identical schedules and hyperparameters. A plot of BPC vs. depth (1–5 layers), with widths chosen to match parameter count, would help.
- Fairness of comparisons: Although total parameters are matched, the number of units and fan-in/fan-out distributions differ. Include baselines that match both parameter count and unit count where possible, to disentangle capacity from architectural inductive bias.
- Comparisons: Consider including results or discussion vis-à-vis Mikolov et al. on Wikipedia compression/language modeling, and contemporary stacked LSTM baselines. Also report sensitivity to BPTT truncation length, as it can strongly affect long-range modeling.
- Layer “surgery” caveat: The removal of layer outputs is useful but difficult to interpret due to interdependencies (additive interactions, compensatory biases). Please present these as suggestive rather than definitive, and consider complementary diagnostics (e.g., causal interventions via re-training without a given layer’s head, or representational probes).

Additional suggestions:
- Explore adding extra nonlinear transformations within each time step (deep transition RNNs or residual/skip transitions) to test whether stacking across time is the main driver versus richer per-step processing.
- Report variability across random initializations (even a small N) to assess robustness given the long training times.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong empirical results with a simple, transparent model and optimizer.
- Convincing analysis of emergent time scales and long-term dependencies.
- Addresses a timely question about depth in recurrent architectures, with practical training insights.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited ablations leave open key questions about the source of gains (depth vs. width vs. training schedule).
- Missing comparisons to strong gated RNN baselines and gradient clipping methods.
- Some evaluation choices (layer-output ablations) are hard to interpret causally.

Overall: A worthwhile and largely convincing paper that advances understanding of deep recurrent architectures. With a few additional baselines and clarifications, it would make a strong NIPS contribution.
