This paper proposes and analyzes Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), an architecture constructed by stacking multiple RNN layers. The core idea is that each layer in the stack receives the hidden state sequence from the layer below it as input. This structure is motivated by the hypothesis that many time series possess a temporal hierarchy, which DRNNs can explicitly model. Unlike standard "shallow" RNNs where the input at each timestep passes through only one non-linear transformation before influencing the state and output, DRNNs allow for a multi-layer, hierarchical processing of the input at each step. The authors evaluate this architecture on the task of character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus. They demonstrate that a 5-layer DRNN, trained with simple stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and a gradient normalization heuristic, achieves state-of-the-art results, comparable to those obtained by more complex second-order training methods like Hessian-free optimization on a similar model. The paper also provides an analysis of the trained models, suggesting that different layers learn to operate on different time scales and that the model can capture very long-term dependencies, such as closing parentheses correctly over hundreds of characters. This work connects to prior uses of stacked RNNs (e.g., Graves et al., 2013) but provides a more focused analysis of the architectural benefits themselves, rather than just using it as a tool to achieve a high score on a benchmark.

### Strengths

1.  **Conceptual Clarity and Significance:** The paper presents a simple, intuitive, and powerful architectural idea. The motivation that temporal data often has a hierarchical structure and that a deep architecture is a natural way to model this is very compelling. The argument that older data in a standard RNN does not necessarily require more processing (more passes through the recurrence) than new data is insightful and provides a strong rationale for exploring depth in the per-timestep computation. This work helpfully shifts some of the focus in the field from complex training algorithms back to fundamental architectural design.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates impressive performance on a challenging, large-scale character-level language modeling task. Achieving state-of-the-art results (for RNNs at the time) using a relatively simple SGD-based training method is a significant contribution, suggesting that architectural improvements can be as, or more, impactful than algorithmic ones.

3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The qualitative analysis of the trained models is a major strength. The text generation examples in Table 2, particularly the "layer surgery" experiments where outputs from specific layers are ablated, provide persuasive (though not definitive) evidence for the different roles of lower and higher layers. The experiment analyzing the model's ability to handle long-range parenthesis dependencies is also very effective at demonstrating the long-memory capabilities of the architecture.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Need for More Controlled Baselines:** The central claim is that the *hierarchical* (deep) structure is superior to a *flat* (shallow) one. While the authors compare their DRNN to a single-layer RNN with a similar number of parameters, this comparison could be made more rigorous. A key missing experiment is a more direct test of depth versus width. The authors should consider training single-layer RNNs that are made progressively wider to match the parameter count of 2-layer, 3-layer, etc., DRNNs. This would more directly address the question of whether the performance gain comes from the hierarchical organization or simply from a different parameterization of a large model.

2.  **Weakness of the Layer Ablation Analysis:** The analysis in Figure 2, which measures the performance drop from removing a single layer's output contribution from the fully trained DRNN-AO model, is interesting but potentially misleading. The layers are not trained to be independent modules; they develop complex interdependencies. Removing one layer's contribution post-training does not necessarily reveal its intrinsic importance during training or inference, but rather how much the final system relies on it. A more systematic and convincing study would be to plot the final test performance as a function of network depth (e.g., for 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5 layers), where each network is trained from scratch. This would provide a clearer picture of the benefits of adding each new layer.

3.  **Insufficient Detail on Training Heuristics:** The gradient normalization trick (Eq. 3) is critical to the paper's success with SGD, yet it is not thoroughly explained or justified.
    *   The claim that it helps "avoid the problem of bifurcations" is vague and should be substantiated. How critical is this step to achieving the reported performance?
    *   Normalizing the L2 norm of the gradient for each minibatch discards information about the relative magnitude of gradients across different batches. This could potentially disrupt the averaging behavior of SGD. A discussion of this potential side effect would be welcome.
    *   The authors should connect this heuristic to more principled approaches for handling exploding gradients in RNNs, such as the gradient clipping/truncation methods analyzed in the contemporary work of Pascanu et al. (ICML 2013), and discuss the relationship.

4.  **Additional Comparisons:** While the comparison to Sutskever et al. (2011) is appropriate as it uses the same dataset, the paper would benefit from contextualizing its results with respect to other major language modeling works of the era, such as those by Mikolov et al., to give the reader a broader sense of the state of the art.

### Detailed Questions

*   The paper explores depth by stacking recurrent layers. An alternative way to introduce depth is to add more non-linear layers to the transition function *within* a single time step (i.e., making the computation from `a(t-1)` and `s(t)` to `a(t)` deeper). Was this architectural variation considered or tested?
*   While the acronyms DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO are defined, their meaning could be made more explicit in the text when they are introduced to improve readability (e.g., "DRNN with All-layer Outputs" and "DRNN with Top-layer-Only Output").

### Summary

Overall, this is a well-written, convincing, and worthwhile paper. The core idea is elegant, the results are strong, and the analysis provides valuable insights. Its primary weakness lies in the lack of a fully comprehensive set of baseline experiments to definitively prove that the hierarchical structure, and not just a different parameterization, is the source of the performance gains. Despite this, the paper makes a strong case for the effectiveness of deep recurrent architectures and is a solid contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors incorporate a more controlled study of the depth-vs-width trade-off to strengthen their central claim.
