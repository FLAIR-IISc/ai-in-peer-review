This paper studies the properties and effectiveness of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), where multiple RNN layers are stacked hierarchically. The main contribution is an empirical analysis on a large-scale, character-level language modeling task. The authors demonstrate that a 5-layer DRNN outperforms a standard shallow RNN with the same number of parameters. The paper also presents two interesting findings: first, that deeper layers in the DRNN learn to operate on longer timescales, creating an emergent temporal hierarchy; and second, that plain backpropagation through time (BPTT) is sufficient to train RNNs to model long-range dependencies like balancing parentheses, a task previously thought to require more complex methods like Hessian-Free (HF) optimization.

The primary strength of this work is its detailed analysis. While the DRNN architecture itself is not novel—similar models were used by Graves et al. (2013) for speech recognition—the paper provides a valuable investigation into *why* such models are effective. The finding that standard BPTT can handle parenthesis balancing is a significant contribution that questions the necessity of more complex optimizers for certain tasks. The results are strong, achieving performance comparable to the state-of-the-art while using a much simpler training algorithm.

However, the paper has two main weaknesses. First, the authors should more clearly position their contribution as an analysis of an existing architecture, rather than implying they are introducing it. The originality lies in the experimental insights, not the model. Second, and more critically, the claims about the benefits of depth are based entirely on a single task. I mostly agree with the "one task" criticism raised during the author response period; the paper's conclusions would be far more compelling if the analysis were replicated on a different sequential modality, such as speech, to demonstrate that the findings are not specific to character-level language modeling.

Furthermore, the comparison against the computationally intensive HF method is interesting, but it lacks a more practical baseline. I would strongly suggest the authors test truncated BPTT with a shorter context (e.g., ~30 steps). While this approach might fail to capture the parenthesis dependencies, it could potentially match the overall entropy scores at a substantially lower computational cost, providing a more complete picture of the trade-offs involved.
