This paper investigates the properties of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), an architecture where multiple RNN layers are stacked hierarchically. The central hypothesis is that this deep structure allows the network to learn a temporal hierarchy, with different layers processing information at different time scales, analogous to how deep feed-forward networks learn a hierarchy of spatial features. The authors apply this architecture to the task of character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus and compare its performance to a standard, single-layer RNN with a similar number of parameters.

The main contributions are an empirical demonstration that DRNNs outperform a standard RNN on this task and an in-depth analysis of the trained models. The analysis provides evidence that higher layers in the network learn longer time-scale dependencies. A particularly notable finding is that these models can be trained to state-of-the-art performance using simple stochastic gradient descent with gradient clipping, successfully learning long-range dependencies (e.g., balancing parentheses over hundreds of characters) that were previously thought to require more sophisticated optimization techniques like Hessian-Free (HF) optimization.

**Quality**
The paper is of high quality. The experimental setup is sound, particularly the decision to keep the total number of trainable parameters constant when comparing the shallow RNN with the deep variants. This ensures a fair comparison of architectural depth. The analysis presented is thorough and provides compelling evidence for the claims. The experiments analyzing the emergent time scales in different layers (Figure 3) and the model's ability to handle long-term dependencies like parentheses (Figure 4) are insightful and go beyond simply reporting a lower bits-per-character (BPC) score. The work is a complete piece of research, not just a position paper.

**Clarity**
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is well-explained in the introduction, the architecture is described precisely, and the experimental results are presented effectively through tables and figures. The authors provide sufficient detail about their training setup for an expert to understand the methodology.

**Originality**
The primary weakness of the paper lies in its claims of originality regarding the architecture. The paper presents the DRNN as a novel contribution, but similar deep or stacked (L)STM architectures have been used in prior work. For example, Graves et al. ("Speech recognition with deep recurrent neural networks," ICASSP 2013) successfully employed a very similar deep bidirectional LSTM model for speech recognition. The authors cite this work but downplay its architectural contribution, stating it is "strongly focused on reaching good performance" with "little analysis". While this is true, it does not change the fact that the architecture itself is not new. The main novelty of this paper lies in its detailed *analysis* of the DRNN's properties in the context of language modeling and the demonstration of its effectiveness with a simple training algorithm. This is a valuable contribution in its own right, and the paper would be stronger if it were framed as such.

**Significance**
The paper's findings are significant for several reasons.
1.  It provides strong evidence that depth is a beneficial architectural principle for RNNs, just as it is for feed-forward networks.
2.  The analysis of emergent temporal hierarchies offers valuable insight into the inner workings of these models and provides a clear motivation for using deeper architectures on tasks with complex temporal structures.
3.  The demonstration that SGD with gradient clipping can achieve results competitive with Hessian-Free optimization on a task requiring long-term memory is an important contribution. It suggests that simpler, more scalable training methods may be more viable for very large RNNs than previously thought.

However, the significance of the claims is somewhat limited by the fact that they are supported by experiments on only a single task. While character-level language modeling is a challenging benchmark, the claim that DRNNs are generally superior for hierarchical temporal processing would be much more convincing if validated on other domains, such as speech recognition or word-level language modeling.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a clear and convincing demonstration that deeper RNNs outperform shallow ones when controlling for the number of parameters.
*   The analysis of the learned temporal hierarchy in different layers is novel and insightful, providing a better understanding of why these models work.
*   Challenges the prevailing view that complex second-order methods are necessary for training RNNs on tasks with long-range dependencies, showing that SGD can be surprisingly effective.
*   The results on the Wikipedia benchmark are state-of-the-art for this type of model and training method.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper overstates the novelty of the DRNN architecture, which has been explored in prior work. Related work should be discussed more accurately.
*   The claims about the general benefits of DRNNs are based on a single task. The lack of experiments on other sequential data types (e.g., speech) weakens the generality of the conclusions.
*   The computational cost of the training method (full BPTT over 250 steps) is very high. The paper would be strengthened by a comparison to more efficient, commonly used techniques like truncated BPTT. It is possible that a much cheaper training setup could achieve similar BPC scores, even if it sacrifices the ability to model very long-range dependencies like parentheses.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
I would encourage the authors to tone down the claims of architectural novelty and instead emphasize their contribution in analyzing this architecture and demonstrating its effectiveness with simple training methods. To strengthen the paper's claims, I would suggest two avenues for future experiments:
1.  Replicate the analysis on at least one other sequential task (e.g., speech) to demonstrate the generality of the findings.
2.  Include a baseline trained with truncated BPTT (e.g., with a window of 30-50 steps). This would provide a valuable data point on the trade-off between computational cost and the ability to capture extremely long-range dependencies.

Overall, despite the issues with originality and the scope of evaluation, the paper presents a significant and insightful piece of work that will be of interest to the NIPS community. The empirical results and analysis are strong, and the finding regarding the effectiveness of SGD is particularly valuable. I lean towards acceptance.
