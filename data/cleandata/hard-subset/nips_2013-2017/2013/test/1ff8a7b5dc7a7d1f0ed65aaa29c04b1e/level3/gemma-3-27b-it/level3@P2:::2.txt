This paper investigates the usefulness of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) for character-level language modeling. The central result demonstrates that deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs when the number of parameters is kept constant. Further analysis reveals that deeper layers encode more long-range information and, surprisingly, that plain backpropagation through time (BPTT) can achieve state-of-the-art performance on this task – previously thought to require more sophisticated techniques like Hessian-free optimization.

The work is well-motivated, building on the success of deep neural networks in other domains and recognizing the limitations of standard RNNs in capturing hierarchical temporal structure. The authors appropriately relate their work to previous efforts in hierarchical RNNs [3, 5, 6] and stacked LSTMs [8], clearly delineating their contributions. The experimental setup is thorough, including analysis of layer contributions, time scales, and the ability to model long-term dependencies like balanced parentheses. The visualizations and results concerning the learned time scales (Figure 3) are particularly insightful.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper convincingly demonstrates the performance benefits of deeper RNNs on a challenging task. Reaching state-of-the-art performance with simple SGD is a notable achievement.
*   **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of learned time scales and layer contributions provides valuable understanding of how these networks operate. The parentheses example is a compelling demonstration of long-range dependency modeling.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the architecture and experimental setup.
*   **Relevance:** Character-level language modeling is a relevant and increasingly important problem in machine learning.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Novelty Concerns:** The authors initially present the DRNN architecture as novel, but acknowledge in the rebuttal (and as noted in the literature, e.g., [7]) that similar models have been explored previously. While focusing on analysis rather than architectural innovation is acceptable, the paper should more explicitly acknowledge this prior work upfront.
*   **Task Specificity:** The results are primarily demonstrated on a single task (character-level language modeling). While the task is challenging, the conclusions about the benefits of deep RNNs may not generalize to other domains (e.g., speech recognition). Replicating the analysis on additional tasks would significantly strengthen the claims.
*   **Computational Cost:** The reliance on full BPTT is computationally expensive. The reviewer correctly suggests that truncated BPTT, as commonly used by Mikolov et al., could provide a more efficient alternative. Testing truncated BPTT (e.g., with ~30 steps) would be valuable to assess whether it can achieve comparable performance at a lower cost, and potentially address the concern about long-range dependencies (like parentheses) being missed.
*   **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper provides details on the training setup, a more thorough discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., learning rate, number of neurons) would be beneficial.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are generally well-supported by experimental results. While the initial presentation of novelty was overstated, the subsequent focus on analysis is valuable. The work demonstrates a careful and systematic investigation of the proposed architecture.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to understand the key ideas and results.

**Originality:** The originality lies primarily in the analysis of the learned time scales and layer contributions, rather than the architecture itself. The acknowledgement of prior work mitigates the initial concern about novelty.

**Significance:** The results are significant because they demonstrate the potential benefits of deep RNNs and challenge the assumption that Hessian-free optimization is necessary for achieving state-of-the-art performance on character-level language modeling. The insights gained from the analysis could inform the design of future recurrent neural network architectures.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates a clear performance improvement with deeper RNNs.
*   Provides valuable analysis of learned time scales and layer contributions.
*   Challenges existing assumptions about the necessity of advanced optimization techniques.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Initial overstatement of architectural novelty.
*   Limited task specificity of the results.
*   Computational cost of full BPTT could be mitigated with truncated BPTT.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should:

1.  More explicitly acknowledge prior work on similar architectures (e.g., [7]) in the introduction.
2.  Discuss the limitations of task specificity and suggest future work to replicate the analysis on other domains.
3.  Perform experiments with truncated BPTT to assess its performance and computational efficiency.
4.  Briefly discuss the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings.
