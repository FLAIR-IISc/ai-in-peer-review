- Introduces Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN): stacked RNN layers where each layer takes the hidden state of the layer below to build an explicit temporal hierarchy.  
- Motivates DRNN to enable processing at multiple time scales simultaneously, arguing depth provides hierarchical processing beyond mere memory.  
- Evaluated on large-scale character-level language modeling (Wikipedia corpus) and achieves state-of-the-art performance.  
- Achieves competitive results using simple SGD with gradient normalization, comparable to models trained with second-order methods like Hessian-free optimization.  
- Provides strong analyses demonstrating emergent layer-wise time scales, long-range dependency capture (e.g., parenthesis balancing), and distinct roles of lower vs. upper layers.  
- Main strength: clear motivation plus persuasive empirical results showing practical value of stacked recurrent layers.  
- Important weakness: missing critical baseline — a single wide RNN (same total units) or RNN with intra-timestep feedforward layers to isolate benefits of depth vs. capacity.  
- Layer-wise pretraining for DRNN-1O complicates fair comparison with models trained end-to-end; should train networks of varying depths from scratch with controlled parameter/unit counts.  
- Gradient normalization usage is inadequately explained and justified; unclear what "bifurcations" refer to and how normalization impacts SGD dynamics.  
- Should cite and discuss prior work on gradient clipping/truncation (e.g., University of Montreal ICML 2013) and quantify normalization’s importance.  
- Originality: stacking recurrent layers is not entirely new, but this is one of the first successful large-scale applications with detailed analysis of emergent temporal hierarchy.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written with clear equations and diagrams, but captions use DRNN-1O/DRNN-AO before they are defined in the main text.  
- Pro-accept: simple, well-motivated architecture; SOTA results with simpler optimization; insightful internal-dynamics analysis; likely influential.  
- Con-accept: lacks key control experiments and stronger baselines (e.g., Mikolov et al. on Wikipedia), training comparisons are not fully controlled, and technical details on gradient normalization are missing.
