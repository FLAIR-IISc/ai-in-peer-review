This paper investigates the effect of a hierarchical structure on recurrent neural networks (RNNs), proposing a “deep recurrent neural network” (DRNN) where each layer is an RNN receiving the hidden state of the previous layer. The authors hypothesize that this architecture allows for processing time series at multiple time scales and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on character-level language modeling using a Wikipedia corpus, achieving comparable results to models employing more complex optimization techniques like Hessian-free optimization, while relying solely on stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The paper also includes an analysis of emergent time scales within the DRNN.

This work builds upon the well-established success of deep neural networks (DNNs) in various fields, including speech and image recognition (e.g., [4, 17]), and extends the concept of depth to the recurrent domain. While RNNs can be unfolded in time to resemble DNNs, the authors rightly point out that traditional RNNs primarily focus on memory, not hierarchical processing. Previous work explored hierarchical RNNs [3, 5, 6], notably [5] focusing on artificial tasks and [8] achieving strong performance on the TIMIT dataset but lacking detailed architectural analysis. The DRNN architecture, already utilized in [8], is investigated here with a stronger emphasis on understanding its internal dynamics.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The systematic study of stacked RNNs as a natural way to introduce hierarchical processing of time series is a valuable contribution. The authors convincingly argue for the benefits of this architecture in capturing temporal hierarchies.
* **Performance:** Achieving state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance on a challenging character-level language modeling task using a relatively simple training algorithm (SGD) is a significant accomplishment.
* **Analysis:** The attempt to analyze the emergent time scales within the DRNN and understand the roles of different layers, using perturbation experiments and examining parenthesis handling, is insightful. The results suggesting different layers operate at different timescales are particularly interesting.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, architecture, and experimental setup. The figures are helpful in illustrating the DRNN structure.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Gradient Normalization:** The reliance on gradient normalization to “avoid the problem of bifurcations” is a critical point that requires further justification. While effective, the authors do not thoroughly explain the mechanism by which this works or the potential downsides. The connection to the ICML 2013 work from U. of Montreal on gradient truncation/optimization should be discussed, as it provides a related approach to addressing gradient instability. A potential disruption of SGD's averaging behavior by gradient normalization is also a valid concern and could be addressed.
* **Comparison Baselines:** The comparison to existing literature, particularly [19], is slightly problematic. While the authors aim for similar parameter counts (4.9 million), a more direct comparison with baselines having the *same* number of parameters or units would strengthen the claims. Plotting performance for various DRNN depths (trained from scratch, including wider layers to maintain parameter equivalence) instead of the current Figure 2 would provide a more comprehensive assessment.
* **Layer Interpretation:** The “layer surgery” experiments (removing layer contributions) offer limited interpretability due to complex interdependencies. It’s difficult to definitively conclude the “role” of each layer based solely on these results.
* **Missing Experiment:** A crucial missing experiment is to test whether higher layers genuinely benefit from the processing performed by upstream layers. This could be achieved by effectively “relocating” the units from the higher layers to the first layer and observing the performance difference. This would determine if the hierarchical structure is genuinely adding value or if the parameters could be equally effective in a single layer.
* **Definitions:** The acronyms DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO are introduced in Figure 1 but not explicitly defined in the text before their first use, which slightly hinders readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated architecture (DRNN) for processing time series.
* It achieves competitive performance on a challenging task using a simple training algorithm.
* The analysis of emergent time scales provides valuable insights into the internal dynamics of the DRNN.
* The paper is clearly written and relatively easy to follow.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on gradient normalization without a deeper explanation is concerning.
* The comparisons to existing literature could be more rigorous.
* The interpretation of layer contributions is limited by interdependencies.
* The absence of a critical experiment to validate the benefits of the hierarchical structure weakens the overall argument.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a worthwhile paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The idea of using a hierarchical structure of RNNs is compelling, and the experimental results are promising. However, the concerns regarding gradient normalization, baseline comparisons, and the need for a more definitive experiment on the benefits of the hierarchical structure prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent upon addressing the weaknesses outlined above, specifically elaborating on the gradient normalization technique and conducting the proposed experiment to validate the hierarchical benefits. A clearer discussion of comparisons to Mikolov et al. work would also be beneficial.
