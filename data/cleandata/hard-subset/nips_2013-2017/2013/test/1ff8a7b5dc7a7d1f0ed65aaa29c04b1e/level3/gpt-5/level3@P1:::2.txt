This paper studies stacked recurrent neural networks (“deep RNNs,” DRNNs) for character-level language modeling, with each layer a tanh RNN receiving the previous layer’s hidden state. The authors compare two output configurations: top-layer-only (DRNN-1O) and all-layers-to-output (DRNN-AO). Trained with plain SGD (gradient normalization and linear learning-rate decay), DRNNs match or slightly improve over a single-layer RNN at fixed parameter budget (~4.9M) on the Wikipedia benchmark used by Sutskever et al. (2011), and approach the performance of multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free (HF) optimization. The core contribution is an analysis showing that deeper layers encode longer temporal dependencies and that plain BPTT suffices to learn long-range signals such as balanced parentheses.

Relation to prior work: The idea of hierarchical/stacked recurrent architectures is not new (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio, NIPS’95; Fernández, Graves & Schmidhuber, IJCAI’07; Graves, Mohamed & Hinton, ICASSP’13, which used stacked bidirectional LSTMs). The architecture here is essentially stacked tanh RNNs; the novelty lies less in the model and more in the empirical analysis at character level and the demonstration that simple SGD can be competitive with HF on this task. The paper usefully complements prior work that focused on performance (e.g., Graves et al. on TIMIT) by probing time scales, layer contributions, and qualitative behaviors.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The training setup and ablations (top vs all-layer outputs; zeroing a layer’s contribution; perturbation/“typo” tests; context-switch experiments) are sensible and generally support the claims. The parentheses analysis is compelling and connects to established long-range memory benchmarks.
- Empirical support: Results are reported on a single large task with fixed parameter budgets and limited runs (three models). Comparisons to MRNN+HF and compression baselines are informative but not fully controlled (different architectures/training budgets). Variance across seeds and more systematic hyperparameter sweeps are not reported.
- Methodological choices: Backpropagation-through-time over sequences of length 250 (ignoring the first 50) is reasonable; however, the paper does not compare to truncated BPTT with shorter unrolls, which is known to be effective for RNN LMs (e.g., Mikolov). Given the 10-day training cost per model, testing TBPTT (~20–40 steps) could show similar BPC at much lower cost (even if missing very long-range effects like parentheses).

Clarity
- The exposition is clear and well organized. Architectural and training details (initialization, sequence lengths, batch setup, gradient normalization, layer-wise schedule) are described sufficiently to enable reproduction. A few places could be crisper: explicitly state the effective BPTT truncation length; clarify whether DRNN-AO also benefited from the incremental layer-wise schedule (it seems not), to ensure a fair comparison; and report wall-clock vs data-pass normalization to better contextualize the computational cost.

Originality
- Architectural novelty is limited; stacked recurrent models have multiple precedents, including stacked LSTMs. The paper’s contribution is primarily an empirical analysis of time-scale emergence and a demonstration that plain SGD can train deep vanilla RNNs competitively on a challenging character LM. This is a useful but incremental advance. The claim of introducing DRNNs should be toned down and properly credit prior stacked/ hierarchical RNN work (e.g., Graves et al., El Hihi & Bengio).

Significance
- The finding that deeper RNNs outperform a single-layer RNN at fixed parameter count on a large character LM is valuable and likely to influence practice. The time-scale analyses (typo decay, context switching, per-layer ablations) provide insight into how depth is used and will interest the NIPS audience. However, generality beyond the single task is uncertain. Extending the analysis to another domain with known temporal hierarchies (e.g., speech, music, sequential labeling) would strengthen the case.

Specific suggestions
- Compare full BPTT to truncated BPTT (e.g., 20–40 steps) for cost/accuracy trade-offs; report BPC and training time. Even if parentheses closure degrades, similar entropies at far lower cost would be practically important.
- Ensure apples-to-apples training schedules: try the incremental layer-wise schedule for DRNN-AO; conversely, train DRNN-1O end-to-end to isolate the effect of output connectivity vs training curriculum.
- Include variance over multiple initializations and report confidence intervals.
- Add baselines with stacked LSTM or GRU to disentangle the benefits of depth vs gating.
- Evaluate on at least one additional task (e.g., TIMIT/WSJ phoneme or word-level LM), or incorporate comparisons to the stacked LSTM results already cited, to address the “one task” concern raised in the discussion.
- Reframe novelty claims; cite prior stacked/hierarchical RNNs explicitly and position the main contribution as analysis and training observations.

Pros
- Clear empirical evidence that stacked vanilla RNNs improve over single-layer RNNs at fixed parameter budget on a challenging, large-scale dataset.
- Insightful analyses revealing emergent layer-wise time scales and long-range behaviors (parentheses).
- Demonstrates that plain SGD with simple gradient normalization suffices to reach near-HF performance.
- Well written and reproducible details.

Cons
- Limited novelty in architecture; prior work has used stacked recurrent models (including on speech).
- Single-task evaluation; generality not established.
- Comparisons not fully controlled (different curricula across architectures; no TBPTT baseline; no gated baselines).
- Heavy training cost; missing a systematic study of cost vs accuracy.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper’s analytical insights and practical message—that depth in RNNs yields useful temporal hierarchies and that plain SGD can be competitive—are valuable to the community, despite limited architectural novelty and single-task scope. Strengthening the positioning relative to prior stacked RNN/LSTM work and adding a TBPTT comparison would further improve the paper.
