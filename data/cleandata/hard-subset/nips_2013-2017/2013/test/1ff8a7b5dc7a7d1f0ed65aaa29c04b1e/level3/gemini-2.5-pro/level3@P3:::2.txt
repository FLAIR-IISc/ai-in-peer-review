This paper studies the properties of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), an architecture where multiple RNN layers are stacked hierarchically. The authors apply this model to character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia dataset and compare its performance against a standard, single-layer RNN. The main finding is that a 5-layer DRNN outperforms a shallow RNN when the total number of parameters is held constant.

The paper’s core contributions are analytical. First, it provides experimental evidence that deeper layers in the DRNN learn to operate on longer time scales, creating a temporal hierarchy that is beneficial for modeling the complex structure of text. Second, it demonstrates that these deep models can be trained effectively with standard backpropagation through time (BPTT) and stochastic gradient descent, achieving performance comparable to prior state-of-the-art results that relied on the more complex Hessian-Free (HF) optimizer. A notable part of this demonstration is showing that a DRNN trained with BPTT can successfully learn to balance parentheses over long distances, a task previously thought to require methods like HF.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Valuable Analysis:** The primary strength of this work is its focus on analyzing and understanding the behavior of deep RNNs. Rather than just presenting a new architecture, the authors conduct a series of insightful experiments to probe the model's internal dynamics. The analysis showing the emergence of different time scales across layers (Figure 3) provides concrete evidence for the hypothesized benefits of depth in processing hierarchical temporal information.
2.  **Challenging Prior Assumptions:** The finding that plain BPTT is sufficient to train a model to handle long-range dependencies like parenthesis balancing is significant. It challenges the prevailing view at the time that complex second-order optimization methods were necessary for such tasks, suggesting that architectural choices (i.e., depth) can be as important as the choice of optimizer.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark using a relatively simple training algorithm, which is an important contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Originality and Positioning:** A major weakness is the paper's positioning of the DRNN architecture as a novel contribution. Stacked recurrent architectures were not new at the time of publication; for instance, Graves et al. (ICASSP 2013) had already used stacked bidirectional LSTMs to achieve state-of-the-art results in speech recognition. The paper should be revised to clearly acknowledge this prior work and frame its contribution as a detailed *analysis* of the architecture on a new domain, rather than an architectural innovation. This would not diminish the paper's value but would position it more accurately within the literature.

2.  **Generality of Claims:** The conclusions are drawn exclusively from experiments on character-level language modeling. While this is a challenging and suitable task, the claim that DRNNs are generally effective at learning temporal hierarchies would be much stronger if supported by results from at least one other domain, such as speech recognition or word-level language modeling. Without this, it is difficult to disentangle properties of the model from properties of the specific task. I tend to agree with the "one task" criticism that is often leveled against papers making broad architectural claims.

3.  **Training Efficiency:** The experiments use BPTT over long sequences (200 steps for the backward pass), which is computationally expensive. It is not clear whether this is necessary for achieving the reported bits-per-character (BPC) scores. Much contemporary work (e.g., by Mikolov) achieved strong results on language modeling with truncated BPTT (TBPTT) using a much smaller window (~20-40 steps). It would be highly beneficial to include a baseline trained with TBPTT. It is plausible that a model trained with a truncation window of ~30 steps could achieve a nearly identical BPC score at a fraction of the computational cost, even if it fails on the specific long-range parenthesis task. Such an experiment would provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs between training cost and the ability to capture dependencies of different lengths.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-Acceptance:**
    *   Provides a thorough and insightful analysis of how depth affects the dynamics of RNNs.
    *   Demonstrates that simple SGD/BPTT can achieve state-of-the-art results, challenging the necessity of more complex optimizers.
    *   The experimental results on a difficult benchmark are strong.

*   **Con-Acceptance:**
    *   The novelty of the architecture itself is overstated; similar models existed in prior work.
    *   The claims about the general benefits of depth are based on a single task, limiting their perceived generality.
    *   The necessity of the computationally expensive training procedure (full BPTT) is not justified against more efficient alternatives like TBPTT.

Overall, this is a valuable paper due to its careful analysis, but its claims should be tempered and better contextualized. The weaknesses regarding originality and generality are significant but could be addressed through careful rewriting and additional experiments.
