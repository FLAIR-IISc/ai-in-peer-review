## Review of "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks"

This paper investigates the benefits of depth in recurrent neural networks (RNNs), motivated by the success of deep architectures in other domains. The authors propose and analyze Deep RNNs (DRNNs), stacking RNN layers to create a hierarchy capable of processing time series with multiple timescales. They demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on character-level language modeling using simple stochastic gradient descent (SGD), a compelling result given previous reliance on more complex training methods. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and largely convincing.

A significant strength is the observation that deeper RNNs can implicitly capture temporal hierarchies, and the empirical support for this through the language modeling task and analysis of time scales (Figure 3). The ablation study in Table 2, while acknowledging the complexities of interpreting layer contributions, offers valuable insight into the roles of different layers in the DRNN-AO architecture. The text generation examples further illustrate the network’s ability to capture long-range dependencies, such as parentheses matching. The authors also rightly point out that DRNNs can implicitly learn temporal hierarchies, adding to previous work like El Hihi & Bengio (2009) which explicitly designed hierarchies.

However, several areas require further investigation. While the authors mention normalization of gradients to avoid bifurcations, the exact mechanism and its importance remain unclear. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of *how* this normalization avoids the described issues, and a discussion of its potential impact on the behavior of SGD, as alluded to by the Montreal ICML 2013 paper on gradient truncation (Pascanu et al., 2013). A crucial missing experiment is a control study: can the performance gains from higher layers be achieved simply by increasing the number of units in the *first* layer, effectively relocating capacity rather than adding hierarchical processing?  

The comparison to Hessian-free optimization in Mikolov et al. is good, but the parameter count differences are concerning.  Presenting results alongside baselines with comparable parameter counts, or plotting performance against depth *while controlling for total parameters* (as suggested in Figure 2), would strengthen the conclusions. Finally, DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO are introduced in Figure 1 before being defined in the text; this should be rectified. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of deep learning principles to RNNs.
*   State-of-the-art results achieved with a simple training algorithm.
*   Convincing evidence for emergent temporal hierarchies.
*   Clear writing and good motivation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Missing control experiments to isolate the benefits of depth vs. capacity.
*   Lack of detailed explanation regarding gradient normalization.
*   Potential for unfair comparison due to parameter count differences.



Overall, this is a worthwhile contribution with the potential to advance our understanding of RNNs. Addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly the control experiments and discussion of gradient normalization, would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
