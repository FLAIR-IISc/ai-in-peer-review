- Proposes stacked RNNs (DRNN) to introduce hierarchical processing of time series where each layer receives hidden state from the previous layer.  
- Claims DRNN captures multiple time scales and shows emergent hierarchical temporal behavior.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art character-level language modeling on a Wikipedia corpus using only SGD.  
- Provides analysis of layer roles and time scales using perturbation experiments and parenthesis-handling tests.  
- Paper is generally well-written with clear motivation, architecture description, and helpful figures.  
- Novelty: systematic study of stacked RNNs as a natural extension of depth to recurrent models.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance with a simple training algorithm (SGD).  
- Strength: insightful analysis suggesting different layers operate at different timescales.  
- Weakness: heavy reliance on gradient normalization to avoid bifurcations without sufficient explanation or analysis of downsides.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of related optimization work (e.g., ICML 2013 U. of Montreal on gradient truncation/optimization).  
- Weakness: baseline comparisons are not rigorous enough—need baselines with identical parameter counts/units and plots for various DRNN depths.  
- Weakness: “layer surgery” removals offer limited interpretability due to complex interdependencies between layers.  
- Missing experiment: relocating higher-layer units to the first layer to test whether hierarchical structure adds real value versus parameter reallocation.  
- Minor clarity issue: acronyms DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO are introduced in Figure 1 before being defined in text.  
- For acceptance: novel, well-motivated architecture; competitive results with simple training; useful analysis of emergent time scales; clear writing.  
- Against acceptance: unexplained gradient normalization, weaker baseline comparisons, limited layer-interpretation, and absence of the critical relocation experiment.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on deeper explanation of gradient normalization, stronger baseline/ablation experiments (including the relocation test), and clearer comparisons to prior work (e.g., Mikolov et al.).
