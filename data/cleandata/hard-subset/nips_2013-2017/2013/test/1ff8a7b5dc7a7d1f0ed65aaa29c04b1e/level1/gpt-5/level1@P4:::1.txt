Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies stacked recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) for sequence modeling. Each layer is a simple tanh RNN; higher layers receive the hidden state of the layer below. Two output variants are evaluated: (i) only the top layer connects to the output (DRNN-1O) and (ii) all layers connect to the output (DRNN-AO). Models are trained with SGD and gradient normalization, with incremental layer-wise training for DRNN-1O. Experiments focus on character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus, measuring bits-per-character (BPC). The paper also analyzes emergent time scales using perturbation (“typo”) tests, context-switch experiments, ablations of layer outputs, and a targeted long-range dependency probe (closing parentheses).

The approach builds on prior hierarchical/stacked RNNs (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio 1995; Fernández et al. 2007) and stacked LSTM architectures for speech (Graves et al. 2013). It also relates to work on character-level RNN language models and advanced training (Sutskever et al. 2011; Martens & Sutskever 2011). The primary contributions here are (a) showing that stacked simple RNNs trained with vanilla SGD can match prior recurrent state of the art on a large-scale char-LM task at similar parameter counts and (b) providing empirical analysis indicating layer-wise temporal hierarchies.

Strengths
- Clear and simple architecture; training details (BPTT length, batch setup, parameter budgets) are sufficiently described to be reproducible in principle.
- Competitive BPC on a large Wikipedia corpus with ~5M parameters, comparable to multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free methods.
- Insightful analyses: layer ablation suggests different roles for layers; perturbation and context-switch experiments show emergent time scales; parentheses closure probe demonstrates long-range dependencies beyond training context length.
- The “all-layers-to-output” variant provides both a training signal to lower layers and a handle to probe layer contributions.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is mostly incremental; stacking RNNs is known, and stacked LSTMs had demonstrated strong results. The architectural contribution is modest; the main new elements are the systematic analysis and the output-at-all-layers variant.
- Limited experimental breadth: only three model instantiations, single dataset, and no comparisons to LSTM baselines or n-gram/KN backoff models common in language modeling. The vocabulary mismatch with [19] complicates exact comparability.
- Lack of statistical rigor: no variance across random initializations or seeds, and few ablations on depth, width, and training schedules at fixed parameter counts.
- The training protocol differs across variants (incremental pretraining for DRNN-1O), which may confound architectural comparisons.
- Some methodological choices merit more justification (e.g., full gradient normalization rather than clipped gradients; zero initialization of output layers; sensitivity to BPTT length).

Quality
Technically sound overall. The architecture and training are standard; analyses are carefully designed and plausible. Claims about “state-of-the-art for recurrent networks” are cautiously framed with parameter counts, but would be stronger with standardized evaluation (identical character set and preprocessing) and stronger baselines (e.g., stacked LSTMs). The time-scale analyses are compelling, though largely qualitative; additional quantitative summaries (e.g., characteristic decay constants with confidence intervals) would be helpful. The parentheses study is well-motivated and informative, with clear success/failure patterns.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The model definitions and training details are explicit. Figures and ablations are easy to interpret. Some references are incomplete; ensure full bibliographic entries. It would help to:
- Report precise training throughput (characters processed) and effective epochs across models.
- Detail the impact of gradient normalization vs clipping and sensitivity to η0, BPTT truncation, layer count.
- Clarify how the different character vocabularies affect BPC comparability with prior work.

Originality
The idea of stacking recurrent layers and using multiple time scales is not new; however, the combination of a simple stacked tanh RNN, an “all-layers-to-output” training head, and a focused suite of temporal analyses for character-level modeling provides a useful and timely empirical contribution. The novelty resides primarily in analysis and in demonstrating that simple SGD can match HF-trained MRNNs at similar scale on this task.

Significance
Character-level language modeling is an important benchmark, and showing competitive performance with simpler training is practically valuable. The analyses of emergent time scales and layer contributions offer insights that may influence model design and diagnostic practice. Nonetheless, impact is moderated by limited comparisons (notably absence of LSTM baselines) and the incremental nature of the architecture.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide results for stacked LSTMs at similar parameter budgets for a stronger baseline, and/or compare to single-layer LSTM RNNs.
- Report variability over multiple seeds. Include depth sweep (2–6 layers) at fixed parameter counts to substantiate claims about hierarchy.
- Standardize the dataset to match prior work’s character set, or quantify the effect of vocabulary differences on BPC.
- Quantify time-scale measurements (e.g., fit exponential decays) and report statistics. Extend the parentheses probe to aggregate success vs. span length and examine nested cases quantitatively.
- Discuss why DRNN-AO slightly underperforms DRNN-1O despite seemingly better gradient flow; is this due to training schedule or representational trade-offs?

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid empirical performance on a large, relevant benchmark with a simple training pipeline.
- Useful and careful analyses illuminating emergent temporal hierarchy in stacked RNNs.
- Practical takeaway that SGD with gradient normalization suffices at scale.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to existing stacked RNN/LSTM architectures; architectural contribution is modest.
- Missing strong baselines and broader experimental validation; conclusions about superiority are not fully substantiated.
- Few seeds and limited ablations hinder assessment of robustness.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper’s analytical insights and competitive results are valuable, but the case would be stronger with additional baselines and broader experimental validation.
