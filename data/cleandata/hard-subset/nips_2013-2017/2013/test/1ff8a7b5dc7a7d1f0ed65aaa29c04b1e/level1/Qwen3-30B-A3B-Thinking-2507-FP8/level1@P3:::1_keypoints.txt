- Introduces Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs): hierarchical RNN layers receiving previous layer hidden states to explicitly model temporal hierarchies.  
- Claims DRNNs develop different time scales per layer and outperform standard RNNs on character-level language modeling using only SGD.  
- Builds on prior hierarchical and stacked RNN/LSTM work but positions DRNNs as explicitly embedding multiple time scales.  
- Key contribution: empirical analysis showing layers specialize to different time scales via perturbation experiments and a parentheses-closing task.  
- Strength: compelling conceptual case for temporal hierarchy with insightful perturbation analysis (Figure 3).  
- Strength: convincing experimental results—state-of-the-art RNN performance on Wikipedia corpus with plain SGD.  
- Strength: parentheses-closing experiment demonstrates DRNNs capture long-term dependencies beyond training length.  
- Strength: clear distinction and analysis between DRNN variants (DRNN-1O vs DRNN-AO) and layer contributions.  
- Weakness: lacks theoretical justification for why DRNNs improve learning dynamics or generalization.  
- Weakness: comparisons to prior hierarchical RNN work are superficial; insufficient differentiation from related approaches.  
- Weakness: limited experimental scope—only character-level language modeling on a single corpus.  
- Weakness: layer-wise training for DRNN-1O is used but not well-justified, raising scalability/robustness concerns.  
- Weakness: marginal performance gains versus increased architectural complexity and unclear computational cost trade-offs.  
- Critical assessment: strong empirical evidence for temporal hierarchy but largely empirical without broad/general or theoretical support.  
- Recommendation: accept, contingent on addressing theoretical gaps, broader validation across tasks, and clearer differentiation from prior work.  
- Pro-accept: novel time-scale analysis, simple SGD SOTA result, insightful perturbation experiments, clear writing and experimental design.  
- Con-accept: limited theory, marginal gains for added complexity, shallow related-work comparison, narrow task evaluation, unjustified layer-wise training.
