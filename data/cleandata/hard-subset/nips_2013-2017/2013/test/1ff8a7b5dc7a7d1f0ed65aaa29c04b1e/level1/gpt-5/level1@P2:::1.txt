Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies stacked recurrent neural networks (“Deep RNNs”, DRNNs) for character-level language modeling. Each layer is a tanh RNN receiving the lower layer’s hidden state as input; outputs are either from the top layer only (DRNN-1O) or “deeply supervised” with outputs from all layers summed (DRNN-AO). The authors train with SGD and gradient normalization, and analyze emergent time scales via perturbation studies, ablations, and a focused test on long-term dependencies (closing parentheses). On a large Wikipedia corpus, 5-layer DRNNs with ~4.9M parameters match prior state-of-the-art for recurrent models achieved by multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free (HF) optimization, using only SGD.

This work connects to: hierarchical/stacked RNNs (El Hihi & Bengio ’96; Fernández et al. ’07); stacked LSTMs for speech (Graves et al., ICASSP ’13) focusing on accuracy rather than analysis; and HF-optimized MRNNs for character modeling (Sutskever et al., ICML ’11/’13). The paper’s distinctive contribution is a simple stacked tanh-RNN architecture plus an empirical analysis revealing layer-wise time scales and long-term behaviors.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The model definition and training protocol are clear (tanh units, per-layer recurrences, BPTT over 250 steps, gradient normalization, linear LR decay). The “deep supervision” variant (outputs from all layers) is well-motivated to improve gradient flow and interpretability. The incremental layer-wise training for DRNN-1O is a sensible supervised pretraining approach. Analyses (typo perturbation, context-switch BPC increase, layer ablations, generative samples, parentheses tracking) are thoughtfully designed and consistent.
- Empirical support: Results on a 1.4B-character Wikipedia corpus show DRNNs outperform a single-layer RNN and reach BPC comparable to MRNN+HF with matched parameter counts. The time-scale measurements convincingly demonstrate longer effective context for DRNNs and a hierarchy of time scales, especially in the AO variant. The parentheses experiments provide targeted evidence of long-range dependencies.
- Limitations: Comparisons are limited. There is no LSTM/GRU baseline, no MRNN reimplementation, and only one dataset/task. Only three model instantiations are tried, with no variance across random seeds. The character set differs (95 vs. 86), making cross-paper BPC comparisons imperfect. The optimization heuristic (normalized gradients) is unusual and its impact is not ablated. The incremental training in DRNN-1O confounds architectural versus training effects in the time-scale analysis.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The architecture and training setup are precisely specified, with helpful distinctions between DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO. The analyses are explained and interpreted carefully. Suggestions to improve clarity:
- Report development/validation protocol (how hyperparameters were chosen without overfitting to test).
- Provide exact BPC numbers in a consolidated table with parameter counts and character set details, and include standard deviations when possible.
- Explain gradient normalization more formally (e.g., clip vs. normalize; typical norms observed; sensitivity to η0).
- Include training curves and per-layer norm/activation ranges to complement the time-scale analysis.

Originality
Stacked RNNs per se are not new, nor is connecting intermediate layers to outputs in deep networks. The novelty here lies in the combination of: (a) simple stacked tanh-RNNs trained with SGD achieving competitive BPC, (b) the deep-supervision variant for RNNs, and (c) the systematic empirical analysis of emergent time scales and long-range behaviors on a large realistic dataset. While incremental supervised pretraining is a known idea, its application to DRNN-1O is pragmatic and informative. Overall, moderate originality with a strong analytical component.

Significance
The results support a simple and scalable recipe (stack depth + SGD + gradient normalization) to close much of the gap with more complex, HF-trained models on char-level language modeling. The analyses provide insight into why depth helps (layer-wise time scales, division of labor across layers), which could inform future architectures and training strategies. However, the lack of gated RNN baselines and broader tasks tempers the impact. Still, for NIPS 2013, demonstrating that depth alone (even with plain tanh units) yields strong performance and richer temporal structure is a meaningful contribution.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architecture and training; practical and reproducible.
- Competitive BPC with prior state-of-the-art recurrent models using only SGD.
- Convincing, multifaceted analysis of temporal hierarchies and long-range effects.
- Careful ablations (per-layer output removal) and targeted behavioral tests (parentheses).
- Discussion of training strategies (deep supervision, incremental layer-wise training).

Weaknesses
- Limited comparisons: no LSTM/GRU or MRNN baselines under a common setup; single dataset.
- Few runs and no uncertainty estimates; claims of “state-of-the-art” depend on imperfect cross-paper comparability (different character set).
- Training heuristics (gradient normalization, linear annealing) not ablated; unclear role of each.
- The DRNN-1O time-scale findings are confounded by its staged training procedure.
- No regularization/overfitting analysis; no validation split described.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add LSTM (and ideally MRNN) baselines with matched parameter counts and identical preprocessing; report BPC and training cost.
- Use a held-out validation set for hyperparameter selection and early stopping; report multiple seeds.
- Ablate gradient normalization and deep supervision to quantify their contributions.
- Evaluate on an additional corpus (e.g., text8, Penn Treebank chars) for robustness.
- Probe more structured long-range patterns (e.g., quotes, brackets of different types, indentation in code).
- Release code and scripts for reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Empirically strong result with a simple method on a large, realistic benchmark.
- Insightful analyses that enhance understanding of deep RNNs’ temporal behavior.
- Practical training recipe (SGD + depth + deep supervision) likely to be adopted.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incomplete baselines and limited experimental breadth reduce confidence in the central performance claim.
- Architectural novelty is incremental relative to prior stacked RNNs.
- Methodological choices (gradient normalization, staged training) not isolated in controlled studies.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a solid empirical advance with a valuable analytical contribution, despite limited baselines and scope.
