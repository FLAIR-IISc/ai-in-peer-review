- Study of stacked tanh RNNs (DRNNs) for character-level language modeling, comparing top-layer-only vs all-layers-to-output architectures under a fixed ~4.9M parameter budget on a 1.4B-char Wikipedia corpus.
- Training uses SGD with gradient-norm normalization and incremental layer-wise training for DRNN-1O; evaluation controlled for BPTT length and burn-in.
- Results: DRNNs achieve bits-per-character comparable to multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization.
- Analysis includes perturbation/ablation studies and a parenthesis-closing probe showing emergent temporal hierarchies across layers.
- Novelty is limited in architecture (stacking and intermediate outputs are known); main contributions are simple SGD training that is competitive and careful analysis of time scales.
- Experimental protocol strengths: matched parameter counts, BPTT length, and reproducible-seeming analysis methods.
- Empirical weaknesses: narrow model set (only three instantiations) and no variance across random seeds.
- Missing important baselines, notably matched-parameter stacked LSTM/GRU on the same dataset.
- Comparability concerns due to differing character sets and unspecified differences in training budgets versus prior work.
- Claims of “state-of-the-art for recurrent networks” are weak without standardized preprocessing and wall-clock/epoch-normalized training budgets.
- Clarity is good with precise equations and training details, but exact BPC numbers and full configuration/code are requested for reproducibility.
- Pros for acceptance: shows simple stacked RNNs + SGD can be competitive, provides thoughtful temporal analyses, and offers a practical training recipe.
- Cons for acceptance: lacks LSTM/GRU comparisons, limited hyperparameter exploration and statistical analysis, and comparability issues.
- Suggested fixes: add matched LSTM/GRU baselines, report exact BPCs and training throughput, ablate gradient normalization and incremental schedule, and release code/configurations.
