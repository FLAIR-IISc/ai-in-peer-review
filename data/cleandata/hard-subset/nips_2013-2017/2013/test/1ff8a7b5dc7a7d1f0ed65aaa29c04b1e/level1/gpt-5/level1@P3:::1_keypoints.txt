- Paper studies stacked vanilla RNNs (DRNNs) with two output schemes (top-only DRNN-1O with incremental training, and all-outputs DRNN-AO) for character-level language modeling.
- Using simple SGD with gradient normalization and decaying LR, authors achieve competitive BPC on a large Wikipedia corpus with ~4.9M parameters.
- Analysis includes per-layer output ablations, typo decay and context-switch perturbation tests, and qualitative parenthesis-closing behavior to probe emergent time scales.
- Contribution sits among prior stacked/hierarchical RNN work and deep LSTM systems, adding empirical performance and temporal-scale analysis for vanilla RNN stacks.
- Strength: clear, simple architecture and training protocol showing SGD + gradient normalization can train deep vanilla RNNs effectively.
- Strength: competitive performance to HF-trained multiplicative RNNs using a single GPU and simpler optimization.
- Strength: well-designed temporal-hierarchy analyses that probe layer roles and long-range behavior.
- Strength: the all-outputs variant serves as deep supervision and is experimentally explored.
- Weakness: incomplete comparisons — no LSTM/deep LSTM baselines on the same dataset and parameter budget, and preprocessing differences complicate state-of-the-art claims.
- Weakness: limited experimental breadth — only three models, no random-seed variance, no depth/width trade-off exploration, and no single-layer wider baseline with equal params.
- Weakness: incremental DRNN-1O training confounds interpretations of emergent time scales due to pretraining effects.
- Weakness: methodological details unclear — gradient “normalization” vs clipping, zeroed output initialization, and effects of BPTT truncation not quantified.
- Weakness: analyses are largely qualitative/heuristic; more quantitative measures (mutual information, spectral analyses) would strengthen claims.
- Quality: technically sound and carefully executed within scope, but limited runs and baselines weaken causal attributions and state-of-the-art claims.
- Clarity: well written and organized; needs precise definitions (gradient norm vs clipping), preprocessing clarifications, and sensitivity details; report error bars.
- Originality: stacking RNNs is not novel, but showing strong results with simple SGD, proposing all-outputs variant, and analyzing emergent time scales are useful empirical contributions.
- Significance: moderate — valuable to practitioners and informative for diagnostics, but impact limited without broader controlled comparisons.
- Suggestions: add same-parameter single-layer and LSTM baselines, report on standard benchmarks, show multiple seeds/confidence intervals, quantify AO effects on gradients/training, and analyze spectral properties.
- Arguments for acceptance: simple effective approach, insightful temporal analyses, clear exposition, and practical training recipe.
- Arguments against acceptance: missing strong baselines, limited experimental breadth, and partially supported claims about emergent time scales and state-of-the-art.
- Recommendation: borderline accept; strengthen experimental comparisons to make a clearer accept.
