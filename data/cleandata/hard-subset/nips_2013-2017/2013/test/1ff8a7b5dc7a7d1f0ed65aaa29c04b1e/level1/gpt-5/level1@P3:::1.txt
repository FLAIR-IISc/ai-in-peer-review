Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies “deep” recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), formed by stacking multiple vanilla RNN layers, each receiving as input the hidden state of the layer below. Two output schemes are considered: (i) DRNN-1O, where only the top layer connects to the softmax, trained with an incremental layer-wise schedule; and (ii) DRNN-AO, where each layer has a direct output connection (“all outputs”). Using simple SGD with gradient normalization and a linearly decaying learning rate, the authors report state-of-the-art bits-per-character (BPC) on a large Wikipedia corpus for RNNs with ≈4.9M parameters, competitive with multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-Free (HF) methods [18,15,16]. They further analyze emergent time scales by perturbation (typo) and context-switch experiments and present qualitative evidence that DRNNs capture long-range phenomena such as closing parentheses.

This work sits among a line of hierarchical/stacked recurrent models [5,6] and recent deep LSTM systems for speech [8,7]. Compared to [8], which focused on performance with stacked LSTMs, this paper provides more architectural and temporal-scale analysis for stacked vanilla RNNs. It also relates to character-level language modeling with mRNNs and HF training [18], and to classical work on long-term dependencies (LSTM [11]) and vanishing/exploding gradients. While stacking RNNs is not new (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio [5], Fernández et al. [6]), the combination of simple SGD training, empirical performance on a large text task, and careful analysis of emergent time scales is a useful contribution.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architecture and training protocol; results show that careful SGD with gradient normalization can train deep vanilla RNN stacks effectively on challenging character-level LM.
- Competitive BPC to HF-trained mRNNs at similar parameter budgets, with a single GPU and simpler optimization.
- Well-designed analyses of temporal hierarchies: per-layer ablations of output contributions; typo decay and context-switch tests to quantify layer-wise time scales; qualitative parenthesis-closing behavior assessed both in discriminative mode and during generation.
- The “all outputs” variant doubles as a form of deep supervision that may mitigate gradient attenuation, and the experiments probe its effect.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Comparisons are incomplete: no LSTM (or deep LSTM) baselines on the same dataset/parameter budget, despite [8,7] indicating the relevance. mRNN and PAQ comparisons differ in corpus preprocessing (e.g., 95 vs 86 characters), and training budgets/hyperparameters are not strictly controlled across works, complicating claims of state-of-the-art.
- Limited experimental breadth: only three model instantiations, no variance across random seeds, no exploration of depth/width trade-offs, and no control showing that depth beats a wider single-layer RNN with the same parameter count.
- The incremental training schedule for DRNN-1O confounds the interpretation of emergent time scales (as acknowledged by the authors): intermediate layers effectively learn as top layers during pretraining.
- Methodological details could be clearer: gradient “normalization” (always to unit norm) differs from standard clipping; the choice of zero-initialized output weights may slow convergence; the effect of BPTT truncation (250 steps, first 50 ignored) on long-range modeling is not quantified.
- The analyses, while insightful, are mostly qualitative or heuristic. Quantitative attributions (e.g., layer-wise mutual information with future tokens, spectral analyses of recurrent matrices) would strengthen the claims.

Quality
The paper is technically sound and the empirical study is carefully executed within its scope. The training method (SGD with gradient normalization) is reasonable, and the analyses of time scales are thoughtfully designed and repeatable. However, the limited number of runs and baselines makes it hard to conclusively attribute gains to depth versus capacity or training schedule. Claims of “state-of-the-art” should be tempered given corpus/preprocessing differences and the absence of LSTM baselines.

Clarity
Overall clearly written and well organized. The architectural description and training procedures are easy to follow. Suggestions:
- Precisely define gradient normalization versus clipping, and justify the choice.
- Clarify corpus preprocessing differences relative to [18] and whether BPC is directly comparable.
- Provide more detail on initialization (particularly zeroing output layers) and sensitivity to sequence length/truncation.
- Where possible, include error bars or multiple runs for key numbers.

Originality
Stacking RNNs is not novel per se, but the paper’s contribution lies in:
- Demonstrating strong character-level results with simple SGD on deep vanilla RNNs.
- Introducing the all-outputs variant as an effective training aid and diagnostic for per-layer roles.
- Providing a careful empirical analysis of emergent time scales and specific long-range behaviors (parentheses).
This is a useful empirical and analytical contribution, though not conceptually groundbreaking.

Significance
Moderate. The work will interest practitioners of sequence modeling by showing that simple training suffices for deep RNNs and by offering insight into temporal hierarchies. The analyses may inform architecture design and diagnostics. The impact would be stronger with broader and more controlled comparisons (especially to LSTM/deep LSTM) and more rigorous quantification of temporal scales.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Include a same-parameter, single-layer wider RNN baseline to isolate the effect of depth.
- Add at least one LSTM baseline (e.g., 1–2 layers, matched parameters) on the same corpus.
- Report results on a standard small benchmark (e.g., text8 or PTB-char) for easier comparability.
- Quantify the effect of the all-outputs scheme on gradient norms and training speed; relate to deep supervision effects.
- Provide multiple random seeds or confidence intervals for BPC.
- Explore whether learned time scales correlate with spectral properties (eigenvalues) of Wi across layers.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, effective approach with competitive results on a large-scale, relevant task.
- Insightful and well-executed analyses of temporal hierarchies and long-range behavior.
- Clear exposition; practical training recipe likely to be adopted.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing strong baselines (LSTM/deep LSTM), and fairness of comparisons is debatable.
- Limited experimental breadth (few runs, minimal hyperparameter exploration).
- Some claims (emergent time scales, state-of-the-art) are only partially supported by quantitative evidence.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The empirical insights and analyses are valuable, and the paper is carefully executed. Strengthening the experimental comparisons (especially to LSTMs and to a wider single-layer RNN) would make this a clear accept.
