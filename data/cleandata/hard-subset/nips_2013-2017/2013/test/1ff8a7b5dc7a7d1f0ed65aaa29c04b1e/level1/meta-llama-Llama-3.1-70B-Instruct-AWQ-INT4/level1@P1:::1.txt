**Summary of Main Ideas**

The paper "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks" introduces a novel architecture called Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), which combines the strengths of deep neural networks and recurrent neural networks. The authors propose a hierarchical structure where each layer is a recurrent neural network that receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input. This architecture is designed to capture temporal hierarchies in time series data, particularly in character-level language modeling tasks. The paper presents an analysis of the emergent time scales in DRNNs and demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on a Wikipedia-based text corpus using simple stochastic gradient descent.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The idea of hierarchical recurrent neural networks is not new, and previous work has explored similar architectures [3, 5, 6]. However, the authors' approach is distinct in that it combines the strengths of deep neural networks and recurrent neural networks, allowing for more natural capture of temporal hierarchies. The paper also builds upon the work of [8], which employed stacked bi-directional LSTM networks for speech recognition tasks.

2. **State-of-the-Art Performance**: The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on a Wikipedia-based text corpus using simple stochastic gradient descent, which is a significant achievement.
3. **Analysis of Emergent Time Scales**: The paper provides a detailed analysis of the emergent time scales in DRNNs, which is a valuable contribution to the understanding of these models.
4. **Well-Written and Well-Organized**: The paper is well-written and well-organized, making it easy to follow and understand.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Experiments**: The paper presents limited experiments, with only three network instantiations tested. More experiments would be necessary to fully understand the strengths and weaknesses of DRNNs.
2. **Lack of Comparison to Other Architectures**: The paper does not provide a detailed comparison to other architectures, such as LSTMs or Echo State Networks, which would be useful for understanding the relative strengths and weaknesses of DRNNs.
3. **Training Method**: The paper uses a simple stochastic gradient descent method, which may not be the most effective method for training DRNNs. More advanced training methods, such as Hessian-free optimization, may be necessary to fully unlock the potential of DRNNs.
