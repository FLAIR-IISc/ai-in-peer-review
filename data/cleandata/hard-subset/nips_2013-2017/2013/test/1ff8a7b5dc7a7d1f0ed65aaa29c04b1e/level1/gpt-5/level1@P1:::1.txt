Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies “deep” recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) formed by stacking recurrent layers, where each layer is a tanh RNN that consumes the previous layer’s hidden state. Two output variants are considered: DRNN-1O (only top layer to output) and DRNN-AO (outputs from all layers summed). Using simple SGD with gradient normalization and a linear decay schedule, the authors report state-of-the-art bits-per-character (BPC) on a large Wikipedia character-level language modeling corpus with ~4.9M parameters, competitive with multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free (HF) optimization (Sutskever et al., ICML’11). Beyond accuracy, the paper analyzes emergent time scales via perturbation (“typo”) experiments, context-switching tests, layer-ablation at the output, and a qualitative/quantitative “parentheses closing” study, arguing that stacked RNNs naturally induce a temporal hierarchy.

Relative to prior work, hierarchical/stacked RNNs have appeared (El Hihi & Bengio ’95; Fernández et al. ’07; Graves et al. ICASSP’13 stacked LSTMs), often emphasizing performance on speech. This work differs by (i) using simple tanh RNNs trained with SGD to achieve competitive BPC on large-scale text; and (ii) offering a systematic analysis of emergent time scales and layer roles in a real-world language task. The output-at-all-layers variant connects to multi-loss deep networks used to ease optimization, and the incremental supervised layerwise training for DRNN-1O is a practical contribution.

Quality
Strengths:
- Methodology is clearly specified: architecture, training (BPTT length ~200, sequences of 250 chars with 50 burn-in), gradient normalization (unit-norm), linear learning-rate decay, parameterization to match ~4.9M parameters across models, and controlled hardware/time budgets. 
- Comparisons include shallow RNN and two DRNN variants; analyses include layer ablations, typo/context perturbations, and a targeted long-term dependency probe (parentheses). These go beyond a pure leaderboard result and give convincing evidence of emergent time scales and long-context usage.
- The finding that DRNN-1O benefits from incremental supervised pretraining is practically useful.

Weaknesses:
- Limited breadth of baselines and ablations. Only three trained models, a single random seed, and no comparison to LSTM/GRU or stacked LSTMs (which are known to address vanishing gradients and often excel on long-term dependencies; cf. Graves et al. 2013). This leaves open whether depth or gating accounts for the gains.
- SOTA claims rely on results that differ slightly in character set (95+UNK vs 86) and potentially data splits; while authors argue this is negligible, it complicates apples-to-apples comparisons. Please report training set size actually seen by each model and confirm test protocol matches [19].
- The gradient “normalization” (divide by norm) is akin to global clipping to a fixed radius; citing and contrasting with adaptive clipping (Pascanu, Mikolov & Bengio, 2013) would strengthen the training discussion and possibly improve stability/efficiency.
- Some choices merit more justification: zero-initialized output weights (risk of slow start), same width per layer, and lack of hyperparameter exploration (e.g., truncation length sensitivity, spectral radius of recurrent matrices).
- Statistical robustness is unclear: no variance across runs, and only subjective quality assessment for text samples aside from BPC.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Figures illustrating the architecture and analyses are helpful. A few details would improve reproducibility and clarity:
- Specify hidden-state handling between mini-batches (state carry-over or reset?), sampling strategy over the corpus (contiguous or random segments), and whether dropout/regularization was used.
- Provide exact BPC numbers for all models (train/valid/test), not only relative statements, and include number of characters processed during training to enable fair compute comparisons to HF.
- Clarify whether AO training removed vanishing gradient issues measurably (e.g., gradient norms by layer).
- Reference missing related work: Mikolov’s RNNLMs; Pascanu et al. (2013) on RNN training difficulties and clipping.

Originality
Stacking recurrent layers is not novel per se, and multi-loss supervision has precedents. The paper’s originality lies in:
- Demonstrating competitive large-scale character-level modeling with simple tanh DRNNs trained via SGD, at a time when HF/multiplicative RNNs dominated this benchmark.
- Providing careful empirical analysis of emergent time scales and layer roles on a realistic task, including targeted long-range cues (parentheses).
While incremental, these contributions are meaningful.

Significance
The work is likely to be useful to practitioners and researchers:
- It suggests that architectural depth confers benefits even for simple tanh RNNs and that SGD with basic stabilization can be competitive, which is valuable for settings without second-order tooling.
- The analysis methods (typo perturbation, context reset, layer ablation at output) are broadly applicable to study temporal hierarchies and may inspire further diagnostics.
- That said, omission of LSTM/GRU baselines limits the impact; showing that stacked tanh RNNs close the gap to gated models would raise significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid empirical evidence that stacked RNNs learn a temporal hierarchy and exploit longer contexts.
- Competitive BPC on a challenging corpus with simple training.
- Clear, informative analyses and practical training recipe (incremental supervision).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited baselines (no LSTM/GRU/stacked LSTM) and only one seed.
- SOTA comparison not strictly comparable due to different character sets and unclear data pass counts.
- Some methodological details and citations missing; scope of hyperparameter exploration is narrow.

Suggestions
- Add at least a 2-layer LSTM baseline (similar parameter count) and, if possible, a stacked LSTM, to contextualize the benefit of depth vs gating.
- Report mean±std over multiple seeds or show learning curves across seeds.
- Provide exact BPC numbers and training tokens processed; consider also Penn Treebank char-level for comparability.
- Explore varying truncation lengths and analyze how layer time scales change.
- Cite Pascanu et al. (2013) and compare gradient clipping strategies.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a well-executed study with useful analyses demonstrating emergent temporal hierarchies in stacked RNNs and competitive performance with simple training. Strengthening baselines and clarifying comparability would make it a clear accept.
