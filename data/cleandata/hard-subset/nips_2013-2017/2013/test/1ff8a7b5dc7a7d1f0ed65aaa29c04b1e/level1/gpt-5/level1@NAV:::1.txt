This paper studies stacked recurrent architectures (“deep recurrent neural networks,” DRNNs) for character-level language modeling. Each layer is a tanh RNN receiving the hidden state from the layer below; the authors compare two output schemes: top-layer-only (DRNN-1O) and all-layers-to-output (DRNN-AO). Trained with SGD and gradient-norm normalization, with incremental layer-wise training for DRNN-1O, the models are evaluated on a 1.4B-character Wikipedia corpus under a fixed parameter budget (~4.9M). Results show DRNNs achieve bits-per-character comparable to multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization [19], and the authors provide analyses suggesting emergent time scales across layers using perturbation/ablation studies and a parenthesis-closing probe. The work relates to hierarchical RNNs [5,6] and stacked LSTMs used for speech [8], and to second-order training of RNNs [15,19], positioning its novelty in simple training and systematic analysis rather than architecture per se.

Quality: The experimental protocol is generally sound (matched parameter counts, BPTT length, burn-in). The time-scale and ablation studies are insightful and reproducible in principle. However, the empirical comparison set is narrow (only three model instantiations, no variance across seeds), and key baselines are missing—e.g., stacked LSTM/GRU with similar parameter count on the same dataset. The character set differs from [19], complicating strict comparability. Claims of “state-of-the-art for recurrent networks” would be stronger with standardized pre/post-processing and wall-clock/epoch-normalized training budgets.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized, with precise update equations and training details (window length, learning-rate schedule). Please report exact BPC numbers (not only relative ordering), and provide code or a configuration appendix (initialization, batching, gradient normalization magnitude) to aid reproducibility.

Originality: Stacking RNNs and hierarchical RNNs are not new [5,8], and adding outputs at intermediate layers is a known strategy to improve gradient flow. The novelty lies in (i) showing competitive performance with plain SGD and a simple schedule, and (ii) careful analyses of emergent time scales and long-range behaviors (e.g., parentheses).

Significance: Moderate. Demonstrating that simple training suffices to reach competitive performance is useful, and the analysis tools could inform future architectures. Broader impact would be higher with stronger baselines and cross-dataset validation.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear demonstration that stacked vanilla RNNs trained with SGD can be competitive with HF-trained MRNNs.
- Thoughtful analyses (layer ablations, perturbation-based time scales, generative probes) providing insight into temporal hierarchy.
- Simple, practical training recipe (including incremental layer-wise training).

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing comparisons to LSTM/GRU and stacked LSTM on the same data/compute.
- Limited hyperparameter exploration and no statistical variability.
- Comparability concerns due to character set and training budget differences.

Suggestions: Add matched LSTM/GRU baselines; report exact BPCs and training throughput; ablate gradient normalization and the incremental schedule; consider releasing code.
