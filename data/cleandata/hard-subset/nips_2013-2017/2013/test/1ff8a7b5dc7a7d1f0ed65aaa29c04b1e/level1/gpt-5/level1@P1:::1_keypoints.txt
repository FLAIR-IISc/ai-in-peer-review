- Paper studies stacked tanh RNNs (DRNNs) with two output variants: DRNN-1O (top-layer output) and DRNN-AO (outputs from all layers summed).  
- Trained with simple SGD, gradient normalization (unit-norm), and linear learning-rate decay to reach ~4.9M parameters.  
- Reports state-of-the-art bits-per-character (BPC) on a large Wikipedia character-level corpus, competitive with multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization.  
- Provides analyses of emergent temporal hierarchies via typo perturbations, context-switch tests, layer ablations, and a parentheses-closing probe.  
- Differs from prior stacked RNN work by using simple tanh RNNs with SGD at scale and offering systematic analysis of layer time scales on a real language task.  
- Methodology is clearly specified (BPTT length, sequence lengths, gradient norm, parameter matching, controlled compute budgets).  
- Comparisons include shallow RNN and two DRNN variants with multiple diagnostic analyses beyond leaderboard results.  
- Incremental supervised pretraining for DRNN-1O is a useful practical finding.  
- Weakness: limited baselines and ablations—only three models, one random seed, no LSTM/GRU or stacked LSTM comparisons.  
- Weakness: SOTA claims complicated by differing character sets and potential data-split differences; require reported training set sizes and test protocol confirmation.  
- Weakness: gradient “normalization” is similar to global clipping; should cite/compare to adaptive clipping (Pascanu et al., 2013).  
- Weakness: some design choices need justification (zero-initialized output weights, uniform layer widths) and hyperparameter exploration is lacking (truncation length, spectral radius).  
- Weakness: statistical robustness unclear—no variance across runs and mostly subjective text sample evaluation aside from BPC.  
- Clarity: paper well written and organized, but missing details on hidden-state handling across minibatches, sampling strategy, and use of dropout/regularization.  
- Clarity: ask for exact BPC numbers (train/valid/test), number of characters processed during training, and whether AO training measurably reduced vanishing gradients.  
- Missing related-work citations: Mikolov’s RNNLMs and Pascanu et al. (2013) on RNN training difficulties and clipping.  
- Originality: stacking and multi-loss supervision are not novel, but contribution lies in showing SGD-trained tanh DRNNs competitive at scale and in the empirical analysis of time scales.  
- Significance: useful to practitioners (depth benefits, simple training recipe) and provides broadly applicable analysis methods, but impact limited by omission of gated-model baselines.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong empirical evidence of temporal hierarchy, competitive BPC with simple training, and informative analyses plus practical training recipe.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited baselines/one seed, non-strictly comparable SOTA claim, missing methodological details and narrow hyperparameter search.  
- Suggestions: add at least a 2-layer LSTM (and stacked LSTM) baseline with similar parameter count, report mean±std over seeds, provide exact BPC and training tokens, consider Penn Treebank char-level for comparability, vary truncation lengths, and cite/compare gradient clipping strategies.  
- Recommendation: weak accept — solid study with useful analyses but needs stronger baselines and clarity for full acceptance.
