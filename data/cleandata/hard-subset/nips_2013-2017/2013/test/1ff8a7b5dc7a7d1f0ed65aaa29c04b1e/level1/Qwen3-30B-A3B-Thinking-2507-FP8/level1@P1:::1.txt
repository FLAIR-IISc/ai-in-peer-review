

# Peer Review: Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks

## Summary
This paper introduces a "Deep Recurrent Neural Network" (DRNN) architecture, which stacks multiple recurrent neural network layers where each layer receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input. The authors argue that this creates a natural temporal hierarchy, allowing the network to process information at multiple time scales simultaneously. The paper demonstrates that DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks on character-level language modeling tasks using only stochastic gradient descent, whereas previous state-of-the-art required more complex Hessian-free optimization. The authors also provide an in-depth analysis of emergent time scales across layers, showing that different layers operate at different temporal resolutions. This work builds on previous hierarchical RNN approaches [3,5,6,8] but focuses specifically on the architectural implications for temporal processing rather than just performance gains.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with appropriate experimental design and thorough analysis. The authors provide detailed experimental results on a standard Wikipedia text corpus, comparing DRNNs to standard RNNs and previous state-of-the-art methods. Their analysis of time scales through perturbation experiments (typos, context switching) is particularly strong, demonstrating how different layers operate at different temporal resolutions. The paper carefully acknowledges limitations (e.g., the DRNN-1O required a layer-wise training approach) and provides clear evidence that the temporal hierarchy is a learned property rather than an architectural artifact.

**Originality:** The DRNN architecture represents a novel combination of deep neural networks and recurrent networks. While hierarchical RNNs have been proposed before [3,5,6,8], this paper uniquely focuses on how stacking RNNs creates a natural temporal hierarchy, which is a significant conceptual contribution. The authors clearly articulate how their work differs from previous approaches, particularly noting that [8] focused on performance without architectural analysis. The paper's core insight—that DRNNs inherently create multiple time scales—represents a meaningful advancement in understanding recurrent network architectures.

**Significance:** The paper demonstrates that DRNNs can achieve state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks using simpler training methods (SGD vs. Hessian-free optimization), which is significant for practical applications. The analysis of emergent time scales provides new theoretical insights into how recurrent networks process temporal information. The parentheses-closing task provides concrete evidence of DRNNs' ability to model long-term dependencies, which is a substantial contribution to understanding recurrent network capabilities. This work could influence future research on recurrent architectures and training methods.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The architecture is well-explained with Figure 1, and the experimental setup is detailed enough to be reproducible. The analysis of time scales through perturbation experiments is particularly well-presented with Figure 3. The paper effectively connects the architecture to the temporal processing problem, making the contribution clear to readers.

## Weaknesses

**Quality:** While the paper is technically sound, the explanation of the DRNN-1O training approach (layer-wise training with incremental additions) is somewhat confusing. The paper doesn't clearly explain why this approach was necessary for DRNN-1O but not for DRNN-AO. The performance difference between DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO (with DRNN-1O performing slightly better) is not adequately explained, which is counterintuitive given the authors' argument that multiple output connections are beneficial.

**Originality:** The paper could better contextualize its contribution against recent NIPS work. While they reference [8] (which appeared at ICASSP 2013), they don't compare directly with the 2013 NIPS paper "Learning Recurrent Neural Networks with Hessian-Free Optimization" [15] which also addressed training of recurrent networks. A clearer distinction from this recent NIPS work would strengthen the originality claim.

**Significance:** The paper doesn't adequately address the computational cost of DRNNs compared to other architectures. While they mention training time (10 days per network), they don't compare this to the computational requirements of other state-of-the-art methods. The paper also doesn't discuss how DRNNs might scale to larger datasets or more complex tasks.

**Clarity:** Some technical details could be clarified. For example, the explanation of how the DRNN-AO architecture (all layers contributing to output) differs from DRNN-1O (only top layer contributing to output) in terms of training dynamics would benefit from more detail. The description of the "incremental layer-wise training" for DRNN-1O is particularly confusing.

## Recommendation

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Novel architecture that creates a natural temporal hierarchy
- State-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks using simple SGD
- Rigorous analysis of emergent time scales across layers
- Clear connection between architecture and temporal processing problem
- Significant theoretical insights into how recurrent networks process time series

**Con Acceptance:**
- Insufficient explanation for performance difference between DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO
- Limited comparison with recent NIPS work on recurrent network training
- Lack of discussion on computational costs and scalability
- Unclear explanation of the layer-wise training approach

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to understanding recurrent neural network architectures. The core insight about temporal hierarchy created by stacking RNNs is novel and well-supported by experiments. The paper achieves state-of-the-art results with simpler training methods, which is significant for practical applications. While the paper has some weaknesses in explaining certain aspects of the architecture and training, these are outweighed by the paper's technical soundness, originality, and significance. The authors have clearly demonstrated that DRNNs provide a more natural way to process time series with hierarchical structure.

I recommend acceptance. With minor revisions to clarify the training approach for DRNN-1O and better contextualize the work against recent NIPS publications, this paper would be an excellent contribution to the field. The insights about emergent time scales are particularly valuable for future work on recurrent network architectures.
