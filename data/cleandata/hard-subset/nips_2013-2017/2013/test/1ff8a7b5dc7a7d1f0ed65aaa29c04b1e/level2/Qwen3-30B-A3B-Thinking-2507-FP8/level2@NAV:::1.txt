

This paper proposes Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), where each layer is a recurrent network processing the hidden state of the previous layer, enabling hierarchical temporal processing. The main claims are: (1) DRNNs naturally capture multi-scale temporal hierarchies in time series; (2) They achieve state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks on character-level language modeling using only SGD; (3) Layer-wise time scales emerge during training, verified through perturbation analysis and parenthesis-closing tasks.  

The paper demonstrates compelling experimental support for claims (1) and (3). The time-scale analysis (Fig. 3) convincingly shows layer-specific memory decay and context length, with DRNN-AO exhibiting distinct bottom-layer short-term and top-layer long-term processing. The parenthesis-closing task (Fig. 4) further validates DRNNs’ ability to model long-term dependencies beyond standard RNNs (28% failure rate vs. DRNN-AO’s 12%). Performance gains (DRNN-1O: 1.37 BPC vs. RNN: 1.51 BPC) are statistically significant and competitive with Hessian-free baselines [19], though the paper understates the computational cost gap (Hessian-free trained on 8 GPUs vs. single-GPU SGD here).  

Strengths include a clear theoretical motivation for temporal hierarchies, well-executed ablation studies (e.g., layer removal in DRNN-AO), and insightful analysis of emergent time scales. However, the significance claim (2) is partially undermined by the lack of comparison to modern architectures (e.g., Transformers or Transformer-XL) that now dominate language modeling. The experimental scope is limited: only three network instantiations, no hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, and no analysis of DRNN scalability beyond 5 layers. The layer-wise training for DRNN-1O is presented as a necessity without theoretical justification, raising reproducibility concerns.  

The paper correctly cites foundational work (LSTM [11], hierarchical RNNs [5,8]) but misses key recent advances in hierarchical temporal models (e.g., [21] on multi-scale RNNs). While the time-scale analysis is novel and well-supported, the paper overstates the novelty of the architecture itself (stacked LSTMs were used in [8] for speech).  

To strengthen the contribution, the authors should: (1) Compare against Transformer-based baselines; (2) Analyze DRNN performance on tasks requiring explicit hierarchical processing (e.g., multi-resolution video); (3) Discuss why layer-wise training is needed for DRNN-1O. The core insight about emergent time scales is valuable, but the paper’s narrow experimental scope limits its impact. With these revisions, it could make a strong contribution to temporal modeling.
