

This paper proposes Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), where each layer is a recurrent network that processes the hidden state of the layer below, creating a temporal hierarchy. The authors argue that this architecture naturally captures multi-scale temporal dependencies in time series, unlike standard RNNs. They evaluate DRNNs on character-level language modeling (Wikipedia corpus), showing state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks using only SGD (previously requiring Hessian-free optimization), and analyze emergent time scales through perturbation experiments and a parentheses-closing task.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Originality & Significance:** The core idea of stacking RNNs to create explicit temporal hierarchies is novel for language modeling (extending [5,8] by providing deeper analysis). The paper convincingly demonstrates that DRNNs learn distinct time scales per layer (e.g., bottom layers handle short-term dependencies like character statistics, top layers capture long-term structures like parentheses), which is a significant insight into RNN dynamics. The SOTA results with simple SGD (BPC=1.15 for DRNN-1O vs. 1.16 for Hessian-free MRNN [19]) are impactful, as they challenge the assumption that complex optimization is necessary for high-performing RNNs.  
2. **Quality & Analysis:** The experimental design is rigorous. The typo-perturbation test (Fig 3) and parentheses-closing task (Fig 4) provide compelling evidence for the temporal hierarchy. The ablation studies (e.g., disabling layer outputs) effectively quantify each layer’s contribution, addressing a key gap in prior work [8]. The comparison to standard RNNs and DRNN variants (1O vs. AO) is well-motivated and clarifies architectural trade-offs.  
3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized, with clear figures (Fig 1, 3, 4) and concise notation. The motivation for DRNNs (Section 1) effectively contrasts them with standard RNNs and DNNs, setting up the novelty. The language modeling task is well-suited to validate the hypothesis, as character-level text inherently has multi-scale dependencies.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Methodological Gaps:** The DRNN-1O training procedure (layer-wise incremental training) is described briefly but lacks critical details. For example, how were the 5 layers added sequentially? Why did the top layer require a lower initial learning rate (η₀=0.25)? Without this, reproducibility is hampered. The claim that DRNN-1O outperforms DRNN-AO (Table 1) isn’t fully explained—why would output connections at all layers reduce performance? A brief discussion of this trade-off would strengthen the analysis.  
2. **Scope Limitations:** The paper focuses solely on character-level language modeling. While this is a strong validation task, it’s unclear whether the DRNN architecture generalizes to other domains (e.g., speech or video). The authors briefly mention future work on "tasks introduced in [11]" but provide no evidence that DRNNs outperform standard RNNs on those benchmarks. A single additional experiment on a different dataset (e.g., Penn Treebank for word-level modeling) would significantly bolster significance.  
3. **Reproducibility:** The training setup (Section 3.1) states networks were trained on "a single GPU" but omits key hyperparameters: batch size, sequence length (beyond 250 characters), and exact number of epochs. The statement "we only tested three network instantiations" (due to computational cost) limits statistical confidence—more trials would better justify the results.  

**Recommendation:**  
This is a strong paper with clear novelty, rigorous analysis, and impactful results. The core contribution—demonstrating that stacked RNNs create natural time scales and achieve SOTA with simple SGD—is significant for the field. The weaknesses are addressable in revision (e.g., clarifying training details, adding a brief generalization experiment). It aligns well with NIPS themes (deep learning, temporal modeling) and advances the understanding of RNN architectures beyond optimization.  

**Pro-accept arguments:** Novel architecture with interpretable temporal hierarchy, SOTA results using SGD (reducing reliance on complex optimization), thorough empirical analysis of time scales, and clear relevance to real-world time-series tasks.  
**Con-accept arguments:** Limited task scope, minor methodological ambiguities in training, and insufficient generalization evidence.  

**Decision:** Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding RNNs as hierarchical temporal processors. With minor clarifications (e.g., training details, scope expansion), it would be an excellent addition to NIPS.
