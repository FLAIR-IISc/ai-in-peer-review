Summary
This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) for sequence modeling, obtained by stacking recurrent layers where each layer receives the previous layer’s hidden state as input. Two output variants are considered: top-layer-only (DRNN-1O) and all-layers-to-output (DRNN-AO). The authors train with simple SGD and gradient normalization, introduce an incremental layer-wise training strategy for DRNN-1O, and evaluate on large-scale character-level language modeling on a Wikipedia corpus. With ~4.9M parameters, both DRNN variants approach or match the best previously reported RNN results on this dataset (multiplicative RNN trained with Hessian-free optimization). Beyond accuracy, the paper analyzes emergent time scales via perturbation experiments, layer ablations, and a focused study of long-term dependencies (closing parentheses), arguing that stacked RNNs naturally develop a temporal hierarchy.

Relation to prior work
Stacked/hierarchical RNNs have prior art (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio ’96; Fernández et al. ’07; stacked LSTMs for speech in Graves et al. ’13). Sutskever et al. and Martens & Sutskever studied character-level RNNs and second-order training (HF) and multiplicative RNNs (MRNN). Compared to these, the present paper emphasizes a simple stacked tanh-RNN trained with SGD and contributes a systematic analysis of per-layer time scales and contributions. The “all-layers-to-output” design resembles deep supervision ideas, though in a recurrent setting. The analysis complements prior performance-driven works on deep recurrent models.

Quality
- Methodology: The architecture is straightforward and well-defined. Training uses truncated BPTT (unrolled for 250 steps, backpropagating through 200) with gradient normalization and a linear learning-rate decay. The incremental layer-wise training for DRNN-1O is clearly described.
- Experiments: The evaluation uses a large corpus with ~1.4B characters and a fixed test set. Models are matched for parameter count (~4.9M). The reported BPC is competitive with MRNN+HF. The layer ablations, perturbation-based time-scale measurements, and parentheses experiments are informative and generally well executed.
- Limitations: Only three model instantiations were tried due to computational cost; no variance estimates are reported. A key baseline—single- and multi-layer LSTM (or gated RNNs)—is missing, as are comparisons to MRNN trained with first-order methods. The character set differs slightly from earlier work, which may affect BPC comparability. The data split and validation protocol are under-specified, and hyperparameter selection is limited. The truncated-BPTT setup with randomly sampled sequences likely resets hidden states between segments; an explicit statement and an analysis of its effect on long-term modeling would strengthen claims about very long dependencies.

Clarity
The paper is generally clearly written and organized. The architecture and training procedures are described concisely. The analysis sections (layer ablations, time-scale perturbations, parentheses) are easy to follow. A few details would benefit from clarification: whether hidden states are reset between sampled sequences; the exact character vocabulary mapping; whether a validation set was used; and the rationale for zero-initializing output layers.

Originality
Stacking RNNs is not new; novelty lies more in the training strategy for the top-layer-only variant, the explicit all-layers-to-output configuration, and especially the breadth of analyses tying layers to emergent time scales and long-term dependencies. While incremental, these analyses are valuable and not commonly presented at this scale.

Significance
The empirical result that a simple stacked tanh-RNN trained with SGD attains near state-of-the-art BPC with matched parameter counts (previously achieved with sophisticated HF training on MRNNs) is of practical interest. The analysis of temporal hierarchies provides insight likely to inform future deep recurrent model design and diagnostics. Broader impact is moderate given the missing LSTM/gated baselines, but the work advances understanding and offers a strong, simple baseline for character-level modeling.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architecture with competitive performance using first-order optimization.
- Careful parameter-matched comparisons and large-scale experiment.
- Insightful analyses: per-layer ablations, perturbation-based time scales, context switch sensitivity, and targeted long-range dependency (parentheses) study.
- The incremental layer-wise training strategy for DRNN-1O seems effective and is practically useful.

Weaknesses
- Missing key baselines (single/multi-layer LSTM, MRNN with SGD), limiting conclusions about relative merit.
- Limited experimental breadth (only three configurations, no variance, no validation details).
- Comparability issue due to different character set; the effect on BPC is asserted but not quantified.
- Truncated BPTT with likely hidden-state resets conflicts with claims about modeling sequences longer than backprop horizon; needs explicit handling or analysis.
- The all-layers-to-output approach affects optimization and interpretability; weightings across layers are not explored (e.g., learned vs fixed, regularization).

Questions for the authors
1) Are hidden states reset between randomly sampled sequences? If so, how does this affect long-range generalization, and did you try TBPTT with state carryover?
2) What is the exact dev/validation protocol and did you tune η0 or layer widths on a held-out set?
3) Can you quantify the impact of using 95 vs 86 characters on BPC by re-running evaluation with a mapped alphabet?
4) How sensitive are results to the gradient normalization strategy and the linear LR schedule?
5) Could you report results for a 1–2 layer LSTM with similar parameter counts to contextualize gains from depth vs gating?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong large-scale results with a simple method and matched parameter count.
- Novel, thorough analyses of emergent time scales and layer roles in DRNNs.
- Practical training insights (incremental layering, all-layers outputs) that others can adopt.

Cons for acceptance
- Architectural novelty is incremental given prior stacked/hierarchical RNNs.
- Missing standard baselines (LSTM) and limited ablations reduce the generality of conclusions.
- Some experimental details and comparability issues remain.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers competitive empirical results with a simple, accessible approach and provides insightful analyses of temporal hierarchy in deep RNNs. Addressing the questions above—especially clarifying sequence handling, adding an LSTM baseline, and quantifying the vocabulary effect—would further strengthen the contribution.
