This paper studies stacked recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) for character-level language modeling, proposing two variants: a single-output head from the top layer (DRNN-1O) and all-layers-to-output (DRNN-AO). Using simple SGD with gradient-norm normalization and an incremental layer-wise training schedule for DRNN-1O, the authors match state-of-the-art results for recurrent models of comparable size on a large Wikipedia corpus, previously achieved by multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free methods. Beyond accuracy, the paper analyzes emergent time scales across layers via perturbation experiments, context-switch tests, and bracket-closing behavior, arguing that stacked recurrence induces a temporal hierarchy. This connects to prior work on hierarchical RNNs (El Hihi & Bengio), stacked bi-LSTMs for speech (Graves et al.), and second-order training for RNNs (Martens & Sutskever), but places emphasis on architecture and analysis over training algorithms.

Quality: The methodology is generally sound, with clear model definitions, training details, and ablations (layer contribution tests). The analyses of time scales and long-range dependencies are insightful. However, the empirical support is limited to one dataset and three model instantiations, without variance across seeds or statistical tests. Important baselines (stacked LSTM/GRU; HF on stacked vanilla RNNs) are missing.

Clarity: The paper is well organized and readable. Equations and training schedule are explicit. Some reporting could be clearer (e.g., exact data passes for all models, gradient normalization vs clipping, wall-clock/compute budgets).

Originality: Stacking RNNs is not new, but combining all-layers outputs, the incremental training protocol, and the thorough temporal analysis constitute a useful contribution. The parenthesis case study and perturbation-based time-scale probes are novel and compelling.

Significance: Results suggest a simple, accessible alternative to second-order methods and provide tools to interpret temporal hierarchies. The impact would be higher with stronger baselines and broader evaluation.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid performance with simple SGD on a challenging corpus.
- Careful analyses demonstrating emergent multi-scale dynamics.
- Practical insights on training deep recurrence (incremental schedule, all-layers output).

Cons for acceptance:
- Narrow empirical scope; no comparisons to gated RNNs (LSTM/GRU).
- Limited statistical rigor (few runs, no error bars).
- Reproducibility hampered by long training and absence of code.

Constructive suggestions:
- Add strong baselines (stacked LSTM/GRU) and report multiple seeds.
- Provide compute metrics and clarify gradient handling vs clipping.
- Evaluate on additional corpora and report per-layer parameterization/compute trade-offs.
- Release code and scripts; extend analysis with quantitative measures of effective context per layer.

Overall, promising idea and analyses; with stronger baselines and broader experiments, this would be a clear accept.
