Summary
The paper studies stacked recurrent architectures in which each layer is itself a simple tanh RNN that receives the previous layer’s hidden state as input (deep recurrent neural networks, DRNNs). Two output topologies are explored: (a) output from the top layer only (DRNN-1O) trained with an incremental layer-wise schedule, and (b) outputs from all layers (DRNN-AO). Using only SGD with gradient-norm normalization and backpropagation through time, the work reports state-of-the-art bits-per-character (BPC) for recurrent networks on a large Wikipedia corpus under a comparable parameter budget to prior multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free methods [Sutskever et al., ICML’11; Martens & Sutskever, ICML’11/12]. Beyond accuracy, the paper analyzes emergent time scales across layers via perturbation experiments, context-switch tests, ablations of layer outputs, and a case study on long-range dependencies (closing parentheses), providing evidence that higher layers encode longer temporal context.

Relation to prior work: Hierarchical and stacked RNNs have a history (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio 1996; Fernández et al., IJCAI 2007; stacked LSTMs for speech, Graves et al., ICASSP 2013). The paper differs by (i) focusing on a simple stacked tanh-RNN architecture trained with plain SGD on a challenging character-level language modeling task, (ii) systematically comparing output-connection strategies, and (iii) providing analyses of learned time scales and dependencies.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architecture that bridges deep feedforward hierarchies and temporal modeling; easy to reproduce with modern toolkits.
- Competitive BPC on a large, realistic text corpus using first-order optimization, comparing favorably to HF-trained MRNNs with a similar parameter budget.
- Careful analyses: ablations of per-layer outputs, perturbation-based time-scale characterization, and concrete long-range behavior (parentheses). These provide insight beyond leaderboard results.
- Training insights: the incremental layer-wise supervised schedule appears effective for DRNN-1O and is clearly documented.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Fairness and comparability of experiments: DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO differ not only in output topology but also in training schedules and total passes through data (about 10 vs 7). This confounds the conclusion that DRNN-1O is inherently better; the advantage may stem from more compute or the pretraining schedule.
- Benchmark comparability: results are compared to prior work using a slightly different character set (95+1 vs 86). While claimed negligible, BPC comparisons across alphabets and splits can be sensitive. A matched setup or normalization (e.g., bits-per-byte) would strengthen SOTA claims.
- Baselines: no comparison to stacked LSTM/biLSTM on the same corpus and budget, although related speech results exist [Graves et al., 2013]. Given the thesis about hierarchical time scales, a stacked LSTM baseline would be informative.
- Limited ablation and variance reporting: only three configurations due to computational constraints and apparently single random initializations. Absence of variability estimates makes it difficult to assess robustness.
- Analysis methodology: time-scale inferences rely on Euclidean distances in hidden-state space, which can be scale-dependent; complementary measures tied directly to predictive uncertainty (e.g., change in cross-entropy contribution per layer) would make conclusions crisper.

Quality
Overall technically sound. Training details are explicit (BPTT length, batch construction, gradient normalization, learning-rate schedule). The incremental layer-wise procedure is well described. One minor issue: Equation (3) appears to contain a typographical sign error in the learning-rate schedule; it likely intends η0(1 − j/T). Claims are mostly supported by experiments; SOTA statements would benefit from stricter dataset parity.

Clarity
Well written and organized. The motivation for hierarchical time scales and the architectural diagrams are clear. The analysis sections are accessible and insightful. Suggestions: clarify the exact gradient normalization threshold and whether clipping vs normalization to unit norm is used; specify random seed handling; and make explicit the exact Wikipedia preprocessing to facilitate replication.

Originality
Stacking RNNs is not new per se (El Hihi & Bengio; Graves et al.), but the combination of simple tanh DRNNs trained with SGD on large-scale character modeling, together with the presented analyses of emergent time scales, constitutes a useful and timely contribution. The incremental supervised layer-wise training for DRNN-1O is a pragmatic twist.

Significance
Character-level language modeling remains a challenging benchmark. Showing that stacked simple RNNs with SGD can match HF-trained MRNNs at a similar parameter budget is practically significant. The analysis of temporal hierarchies is likely to influence design and diagnostics of deep recurrent models. Broader impact would be strengthened by stronger baselines and matched comparisons.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Equalize training budget between DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO (same total updates/passes), or report results normalized by compute, to disentangle topology from schedule effects.
- Provide a matched evaluation against a stacked LSTM with comparable parameters and training budget, or carefully scope SOTA claims to “tanh-RNNs” if LSTM results are unavailable.
- Clarify and correct Eq. (3) and detail the gradient normalization (threshold, whether per-parameter-group or global).
- Address alphabet comparability: either report results on the 86-char setup of [Sutskever et al. 2011] or provide a conversion demonstrating minimal impact.

Nice-to-have (optional):
- Report variability across at least 3 random seeds for one configuration (mean±std BPC).
- Add a small study varying depth vs width under a fixed parameter budget to support the “depth induces time-scale hierarchy” claim.
- Complement hidden-state distance analyses with output-centric measures (e.g., per-layer contribution to cross-entropy under perturbations).
- Release trained models and code to aid reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong empirical performance with a simple, accessible approach.
- Valuable analyses elucidating how depth yields temporal hierarchies and long-range behavior.
- Clear presentation and practical training insights (incremental supervised schedule).

Arguments against acceptance
- Incomplete baseline coverage and compute-matched comparisons weaken the strength of conclusions.
- SOTA claim relies on slightly different data/character set and lacks variance estimates.
- Missing comparison to stacked LSTMs on the same task limits significance relative to contemporary recurrent architectures.

Overall, the work offers a solid empirical and analytical contribution on deep recurrent architectures for character-level modeling, with actionable training insights. With tighter comparisons and clarifications, it would be a strong fit for NIPS.
