Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) formed by stacking vanilla RNN layers, where each layer is recurrent and receives the previous layer’s hidden state as input. Two output schemes are considered: only the top layer to output (DRNN-1O) and outputs from all layers summed before the softmax (DRNN-AO). Using SGD with gradient normalization and incremental deepening for DRNN-1O, the authors report state-of-the-art bits-per-character (BPC) on a large Wikipedia character-level language modeling corpus with ~5M parameters, comparable to multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization (Sutskever et al., ICML’11). Beyond performance, the paper analyzes emergent temporal hierarchies via (i) layer ablations, (ii) “typo perturbation” and context-switch experiments to estimate layer-wise time scales, and (iii) qualitative and quantitative tests on closing parentheses, indicating long-range dependencies.

Stacked/“deep” RNNs and hierarchical RNNs have a long history (El Hihi & Bengio ’95; Fernández et al. ’05; Graves et al. ’13), including stacked LSTMs achieving strong results in speech (Graves et al., ICASSP’13). Multiplicative RNNs and HF training (Martens & Sutskever ’11) are the closest prior on this dataset. The main novelty here is showing that simple stacked tanh RNNs trained with SGD can be competitive, coupled with a careful analysis of emergent time scales and the effect of deep supervision (outputs from all layers) vs. incremental deepening.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-motivated architecture and training procedures. The incremental layer-wise training for DRNN-1O is simple and effective.
- Competitive results on a large, challenging dataset using plain SGD, narrowing the gap with HF-trained MRNNs at similar parameter counts.
- Thoughtful empirical analyses probing time scales (typo/context) and long-range behavior (parentheses), with plausible and interpretable findings (e.g., higher layers encode longer contexts; DRNNs leverage longer context than single-layer RNNs).
- Useful ablation by removing layer outputs in DRNN-AO to assess direct predictive contribution across depths.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Novelty is incremental: stacked RNNs and hierarchical recurrent architectures are not new; deep supervision (outputs at multiple layers) has been explored in other settings. The contribution is primarily empirical and analytical rather than architectural.
- Baselines are limited. There is no LSTM or stacked LSTM baseline on the same data/hyperparameter budget, which is a critical omission given LSTM’s role in modeling long-term dependencies. The single vanilla RNN baseline is insufficient to establish significance.
- Comparability of BPC claims is muddied by differences in character set (95 vs 86) and potential differences in preprocessing/splits. The paper asserts negligible impact but does not quantify it; “state-of-the-art” should be qualified or complemented with results on a standardized benchmark (e.g., enwik8).
- Limited runs and no variance estimates. The authors trained only three models and state that initialization variance is small, without empirical support. This weakens confidence in fine-grained differences (e.g., DRNN-1O vs DRNN-AO).
- The ablation “zeroing a layer’s output” can be confounded by inter-layer synergies and compensations. More principled probes (e.g., training separate linear readouts from frozen layer states) would strengthen conclusions about layer roles.
- The observed time-scale hierarchy depends strongly on training scheme (e.g., DRNN-1O’s long time scales across top layers likely induced by incremental deepening). This suggests the hierarchy is learned and training-dependent rather than inherently architectural; the paper acknowledges this but could analyze it more rigorously.
- Some training choices need justification (zero-initialized output weights, fixed BPTT window of 200, single depth/width trade-off). The claim that DRNNs remember >500 characters is interesting, but it arises without gradient signals at those horizons; more discussion would be helpful.

Quality:
Overall technically sound. Experimental design for time-scale analysis is reasonable and clearly described. However, the empirical evidence for “state-of-the-art” is not fully controlled due to missing LSTM baselines and dataset mismatch. Claims about temporal hierarchy are supported but partly confounded by training strategy.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and organized; equations and training details are mostly sufficient for reproduction. Some reference entries are incomplete. It would help to more explicitly detail preprocessing, hidden/state initialization, and any regularization.

Originality:
Moderate. The architecture is a straightforward stack of vanilla RNNs; the main original aspects are (i) demonstrating competitive performance with simple SGD and (ii) the analysis toolkit for emergent time scales and long-range effects. Deep supervision in RNNs is a minor tweak.

Significance:
Moderate. Results will be of interest to practitioners seeking strong char-level models without second-order optimization, and to researchers studying temporal representations in deep RNNs. Broader impact would be strengthened by stronger baselines and standardized benchmarks.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add LSTM and stacked LSTM baselines (similar parameter counts) trained with SGD/gradient clipping to contextualize gains from depth vs gating.
- Report results on a standardized dataset (e.g., enwik8 with 205 tokens) and/or match the 86-char setup to ensure comparability; quantify the impact of character set differences.
- Provide multiple runs with mean±std BPC to assess variance; include training/validation curves to discuss overfitting.
- Explore depth/width trade-offs at fixed parameter budget; include shallower/wider and deeper/narrower variants.
- Replace or complement layer-output ablations with linear probes trained on frozen layer states to measure predictive information across depths.
- Analyze layer dynamics more directly (spectral norms/Jacobians per layer, effective memory via linear system identification) to corroborate time-scale claims.
- Justify/compare training choices (zero vs small random output init; gradient normalization vs clipping; different BPTT lengths).
- Release code/configs to facilitate replication.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear empirical evidence that stacked vanilla RNNs trained with SGD can be competitive on large-scale char modeling.
- Interesting and well-executed analyses of temporal hierarchy and long-range behavior.
- Simple training recipe (including incremental deepening) that others can adopt.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited novelty beyond careful empirical study.
- Missing strong baselines (LSTM/stacked LSTM), and comparability caveats diminish the strength of the “state-of-the-art” claim.
- Some analyses (layer ablations) are suggestive but not definitive; dependence on training scheme complicates interpretation.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The empirical analysis is insightful and the results are practically useful, but adding stronger baselines and addressing comparability would substantially strengthen the paper.
