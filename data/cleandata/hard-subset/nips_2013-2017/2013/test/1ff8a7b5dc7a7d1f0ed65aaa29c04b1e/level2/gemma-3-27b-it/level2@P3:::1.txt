This paper investigates the effect of a hierarchical structure of recurrent neural networks (RNNs) on processing time series data, specifically character-level language modeling. The core idea is to stack RNNs, where each layer receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input, enabling hierarchical processing and capturing multi-scale temporal dependencies. The work demonstrates state-of-the-art performance using a relatively simple training method (SGD) and provides an analysis of emergent time scales within the network.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of standard RNNs in handling hierarchical temporal structures. It effectively connects the success of deep neural networks to their hierarchical processing capabilities, and argues for extending this concept to the recurrent domain. The introduction provides a good overview of related work, including hierarchical RNN architectures [3, 5, 6] and stacked bi-directional LSTMs [8], highlighting the novelty of the presented approach through its emphasis on analysis alongside performance. The proposed DRNN architecture is straightforward and well-defined, and the mathematical formulation is clear. 

A significant strength of the paper lies in the empirical evaluation. Achieving state-of-the-art results on a challenging character-level language modeling task is a substantial contribution. The detailed analysis of emergent time scales, using both typo perturbation and context switching experiments, offers valuable insights into the network's internal dynamics. The visualizations of layer contributions during parenthesis matching are particularly compelling and demonstrate the network's ability to learn long-range dependencies. The inclusion of ablation studies, such as removing layer outputs, further strengthens the analysis.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the related work section covers relevant literature, it could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of existing hierarchical RNN approaches. Specifically, the connection to the work in [5] which explicitly addresses multi-scale processing via subsampling, feels somewhat superficial. The justification for the chosen network size (approximately 4.9 million parameters) is not fully clear; a more thorough exploration of the effect of network capacity would be beneficial. 

The training setup section is quite dense and contains several implementation details (e.g., gradient normalization, layer-wise training for DRNN-1O) that could be better explained or moved to an appendix. The motivation behind the specific learning rate schedule and initialization schemes is not always clear. The comparison to Hessian-free optimization [19] is valuable, but it would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the computational cost and scalability of both methods. The results concerning parenthesis matching, while interesting, are somewhat limited in scope; exploring other linguistic structures or tasks that require long-range dependency modeling could enhance the impact.

The paper’s clarity is generally good, but the sheer number of details, particularly in the training setup, can make it challenging to follow. More concise writing and better organization of the experimental setup would improve readability. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   State-of-the-art performance on a challenging task.
*   Novel application of deep architectures to recurrent networks.
*   In-depth analysis of emergent time scales.
*   Compelling visualizations of network behavior (e.g., parenthesis matching).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Related work discussion could be more nuanced.
*   Justification for network size is lacking.
*   Training setup section is overly dense and complex.
*   Limited scope of some experiments (e.g., parenthesis matching).

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of recurrent neural networks. The proposed DRNN architecture, combined with a thorough empirical evaluation and insightful analysis, demonstrates the benefits of hierarchical processing for time series data. While some aspects of the presentation and experimental setup could be improved, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. The paper is suitable for NIPS, and the authors have made a convincing case for the potential of deep recurrent networks.

**Recommendations:**

*   **Essential:** Clarify the rationale behind the chosen network size and explore the effect of varying the number of layers and neurons per layer. Simplify the training setup section, potentially moving detailed implementation specifics to an appendix.
*   **Important:** Provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed approach to existing hierarchical RNN architectures, specifically addressing the differences and similarities in their design and performance.
*   **Optional:** Expand the experimental evaluation to include additional tasks or linguistic structures that require long-range dependency modeling. Investigate the computational cost and scalability of the proposed approach compared to Hessian-free optimization in more detail.
