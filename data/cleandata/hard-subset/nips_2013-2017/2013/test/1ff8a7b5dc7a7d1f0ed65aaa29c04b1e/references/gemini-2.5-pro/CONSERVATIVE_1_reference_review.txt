**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes and analyzes a "deep recurrent neural network" (DRNN), which is constructed by stacking multiple recurrent layers, such that the hidden state of each layer serves as the input sequence for the layer above it. The authors motivate this architecture by arguing that it naturally introduces a temporal hierarchy, allowing different layers to operate on different time scales, which is beneficial for tasks with complex temporal dependencies like character-level language modeling. Two variants are explored: one with an output only from the top layer (DRNN-1O) and another where all layers contribute to the output (DRNN-AO). The models are evaluated on a large Wikipedia text corpus, where they are shown to outperform a standard, wide RNN with a comparable number of parameters. Notably, the DRNN-1O model achieves performance competitive with the state-of-the-art result from Sutskever et al. (2011), which was obtained using a more complex model and a second-order optimization method (Hessian-Free). The paper's main contribution is not just the model's performance but also a series of detailed analyses aimed at demonstrating the emergence of a temporal hierarchy and the model's ability to capture long-range dependencies.

**Strengths**

1.  **Thorough Empirical Analysis:** The primary strength of this work is the extensive set of experiments designed to understand the model's behavior. The authors go beyond simply reporting a final performance metric. The analyses—measuring the decay of perturbations in hidden states (Fig. 3, left), quantifying the effective context length (Fig. 3, right), and the detailed case study on closing parentheses—provide compelling evidence that the stacked architecture encourages a hierarchy of time scales. This kind of analysis is valuable and often missing in papers focused purely on performance.

2.  **Strong Results with a Simple Optimizer:** The paper demonstrates that a relatively straightforward architecture, trained with standard SGD and gradient normalization, can achieve performance on par with more sophisticated models (MRNN) trained with powerful second-order methods. This is a significant result, suggesting that architectural design can be a viable alternative to complex optimizers for overcoming difficult training landscapes.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The model is described precisely, and the experimental setup is detailed. The figures effectively illustrate the key findings of the analysis.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The core idea of stacking recurrent layers is not new. As the authors themselves note, stacked bi-directional LSTMs were used to achieve state-of-the-art results in speech recognition (Graves et al., 2013). While the analysis provided here is novel and insightful, the architectural contribution itself is incremental. The paper would be stronger if it more clearly positioned its contribution as an analysis of an existing architectural paradigm rather than the introduction of a fundamentally new one.

2.  **Absence of a Crucial Baseline:** The most significant methodological flaw is the lack of a comparison against a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network. LSTMs were specifically designed to handle long-range dependencies and are the established standard for such tasks. A single-layer, wide LSTM would be a much stronger baseline than the vanilla RNN presented. Without this comparison, it is difficult to conclude whether the observed performance gains stem from the "deep" hierarchical structure or simply from creating a model that is more effective at mitigating the vanishing/exploding gradient problem than a simple RNN. The benefits of the DRNN could potentially be replicated or exceeded by a standard LSTM.

3.  **Ad-Hoc Training Procedure:** The best-performing model, DRNN-1O, requires a complex, incremental layer-wise training strategy that feels heuristic. The authors state this was "necessary to reach good performance," which suggests that the architecture is inherently difficult to train end-to-end. The fact that the simpler end-to-end trained DRNN-AO performs worse supports this concern. Established models like LSTMs do not typically require such specialized training schemes. This reliance on a multi-stage, manually-tuned procedure reduces the practical appeal and generality of the proposed method.

4.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** The authors report training only three models due to high computational cost, justifying this by claiming that "there is very little difference in performance for different initialisations." For models of this scale and complexity, this is a very strong claim that is not substantiated by evidence. A more rigorous evaluation would involve multiple runs to establish variance and ensure the results are robust.

**Critique based on NIPS criteria**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound for the most part, but the experimental design has a major weakness in its choice of baselines. The analysis is of high quality, but the claims about the superiority of the DRNN architecture are not fully supported without a comparison to LSTMs. The reliance on a specific, non-standard training procedure for the best model also raises concerns.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The exposition of the model, training, and analysis is a key strength.

*   **Originality:** The originality of the architecture is low. However, the originality of the *analysis*—providing quantitative and qualitative evidence for the emergence of a temporal hierarchy in stacked RNNs—is high. The paper's contribution should be viewed through this lens.

*   **Significance:** The finding that a deep architecture trained with SGD can match a model trained with Hessian-Free optimization is significant. It reinforces the importance of architectural priors in machine learning. The analysis techniques could also serve as a useful blueprint for future work on understanding deep sequence models. However, the overall significance is tempered by the methodological concerns mentioned above. If an off-the-shelf LSTM performs just as well, the practical impact of this specific DRNN architecture would be limited.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents an interesting analysis and strong empirical results. The key contribution is the detailed investigation into the internal dynamics of stacked RNNs. However, the work is let down by limited architectural novelty and, more critically, the omission of a state-of-the-art baseline (LSTM). The special training procedure required for the best model also complicates the story.

I am on the borderline, but I lean towards acceptance on the condition that the authors address these points. Specifically, the paper would be much stronger if the authors:
1.  Acknowledge the limited architectural novelty more directly and frame the paper primarily as an *analysis* of deep recurrent structures.
2.  Add results for a properly tuned LSTM baseline with a similar parameter count. This is essential for contextualizing the performance of the DRNN.
3.  Provide more discussion and justification for the layer-wise training scheme. Is it a necessary evil, and what does it imply about the optimization landscape of these models?

Without these additions, the paper's conclusions, while intriguing, remain unproven against established and powerful alternatives.
