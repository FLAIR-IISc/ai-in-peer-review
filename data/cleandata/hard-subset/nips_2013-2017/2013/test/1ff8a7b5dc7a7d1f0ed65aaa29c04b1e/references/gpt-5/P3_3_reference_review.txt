Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies stacked recurrent neural networks—“deep recurrent neural networks” (DRNNs)—for character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus. Each layer is a vanilla tanh RNN; higher layers receive the previous hidden state of the lower layer. Two output designs are compared: DRNN-1O (only the top layer connects to the softmax) and DRNN-AO (all layers connect, contributions summed before softmax). The authors train with SGD, gradient normalization, and a linear learning-rate decay. They analyze: (i) performance vs. a single-layer RNN at matched parameter budget, (ii) layer output ablations, (iii) per-layer time scales via hidden-state perturbation and context-switching experiments, and (iv) long-term dependency behavior via parenthesis prediction and generation.

Stacked/Hierarchical RNNs are not new (El Hihi & Bengio 1996; Fernández/Graves/Schmidhuber 2005; Graves et al. stacked LSTMs for speech). Sutskever et al. (2011) introduced multiplicative RNNs with Hessian-free (HF) optimization for the same dataset. The present work differs by showing that simple stacked tanh RNNs trained with SGD can match prior RNN results at comparable parameter counts, and by providing detailed analyses that suggest emergent temporal hierarchies across layers. The “all outputs” variant is similar in spirit to deep supervision/auxiliary classifiers (though those references postdate some of this work), here motivated as a way to alleviate vanishing gradients and enable interpretability.

Quality:
- Methodology: The model definition is clear. The training uses BPTT on sequences of length 250 with a 50-step warmup; batches of 75 random sequences. The gradient is normalized by its L2 norm, and a linear learning-rate decay is used. However, Eq. (3) appears to have a typographical error: θ(j+1)=θ(j)−η0(−j/T)∇θ/||∇θ|| presumably should be θ(j+1)=θ(j)−η0(1−j/T)∇θ/||∇θ||. Please clarify. Also, is the gradient always normalized to unit norm (not clipped to a threshold)? If so, that effectively enforces a fixed step size per update, which is unusual; a comparison to standard gradient clipping would be informative.
- Experiments: The authors train three models with ~4.9M parameters: single-layer RNN, 5-layer DRNN-1O, and 5-layer DRNN-AO. At this scale, DRNNs outperform the simple RNN and approach the MRNN+HF results reported by Sutskever et al. The ablations (zeroing per-layer outputs) and perturbation/context-switch analyses are carefully designed and insightful, suggesting layer-wise differences in time scales and the impact of the training regime (the incremental supervised pretraining for DRNN-1O appears to imprint long time scales on upper layers).
- Limitations: Only one dataset and three settings are tested; no comparison to LSTM/stacked LSTM, which is a major omission given the topic and prior evidence that gating improves long-term dependencies. The dataset and preprocessing (95 vs. 86 characters) differ from prior work; while the authors argue the difference is negligible, BPC comparability remains uncertain without controlled re-evaluation. The claim of “state-of-the-art for recurrent networks” should be bounded more carefully (same data split, same character set, same budget). No variance across random seeds or hyperparameters, no validation set or early stopping, and no regularization analysis. The random sampling of sequences for BPTT breaks long-range continuity between mini-batches; discussing whether hidden states are reset vs. carried over would help interpret “long memory” claims.
- Analysis: The time-scale experiments are a strong point, but the Euclidean distance between hidden states can be scale-dependent; consider normalizing per neuron or reporting cosine distances. The generative parenthesis experiments are compelling; testing on synthetic data with known nesting distributions would strengthen claims. The “pseudo-stable attractor” interpretation is plausible but speculative.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The architectural and training details are provided at a useful level. A few issues:
- Fix Eq. (3) and specify whether gradients are clipped or normalized, and to what threshold.
- Clarify whether hidden states are reset between random sequences and how BPTT truncation interacts with training dynamics.
- Provide the exact BPC numbers and standard deviations; explicitly reconcile the different character sets with prior results.
- Minor typos and footnote placement detract slightly from readability.

Originality:
Architecturally, stacking RNNs and adding outputs at multiple depths are incremental ideas relative to prior hierarchical and deep RNN work. The supervised, incremental layer-wise training is a pragmatic twist reminiscent of layer-wise pretraining, but again not conceptually new. The main novelty lies in the empirical demonstration that simple stacked tanh RNNs trained with SGD can be competitive at scale and in the thorough analysis of emergent time scales and long-term behavior. This analysis component is the most original and valuable contribution.

Significance:
Character-level modeling is an important benchmark. Showing that SGD-trained stacked vanilla RNNs approach MRNN+HF performance is practically meaningful. The time-scale and ablation analyses provide insights likely to inform future architectures and training practices. However, the absence of LSTM comparisons limits the impact; many practitioners would want to know whether DRNNs offer advantages over stacked LSTMs at the same parameter budget and training cost. The work’s significance thus hinges on its analyses more than on SOTA claims.

Pros:
- Clear, simple architecture; strong empirical performance with SGD at scale.
- Thoughtful analyses: per-layer ablations, perturbation tests, context-length effects, and parenthesis behavior.
- Evidence of emergent temporal hierarchy and of training schedule shaping time scales.
- Detailed training setup; reproducible in principle.

Cons:
- Limited novelty in architecture; missing direct comparisons to LSTM/stacked LSTM.
- Comparability of BPC to prior work is imperfect (different character sets/splits).
- Sparse hyperparameter exploration; no variance reporting; only one dataset.
- Some methodological ambiguities (Eq. 3, gradient normalization vs clipping, state resets).
- Conclusions about “unlimited memory” should be tempered; nested parentheses failure suggests limits.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add baselines: single- and multi-layer LSTMs at matched parameter counts, and possibly MRNN with SGD.
- Report multiple runs and standard deviations; include a validation set and hyperparameter search procedure.
- Compare gradient normalization to gradient clipping; ablate the incremental layer-wise pretraining for DRNN-1O.
- Standardize evaluation to a common benchmark split/character set (e.g., enwik8) for fair comparison.
- Deepen analysis with normalized distance metrics and probing classifiers that predict long-range tokens from per-layer states.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The architectural novelty is modest, but the empirical results and especially the careful analyses of temporal hierarchies and long-term behavior provide useful insights for the community.

Confidence: High (familiar with RNN training and character-level modeling).
