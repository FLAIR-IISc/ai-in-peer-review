Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) formed by stacking vanilla tanh RNN layers and examines two output configurations: (i) DRNN-1O, where only the top layer connects to the softmax output, and (ii) DRNN-AO, where all layers connect to the output (deep supervision across time). Training uses BPTT with stochastic gradient descent, global gradient normalization, and a linear learning-rate decay. For DRNN-1O, the authors adopt an incremental layer-wise training regime where a new top layer is added and becomes the sole output layer at each stage.

The main application is large-scale character-level language modeling on a 1.4B-character Wikipedia corpus; the metric is bits-per-character (BPC). The paper compares a single-layer RNN (~2.1k units) to 5-layer DRNNs (~4.9M parameters total) and reports performance comparable to the multiplicative RNN (MRNN) trained with Hessian-free optimization (Sutskever et al., ICML 2011), at a similar parameter budget but using only SGD on a single GPU. The paper further analyzes learned time scales via hidden-state perturbation experiments (typo injection and context switching), layer ablations at the output, and a qualitative/quantitative study of closing parentheses as an example of long-range dependence.

This builds on prior hierarchical/stacked RNN work (El Hihi & Bengio, NIPS 1995; Graves et al., ICASSP 2013) and character-level modeling with MRNNs (Sutskever et al., ICML 2011). The idea that stacking induces longer effective time constants at higher layers is known, but this paper provides targeted analyses and empirical evidence on a large-scale language task.

Strengths
- Empirical contribution: On a large Wikipedia dataset, stacked vanilla RNNs trained with simple SGD achieve BPC comparable to prior state-of-the-art recurrent models trained with second-order methods, at similar parameter counts. This is practically relevant.
- Architectural analysis: Clear experiments suggest emergence of a temporal hierarchy—lower layers respond to short-term perturbations while higher layers sustain longer memories. The analyses (typo perturbation, context-switch BPC degradation, layer-wise ablation) are informative.
- Training insights: The incremental layer-wise training for DRNN-1O and the use of output connections at all layers (for DRNN-AO) are reasonable strategies to mitigate vanishing gradients and to supervise each layer. The gradient normalization heuristic is simple and effective.
- Qualitative evaluation: The parentheses experiments and text-generation examples provide intuitive evidence of long-range modeling (including a quantitative success rate for closing parentheses).

Weaknesses
- Comparability of results: Reported BPC comparisons to prior work are not fully apples-to-apples due to differences in character vocabularies (95 vs. 86), dataset preprocessing, and splits. The paper acknowledges this but still frames results as “state-of-the-art,” which would benefit from matched settings or a re-run of published baselines under the exact setup.
- Missing baselines: No comparison to LSTM/stacked LSTM (e.g., Graves et al.) or GRU models on the same data. Given the prominence of gated RNNs for long-term dependencies, this limits the significance of the claims about DRNNs’ advantages.
- Limited experimental breadth/variance: Only three model instantiations were trained, with no repeated runs or variance reporting. This makes it hard to assess robustness to initialization and hyperparameters.
- Ablation interpretability: The summed pre-softmax outputs from all layers (DRNN-AO) make ablations informative but potentially confounded (e.g., compensatory biases across layers). Some care is taken to note this, but more controlled analyses (e.g., retraining after ablation or orthogonalization of contributions) would strengthen conclusions.
- Some training details merit clarification: Output weights are initialized to zero, which initially blocks gradient flow to hidden states; while this is workable, a brief justification or comparison to small random init would help. Justification for initialization scales (e.g., Z1 ∼ N(0,1)) and their interaction with gradient normalization would also be useful.

Quality
Overall technically sound. The training procedure (BPTT with global-norm scaling), evaluation protocol, and analyses are coherent. The main caveats are the comparability to prior SOTA and omission of standard gated baselines. The perturbation-based time-scale measurement is sensible, though Euclidean distances of hidden states can depend on scaling; the corroborating context-switch BPC analysis shores this up.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful equations and descriptions of the two architectures and training regimes. A few points would improve clarity: precise dataset preprocessing details, exact BPC numbers in a matched table with standard deviations if possible, and more explicit computational cost (epochs, tokens processed) for fairer comparison to HF-trained MRNNs.

Originality
Moderate. Stacking RNNs and multi-scale temporal behavior have been explored; the novelty lies in the combination of (i) deep supervision at all layers for RNNs, (ii) a pragmatic incremental training schedule for top-only outputs, and (iii) comprehensive analyses of layerwise time scales on large-scale character modeling.

Significance
Moderate. Demonstrating that simple SGD on stacked vanilla RNNs can match prior HF-trained recurrent models is practically valuable. The time-scale analyses may inform future architectural choices. However, the lack of comparisons to LSTM/GRU diminishes broader impact.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Strong, large-scale empirical results with simple training.
- Convincing analyses of temporal hierarchies in stacked RNNs.
- Useful training insights (deep supervision; incremental top-only training).

Cons:
- No controlled comparisons to LSTM/GRU on the same corpus.
- Non-strict comparability to prior SOTA due to dataset/vocabulary differences.
- Limited ablation/reproducibility evidence (few runs, no variance).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines with (stacked) LSTM or GRU under the same setup.
- Provide matched experimental conditions for MRNN or re-evaluate DRNNs on the exact dataset/vocabulary used in prior work.
- Report multiple seeds and variance; include validation splits and model selection criteria.
- Compare zero vs. small-random output weight initialization; report sensitivity to BPTT length and gradient-norm threshold.
- Consider additional diagnostics (e.g., per-layer effective receptive fields via influence functions; gating/skip connections) and more challenging long-range tasks.

Overall
A solid empirical and analytical study of stacked vanilla RNNs for character-level language modeling. The work is technically sound and clearly presented, with informative analyses of temporal hierarchies. The main limitations are missing gated-RNN baselines and imperfect comparability to prior SOTA.
