Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) formed by stacking simple tanh RNN layers, with two output wiring schemes: output only from the top layer (DRNN-1O) and outputs from all layers summed at the softmax (DRNN-AO). The authors train on a large Wikipedia character-level language modeling task and analyze (i) performance vs. a single-layer RNN and prior MRNN/Hessian-free results, and (ii) the temporal behavior of layers via perturbation tests, context-switch experiments, and a focused analysis of long-term dependencies (parentheses). They show that DRNNs achieve competitive bits-per-character (BPC) to MRNNs trained with Hessian-free methods at similar parameter budgets using SGD, and they provide evidence that stacked RNNs learn distinct time scales across layers, with upper layers contributing longer memory. The work builds on longstanding ideas of hierarchical/stacked RNNs (El Hihi & Bengio; Graves et al.’s stacked bi-LSTMs) but shifts focus to simple tanh RNNs, training regimes, and detailed behavioral analysis.

Quality
Strengths:
- The experimental protocol is described in fair detail (sequence length, BPTT truncation, parallel sequences, parameter counts, initialization). The use of gradient normalization stabilizes training and is clearly specified (Eq. 3).
- The analyses are thoughtful and go beyond scores: ablations of per-layer output contributions, perturbation-based time-scale measurement, context-switch recovery, and parentheses tracking. These collectively support the claim that stacked RNNs learn a temporal hierarchy and leverage long context for prediction.
- The comparison to prior SOTA MRNN on the same corpus size/parameter budget is informative; showing that simple tanh DRNNs trained with SGD can approach MRNN+HF results is practically relevant.

Weaknesses:
- Limited experimental breadth: only three models, single seeds, and no validation-based hyperparameter selection or report of variance. Training choices (e.g., learning rates, layer sizes) are not justified via sweeps.
- Comparisons omit key baselines: notably LSTM (or stacked LSTM), which is a natural point of comparison for modeling long-term dependencies and is cited as state of the art in similar tasks. Without this, it’s hard to assess how much of the gain is due to depth vs. gating.
- Comparability concerns: the character set differs from Sutskever et al.’s setup (95 vs 86 chars). The authors argue the effect is negligible, but do not quantify it; this affects fairness of BPC comparisons.
- The DRNN-1O training relies on incremental supervised pretraining (adding layers with temporary output heads), which confounds the architectural vs. training contributions. It also suggests that naïve end-to-end training of DRNN-1O is difficult; a direct comparison (with and without the layer-wise procedure) is missing.
- The claim that stacking “automatically” induces multi-scale processing is partly contradicted by the observation that DRNN-1O’s top three layers share similar time scales due to the training regimen. A deeper analysis (e.g., gradients per layer, spectral radii, or Jacobian norms) would strengthen causal claims.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized, with useful figures and concrete training details. The perturbation procedures and BPC metric are explained.
- Some details are missing or could be clearer: whether a validation set is used; test set usage appears intertwined with analysis; precise handling of biases (augmentation is noted but not shown in the equations); exact gradient normalization (clipping vs. rescaling is given, but thresholds and any exceptions are not discussed). Minor typos and footnote formatting issues are present.

Originality
- Architecturally, stacking RNNs and even wiring outputs at multiple depths are not new concepts; the novelty lies more in the combination of simple tanh DRNNs, a pragmatic training scheme (gradient normalization and incremental layer-wise training), and the suite of analyses on time scales and long-context behavior.
- The parentheses analysis and the perturbation-based measurement of layer-wise forgetting are useful and, to my knowledge, not widely documented for stacked simple RNNs at the time.

Significance
- The empirical result that SGD-trained stacked tanh RNNs with ~5M parameters approach MRNN+HF performance is practically valuable, suggesting that expensive second-order methods may not be strictly necessary for strong performance in this setting.
- The analysis that upper layers carry longer time scales provides insight likely to influence architecture and training choices.
- However, the lack of LSTM baselines and limited experimental breadth temper the impact. A strong comparison to stacked LSTM would significantly increase the paper’s significance.

Pros
- Clear empirical evidence that depth helps over single-layer RNNs on large-scale character LM.
- Thoughtful and convincing analyses of temporal hierarchy and long-term dependencies.
- Practical training insights (gradient normalization; supervised layer-wise training) that may transfer.

Cons
- Limited baselines (no LSTM), few runs, and modest hyperparameter exploration.
- Comparability caveats on character set; no statistical reporting.
- Architectural claims partially conflated with training procedure.

Questions for authors
- How do results change with stacked LSTMs or gated RNNs at the same parameter budget?
- Did you try end-to-end training of DRNN-1O without incremental layer addition? How much does incremental training contribute?
- Can you quantify the effect of 95 vs 86 character vocabulary on BPC?
- Do you observe similar temporal hierarchies when increasing depth or varying hidden sizes per layer?

Suggestions
- Add an apples-to-apples LSTM baseline and report multiple seeds with mean/std.
- Include a validation set, clearer early stopping/regularization details, and training/test BPC to assess overfitting.
- Provide gradient norm per layer during training to substantiate the deep supervision argument.
- Release code and scripts for reproducibility.

Recommendation
- Score: Weak accept (borderline). The paper is technically sound and provides useful analyses and a practically relevant result, but would be stronger with broader baselines and tighter comparisons. My confidence is medium.
