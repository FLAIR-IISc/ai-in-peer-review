- Paper studies stacked tanh RNNs (DRNNs) for character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus, comparing top-layer-only (DRNN-1O) vs all-layers-to-output (DRNN-AO).
- With ≈4.9M parameters and first-order SGD, DRNNs match competitive BPCs and claim emergent temporal hierarchies across layers.
- Strength: large dataset (1.4B chars) and sensible parameter-budget-controlled comparisons.
- Strength: achieves competitive performance using simple tanh RNNs without HF or multiplicative interactions.
- Strength: careful temporal probing (typo perturbations, context-switch BPC degradation, layer ablations) supports learned time-scale stratification.
- Strength: long-range dependency case study (parentheses) uses both conditional traces and generation success to show memory beyond BPTT window.
- Strength: practical training insights (incremental layerwise training for DRNN-1O, gradient normalization to mitigate explosions).
- Major weakness: no matched LSTM/GRU baselines (stacked or single-layer) under the same parameter budget.
- Weakness: main external comparator (MRNN/HF) uses a different character set and setup, making the SOTA comparison not apples-to-apples.
- Weakness: limited depth-vs-width exploration—only one depth (5) reported and no systematic sweep to isolate depth effects.
- Weakness: optimization choices under-specified and unusual (dividing by full gradient norm every step); no ablation vs standard clipping or adaptive optimizers.
- Weakness: truncated BPTT (250 steps, 50 ignored) conflicts with claims of very long memory; need discussion/ablations on how long-range memory is learned and sensitivity to truncation.
- Weakness: limited statistical rigor—only three models trained, no variance estimates, sparse hyperparameter justification, and no promised code/configs.
- Weakness: interpretation caveats—layer Euclidean distances depend on scaling; AO vs 1O tension needs deeper analysis (e.g., gradient interference, auxiliary loss weighting).
- Quality assessment: methodology is sound and analyses thoughtful, but experimental scope and baselines are too narrow to fully support central claims.
- Clarity: paper is well written, but key training choices (gradient normalization vs clipping) and figure error bars should be clarified.
- Originality: stacking tanh RNNs is not novel; AO is a mild novelty; main contribution is empirical analysis at scale.
- Significance: moderate—useful empirical lessons but limited impact without gated-baseline comparisons and broader ablations.
- Pros for acceptance: interpretable temporal analyses, competitive results with simple methods, evidence for emergent temporal hierarchy.
- Cons for acceptance: missing gated baselines, non-matching SOTA comparisons, and ad hoc optimization choices weaken the empirical claims.
- Requests for authors: add matched LSTM/GRU baselines, run depth-vs-width sweeps, compare gradient normalization to clipping, report BPTT sensitivity, fit and report layer-wise time constants with CIs, analyze AO vs 1O more, and match or report multiple preprocessing setups.
- Overall recommendation: borderline reject—interesting analyses but needs stronger, controlled empirical comparisons to be convincing.
