- Studies stacked tanh RNNs (DRNNs) for character-level language modeling with outputs from top-only (DRNN-1O) or all layers (DRNN-AO) and an incremental layer-wise training schedule.
- Trains on a large Wikipedia corpus (~1.4B chars, 95-symbol alphabet) and reports bits-per-character (BPC) comparable to similar-parameter MRNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization.
- Uses SGD with gradient normalization and an incremental supervision schedule that temporarily makes each layer the supervised top for DRNN-1O.
- Provides analyses via perturbation experiments (typos, context-switch), layer ablations, and a parentheses tracking probe to argue that depth induces hierarchical time-scales.
- Builds on prior hierarchical/stacked RNN and LSTM work; architecture is straightforward but the scale and depth analyses are valuable.
- Empirical strength: plain tanh DRNNs trained with SGD reach competitive BPC versus HF-trained models, informing the SGD vs second-order debate.
- Clear presentation of architectural variants and a practical incremental supervision schedule that appears necessary for DRNN-1O’s performance.
- Insightful diagnostic analyses convincingly show emergent temporal hierarchies and some capacity for long-range information retention.
- Argues effectively that deep supervision (outputs from each layer) mitigates vanishing signals and serves as a probe of layer roles.
- Limited novelty: stacked RNNs and depth-induced time scales have been previously hypothesized and demonstrated, so main novelty is in training schedule and analyses.
- Missing critical baselines: no LSTM/GRU comparisons (single or stacked) with matched parameters, weakening claims about long-term dependency modeling.
- Vocabulary mismatch (95 vs 86 symbols) complicates BPC comparability with prior work; rare symbols may affect large-corpus BPCs.
- Limited experimental breadth: only three instantiations, single runs, no error bars, and minimal hyperparameter exploration, reducing robustness of claims.
- Ambiguous training details: possible typo in Eq.(3) learning-rate formula and unclear gradient-normalization procedure (clipping vs always unit-norm).
- Truncated BPTT (~200–250 steps) conflicts with strong claims of extremely long memory; controlled synthetic length-generalization tests would strengthen the claim.
- Analysis metrics may be confounded by scale; Euclidean hidden-state distances could be replaced or complemented by scale-invariant measures (cosine) or output-sensitivity diagnostics.
- Overall quality: largely sound and thoughtful analyses, but methodological ambiguities and missing baselines limit empirical strength.
- Clarity: well-written but needs equation/initialization clarifications, explicit BPC values in text, and validation/tuning descriptions.
- Originality: moderate for architecture, stronger for the incremental supervision schedule and large-scale temporal-hierarchy analyses.
- Significance: moderate — suggests stacked simple RNNs can be competitive with HF-trained complex models and that depth shapes time-scales.
- Key suggestions: add LSTM baselines with matched parameters/alphabet, clarify/ablate gradient normalization and LR decay, report multiple runs/sensitivity, explore layer-width/residual variants, use scale-invariant probes, and test on a second dataset.
- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifications and stronger baseline comparisons.
- Reviewer confidence: high in method/analysis assessment; medium in comparative performance claims due to missing baselines and vocabulary mismatch.
