Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies stacked recurrent architectures (“deep recurrent neural networks,” DRNNs) for character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus. Each layer is a tanh RNN; stacking yields multiple time scales because higher layers see filtered hidden states from lower layers. Two output variants are considered: top-layer-only output (DRNN-1O) and all-layers-to-output (DRNN-AO). Models are trained with SGD, gradient-norm normalization, and a linear learning-rate decay. For DRNN-1O, the authors use incremental layer-wise training (adding layers sequentially, reinitializing the output head each time). With ≈4.9M parameters, DRNNs match the best published BPC for recurrent models with comparable parameter counts (citing multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-Free). The paper emphasizes analysis: ablations of per-layer outputs, perturbation studies (“typos”) to measure forgetting, context switching to probe effective context length, and a case study on modeling long-range dependencies (closing parentheses) in both evaluation and generation.

This work relates to prior hierarchical RNNs (El Hihi & Bengio, Fernández et al.), stacked LSTM architectures (e.g., Graves et al. on TIMIT), and MRNNs/Hessian-free training for character modeling (Sutskever et al.). The “all-layers-to-output” design is in the spirit of auxiliary/deeply supervised losses (though not commonly used in RNNs at the time). The central claim is that stacking vanilla RNNs induces a learned temporal hierarchy and improves performance over a single-layer RNN under a fixed parameter budget.

Strengths
- Clear problem setting and nontrivial dataset scale (1.4B characters). The parameter-budget-controlled comparison is sensible.
- Competitive BPC using simple tanh RNNs with first-order optimization; suggests HF/multiplicative interactions are not strictly necessary for strong char-LMs at this scale.
- Careful probing of temporal behavior: typo-perturbation distances per layer, context-switch BPC degradation, and per-layer contribution ablations. These analyses support the claim that a temporal hierarchy emerges and is learned (not purely architectural).
- Long-range behavioral probe (parentheses): both conditional probability traces and generation success rates offer interpretable evidence of memory beyond the BPTT window.
- Practical training insights: incremental layerwise procedure appears necessary for DRNN-1O; gradient normalization mitigates explosions.

Weaknesses and questions
- Missing strong baselines: no LSTM/GRU baselines (stacked or single-layer) under the same parameter budget. Given the paper’s emphasis on temporal dependencies, this is a major omission for NeurIPS standards.
- Fairness of comparisons: the MRNN/HF numbers come from a slightly different character set (86 vs 95) and training setup; the paper acknowledges this but still uses it as the main external comparator. A matched preprocessing and/or re-run would strengthen claims.
- Limited exploration of depth vs width: only one depth (5) is reported (with “results not shown” motivating it). A sweep (e.g., 1–6 layers) under fixed parameter budget would substantiate the claim that depth, not just capacity, yields gains and produces the time-scale stratification.
- Optimization choices under-specified or unusual: dividing by the full gradient norm at every step fixes step length irrespective of gradient magnitude; there is no ablation against standard gradient clipping (Pascanu et al., 2013) or adaptive optimizers. The schedule (linear decay to zero for each stage) and reinitialization of the output head when adding layers could bias conclusions about DRNN-1O vs DRNN-AO.
- Truncated BPTT length vs claimed long memory: training uses sequences of length 250 (with first 50 ignored in backprop). Demonstrating hundreds-of-steps memory is compelling, but the paper should discuss how such memory can be learned given the truncation (e.g., via learned attractor-like dynamics) and quantify sensitivity to the truncation window in ablations.
- Statistical rigor and reproducibility: only three models were trained due to compute constraints; no variance estimates or validation-based model selection; some hyperparameters (e.g., gradient normalization specifics, initialization rationales for Z1 vs Zi>1) lack justification. Code or detailed configs are not promised.
- Interpretation caveats: per-layer Euclidean distances depend on scaling of hidden states; error bars and fits to obtain characteristic time constants would make claims more quantitative. The AO ablation suggests intermediate layers mostly pre-process while top/bottom layers dominate direct output, yet AO underperforms 1O—this tension deserves more analysis (e.g., gradient interference, auxiliary loss weighting).

Quality
The methodology is sound overall, and the analyses are thoughtfully designed. However, the experimental scope is narrow: too few baselines and ablations to decisively establish that stacked vanilla RNNs are preferable to established gated architectures, or to tease apart depth vs optimization artifacts. Claims about matching SOTA are suggestive but not fully controlled.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. Training details are mostly present, though key choices (gradient normalization vs clipping) should be clarified. Some figures/tables are critical to interpretation; ensure they include error bars and precise experimental settings. Minor typos and footnote formatting can be cleaned.

Originality
Architecturally, stacking tanh RNNs is not new. The AO variant is a mild novelty in this context. The main contribution is empirical: careful temporal analyses on a large real-world corpus showing emergent time scales and long-range behavior. This is a useful addition but incremental.

Significance
Moderate. Practitioners may appreciate that simple, well-tuned SGD-trained DRNNs are competitive and that depth induces temporal stratification. Without comparisons to LSTM/GRU and a broader depth/width study, impact on modeling practice is limited.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, interpretable analyses of temporal behavior in deep RNNs on a challenging dataset.
- Competitive performance with simple methods; useful empirical lesson.
- Evidence for emergent temporal hierarchy that aligns with intuition and prior hypotheses.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing LSTM/GRU baselines and limited ablations weaken the central empirical claim.
- Comparison to prior SOTA not strictly apples-to-apples.
- Some optimization/training choices are ad hoc and insufficiently analyzed.

Requests for authors
- Add matched LSTM (and ideally GRU) baselines under the same parameter budget and training budget; include stacked variants.
- Provide a depth-vs-width study (e.g., 1–6 layers at fixed parameters) to support the “depth yields time-scale hierarchy and accuracy gains” claim.
- Compare gradient normalization to standard clipping; report sensitivity to the BPTT window.
- Quantify layer-wise time scales (e.g., fitted decay constants with confidence intervals) and include error bars in all analysis plots.
- Clarify the effect of AO vs 1O and the role of auxiliary losses (consider weighting or decoupled heads).
- Where possible, match preprocessing to prior work or provide both 86- and 95-char results.

Overall recommendation: borderline reject. The paper contains interesting and carefully executed analyses, but the absence of strong gated baselines and limited ablations make it hard to assess the true merits of the proposed architecture and training scheme. Strengthening the empirical comparisons would likely move this to accept. Confidence: high.
