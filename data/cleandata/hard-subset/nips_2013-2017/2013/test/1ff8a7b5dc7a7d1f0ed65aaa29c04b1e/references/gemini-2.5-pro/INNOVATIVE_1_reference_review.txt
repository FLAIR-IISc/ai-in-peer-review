**Summary**

This paper introduces and analyzes the Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN), an architecture that stacks multiple recurrent layers to form a temporal hierarchy. The core idea is that each layer in the stack operates on the sequence of hidden states from the layer below it, allowing the network to process information at multiple time scales simultaneously. This contrasts with standard "shallow" RNNs, where hierarchical processing is absent, and standard DNNs, which lack temporal feedback loops in each layer. The authors hypothesize that this structure is particularly well-suited for tasks with complex temporal dependencies, such as character-level language modeling.

The paper evaluates two DRNN variants on a large-scale Wikipedia text prediction benchmark: one with output connections from the top layer only (DRNN-1O) and another with connections from all layers (DRNN-AO). Empirically, the proposed DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance, matching results previously obtained only with more complex models and sophisticated second-order optimization methods (Hessian-free). The main contribution, however, is not just the performance but the in-depth analysis. The authors conduct a series of insightful experiments to validate their central hypothesis, demonstrating that a temporal hierarchy indeed emerges during training. They show that higher layers learn longer time scales and are crucial for capturing long-range dependencies, such as closing parentheses over hundreds of characters.

**Strengths**

1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its bold and compelling central idea. While stacking neural network layers is not new, the explicit formulation and analysis of a *deep recurrent* architecture as a mechanism for learning a temporal hierarchy is a significant conceptual contribution. It provides an intuitive and powerful framework for thinking about how to model complex sequential data, moving beyond the "flat" temporal processing of traditional RNNs. This work opens up a clear and promising direction for future research in sequence modeling.

2.  **Excellent In-depth Analysis:** This paper is a model for how to properly analyze a new architecture. The authors do not simply report a state-of-the-art BPC score and declare victory. Instead, they perform a series of creative and illuminating experiments to probe the internal workings of the DRNN. The analysis of how perturbations (typos, context switches) propagate and decay differently across layers provides strong evidence for the emergence of different time scales. The specific investigation into parenthesis-closing is a brilliant and concrete demonstration of the model's ability to capture non-trivial, long-range dependencies. This level of analysis provides genuine insight into *why* the model works, not just *that* it works.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results with Simple Methods:** The fact that the DRNN achieves performance comparable to the Hessian-free trained multiplicative RNN of Sutskever et al. (2011) using only vanilla SGD (with a gradient clipping heuristic) is a very strong statement. It demonstrates that the architectural innovation itself is responsible for the performance gains, rather than a complex and computationally expensive training algorithm. This makes the approach more accessible and underscores the power of the core idea.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Ad-hoc Training Procedures:** The training methods, while effective, have a somewhat heuristic flavor. The layer-wise training scheme required to get the DRNN-1O model to work is complex and specifically tailored. Similarly, the gradient normalization trick, while common practice, lacks a firm theoretical grounding. While these do not invalidate the results, a more unified and principled training approach would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Limited Exploration of Architectures:** Due to the high computational cost of the experiments, the paper only evaluates three specific network instantiations (one RNN and two 5-layer DRNNs). A broader exploration of the effect of depth, layer widths, and other hyperparameters would have provided a more complete picture of the DRNN's properties and sensitivities. As it stands, the choice of a 5-layer network is not deeply justified beyond a brief mention of prior experiments.

3.  **Interpretability of Analysis:** The authors are commendably honest about the limitations of their analysis (e.g., layer output contributions are not independent). The "time scale" measured via perturbation decay is an indirect proxy, and its precise relationship to the network's actual temporal processing is not fully clear.

**Recommendation**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

This work exemplifies the kind of research I believe NIPS should champion. It introduces a bold, conceptually clear, and significant new direction for recurrent neural networks. The central hypothesis—that depth in RNNs creates a temporal hierarchy—is both elegant and powerful. The paper's greatest strength is that it backs up this claim not just with state-of-the-art performance on a very challenging benchmark, but with a series of well-designed and insightful analyses that provide compelling evidence for the model's internal workings.

The weaknesses, such as the heuristic training methods and limited architectural search, are minor in comparison to the magnitude of the contribution. In a project of this scale, such trade-offs are understandable. This paper is not an incremental improvement; it is a foundational work that changes how we should think about designing architectures for sequential data. It is highly likely to be influential and to inspire a great deal of follow-up research.
