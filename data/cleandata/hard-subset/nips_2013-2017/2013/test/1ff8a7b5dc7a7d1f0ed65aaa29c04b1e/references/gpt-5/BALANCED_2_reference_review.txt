Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies stacked recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) for character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus. Each layer is a tanh RNN; higher layers receive the hidden sequence from the layer below. Two output schemes are compared: DRNN-1O (only top layer connects to output) and DRNN-AO (all layers connect to output with additive logits, i.e., deep supervision). Training uses truncated BPTT with stochastic gradient descent and gradient normalization; DRNN-1O employs an incremental, supervised “layer-wise” training protocol where layers are added sequentially and the output is always attached to the current top layer. With ~4.9M parameters, the DRNNs achieve bits-per-character close to a multiplicative RNN (MRNN) trained with Hessian-free (HF) optimization. The paper further analyzes emergent time scales via perturbation tests and a long-term dependency probe (closing parentheses), showing evidence of hierarchical temporal representations.

The work relates to hierarchical/stacked RNNs and multiscale ideas (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio; Graves et al. stacked bidirectional LSTMs for speech), and to MRNN/HF training for character modeling (Sutskever et al.). The main novelty is a careful empirical analysis of temporal hierarchies in stacked vanilla RNNs and a pragmatic training recipe that achieves strong performance with SGD alone.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of the DRNN architecture and training regimes; straightforward and reproducible experimental setup for a challenging corpus.
- Empirical finding that stacked vanilla RNNs with SGD can approach MRNN+HF performance at similar parameter budgets, which is practically relevant.
- Thoughtful analysis: ablation of layer outputs, perturbation-based time-scale probing, and a targeted long-range dependency test (parentheses). The parentheses experiment is particularly compelling and well-presented.
- The deep supervision (all-layer outputs) doubles as a diagnostic tool to assess layer contributions.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in architecture: stacking RNNs and adding auxiliary outputs are established ideas. The contributions are primarily empirical and analytical.
- Missing critical baselines: no LSTM/GRU (single or stacked), no MRNN trained with SGD, no hierarchical/subsampled RNN variants. This makes it hard to assess significance relative to widely used gated RNNs.
- Confounded comparison between DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO: DRNN-1O uses incremental supervised pretraining whereas DRNN-AO is trained end-to-end; the superior DRNN-1O performance may be due to training protocol rather than output placement.
- Limited experimental breadth: only three model instantiations due to computational cost; no multiple seeds or variance estimates; minimal ablations (e.g., number of layers, hidden sizes, truncation length).
- Some training choices are under-justified: zero-initialized output weights, large input weight variance for Z1, gradient normalization in lieu of clipping/Adam, and a linear LR schedule to zero. Potential stability/saturation issues are noted but not deeply analyzed.
- Claims of “state-of-the-art” framed relative to one prior model at a specific parameter budget; broader context (e.g., gated RNNs) is not addressed.

Quality
- Generally sound and carefully executed. The main performance claims are supported, but lack of key baselines weakens the conclusion that DRNNs advance the state of the art.
- The time-scale analyses are insightful but somewhat anecdotal; stronger quantification (e.g., fitted decay constants with confidence intervals, layer-wise contributions across diverse phenomena) would improve rigor.
- The architecture vs. training confound (incremental supervised pretraining) needs clearer disentanglement.

Clarity
- Well-written and organized. Most hyperparameters and dataset details are provided. A few omissions matter for reproduction: how hidden states are initialized at segment boundaries; sampling strategy overlap; regularization (none?); exact handling of rare characters; and whether gradient normalization is per-parameter vector or per-tensor.
- Figures (referenced) appear to be informative; adding precise scales and statistical dispersion would aid interpretation.

Originality
- Moderate. Stacked RNNs and deep supervision are known; the main novelty lies in the analysis methodology and the practical demonstration that SGD suffices for large-scale char-level modeling with stacked vanilla RNNs.

Significance
- Moderate. Practitioners may value a simpler training pipeline that approaches HF-trained MRNN performance. The analysis of emergent temporal hierarchies is informative, but impact is dampened by the lack of LSTM/GRU comparisons, which are dominant in practice for long-term dependencies.

Questions for the authors
- How are hidden states initialized for each 250-char segment? Are states carried across segments within a batch?
- Did you try gradient clipping or adaptive optimizers (e.g., RMSProp/Adam) instead of norm-normalized SGD?
- How sensitive are results to truncation length (200 effective steps) and to the 50-step warmup?
- Can you report variance over several random seeds for at least one setting?
- What is the effect of incremental supervised pretraining if applied to DRNN-AO?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add strong baselines: single/stacked LSTM (and/or GRU), MRNN trained with SGD, and a hierarchical multiscale RNN with subsampling; report BPC and training cost.
- Control for the training-protocol confound: train DRNN-1O end-to-end from scratch and DRNN-AO with the incremental scheme; compare.
- Provide variance across seeds and learning curves; include wall-clock and data pass comparisons to MRNN+HF.
- Explore standard stabilization (gradient clipping) and optimizers; quantify their effect versus gradient normalization.
- Expand analysis: estimate per-layer time constants with confidence intervals; analyze sensitivity to nesting in parentheses; test other long-range constructs (quotes/brackets/indentation); report layerwise mutual information with future outputs.
- Consider code release to facilitate replication and further analysis.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, careful empirical study with practical training insights.
- Useful analyses demonstrating emergent temporal hierarchies and long-range behavior.
- Competitive performance with a simpler training method.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty; architecture and deep supervision are known.
- Missing critical baselines (LSTM/GRU), confounding training differences, and scant ablations undermine claims.
- Narrow evaluation (single dataset, few runs) and partial reporting.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The analysis is valuable and the empirical results are promising, but the paper in its current form lacks key baselines and clearer disentanglement of architectural versus training effects needed for a NeurIPS-level contribution. With the suggested additions and broader comparisons, this work could become a strong submission. Confidence: 0.7/1.0
