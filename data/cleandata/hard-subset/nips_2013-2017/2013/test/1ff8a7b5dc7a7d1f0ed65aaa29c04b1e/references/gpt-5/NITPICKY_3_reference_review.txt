Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies stacked recurrent architectures (“deep recurrent neural networks,” DRNNs) for character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus. Each layer is a vanilla tanh RNN; higher layers consume the hidden state of the layer below. Two output variants are considered: output only at the top layer (DRNN-1O) and outputs from all layers summed before softmax (DRNN-AO). Training uses SGD with gradient normalization and a linear learning-rate decay; DRNN-1O is trained with an incremental layer-wise schedule. The paper reports bits-per-character (BPC) comparable to previously reported recurrent models trained via Hessian-free (HF) optimization, and provides analyses suggesting a hierarchy of time scales across layers (typo-perturbation decay, context reset experiments) and qualitative evidence of long-range behavior (parentheses closure).

The work relates to stacked RNN/LSTM architectures (e.g., Graves et al. 2013, El Hihi & Bengio 1996; Fernández et al. 2005) and to MRNNs trained with HF (Sutskever et al. 2011). The novelty is primarily the systematic analysis of time scales across layers in stacked vanilla RNNs on a large text corpus, and the claim that DRNNs can approach HF-trained models with simple SGD.

Strengths
- Clear, intuitive architecture that bridges stacked deep nets with recurrence; well-motivated focus on temporal hierarchies in character modeling.
- Interesting analytical probes (typo decay, context swap, parentheses behavior) that go beyond raw BPC.
- Parameter-budget-controlled comparison between a single-layer RNN and stacked DRNNs.
- Practical training recipe that is simple (SGD + gradient normalization) yet effective at scale.
- Ablation on per-layer output contributions (for AO) is informative.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Quality/methodology
- Non-comparable training schedules confound conclusions: DRNN-1O is trained with incremental layer-wise stages and more total passes over data (~10×) than DRNN-AO (~7×) and the RNN. The best performance being DRNN-1O could be due to training protocol and/or extra compute rather than architectural superiority. A fair comparison should match total updates/data passes and schedule.
- Missing modern baselines: no LSTM/GRU or stacked LSTM baselines, despite the paper’s own motivation around memory gating. Given the era’s literature, this is a critical omission for significance.
- Dataset comparability is unclear. The paper uses 1.4B characters (last 10M as test), while prior MRNN/PAQ numbers may be on different corpora and character vocabularies. Even within this paper, the 95+UNK character set differs from [19]’s 86; the claim that the difference is negligible is unsubstantiated. Provide results on the exact same dataset/split/charset as the baselines or a controlled study quantifying the effect.
- Equation (3) appears incorrect: η0(- j/T) suggests a negative schedule; presumably η0(1 − j/T) was intended. As written, the update direction also divides by ||∇θ|| without an epsilon or clipping threshold; near-zero gradients would produce very large steps. Clarify whether you used clipping (e.g., rescaling when ||∇|| > c) versus always normalizing.
- BPTT details: Sequences are length 250 with first 50 ignored in backward pass, implying a maximum credit assignment of 200 steps. Yet the paper claims “several hundred characters” long-term memory and shows >200 behavior qualitatively. Please clarify the exact truncation length and whether any longer sequences were backpropagated; otherwise claims about training long dependencies are weakened.
- Initialization: W and Z_{i>1} ~ N(0, N^{-1/2}) but Z1 ~ N(0,1). This mismatch can easily saturate the first layer’s tanh; a motivation and sensitivity analysis are needed. Also clarify the overloaded use of N (neurons per layer vs variance scaling).
- Random sequence sampling breaks continuity and long-context learning; handling of hidden state boundary conditions is unclear (zero reset? carried across batches?). This affects both training dynamics and the interpretation of long-term analyses.
- Time-scale analyses: Euclidean distances of hidden states are not normalized for layer dimensionality or scale; this may bias results toward layers with larger norms. Consider per-unit standardization or cosine distance, and report confidence intervals. The AO “perturb only one layer’s hidden state” experiment is ill-defined because layers are coupled; specify the intervention precisely.
- Output ablations (zeroing a single layer’s contribution) do not account for inter-layer compensation; interpretations should be tempered. A more principled method would be to learn per-layer gates and analyze learned weights.
- Parentheses experiment is largely qualitative; success-rate metric has ad hoc termination and no comparison to strong baselines. Results are interesting but do not firmly establish superiority on long-range structure.

Clarity/presentation
- References are incomplete and inconsistent (missing years, venues, pages; inconsistent punctuation; some entries are placeholders). This needs a thorough cleanup to NeurIPS style.
- Several notation inconsistencies: N used for both neurons and distribution scaling; Zi>1 appears without explicit definition; biases are said to be “augmented” but then omitted in practice (output biases initialized to zero?).
- Typos and grammar: many minor issues (e.g., “wellsuited”, “longterm”, spacing, hyphenation). Footnotes are inlined mid-paragraph and formatted inconsistently.
- Figures/Tables are crucial to the claims (e.g., time-scale plots, generated samples), but the text alone is insufficient to reproduce or assess; ensure axes, units, averaging, and error bars are reported.

Originality
- Stacked RNNs and multi-timescale ideas are not new; the architectural novelty is limited. The primary original contribution is the empirical analysis of learned time scales in stacked vanilla RNNs on a large corpus.

Significance
- Without comparisons to LSTM/GRU (which are the de facto baselines for long-term dependencies) and with confounded training protocols and dataset differences, the strength of the claimed performance contribution is limited. The analytical insights are interesting but would benefit from more rigorous methodology.

Questions for authors
- Please correct Eq. (3) and specify whether gradient clipping or normalization with threshold/epsilon was used.
- How were hidden states initialized and handled across mini-batches? Were sequences contiguous across updates?
- Provide results matching the exact dataset/charset/split as [19], and include LSTM/GRU baselines under the same parameter budget and training budget.
- Report sensitivity to Z1 initialization scale and BPTT truncation length.
- Normalize hidden-state distance metrics and add error bars.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Interesting analyses of temporal hierarchy in stacked RNNs.
- Practical training recipe; large-scale experiment.

Cons:
- Methodological confounds and equation error.
- Missing strong baselines and fair comparisons.
- Weak rigor in analyses; unclear dataset comparability.
- Presentation and reference quality issues.

Recommendation
Reject. While the topic is relevant and some analyses are insightful, the methodological and presentation issues, lack of strong baselines, and confounded comparisons undermine the scientific strength. With corrected methodology, fair baselines, and cleaned presentation, this could be a strong submission in a future round.

Reviewer confidence: high.
