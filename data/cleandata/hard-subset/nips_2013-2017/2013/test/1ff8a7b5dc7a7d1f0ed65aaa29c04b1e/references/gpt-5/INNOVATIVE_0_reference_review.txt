Summary
This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) formed by stacking vanilla RNN layers, with either output from the top layer only (DRNN-1O) or from all layers (DRNN-AO). Using only SGD with gradient norm normalization and a simple linear learning-rate schedule (plus an incremental, supervised layer-wise training scheme for DRNN-1O), the authors achieve state-of-the-art bits-per-character (BPC) among recurrent models of comparable size on a Wikipedia character-level language modeling corpus previously popularized for multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free (HF) optimization. Beyond accuracy, the paper investigates how depth induces a temporal hierarchy: ablations per layer, perturbation/typo experiments to assess forgetting curves, context-switch experiments, and targeted probes (parentheses closing) to illustrate long-range dependencies likely captured by higher layers.

Relation to prior work
Prior hierarchical/stacked recurrent architectures have been studied (El Hihi & Bengio; Graves et al. with deep bi-LSTMs; hierarchical RNNs operating at different time scales), mostly emphasizing performance or synthetic tasks. This work differs by: (a) analyzing time-scale stratification emergent from simple stacked vanilla RNNs, (b) showing that SGD can train large DRNNs to competitive BPC on a challenging real-world corpus, and (c) proposing and testing output connections from all layers to aid training and interpretability.

Strengths
- Clear, empirically supported claim that stacked RNNs learn a temporal hierarchy, validated via multiple complementary probes (layer ablations, typo-based divergence decay, context-switch BPC, and parentheses behavior).
- Competitive language modeling results on a large real-world dataset using only SGD, closing much of the gap to HF-trained MRNNs with similar parameter budgets.
- Practical training recipe: gradient normalization and a simple supervised “layer-wise” schedule that seems to greatly help DRNN-1O.
- Interesting qualitative analyses (generations, parentheses tracking) that give insight beyond aggregate BPC.
- Ablation showing lower layers capture short-term, higher layers longer-term structure, aligning with the central hypothesis.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is primarily in analysis and training insights; the core architecture (stacked RNN, multi-output heads) is not fundamentally new.
- Baselines are limited: no LSTM/GRU or wider single-layer RNN with equal parameter count, making it hard to disentangle depth vs. capacity or gating effects.
- Comparisons to prior MRNN/HF results are not perfectly controlled (slightly different character set; unclear training budget equivalence), and only three model instantiations were run due to compute constraints.
- Some training choices are under-motivated or under-explained (e.g., initializing output weights at zero; linear LR to zero; effect of gradient normalization vs. clipping).
- Figures/tables and references appear incomplete or inconsistently formatted in the draft, hindering reproducibility.

Quality
The empirical methodology is generally sound and the analyses are thoughtful. The perturbation and context experiments convincingly demonstrate layer-dependent time scales and longer effective context than a single-layer RNN. Claims about “state-of-the-art for recurrent networks with similar parameter counts” are credible but would benefit from tighter controls (same character set, standardized data passes). The qualitative parentheses probe is compelling but could be strengthened with aggregate statistics beyond failure rates (e.g., probability dynamics averaged over many instances, nested parentheses frequency).

Clarity
The paper is readable and largely well-structured. The motivation for DRNNs as temporal hierarchy builders is clear, and the analyses are easy to follow. However, the draft contains missing figure/table details and some footnote/formatting issues; training hyperparameters and compute budgets could be documented more precisely (e.g., exact BPTT length used for gradients, gradient norm threshold/always normalized, number of epochs/data passes for each model). Reproducibility would benefit from more explicit implementation details and, ideally, code.

Originality
Stacking RNNs and connecting intermediate layers to the output are known ideas, but the paper offers a novel and useful combination of: (i) a simple supervised incremental training schedule that works well with SGD, and (ii) a careful, multi-angle empirical analysis demonstrating emergent temporal hierarchies and long-range behavior in real-world text. The analytical angle is the most original contribution.

Significance
The practical takeaway—that depth induces usable time-scale separation even in vanilla RNNs and that simple SGD can train large DRNNs well—should interest practitioners. The analysis methods can inspire future interpretability studies for recurrent and transformer architectures. While not a singular architectural breakthrough, the paper advances understanding and offers a pragmatic training recipe, likely to be used and extended.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Add LSTM/GRU and “wide 1-layer RNN” baselines with matched parameter counts to isolate the effect of depth versus gating and parameter budget.
- Report exact BPC numbers (with variance over multiple runs if feasible), align the character set with prior work, and specify total training tokens seen to enable fair comparison to HF/MRNN.
- Clarify gradient normalization (always unit-norm? any clipping threshold?), rationale for zero-initialized output weights, and sensitivity to LR schedule.
- Explore deeper/wider stacks and parameter allocation per layer to test where temporal hierarchy and performance saturate.
- Provide quantitative aggregates for parentheses behavior (e.g., average probability trajectories, nested cases) and additional long-range phenomena (quotes, XML tags).
- Release code or pseudocode for the incremental supervised layer-wise training procedure.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid, multi-faceted empirical evidence that depth yields temporal hierarchy in RNNs on real text.
- Competitive performance with a simple optimizer and transparent training, which challenges the necessity of second-order methods for such tasks.
- Useful, general analysis methodology and practical training insights with potential impact beyond this specific model.

Arguments against acceptance
- Architectural novelty is limited; stacked RNNs and multi-head outputs are known.
- Experimental scope is narrow: few baselines, limited ablations/hyperparameter sweeps, and imperfectly matched comparisons.
- Some presentation and reproducibility gaps.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The work is not a radical architectural advance, but the analysis is insightful and the training findings are practically valuable. Given the demonstrated temporal hierarchy and competitive results with simple methods, I believe it merits presentation and will stimulate follow-up work.
