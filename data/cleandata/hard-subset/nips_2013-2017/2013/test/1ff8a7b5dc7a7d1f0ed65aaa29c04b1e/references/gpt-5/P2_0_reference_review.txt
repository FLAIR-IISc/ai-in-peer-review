Summary
The paper studies stacked recurrent architectures (“deep recurrent neural networks,” DRNNs) for character-level language modeling. Each layer is a tanh RNN; higher layers receive the hidden state sequence of the layer below. Two output designs are considered: DRNN-1O (only top layer to softmax) and DRNN-AO (deep supervision: all layers connect to softmax). Models are trained with SGD, backpropagation through time (BPTT), gradient-norm normalization, and (for DRNN-1O) incremental layer-wise training where each new top layer is added and trained while earlier output layers are removed. On a large Wikipedia corpus, DRNNs with ~4.9M parameters achieve bits-per-character (BPC) comparable to the best recurrent models of similar size trained with Hessian-free (HF) optimization (multiplicative RNNs). The paper contributes analyses showing: (i) different layers operate at different time scales (via perturbation/typo divergence and context-switch experiments); (ii) layer ablations in DRNN-AO indicate top and bottom layers contribute most directly to prediction; and (iii) long-range dependencies, illustrated by tracking and generating closing parentheses over hundreds of characters.

Relation to prior work
The idea of hierarchical/stacked RNNs is not new (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio; Fernández/Graves/Schmidhuber; Graves et al. with stacked bi-LSTMs for speech). Multiplicative RNNs with HF have set strong baselines on the same corpus. This paper differs by: (a) demonstrating that simple stacked tanh RNNs trained with SGD can match those HF results at similar parameter counts; (b) systematically analyzing emergent time scales across layers; and (c) using deep supervision (outputs from all layers) both to aid training and to probe contributions. The empirical analyses are more extensive than typical performance-focused papers.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architecture and training are standard and sound. The perturbation and context experiments are reasonable probes of time scales; the parentheses experiments provide qualitative and quantitative evidence of long-term structure.
- Empirical support: Results on a large public corpus are solid and include ablations (layer outputs) and behavioral diagnostics. However, there are important shortcomings:
  - Baselines are limited: no comparisons to LSTM/GRU (or stacked LSTM) despite their prominence for long-term dependencies.
  - Reporting/statistics: only three models due to compute; no variance across runs or sensitivity to hyperparameters; limited hyperparameter description; no validation set usage is described.
  - Evaluation comparability: character set differs (96 vs 86 used in MRNN baseline) and the fairness of this comparison is only briefly argued.
  - Some training details are unconventional or under-specified (e.g., gradient normalization to unit norm, zero-initialized output weights, exact BPTT horizon; the LR decay formula in Eq. 3 appears to have a sign/parentheses typo—likely η=η0(1−j/T)).
Overall, the work is a complete empirical study rather than a theoretical contribution, and the claims are mostly supported by the presented evidence.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, well-structured, and places its work in context. The motivation for deep supervision and incremental layer-wise training is explained and connected to optimization issues. Figures/tables (referenced) appear to support the narrative. Minor clarity issues:
- Fix or clarify the learning-rate schedule in Eq. 3 (current expression reads oddly).
- Provide exact BPTT truncation length used for learning (first 50 chars ignored; sequences of 250—so effective 200 steps?).
- Clarify whether a validation split was used and how hyperparameters were selected.
- Briefly justify zero initialization of output weights (symmetry concerns) and compare to small random initialization.

Originality
Moderate. Stacking RNNs and deep supervision are known ideas; the main novelty is the careful analysis of emergent time scales and the demonstration that simple stacked RNNs with SGD can reach competitive BPC on a large corpus. The layer-wise incremental training for DRNN-1O is a pragmatic training recipe. The perturbation/time-scale methodology and parentheses probe are useful and could be adopted by others.

Significance
Moderate to good. Practically, showing that plain stacked RNNs trained with SGD can approach HF-trained MRNNs at similar parameter counts is useful. Conceptually, the analyses provide evidence for temporal hierarchies in stacked RNNs and show long-range tracking in real text. The impact would be stronger with stronger baselines (LSTM/GRU) and broader ablations, but the work should be of interest to the sequence modeling community and could inform future architecture/training design.

Strengths
- Clear problem and motivation; relevant to NIPS audience.
- Competitive results on a large, realistic dataset with relatively simple methods.
- Insightful analyses of time scales and layer contributions; informative qualitative and quantitative diagnostics (typos, context switches, parentheses).
- Practical training heuristics (deep supervision, incremental layering, gradient-norm normalization).

Weaknesses
- Missing strong baselines (stacked LSTM/GRU), limiting significance of performance claims.
- Limited experimental breadth and statistics; only three runs; little hyperparameter sensitivity.
- Comparison to prior SOTA differs in character set; fairness only briefly argued.
- Some training details need clarification (LR schedule typo, BPTT specifics, initialization choices, regularization).
- The “DRNN” label is generic; conceptual novelty is mainly in analysis rather than architecture.

Questions for authors
- Can you report validation-based early stopping or tuning, and test/validation splits?
- How does DRNN compare to a similarly sized stacked LSTM under your training setup?
- What is the exact BPTT truncation length used to compute gradients?
- Did you try gradient clipping to a threshold vs full normalization? Any performance differences?
- Any evidence that zero output-weight init helps vs small random init?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a stacked LSTM baseline (and/or GRU), even at smaller scale, to contextualize DRNNs.
- Provide standard deviations over multiple seeds or at least some sensitivity analyses.
- Harmonize the character set with prior work or quantify the exact BPC impact.
- Clarify/clean up training details (Eq. 3, initialization, BPTT horizon), and report validation usage.
- Explore different depth/width allocations across layers; your analyses suggest bottom/top layers matter most.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid empirical contribution on a challenging real dataset with simple methods.
- Useful, carefully executed analyses elucidating temporal hierarchies and long-range behavior.
- Practical training recipe that others can adopt.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty in architecture; missing key baselines (LSTM/GRU) weakens significance.
- Sparse experimental breadth and statistical rigor.
- Some evaluation comparability concerns and minor clarity issues.

Recommendation: Weak accept
Confidence: Medium
