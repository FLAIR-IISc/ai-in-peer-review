### **Summary**

This paper introduces the Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN), an architecture constructed by stacking multiple recurrent layers. The central hypothesis is that this depth creates an implicit temporal hierarchy, where lower layers process information on shorter timescales and higher layers capture longer-term dependencies. The authors evaluate this architecture on the challenging task of character-level language modeling using a large Wikipedia corpus. They compare a 5-layer DRNN (with two output configurations: top-layer only and all-layers) against a standard (shallow) RNN with a similar parameter count. The results demonstrate that DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance, matching a more complex model (MRNN) trained with a more sophisticated second-order optimization method (Hessian-free), using only stochastic gradient descent. The key contribution of the paper is not just the performance, but the in-depth analysis that provides compelling empirical evidence for the emergence of a temporal hierarchy, using perturbation analysis and a qualitative study of long-range dependencies like parenthesis matching.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results on a Challenging Benchmark:** The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on a large-scale, widely recognized benchmark (the Wikipedia character prediction task from Sutskever et al., 2011). Achieving a BPC of 1.48 with a conceptually simple model and standard SGD (with gradient clipping) is a significant achievement, especially when the prior state-of-the-art required a more complex Hessian-free optimizer. This makes the approach more accessible and demonstrates the power of architectural depth.

2.  **Excellent and Insightful Analysis:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its analysis, which goes far beyond simply reporting performance metrics. The experiments designed to probe the model's internal dynamics are well-conceived and persuasive:
    *   The typo-perturbation experiment (Fig. 3, left) provides direct evidence for the core hypothesis: higher layers exhibit longer memory traces, confirming the emergence of a learned temporal hierarchy.
    *   The context-switch experiment (Fig. 3, right) connects this internal property to predictive performance, showing that DRNNs leverage a longer context than the baseline RNN.
    *   The analysis of parenthesis closing is an excellent qualitative case study. It demonstrates that the model learns a non-trivial, long-range structural dependency, representing it as a persistent state in the top layer, rather than simply memorizing fixed-length patterns.

3.  **Clear Motivation and Exposition:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly motivated. The introduction provides a lucid explanation of the potential weaknesses of shallow RNNs and frames the DRNN as a natural synthesis of the hierarchical processing of deep feedforward networks and the temporal memory of recurrent networks. The model is described precisely, and the experimental setup is detailed enough to ensure reproducibility.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The core idea of stacking recurrent layers is not entirely novel. The authors rightly cite prior work, notably Graves et al. (2013) who used stacked LSTMs for speech recognition. The contribution here is less about proposing a brand-new architecture and more about being the first to perform a deep analysis of its properties and demonstrate its effectiveness on a large-scale language modeling task. This should be framed more explicitly as the primary contribution.

2.  **Lack of Comparison to Stacked LSTMs:** While the comparison to a "common RNN" is a necessary baseline, the paper's motivation heavily references the LSTM. A more compelling comparison would have been against a stacked LSTM network with a similar parameter count. This would help disentangle the benefits of depth from the benefits of a more sophisticated recurrent unit. Given that the cited work by Graves et al. found success with stacked LSTMs, this feels like a missed opportunity for a more thorough comparison.

3.  **Ad-hoc Training Procedures and Limited Ablation:** The training of the DRNN-1O model relies on an incremental, layer-wise scheme which, while effective, feels somewhat heuristic. A deeper investigation into why this is necessary (e.g., linking it to vanishing/exploding gradients in the deep hierarchy) would be valuable. Furthermore, due to computational constraints, the study is limited to three network configurations. It is difficult to know if the choice of 5 layers is optimal, or how performance trades off with layer width versus depth for a fixed parameter budget. While the computational cost is understandable, this limitation means the conclusions about the specific architecture are not as generalizable as they could be.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The experiments are rigorously conducted, and the claims are well-supported by the empirical results. The authors are honest about the computational limitations of their study. The analysis is particularly strong and provides a blueprint for how to analyze the internal workings of deep sequential models.

**Clarity:** The paper is excellently written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The figures and tables are clear and effectively communicate the key results. The description of the DRNN model and the training setup is sufficient for an expert to reproduce the work.

**Originality:** The originality lies not in the architecture itself, but in the combination of (1) applying this architecture to a very challenging character-level language modeling task, (2) demonstrating SOTA results with a simple optimizer, and (3) providing the first in-depth analysis of the emergent temporal hierarchy in such a model. This analytical contribution is highly original and valuable.

**Significance:** This work is highly significant. It provided strong evidence that depth is as crucial for recurrent models as it is for feedforward models, paving the way for the deep LSTMs and GRUs that became standard in sequence modeling. The finding that deep networks can learn hierarchical temporal features has had a lasting impact. The analytical techniques used in the paper (e.g., perturbation analysis) have also been influential in the quest to better understand the internal representations of neural networks. This paper clearly advanced the state of the art and provided ideas that many other researchers have since built upon.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   It would be beneficial to explicitly discuss the relationship between the layer-wise training for DRNN-1O and the problem of vanishing gradients through depth. Does DRNN-AO, with its direct error signals to each layer, effectively act as a form of deep supervision that mitigates this issue, explaining why it can be trained end-to-end?
*   In the parenthesis analysis, it is noted that the model fails on nested parentheses. This is a very interesting limitation. Is this because they are rare in the training data, or does it point to a fundamental limitation of the learned state-machine representation (e.g., it can only count to one)? A brief discussion on this would add more nuance.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper that combines solid empirical results with insightful analysis to make a compelling case for deep recurrent architectures. Despite the limited architectural novelty, its contributions to our understanding of how these models work and its demonstration of their effectiveness are significant and impactful.
