Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), defined as stacked vanilla RNN layers where each layer has its own recurrent state and receives as input the previous layer’s hidden state. Two output schemes are compared: output only from the top layer (DRNN-1O) and outputs from all layers aggregated before softmax (DRNN-AO). The authors argue that stacking induces a temporal hierarchy—lower layers capture short contexts, higher layers longer ones—and present analyses to support this. They apply the models to character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus, trained with SGD and gradient normalization, and report bits-per-character (BPC) comparable to a multiplicative RNN trained with Hessian-free (HF) optimization (Sutskever et al., ICML’11). They also analyze layer contributions via ablation, measure layer-specific time scales using a “typo perturbation” and a context-swap experiment, and probe long-range structure through parenthesis-closing behavior in both predictive and generative modes.

This builds on a line of work on deep/stacked RNNs and hierarchical temporal models (El Hihi & Bengio 1996; Fernández et al. 2007; Graves et al. 2013 for deep bi-LSTMs in speech). It complements the MRNN + HF results on the same dataset (Sutskever et al. 2011) by showing that relatively simple tanh DRNNs trained with SGD can be competitive, and contributes analysis of emergent temporal hierarchies.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architecture and training setup, with a thoughtful comparison of two output schemes (top-only vs all-layers).
- Competitive BPC with a large-scale dataset using plain SGD, suggesting that careful architecture and training suffice without HF for this task size.
- Interesting and concrete analyses of temporal behavior: typo perturbation, context swap, layer ablations, and a focused long-range phenomenon (parentheses). The latter gives compelling qualitative evidence that higher layers carry long-range signals.
- The incremental layer-wise training scheme for DRNN-1O is practical and appears effective at mitigating vanishing gradients to lower layers.
- The paper is generally well written and situates itself with relevant prior work on hierarchical/stacked RNNs and HF-trained RNNs.

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines: No LSTM or deep LSTM comparisons on the same dataset; the main comparison is to MRNN+HF. Given LSTM’s established ability to capture long dependencies, an LSTM or stacked LSTM baseline would substantially strengthen claims.
- Comparability of BPC is confounded by different character vocabularies (95+UNK vs 86 used by MRNN). The claim that the difference is negligible is plausible but not demonstrated; a matched evaluation or a conversion would be preferable.
- The DRNN-1O vs DRNN-AO comparison is not fully controlled: the 1O model is trained with a different curriculum and larger effective budget (about 10 vs 7 passes), confounding the conclusion that 1O is “best.”
- Gradient normalization: Equation (3) appears to rescale every update to unit norm (after annealing), not clip to a maximum norm. This is unusual and may overstep on small gradients; more justification/ablation is needed. It is unclear whether normalization is per-parameter tensor, per-layer, or global.
- Time-scale analysis relies on Euclidean distances in hidden-state space with tanh units; this metric can be hard to interpret due to saturation and scaling. The context-swap BPC analysis is more directly tied to prediction and should be emphasized.
- Scope of experiments is narrow (three models), with little hyperparameter exploration. Some choices (e.g., output weights initialized to zero, input weight scale differences across layers) are unusual and merit justification/ablation.
- The state-of-the-art claim for “recurrent networks” is not fully substantiated without stronger baselines and matched evaluation protocol.

Quality
Technically sound overall; the modeling and training are standard, and the analyses are carefully described. However, the experimental evaluation could be more thorough: control training budgets across variants; include LSTM baselines; match character sets; and clarify the gradient normalization. The parentheses study is a nice idea, but more systematic quantification (beyond one example and a success rate) would help.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The description of the two output schemes, training curriculum, and evaluation metrics is easy to follow. Please clarify:
- The precise gradient normalization procedure (global vs per-layer, clipping vs normalization).
- BPTT truncation details (backprop length is 200 steps, with a 50-step burn-in—confirm).
- Data preprocessing and character mapping vis-à-vis prior work to ensure comparability.

Originality
Stacking RNNs is not new, and using outputs at multiple depths has precedents in auxiliary classifiers/deep supervision ideas. The contributions here are incremental: a clean DRNN instantiation, a practical training curriculum, and analyses demonstrating emergent temporal hierarchies. The analysis suite (typo perturbation, context swap, parentheses) is a valuable and somewhat novel empirical characterization.

Significance
Moderate. Showing that stacked tanh RNNs trained with SGD can approach MRNN+HF performance on large-scale character modeling is useful. The analyses of temporal hierarchy are likely to influence how practitioners think about depth in RNNs. However, absent comparisons to LSTM/stacked LSTM and without matched evaluation protocols, the empirical significance is limited.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Provide results on the exact 86-character setup of Sutskever et al. (or report a conversion) to make BPC directly comparable.
- Add at least one LSTM baseline (single-layer and stacked) with a similar parameter budget, even if trained for fewer updates, to contextualize the gains from depth vs gating.
- Equalize training budgets and curricula across DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO to isolate the effect of output connectivity.
- Clarify and, if possible, ablate the gradient normalization (true clipping to a threshold vs full normalization) and the zero initialization of output weights.
- For the time-scale analysis, complement Euclidean hidden-state distances with output-level measures (as you partially do) and possibly cosine distances or per-unit normalization to mitigate saturation effects.
- Release trained models or code to aid reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid large-scale results with a simple architecture/training, approaching strong baselines.
- Insightful empirical analyses demonstrating an emergent temporal hierarchy and long-range effects.
- Clear writing and broadly relevant ideas for sequence modeling.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing strong baselines (LSTM), and imperfect comparability to MRNN due to different vocabularies.
- Confounded comparison between DRNN variants due to differing training curricula/budgets.
- Some methodological oddities insufficiently justified (gradient normalization scheme).

Recommendation
Borderline accept. If the authors can address comparability (matched vocab), add or cite strong LSTM baselines, and clarify the optimization details, the paper would make a useful contribution on training and analyzing deep RNNs at scale. As it stands, it is a well-executed empirical study with interesting analyses but limited novelty and incomplete baselines.
