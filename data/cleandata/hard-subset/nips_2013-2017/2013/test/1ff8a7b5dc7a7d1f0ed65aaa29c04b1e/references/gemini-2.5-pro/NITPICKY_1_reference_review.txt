**Review of "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks"**

**Summary**

This paper introduces a deep recurrent neural network (DRNN) architecture, which consists of multiple stacked RNN layers. The authors propose that this structure is well-suited for modeling data with temporal hierarchies, such as natural language. They evaluate the DRNN on a character-level language modeling task using a large Wikipedia corpus. Two variants are tested: one with output connections from the top layer only (DRNN-1O) and one with connections from all layers (DRNN-AO). The authors show that their models, trained with standard stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and gradient norm clipping, achieve state-of-the-art performance, comparable to that of more complex models trained with second-order methods like Hessian-free optimization. The paper includes an analysis of the trained models, providing evidence that the stacked layers learn to operate on different time scales and can capture long-range dependencies, such as closing parentheses correctly over long distances.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Empirical Results:** The primary strength of this work is its impressive performance on a challenging, large-scale benchmark. Achieving results comparable to the state-of-the-art (Sutskever et al., 2011) using only SGD is a noteworthy contribution. It provides compelling evidence that deep recurrent architectures are powerful and may not necessarily require complex second-order optimization methods to be trained effectively.

2.  **Insightful Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting performance metrics. The analysis in Section 3 provides valuable insights into the behavior of these deep models. The experiments visualizing the decay of perturbations (Figure 3) effectively demonstrate the emergence of a temporal hierarchy, where higher layers exhibit longer memory. The case study on parenthesis-closing is a clear and convincing demonstration of the model's ability to learn non-trivial, long-range dependencies.

3.  **Clear Architectural Motivation:** The motivation for stacking RNNs to combine the hierarchical processing of deep networks with the temporal memory of recurrent networks is well-articulated in the introduction. The exploration of two different output schemes (DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO) is a thoughtful design choice that also facilitates further analysis.

**Weaknesses**

While the core ideas and results are promising, the manuscript is undermined by a significant number of methodological shortcuts, presentation flaws, and a general lack of rigor expected for a NIPS publication.

1.  **Insufficient Experimental Rigor:** The authors state they only tested three network instantiations (one for each model type) due to long training times. The justification in footnote 3 ("In our experience the networks are so large that there is very little difference in performance for different initialisations") is an anecdotal claim, not a scientific one. Without multiple runs with different random seeds, it is impossible to assess the stability and variance of the results. This is a major methodological flaw that weakens the central performance claims.

2.  **Unjustified and Ad-Hoc Methodological Choices:**
    *   The incremental, layer-wise training scheme for the DRNN-1O is highly specific and feels heuristic. The number of parameter updates increases linearly with each added layer (`T = {0.5, 1, 1.5, 2, 2.5} × 10^5`). There is no justification provided for this particular schedule. Why not a fixed number of updates per stage?
    *   The input weight initialization `Z1` is drawn from `N(0, 1)`. This is a high-variance distribution compared to the more standard `N(0, N−1/2)` used for other weights, and its use is not explained or justified.
    *   Gradient clipping is described as an "often-used trick" and a "simple heuristic." While common, this informal language obscures the specific technique used (gradient norm clipping), and a more formal presentation would be appropriate.

3.  **Inconsistencies and Overstatements:**
    *   The introduction claims that "stacking RNNs automatically creates different time scales." However, the analysis at the end of Section 3 concludes that "The big differences we see in the trained DRNNs are hence a learned property," and an experiment on an untrained DRNN showed far smaller differences. This is a direct contradiction. The hierarchy is not automatic; it is an emergent property of training.
    *   The assertion that the model learns a "pseudo-stable attractor-like state" to handle parentheses is a strong claim borrowed from dynamical systems theory. This claim is not supported by any formal analysis (e.g., of the system's Jacobian or fixed points) and amounts to speculative over-interpretation of the observed behavior.

4.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:**
    *   The paper relies excessively on footnotes to convey important experimental details (e.g., dataset differences, rationale for limited runs, computational cost comparisons). This information should be integrated into the main text for clarity and flow.
    *   The reference section is incomplete and sloppily formatted. Many entries are missing essential information such as the year, conference or journal name, volume, or page numbers. For example, [1], [2], [3], [7], and [12] are all incomplete. This demonstrates a lack of care in the manuscript's preparation.
    *   Minor grammatical errors are present, such as the opening sentence: "The last decade, machine learning has seen...".

5.  **Weak Comparisons:** The performance comparison to the Hessian-free trained MRNN is a key result, but the computational cost comparison is dismissed in footnote 6: "an honest comparison is hard to make." This weakens the claim of achieving similar results more efficiently. While a perfect comparison is difficult, a more thorough attempt (e.g., reporting total floating point operations or a more detailed hardware/time analysis) is necessary.

**Decision**

I recommend **rejection**.

The paper explores an interesting and important direction and presents compelling headline results. However, the work in its current form is not ready for publication at NIPS. The lack of experimental rigor due to single training runs for each model is a critical flaw. This, combined with a multitude of smaller issues—poorly justified methodological choices, internal inconsistencies, over-interpretation of results, and careless presentation (especially the references)—collectively erodes confidence in the work's reliability and completeness. While many of these are fixable, the sheer number of them suggests the work is premature. A substantially revised version that addresses the experimental validation and provides clearer justification for its methods could be a strong future submission.
