

# Review: "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks"

This paper proposes "Deep Recurrent Neural Networks" (DRNNs), which stack RNN layers such that each layer receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input. The authors claim this creates a temporal hierarchy with different layers operating at different time scales, which they argue is beneficial for modeling long-term dependencies in language tasks. The paper presents experiments on character-level language modeling on Wikipedia text, comparing DRNNs to standard RNNs.

The paper builds on existing work in hierarchical RNNs [3,5,6,8], particularly citing Graves et al.'s stacked LSTM work [8]. However, the novelty claim is overstated - the proposed architecture is essentially a stacked RNN, which is a straightforward extension of existing approaches rather than a fundamentally new concept. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from standard deep RNNs or recent advances like LSTMs and GRUs, which have been shown to effectively model long-term dependencies.

**Quality:** The experimental setup is somewhat limited. The authors test only three network instantiations on a single dataset with minimal ablation studies. The claimed "state-of-the-art" performance is questionable, as they compare to a common RNN (not the current SOTA) and mention their DRNN-1O achieves results comparable to a Hessian-free trained MRNN [19], but don't provide a fair comparison to more recent RNN architectures. The paper lacks proper benchmarking against contemporary methods like LSTMs with attention mechanisms or recent transformer variants. The analysis of time scales is interesting but suffers from limited interpretation - for instance, the authors observe that DRNN-AO has short time scales in bottom layers and longer in top layers, but don't establish why this is beneficial rather than simply a consequence of the architecture.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The architecture is well-described with Figure 1, and the experimental setup is adequately detailed. However, the paper would benefit from more thorough discussion of how DRNNs differ from standard deep RNNs, and why the specific architecture proposed provides advantages over simpler alternatives.

**Originality:** The core idea of stacking RNN layers is not novel. The paper cites [8] which used stacked bi-directional LSTMs with similar architecture. While the authors claim to provide "more in-depth analysis," they don't sufficiently demonstrate how their work fundamentally advances beyond existing approaches. The claim that DRNNs "embed different time scales directly in their structure" is not convincingly supported by their experiments, as this behavior could emerge in any deep RNN architecture.

**Significance:** The paper makes modest contributions to the understanding of temporal hierarchies in RNNs but fails to demonstrate significant practical advantages over existing methods. The claimed benefits for language modeling are not compelling, as the performance gains are marginal compared to existing architectures that are more widely adopted and better understood. The paper doesn't provide evidence that DRNNs solve problems that standard RNNs cannot, nor does it address the computational cost of training DRNNs versus standard approaches.

**Strengths:** The paper provides some interesting analysis of layer contributions and time scales in DRNNs. The experiments on parenthesis closing demonstrate a potentially useful capability, though this is a narrow task that may not generalize to broader language modeling.

**Weaknesses:** The most significant weakness is the lack of rigorous comparison to current state-of-the-art methods. The paper compares to a common RNN (not SOTA) and mentions results comparable to Hessian-free trained MRNNs, but doesn't demonstrate how their simpler SGD approach compares to more recent RNN architectures trained with standard methods. The paper also doesn't sufficiently address why DRNNs would be preferred over using a deeper LSTM or GRU network, which have been shown to work well for sequential tasks.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Provides some interesting empirical evidence about temporal hierarchies in deep RNNs
- Offers an alternative perspective on how deep RNNs might develop time-scale hierarchies
- The architecture is conceptually straightforward and could be useful for specific applications

*Con:*
- Overstated novelty claim; builds directly on existing stacked RNN work
- Limited experimental validation with minimal comparison to current SOTA
- Fails to demonstrate clear advantages over standard deep RNN approaches
- Lacks theoretical justification for why the proposed architecture is fundamentally better
- Does not address computational cost or practical benefits of the approach

**Conclusion:**

While the paper presents an interesting concept, it fails to provide sufficient evidence that the proposed DRNN architecture offers significant advantages over established deep RNN approaches. The experimental validation is limited and doesn't convincingly demonstrate superiority over current state-of-the-art methods. The paper would benefit from more thorough comparison to recent work, stronger theoretical justification, and clearer demonstration of the unique value proposition of DRNNs. As it stands, the paper makes only incremental contributions to the field and fails to provide compelling evidence that the proposed architecture is substantially better than existing approaches. I recommend rejection for the lack of sufficient evidence to support the claims of novelty and advantage over established methods.
