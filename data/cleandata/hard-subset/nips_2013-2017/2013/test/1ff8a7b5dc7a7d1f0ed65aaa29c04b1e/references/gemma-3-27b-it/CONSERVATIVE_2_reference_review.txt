This paper explores Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs) for character-level language modeling, aiming to combine the benefits of DNNs and RNNs by stacking recurrent layers. While the motivation—capturing hierarchical temporal dependencies—is reasonable, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating a significant advance over established methods. My primary concern is the limited scope of experimentation and the lack of thorough comparison to strong baselines *beyond* performance numbers presented in a table.

The core idea of stacking RNNs to create implicit time-scale hierarchies is not entirely novel, as the authors acknowledge (citing [5, 8]). However, the presented analysis of *how* these hierarchies emerge and contribute to performance is superficial. The visualizations (Figures 2 & 3) are marginally informative, and the interpretations offered (e.g., layer roles based on output contribution) are speculative without more rigorous analysis. The observation that removing the top layer has the largest impact isn't surprising – it's where the final prediction happens. The "typo" experiment is a creative attempt to probe time scales, but the interpretation of the resulting curves feels hand-wavy and doesn't offer a quantifiable measure of hierarchy. 

The claim of achieving state-of-the-art performance is weakened by the fact that the comparison is made against a single MRNN implementation utilizing a significantly more computationally expensive Hessian-free optimization method. While the authors acknowledge this, the comparison feels somewhat disingenuous. Achieving comparable results with SGD is admirable, but a fairer comparison would involve a more direct implementation of a similar architecture using SGD, or a more thorough ablation study to demonstrate the DRNN’s inherent advantages. The reliance on a single dataset (Wikipedia) also limits the generalizability of the findings.

Methodologically, the training procedure itself is described in sufficient detail, but feels somewhat ad-hoc. The incremental layer-wise training for DRNN-1O, while justified, feels like a workaround rather than a principled approach. The justifications provided for gradient normalization and learning rate scheduling are standard heuristics, but lack detailed explanation or ablation studies.

The paper also suffers from clarity issues. The transition between describing the motivations and introducing the architecture feels abrupt. While the equations are correct, the explanation of their significance is limited. More intuition for *why* these specific equations are chosen (beyond simply being a stacked RNN) would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates comparable performance to state-of-the-art on a standard benchmark.
*   Presents a relatively simple architectural modification that combines elements of DNNs and RNNs.
*   Identifies potential benefits of hierarchical recurrent structures.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental validation beyond a single dataset and comparison to a single, computationally expensive baseline.
*   Superficial analysis of the emergent temporal hierarchies.
*   Lack of rigorous comparison to strong, more direct baselines using SGD.
*   Ad-hoc training procedure.
*   Limited discussion of limitations and potential failure modes.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the idea is interesting and the initial results are promising, the lack of thorough validation, strong baselines, and in-depth analysis prevents me from recommending acceptance to NIPS. The paper feels more like a preliminary exploration than a fully developed contribution. The authors would significantly strengthen their work by conducting a more comprehensive set of experiments, including comparisons to more widely available and readily reproducible baselines, and providing a more rigorous analysis of the learned temporal hierarchies. The current submission lacks the novelty, significance, and methodological rigor expected of a NIPS publication.
