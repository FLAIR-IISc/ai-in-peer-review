This paper explores deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), combining the hierarchical processing of deep neural networks with the temporal memory capabilities of recurrent neural networks. The authors propose stacking RNNs to create a hierarchy of timescales, analyzing this architecture in the context of character-level language modeling. They demonstrate competitive performance on a Wikipedia text corpus, achieving state-of-the-art results for recurrent networks using only stochastic gradient descent (SGD).

The paper is well-motivated, positioning DRNNs as a potential solution to limitations of both standard DNNs and RNNs. The idea of stacking RNNs to implicitly create multiple timescales is insightful and directly addresses a known challenge in sequence modeling. The empirical evaluation on a large text corpus is a strength, providing a concrete demonstration of the DRNN’s capabilities. The analysis of layer contributions and time scales is valuable and goes beyond simply reporting performance numbers. The connection to existing work, including references to hierarchical RNNs [3, 5, 6] and LSTM networks [11], is appropriate.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The experimental setup, while substantial, feels somewhat limited. Only three network instantiations were tested, and the sensitivity to hyperparameters, beyond the initial learning rate, is not thoroughly explored. While the authors mention experimenting with a larger network (9.6M parameters), the results are only briefly mentioned.  A more systematic hyperparameter search and ablation study would strengthen the conclusions.

The description of the training process is detailed, but also complex. The incremental layer-wise training for DRNN-1O and the differing training lengths for each layer add complexity that is not fully justified. It would be beneficial to discuss the rationale for these choices in greater detail and explore alternatives. The paper also lacks a rigorous comparison to other contemporary language models, such as Transformers, which have become the dominant approach in recent years.  While the authors acknowledge Hessian-free optimization [19] as a strong baseline, a comparison against more modern techniques would provide further context.

The analysis of time scales, while intriguing, relies on somewhat artificial perturbations (introducing typos). The interpretation of these experiments could be more nuanced. For example, the observed differences in decay rates between the DRNN-AO and DRNN-1O could be due to the different training procedures rather than inherent architectural differences.  Similarly, the analysis of parenthesis closure, while illustrative, doesn’t quantify the improvement over a standard RNN in a statistically significant manner. 

Finally, while the paper mentions potential future work, it lacks a clear statement of the DRNN’s limitations. What types of sequences or tasks would likely be *unsuitable* for this architecture? Addressing this would demonstrate a more critical self-assessment.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of DNN and RNN concepts, creating a hierarchy of timescales.
*   Strong empirical results on a challenging language modeling task, achieving state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks with SGD.
*   In-depth analysis of layer contributions and time scales, providing insights into the network’s behavior.
*   Clear and generally well-written presentation of the architecture and experiments.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   Limited hyperparameter search and ablation study.
*   Lack of comparison to more recent and competitive language models (e.g., Transformers).
*   Analysis of time scales based on potentially artificial perturbations.
*   Insufficient discussion of the architecture’s limitations.
*   Complex training procedure not fully justified.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a compelling idea and demonstrates promising results. However, the limited experimental scope and lack of comparison to more modern techniques prevent it from reaching the high bar for acceptance at NeurIPS. While the insights into time scale hierarchies are valuable, the analysis would be strengthened by more rigorous experimentation and a more comprehensive comparison to existing literature.

**Recommendation:** Reject. However, I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit a revised version. Specifically, I recommend a more thorough hyperparameter search, a comparison to Transformer-based models, and a more nuanced analysis of the time scale experiments.  A clearer discussion of the DRNN’s limitations would also be beneficial.
