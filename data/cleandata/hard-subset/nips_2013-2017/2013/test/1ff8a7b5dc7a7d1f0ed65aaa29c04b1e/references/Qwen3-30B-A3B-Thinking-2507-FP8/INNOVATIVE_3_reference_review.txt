

# Review of "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks"

This paper introduces Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), a novel architecture that combines deep neural networks with recurrent structures. The key insight is that stacking RNNs (where each layer receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input) creates a natural temporal hierarchy, with different layers operating at different time scales. This contrasts with standard RNNs that process information at a single time scale, and with previous hierarchical RNN approaches that used subsampling. The authors argue that this architecture is particularly suited for tasks with long-term dependencies, such as character-level language modeling, where information spans multiple time scales (phonemes, syllables, words, etc.).

The paper's main contributions are threefold: (1) a novel DRNN architecture that inherently creates temporal hierarchies; (2) empirical evidence that DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks on Wikipedia character-level language modeling; and (3) analysis showing that DRNNs develop different time scales at different layers, with the top layers handling longer-term dependencies (evidenced through the parentheses-closing task and time-scale experiments).

The paper excels in originality. While there are related works (e.g., stacked LSTMs in [8] and hierarchical RNNs in [5]), the DRNN architecture proposed here is conceptually distinct—it's not merely stacking RNNs but creating a hierarchy where each layer processes information at a progressively longer time scale. This is a bold conceptual leap that rethinks how RNNs can be structured to handle temporal hierarchies. The paper's theoretical justification for why DRNNs create temporal hierarchies is clear and well-motivated, and the empirical validation through both performance metrics and the parentheses-closing task is compelling.

The paper's significance is strong. It addresses a fundamental limitation of standard RNNs (inability to handle multiple time scales) with a simple yet elegant architectural change. The results showing that DRNNs can model dependencies spanning hundreds of characters—far beyond what standard RNNs can achieve—demonstrate the practical impact of this insight. The analysis of layer contributions (e.g., the bottom layer being essential for basic character statistics, while the top layer captures longer patterns) provides valuable theoretical insights into how deep recurrence works.

While the paper is strong, it has minor limitations. The experiments are limited to only three network instantiations due to computational constraints, and the training time (10 days per network) is quite high. The comparison with state-of-the-art methods is not exhaustive (e.g., no direct comparison with modern RNN variants like LSTMs or GRUs), and the theoretical analysis of why DRNNs train more effectively could be deeper. However, these are minor concerns given the paper's conceptual novelty.

The paper's approach aligns perfectly with NeurIPS's preference for bold, novel ideas over incremental improvements. It's not merely a better RNN—it's a new way of thinking about recurrence, with clear theoretical justification and empirical validation. The authors have identified a fundamental limitation of standard RNNs and proposed a simple yet powerful architectural solution that creates natural temporal hierarchies.

I find the paper's main contribution to be highly significant. The DRNN concept could reshape how we design recurrent architectures, particularly for tasks with multi-scale temporal dependencies. The fact that the authors achieved state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks using only SGD (while others required Hessian-free optimization) further underscores the practical value of their approach.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Novel architecture that creates natural temporal hierarchies (conceptual breakthrough)
- Clear theoretical explanation of how DRNNs differ from standard RNNs
- Strong empirical validation on character-level language modeling
- Demonstrated ability to handle long-term dependencies (parentheses task)
- Opens new research directions (supervised pre-training, second-order methods)

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- Limited experimental scope (only three network instantiations)
- High computational cost (10 days per network)
- Some comparisons with standard RNN variants could be more thorough

Despite these minor limitations, this paper makes a substantial conceptual contribution that advances the field of deep learning. The DRNN architecture is not just another RNN variant—it fundamentally rethinks how recurrence can be structured to handle temporal hierarchies. Given NeurIPS's emphasis on novel ideas and bold approaches, this paper deserves acceptance. The authors have presented a compelling case for a new direction in recurrent network design, with both theoretical insights and empirical validation.
