This paper investigates deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) for character-level language modeling, combining aspects of deep neural networks and recurrent neural networks to create a hierarchical temporal structure. While the motivation for exploring such an architecture is reasonable – addressing potential limitations of standard RNNs in capturing multi-scale temporal dependencies – the presented work feels incremental and lacks the compelling evidence needed to justify its novelty within the context of NIPS.

The core idea of stacking RNNs to implicitly create different timescales is not entirely new, as acknowledged by the authors (citing [5]). The key contribution seems to be the specific DRNN architecture and its application to character-level language modeling, achieving competitive performance. However, the performance gains, while reported as state-of-the-art *for recurrent networks* with a similar parameter count, are not definitively shown to surpass results obtained with significantly different, more established methods like those employing Hessian-free optimization on the same dataset (as noted in the results section). The claim of outperforming prior work using only SGD is tempered by the fact that the prior state-of-the-art was achieved with a significantly different optimization strategy.  A direct comparison with Hessian-free optimization applied to the DRNN architecture would be critical.

The analysis of the learned timescales and layer contributions, while interesting, feels somewhat superficial. Figures 2 and 3 offer some insights, but the interpretations are tentative and lack quantitative rigor. The analysis of parenthesis matching, though illustrative, doesn’t constitute a robust evaluation of the model’s capabilities. The observations regarding the DRNN’s inability to handle nested parentheses or sentences starting with opening brackets are useful negative results, but don’t fundamentally validate the architectural design. 

Methodologically, the training procedure is described adequately, but the justification for the layer-wise training for DRNN-1O is weak. It’s presented as a necessity for achieving good performance, but lacks a clear explanation of *why* this is the case.  The parameter initialization details are reasonable, but the limited number of network instantiations (three) is a significant concern. Given the stochastic nature of training and the sensitivity of deep networks to initialization, three runs are insufficient to draw strong conclusions. The GPU constraints are understandable, but this limitation should be explicitly acknowledged as impacting the robustness of the results.

**Strengths:**

*   The DRNN architecture is a reasonable extension of existing RNN and DNN concepts.
*   The achieved performance is competitive with other recurrent network models on the chosen dataset.
*   The initial analysis of layer contributions and timescales provides some intuition about the network's behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The architectural novelty is limited, building on existing ideas in hierarchical RNNs.
*   The performance gains are not decisively superior to existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly those employing different optimization techniques.
*   The analysis is largely qualitative and lacks sufficient quantitative rigor.
*   The limited number of experimental runs weakens the statistical significance of the results.
*   The justification for the layer-wise training is not well explained.
*   Lack of comparison to more established baselines beyond recurrent networks.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a well-defined and implemented architecture.
*   The results are promising and warrant further investigation.
*   The analysis provides some initial insights into the behavior of DRNNs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks a compelling demonstration of significant novelty or advantage over existing methods.
*   The analysis is insufficient to fully understand the benefits of the DRNN architecture.
*   The limited experimental validation raises concerns about the robustness of the findings.
*   The paper largely re-confirms existing intuitions about hierarchical processing in recurrent networks without a substantial breakthrough.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper is technically sound and clearly written, it doesn't present a sufficiently compelling contribution to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The novelty is incremental, the performance gains are not definitive, and the analysis is limited. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art methods, a more rigorous analysis of the learned representations, and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation. The work, as presented, feels more suitable for a conference with less stringent requirements for novelty and impact.
