Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies stacked recurrent neural networks (“deep recurrent neural networks”, DRNNs) for character-level language modeling. Each layer is a simple tanh RNN that receives the hidden state of the previous layer; outputs are produced either only from the top layer (DRNN-1O) or from all layers (DRNN-AO). Training uses SGD with gradient normalization and, for DRNN-1O, an incremental layer-wise schedule in which each layer is temporarily the top supervised layer. On a large Wikipedia corpus (≈1.4B chars, 95-character alphabet), the authors report bits-per-character (BPC) comparable to prior state-of-the-art recurrent models of similar parameter count trained with Hessian-free (HF) optimization. They then analyze temporal dynamics via perturbation experiments (typo and context-switch), layer ablations, and a focused study of long-range tracking (parentheses), arguing that depth induces a hierarchy of time scales.

This work builds on a long line of hierarchical/stacked recurrent models: early hierarchical RNNs (El Hihi & Bengio, 1995), stacked/bidirectional LSTMs (Graves et al., 2013), multiplicative RNNs trained with HF (Sutskever et al., 2011), and related deep nets in speech and vision. The architectural idea here (stacked Elman RNNs) is straightforward, but the careful empirical analysis of time scales and layer contributions on a large, realistic corpus is valuable.

Strengths:
- Empirical result: With plain tanh RNNs and SGD (no HF), DRNNs achieve BPC in the ballpark of MRNNs with HF given a similar parameter budget. This is a useful datapoint in the SGD vs second-order debate.
- Clear architectural variants (top-only vs all-layer outputs) and a practical training schedule (incremental supervision) that seems necessary for DRNN-1O to work well.
- Insightful analyses: layer ablations, hidden-state perturbation decay, context-switch impact on BPC, and the parentheses tracking probe convincingly demonstrate emergent temporal hierarchies and the model’s ability to maintain long-range information.
- The paper articulates why deep supervision (outputs from each layer) can mitigate vanishing signals to lower layers and also serve as a probe for layer roles.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Novelty/significance of architecture: Stacked RNNs per se are not new, and prior work has argued for and demonstrated multiple time-scales in hierarchical recurrent models. The main novelty lies in the training schedule for DRNN-1O and the analyses. The paper would benefit from a more explicit contrast to existing HRNN/stacked RNN/LSTM results, and from acknowledging that depth-induced time scales have been hypothesized before.
- Missing baselines: No comparison to LSTM/GRU baselines (single-layer or stacked) on the same data and parameter budget. Given that LSTMs are specifically designed for long-term dependencies, this omission weakens the empirical claim about DRNNs modeling long dependencies. Even a single strong LSTM baseline would anchor results.
- Comparability of BPC: The alphabet differs from Sutskever et al. (95 vs 86 symbols). The authors argue the difference is negligible, but even rare symbols influence BPC on large corpora. A matched-vocabulary evaluation or a sensitivity check would strengthen the comparison.
- Limited experimental breadth and variance: Only three model instantiations, single runs, no error bars, and minimal hyperparameter exploration due to compute constraints. This makes it hard to assess robustness (e.g., learning rate choices, number of layers, width per layer).
- Training details clarity: The update rule in Eq. (3) seems inconsistent; “− η0 (− j/T)” would imply ascent for j>0. Presumably it should be η0 (1 − j/T). Please clarify. Also, gradient normalization is described as always normalizing by the norm; is there a threshold (i.e., clipping) or always unit-norm steps? Always-normalized steps approximate sign-SGD and can materially affect convergence; a brief ablation would be informative.
- Truncated BPTT vs claims of extremely long memory: Gradients are backpropagated at most ≈200 steps (250 length with 50 burn-in). The parentheses tracking beyond that horizon is interesting, but the paper should temper claims: generalization to longer spans may emerge from training on shorter spans, yet the model is not explicitly trained for longer horizons. A controlled synthetic task or a systematic length generalization test would support the claim more rigorously.
- Analysis methodology: Hidden-state distance uses Euclidean norm; because hidden activations can differ in scale across layers, a scale-invariant measure (cosine distance) or alignment to output sensitivity (e.g., influence on logits) could strengthen conclusions. The per-layer output ablation is informative but confounded by non-linear interactions; additional diagnostics (e.g., linear probes or information-theoretic measures) could provide cleaner attribution.

Quality:
The work is largely sound and the analyses are thoughtfully designed. Some methodological ambiguities (update rule, gradient normalization) and the lack of strong baselines limit the strength of the empirical claims.

Clarity:
Overall well-written and easy to follow. A few equations/initialization details need clarification (Eq. (3), justification for zero-initialized output weights, and precise gradient normalization procedure). Including the actual BPC numbers in the text (not just the table) and a brief description of validation/tuning would help.

Originality:
Moderate in architecture, stronger in analysis. The training schedule (incremental top-supervision) is a practical contribution. The temporal hierarchy analyses on a large corpus are useful and, to my knowledge, not commonly presented with this level of detail for stacked vanilla RNNs.

Significance:
Moderate. The results suggest that stacked simple RNNs trained with SGD can be competitive with more complex models trained with HF and that depth induces useful time-scale structure. The analyses are likely to be of interest to researchers thinking about depth in recurrent models and about training dynamics.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add at least one LSTM baseline (single- and stacked) with matched parameters, and report matched-alphabet BPC.
- Clarify and, if possible, ablate the gradient normalization/clipping and the linear LR decay schedule.
- Provide more detail on validation/tuning and report multiple runs or at least sensitivity to key hyperparameters.
- Explore varying layer widths (e.g., narrower lower layers, wider top) and residual/skip connections to test whether time scales can be shaped architecturally.
- Strengthen analysis with scale-invariant hidden-state metrics and probing classifiers for layer content (characters, n-grams, word boundaries).
- Consider evaluating on a second standard dataset (e.g., enwik8/text8 or PTB-char) to assess generality.

Recommendation: Weak accept. While the architectural novelty is limited, the empirical findings are solid and the analyses provide meaningful insights into how depth shapes temporal processing in RNNs. With clarifications and a stronger baseline comparison, this could be a valuable contribution.

Confidence: High in assessment of method/analysis; medium in comparative performance claims due to missing baselines and vocabulary mismatch.
