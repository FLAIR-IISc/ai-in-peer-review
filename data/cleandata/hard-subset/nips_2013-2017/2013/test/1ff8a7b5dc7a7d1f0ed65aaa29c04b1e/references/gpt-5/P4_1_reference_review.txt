Summary and relation to prior work
The paper investigates deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) formed by stacking simple tanh RNN layers, with temporal feedback within each layer and feedforward connections between layers. Two output configurations are studied: DRNN-1O (only top layer connects to softmax) and DRNN-AO (all layers connect to softmax and their logits are summed). The work targets character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus and emphasizes analysis: per-layer ablations, perturbation-based timescale measurements, and qualitative/quantitative evaluations of long-range dependencies (e.g., closing parentheses). Training uses SGD with backpropagation through time (BPTT), gradient normalization to unit norm, and a linearly decaying learning rate. For DRNN-1O, an incremental, layer-wise training schedule is used where each newly added layer becomes the sole output layer during its stage.

The paper situates itself relative to prior hierarchical RNN models [3,5,6] and stacked LSTMs [8], as well as multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization [19]. The main claims are: (i) DRNNs induce an emergent hierarchy of temporal scales across layers, (ii) they capture long-term dependencies hundreds of characters long, and (iii) with plain SGD they achieve competitive bits-per-character (BPC) to Hessian-free trained MRNNs at similar parameter counts.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architecture and training procedure; results attainable with SGD and standard BPTT.
- Thorough empirical analyses: per-layer output ablations, perturbation experiments (typo and context switch), and targeted behavioral probe (parentheses closure), all averaged over large samples.
- Evidence for emergent layer-wise temporal scales, including differences between DRNN-1O (long memory in top layers) and DRNN-AO (long memory concentrated at highest layers).
- Competitive character-level modeling results given similar parameter budgets, and a well-defined, widely used dataset.
- The incremental training for DRNN-1O is practical and helps mitigate vanishing gradients to lower layers.

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines and fairness: no direct comparison to stacked LSTMs/GRUs on the same dataset/parameter budget; the DRNN-1O benefits from staged training whereas DRNN-AO does not, complicating comparisons between the proposed variants and to prior work [8].
- Optimization details raise questions: gradient is normalized to unit norm at every step (uncommon; different from gradient clipping), and Eq. (3) appears to contain a sign/typo (the factor should be proportional to 1 − j/T, not − j/T). No comparison to standard clipping or adaptive optimizers.
- Experimental scope: only three model instantiations, single seeds, and no statistical variability estimates. No validation set or early stopping is described; overfitting/underfitting behavior is not reported.
- Dataset and evaluation details: the sequence sampling strategy resets hidden states between 250-character segments; the effect of truncation length (effective BPTT ~200 steps) on long-context modeling is not explored.
- Related work coverage: hierarchical/time-scale RNNs are cited, but the work would benefit from a more explicit contrast to stacked-gated RNNs on char-level modeling and to multi-timescale architectures (e.g., subsampling/clocked variants) addressing similar goals.
- Some design choices are insufficiently justified (zero initialization of output weights; identical layer widths; absence of regularization such as dropout/weight decay).

Quality
The methodology is generally sound. The analyses align with the claims about temporal hierarchy and long-term memory, and the ablations behave sensibly (top and bottom layers contribute most directly to output in DRNN-AO). The perturbation-based timescale measures are well thought out and averaged over many instances, though they rely on Euclidean distances in tanh space; more invariant probes (e.g., linear probes or CCA) could strengthen conclusions. The parentheses experiments convincingly show long-range tracking. Some optimization details are either unclear (Eq. 3) or unconventional (always-normalized gradients) without ablations to show their necessity versus standard practices.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and organized. The architectural description, training procedure, and experiments are easy to follow. Missing or unclear details include: exact hidden-state initialization per sequence chunk; validation protocol; rationale and effect of zero-initialized output weights; and a precise statement of BPTT truncation and its implications. Improving the exposition of Eq. (3) and explicitly contrasting DRNN-1O staged training with DRNN-AO training would aid clarity.

Originality
Stacking RNNs and exploiting hierarchical time scales have precedents [3,5,6,8]. The main novelty lies in (i) the simple stacked-tanh design trained end-to-end with SGD, (ii) the per-layer-output variant, and (iii) the set of analyses demonstrating emergent temporal hierarchies and long-context behavior on a real-world corpus. As an architectural contribution, this is incremental; as an empirical/analytical study, it is informative.

Significance
The results are practically relevant for character-level modeling and provide useful insights into how depth in RNNs can encode temporal hierarchies. The competitive BPC with simple training is valuable. However, the absence of comparisons to stacked LSTMs/GRUs and the limited breadth of baselines reduce the impact. The analytical methods could be adopted by others studying temporal behavior in deep sequence models.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Please correct/clarify Eq. (3). Is the intended schedule η(j) = η0(1 − j/T)? Are gradients always L2-normalized, or only clipped above a threshold?
- How are hidden states initialized between sampled sequences? Are states carried over between consecutive segments from the same document?
- Was a validation set used to select learning rates, truncation length, number of layers, or to determine the linear decay schedule?
- Can you provide results for DRNN-AO with the same staged training used for DRNN-1O, to ensure a fair comparison?
- How sensitive are results to BPTT length, gradient normalization vs clipping, and zero vs small random initialization of output weights?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear empirical evidence of emergent temporal hierarchy in stacked RNNs on a large, realistic task.
- Competitive performance with simple training; informative analyses and ablations.
- Useful methodological probes (perturbations, context switches, parentheses) that others can reuse.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited and potentially unfair baseline comparisons; missing stacked LSTM/GRU baselines.
- Unconventional optimization choices without ablation; some details unclear.
- Small number of runs; lack of variance estimates and validation protocol.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The analytical contributions and competitive results are valuable, but the paper would be strengthened by fairer baselines and clearer optimization details.
