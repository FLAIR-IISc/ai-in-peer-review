Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies stacked recurrent neural networks (deep RNNs; DRNNs) for character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus. Each layer is a tanh RNN; higher layers take the previous layer’s hidden state as input. The authors examine two output schemes: DRNN-1O (only the top layer connects to the softmax) and DRNN-AO (all layers connect to the softmax and their logits are summed). Training uses stochastic gradient descent with gradient normalization and a linearly decaying learning rate; DRNN-1O is trained in an incremental layer-wise fashion. The models are evaluated by bits-per-character (BPC), and analyzed for emergent time scales via perturbation experiments, for the contribution of individual layers (via ablating per-layer outputs), and for specific long-range dependency phenomena (parenthesis closing).

Relative to prior work: hierarchical/stacked recurrent architectures have been explored (e.g., El Hihi and Bengio, 1996; Fernández et al., 2005; Graves et al., 2013 on deep/stacked LSTM). Character-level modeling with RNNs including multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization on Wikipedia has been reported (Sutskever et al., 2011). The present work’s primary contributions are (i) a systematic empirical analysis of emergent layer-wise time scales in stacked simple RNNs trained by SGD, (ii) competitive BPC with simpler training compared to HF on models of comparable parameter count, and (iii) qualitative/quantitative probes of long-term dependencies (e.g., parentheses).

Strengths
- Clear architecture and training description; focuses on a practically relevant, large-scale character-level LM task.
- Empirical analyses of time scales are thoughtful: typo perturbation and context-switching tests quantify memory decay per layer and effect on prediction accuracy.
- Ablation of per-layer outputs provides insight into functional roles of layers (e.g., lower layer modeling local statistics; top layer capturing longer-range dependencies).
- Competitive BPC compared to prior RNNs of comparable size trained with HF, achieved with SGD and gradient normalization.
- The parentheses experiments (both discriminative probability traces and generative success rates) credibly demonstrate some long-range structure capture.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited. Stacking RNN layers and connecting multiple layers to outputs are known ideas; the main novelty lies in the analysis rather than in architecture or training methodology.
- Baseline coverage is incomplete. No LSTM/GRU baselines are reported, despite their prominence for long-term dependencies. The primary comparison is to mRNN + HF and PAQ, with some dataset/setup differences (95 vs 86 characters); this makes “state-of-the-art for recurrent networks” claims harder to substantiate.
- Only three model instantiations were tested, each trained once; there is no variance analysis across random seeds. The assertion that large models have “very little difference” across initializations is not quantified.
- Some training details are ambiguous or potentially problematic:
  - The learning rate schedule in Eq. (3) appears to contain a sign/typo (η0(−j/T) would invert sign mid-training); likely η0(1 − j/T). Please clarify.
  - Normalizing gradients to unit norm at every step is a strong heuristic; contrast with clipping (rescaling only above a threshold). Convergence properties and sensitivity are not examined.
  - Output weights initialized to zero may induce symmetry; a brief justification or ablation would help.
  - No validation protocol is described (e.g., for early stopping or schedule tuning); overfitting is not discussed.
- The DRNN-1O “incremental layer-wise” training discards previously learned output weights when adding layers; it would be useful to quantify the cost/benefit versus retaining or jointly fine-tuning all outputs.
- BPTT truncation to 200 steps contrasts with claims of several-hundred-character dependencies; while the qualitative experiments are suggestive, a controlled sweep of truncation lengths would strengthen the claim that attractor-like states enable generalization beyond the truncated horizon.
- Related work could be broadened (e.g., Mikolov’s RNN language models; work on gradient clipping/rescaling for RNNs; more on stacked RNNs in language modeling).

Quality
- The empirical methodology is generally sound, and the analyses are insightful. However, the lack of stronger baselines (LSTM/GRU) and limited runs constrain the strength of the performance claims. A few technical ambiguities (learning rate schedule, gradient normalization protocol) should be corrected/clarified. Statistical variability and hyperparameter sensitivity are not explored.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear and well-organized. Figures (referred to but not shown here) seem to play an important role in the analysis. Minor notational issues: clarify Eq. (3) and the gradient normalization rule; explicitly state whether biases are trained; ensure consistent reporting of character set differences and their expected impact. Reference formatting is uneven.

Originality
- Architectural novelty is modest; stacked RNNs and multi-output connections have prior art. The paper’s originality lies in the careful analyses of emergent temporal hierarchies and the practical demonstration that simple SGD with gradient normalization suffices to reach competitive performance on large-scale character LM with stacked simple RNNs.

Significance
- The analysis of time scales in DRNNs is valuable and likely to inform future design/diagnostics. The performance results, while competitive within simple-RNN families, would be more impactful with stronger baselines and tighter experimental controls. The training insights (incremental layer-wise, per-layer outputs) may be useful to practitioners.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Insightful analyses demonstrating learned temporal hierarchies across layers.
- Competitive performance with simple training on a large corpus.
- Clear experimental probes (typo perturbations, context switch, parentheses).

Cons:
- Limited novelty in architecture; incomplete baselines (no LSTM/GRU).
- Ambiguities in training details; limited runs and no variance analysis.
- Dataset/setup differences complicate SOTA claims.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add LSTM/GRU baselines of comparable parameter count and training budget.
- Clarify Eq. (3), document gradient normalization (thresholds, effect), and report validation protocol.
- Report mean ± std over multiple seeds for key results; include sensitivity to BPTT length.
- Explore retaining multi-layer outputs during incremental training; report impact.
- Expand related work (RNN LMs by Mikolov; gradient clipping/rescaling for RNNs) and discuss distinctions.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper’s main contribution is a careful empirical analysis of stacked simple RNNs revealing emergent temporal hierarchies and demonstrating solid performance with simple training. Strengthening baselines and clarifying training details would substantially improve the contribution.
