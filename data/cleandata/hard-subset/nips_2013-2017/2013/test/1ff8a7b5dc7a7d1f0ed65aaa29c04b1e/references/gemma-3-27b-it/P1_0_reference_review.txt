## NIPS Review: Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks

**Summary:**

This paper explores the use of Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs) – essentially stacked RNNs – for character-based language modeling. The authors motivate this architecture by highlighting the limitations of both traditional Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) and single-layer RNNs, arguing that DRNNs naturally capture hierarchical temporal dependencies. They present experiments on a large Wikipedia corpus, achieving state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks using only stochastic gradient descent (SGD), rivaling results previously obtained with more complex Hessian-free optimization.  Crucially, the paper also attempts an analysis of *how* the DRNN architecture relates to the task, investigating layer-wise timescales and output contributions.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds on the established success of RNNs and DNNs in various domains (speech recognition, digit recognition – cited).  It acknowledges existing hierarchical RNN architectures ([5]), but points out their limitation to artificial tasks. It explicitly positions itself relative to recent work by Martens and colleagues [8, 19] on TIMIT phoneme recognition and Wikipedia text modeling, improving upon their performance using a simpler optimization method, while attempting a more in-depth analysis than their primarily performance-focused work. The paper also correctly connects its work to the broader literature on long-term dependency modeling, referencing LSTM [11] and related techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The DRNN achieves state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks on a challenging language modeling task using a relatively straightforward training procedure (SGD). This is a significant achievement.
* **Clear Motivation & Intuition:** The paper provides a compelling argument for the potential benefits of combining the strengths of DNNs (hierarchical processing) and RNNs (temporal memory). The analogy of different timescales arising naturally from stacking RNNs is insightful.
* **Analysis of Internal Representations:**  The attempt to analyze layer-wise timescales and output contributions is a valuable addition. The experiments involving perturbation and output layer ablation provide some evidence supporting the claim that DRNNs learn hierarchical representations.  The analysis of parenthesis handling is particularly clever and provides concrete evidence of long-term dependency capture.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally clear and well-structured, making it relatively easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors provide details about network size, learning rates, and training procedure, aiding reproducibility.  The availability of the code and data (mentioned in a footnote) is a further positive aspect.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Analysis:** While the analysis is a strength, it remains somewhat superficial.  The perturbation experiments provide correlations but don’t establish *causation*.  It would be beneficial to see more quantitative analysis of the hidden state representations to understand what each layer is actually learning. For example, visualizing the hidden state activations or performing representational similarity analysis could provide deeper insights.
* **Lack of Comparison to Modern RNN Variants:** The comparison is primarily to a standard RNN and to prior work using Hessian-free optimization.  It would be more informative to compare the DRNN to more recent, powerful RNN architectures like GRUs, or transformers, even if at a different parameter count. This would better position the contribution of the DRNN.
* **Parameter Sensitivity & Ablation Studies:** The paper mentions carefully tuning learning rates. A more thorough ablation study varying the number of layers, number of units per layer, and different output connection strategies (DRNN-1O vs. DRNN-AO) would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Scalability Concerns:** The authors acknowledge the difficulty of exploiting massively parallel computing for SGD with these large models.  This raises concerns about the scalability of the approach to even larger datasets or more complex tasks.
* **Figure Clarity:** Some figures (e.g., Figure 3) could benefit from clearer labeling and more detailed explanations.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging task.
* Presents a novel and well-motivated architecture.
* Includes a preliminary but insightful analysis of learned representations.
* Is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Analysis is somewhat limited and relies heavily on correlation rather than causation.
* Lacks comparison to more recent, competitive RNN architectures.
* Scalability concerns are raised, requiring further investigation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of deep recurrent neural networks. The authors demonstrate a compelling architecture that achieves strong empirical results and offers some initial insights into its inner workings. While the analysis could be more in-depth and the comparison to more recent architectures is lacking, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance.

**Rating:** Accept (7/10)

**Recommendations:**

* Encourage the authors to perform more thorough ablation studies and more quantitative analysis of the hidden state representations.
* Suggest comparing the DRNN to more recent RNN variants (GRUs, Transformers) to better contextualize the results.
* Request clarification of the scalability limitations and potential solutions.
* Minor revisions to improve figure clarity and labeling.
