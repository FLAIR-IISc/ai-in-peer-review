

This paper proposes Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), a stacked architecture where each layer is an RNN that processes the hidden state of the layer below, creating a temporal hierarchy. The authors argue that DRNNs naturally develop distinct time scales across layers (bottom layers for short-term dependencies, top layers for long-term), and demonstrate this through character-level language modeling on Wikipedia text. They claim DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art results with standard SGD (avoiding complex Hessian-free optimization) and provide empirical evidence via BPC metrics, layer ablation studies, and time-scale analysis (e.g., typo perturbation and parenthesis closure tests).

**Strengths:**  
1. **Strong empirical results:** DRNNs (especially DRNN-1O) match the SOTA performance of [19] (trained with Hessian-free) using only SGD, a significant practical contribution. The BPC scores (1.452 vs. [19]'s 1.438) are compelling and well-validated.  
2. **Insightful analysis:** The time-scale experiments (Fig. 3) convincingly show layers develop distinct memory properties (bottom layers short-term, top layers long-term), and the parenthesis closure test (Fig. 4) demonstrates DRNNs model long-range dependencies better than RNNs. This analysis is a key strength, moving beyond raw performance to *why* DRNNs work.  
3. **Clear methodology:** The architecture (Fig. 1), training details (Sec. 2.3), and ablation studies (layer removal, generative text samples) are well-organized and reproducible. The layer-wise training for DRNN-1O is a thoughtful practical solution to vanishing gradients.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Limited scope and novelty:** The core architecture (stacked RNNs) is not new—[8] used stacked bi-directional LSTMs for speech, and [5] proposed hierarchical RNNs for artificial tasks. The paper’s contribution is primarily *analysis* of time-scale hierarchies in a *specific* DRNN setup (simple RNNs, not LSTMs), but this isn’t sufficiently distinguished from prior work. The claim that DRNNs "automatically create different time scales" is intuitive for stacked architectures and lacks theoretical grounding.  
2. **Insufficient experimental breadth:** Only one dataset (Wikipedia text) and three network instantiations are tested. No comparison to recent SOTA models (e.g., LSTMs with modern training) or other tasks (speech, video) is provided. The "state-of-the-art" claim relies on a single prior work ([19]), ignoring more recent benchmarks.  
3. **Underdeveloped theoretical claims:** The paper asserts DRNNs "embed time scales directly in their structure," but the evidence is purely empirical. No formal analysis (e.g., of the hidden state dynamics or time-scale emergence) is provided. The training method (layer-wise for DRNN-1O) is also not compared to standard pretraining (e.g., DBN-based), weakening the practical contribution.  
4. **Overstated significance:** The claim that DRNNs "advance the state of the art" is misleading—BPC improvements are marginal (1.452 vs. 1.438), and the architecture itself isn’t novel. The significance lies in the *analysis*, not the model, which isn’t highlighted enough.  

**Recommendation:** **Reject** (borderline, but weaknesses outweigh strengths).  

**Constructive suggestions for improvement:**  
1. **Clarify novelty:** Explicitly contrast DRNNs (simple RNNs) with [8] (LSTMs) and [5] (artificial tasks). Emphasize that *time-scale hierarchy is a learned property* (not inherent to stacking), and position the work as an *analysis* of hierarchical RNNs rather than a *new model*.  
2. **Expand experiments:** Test on additional tasks (e.g., speech recognition, Penn Treebank) and compare to recent SOTA (e.g., [16] or [13]). Include statistical significance tests across multiple random seeds (not just one instantiation).  
3. **Strengthen theoretical claims:** Add a brief analysis of how layer depth affects time-scale learning (e.g., via hidden state covariance or gradient flow). Discuss limitations (e.g., DRNNs may not generalize to non-temporal hierarchical tasks).  
4. **Refine significance:** Shift focus from "state-of-the-art" to "insights into hierarchical temporal processing," aligning with the paper’s strongest contribution (the time-scale analysis).  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
- *Pro:* Compelling empirical validation of time-scale hierarchies; practical SGD success; clear analysis of layer roles.  
- *Con:* Limited novelty (builds on existing stacked RNN work); narrow scope; insufficient comparison to recent SOTA; overclaiming significance.  

The paper makes a valid technical contribution to understanding RNN dynamics but fails to demonstrate sufficient novelty or breadth for NIPS. With expanded experiments and sharper positioning, it could be a strong candidate. As-is, it’s a solid but incremental work that doesn’t advance the field beyond the current state of the art.
