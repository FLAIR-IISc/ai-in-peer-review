Summary
This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) formed by stacking vanilla tanh RNN layers, with two output designs: (i) DRNN-1O, where only the top layer connects to the softmax; and (ii) DRNN-AO, where all layers contribute to the output. The authors train with SGD, gradient normalization (global norm), and a linearly decaying learning rate; for DRNN-1O they use an incremental layer-wise training schedule where each added layer becomes the sole output layer for a stage. On a large Wikipedia character-level language modeling task, both DRNN variants outperform a single-layer RNN under a fixed parameter budget (~4.9M) and approach the bpc of multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-Free (HF) optimization. Beyond accuracy, the paper analyzes temporal behavior: (a) per-layer “time scales” via a typo-perturbation and context-switch experiments; (b) ablation of per-layer readout contributions; and (c) qualitative and quantitative evidence that DRNNs maintain long-range dependencies (e.g., closing parentheses) over hundreds of characters. The work relates to stacked/bi-directional LSTMs for speech (Graves et al.) and earlier hierarchical RNNs (El Hihi & Bengio; Fernández et al.), and to MRNNs trained with HF (Sutskever et al.).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The models and training are standard; the analysis methods are sensible. The gradient normalization and linear LR decay are reasonable heuristics. The temporal analyses (state-distance after a typo, context-switch bpc, per-layer ablations) provide informative diagnostics.
- Empirical support: Results show consistent gains over a matched-parameter single-layer RNN and competitive bpc vs MRNN+HF. The qualitative generation and quantitative parentheses test further support the claim that DRNNs learn multi-scale structure and long-range dependencies.
- Caveats:
  - Novelty in modeling is limited: DRNN is essentially a stacked RNN with either top-only or all-layer readouts; stacked RNNs are known and used (e.g., stacked LSTMs). The contribution is more empirical/analytical than architectural.
  - Comparisons are incomplete: No LSTM/GRU baselines (single or stacked), which are de facto standards for long dependencies. The MRNN comparison uses different training algorithms; training budgets differ and are not strictly controlled for passes over data and compute.
  - Fairness of DRNN-1O vs DRNN-AO comparison: they use different training schedules and total data passes (about 10 vs 7), which likely affects the slight performance gap.
  - Limited robustness: Only three model instantiations due to compute; no variance across random seeds, no hyperparameter sweeps, and no regularization exploration.
  - Some design choices lack justification: initializing output weights at zero and using Z1 ~ N(0,1) vs other weights ~ N(0,N^{-1/2}) may cause scale mismatches; implications are not analyzed. Truncated BPTT length (useful context during backprop ~200) could constrain learnable dependencies, yet the paper claims very long-term modeling; more controlled tests would help.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The architecture and training are described precisely; experiments are explained and figures/tables (as described) seem informative. A few suggestions:
- Clarify the exact number of epochs/data passes and wall-clock cost for each model, to enable fairer comparison against MRNN+HF and between DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO.
- Provide rationale for initialization schemes and zero-initialized output weights.
- Explicitly state the truncation length of BPTT used for gradient propagation (after discarding the first 50 steps).
- Clean up footnotes and provide complete, consistent references.

Originality
Architecturally, stacking vanilla RNNs and using per-layer readouts is a relatively modest extension of prior work. The main novelty lies in:
- Demonstrating that deep vanilla RNNs trained with simple SGD can approach MRNN+HF performance at comparable parameter counts on a large text task.
- Careful, layer-wise temporal analyses showing emergent time scales and long-term interactions (e.g., parentheses), and ablation-based insights into which layers contribute to different aspects of prediction.
These analyses are useful and, to my knowledge, more thorough than typical at the time for vanilla stacked RNNs.

Significance
The work offers practical evidence that depth alone, even without gating, improves character-level modeling, and that SGD with basic stabilization can be competitive with HF on large corpora. The temporal diagnostics are valuable to the community and may inspire further probing of hierarchical temporal representations. However, the impact is somewhat limited by missing comparisons to LSTM/GRU, which were already influential for long-range dependencies, and by the modest architectural novelty.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Solid empirical improvements over a strong single-layer RNN at matched parameter count.
- Competitive results with a simpler training algorithm than MRNN+HF.
- Insightful analyses demonstrating emergent temporal hierarchies and long-term dependency handling, with both quantitative (bpc perturbations, parentheses success rates) and qualitative evidence.
- Clear presentation and reproducible-enough training details.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Limited novelty: stacked RNNs and multi-scale intuitions are not new; prior work (e.g., stacked LSTMs) already reports strong gains.
- Missing key baselines (LSTM/GRU), making it hard to position the contribution relative to state of the art.
- Unequal training schedules between DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO; unclear compute/data parity vs MRNN+HF.
- Minimal hyperparameter exploration and no variance reporting; only three total runs.
- Some design/initialization choices unmotivated; references incomplete.

Questions for the authors
1. How do results compare to stacked LSTM or GRU under the same parameter budget and training budget? Can you include at least a 2-layer LSTM baseline?
2. Can you equalize the number of data passes for DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO to isolate the effect of the readout design?
3. What is the exact truncated BPTT length used for gradients, and how sensitive are results to this choice?
4. Why initialize Z1 with larger variance than other matrices, and why zero-initialize U? Did you try alternative schemes (e.g., orthogonal recurrent init, Glorot/He for input/output)?
5. How variable are results across random seeds?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add LSTM/GRU baselines and report multiple seeds.
- Report compute budget (GPU hours), data passes, and parameter-update counts per model to enable fair comparisons.
- Explore per-layer sizes or bottlenecks; consider residual/skip connections to ease credit assignment.
- Compare gradient normalization with gradient clipping; analyze training stability quantitatively.
- Release code and trained models, and provide full reference details.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The architectural novelty is modest, but the empirical findings and temporal analyses are informative and of interest to the NIPS community. Strengthening baselines and clarifying training budgets would significantly improve the paper. Confidence: medium.
