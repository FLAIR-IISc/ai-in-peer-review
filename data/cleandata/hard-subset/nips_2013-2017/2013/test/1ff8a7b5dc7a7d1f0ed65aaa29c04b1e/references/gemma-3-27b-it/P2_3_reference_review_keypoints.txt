- Paper studies Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (stacked RNNs) for character-level language modeling and argues they capture multi-scale temporal dependencies.
- Achieves state-of-the-art performance on a Wikipedia corpus using simple SGD, comparable to Hessian-free results.
- Provides empirical analysis of learned temporal hierarchy: layer-wise time scales, per-layer contribution to output, and handling of long-range dependencies.
- Novel combination of DNN and RNN layers that addresses multi-scale temporal processing, though stacking RNNs has prior precedents.
- Strong empirical results demonstrating effectiveness and relative ease of training compared to more complex methods.
- Analysis of internal dynamics is a key strength, offering insight beyond performance numbers.
- Clear writing and well-explained motivation, architecture, and experiments.
- Thorough experimentation including ablations and multiple analyses supporting claims.
- Limited theoretical justification for why stacking yields different time scales; explanations are mainly intuitive.
- Comparison to baselines is limited—needs more benchmarks with other RNN variants (e.g., LSTM, GRU) under comparable settings.
- Training details (learning rate schedules, initialization choices) are under-specified and not fully justified.
- Analysis is task-limited (single character-level modeling task), so generalizability to other sequence tasks is unclear.
- Some figure quality issues (Figure 3 lacks clear axis labels/units).
- Evaluation scores: Quality 4/5, Clarity 5/5, Originality 3.5/5, Significance 4/5.
- Pros for acceptance: strong empirical results, insightful analysis, useful architecture, clear presentation.
- Cons for acceptance: limited theoretical grounding, incremental novelty, limited demonstrated generalization.
- Overall assessment: strong accept; paper is valuable but would benefit from stronger theory and more training/baseline details.
