

# Review of "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks"

This paper proposes a "Deep Recurrent Neural Network" (DRNN) architecture that combines elements of deep neural networks and recurrent neural networks, claiming to create a natural temporal hierarchy with different time scales at different layers. The authors present experiments on character-level language modeling, claiming state-of-the-art performance with standard SGD training, and argue that DRNNs better capture temporal hierarchies compared to conventional RNNs.

The paper builds on prior work exploring hierarchical RNNs (citing [3,5,6,8]), but its core contribution appears to be a specific implementation where each layer is a recurrent unit that receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input. The authors make several claims: (1) DRNNs automatically create different time scales at different layers, (2) DRNNs can model long-term dependencies more effectively than conventional RNNs, and (3) DRNNs can achieve state-of-the-art performance with standard SGD training rather than more complex optimization methods.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with helpful figures illustrating the architecture and experimental results. The experimental design includes interesting tests to probe temporal hierarchies (typos, parentheses closing), and the paper acknowledges limitations in its experimental scope (e.g., limited number of network instantiations, single GPU experiments). The authors are transparent about the marginal performance improvements compared to previous work.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the promising claims, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses that undermine its contribution. Most critically, the claimed advantages of DRNNs over conventional RNNs are not rigorously validated. The performance gains are marginal (e.g., DRNN-1O achieves 1.45 BPC vs. RNN's 1.51 BPC), and the paper fails to demonstrate that these gains are statistically significant or robust across multiple datasets and architectures. The comparison to state-of-the-art methods is inadequate; the paper cites [19] as the benchmark, but this work itself used Hessian-free optimization and was trained on a different dataset (with different character sets and evaluation metrics). The authors do not compare to modern RNN variants like LSTMs or GRUs, which have become standard for language modeling tasks.

The paper's central claim about "temporal hierarchy" is particularly under-supported. While the authors present some interesting experiments showing different layers respond to perturbations at different time scales, they don't convincingly demonstrate that this hierarchy provides a fundamental advantage for language modeling. The experiments on parentheses closing (Figure 4) are intriguing but limited to a single task, and the paper doesn't show how these capabilities translate to broader language modeling performance.

Most concerning is the lack of thorough validation against established baselines. The authors claim their DRNN-1O "reaches state-of-the-art performance" but provide no comparison to the standard LSTM or GRU architectures that have dominated language modeling for years. The paper's focus on matching the results of [19] (which used a different training method and dataset) rather than comparing to more relevant baselines suggests the claimed advantages are marginal at best.

The paper also fails to adequately address why DRNNs would be preferable to existing approaches. The authors mention that their DRNN-AO "suffers from pathological curvature" but don't demonstrate how their solution (output connections at each layer) is superior to existing techniques like layer-wise pre-training or skip connections. The training method described for DRNN-1O (incremental layer-wise training) is not novel and has been used in many deep learning contexts.

**Significance and Originality:** The DRNN architecture represents a minor variation of existing hierarchical RNN approaches rather than a significant conceptual advance. Previous work by El Hihi & Bengio [5] and Graves et al. [8] has already explored similar hierarchical RNN architectures, and the current paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that its specific implementation offers meaningful improvements. The paper's main contribution appears to be empirical validation of an existing concept rather than a novel methodological advance.

**Conclusion:** While the paper presents an interesting architecture and some intriguing experimental results, the lack of rigorous validation against established baselines and the marginal performance gains over existing methods significantly undermine its contribution. The paper makes several bold claims about temporal hierarchy and performance advantages that are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. Given the current state of the art in RNNs (where LSTMs and GRUs have become standard), the paper fails to demonstrate that DRNNs offer a meaningful advance in either methodology or performance. The experiments are limited in scope and fail to provide conclusive evidence for the claimed advantages.

I recommend rejection. The paper would be significantly strengthened by (1) more comprehensive comparison to standard RNN variants like LSTMs and GRUs, (2) larger-scale experiments across multiple datasets, (3) rigorous statistical validation of performance differences, and (4) clearer demonstration of why the temporal hierarchy is beneficial rather than just observing its presence. As currently written, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to justify its claims or demonstrate a meaningful contribution to the field.
