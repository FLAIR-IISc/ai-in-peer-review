Summary
The paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) formed by stacking vanilla RNN layers, with recurrent connections within each layer and feedforward connections between layers. Two output configurations are considered: (i) top-layer only (DRNN-1O) and (ii) all layers connected to the output (DRNN-AO), the latter doubling as “deep supervision” that eases optimization and enables per-layer contribution analyses. Training is done with SGD, backpropagation through time, gradient normalization, a linearly decaying learning rate, and an incremental layer-wise schedule for DRNN-1O.

The main empirical focus is character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus (≈1.4B chars; 10M for test), keeping total parameters ≈4.9M across models for comparability. The paper reports that both DRNN variants substantially outperform a single-layer RNN of the same size and are competitive with multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization. Beyond accuracy, the paper provides a series of analyses showing that (i) stacked RNNs learn a temporal hierarchy (lower layers react to short-term perturbations, upper layers retain long memory), (ii) context length used for prediction is longer in DRNNs than in a standard RNN, and (iii) the model captures long-term dependencies such as closing parentheses in both discriminative and generative modes. The authors argue that DRNNs can model multi-scale temporal structure and long-range dependencies without specialized gating and that simple SGD suffices at this scale.

Relation to prior work
– Hierarchical/stacked RNNs have a history (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio; Fernández/Graves/Schmidhuber), and stacked bi-directional LSTMs have achieved strong speech results (Graves et al.). The paper positions itself as a simpler stacked vanilla RNN architecture with analysis.
– Multiplicative RNNs trained with HF (Sutskever et al.) are the main performance comparator on similar data.
– The all-layers-to-output design relates to deep supervision strategies and multi-scale outputs in deep networks.
The contribution over prior work is less about a new architectural primitive and more about (a) systematic empirical analysis of emergent time scales in stacked vanilla RNNs on a real, large-scale text task, and (b) showing competitive performance with SGD-only training.

Strengths
– Clear, compelling empirical analyses of temporal hierarchy: typo-perturbation decay, context switch effects on BPC, and per-layer output ablations. These are insightful and likely to be used by others.
– Demonstrates that simple stacked RNNs trained with SGD can be competitive with more complex models/training (MRNN + HF) at similar parameter budgets; this is a valuable message for practitioners.
– The incremental training scheme for DRNN-1O is pragmatic and appears to induce longer time scales in higher layers; the comparison between DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO is informative.
– The parentheses case study (both discriminative outputs and generative success rates) provides concrete, interpretable evidence of long-term dependency handling.
– Paper is generally well written and organized, and places the work in context.

Weaknesses
– Novelty is moderate. Stacking RNNs and feeding outputs from all layers are known ideas; the architectural contribution per se is incremental. The paper’s unique value lies mainly in the analyses rather than a fundamentally new model.
– Missing strong baselines: no LSTM/GRU comparison. Given that LSTMs are the de facto approach for long-term dependencies, this omission weakens claims about long-range modeling advantages and practical significance.
– Limited experimental breadth and ablation rigor: only one dataset, three model instantiations, and few hyperparameter/control checks. The layer-wise training’s effect on performance vs. timescale properties is acknowledged but not deeply quantified.
– Fairness of “state-of-the-art” claim: the character vocabulary differs from the MRNN setup; while argued to be negligible, the paper should report exact BPCs side-by-side with clear training budgets (updates, passes over data) to support the claim.
– Reproducibility gaps: gradient normalization details (epsilon, clipping vs. normalization), exact batching/sampling protocol, and code availability are not specified. Output weights initialized at zero is unusual; some justification and sensitivity analysis would help.
– Some analyses (e.g., removing a single layer’s output) have limited interpretability due to inter-layer dependencies; the paper notes this but still leans on these results.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with sensible training and evaluation. The analytical experiments are well designed and averaged over many instances. However, the empirical claims would be strengthened by (i) LSTM baselines, (ii) reporting variance over runs, and (iii) tighter accounting of training compute.

Clarity
Mostly clear. Figures/tables (not included here) reportedly support claims. Suggested clarifications: exact BPC numbers for all models; precise parameter counts per component; gradient normalization procedure; sampling scheme (overlap, reset state policy); and the effect of different BPTT lengths.

Originality
Architecturally incremental; analytical contributions are the most original aspect. The demonstration that stacked vanilla RNNs learn layer-wise time scales on a large, real dataset is a valuable insight.

Significance
Moderate to high. The analyses are likely to influence how researchers think about depth in RNNs, and the observation that simple SGD suffices at this scale is practically important. Lack of LSTM comparisons limits impact.

Questions for authors
1) Please report exact test BPCs for all models alongside parameter counts and training updates/passes to substantiate “state-of-the-art among RNNs” claims.
2) How do results compare to LSTM/GRU with similar parameter budgets and training compute?
3) What are the details of gradient normalization (norm target, epsilon), and how sensitive are results to this choice?
4) How are hidden states handled between minibatches? Do you reset at sequence boundaries?
5) How were per-layer outputs combined in DRNN-AO (simple sum followed by softmax); did you try learnable layer-wise weights?
6) Any regularization (dropout, weight decay)? If not, did you observe overfitting?
7) Will code and scripts be released?

Pros for acceptance
– Insightful, convincing analyses of emergent temporal hierarchies in stacked RNNs.
– Strong empirical performance with simple training; practical relevance.
– Clear and timely message that depth in RNNs can capture multi-scale dynamics without gates.
– Useful ablations (DRNN-1O vs DRNN-AO; per-layer contributions; generative behavior).

Cons for acceptance
– Architecturally incremental; novelty rests mainly in analysis.
– Missing LSTM/GRU baselines reduce the strength of conclusions.
– Limited experimental breadth; only one dataset and few configurations.
– Some reproducibility and fairness details under-specified.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper’s analytical contributions and the practical demonstration that stacked vanilla RNNs trained with SGD achieve competitive performance merit publication, despite the incremental architecture and missing modern baselines. Strengthening the empirical section with LSTM comparisons and clearer reporting would elevate the paper further.

Reviewer confidence
Medium.
