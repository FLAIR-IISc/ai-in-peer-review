

# Peer Review: "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), a novel architecture that combines deep neural networks with recurrent structures. The authors propose a multi-layer architecture where each layer is a recurrent network, and each layer receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input. They claim this automatically creates different time scales at different levels, forming a temporal hierarchy. The paper demonstrates DRNNs on character-level language modeling (Wikipedia text corpus), showing state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks using only SGD, which previously required more advanced Hessian-free optimization.

The paper builds on prior work on hierarchical RNNs [3,5,6] and references [8] which used stacked bidirectional LSTMs for TIMIT phoneme recognition but lacked architectural analysis. The authors correctly note that previous deep RNN work has focused on performance rather than understanding the architecture's properties, which this paper attempts to address.

## Strengths

The paper makes several valuable contributions. First, the empirical demonstration that DRNNs can achieve state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks using only SGD is significant, as it challenges the notion that advanced optimization methods are necessary for RNNs. Second, the analysis of time scales across layers is compelling - the paper provides concrete evidence through the typo-perturbation experiment that different layers operate at different temporal scales, with bottom layers having shorter time scales and top layers having longer memory. Third, the parentheses-closing test is an elegant demonstration of the DRNN's ability to model long-term dependencies, showing that the top layer maintains high probability for closing parentheses even after hundreds of characters.

The paper also offers useful methodological insights, particularly regarding the training of DRNNs. The authors demonstrate that the DRNN-AO architecture (with outputs from all layers) helps mitigate the vanishing gradient problem in deep RNNs, and their analysis of layer importance through output contribution removal provides valuable diagnostic tools.

## Weaknesses

Despite these strengths, the paper has several significant weaknesses. First, the novelty claim is questionable. The DRNN architecture closely resembles stacked LSTMs or GRUs, which have been extensively studied and used in practice (e.g., [8, 11, 16]). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish their architecture from these existing approaches, failing to clarify what makes their DRNN fundamentally different from standard stacked RNNs.

Second, the theoretical justification for the temporal hierarchy is weak. The paper claims that "stacking RNNs automatically creates different time scales," but provides no theoretical analysis to support this. The empirical evidence is limited to a single dataset and task, without exploring how the time scale hierarchy might vary across different data modalities or tasks.

Third, the comparison to previous work is superficial. The paper cites [19] as the state-of-the-art, but doesn't compare to more recent work on deep RNNs (e.g., [16, 19] themselves have been followed by more recent advances). The claim of "state-of-the-art performance" is questionable given that [19] itself was later surpassed by other methods.

Fourth, the paper doesn't adequately address computational costs. While they claim their DRNN achieves similar performance to Hessian-free methods with "roughly 4 times less computational cost," this is misleading since they don't specify the exact hardware or training duration comparison. The paper's training takes "about 10 days" on a single GPU, while [19] used "a cluster of 8 GPUs" for "5 days" - the actual computational cost ratio is unclear.

Finally, the paper's analysis of layer importance is limited. The experiments where they set layer outputs to zero are not fully interpretable due to potential synergistic effects between layers, as the authors themselves acknowledge.

## Significance and Recommendation

The paper makes a modest contribution to understanding how deep architectures can be combined with recurrence, but the novelty is limited and the theoretical foundation is weak. The empirical results are interesting but not transformative - the performance gains are marginal compared to existing stacked RNN approaches, and the computational cost comparison is flawed.

The paper's significance is primarily methodological rather than empirical. It provides useful insights about the temporal hierarchy in DRNNs, but these insights don't clearly translate to major advances in the field. For NIPS, the paper would be stronger if it provided a more rigorous theoretical foundation for the time-scale hierarchy, better distinguished from existing deep RNN architectures, and offered more compelling evidence of the DRNN's advantages beyond the specific language modeling task.

**Recommendation: Weak Reject**

The paper has interesting ideas but falls short on novelty, theoretical foundation, and thorough comparison with prior work. The claims of state-of-the-art performance are not sufficiently supported, and the methodological contribution is limited compared to the extensive literature on deep RNNs. The paper would benefit from significantly more careful comparison with existing work, clearer theoretical justification, and more rigorous experimental design before being suitable for NIPS.
