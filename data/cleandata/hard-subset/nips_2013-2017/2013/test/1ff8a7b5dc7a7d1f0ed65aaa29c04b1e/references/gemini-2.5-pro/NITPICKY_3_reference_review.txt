**Summary:**

This paper investigates the properties of Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), constructed by stacking multiple RNN layers. The authors propose and compare two variants: one with output connections only from the top layer (DRNN-1O) and one with connections from all layers (DRNN-AO). They evaluate these architectures on a large-scale character-level language modeling task (Wikipedia corpus), achieving performance comparable to the state-of-the-art Hessian-Free trained models using only stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The paper's main contribution is not just the performance, but an analysis that suggests these deep architectures learn a temporal hierarchy, with higher layers operating on longer time scales, which they demonstrate through a series of analytical experiments.

**Strengths:**

1.  The paper addresses an important and intuitive architecture—the stacking of RNNs—which is a natural extension of deep learning principles to recurrent models.
2.  The experimental analysis goes beyond simply reporting a final performance metric. The authors attempt to probe the internal dynamics of the trained models to understand how different layers contribute and what time scales they operate on, which is a valuable direction for research.
3.  Achieving results competitive with more complex second-order optimization methods using a simpler SGD-based approach (with gradient clipping) is a practical and noteworthy result.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the interesting topic and promising results, the paper is marred by a significant number of flaws, ranging from minor presentational issues to more substantial methodological and experimental concerns.

**1. Clarity and Presentation:**

*   **Formatting and Typos:** The paper exhibits a general lack of care in its preparation. The reference list is inconsistently and incompletely formatted (e.g., [1], [2], [9] are missing crucial publication details like year or venue). There are grammatical errors ("The last decade," instead of "In the last decade,") and typos (a missing parenthesis in Equation 3). Figures 1, 2, 3, and 4 are all referenced but are not included in the provided text, making it impossible to fully evaluate the experimental claims. Such sloppiness undermines confidence in the technical work.
*   **Vague Descriptions:** Several key methodological details are described in a hand-wavy manner. The use of gradient normalization is called a "trick" that "seems to be effective," which lacks rigor. The choice of 5 layers is justified by "a previous set of experiments (results not shown)," which is unacceptable for a peer-reviewed publication. The claim in footnote 3 that different initializations yield very little performance difference is asserted without evidence.

**2. Methodological Rigor:**

*   **Training of DRNN-1O:** The incremental layer-wise training scheme for the DRNN-1O is described as "necessary to reach good performance." This is a critical detail that suggests a significant optimization pathology. However, the paper provides no analysis of *why* this is the case. Is it a vanishing/exploding gradient problem specific to this architecture? The training schedule itself (`T = {0.5, 1, 1.5, 2, 2.5} × 10^5` updates per stage) seems arbitrary and is presented without justification, making the procedure appear ad-hoc and difficult to reproduce or generalize.
*   **Novelty:** The core architecture of stacked RNNs/LSTMs is not new, as the authors acknowledge by citing Graves et al. [8]. The paper's primary claim to originality rests on the in-depth analysis. While the analysis is interesting, some of it is anecdotal (e.g., the text generation samples) or rests on strong, unverified assumptions (e.g., the claim of a "pseudo-stable attractor-like state" for parentheses). The novelty of the analysis, while present, may not be as substantial as implied.
*   **Analysis Techniques:** The experimental analyses have limitations. Measuring the Euclidean distance between hidden state vectors (the "typo" experiment) is a plausible but potentially noisy proxy for information persistence. The interpretation of the results for the DRNN-1O (attributing the long timescales in lower layers to the training procedure) is presented as a post-hoc speculation rather than a tested hypothesis. The analysis where individual layer outputs are zeroed out is also problematic, as the authors themselves admit that "individual layer contributions are likely not independent," which significantly limits the interpretability of this experiment.

**3. Experimental Evaluation:**

*   **Limited Runs:** The authors state they "only tested three network instantiations" and that "each experiment was run on a single GPU." This implies that the main results in Table 1 are based on a single training run for each model configuration. Given the stochastic nature of initialization and SGD, reporting results from a single run is insufficient to make robust claims about the superiority of one architecture over another. The performance difference between DRNN-AO (1.59 BPC) and DRNN-1O (1.56 BPC) is small and could easily be due to random chance.
*   **Unfair Comparisons:** The comparison to the Hessian-Free (HF) trained model [19] is tenuous. The authors note the difficulty in comparing computational cost, which makes it hard to evaluate the efficiency trade-offs. Furthermore, footnote 5 reveals that a larger DRNN-AO model achieved a much better BPC of 1.472. This suggests that network size may be a more dominant factor for performance than the architectural details or training method discussed, a point that is not adequately addressed in the main body of the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper explores an important class of models (deep RNNs) and provides evidence that they can achieve state-of-the-art performance.
*   It presents a series of analyses aimed at understanding the internal workings of these models, which is a valuable contribution beyond pure performance benchmarking.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The manuscript suffers from a notable lack of polish, including numerous formatting, grammatical, and typographical errors, and missing figures.
*   Key methodological choices, particularly the training procedure for DRNN-1O, are poorly justified and appear ad-hoc.
*   The experimental evaluation is weak, relying on single runs for the main comparison, which is not scientifically rigorous.
*   Several analytical claims are anecdotal or based on questionable experimental designs, weakening the paper's core contribution of providing an "in-depth analysis."

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper tackles an interesting problem and the core ideas are promising, the execution is severely flawed. The combination of sloppy presentation, insufficiently justified methodological choices, and a lack of experimental rigor (N=1 runs) falls well below the standard expected for NIPS. The numerous minor issues collectively create an impression of a rushed and incomplete work. Before this paper can be considered for publication, it would require a substantial revision to address the clarity of its methods, the robustness of its experimental results, and the overall quality of the manuscript.
