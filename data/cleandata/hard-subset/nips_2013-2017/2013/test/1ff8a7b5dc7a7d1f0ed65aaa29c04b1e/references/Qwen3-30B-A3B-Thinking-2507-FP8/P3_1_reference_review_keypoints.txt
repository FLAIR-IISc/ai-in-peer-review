- Proposes DRNN: stacked recurrent layers intended to produce a temporal hierarchy for different time scales.  
- Evaluated on character-level Wikipedia language modeling; claims strong performance using simple SGD.  
- Architecture details and experiments are technically sound and well-defined, but temporal-hierarchy claim lacks theoretical justification.  
- Experimental results support task-specific improvements but overstate "state-of-the-art" because no LSTM/GRU comparisons are provided.  
- Comparison to Hessian-free methods is valid, but omission of LSTM trained with SGD is a major gap.  
- Paper is essentially a stacked RNN (tanh) and lacks clear novelty relative to prior stacked/hierarchical RNN and LSTM work.  
- DRNN-1O (output from top layer) empirically outperforms DRNN-AO (outputs from all layers).  
- Analysis of layer time scales and tasks (typo sensitivity, parenthesis closing) is interesting but interpretively vague.  
- Lacks evaluation on additional datasets/tasks (e.g., word-level modeling, Penn Treebank), limiting generality.  
- No analysis of computational cost or scaling behavior versus standard RNNs/LSTMs.  
- Reproducibility: sufficient training details given but missing hyperparameter search specifics and exact initial learning rate selection.  
- Clarity: paper is well written overall but fails to clearly distinguish DRNN from stacked LSTMs and prior hierarchical RNNs.  
- The claim that DRNNs "embed different time scales directly" is presented empirically without rigorous explanation.  
- Prior work (stacked bidirectional LSTMs and hierarchical RNNs) is cited but not adequately contrasted or differentiated.  
- Time-scale experiments suggest bottom layer models basic statistics, but the interpretation lacks precision and deeper analysis.  
- Main suggested improvements: add LSTM/GRU comparisons with matched parameters, provide theoretical analysis of the hierarchy, broaden datasets/tasks, and discuss computational cost.  
- Overall assessment: modest contribution with promising task results but insufficient novelty, theoretical grounding, and comparative evidence.  
- Recommendation: Weak Accept — interesting insights but needs major revisions, especially comparison to LSTM-based baselines.
