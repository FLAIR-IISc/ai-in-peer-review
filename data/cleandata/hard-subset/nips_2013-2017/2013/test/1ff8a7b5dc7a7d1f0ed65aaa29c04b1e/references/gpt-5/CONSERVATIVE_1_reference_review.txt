Summary
The paper studies “deep recurrent neural networks” (DRNNs), i.e., stacked vanilla RNN layers with temporal feedback in each layer. Two readout variants are considered: output from the top layer only (DRNN-1O) and outputs from all layers combined (DRNN-AO). Using SGD with gradient-norm normalization and a linearly decaying learning rate, the authors train character-level language models on a large Wikipedia corpus and report bits-per-character (BPC) comparable to prior multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free (HF). They analyze layer contributions by ablating outputs, probe emergent time scales via typo perturbations and context switches, and examine long-term dependencies (e.g., matching parentheses in generation).

Relation to prior work
Stacked/hierarchical RNNs date to El Hihi & Bengio (NIPS’95), Fernández/Graves/Schmidhuber (IJCAI’07), and stacked LSTMs for speech (Graves et al., ICASSP’13). Character-level modeling with HF-trained MRNNs (Sutskever et al., ICML’11) is the main SOTA comparator. The paper’s novelty lies in: (i) applying stacked vanilla RNNs to large-scale character modeling with SGD, (ii) a simple training heuristic (incremental layer-wise training for the top-output variant), and (iii) empirical analyses of layer time scales and long-term effects. The architectural idea is not new; the analyses are the more distinctive aspect.

Strengths
- Clear problem setting and straightforward model definition; code appears feasible to reproduce with Theano.
- Solid large-scale experiment on a challenging dataset; reporting BPC aligns with prior work.
- Empirical layer-wise analyses (ablation, perturbation-based time-scale probing) are insightful and go beyond raw numbers.
- Demonstrates that SGD with gradient normalization can approach MRNN+HF results at similar parameter counts, which is practically relevant.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/methodological rigor
- Limited baselines. No comparison to established gated architectures (LSTM/GRU), which are the de facto standard for long-term dependencies and stacked configurations. Prior work already shows stacked LSTMs perform strongly on sequence tasks; without these baselines it is hard to assess the value of stacking vanilla RNNs.
- Comparability to MRNN results is not airtight: the character vocabulary differs (95+UNK vs 86), and dataset preprocessing details are not fully matched. The claim of “state-of-the-art for recurrent networks with the same number of parameters” is therefore weaker than presented.
- Training protocol differences confound conclusions. The DRNN-1O uses incremental layer addition with output re-initialization, while DRNN-AO and the plain RNN are trained differently. Gains may arise from the training curriculum and deep supervision rather than from the architecture per se. Ablations on training strategy (e.g., DRNN-1O without incremental schedule; DRNN-AO with/without deep supervision) are missing.
- Hyperparameter exploration appears minimal (“only three instantiations” due to compute). Key sensitivities (layer count, width per layer, truncation length, gradient norm setting, learning-rate schedule) are not systematically studied, reducing confidence in robustness.
- Important training details are unclear: Are hidden states reset between sequences/minibatches? What is the exact gradient-norm threshold (Eq. 3 always normalizes to unit norm)? Any regularization (weight decay, dropout)? How is early stopping/validation handled?
- Statistical rigor: no variance across seeds, no confidence intervals, and only qualitative assessments for generation tasks.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but some choices need justification: zero-initialized output weights; why 5 layers (prior “results not shown”); rationale for the exact BPTT length (250 with first 50 ignored).
- Figures/tables are referenced but not fully specified here (e.g., exact BPC numbers in Table 1, full ablation effects).

Originality
- The model is a straightforward stacking of vanilla RNN layers with optional deep supervision; this is not conceptually novel. The analytical probes of time scales are useful but incremental. The training heuristic (incremental layer-wise training with full-network updates) resembles supervised layer-wise training and is not unprecedented.

Significance
- Showing that simple SGD can approach HF-trained MRNN BPC at fixed parameter counts is practically interesting, but the absence of standard gated baselines limits impact. The time-scale analyses are interesting diagnostics but do not yet yield actionable design principles or strong new scientific conclusions about hierarchy beyond qualitative evidence.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide apples-to-apples comparisons: can you replicate the MRNN setup’s character set and preprocessing, or report BPC under both 86 and 95 vocabularies?
2. Include LSTM (stacked) and/or GRU baselines with similar parameter budgets and training budgets. How do DRNNs compare?
3. Clarify hidden-state handling across minibatches and epochs. Are states reset or carried over? This materially affects long-range conditioning.
4. Detail the gradient normalization: is the norm always scaled to 1 (Eq. 3), or is there a clipping threshold? What is the typical norm distribution?
5. Report variance across runs and provide a validation-based early stopping criterion. How sensitive are results to learning-rate schedules and truncation length?
6. Ablate training strategy: (a) DRNN-1O without incremental layer addition; (b) DRNN-AO without intermediate-layer supervision; (c) varying number of layers.
7. Justify zero-init of output weights and discuss any symmetry-breaking concerns.

Pros for acceptance
- Large-scale experiment; practical training finding (SGD works competitively).
- Interesting and reasonably thorough qualitative/diagnostic analyses of temporal hierarchy.
- Clear write-up and reproducible ingredients.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty in architecture; insufficient baselines against established gated RNNs.
- Questionable fairness of SOTA claims due to dataset/vocabulary mismatches and differing training protocols.
- Sparse hyperparameter and ablation studies; several training details unclear; no variance reporting.

Recommendation
Lean reject. While the empirical analyses are interesting and the practical message about SGD is useful, the architectural novelty is limited and the experimental evidence is not rigorous enough (missing gated baselines, ablations, and apples-to-apples comparisons) to support strong claims. Strengthening the baseline coverage, clarifying training details, and providing more systematic ablations would significantly improve the paper. Confidence: high.
