Summary
The paper studies “deep recurrent neural networks” (DRNNs): stacked vanilla RNN layers where each layer is recurrent in time and feeds its hidden state upward. Two output strategies are compared: (i) DRNN-1O, only the top layer connects to the softmax; (ii) DRNN-AO, all layers connect to the softmax and their logits are summed. Models (≈4.9M parameters) are trained with SGD, truncated BPTT (200 steps), and a global gradient normalization heuristic, on a 1.4B-character Wikipedia corpus (95+UNK charset; last 10M for test). The paper claims state-of-the-art BPC for recurrent models of similar size previously obtained by multiplicative RNNs with Hessian-free training. It further analyzes emergent time scales via perturbations (“typo” experiments), context-switch sensitivity, and long-term structure via parenthesis-closing behavior, arguing stacked RNNs learn a temporal hierarchy and capture dependencies over several hundred characters.

Strengths
- Clear empirical demonstration that stacking simple RNNs can be competitive on large-scale character-level LM with straightforward SGD, without LSTM/GRU or second-order methods.
- Interesting analyses: layer ablations for AO, typo perturbation decay, context-switch impact, and qualitative/quantitative evaluation of parenthesis closing. These go beyond pure leaderboard results and probe what different layers may be doing.
- Practical training trick for DRNN-1O (incremental layer-wise addition with re-initialized top softmax) that seems to stabilize training and encourages longer time scales.
- The observation of learned temporal hierarchies is plausible and supported by multiple diagnostics.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/methodological
- Baselines are insufficient. No LSTM/stacked LSTM/GRU baselines, despite [8] showing strong stacked LSTMs on speech. Comparing only to MRNN/HF from the literature is not enough to substantiate a state-of-the-art claim for “recurrent networks” writ large.
- Fairness/comparability: authors use 95+UNK characters vs 86 in [19]; they assert negligible impact but provide no controlled comparison. Training budgets differ, and it is unclear how many effective passes or tokens the MRNN/HF used. Claims of state of the art are thus weak.
- Only three models, apparently single runs with no variance/confidence intervals. No validation set is described, no early stopping, and no hyperparameter search details. The reported differences between DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO could be within noise.
- Gradient “normalization” is underspecified and unusual: dividing updates by ||∇θ|| yields a constant step size in parameter space. No ε is specified to avoid divide-by-zero; no comparison to standard gradient clipping is provided; no ablation shows its necessity. The claim about “bifurcations” is vague.
- Equation (3) appears incorrect: θ(j+1)=θ(j)−η0(−j/T)∇θ/||∇θ|| contradicts the stated linear decay. Presumably η(j)=η0(1−j/T). As written, the sign is wrong, and at j=0 the step is zero.
- Output weights initialized to zero is atypical; symmetry issues are hand-waved. No justification or ablation. Bias handling is obscured by “augment with ones” but training of bias parameters is not explicitly described.
- Parameter initialization asymmetry: Z1 ~ N(0,1) while Wi, Zi>1 ~ N(0, N−1/2). This can cause saturation in the first layer with tanh, yet no rationale is given beyond noting saturation issues for DRNN-1O’s top layer. No layer-wise variance scaling or orthogonal inits considered.
- Training procedure for DRNN-1O discards and reinitializes the output layer at each stage. This could waste learning and complicate comparisons. No ablation vs. retaining/finetuning the output layer is shown.
- BPTT length is 200, yet claims of several-hundred-character dependencies are made. While possible via forward recurrence, this deserves a controlled analysis (e.g., vary truncation length) to support the long-range claim quantitatively.
- The layer-specific perturbation test for AO that mixes perturbed hidden state for one layer with unperturbed states of others may be ill-posed given inter-layer dependencies and could produce artifacts; this caveat is not discussed.

Clarity/reproducibility
- Many crucial details are missing: random seeds, batching/sequence sampling strategy, optimizer specifics (momentum? weight decay? any regularization?), schedule per-layer for DRNN-1O, handling of sequence boundaries, and exact BPC numbers in the main text. Figures and tables (1–4) are referenced but not present here; even if omitted in this copy, the narrative relies on them.
- No code or pseudo-code. The description is insufficient for exact reproduction by an expert (especially the gradient normalization and training schedule).

Originality
- Stacked RNNs and hierarchical RNNs are not new ([5], [6], [8]); the “all-output” variant resembles deep supervision. The contribution is primarily empirical/analytical on a specific large-scale character LM setup. Positioning vs. earlier stacked vanilla RNNs (e.g., prior ICML/NIPS work) is limited.

Formatting/references
- Numerous typos and inconsistencies: “wellsuited”; stray commas and double punctuation; inconsistent quotation marks; superscripts/footnotes (“1–9”) embedded awkwardly in text; “Z_i>1” typesetting; “In To appear in ICASSP 2013”.
- References are severely incomplete (missing years, venues, pages; inconsistent formatting) and in some cases inaccurate or too vague to locate. This does not meet NeurIPS standards.

Questions for authors
- Provide exact BPC numbers with standard deviations across runs, and a clear, fair comparison setup to [19] (same charset, same train/test split, similar training budget).
- How does DRNN compare to a similarly sized stacked LSTM/GRU? To a single-layer large RNN with the same parameter count?
- Clarify Equation (3), specify ε for gradient normalization, and compare to gradient clipping and to Adam/SGD+momentum.
- Justify zero initialization of output weights and the first-layer initialization scale; provide ablations.
- Describe the data pipeline (train/val/test splits, shuffling, overlap, boundaries) and hyperparameter selection protocol.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, scalable recipe showing stacked vanilla RNNs can be competitive on large-scale char LM with insightful analyses of temporal behavior.
- Potentially useful training heuristic (incremental DRNN-1O) and layer-output ablations.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient baselines and fairness; claims of SOTA not convincingly supported.
- Methodological ambiguities and likely error in the main training equation.
- Sparse experimental breadth, no variance, limited reproducibility.
- Numerous presentation/formatting/reference issues below NeurIPS bar.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper has interesting ideas and analyses, but the combination of inadequate baselines/comparisons, methodological ambiguities (including a likely erroneous update equation), lack of reproducibility details/variance, and extensive presentation issues outweigh the contributions. I encourage resubmission after strengthening baselines, clarifying and correcting the training procedure, expanding experiments (with variance), and fixing referencing/formatting.
