

# Peer Review: "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces a "Deep Recurrent Neural Network" (DRNN) architecture, which consists of multiple recurrent layers stacked vertically, where each layer receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input. The authors argue that this architecture naturally creates a temporal hierarchy, with different layers operating at different time scales, which is beneficial for modeling tasks with both short- and long-term dependencies. They evaluate DRNNs on character-level language modeling using a Wikipedia text corpus, claiming state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks with simple SGD training, previously only achieved with more complex Hessian-free optimization methods. The paper also provides analysis of the temporal dynamics within the DRNN layers through various experiments, including measuring the effect of typos on hidden states and evaluating the networks' ability to close parentheses in long phrases.

The paper builds upon prior work on hierarchical RNNs (e.g., [5]) and stacked LSTMs [8], but claims that their DRNN architecture creates a more natural temporal hierarchy. The authors also explore two output configurations: DRNN-1O (output only from top layer) and DRNN-AO (output from all layers), and provide evidence that DRNN-1O outperforms DRNN-AO.

## Quality Assessment

**Technical Soundness:** The DRNN architecture is technically sound and well-defined. The authors provide clear mathematical formulations for the hidden state evolution and output generation. The experimental methodology is generally appropriate for the task, with careful attention to training parameters and procedures. However, the paper lacks theoretical justification for why stacking RNNs creates a temporal hierarchy—this is presented as an empirical observation rather than a theoretically grounded claim.

**Claims Support:** The experimental results are reasonably well-supported for the specific task (character-level language modeling on Wikipedia), but the claims are overstated in several ways. The paper claims to achieve "state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks" but fails to compare against LSTMs or GRUs, which were already the standard for language modeling at the time (see [11, 16, 19]). The comparison to Hessian-free training is valid, but the paper doesn't compare to an LSTM trained with SGD, which would be a more relevant comparison.

**Completeness:** The paper presents a new architecture and some analysis, but it's not a complete piece of work. It lacks:
- A thorough comparison to LSTMs/GRUs, which were the dominant approaches for language modeling
- Analysis of how DRNNs scale to larger datasets or more complex tasks
- Discussion of the computational cost compared to standard RNNs/LSTMs
- Theoretical analysis of why the temporal hierarchy emerges

**Evaluation of Strengths/Weaknesses:** The authors acknowledge that DRNN-1O outperforms DRNN-AO and discuss the limitations of their approach. However, they don't adequately address the fact that their architecture is essentially a stacked RNN, which was already explored in [8] (which they cite). They also don't discuss why their tanh-based DRNN outperforms the bidirectional LSTM approach in [8]—the paper mentions the difference but doesn't analyze it.

## Clarity Assessment

**Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The figures are helpful for understanding the architecture and experimental results. However, some parts of the paper could be clearer:
- The distinction between DRNN and stacked LSTMs is not sufficiently explained
- The explanation for why the bottom layer is more important for basic statistics (Section 3.2) is vague
- The temporal hierarchy explanation (Section 2.1) lacks mathematical rigor

**Reproducibility:** The paper provides sufficient details on the experimental setup for replication (network sizes, training parameters, datasets), but lacks specific implementation details that would be needed to reproduce the exact results. For instance, they don't provide the exact hyperparameter search space or how they selected the initial learning rates.

## Originality Assessment

**Novelty:** The core idea of stacking RNNs is not novel. The paper cites [8], which already uses stacked bidirectional LSTMs for language modeling, and the DRNN architecture described in this paper is essentially a stacked RNN with tanh activation rather than LSTM gates. The authors claim their architecture "embeds different time scales directly in their structure," but this is not significantly different from the hierarchical RNN approach in [5], which they cite but don't adequately distinguish from.

**Relation to Previous Work:** The paper references related work but fails to adequately distinguish its contribution from prior hierarchical RNN approaches. The paper states that [5] "limited themselves to artificial tasks," but doesn't explain why the DRNN would be better for real-world tasks. The paper also fails to compare with LSTMs, which had become the standard for language modeling by the time of this submission.

**NIPS Proceedings Check:** A quick check of recent NIPS proceedings (2013-2014) shows that stacked RNNs and hierarchical RNNs had already been explored in several papers, including [8] (which the authors cite), [5], [6], and [3]. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how it differs from these prior works.

## Significance Assessment

**Importance:** The results are promising for a specific task (character-level language modeling), but the significance is limited because:
1. The task is relatively narrow (character-level modeling vs. word-level)
2. The comparison to state-of-the-art is incomplete (no LSTM comparison)
3. The results don't generalize to other NLP tasks

**Impact:** The paper doesn't provide strong evidence that DRNNs would be widely adopted in practice. The fact that DRNN-1O outperforms DRNN-AO is interesting, but it's unclear if this is a general phenomenon or specific to this task. The paper doesn't address how DRNNs would scale to larger, more complex language modeling tasks.

**Advancement of State of the Art:** The paper claims to achieve state-of-the-art results with simpler training, but without comparison to LSTM-based methods, it's hard to assess this claim. The paper's results are comparable to [19], but [19] used Hessian-free optimization, while the authors claim to achieve similar results with simple SGD. However, they don't train an LSTM with SGD for comparison, which would be a more direct comparison.

## Specific Critiques

1. **Lack of Comparison to LSTMs/GRUs:** This is the most significant weakness. By 2013-2014, LSTMs were the standard for language modeling. The paper doesn't compare DRNNs to LSTMs on the same task, which makes the "state-of-the-art" claim questionable. A fair comparison would be to train an LSTM with the same number of parameters using SGD and compare results.

2. **Weak Theoretical Justification:** The paper claims that stacking RNNs creates a temporal hierarchy, but provides no theoretical analysis to support this. The explanation in Section 2.1 is vague and lacks mathematical rigor. A theoretical analysis of how the time scales emerge would strengthen the paper significantly.

3. **Limited Experimental Scope:** The paper tests on a single dataset (Wikipedia text) and a single task (character-level language modeling). It would be more convincing to show results on additional datasets or tasks (e.g., Penn Treebank for word-level modeling).

4. **Overstated Claims:** The paper claims that "DRNNs embed these different time scales directly in their structure," but this is presented as an empirical observation rather than a theoretical property. The paper doesn't provide a rigorous explanation of how the time scales emerge from the architecture.

5. **Inadequate Distinction from Prior Work:** The paper cites [8] which uses stacked bidirectional LSTMs, but doesn't clearly explain how their DRNN differs from this approach. The key difference appears to be the use of tanh instead of LSTM gates, but they don't analyze why this choice matters.

6. **Analysis of Time Scales:** The time scale analysis in Section 3.3 is interesting, but the interpretation of the results (e.g., "the bottom layer is essential for modeling basic statistics") is somewhat vague. More precise analysis of what the different layers are actually learning would strengthen this part.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a comparison to LSTM-based models (with the same number of parameters) trained with SGD to properly evaluate the claimed "state-of-the-art" performance.

2. Provide a theoretical analysis of how the temporal hierarchy emerges from the DRNN architecture.

3. Expand the experimental scope to include additional datasets or tasks (e.g., word-level language modeling on Penn Treebank).

4. Clarify the distinction between DRNNs and prior stacked RNN approaches (especially [8] and [5]).

5. Include a discussion of the computational cost of training DRNNs compared to standard RNNs and LSTMs.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an interesting architecture for recurrent neural networks that creates a temporal hierarchy through stacking. The experimental results on character-level language modeling are promising, but the paper falls short in several critical areas. The most significant weakness is the lack of comparison to LSTMs, which were the dominant approach for language modeling at the time. Without this comparison, the claimed "state-of-the-art" performance is difficult to evaluate.

The paper makes a modest contribution to the field but doesn't provide a clear advantage over existing approaches. While the temporal hierarchy analysis is interesting, it's not sufficiently supported by theory or broader experimental evidence. The paper would be much stronger with a more thorough comparison to standard LSTM approaches and a clearer theoretical foundation for the temporal hierarchy claim.

## Recommendation

**Weak Accept** - The paper has some interesting insights but lacks sufficient novelty and comparison to establish its significance. It would benefit from major revisions to address the weaknesses identified above, particularly the lack of comparison to LSTM-based models.

**Pro Accept Arguments:**
- Clear description of a new architecture (DRNN)
- Interesting empirical evidence of temporal hierarchy in layers
- Demonstrates that simple SGD can achieve good results
- Promising results on character-level language modeling

**Con Accept Arguments:**
- Architecture is not significantly different from prior work (stacked RNNs)
- Fails to compare with standard LSTM approach
- Lacks theoretical justification for key claims
- Limited experimental scope
- Overstated claims about "state-of-the-art" performance

The paper would be significantly improved with a more thorough comparison to LSTM-based models and a clearer theoretical foundation for the temporal hierarchy claim. Without these revisions, the paper's contribution is limited and it's difficult to assess its true significance.
