Summary
The paper proposes and studies Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs): stacked, vanilla tanh RNN layers where each layer has its own recurrent dynamics and receives the previous layer’s hidden state as input. Two output configurations are considered: DRNN-1O (only top layer connects to softmax) and DRNN-AO (all layers connect to the softmax with additive contributions). The authors train character-level language models on a large Wikipedia corpus using SGD with gradient-normalization and a linearly decaying learning rate. For DRNN-1O, they use incremental, supervised layer-wise training (adding layers and reattaching the softmax to the new top layer). With ≈4.9M parameters, both DRNN variants outperform a single-layer RNN and approach prior state-of-the-art BPC for RNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization and multiplicative interactions. The paper’s second contribution is an empirical analysis of temporal hierarchies that emerge in DRNNs: per-layer ablations show differing contributions to output, perturbation experiments (single-character “typo” and context-switch tests) reveal longer time scales in higher layers, and the model appears to track long-range parentheses dependencies in both analysis and generation.

Relation to prior work
The paper is closely related to stacked RNNs and hierarchical recurrent neural networks (El Hihi & Bengio, 1996; Fernández et al., 2007), and the stacked LSTM work of Graves et al. (2013) that achieved strong ASR results. The authors also relate to Sutskever et al.’s MRNNs trained with HF optimization for character LM, arguing DRNNs trained with SGD can be competitive at similar parameter budgets. The idea of connecting intermediate layers to the output resembles “deep supervision” (though contemporaneous terminology varies) and auxiliary losses that help optimization. The novelty here lies less in architecture per se and more in the systematic, quantitative analysis of per-layer time scales and contributions in stacked vanilla RNNs for a realistic text task.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The models and training are standard and clearly implemented at scale. The analysis is thoughtful and uses large test averages (50k instances) for stability. However:
  - Equation (3) appears to have a sign/typo: η0(- j/T) should likely be η0(1 − j/T). Please clarify.
  - The “gradient normalization” divides by ||∇θ||, implying constant step-size in parameter space. This is nonstandard relative to gradient clipping; more discussion of stability (e.g., epsilon handling for near-zero gradients) and sensitivity would help.
  - Fairness of comparisons: the MRNN baseline used a slightly different alphabet (86 vs 96 tokens) and HF training on different hardware; the paper attempts to contextualize but a clearer apples-to-apples comparison or additional strong baselines (e.g., LSTM/GRU) would strengthen claims.
  - Experimental breadth is limited: only three model instantiations, no error bars or variance over seeds, and little hyperparameter exploration.
  - Some training details are missing or ambiguous (e.g., hidden-state initialization and whether states are reset between sampled sequences; effect of truncated BPTT length 200 on claimed long-range behavior).
- Empirical evidence largely supports claims: DRNNs outperform a single RNN at equal parameter budgets; ablations and perturbations indicate layer-wise time scales; parentheses tracking experiments are convincing and practical.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written, with a clear problem framing and motivation for deep recurrence and multi-timescale processing. The analysis sections are insightful and easy to follow. Minor issues:
- Fix Eq. (3) and describe epsilon in gradient normalization.
- State clearly how hidden states are initialized and whether they persist across minibatches.
- Provide complete bibliographic details; many references lack venue/year/pages.
- Clarify the exact dataset preprocessing and alphabet differences in comparison baselines.

Originality
Stacking RNNs and using auxiliary outputs are not new, but the combination with a careful, large-scale analysis of emergent time scales, layer contributions, and specific long-range behaviors (parentheses) on a realistic text corpus is a meaningful empirical contribution. The training recipe (incremental supervised pretraining for DRNN-1O) is a practical insight.

Significance
Character-level LM is a challenging, relevant task. Showing that simple stacked tanh RNNs trained with SGD can approach HF-trained MRNNs at similar parameter counts is useful. The analysis of temporal hierarchies will likely interest practitioners designing deep recurrent models and informs intuitions about what higher layers learn. The lack of comparisons to LSTM/GRU limits the impact, as gated RNNs are the dominant approach for long-range dependencies.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for deep recurrence and multi-timescale processing; practical architecture.
- Solid large-scale experiments on a realistic dataset with competitive BPC for vanilla RNNs.
- Insightful analyses: per-layer ablations, perturbation-based time-scale estimates, context-switch recovery, and parentheses tracking (both analysis and generative tests).
- Practical training insights (deep supervision via DRNN-AO; incremental supervised pretraining for DRNN-1O).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Baselines: Include gated baselines (stacked LSTM/GRU) at comparable parameter counts; also report a DRNN with multiplicative or gated recurrent transitions if possible.
- Comparability: Align character set exactly with MRNN baselines or report matched results; provide training-set pass counts for MRNN if available, or tone down SOTA claims.
- Robustness: Report variance across seeds; ablate number of layers and layer widths (heterogeneous widths) to test the “hierarchy” hypothesis more thoroughly.
- Training details: Clarify Eq. (3), epsilon for gradient normalization, hidden-state handling across sequences, and exact BPTT truncation; include dev-set performance and training curves to assess overfitting.
- Regularization: Discuss any use of dropout/weight decay; explore whether AO acts as a regularizer beyond easing optimization.
- Analysis: Complement Euclidean distance with task-relevant probes (e.g., linear classifiers on hidden states to predict bracket depth or part-of-text position), and quantify per-layer effective context via mutual information or linear influence measures.

Arguments for acceptance
- Advances understanding of deep recurrent architectures with careful, large-scale analyses.
- Demonstrates competitive performance for simple tanh DRNNs using SGD, with practical training tricks.
- Provides useful empirical evidence for emergent temporal hierarchies and long-range behavior.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited baselines (no LSTM/GRU) and imperfect comparability to MRNN weaken the strength-of-claim on performance.
- Small number of model configurations and missing robustness metrics.
- Some methodological details ambiguous (Eq. (3), state handling), and references incomplete.

Overall, I lean toward weak accept for the empirical insights and practical training contributions, contingent on clarifying training details and tempering/strengthening performance claims with stronger baselines.
