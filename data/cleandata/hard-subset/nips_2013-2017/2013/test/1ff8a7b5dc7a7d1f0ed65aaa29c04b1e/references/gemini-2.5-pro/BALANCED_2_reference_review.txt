### **Summary**

This paper introduces a deep recurrent neural network (DRNN) architecture, which is constructed by stacking multiple recurrent layers. The hidden state of each layer serves as the input sequence for the subsequent layer. The authors hypothesize that this hierarchical structure allows the model to naturally learn representations at different time scales, which is beneficial for tasks with complex temporal dependencies. They apply this architecture to character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus. Two variants are explored: one with an output connection only from the top layer (DRNN-1O) and another with output connections from all layers (DRNN-AO). Using only stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with gradient clipping, the proposed DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance, comparable to that of more complex multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization (Sutskever et al., 2011). The main contribution is not just the performance, but also a series of detailed analyses that provide strong evidence for the emergence of a temporal hierarchy, where lower layers handle short-term statistics and higher layers capture long-range dependencies, such as tracking parenthesis closures over hundreds of characters.

### **Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results with a Simple Method:** The paper demonstrates that a conceptually simple architecture (stacked RNNs) trained with a standard first-order method (SGD) can achieve state-of-the-art results on a challenging, large-scale benchmark. This is a significant finding, as it provides a simpler and potentially more accessible alternative to models requiring complex second-order optimization techniques like Hessian-free optimization.

2.  **Excellent and Insightful Analysis:** The primary strength of this work lies in its thorough analysis, which goes far beyond simply reporting a final performance metric. The authors conduct a series of well-designed experiments to probe the internal dynamics of the trained models:
    *   The "typo perturbation" experiment (Fig. 3, left) convincingly shows that higher layers have a longer memory of past events, providing direct evidence for the hypothesized temporal hierarchy.
    *   The "context switch" experiment (Fig. 3, right) demonstrates that DRNNs leverage a longer context for prediction compared to a shallow RNN.
    *   The analysis of parenthesis closure (Fig. 4) is particularly compelling. It shows that the top layer can maintain a memory of an open parenthesis for hundreds of time steps, far longer than the BPTT training window, suggesting the model has learned an abstract, robust representation rather than simply memorizing sequences.
    *   The ablation study on layer contributions in the DRNN-AO model (Fig. 2) provides valuable insight into the functional roles of different layers.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The DRNN architecture is clearly defined, the experimental setup is detailed, and the results of the analyses are presented logically with clear figures. The motivation for stacking RNNs to create a temporal hierarchy is intuitive and well-argued.

4.  **Novelty in Analysis:** While stacked RNNs had been explored before (e.g., Graves et al., 2013), this paper is one of the first to provide such a deep and systematic analysis of *why* and *how* this depth is beneficial in a recurrent context. This analytical contribution is arguably more important than the performance result itself and has likely inspired subsequent work on understanding deep sequential models.

### **Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection/revision)**

1.  **Limited Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The primary baseline is a "common RNN" (presumably an Elman-style network). While this is a reasonable starting point, a more powerful baseline like a single-layer LSTM with a comparable number of parameters would have been much more informative. LSTMs are specifically designed to capture long-range dependencies, and it would be crucial to know if the observed benefits stem from depth itself or if a sufficiently large shallow LSTM could perform similarly. This omission makes it harder to definitively attribute the performance gains solely to the deep architecture.

2.  **Ad-hoc Training Procedure for DRNN-1O:** The incremental, layer-wise training scheme for the DRNN-1O is presented as a necessary trick to achieve good performance. While interesting, it feels somewhat ad-hoc. The paper lacks an ablation study comparing this method to standard end-to-end training of the full 5-layer network from the start. Without this comparison, it is unclear how much of the DRNN-1O's success is due to the architecture versus this specific training curriculum. This also raises questions about the generalizability of the training approach.

3.  **Ambiguity in Architectural Choices:** The paper states that the choice of 5 layers was based on prior experiments with "results not shown." While understandable due to computational constraints, this weakens the argument for this specific architectural choice. Similarly, all layers have the same size. An exploration or discussion of varying layer sizes (e.g., a funnel-like structure) could have provided further insights into efficient architectural design.

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   The analysis of parenthesis closure is excellent. To make it even stronger, it would be interesting to analyze failure cases. The paper mentions that the model fails with nested parentheses or when sentences start with an opening bracket. A qualitative analysis of these failures could provide deeper insights into the model's limitations and its learned syntactic rules.
*   In Section 2.3, the use of gradient normalization (`∇θ(j) / ||∇θ(j)||`) is mentioned as a "trick." This is effectively a per-update learning rate adaptation and is now more commonly known as gradient clipping (by norm). It would be helpful to frame it as such and perhaps cite relevant work on why this is effective for RNNs (e.g., Pascanu et al., "On the difficulty of training Recurrent Neural Networks").
*   Regarding the comparison with Sutskever et al. (2011), the paper speculates on the relative computational cost. A more concrete comparison based on the total number of data points processed (or epochs) or total training time under specified hardware would strengthen the claim of efficiency.
*   For future work, applying the same deep analysis to a stacked LSTM architecture would be a fascinating and valuable comparison. This would help disentangle the effects of depth from the effects of the specific recurrent unit used (simple tanh vs. LSTM cell).

### **Final Decision**

This is a strong paper that I would recommend for **acceptance**. The core contribution is the combination of a simple, effective deep recurrent model with a rigorous and insightful analysis that provides compelling evidence for the emergence of a temporal hierarchy. The paper is well-written, the experiments are well-designed, and the results are significant. While the baseline comparisons could be stronger and some training details are ad-hoc, these weaknesses do not undermine the main contributions. The analytical part of the paper, in particular, sets a high standard and offers valuable insights into the functioning of deep sequential models that are of broad interest to the community.
