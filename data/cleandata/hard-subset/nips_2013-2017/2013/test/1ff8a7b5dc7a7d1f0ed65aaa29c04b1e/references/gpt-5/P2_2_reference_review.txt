Summary
The paper studies stacked recurrent neural networks (“Deep RNNs,” DRNNs) for character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus. Two variants are considered: DRNN-1O, where only the top layer connects to the softmax output, and DRNN-AO, where all layers contribute to the output. Training uses SGD with gradient normalization and a linear learning rate decay; DRNN-1O employs an incremental layer-wise training schedule in which layers are added progressively and the output layer is reinitialized to connect from the new top. With roughly 4.9M parameters, both DRNNs achieve bits-per-character (BPC) comparable to previously reported state-of-the-art recurrent models trained with Hessian-free optimization, and improve over a similarly sized single-layer RNN trained with the same regime. The paper analyzes learned temporal hierarchies via perturbation (“typo”) experiments, context-switch tests, ablations of per-layer output contributions, and a qualitative/quantitative study of long-range dependencies such as closing parentheses.

Relation to prior work
The idea of stacking recurrent layers is known (e.g., hierarchical RNNs, deep/bidirectional LSTMs for speech), but prior work emphasized performance rather than analysis. Multiplicative RNNs and Hessian-free training have been strong baselines for this dataset. The paper’s main novelty lies in: (i) showing that plain SGD can train deep vanilla RNN stacks to competitive performance on a large-scale character task, (ii) comparing “all-layers-to-output” vs. “top-only” output connections, and (iii) empirical analyses that layers learn different time scales and can capture long-range structures (e.g., parentheses).

Strengths
- Solid empirical result: DRNNs outperform a single-layer RNN and approach prior SOTA for similar parameter budgets on a challenging, large corpus using relatively simple training.
- Careful architectural/optimization choices: gradient normalization, linear LR decay, and incremental layer-wise training for DRNN-1O; these are simple and broadly applicable.
- Insightful analyses: per-layer output ablations, hidden-state perturbation studies to reveal time scales, context-switch impact on BPC, and a focused long-range phenomenon (parentheses) with both qualitative samples and quantitative success rates.
- Clear articulation that temporal hierarchies emerge and are learned, not just a consequence of depth per se.

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines: no comparisons to gated architectures (LSTM/GRU) or to deep LSTM stacks that were contemporaneously competitive on sequence tasks; this limits claims about significance relative to the most relevant alternatives.
- Scope of experiments: only one dataset and three model instantiations due to compute constraints; hyperparameter exploration is minimal.
- Some training/evaluation details are underspecified for reproducibility: whether hidden states are reset between mini-batches, exact gradient normalization scheme (target norm), handling of sequence boundaries during evaluation, and whether test BPC uses persistent state across the test stream.
- The “all-layers-to-output” variant slightly underperforms the “top-only” variant; more discussion/diagnostics would help (e.g., gradient interference, regularization).
- Time-scale metrics (Euclidean distance between hidden states post-perturbation) are heuristic and can depend on scaling/saturation; complementary measures (e.g., effect on loss, linear response, or mutual-information-style diagnostics) would strengthen the claims.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The training scheme and ablations are sensible; analyses support the main claims. The results are credible and the parentheses study is convincing. However, the absence of LSTM/GRU baselines is a notable gap. Statistical variability (multiple seeds) and more thorough hyperparameter sweeps would improve robustness of conclusions. Some reporting omissions (resetting state, test protocol details) hinder reproducibility.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. Figures and experiments are explained plainly, and the motivation for per-layer outputs and incremental training is clear. A few implementation details should be added for clarity (state handling across sequences, exact gradient norm, initialization choices like zero-initialized output weights, and evaluation protocol on the test stream).

Originality
Moderate. Stacking RNNs is not new; connecting all layers to output has precedent in deep nets. The novelty is in the systematic analysis of temporal hierarchies and the demonstration that simple SGD suffices to train deep vanilla RNN stacks to near-SOTA on large-scale character modeling. The parentheses analysis is a nice, concrete case study.

Significance
Moderate to high if positioned as: “simple training of deep vanilla RNNs can close much of the gap to more complex second-order methods; stacked RNNs learn temporal hierarchies that are useful for long-range dependencies.” Broader impact is somewhat constrained by missing gated baselines, which (if comparable or superior) would reduce the practical significance of advocating DRNNs without gates. The analytical insights may be of lasting interest.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Include comparisons to LSTM (single-layer and stacked) with similar parameter counts, and/or to GRU, to contextualize DRNNs against widely used gated models.
- Clarify training/evaluation details: do you reset hidden states between mini-batches and at test time? Is test BPC computed with a single pass and persistent state? Specify the target norm used for gradient normalization.
- Report exact BPC numbers and variance (multiple seeds) for all models; specify the character vocabulary differences and confirm negligible impact.
- Analyze why DRNN-AO underperforms DRNN-1O; e.g., inspect gradient norms per layer, add per-layer regularization or learned gating of layer contributions to output.
- Consider complementary time-scale diagnostics (effect on loss, controlled synthetic tasks with known horizons) to corroborate the hidden-state distance findings.
- Release code or detailed pseudo-code to improve reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical results on a large, relevant dataset with simple training.
- Insightful analysis of temporal hierarchies and long-range behavior.
- Clear, focused writing with meaningful ablations.

Cons against acceptance
- Missing comparisons to gated RNNs reduces significance.
- Limited experimental breadth and hyperparameter exploration.
- Some reproducibility details are missing; time-scale metrics are heuristic.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers solid empirical evidence and useful analyses about deep recurrent stacks and their learned temporal structure, with results competitive to more complex training schemes. Addressing the missing gated baselines and clarifying training/evaluation details would substantially strengthen the contribution.
