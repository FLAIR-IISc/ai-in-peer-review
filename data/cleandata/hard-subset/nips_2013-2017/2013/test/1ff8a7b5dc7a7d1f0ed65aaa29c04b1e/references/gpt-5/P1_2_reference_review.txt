Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs): stacks of simple tanh RNN layers where each layer is recurrent and feeds its hidden state to the next. Two output configurations are considered: top-layer-only (DRNN-1O) and all-layers-to-output (DRNN-AO). Trained with truncated BPTT and plain SGD with gradient normalization (no second-order methods), the models are evaluated on character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus with parameter budgets matched (~4.9M). The authors report that DRNNs outperform a single-layer RNN and reach performance comparable to prior state-of-the-art recurrent models trained with Hessian-free (HF) methods (multiplicative RNN, MRNN). They then analyze temporal behavior via (i) single-layer ablations, (ii) “typo” perturbations and context switches to estimate per-layer time scales, and (iii) long-range structure tests (closing parentheses) in both predictive and generative modes. Results suggest a learned temporal hierarchy across layers and ability to model dependencies spanning several hundred characters. The work relates to early hierarchical RNN work (El Hihi & Bengio; Fernández et al.) and stacked bidirectional LSTMs for speech (Graves et al.), and to character-level modeling with MRNNs (Sutskever et al.).

Strengths
- Clear and timely question: does stacking simple RNNs yield emergent temporal hierarchies and improved character-level modeling?
- Sound empirical methodology within constraints: parameter-matched models, reasonable BPTT length, and insightful analyses (layer ablations; per-layer perturbation responses; long-range bracket tracking).
- Practical training recipe: plain SGD with gradient normalization and an incremental layer-wise supervised pretraining scheme for DRNN-1O; useful for practitioners avoiding HF/second-order methods.
- Interesting qualitative diagnostics: per-layer contributions and generative text under layer ablations; parentheses tracking shows nontrivial long-distance interactions.
- The work ties performance claims to analyses of how the architecture uses temporal context.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Comparisons and confounds: The contrast between DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO mixes architectural differences with different training curricula (DRNN-1O uses incremental layerwise training; DRNN-AO does not). The observed time-scale differences may reflect training protocol rather than architecture. A fair control would train DRNN-AO with the same incremental scheme, or train DRNN-1O end-to-end from scratch.
- Baselines: Missing strong, widely used baselines (e.g., 1–2 layer LSTM/GRU, stacked LSTM) on the same dataset and parameter budget. Given known advantages of LSTM on long-term dependencies, this omission weakens claims about DRNN advantages.
- Statistical rigor: Only three models (single seeds) due to compute constraints; authors assert low variance but provide no evidence (no error bars/seed variability). Conclusions about small differences (e.g., DRNN-1O vs DRNN-AO) are therefore tentative.
- Dataset/protocol comparability: The MRNN comparison uses a slightly different character set (86 vs 96 incl. unknown). Authors argue negligible effect, but reporting results on the identical setup (or re-evaluating MRNN under the present character set) would strengthen claims.
- Truncation vs long-term claims: BPTT effectively uses 200 steps. The paper claims memory of “several hundred characters” and shows a few qualitative examples (parentheses). Please quantify performance as a function of bracket span (e.g., accuracy/probability vs span length), and report the distribution of spans in training data to support generalization beyond the truncation window.
- Optimization details: Equation (3) appears to have a sign/typo; the schedule is said to “drop linearly,” but the formula shows η0(− j/T). Please clarify. Normalizing the gradient to unit norm each step is unusual; comparing to standard gradient clipping, RMSProp/AdaGrad, or momentum would help isolate what matters.
- Reproducibility: Important hyperparameters (exact gradient norm, clipping thresholds if any, batch composition, random seeding) and implementation details for the layerwise curriculum and zero initialization of output weights should be fully specified. Code availability would help.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized and readable, with helpful figures and qualitative samples. A few footnotes/formatting issues and the Eq. (3) confusion should be fixed. Provide actual BPC numbers in-text (Table 1 is referenced but not all values are visible here).

Originality
- Stacked/“deep” RNNs are not new; prior work has used hierarchical or stacked RNNs and stacked (bi)LSTMs. The main novelty here is an empirical study demonstrating emergent per-layer time scales in simple stacked tanh RNNs trained with SGD, with neat diagnostics (typo/context switch/parentheses), and a practical supervised layerwise pretraining procedure. While incremental, this is a useful empirical contribution.

Significance
- Character-level modeling remains a meaningful benchmark. Showing that simple stacked tanh RNNs can approach MRNN/HF performance with SGD is practically relevant. The temporal analyses are insightful and may inform architecture/training choices. However, absent comparisons to LSTM/GRU baselines and stronger ablations, the broader impact is limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Control the training-protocol confound between DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO; add a matched curriculum or end-to-end variants.
- Add LSTM/GRU baselines (single- and multi-layer) under the same budget and BPTT.
- Report multiple seeds and error bars. Provide learning curves and report compute/data throughput.
- Quantify temporal analyses: per-layer perturbation half-lives; BPC recovery time constants; aggregate parentheses (and quotes/brackets) closure accuracy as a function of span length; test nested parentheses if possible.
- Clarify Eq. (3) and justify design choices (zero init for output weights; input weight scales; choice of 5 layers).
- Evaluate on an additional corpus (e.g., enwik8/text8/PTB char) to test robustness.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid empirical evidence that stacking simple RNNs improves char-level modeling under a fixed parameter budget.
- Insightful, carefully designed analyses of temporal hierarchy and long-term interactions.
- Practical training recipe (SGD + gradient normalization + incremental pretraining) that achieves competitive results.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing strong baselines (LSTM/GRU), and conflation of architecture with training curriculum.
- Limited statistical rigor (few runs; single dataset).
- Some methodological ambiguities (learning-rate schedule; truncation vs long-term claims).

Overall, this is a well-executed empirical study with informative analyses and practical relevance. With stronger baselines and clearer controls, it would be a strong contribution. I lean toward weak accept if space allows, primarily for the analysis and practical training insights.
