Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs): stacks of simple tanh RNN layers with recurrent connections within each layer. Two output topologies are considered: DRNN-1O (only the top layer connects to the softmax) and DRNN-AO (all layers connect to output and are summed before the softmax). Training uses SGD with per-update gradient normalization and, for DRNN-1O, an incremental layer-wise schedule where layers are added sequentially and the output head is reattached to the current top layer. The main empirical task is character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus; models are matched in parameter count (~4.9M). The paper reports competitive bits-per-character (BPC) relative to multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization, and presents analyses suggesting a learned temporal hierarchy across layers, including perturbation-based time-scale measurements and a qualitative/quantitative study of long-term dependencies (e.g., closing parentheses) in generative mode.

This connects to prior work on hierarchical/stacked recurrent nets (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio; Fernández et al.; Graves et al.’s stacked bi-LSTMs for speech) and on training RNNs with advanced second-order methods (Martens; Sutskever et al.’s MRNN). The novelty here is using stacked simple RNNs trained with SGD at scale, plus systematic analyses of time scales and layer contributions for character modeling.

Quality
Strengths:
- The experimental setup is sensible: same parameter budget across baselines, explicit training schedule, and a widely used large corpus. The DRNNs outperform a plain RNN and are competitive with the MRNN despite simpler units and first-order training, which supports the claim that depth in recurrent architectures helps.
- The analysis is a real contribution: (i) ablation of layer outputs to gauge contribution, (ii) perturbation experiments (typo injection and context switching) to estimate time scales and effective context length, and (iii) a focused long-range dependency probe (parentheses). These triangulate the claim that stacking induces a temporal hierarchy and that upper layers capture longer-range statistics.
- The paper is honest about the training dynamics (e.g., gradient issues, motivation for output connections at all layers, and how the pretraining schedule affects the observed time scales in DRNN-1O).

Weaknesses:
- Baselines are limited. Given the focus on temporal hierarchies and long-range dependencies, comparisons to LSTMs (single and stacked) or gated RNNs are natural and important. The paper cites stacked bi-LSTMs in speech, but does not include LSTM baselines on the same task. This omission limits the strength of the claims about DRNNs’ advantages for temporal hierarchies.
- Statistical support is thin: only three networks (one per architecture) were trained due to compute. There is no report of variability across random seeds, sensitivity to hyperparameters, or ablations of the gradient normalization versus standard clipping.
- The training scheme for DRNN-1O (incremental layer-wise with the output head moved upward) likely induces long timescales in intermediate layers, confounding the architecture-versus-training contribution. The authors note this, but the analysis would be stronger with a control (e.g., DRNN-1O trained from scratch).
- Some comparability issues: the character inventory differs from [19], though the authors argue the impact is negligible; the number of passes over data and wall-clock comparisons to MRNN are rough; and for the larger 9.6M model only a single BPC point is given.
- Truncated BPTT uses 200 steps of context (250-length segments with first 50 excluded from backprop), yet the paper argues for long-range modeling beyond that. While the generative parentheses test is suggestive, it would help to quantify how performance changes as the truncation window varies.

Clarity
Overall the paper is clearly written and well organized. The model and training are specified concisely, and the analysis is explained with reasonable care. A few suggestions:
- Report exact BPC numbers (with at least a couple of significant digits) for all models, including validation curves, and include any uncertainty across runs if available.
- Elaborate on the gradient normalization choice (normalizing to unit norm vs clipping to a threshold), and show sensitivity analyses.
- Provide more detail on data preprocessing, vocabulary choices, and exact BPTT windowing, and clarify axes/units for time-scale plots.
- Some references are incomplete; tighten related work to better position novelty relative to stacked LSTMs on language modeling.

Originality
Stacking RNN layers is not new per se, and output connections from multiple layers have precedents in deep architectures. The originality lies in: (i) demonstrating that stacked simple RNNs trained with SGD can match MRNN performance on large-scale character modeling; (ii) the incremental layer-wise training schedule for DRNN-1O; and (iii) the systematic time-scale and dependency analyses. While not a conceptual breakthrough, this is a useful and timely contribution with clear insights.

Significance
The empirical result that first-order SGD on simple stacked RNNs can rival more complex MRNN/Hessian-free setups is practically valuable and likely to influence practitioners. The analyses provide evidence for emergent temporal hierarchies, offering intuition that can guide architecture design. However, the lack of LSTM/gated comparisons tempers the broader impact, as LSTMs are a dominant baseline for long-range sequence modeling.

Pros
- Clear demonstration that depth improves simple RNNs on large-scale character modeling.
- Competitive performance with a simpler training pipeline.
- Thoughtful and multifaceted analysis of time scales and long-range behavior.
- Practical training insights (gradient normalization, multi-output heads, incremental layering).

Cons
- Missing LSTM/gated baselines and limited breadth of experiments.
- Training schedule may confound architectural conclusions.
- Sparse reporting on variability and sensitivity.
- Minor clarity/comparability issues.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper is technically sound overall, well written, and offers both a practical message (stacked simple RNNs + SGD can work well) and insightful analysis of temporal hierarchies. Addressing the baseline and methodological concerns—ideally by adding at least one LSTM baseline and clarifying training/analysis details—would strengthen the case, but the current contribution is still valuable for the NeurIPS audience.
