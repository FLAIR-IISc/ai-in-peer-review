Summary and relation to prior work
This paper investigates deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) formed by stacking vanilla RNN layers with per-layer recurrence and either (a) a single output head at the top (DRNN-1O) or (b) output connections from every layer (DRNN-AO). The authors train these models with SGD and gradient normalization, and study character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus. They report bits-per-character (BPC) comparable to prior state-of-the-art recurrent models of similar parameter count, notably multiplicative RNNs (MRNN) trained with Hessian-free methods (Sutskever, Martens & Hinton). Beyond accuracy, they analyze temporal properties: ablations of layer outputs, perturbation tests to estimate per-layer timescales, context-switch experiments, and a focused analysis of long-range dependencies such as closing parentheses.

This work is related to hierarchical/stacked RNNs (El Hihi & Bengio; Fernández et al.; Graves et al.) and to deep architectures for sequence tasks (e.g., stacked LSTM for TIMIT). The use of output connections at multiple depths partly echoes deep supervision ideas, and the layer-wise training schedule for DRNN-1O resembles supervised pretraining. The empirical comparison to MRNN/Hessian-free optimization relates to the broader debate on SGD vs. second-order methods.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated architecture that unifies depth and recurrence; straightforward to implement.
- Solid empirical study on a large, realistic dataset with parameter-matched models; training entirely with SGD is a useful counterpoint to second-order methods.
- Insightful analyses of temporal hierarchies: typo perturbations, context-switch BPC decay, and qualitative/quantitative parentheses experiments. These provide evidence that stacked RNNs learn different effective timescales.
- Practical training ideas: gradient normalization and an incremental, supervised layer-wise schedule for DRNN-1O.
- Ablation of per-layer outputs (for DRNN-AO) gives some interpretability into how layers contribute to prediction.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Missing strong baselines: no comparison to gated architectures (LSTM/GRU) or to stacked LSTMs that are widely known to handle long-term dependencies and have been used in speech and text. Given the focus on long-range structure, this is a significant omission.
- Comparability concerns: vocabulary differs from Sutskever et al. (95/96 vs 86 characters). The paper asserts the difference is negligible but does not quantify the impact; this weakens state-of-the-art claims.
- Limited experimental breadth: only three model instantiations and one dataset; no exploration of depth/width trade-offs, BPTT truncation lengths, or training schedules beyond the two proposed architectures.
- The gradient normalization update (scaling by 1/||∇|| with a decaying scalar) is unusual; no ablation shows its necessity vs. standard gradient clipping or Adam/SGD with momentum. Zero-initialization of output weights is also unconventional and not justified.
- The DRNN-1O training procedure repeatedly discards and reinitializes the output layer; this changes the effective objective and complicates fairness when comparing to DRNN-AO/RNN.

Clarity
- Generally well written with clear equations, but important details are missing: exact BPC values with variance, training time/throughput per update, and code/configs for reproducibility. Some key results are referenced (“Table 1/2”) without enough context to independently assess statistical significance.
- The analysis figures are informative, but timescale quantification could be defined more rigorously (e.g., providing half-life estimates with confidence intervals).

Originality
- Stacking RNNs is not novel, and multi-timescale behavior in hierarchies has been discussed previously. The multi-output head and the specific training regime are incremental. The analysis suite is a contribution, but not fundamentally new methods.

Significance
- Demonstrates that stacked vanilla RNNs trained with SGD can approach MRNN/Hessian-free results, which is practically useful. The interpretability/temporal analyses may inform future hierarchical designs.
- However, without surpassing prior results, and lacking LSTM/GRU baselines, the broader impact is moderate.

Pro acceptance
- Clear empirical evidence of emergent temporal hierarchy in stacked vanilla RNNs.
- Useful engineering insights (SGD-only recipe) and interpretability analyses on a large dataset.
- Relevance to the ongoing optimization debate (SGD vs second-order).

Con acceptance
- Missing comparisons to LSTM/GRU and stacked gated RNNs; originality is incremental.
- Comparability caveats (vocabulary; differing training budgets) and limited experimental breadth.
- Some methodological choices lack ablation/support.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add strong baselines: single- and multi-layer LSTM/GRU with comparable parameter budgets; ideally also a stacked LSTM with and without multi-head outputs.
- Ensure comparability: report results with the exact 86-character setup and on enwik8 (Hutter prize) for direct comparison to compression baselines; control for training budget (epochs/updates) across models.
- Provide ablations: gradient normalization vs standard clipping; momentum/Adam; effect of output zero-initialization; effect of depth and per-layer width; different BPTT truncation lengths.
- Deepen analysis: quantify per-layer timescales with explicit half-life metrics and error bars; probe robustness to nested parentheses and other long-range syntactic markers; ablate by zeroing hidden states or recurrent weights per layer to disentangle preprocessing vs direct-output roles.
- Reproducibility: release code, configs, and pretrained models; report variance across seeds.
- Discuss relation to prior hierarchical RNNs more concretely, clarifying what is new beyond prior stacked/LSTM architectures.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the gradient normalization heuristic and to the learning rate schedule?
- What are the exact BPC values (with variability) for all models, and how do they change under the 86-character setting?
- How do DRNNs compare to parameter-matched stacked LSTMs under your training pipeline?
- Does the DRNN-1O still outperform AO if both are trained with identical update budgets and auxiliary losses tuned?

Recommendation
Reject (weak). The paper is clear and contains interesting analyses, but the methodological novelty is limited and key baselines (LSTM/GRU) are missing. With stronger comparisons and broadened experiments, this could become a solid contribution.

Confidence
High on the assessment of missing baselines and experimental limitations; medium on precise state-of-the-art positioning due to dataset differences.
