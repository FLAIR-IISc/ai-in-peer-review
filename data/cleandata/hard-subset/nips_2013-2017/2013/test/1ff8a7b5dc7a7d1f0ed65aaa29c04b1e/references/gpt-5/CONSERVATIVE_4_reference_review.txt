Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies “deep recurrent neural networks” (DRNNs), i.e., stacked vanilla RNN layers where each layer is itself recurrent, with two output variants: only the top layer connects to the softmax (DRNN-1O) or all layers do (DRNN-AO). Training is by SGD with gradient normalization and a linear learning-rate decay; for DRNN-1O, layers are added incrementally with full-network training after each addition. The main application is character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus. The authors report bits-per-character (BPC) comparable to the multiplicative RNN (MRNN) trained with Hessian-free (HF) methods. They provide qualitative analyses suggesting emergent temporal hierarchies across layers (via perturbation/“typo” experiments) and long-range phenomena (parenthesis closing), and simple ablations (zeroing a layer’s output contribution).

The work relates to prior hierarchical and deep RNNs (El Hihi & Bengio; Fernández et al.; Graves et al.) and to MRNN/HF training (Sutskever, Martens & Sutskever). The “all-layers-to-output” variant aligns with ideas of deep supervision/auxiliary losses used in deep nets, though not explicitly cited in that context.

Strengths
- Addresses a large-scale, real dataset with character-level modeling and reports competitive BPC using simple SGD, which is valuable given HF’s complexity.
- The architecture is simple, clear, and easy to reproduce conceptually (stacked RNNs with optional multi-layer outputs).
- Analyses are interesting and intuitive: perturbation-based time-scale probes, layer ablations for output contribution, and qualitative generative behavior (parentheses).
- The incremental layer-wise training for DRNN-1O is a practical training heuristic and may be of independent interest.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: stacking vanilla RNNs and adding outputs at multiple depths is a straightforward combination of known ideas. Prior work on hierarchical RNNs and stacked (bi)LSTMs is acknowledged but not rigorously distinguished. The paper’s contribution is largely empirical and exploratory.
- Insufficient empirical validation against strong baselines. There is no comparison to LSTM/GRU (single-layer or stacked), which had already been shown to handle long dependencies. The “state-of-the-art for recurrent networks” claim is based on comparison to a single MRNN; no results versus stacked LSTMs on character modeling, nor to other standard baselines (e.g., KN char n-grams), are provided.
- Experimental coverage is narrow: one dataset, three model instantiations, no variance over random seeds, minimal hyperparameter exploration, and no ablations of key training choices (gradient normalization vs clipping; role of incremental layer-wise training; number of layers; hidden size trade-offs).
- Questionable fairness and clarity in comparisons: the character set differs (95 vs 86 in MRNN), the training budgets differ (layer-wise schedule vs flat), and the amount of data processed is not strictly matched. The paper asserts these differences are negligible but does not verify empirically.
- Analysis, while appealing, is ad hoc and at times confounded. The time-scale differences in DRNN-1O may be an artifact of the staged training (authors note this). Euclidean hidden-state distances are a crude proxy; no statistical tests or alternative measures (e.g., autocorrelation, linear response) are provided.
- Methodological details are underspecified for reproducibility: no epsilon in gradient normalization, no reporting of gradient norms/clipping statistics, no regularization details, whether hidden states are reset between sequences, data shuffling scheme, etc. Output layers are initialized at zero without discussion of potential optimization issues.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, but the empirical evidence is not comprehensive enough to support the strongest claims, and certain choices (gradient normalization by full norm, zero output init) are unusual and under-justified. The analyses are suggestive rather than rigorous.

Clarity
Generally well written and easy to follow. However, some references are incomplete, and several experimental details needed for reproduction are missing. Claims about “state-of-the-art” should be carefully qualified.

Originality
Modest. The architecture combines known components (stacked RNNs, deep supervision-like outputs). The incremental training tactic is practical but not conceptually new.

Significance
Potentially useful as a practical recipe (stacking improves capacity; auxiliary outputs ease training), and the qualitative analyses may inspire further study. However, without stronger baselines (especially LSTM/GRU) and broader evaluation, impact is limited.

Questions for the authors
1) Please report comparisons to LSTM/GRU (single and stacked) with matched parameter counts and training budgets on the same dataset and character set. Absent that, evaluate on widely used char-LM benchmarks (e.g., text8, PTB) for comparability.
2) Clarify preprocessing and splits, whether hidden states are carried across sequences or reset, and how batches are constructed.
3) Provide ablations: effect of gradient normalization vs clipping; effect of auxiliary outputs; effect of incremental training; number of layers; larger single-layer RNN vs multi-layer with equal parameters.
4) Justify zero initialization of output weights and report whether it impacted optimization.
5) Quantify variability across runs (mean±std BPC) and provide training curves.
6) Verify the impact of using 95 vs 86 characters by recomputing MRNN or your models with matched vocabularies, or by reporting controlled experiments.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Simple, scalable approach shows competitive performance on a large dataset using SGD.
- Interesting analyses indicating emergent temporal hierarchies and long-range behaviors.
- Practical training insights (auxiliary outputs, incremental layer-wise training).

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Limited novelty relative to established hierarchical/stacked RNN literature.
- Weak empirical validation: missing comparisons to standard gated RNN baselines; one dataset; few runs.
- Fairness concerns in SOTA claim; differences in character set and training budgets not controlled.
- Analyses are qualitative/ad hoc and confounded by training procedure.
- Reproducibility and methodological details are incomplete.

Recommendation
Lean towards rejection. The paper is clear and contains appealing observations, but the contribution is incremental and the empirical support is insufficiently rigorous against established baselines. Strengthening the experimental comparison (especially against stacked LSTMs/GRUs), adding thorough ablations, and tightening methodological details would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
