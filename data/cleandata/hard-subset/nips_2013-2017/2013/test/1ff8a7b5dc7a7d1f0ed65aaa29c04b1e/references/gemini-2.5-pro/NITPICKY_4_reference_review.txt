**Summary:**
This paper investigates the properties of Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs) for character-level language modeling. The authors propose and analyze a stacked RNN architecture where each layer receives the hidden state sequence from the layer below. Two variants are explored: one with output connections only from the top layer (DRNN-1O) and one with output connections from all layers (DRNN-AO). The central hypothesis is that this deep structure naturally learns a temporal hierarchy, with higher layers operating on longer time scales. Through a series of experiments on a large Wikipedia corpus, the authors demonstrate that DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance, matching results obtained with more complex Hessian-free optimization methods. The analysis, including perturbation studies and an examination of long-range dependencies like parenthesis balancing, provides evidence for the emergence of a hierarchy of time scales within the network's layers.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significant Contribution:** The paper's primary strength lies in its in-depth analysis of *why* deep RNNs are effective. While the architecture itself has been used before [8], this work provides novel and compelling experimental evidence that the stacked structure induces a learned temporal hierarchy.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The models achieve state-of-the-art performance on a challenging, large-scale benchmark using only stochastic gradient descent, which is an impressive result and relevant to the broader discussion on the trade-offs between first- and second-order optimization methods [2].
*   **Thoughtful Experimental Design:** The analyses are well-designed to probe the internal dynamics of the models. The typo-perturbation experiment, the context-switching experiment, and the specific analysis of parenthesis-closing provide concrete evidence supporting the main claims about temporal hierarchies and long-term memory.
*   **Honest Reporting:** The authors are commendably transparent about the limitations and failure modes of their model, such as the inability to handle nested parentheses or the forgetting of parentheses after a full stop. This adds credibility to the analysis.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, despite its valuable contributions, suffers from a substantial number of minor flaws, methodological shortcomings, and a general lack of polish that detract from its overall quality.

**1. Methodological and Reporting Deficiencies:**
*   **Lack of Statistical Rigor:** The most critical flaw is that each model was trained only once ("we only tested three network instantiations"). The justification in footnote 3—that network size minimizes performance variance—is an anecdotal claim and is not a substitute for proper experimental validation. Without multiple runs and variance reporting, it is impossible to know if the reported performance differences are statistically significant or merely the result of a fortuitous initialization. For a top-tier publication, this is a major methodological omission.
*   **Unjustified Hyperparameters:** Key architectural and training choices are either poorly justified or not justified at all. The choice of 5 layers is based on "results not shown" (footnote 4). The initialization of input weights `Z1` from `N(0, 1)` is non-standard and has a high variance, risking immediate saturation of the first layer's tanh units; this choice is not explained. The specific schedules for the layer-wise training of the DRNN-1O seem arbitrary.
*   **Ambiguous Computational Comparison:** The paper claims to achieve similar performance to Hessian-free methods at a lower computational cost, but then concedes that "an honest comparison is hard to make" (footnote 6). This weakens a key selling point of the work.

**2. Clarity and Reproducibility Issues:**
*   **Critical Typo in Core Equation:** Equation (3) for the parameter update contains a significant error. The learning rate schedule is given as `η0(- j/T)`, which is nonsensical. It should presumably be `η0(1 - j/T)`. Such an error in a fundamental equation raises concerns about the care with which the work was prepared and documented.
*   **Vague Terminology:** The paper refers to gradient normalization as an "often-used trick" rather than using the standard term "gradient clipping" (specifically, clipping the L2 norm to 1). While the equation makes the operation clear, using precise terminology is preferable.
*   **Omitted Details:** The paper states that models were constructed to have approximately 4.9 million parameters, but does not provide the calculations to show how the reported numbers of units per layer (e.g., 727 for DRNN-1O) were derived. This hinders exact replication of the model architectures.

**3. Formatting and Presentation:**
*   **Unacceptable Referencing:** The bibliography is in a state of extreme disrepair. Many entries are incomplete, containing stray commas as placeholders for missing information like year, venue, or page numbers (e.g., [1], [2], [3]). This demonstrates a profound lack of attention to detail and is not acceptable for a formal publication.
*   **Minor Phrasing and Typos:** The paper contains minor awkward phrases (e.g., "The last decade, machine learning has seen...") and informalities that could be improved with more careful proofreading. Footnote 2 mentions 86 characters in [19] but doesn't state how many their set of 95 shares, making the claim that the difference is not "meaningfully" affecting cross-entropy difficult to verify.

**Decision:**
While the paper presents interesting ideas and a valuable analysis of DRNNs, the accumulation of minor flaws is substantial. The methodological issue of running each experiment only once is a serious concern. This, combined with a critical typo in a core equation, numerous small clarity issues, and an exceptionally poorly formatted reference list, suggests that the paper is not yet ready for publication at NIPS. The work has clear potential, but it requires a significant revision to address the issues of experimental rigor, clarity for reproducibility, and overall scholarly presentation. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**.
