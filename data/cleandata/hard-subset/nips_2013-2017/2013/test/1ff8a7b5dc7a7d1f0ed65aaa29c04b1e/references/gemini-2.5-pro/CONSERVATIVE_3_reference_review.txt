### Review of "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN), an architecture constructed by stacking multiple recurrent layers. The core hypothesis is that this deep structure naturally induces a temporal hierarchy, where lower layers process information on shorter timescales and higher layers capture longer-term dependencies. The authors evaluate this model on the task of character-level language modeling using a large Wikipedia corpus. They present two variants: one with an output connection only from the top layer (DRNN-1O) and another with connections from all layers (DRNN-AO). The main empirical result is that these models, trained with standard Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD), achieve performance comparable to the state-of-the-art established by more complex models (Multiplicative RNNs) trained with advanced second-order optimization methods (Hessian-Free). The paper dedicates significant effort to analyzing the trained models, providing experimental evidence for the emergence of different timescales across layers and demonstrating the model's ability to capture long-range dependencies, such as closing parentheses.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results with a Simple Method:** The paper's most significant contribution is achieving state-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark using a conceptually simple architecture (stacked RNNs) and a first-order optimizer (SGD). This result provides a valuable counterpoint to the trend of using increasingly complex models and computationally expensive optimizers for this task, suggesting that architectural choices can be as impactful as optimization algorithms.

2.  **Thorough Analysis:** The authors go beyond simply reporting a performance metric. The series of analytical experiments—investigating layer contributions, visualizing timescale differences through perturbation analysis, and probing the model's handling of long-range syntax like parentheses—provides compelling evidence for their central hypothesis. This level of analysis is commendable and adds significant depth to the paper, offering insights into *why* the architecture might be effective.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and the proposed architecture is explained with sufficient detail. The motivation for stacking recurrent layers to form a temporal hierarchy is intuitive and well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Baselines:** The primary weakness of this work lies in its experimental comparison. While the DRNN is compared to a single-layer "common RNN" of similar capacity, this is not the strongest or most relevant baseline. The Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network [11] is the standard and widely accepted model for tasks requiring long-term memory. Given that the paper's introduction explicitly mentions LSTMs and the core task relies heavily on long-range dependencies, the absence of a single- or multi-layer LSTM baseline is a major omission. It is difficult to assess the true benefit of the proposed DRNN architecture without knowing how it compares to a well-tuned, stacked LSTM of similar parameter count. The current comparison only demonstrates superiority over a vanilla RNN, which is already known to be difficult to train for long-term dependencies.

2.  **Limited Experimental Rigor:** The authors state they only tested three network instantiations due to computational constraints. While understandable, this lack of multiple runs with different random initializations makes it difficult to ascertain the statistical significance of the results. The performance differences between the DRNN variants and the cited state-of-the-art are small, and could potentially fall within the range of experimental variance. The assertion that "there is very little difference in performance for different initialisations" for such large networks is a strong claim that is not substantiated with evidence.

3.  **Ad-Hoc Training Procedure:** The DRNN-1O model, which achieves the best performance, requires a specific, incremental layer-wise training scheme to perform well. This feels like an engineered solution rather than a generalizable training principle and detracts from the claimed simplicity of the approach. It suggests that the standard end-to-end training is unstable or ineffective for this variant, pointing to optimization challenges that are not fully addressed. This procedural complexity undermines the argument that a simple architecture with SGD is sufficient.

4.  **Limited Novelty of the Architecture:** The concept of stacking recurrent layers is not entirely novel. The authors themselves cite [8] (Graves et al.), which employed stacked bi-directional LSTMs for speech recognition. While this paper's contribution lies more in the in-depth analysis and its application to character-level language modeling, the architectural innovation itself is incremental. The main value is therefore in the analysis and the demonstration of SGD's effectiveness, but this should be framed more clearly.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Achieves strong, near state-of-the-art results on a difficult benchmark using a standard optimizer (SGD).
*   Provides a detailed and insightful analysis of the model's internal dynamics, supporting the central hypothesis of a learned temporal hierarchy.
*   The work is clearly presented and the experiments are described in enough detail for reproducibility.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper fails to compare against the most relevant and established baseline for this task: the LSTM network. This is a critical flaw in the experimental design.
*   The lack of multiple experimental runs and error bars makes it difficult to confidently assess the significance of the reported performance gains.
*   The best-performing model relies on an ad-hoc, layer-wise training procedure, which complicates the training process and weakens the paper's claims of simplicity.
*   The architectural novelty is limited.

**Conclusion:**

This paper presents an interesting exploration of stacked RNNs and provides a valuable analysis of their emergent properties. The performance achieved with a simple optimizer is notable. However, the work is severely hampered by a weak baseline comparison. Without a direct, rigorous comparison to an LSTM-based model of similar capacity, the central claims about the DRNN's effectiveness remain unproven. While the analysis is well-executed, it supports an architecture whose advantages over established methods have not been demonstrated. Due to these significant concerns about methodological soundness and the lack of a crucial baseline, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.
