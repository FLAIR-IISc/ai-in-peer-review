### NIPS Review

**Summary:**
This paper introduces the Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN), an architecture constructed by stacking multiple recurrent layers. The core hypothesis is that this deep structure allows the model to develop a temporal hierarchy, where different layers process information at different time scales. This contrasts with standard "shallow" RNNs that have a single recurrent layer, and standard feedforward DNNs that lack temporal memory. The authors propose two variants: one with an output connection only from the top layer (DRNN-1O) and another with connections from all layers (DRNN-AO). They evaluate these models on the challenging task of character-level language modeling using a large Wikipedia corpus. Through a series of insightful analyses, they provide evidence that the DRNN learns a hierarchy of time scales and is capable of capturing very long-term dependencies, achieving state-of-the-art results for recurrent networks on this task using only stochastic gradient descent.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this paper is its bold and compelling central idea. While the concept of stacking layers is fundamental to deep learning, its application to RNNs to explicitly create a temporal hierarchy is a simple, elegant, and powerful innovation. The authors move beyond the common trend of merely applying deeper networks for better performance; they propose a clear hypothesis for *why* a deep recurrent architecture should be effective for sequential data and then provide a thoughtful analysis to support it.

The analysis is the paper's most significant contribution and sets it apart from works focused purely on benchmark performance. The experiments designed to probe the model's internal state are excellent.
1.  The "typo-perturbation" test (Figure 3, left) provides direct and convincing evidence that different layers learn to operate on different time scales, with higher layers exhibiting longer memory. This beautifully validates the paper's core premise.
2.  The analysis of long-term dependencies via parenthesis-closing is particularly insightful. It demonstrates that the model learns something more profound than simple sequence memorization—it appears to learn an abstract, state-based representation of "being inside parentheses," a hallmark of true grammatical understanding.
3.  The qualitative analysis of generated text after ablating specific layers offers a fascinating glimpse into the functional specialization of the hierarchy, suggesting lower layers handle basic statistics while upper layers model higher-order structure.

Furthermore, the paper's results are highly significant. Achieving performance comparable to the state-of-the-art set by a much more complex Hessian-Free optimization method, using only a simple (though cleverly applied) SGD procedure, is a major accomplishment. It strongly suggests that the architectural innovation of the DRNN is the primary driver of performance, rather than a more powerful optimizer. This has important practical implications for the field.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper is not without its flaws, though in my view, they are minor in the face of its conceptual contribution.
1.  The empirical validation is somewhat limited. The authors report results from only a single run for each of their three network instantiations, citing computational cost. While understandable, this prevents a robust assessment of the variance in performance and the stability of the training process.
2.  The layer-wise training scheme used for the DRNN-1O, while effective, feels a bit ad-hoc. It would be beneficial to have a more thorough analysis or justification for this specific training schedule.
3.  The comparison to the Hessian-Free baseline is not perfectly controlled, as the authors acknowledge. A more direct comparison of computational cost (e.g., total floating point operations or wall-clock time to convergence on identical hardware) would have strengthened their claims of efficiency.

**Recommendation:**
This paper is a clear and enthusiastic **Accept**.

The weaknesses identified are largely related to the completeness of the experimental validation, which is a secondary concern given the strength and novelty of the core ideas presented. This work is precisely the kind of research NIPS should champion: it introduces a significant new architectural concept, supports it with strong performance on a difficult task, and, most importantly, provides deep analysis and insight into *why* the model works. The paper doesn't just push a number on a leaderboard; it advances our fundamental understanding of how to model hierarchical structures in time. The analyses of learned time scales and long-term dependencies are exemplary and will likely inspire a new wave of research into the properties and capabilities of deep recurrent models. This is a foundational piece of work that others will build upon for years to come.
