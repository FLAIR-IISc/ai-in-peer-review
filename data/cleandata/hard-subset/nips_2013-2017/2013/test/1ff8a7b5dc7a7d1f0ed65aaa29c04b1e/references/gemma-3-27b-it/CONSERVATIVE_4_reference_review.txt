This paper explores deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), essentially stacking RNNs to create a temporal hierarchy. While the motivation – addressing limitations of single-layer RNNs in capturing multi-timescale dependencies – is reasonable, the presentation feels somewhat incremental and the evidence provided, while promising, doesn’t yet justify a strong endorsement for NIPS.

The core idea of stacking RNNs isn’t entirely novel (acknowledged by the authors referencing [5, 8]), and the paper doesn't present a fundamentally new architectural component. The key contribution appears to be the empirical demonstration of performance on a character-level language modeling task, achieving state-of-the-art results comparable to MRNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization, but using only SGD. This is a positive finding, but it’s framed as a comparison to *one* specific baseline using a different (and computationally expensive) optimization method. A more comprehensive comparison to other readily reproducible RNN architectures (e.g., standard LSTMs trained with SGD) would significantly strengthen the claims.

My primary concern lies with the depth of analysis. The authors claim a hierarchy of timescales emerges, and offer some experimental evidence (typo perturbation, parenthesis closure). However, the analysis is largely descriptive. For instance, the differences in timescale observed in DRNN-1O versus DRNN-AO are attributed to the training method, but this feels speculative and lacks rigorous investigation. What specific aspects of the training process induce this difference? Furthermore, the explanation for why DRNN-1O remembers longer dependencies – relating it to the training procedure – feels like post-hoc rationalization rather than a theoretically grounded explanation. The analysis of parenthesis closure is interesting, but doesn’t address more complex syntactic structures.

The paper is generally clearly written, but the notation could be improved. The distinction between `s(t)` and `y*(t)` isn’t immediately clear and feels unnecessary. The extended description of the training setup (section 2.3) is detailed but slightly verbose. 

The originality is moderate. The DRNN architecture itself isn’t radically new; the contribution is more in the effective application and empirical validation on a specific task. The significance is potentially high – a more accessible path to SOTA language modeling results is valuable. However, the lack of comprehensive baselines and detailed analysis prevents a strong assessment of the true impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Achieves competitive performance on a challenging task using a relatively simple training procedure (SGD).
*   Provides some preliminary evidence supporting the claim of emergent temporal hierarchies.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in sequence modeling – capturing multi-timescale dependencies.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited comparative analysis against established baselines (particularly LSTMs trained with SGD).
*   Analysis of the emergent temporal hierarchies is descriptive rather than rigorous.
*   Claims of architectural advantages are not strongly supported by theoretical analysis.
*   The incrementality of the work doesn't quite justify NIPS acceptance without stronger validation.



**Overall assessment:** Reject with encouragement to resubmit after more thorough experimentation and analysis. The authors should:

1.  Include a comprehensive comparison to standard LSTM networks trained with SGD.
2.  Provide a more rigorous analysis of the emergent timescales, perhaps using information-theoretic measures or probing techniques.
3.  Explore the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters and network size.
4.  Address the limitations of the analysis (e.g., the focus on parentheses) and consider more complex linguistic structures.
5.  Investigate the reasons behind the differing timescale behaviors in DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO.
