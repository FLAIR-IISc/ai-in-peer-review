

# Review of "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks"

This paper introduces a Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN) architecture that combines the hierarchical processing of deep neural networks with the temporal memory of RNNs. The authors claim that DRNNs can automatically create different time scales at different layers, enabling effective modeling of both short- and long-term dependencies in sequential data. They evaluate their approach on character-level language modeling using a Wikipedia corpus, reporting state-of-the-art results with standard SGD training.

## Strengths
The paper makes a clear contribution to understanding how hierarchical RNN architectures can learn temporal hierarchies. The experimental analysis of time scales through the typo-perturbation test and parentheses-closing task provides valuable insights into DRNN behavior. The comparison to standard RNNs and the analysis of layer contributions through output ablation are particularly well-executed.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Inconsistencies and Errors
The paper contains multiple mathematical notation inconsistencies that undermine technical credibility. The notation "N (0, N−1/2)" appears multiple times, but should be formatted as "N(0, N^{-1/2})" with proper superscript. The BPC metric is written as "BPC = −〈log2 pc〉" without proper mathematical symbols (should be "BPC = -⟨log₂ p_c⟩"). The paper inconsistently uses "time scales" and "time-scales," which should be standardized.

The paper claims "the DRNN-AO has very short time-scales in the three bottom layers," but fails to clarify whether "bottom" refers to layers 1-3 or 3-5. The description of training protocols contains critical ambiguities: "T = {0.5, 1, 1.5, 2, 2.5} × 10^5" is unclear about whether these represent epochs or parameter updates. The statement "the DRNN-AO sees the full training set about 7 times" is mathematically nonsensical since the number of training set passes is determined by the number of epochs, not parameter updates.

### Methodological Concerns
The claim that "using only stochastic gradient descent (SGD) we are able to get state-of-the-art performance... which was previously only obtained using the far more advanced Hessian-free training algorithm" is misleading. The authors don't provide a direct comparison of training time or computational resources between their approach and the Hessian-free method, making the claim unsubstantiated.

The statement "We need time in the order of 10 days to train a single network, largely due to the difficulty of exploiting massively parallel computing for SGD" is factually incorrect. SGD is highly parallelizable, and the authors' single-GPU implementation (NVIDIA GeForce GTX 680) is not representative of modern deep learning practices where multiple GPUs or TPUs are standard for such tasks.

### Clarity and Presentation Issues
The paper uses inconsistent terminology without clear definitions. "DRNN-1O" and "DRNN-AO" are used without explaining the "1O" and "AO" acronyms. The paper claims "the DRNNs can have extensive memory of several hundred characters long" but only demonstrates memory for phrases up to a few hundred characters in the parentheses test, which doesn't constitute "extensive" memory.

The reference list contains incomplete entries, including "The tradeoffs of large-scale learning. Optimization for Machine Learning, page, L. Bottou, O. Bousquet, , ," which lacks page numbers and publication details.

### Experimental Limitations
The paper claims "we only tested three network instantiations" due to "the difficulty of exploiting massively parallel computing for SGD," but this is a poor justification given the modest scale of the experiments. The paper doesn't explain why they couldn't run multiple initializations to confirm the robustness of their results.

The paper states "In our experience the networks are so large that there is very little difference in performance for different initialisations," but this claim is not supported by any data or analysis.

## Conclusion
While the paper makes a potentially interesting contribution to understanding hierarchical RNN architectures, the numerous technical inconsistencies, methodological shortcomings, and presentation issues significantly undermine its credibility. The mathematical notation errors and ambiguous descriptions of experimental protocols make it difficult to assess the validity of the claims. The paper's assertion that their DRNN approach achieves state-of-the-art results with standard SGD training is not adequately supported by the experimental evidence provided.

The paper would benefit from significant revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication at NIPS. As written, it contains too many minor technical flaws that collectively diminish confidence in the work's validity.

Recommendation: Reject
