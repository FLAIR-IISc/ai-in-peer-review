- Paper studies stacked tanh RNNs (DRNNs) for character-level language modeling with two output designs: DRNN-1O (top-layer output) and DRNN-AO (deep supervision from all layers).

- Training uses SGD, BPTT, gradient-norm normalization, and incremental layer-wise training for DRNN-1O.

- On a large Wikipedia corpus, DRNNs (~4.9M params) achieve bits-per-character comparable to HF-trained multiplicative RNNs of similar size.

- Analyses show different layers operate at different time scales using perturbation/typo divergence and context-switch experiments.

- Layer ablations in DRNN-AO indicate top and bottom layers contribute most directly to prediction.

- Long-range dependencies are demonstrated qualitatively and quantitatively via tracking and generating matching parentheses over hundreds of characters.

- Stacked RNNs and deep supervision are known ideas; multiplicative RNNs with HF are strong baselines on the same corpus.

- Novelty lies in demonstrating simple stacked tanh RNNs trained with SGD can match HF results, systematic time-scale analyses, and use of deep supervision as both training aid and probe.

- Architecture and experiments are technically sound; perturbation/context experiments and parentheses probes are reasonable diagnostics.

- Empirical results are solid and include ablations and behavioral diagnostics, but have notable shortcomings.

- Missing key baselines: no comparisons to LSTM/GRU or stacked LSTMs despite their relevance for long-term dependencies.

- Limited reporting/statistics: only three models, no variance across runs, scant hyperparameter sensitivity or detailed hyperparameter descriptions.

- Unclear validation usage: no explicit mention of validation split or early stopping and unclear hyperparameter selection process.

- Evaluation comparability issues: character set differs from prior MRNN baseline (96 vs 86 chars) and fairness is only briefly argued.

- Some training details are under-specified or unconventional (gradient normalization to unit norm, zero-initialized output weights, unclear BPTT horizon, and a likely typo in the LR decay formula).

- Paper is generally clear and well-structured; motivation for deep supervision and incremental training is explained and supported by figures and tables.

- Originality is moderate: methodological ideas are known but analyses and pragmatic training recipes add value.

- Significance is moderate-to-good: practical demonstration and analyses relevant to sequence modeling, but would be stronger with stronger baselines and broader ablations.

- Strengths: clear motivation, competitive results on a large dataset with simple methods, insightful temporal analyses, and practical training heuristics.

- Weaknesses: missing LSTM/GRU baselines, limited experimental breadth and statistical rigor, character-set comparability concerns, and unclear training specifics.

- Questions for authors include: report of validation splits/early stopping, comparison to similarly sized stacked LSTM/GRU, exact BPTT truncation length, gradient clipping vs normalization experiments, and effects of zero vs small-random output-weight initialization.

- Suggestions: add stacked LSTM/GRU baselines, report standard deviations/multiple seeds, harmonize or quantify character-set impact, clarify training details (Eq. 3, BPTT horizon, initialization), and explore depth/width allocations.

- Arguments for acceptance: solid empirical contribution, careful analyses elucidating temporal hierarchies and long-range behavior, and practical training recipe.

- Arguments against acceptance: limited architectural novelty, missing crucial baselines weakening significance, sparse experimental rigor, and minor clarity issues.

- Recommendation: Weak accept.
