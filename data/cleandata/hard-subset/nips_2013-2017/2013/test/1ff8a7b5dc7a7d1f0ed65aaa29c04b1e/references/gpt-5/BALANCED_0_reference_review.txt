Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies “deep recurrent neural networks” (DRNNs), obtained by stacking vanilla tanh RNN layers, with either (i) a single softmax output on the top layer (DRNN-1O) or (ii) softmax connections from all layers whose logits are summed (DRNN-AO). The authors train with SGD using gradient normalization and a linear learning-rate decay. For DRNN-1O they use an incremental, supervised layer-wise schedule (adding layers sequentially; always training the full network after each addition), arguing it improves optimization.

The empirical focus is character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus (as in Sutskever et al., ICML’11), with ~4.9M parameters to match prior work. They report competitive bits-per-character (BPC) to multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free (HF) optimization, and demonstrate qualitative and quantitative analyses of temporal hierarchy: per-layer ablations, perturbation (“typo”) and context-switch experiments to estimate time scales, and a study of long-range dependencies via closing parentheses in both scoring and generation.

Relative to prior hierarchical RNN work (El Hihi & Bengio, 1996; Fernández et al., 2007) and recent deep/stacked recurrent models (e.g., Graves et al., stacked LSTM; Chen et al., hierarchical RNNs for speech), this paper’s novelty is to use simple stacked RNNs, a pragmatic training scheme, and to analyze emergent time scales and long-term behaviors on a challenging real-world corpus, comparing to MRNN+HF (Sutskever et al., 2011).

Strengths
- Clear empirical demonstration that stacking vanilla RNNs yields a temporal hierarchy: bottom layers capture short-term statistics, upper layers longer contexts, especially evident in the perturbation and context-switch experiments.
- Well-motivated architectural variants (DRNN-1O vs DRNN-AO), with thoughtful training considerations; the incremental supervised layer-wise schedule for DRNN-1O is pragmatic and appears effective.
- Competitive BPC on a large, widely used dataset using only SGD, narrowing the gap to HF-trained MRNNs; helpful engineering insights (gradient normalization).
- Nice qualitative analysis (generation, parentheses tracking and ablation effects) that links layer roles to linguistic phenomena.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Baselines are limited. The comparison omits strong gated architectures (LSTM/GRU), including stacked LSTMs that are explicitly designed to model long-term dependencies and multiple time scales, and would be a natural point of comparison on this task and parameter budget.
- The comparison to MRNN+HF is not strictly apples-to-apples: different character vocabularies (95 vs 86), and the total data seen per update/time is not clearly matched. The claim of “state-of-the-art for recurrent networks” would be more convincing with a more controlled setup and additional baselines.
- Training details that could materially affect results are under-specified: whether hidden states are carried across mini-batches or reset; sequence sampling strategy; any regularization; gradient norm behavior (normalization vs thresholded clipping); choice of BPTT truncation and its effect; variance across seeds (only three runs due to compute).
- The DRNN-1O benefits from a special training curriculum (discarding and reinitializing output weights between stages). It is unclear whether AO or the plain RNN receive comparably helpful schedules, complicating fairness of the architecture comparison.

Clarity
- Generally well-written, with helpful figures. However, some experimental specifics are missing (as noted above), and the rationale for zero-initializing all output weights and its potential optimization implications are not discussed.
- The reported results would benefit from explicit numeric BPC values for all models and references in a single table with clear dataset/protocol notes, plus standard errors.

Originality
- Architecturally, stacking RNNs and connecting all layers to the output are known ideas. The originality lies more in the training heuristic (incremental supervised layer-wise schedule for RNNs) and the careful temporal analysis on real-world data. While not groundbreaking architecturally, this is a useful empirical contribution.

Significance
- Demonstrating that simple stacked tanh RNNs trained with SGD can approach MRNN+HF performance on large-scale character LM is practically interesting. The time-scale analyses and parentheses study provide insight that may guide future hierarchical designs.
- That said, without comparisons to strong gated baselines, it is hard to assess how much this advances the state of the art beyond what is already achieved by LSTMs/GRUs or deeper gated stacks.

Questions for the authors
- Do you carry hidden states across mini-batches (stateful training), or reset at every sequence? How are sequences sampled (contiguous vs random chunks)?
- Did you try thresholded gradient clipping instead of full normalization? Any sensitivity analysis?
- Is the incremental supervised layer-wise schedule also beneficial for DRNN-AO or the plain RNN?
- How robust are results across random seeds? Any variance estimates?
- Any regularization (weight decay, dropout) used?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add parameter-matched baselines with stacked LSTMs (and/or GRUs), and possibly MRNN trained with SGD, to situate DRNNs more convincingly.
- Unify and clarify the training protocol across models; ablate the incremental schedule to isolate its contribution.
- Report precise BPCs with confidence intervals, and harmonize evaluation settings (character set, data passes).
- Clarify hidden state handling, sequence sampling, and regularization; release code/models if possible.
- Explore hybrid designs (e.g., residual or skip connections, gating in upper layers) and analyze their time scales using your perturbation methodology.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid empirical analysis demonstrating emergent temporal hierarchies in stacked RNNs on a large real-world dataset.
- Competitive performance with a simple optimizer; practical training insights.
- Clear, interpretable experiments (ablation, perturbation, parentheses) that the community can build on.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited and potentially unfavorable set of baselines; missing comparison to standard gated recurrent models.
- Some experimental details are under-specified; fairness of comparisons (including the special training schedule) is unclear.
- Architectural novelty is modest; contribution is primarily empirical.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper is well-executed and insightful, but the lack of strong baselines (especially LSTMs/GRUs) and some ambiguities in the experimental protocol make it difficult to judge the significance of the claimed advances. With stronger comparisons and clarified methodology, this would be a solid contribution.
