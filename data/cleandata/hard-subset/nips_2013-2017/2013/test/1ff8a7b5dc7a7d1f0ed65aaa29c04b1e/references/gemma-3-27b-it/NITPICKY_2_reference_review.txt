This paper explores deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), combining DNNs and RNNs to capture temporal hierarchies in character-level language modeling. While the core idea of stacking RNNs to induce multiple timescales is intuitive and builds upon prior work (e.g., [5, 8]), the paper suffers from a multitude of minor issues that, collectively, detract from its impact and suggest insufficient attention to detail.

The introduction is adequate but contains stylistic weaknesses. The phrasing "debatably falls into the category of deep networks" is awkward and unnecessary. The claim that RNNs don't offer hierarchical *processing* solely because older data doesn't require more processing steps is a questionable assertion; the recurrent connections themselves *are* a form of hierarchical representation learning. The discussion of [5] feels dismissive, noting its limitation to artificial tasks without acknowledging the challenges of applying hierarchical RNNs to more complex real-world problems. The concluding sentence regarding state-of-the-art performance achieved with only SGD is overstated; the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a substantial advantage over Hessian-free methods beyond simply achieving comparable performance.

Section 2 presents the DRNN architecture. The notation is generally clear, but the lack of explicit bias terms is a minor inconvenience and should be mentioned explicitly. The argument that stacking RNNs *automatically* creates different timescales is an oversimplification. While this is a potential outcome, it depends heavily on initialization, training dynamics, and network depth – a more nuanced discussion is required. The motivation for DRNN-AO (output connections at all layers) is reasonable, addressing the vanishing gradient problem, but the explanation feels tacked on.

The experimental setup (Section 3) is where the paper’s deficiencies become more pronounced. The choice of 95 characters is reasonable, but the justification for including one 'unknown' character is weak. The statement that training a single network requires 10 days "largely due to the difficulty of exploiting massively parallel computing for SGD" is concerning. This suggests a lack of optimization in the implementation or training process. The details regarding GPU specifications are appreciated, but the lack of details about batch size and precise hyperparameter tuning is problematic. The initialization scheme for weights (N(0, N<sup>-1/2</sup>) and N(0, 1)) is standard but lacks justification. The differing learning rates for DRNN-1O layers (0.25 for the top layer) feels arbitrary and should be explained more thoroughly with an ablation study. The description of the incremental layer-wise training for DRNN-1O is convoluted and could be clearer. The BPTT truncation length (50 characters) is mentioned, but its impact on performance isn’t discussed.

The results section (Section 3.2) presents the BPC scores. The comparison to [19] is fair, but the claims of similarity are weakened by the lack of a thorough statistical analysis. The discussion of Figure 2 (layer contribution) is superficial; it merely states that the top and bottom layers are important without delving into why. The text generation examples (Table 2) are qualitatively assessed, which is acceptable, but the interpretation of the results is again overly assertive. The typo perturbation experiment (Figure 3) is a good idea, but the interpretation of the different timescales between DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO is speculative. The observation about parenthesis handling is interesting, but the discussion lacks rigor. The claim that DRNNs remember parentheses for sequences longer than they were trained on is a strong statement requiring stronger evidence.

The conclusion summarizes the work and suggests future directions. The suggestion of supervised pre-training is reasonable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of DNN and RNN architectures.
*   Achieves competitive performance on a challenging task.
*   Provides some initial insights into the temporal dynamics of DRNNs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Numerous minor methodological flaws and inconsistencies.
*   Lack of thorough experimental analysis and hyperparameter tuning.
*   Overstated claims and speculative interpretations.
*   Insufficient justification for design choices.
*   Weak connection to established literature on hierarchical RNNs.
*   Implementation details suggest room for significant optimization.

**Overall:** While the paper presents a potentially interesting architecture, its numerous shortcomings prevent me from recommending acceptance. The lack of rigor in the experimental design, coupled with the overstated claims, suggests that the results are not as compelling as they could be. A major revision addressing the points raised above is required. I recommend **rejection**.
