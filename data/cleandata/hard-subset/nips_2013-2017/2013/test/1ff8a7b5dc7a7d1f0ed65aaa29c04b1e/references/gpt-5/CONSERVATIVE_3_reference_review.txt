Summary
The paper studies stacked recurrent neural networks (“deep RNNs,” DRNNs) for character-level language modeling. Each layer is a vanilla tanh RNN; the higher layers receive the hidden states of lower layers. Two output designs are considered: DRNN-1O (only top layer connects to softmax) and DRNN-AO (all layers connect). Training uses BPTT with SGD and gradient normalization, with an incremental, layer-wise scheme for DRNN-1O. On a Wikipedia character corpus, the authors report bits-per-character comparable to prior recurrent models of similar parameter count (notably multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization). They further analyze temporal hierarchies via perturbation experiments and demonstrate long-range behavior such as closing parentheses.

Relation to prior work: Hierarchical/stacked RNNs have been explored (El Hihi & Bengio; Fernández/Graves/Schmidhuber; Graves et al. on deep BLSTM). The paper’s novelty is primarily in a simple stacked architecture trained with SGD and an empirical analysis of layer-wise time scales.

Strengths
- Clear, well-written exposition of the DRNN architecture and training; the time-scale analysis (typo perturbations, context switches) is informative and executed at scale (averaged over many instances).
- The layer-output ablation provides useful diagnostic insight; the parentheses experiment is an intuitive probe of long-range behavior.
- Effort to match parameter counts against prior work and to keep training simple (SGD/BPTT) is appreciated.
- Results on a large, real-world corpus, rather than synthetic tasks.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete and confounded empirical validation:
  - Missing standard baselines: no LSTM/GRU (or stacked LSTM as in [8]), despite LSTM being the established method for long-term dependencies. Comparing only to a multiplicative RNN (different architecture/training) is insufficient to support claims of state-of-the-art or architectural advantage.
  - Fairness issues: DRNN-1O receives more parameter updates than DRNN-AO (7.5e5 vs 5e5) and uses a special incremental training schedule; results are not controlled for training budget or schedule, yet conclusions compare architectures.
  - Different character sets (95 vs 86) and unspecified data handling details versus prior work undermine apples-to-apples comparisons. No variance across seeds is reported; only three model instances due to compute constraints limit robustness.
- Methodological ambiguities:
  - The “gradient normalization” divides by the gradient norm at every step, effectively fixing step length; this departs from standard gradient clipping/capping and complicates comparison. There is no ablation isolating its effect.
  - Output layers initialized at zero and reinitialized between stages may introduce optimization biases; again, no ablation.
  - Training protocol lacks key details: are hidden states reset between sequences? Are minibatches contiguous over the corpus? These decisions critically affect language model performance and the interpretation of long-range dependencies.
  - Truncated BPTT length (200 effective steps) versus claims of “several hundred characters” memory needs a more careful, controlled analysis.
- Novelty claim is limited: stacked RNNs and multi-timescale hierarchies have substantial prior art; connecting each layer to the output is not entirely new conceptually (akin to deep supervision/auxiliary classifiers). The main advance appears empirical rather than conceptual.
- Scope of analysis: While the perturbation and parentheses probes are interesting, they are largely qualitative/diagnostic. The paper stops short of rigorous controlled studies (e.g., systematic layer-depth sweeps, equalized training budgets, multiple datasets).
- Reproducibility: No code or exact hyperparameters beyond broad settings; references are incomplete; some experimental choices justified by “results not shown.”

Questions for the authors
1. Why were LSTM/GRU baselines (single-layer and stacked) omitted? Can you provide parameter-matched comparisons on the same corpus and character set?
2. Are hidden states reset between training sequences? Are minibatches contiguous through the corpus or randomly sampled? Please clarify, as this affects context usage and BPC.
3. How sensitive are results to the gradient normalization heuristic? How does standard gradient clipping compare?
4. Can you equalize the number of updates and training schedule between DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO to make a fair architectural comparison?
5. How many seeds were tried? Please report mean ± std for BPC.
6. Can you benchmark on standard corpora (e.g., text8, Penn Treebank) to facilitate comparison?
7. What is the effect of varying the number of layers (2–6) under a fixed parameter budget?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, accessible write-up with thoughtful analyses that may interest the community.
- Demonstrates that simple stacked tanh RNNs trained with SGD can be competitive on large-scale character LM.
- The temporal-hierarchy measurements offer useful insights and tools for future analysis.

Cons for acceptance
- Lacks strong, established baselines (LSTM/GRU), making it difficult to assess significance.
- Architectural comparisons are confounded by unequal training schedules and budgets.
- Novelty over prior hierarchical/stacked RNN work is incremental.
- Reproducibility and methodological details are insufficient; some claims (state-of-the-art, long-range memory) are not rigorously substantiated.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper presents a clean stacked-RNN setup and interesting analyses, but the empirical support is not sufficiently rigorous relative to established baselines, and architectural conclusions are confounded. Strengthening the study with parameter-matched LSTM/GRU baselines, controlled training budgets, multiple datasets, ablations of the training heuristics, and clearer reproducibility details would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
