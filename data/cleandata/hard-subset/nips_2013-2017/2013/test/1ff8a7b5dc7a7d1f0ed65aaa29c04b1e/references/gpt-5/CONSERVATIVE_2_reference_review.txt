Summary
This paper studies “deep recurrent neural networks” (DRNNs), i.e., stacked vanilla RNN layers with temporal recurrence in each layer. Two output topologies are considered: (i) output from the top layer only (DRNN-1O) trained in an incremental, supervised layer-wise manner; and (ii) output from all layers (DRNN-AO, “deep supervision”). Models are trained with SGD and unit-norm gradient normalization, on character-level language modeling over a large Wikipedia corpus. With ~4.9M parameters, the proposed DRNNs achieve bits-per-character (BPC) comparable to prior multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization. The authors further analyze temporal properties via perturbation experiments, layer ablations, generated text, and a “parentheses closing” probe, arguing DRNNs learn a hierarchy of time scales and can maintain long-range information.

Relation to prior work
Stacking RNNs and hierarchical RNNs have substantial precedent (El Hihi & Bengio; Fernández et al.; bidirectional stacked LSTMs for speech by Graves et al.). The idea that deeper recurrent architectures can capture multi-timescale structure is also longstanding. The DRNN-AO topology is related to deep supervision and multi-loss training. The paper’s main novelty lies more in empirical characterization on character LM and qualitative/quantitative analyses of time scales than in architectural innovation.

Strengths
- Clear problem and generally clear exposition with concrete training details (BPTT length, batch construction, parameter counts).
- Simple, widely used architecture and optimizer (SGD with gradient normalization); the performance is competitive given the parameter budget.
- Interesting empirical analyses: layer ablations at output, perturbation (“typo”) experiments to quantify per-layer forgetting, context switch tests, and a focused “parentheses closing” probe with both discriminative and generative evaluations.
- The observation that DRNNs exhibit emergent multi-timescale behavior is plausible and supported empirically, and the analyses are reproducible in spirit.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: stacking vanilla RNNs with either top-only or all-layer outputs is a modest variation on established architectures, and prior work has already shown strong results with stacked LSTMs on real tasks. The paper does not clearly articulate what is fundamentally new beyond the particular combination and analyses.
- Baselines are insufficient and not fully comparable. Critically missing are LSTM (or GRU) baselines with similar parameter counts, and modern clipped-gradient SGD or Adam training. Comparisons to MRNN and PAQ are not apples-to-apples (different character sets, potential preprocessing differences, and unknown data pass counts). The claim of “state-of-the-art for recurrent networks with the same number of parameters” is not convincingly established.
- Experimental breadth and rigor are limited: only three model instantiations due to computational cost; no variance over random seeds; no ablations on number of layers vs width under fixed parameter budget; no exploration of BPTT length sensitivity, gradient normalization vs clipping, or regularization. The DRNN-1O uses layer-wise supervised training while DRNN-AO does not, confounding architectural versus training effects.
- Methodological concerns: 
  - Unit-norm gradient normalization changes the effective step size in a data- and iteration-dependent manner; comparison to standard gradient clipping or second-order methods would be informative.
  - Output weights initialized at zero and training with only top-layer outputs for part of training raise symmetry and optimization questions (the authors note saturation issues but do not systematically investigate).
  - No validation set or early stopping strategy is described beyond linear decay to zero; hyperparameter selection appears ad hoc.
- Analysis, while interesting, is partly confounded: the observed long time scales in DRNN-1O may be driven by the layer-wise training scheme rather than depth per se. Euclidean distance in hidden space is scale-dependent; reporting normalized measures or representational similarity metrics would strengthen claims. Layer-output ablations do not disentangle synergy or redundancy across layers.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, but the empirical claims outpace the evidence given the limited baselines, comparability issues, and lack of statistical rigor (single runs, no error bars). The analysis sections are thoughtful but would benefit from stronger controls.

Clarity
The paper is mostly well written and organized. Some details are missing for reproducibility: exact preprocessing, train/val/test splits (validation usage is unclear), tokenization of 95 vs 86 characters, compute budget per method, and training throughput. Figures referenced for key results are essential to judge impact.

Originality
Low-to-moderate. Stacked RNNs, hierarchical time scales, and deep supervision are established ideas. The paper’s novelty is primarily in the analysis on a large-scale character LM setting.

Significance
Moderate at best. The performance improvements are small and not clearly beyond stronger, established baselines (LSTM). The analyses are informative but not sufficient, by themselves, to warrant acceptance without stronger evidence of clear advantages.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add strong baselines: single-layer and stacked LSTMs (and/or GRUs) matched for parameter count, trained with comparable BPTT horizon and optimization; also include gradient clipping and Adam/SGD-M comparisons.
- Ensure strict comparability: same character set and preprocessing across methods; report number of tokens seen, wall-clock, and throughput.
- Report multiple seeds for variance; include validation-based hyperparameter selection; consider regularization (dropout/weight decay).
- Ablate depth vs width under fixed parameter budget and BPTT length.
- Disentangle architectural vs training effects: train DRNN-1O without layer-wise schedule; train DRNN-AO with and without deep supervision; measure per-layer gradient norms.
- Strengthen analyses with scale-invariant metrics and additional probing tasks (e.g., nested brackets, quotation handling), and quantify rather than only illustrate.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The work is well executed in parts and the analyses are interesting, but the architectural novelty is limited and the empirical evaluation against established baselines is insufficient to support the claimed significance.

Confidence
Moderate.
