Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) formed by stacking vanilla RNN layers, with two output designs: (i) only the top layer connects to the softmax (DRNN-1O), trained with an incremental layer-wise schedule; and (ii) all layers connect to the softmax with additive contributions (DRNN-AO), which also acts as an auxiliary supervision signal. The authors train on a large Wikipedia character-level language modeling corpus using SGD with gradient normalization and a simple linear learning-rate decay, and report bits-per-character (BPC) competitive with prior state-of-the-art recurrent models of comparable size trained with Hessian-free optimization and multiplicative RNNs. Beyond accuracy, the paper provides analyses suggesting that different layers learn different temporal scales, via perturbation/typo experiments, context-switch tests, and a parenthesis-closing study that hints at attractor-like behaviors for long-range structure.

Relative to prior work: hierarchical/stacked RNNs have been proposed (El Hihi & Bengio 1996; Fernández et al. 2005), and stacked bidirectional LSTMs have achieved strong results in speech (Graves et al., ICASSP 2013). Sutskever et al. (2011) demonstrated strong character models with MRNNs using HF. This paper’s main differentiators are: (a) a simple, generic stacked tanh-RNN trained with SGD that reaches comparable BPC; (b) a design with per-layer output connections that both ease optimization and enable layer-wise contribution analysis; and (c) careful empirical probes of time scales and long-range dependencies in character models.

Strengths
- Novelty in analysis and training perspective: While stacked recurrence itself is not new, the auxiliary-output DRNN variant and the incremental training of DRNN-1O are straightforward, general ideas that appear effective and interpretable. The temporal-scale analyses (typo decay, context switch, parentheses) are insightful and go beyond typical benchmarking.
- Significance: Demonstrates that plain RNNs, properly trained with SGD and simple tricks, can match prior HF-trained MRNNs at comparable parameter counts on a large-scale corpus. The findings on emergent temporal hierarchy are likely of interest to practitioners designing deep recurrent architectures.
- Quality: The methodology is sound, and the ablations (zeroing per-layer outputs) and perturbation studies are thoughtfully designed, if imperfect. The parentheses experiment is particularly compelling, indicating long-span structure beyond training truncation length.
- Clarity: The paper is generally clear, with explicit update equations, training protocol, and dataset details; the motivation for per-layer outputs is well articulated.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty of the core architecture: Stacking RNNs and using hierarchical recurrence has a substantial history; the main architectural addition is the auxiliary outputs and a pragmatic training schedule. The work would be stronger with clearer positioning versus stacked LSTMs and hierarchical RNNs beyond the cited speech result.
- Narrow empirical scope: Only one dataset/task (Wikipedia char LM), three model instances, and no in-house baseline comparisons to LSTM/L2-regularized RNNs or MRNNs under the same setup. Relying on literature numbers (and a slightly different 95-character set) may confound SOTA claims.
- Under-specified training details and stability: The gradient normalization to unit norm is unusual and its impact is not quantified; zero initialization of output weights deserves justification; no variance over random seeds; and the computational trade-offs versus HF are not rigorously compared.
- Ambiguity in per-layer ablations: Zeroing a layer’s output to assess importance conflates compensation effects; more controlled diagnostics (e.g., training with auxiliary losses but decoding from top-only) would strengthen conclusions about each layer’s role.
- Analysis stops short of stronger causal tests: The “timescale” claims are plausible but could be bolstered with more formal measures (e.g., effective context length distributions, information decay, or probing classifiers).

Quality
Technically sound overall, with thoughtful experiments and honest discussion. Some choices (e.g., learning-rate to zero before adding a layer; gradient normalization) would benefit from empirical justification.

Clarity
Well written and organized; includes enough detail to roughly reproduce results, though missing some stability/sensitivity analyses.

Originality
Moderate. The novelty lies more in the training/use of auxiliary outputs and the diagnostic analyses than in the core model class.

Significance
Moderate to good. Results suggest a practical, simple path to strong char-level LM and provide evidence for emergent temporal hierarchies in deep recurrence. Likely to influence subsequent designs and analyses.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include a stacked LSTM baseline (e.g., 2–5 layers) under the same training budget; and, if possible, a MRNN reproduction with SGD, to isolate the effect of HF versus architecture.
- Report sensitivity to gradient normalization, learning-rate schedule, truncation length, and number of layers. Provide variance over multiple seeds.
- Evaluate decoding from top-only while keeping auxiliary losses during training to disentangle the benefit of deep supervision from multi-scale ensembling at test time.
- Clarify the 95 vs 86 character set choice and its effect on BPC; provide a matched evaluation if feasible.
- Deepen the timescale analysis with formal measures (e.g., impulse response, mutual information decay) and consider nested parentheses or other structured long-range phenomena.
- Release code and training scripts to enhance reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear empirical evidence that stacking vanilla RNNs with auxiliary outputs yields emergent temporal hierarchies and competitive performance at scale.
- Novel and useful analyses that provide insight into what deep RNN layers learn, beyond headline metrics.
- Practical training insights (deep supervision, incremental stacking) that are likely to be adopted.

Arguments against acceptance
- Architectural novelty is limited; results could be seen as incremental relative to prior hierarchical/stacked RNN/LSTM work.
- Narrow experimental breadth and lack of strong contemporary baselines under identical conditions.
- Some methodological choices are under-justified and not stress-tested.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Despite limited architectural novelty, the paper offers a clear, bold empirical case for deep recurrent architectures with auxiliary outputs and provides insightful analyses of temporal hierarchy and long-range dependencies. The work is likely to stimulate further research and practice.
