Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) formed by stacking simple tanh RNN layers and optionally connecting each layer to the output. The authors consider two variants: DRNN-1O, where only the top layer connects to the softmax output; and DRNN-AO, where all layers contribute to the output via separate linear projections. They train with BPTT using SGD with gradient-norm normalization and a linearly decaying learning rate, and for DRNN-1O use an incremental layer-wise training schedule in which a new layer is added once the network has been trained to convergence with the current top layer.

On a large Wikipedia character-level language modeling task (~1.4B chars; test = last 10M; 96-character alphabet), they compare a single-layer RNN (~2.1k units) to 5-layer DRNNs (hidden widths chosen to match ~4.9M parameters across models). Both DRNNs outperform the single RNN; DRNN-1O achieves the best BPC and is roughly on par with prior state of the art for recurrent models of similar size trained with Hessian-free (HF) optimization on multiplicative RNNs [Sutskever et al. 2011]. Beyond accuracy, the paper analyzes temporal behavior: (i) perturbation experiments (single “typo” divergence and context-switch tests) to estimate layerwise time scales; (ii) ablations of per-layer output contributions in DRNN-AO; and (iii) parenthesis tracking, showing that the top layer maintains elevated probability for closing parentheses across long spans, even beyond BPTT window length. The work relates to hierarchical RNNs [El Hihi & Bengio], stacked LSTMs [Graves et al.], and HF-trained MRNNs [Sutskever et al.], but focuses on simpler stacked tanh RNNs trained with SGD and on analyzing emergent temporal hierarchies.

Strengths
- Clear architectural question and empirical answer: stacking simple RNNs yields emergent multi-timescale dynamics; AO vs 1O clarifies the roles of layers.
- Solid large-scale experiment on a widely used corpus; DRNNs reach competitive BPC with a comparatively simple optimizer.
- Interesting, concrete analyses: typo/context perturbations quantify forgetting at each layer; ablations of AO show top layer contributes most directly to output; parenthesis-tracking illustrates long-term structure capture.
- Training recipe is simple and may be practical for others (gradient normalization, linear LR decay, incremental layering).
- The paper is generally well written and honest about limitations (few runs due to cost, limited scope of tasks).

Weaknesses
- Missing strong baselines: there is no comparison to (stacked) LSTMs or GRUs, which are the canonical approach to long-term dependencies and had already been successful in language modeling. The closest prior stacking result [Graves et al.] is cited but not empirically compared.
- Limited experimental breadth and statistical rigor: only three model instances, single seeds, no variance estimates, and no validation-based hyperparameter selection; results may be sensitive to settings such as BPTT length, gradient normalization, and learning-rate schedule.
- Fairness of state-of-the-art claims is hard to assess: the character set differs (95 vs 86), training passes differ, and compute budgets differ across works. A stronger, apples-to-apples reproduction or standardized split would help.
- Training details raise concerns: always normalizing by the gradient norm changes the effective optimization dynamics; there is no momentum/Adam or standard gradient clipping comparison. No regularization techniques are discussed. The incremental layer-wise procedure for DRNN-1O reinitializes the output layer repeatedly, which may complicate attribution of gains.
- Reproducibility gaps: unclear whether hidden states are reset between BPTT segments, how sequences are sampled (overlaps, strides), exact BPC numbers and per-model hyperparameters, and whether code/models will be released.

Quality
The methodology is generally sound: architecture and equations are standard; training details are described; and analyses are carefully executed. The perturbation experiments are insightful and support the claim of emergent timescales. However, the empirical section is narrow (few configurations, no gated baselines), and some training choices (e.g., gradient normalization, LR to zero per stage) are nonstandard and not ablated. Claims about “state-of-the-art for recurrent networks” are plausible but not rigorously established on an identical setup.

Clarity
The paper is readable and well organized. The motivation, architectures, and experiments are explained clearly. Some details should be added for reproducibility: whether hidden states are carried between batches; sampling protocol for sequences; exact BPC numbers; train/val/test splits; early stopping/selection; and exact gradient normalization (global norm across all parameters per minibatch?).

Originality
Stacking RNNs is not new, nor are multi-timescale hierarchical RNN ideas. The novel aspects are: (a) the DRNN-AO variant and the use of per-layer outputs both for training signal propagation and interpretability; (b) the incremental layer-wise training schedule for DRNN-1O; and (c) the set of temporal analyses (typo/context/parentheses) on a large real corpus. The combination and the analysis are original and useful, even if the architecture itself is straightforward.

Significance
Character-level modeling is an important benchmark; demonstrating competitive performance with simple SGD-trained stacked RNNs is valuable. The temporal analysis offers insights likely to interest the community and to inform future hierarchical RNN designs. However, the lack of LSTM/GRU comparisons limits the impact of the accuracy claims.

Specific suggestions and questions
- Add LSTM/GRU baselines (single and stacked) with matched parameter counts and training budget. Even a smaller-scale comparison would contextualize the benefits of stacking vanilla RNNs.
- Clarify training protocol: Are hidden states reset to zero at the start of each 250-char segment? If so, discuss how long-range effects beyond BPTT arise and whether training with longer truncation changes results.
- Report exact BPC numbers for all models, along with training time, number of tokens seen, and variance across seeds.
- Compare gradient-norm normalization to standard gradient clipping and to optimizers like SGD with momentum/Adam.
- Provide validation set usage (for LR schedule or early stopping) and any regularization attempts (weight decay, dropout).
- Discuss the effect of output-layer zero initialization and repeated reinitialization in the incremental training scheme.
- Release code/configs if possible.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear empirical evidence of emergent temporal hierarchy in stacked RNNs.
- Competitive performance on a large, relevant task with a simple training recipe.
- Well-executed analyses (perturbations, ablations, parenthesis tracking) that yield interpretable insights.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing comparisons to LSTM/GRU undermines claims about the practical advantage of DRNNs.
- Limited experimental breadth and lack of statistical rigor.
- Some training choices are unusual and unablated; reproducibility details are insufficient.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The analysis is insightful and the results are promising, but adding gated baselines and clarifying training/reproducibility would substantially strengthen the paper. If space/time permits, I encourage the authors to include at least a small-scale stacked LSTM comparison and the requested clarifications.
