### NIPS Review

**Paper Title:** Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks

**Reviewer Score:** 6 (Weak Accept)

---

### Summary

This paper introduces a "deep recurrent neural network" (DRNN), an architecture constructed by stacking multiple RNN layers. The central hypothesis is that this deep structure facilitates the learning of a temporal hierarchy, where lower layers process information on shorter time scales and higher layers capture longer-term dependencies. The authors evaluate this model on a large-scale character-level language modeling task using a Wikipedia corpus. They present two variants: one where only the top layer contributes to the output (DRNN-1O) and another where all layers contribute (DRNN-AO). The proposed models, trained with stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and gradient clipping, achieve performance comparable to the state-of-the-art at the time, which was obtained using a more complex multiplicative RNN (MRNN) trained with Hessian-free optimization. The paper's main contribution is not just the model's performance, but also a series of analytical experiments aimed at demonstrating the emergence of this temporal hierarchy and the model's ability to capture long-range dependencies.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The primary strength of this work lies in its empirical results. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on a challenging and large-scale benchmark (Wikipedia character prediction) is a significant accomplishment. Notably, the authors achieve this using a relatively simple optimization method (SGD with gradient normalization), whereas the competing result from Sutskever et al. (2011) relied on the far more computationally intensive Hessian-free optimization. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed architecture, or at least its amenability to first-order training methods.

2.  **Thorough Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply presenting performance metrics and provides a commendable in-depth analysis of the trained models. The experiments designed to probe the time scales of different layers (the "typo" and "context switch" experiments) provide compelling evidence for the central hypothesis that a temporal hierarchy is indeed learned. The specific analysis of parenthesis closing is a well-chosen case study that concretely illustrates the model's capacity for capturing non-trivial long-term dependencies.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and the proposed architecture is straightforward to understand. The motivations for stacking RNNs and for the two output configurations (DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO) are well-articulated.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The concept of stacking recurrent layers is a rather direct extension of the well-established practice of stacking feed-forward layers to create deep networks. Hierarchical recurrent structures had been explored in prior work (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio, 1996), though admittedly not on this scale or with this depth of analysis. The novelty here lies more in the successful application and rigorous analysis on a large-scale task rather than a fundamental architectural innovation.

2.  **Insufficient Baselines and Confounded Comparisons:** My main concern lies with the experimental comparisons, which do not sufficiently isolate the contribution of the deep architecture.
    *   The primary baseline is a single, large "shallow" RNN. While the DRNN outperforms this, it is not clear that the shallow RNN represents the strongest possible baseline. A more crucial and now standard baseline, the Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network, is conspicuously absent. Given that LSTMs were specifically designed to combat the difficulties of learning long-term dependencies, a comparison against a large, well-tuned LSTM with a similar parameter count would be necessary to truly validate the claim that the *deep structure* is the key to this performance, rather than other factors.
    *   The comparison with the Hessian-Free trained MRNN is confounded by the optimization algorithm. The paper demonstrates that a DRNN with SGD can match an MRNN with HF. This is an interesting result about training, but it does not prove the DRNN is a better architecture than the MRNN. It is plausible that a standard RNN or LSTM, if trained with the same careful SGD schedule and gradient normalization, could also achieve much better results than previously thought. The experiment does not decouple the architectural contribution from the training methodology.

3.  **Complex and Ad-Hoc Training Procedure:** The best-performing model (DRNN-1O) requires a complex, incremental layer-wise training procedure. While this form of pre-training is a valid technique, it adds significant complexity and a number of heuristic choices to the training pipeline (e.g., the number of updates per stage). This reliance on a specialized training "trick" to achieve top performance somewhat undermines the elegance and simplicity of the core architectural idea. A key advantage of established models like LSTMs is their relative robustness to standard, end-to-end training.

4.  **Correlational Claims:** The analysis, while strong, establishes a correlation rather than a causal link between the observed temporal hierarchy and the model's performance. It shows that this hierarchy *emerges* during training and co-occurs with good performance, but does not definitively prove that this is the necessary mechanism for success. It is possible that the increased depth simply provides a richer, more easily optimized parameter space, and the temporal hierarchy is a side effect.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a solid piece of empirical work. The authors demonstrate that a relatively straightforward deep recurrent architecture can achieve state-of-the-art results on a difficult benchmark using a simple training algorithm. The analytical experiments are thoughtful and provide convincing evidence that the network learns to represent temporal structures hierarchically.

However, the claims of the paper's significance are tempered by the limited novelty of the architecture and, more importantly, the lack of a truly compelling set of baselines. The absence of a comparison to an LSTM network of similar size is a major omission. Furthermore, the reliance on a complex, layer-wise training scheme for the best results reduces the practical appeal of the method.

While I am skeptical of the claim that this work introduces a fundamentally new or superior approach to sequence modeling, the strong empirical results and the quality of the analysis are undeniable. The paper is a valuable contribution that clearly demonstrates the potential of depth in recurrent networks and provides useful insights into their internal workings. It is a complete and technically sound piece of research that is worthy of publication.
