**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces and analyzes a "deep" recurrent neural network (DRNN), which is constructed by stacking multiple RNN layers. The core hypothesis is that this deep architecture introduces a temporal hierarchy, where lower layers process information on shorter timescales and higher layers capture longer-term dependencies. The authors evaluate this architecture on the task of character-level language modeling using a large Wikipedia corpus. Two variants are proposed: one with output connections only from the top layer (DRNN-1O) and another where all layers contribute to the output (DRNN-AO). The experimental results show that both DRNN models outperform a standard, single-layer RNN baseline with a comparable number of parameters. Notably, the proposed models, trained with simple stochastic gradient descent (SGD), achieve performance on par with the state-of-the-art at the time, which was achieved using a more complex model (multiplicative RNN) and a second-order optimization method (Hessian-free). The paper supplements these performance results with a series of analyses aimed at verifying the emergence of a temporal hierarchy and the model's ability to capture long-range dependencies, such as closing parentheses over long distances.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance with a Simple Method:** The primary strength of this work is demonstrating that a conceptually straightforward architecture—stacked standard RNNs—can achieve state-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark. The fact that this is accomplished using only SGD with gradient normalization is particularly significant, as it provides a simpler and more accessible alternative to the complex Hessian-free optimization required by the contemporary state-of-the-art model [19].

2.  **Valuable Analysis and Intuition:** The paper does not merely present performance metrics. The authors conduct a series of insightful analyses to investigate the internal workings of the DRNN. The experiments measuring the decay of perturbations (the "typo test") and the effect of context switching provide concrete evidence for the central claim that the stacked layers learn to operate on different timescales. The parenthesis-closing experiment is a compelling qualitative demonstration of the model's capacity for capturing long-term dependencies. This focus on analysis and understanding is a commendable aspect of the work.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The model architecture, training procedures, and experimental setups are described with sufficient detail.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The idea of stacking recurrent layers is not fundamentally new. As the authors themselves note, similar architectures had been used previously, for instance in [8] for speech recognition. The main contribution here is not the architecture itself, but rather its thorough application and analysis on a large-scale language modeling task. While the analysis is valuable, the architectural contribution is incremental.

2.  **Insufficient Baseline Comparisons:** The primary baseline is a single, very wide "common RNN." While matching the parameter count is a fair constraint, it is not clear that this is the strongest possible baseline. A single, wide layer can be notoriously difficult to train compared to a moderately deep, moderately wide network. A more convincing comparison would have included other established RNN architectures, such as a 2- or 3-layer DRNN to show the scaling effect, or perhaps an LSTM-based model, which was already an established method for handling long-term dependencies [11].

3.  **Uncontrolled Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison to the Hessian-free trained multiplicative RNN [19] is a key result, but it is not a controlled experiment. The performance similarity could be due to a combination of factors: the DRNN architecture being superior, the MRNN architecture being overly complex, or the specific dataset not requiring the full power of second-order optimization. To properly disentangle the effects of architecture from the optimizer, one would need to see results for training the baseline RNN or the DRNN itself with the Hessian-free method. As it stands, the conclusion that the DRNN architecture is responsible for closing the performance gap is not fully substantiated.

4.  **Heuristic Training and Limited Validation:** The layer-wise training procedure for the DRNN-1O is presented as a necessary trick to achieve good performance. This ad-hoc approach lacks a strong theoretical or empirical justification beyond the authors' observation. Furthermore, the authors state that they only trained three network instantiations due to computational cost, justifying this by an assertion that large networks have little performance variance. This is a significant weakness. Rigorous empirical work requires demonstrating robustness across multiple initializations or providing evidence to support such claims. The lack of hyperparameter sensitivity analysis (e.g., varying the number of layers) further limits the generality of the findings.

**Detailed Comments**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its implementation and the presented analyses are well-conceived. However, the overall quality is hampered by the weaknesses in the experimental design, particularly the limited scope of the baselines and the small number of experimental runs. The claims are generally supported by the presented results, but the strength of these claims would be significantly enhanced by more rigorous validation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exemplary in its clarity. The motivations, methods, and results are presented in a logical and understandable manner.
*   **Originality:** As noted, the architectural originality is modest. The primary novelty lies in the in-depth analysis of how depth affects the temporal properties of RNNs on a real-world, large-scale task, and in demonstrating the effectiveness of this simple architecture with a standard optimizer.
*   **Significance:** The paper holds considerable significance. It provides strong evidence that simply increasing the depth of RNNs is an effective strategy for improving performance on tasks with hierarchical temporal structure. The finding that this can be achieved with SGD makes the approach highly practical and influential. The analytical techniques presented also provide a useful template for investigating the internal dynamics of deep sequential models.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept**

This paper presents a valuable contribution by demonstrating that a simple, deep RNN architecture can achieve state-of-the-art results on a difficult task using a standard training algorithm. The analysis providing evidence for a learned temporal hierarchy is a key strength. However, the work is let down by a lack of methodological rigor in its experimental comparisons. The limited number of baseline models, the uncontrolled comparison to the prior state-of-the-art, and the small number of experimental runs prevent a full endorsement. Despite these reservations, the positive results are compelling and the analysis provides important insights that are likely to be built upon by future research. The paper is therefore marginally above the acceptance threshold.
