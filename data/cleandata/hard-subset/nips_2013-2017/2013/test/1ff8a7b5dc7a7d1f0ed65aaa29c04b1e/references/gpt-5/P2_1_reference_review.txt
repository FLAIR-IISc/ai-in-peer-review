Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs): stacked vanilla RNN layers where each layer has its own recurrent state and receives the previous layer’s hidden state as input. Two output variants are considered: DRNN-1O (only the top layer connects to the softmax) and DRNN-AO (all layers connect to the softmax, summed before softmax). The authors train character-level language models on a large Wikipedia corpus using SGD with gradient-norm normalization and a linear learning-rate decay. They compare against a single-layer RNN of matched parameter count and to published multiplicative RNN (MRNN) results trained with Hessian-free (HF). They provide analyses suggesting that stacking induces a temporal hierarchy (different time scales per layer), using perturbation experiments (typos and context switches), layer ablations, and a qualitative “parentheses closure” probe. They report that DRNNs approach state-of-the-art recurrent results for this task/parameter budget, and that DRNN-AO/DRNN-1O differ in how time scales emerge.

This work sits among hierarchical/stacked RNNs (El Hihi & Bengio 1996; Fernández et al. 2007), stacked LSTMs for speech (Graves et al. 2013), and HF-trained MRNNs for char-LM (Sutskever et al. 2011). The main new elements are: (i) a systematic comparison of stacked vanilla RNNs with different output connections, (ii) an SGD training recipe (including incremental layer-wise training for DRNN-1O), and (iii) empirical analyses of learned time scales and long-range dependencies on real text.

Quality
Strengths:
- Empirical results are solid for the chosen setting: both DRNNs outperform a same-size single-layer RNN; DRNN-1O is best; results are comparable to published HF-trained MRNNs with similar parameter counts (caveats acknowledged).
- The temporal analyses are careful and informative: typo perturbation decay, context-switch recovery, per-layer ablation, and the parentheses probe collectively support the claim that depth yields a hierarchy of time scales and improved long-range modeling.
- Training details are provided (batching, BPTT length, parameter counts, initialization), improving reproducibility.

Weaknesses/Questions:
- Baselines are limited. Given the motivation about long-term dependencies, omission of LSTM/GRU (and stacked LSTM) comparisons is a significant gap; prior work already reports strong performance with stacked LSTMs for sequence tasks.
- The “state-of-the-art” claim is circumscribed (recurrent networks of similar size), but cross-paper comparability is confounded by different character sets and training regimes. Statistically significant superiority over strong baselines is not demonstrated.
- The training scheme for DRNN-1O uses incremental layer-wise stages and zero-initialized output layers; an ablation isolating the contribution of this pretraining is missing. Similarly, the benefit of gradient-norm normalization and linear LR decay is not isolated.
- No validation set or early stopping is described; LR decay to zero is unusual and may cause undertraining or overfitting concerns. Reporting dev-set curves would help.
- Only three models were tried due to compute; variance across random seeds is not quantified.
- Equation (3) is ambiguously typeset (signs/parentheses). Some referencing/footnoting is messy.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The architectural description and experimental procedures are understandable, and the qualitative analyses are clear and compelling. However:
- Clean up Eq. (3) and explicitly define the LR schedule; clarify whether updates go to zero at step T.
- Streamline footnotes and fix typos. Provide figure captions that fully describe setups.
- Expand on the dataset pre-processing, train/val/test split, and character vocabulary differences relative to prior work.

Originality
Moderate. Stacked RNNs and hierarchical RNNs are not new; stacked LSTMs have been applied to speech and sequence tasks. The novelty lies more in:
- The simple stacked vanilla RNN instantiation with all-layers-to-output vs top-only variants and the training scheme.
- The empirical analysis demonstrating emergent time-scale separation on real-world text and probing long-range dependencies (parentheses).
These contributions are incremental but useful.

Significance
Moderate. The findings suggest that stacking vanilla RNNs trained with SGD can approach MRNN/HF performance, and that depth induces interpretable temporal hierarchies. The analysis tools may be of broad interest. However, without comparisons to gated architectures (LSTM/GRU) or modern regularization/optimization baselines, the advance in state of the art is limited.

Pros
- Clear demonstration that depth helps over single-layer RNNs at fixed parameter budget.
- Insightful, well-designed analyses of time scales and long-range behavior.
- Practical, simple training recipe (including gradient normalization and incremental training) that others can adopt.
- Results on a large, realistic corpus; qualitative samples are convincing.

Cons
- Missing comparisons to LSTM/GRU (stacked) and other strong baselines.
- Limited ablations make it hard to attribute gains to architecture vs training tricks.
- Reproducibility/rigor issues: no dev set use, limited runs, ambiguous LR schedule.
- Claims about SOTA rely on imperfect cross-paper comparisons.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add stacked LSTM/GRU baselines (same parameter budget), and, if feasible, DRNNs trained with HF or Adam to separate optimizer effects.
- Provide ablations: with/without gradient normalization, with/without incremental training for DRNN-1O, different BPTT lengths.
- Use a held-out validation set for hyperparameter selection/early stopping; report multiple runs with variance.
- Clarify Eq. (3) and initialization choices (e.g., zero-initialized output layers).
- Consider quantitative measures of time-scale separation (e.g., eigenvalue spectra, effective memory length) to complement perturbation analyses.

Recommendation: Weak accept
Rationale: While the architectural novelty is modest, the empirical study is careful and offers valuable insights into how depth in RNNs yields temporal hierarchies and improves long-range modeling on real data. With stronger baselines and clearer ablations, this would be a clear accept; as is, it remains a useful, informative contribution likely to interest the NIPS community.
