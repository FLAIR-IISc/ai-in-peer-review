Summary
This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), obtained by stacking simple recurrent layers so that each layer is itself a recurrent network receiving the previous layer’s hidden state as input. Two output coupling schemes are considered: (i) DRNN-1O, where only the top layer connects to the softmax; and (ii) DRNN-AO, where all layers contribute linearly to the softmax. The authors train large character-level language models on a Wikipedia corpus using plain SGD with gradient normalization and, for DRNN-1O, an incremental layer-wise supervised training schedule where each layer is temporarily the top layer. They report bits-per-character (BPC) competitive with prior RNN state of the art achieved with multiplicative RNNs trained by Hessian-free optimization. Beyond headline metrics, the paper provides analyses suggesting emergent temporal hierarchies: layer-wise ablations, perturbation (“typo”) response and context switch tests, and a qualitative/quantitative study of long-range dependencies such as closing parentheses.

Relation to prior work
The work situates itself among hierarchical/stacked recurrent models (El Hihi & Bengio; Fernández/Graves/Schmidhuber; Graves et al. ICASSP 2013 stacked bi-LSTMs) and advanced training methods (Martens & Sutskever, Hessian-free; Sutskever et al. MRNNs). The main architectural idea—stacking SRNs—is known, but the paper differentiates itself by (a) a simple, scalable training recipe with SGD, including the “all outputs” variant and incremental supervised pretraining for DRNN-1O, and (b) carefully designed analyses that probe per-layer time scales and the nature of long-term dependencies in a real-world char-level task. The gradient normalization heuristic relates to known practices (e.g., gradient clipping) though that specific connection is not cited.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and simplicity: The DRNN construction is straightforward, easy to implement, and avoids specialized units (e.g., LSTM gating), yet achieves strong results.
- Novel analyses: The typo perturbation, context switch BPC, and layer-contribution ablations provide compelling evidence for an emergent temporal hierarchy and illuminate which layers carry short vs. long context. The parentheses experiments are particularly illustrative of long-range memory in a natural task.
- Training contribution: The incremental supervised pretraining (temporarily attaching the output to each new top layer) is a practical idea likely of interest to practitioners and may transfer to other deep recurrent setups.
- Empirical competitiveness: On a large, standard corpus, DRNNs trained with vanilla SGD match prior RNN results obtained with more complex architectures and second-order training, supporting the thesis that depth in recurrence is beneficial.
- Scope and relevance: Character-level modeling is a stringent testbed; the work speaks directly to the NeurIPS audience interested in sequence modeling, optimization, and representational depth.

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines and breadth: Only three models are tested, on one dataset, with single runs. Crucially, there is no LSTM/GRU baseline (stacked or otherwise), nor a stacked SRN baseline without the “all outputs” modification trained identically for a clean ablation of that design choice.
- Comparability issues: The character set differs slightly from prior work (95 vs. 86), and training budgets/hardware differ, complicating SOTA claims. The paper argues the impact is negligible but does not quantify it.
- Methodological details: The gradient normalization heuristic is under-specified relative to standard clipping; Eq. (3) appears to have a typographical error (learning rate factor should likely be 1 − j/T). Zero initialization of output weights is unusual; rationale and sensitivity are not discussed. No regularization is described.
- Analysis limitations: While the qualitative and semi-quantitative analyses are insightful, they lack statistical treatment (no error bars or multiple seeds) and sometimes conflate architecture with training regimen (the different time-scale profiles of DRNN-1O vs. DRNN-AO may stem largely from the staged training procedure).
- Missing references: Work on exploding/vanishing gradients and gradient clipping (e.g., Pascanu et al., 2013) should be cited given the reliance on gradient normalization.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify Eq. (3): is the schedule η = η0(1 − j/T)? Did you use full gradient normalization to unit norm or thresholded clipping?
- Why zero-initialize output weights? How sensitive are results to this choice and to the variance of weight initializations?
- Can you provide training curves to demonstrate the optimization benefit of the “all outputs” variant?
- How do results change with identical 86-char vocabulary to match [19], and with multiple random seeds?
- Did you try stacked LSTM/GRU baselines with similar parameter counts, or a multiplicative RNN trained with SGD?
- How does performance scale with depth vs. width under fixed parameter budget?
- For the parentheses task, do you have quantitative results on nested or sentence-initial brackets beyond the anecdotes?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add at least one strong gated baseline (stacked LSTM) at similar parameter count, and/or a stacked SRN without AO under identical training for a cleaner ablation.
- Report results with an 86-char set for comparability and include variance across runs.
- Provide more detail on optimization (clipping vs. normalization, truncation length sensitivity, regularization) and fix the learning-rate schedule typo.
- Include training diagnostics showing the effect of AO on gradient flow and convergence.
- Broaden to a second dataset (e.g., text8) or include a scaling experiment to strengthen claims of generality.

Overall assessment
I find this paper a worthwhile contribution. While the architecture itself is not radically new, the combination of a simple training recipe, an effective “all outputs” design, and especially the thorough analyses of emergent time scales and long-term behavior on a real, large-scale task provide fresh insight. The empirical results are competitive and the paper advances our understanding of how depth in recurrent networks organizes temporal processing. Despite limited baselines and some methodological rough edges, the ideas are likely to influence practice and spur follow-up work.

Recommendation: Accept (leaning). My stance prioritizes the conceptual and analytical contributions over exhaustive benchmarking; addressing a few of the questions above in the camera-ready would further strengthen the paper. Confidence: medium.
