Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies “deep recurrent neural networks” (DRNNs): stacked recurrent layers where each layer receives the previous layer’s hidden state and has its own recurrent dynamics. Two output designs are considered: top-layer-only (DRNN-1O) and all-layers-to-output (DRNN-AO). The authors train these models with SGD and gradient-norm normalization, including an incremental layer-wise regime for DRNN-1O, and evaluate them on character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus. They report bits-per-character (BPC) comparable to prior state-of-the-art recurrent models with a similar parameter budget, notably the multiplicative RNN (MRNN) trained with Hessian-free (HF) optimization. Beyond accuracy, they analyze learned time scales via perturbation studies, layer ablations, and a long-range “parentheses closing” probe, arguing that stacked RNNs naturally develop a temporal hierarchy and can maintain several-hundred-character dependencies.

The work connects to a long line of hierarchical/stacked RNNs (El Hihi & Bengio 1995; Fernández et al. 2005), multi-timescale and hierarchical recurrent architectures, and stacked bidirectional LSTMs for speech (Graves et al., ICASSP 2013). The language modeling setup is close to Sutskever, Martens, and Hinton (2011), which introduced the Wikipedia character dataset and MRNN/HF training. While the authors cite several of these, the paper would benefit from engaging more with RNN language modeling literature (e.g., Mikolov et al., Interspeech 2010; related NIPS/ICML works on training RNNs and vanishing/exploding gradients) and with stacked LSTM baselines.

Strengths
- Clear, motivated architecture and training setup; the “all-layers-to-output” variant is a pragmatic approach to improve gradient flow and interpretability.
- Solid large-scale experiment on a standard dataset; performance is competitive with MRNN/HF using only SGD, which is practically relevant.
- Thoughtful analyses: layer ablations, typo-perturbation and context-switch studies to quantify layer-wise time scales, and a targeted long-term dependency probe (parentheses). These give insight into what stacked RNNs learn.
- Simple training heuristics (gradient normalization, incremental training for DRNN-1O) that are easy to reproduce and may be broadly useful.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in architecture: stacked RNNs and multi-timescale hierarchical RNNs are well-established. The main empirical conclusion—that stacking induces a temporal hierarchy—is valuable but expected.
- Comparisons are incomplete. There is no direct comparison to stacked LSTMs/GRUs or to single-layer RNNs with matched parameter counts (the baseline RNN here is smaller than DRNNs per layer and thus conflates capacity with depth). MRNN/HF numbers are cited but training budgets differ, and the character set differs (95 vs 86), complicating claims of parity.
- Training/evaluation details could be tighter: only three configurations due to compute; no variance across seeds; no validation split or early stopping; no regularization; and the learning rate schedule is fixed (linear decay) without tuning. It is hard to assess robustness.
- The interpretation that DRNNs “automatically” learn distinct time scales is partly confounded by training: the incremental layer-wise scheme for DRNN-1O likely biases several layers toward long memory, as the authors themselves note.
- The analysis, while interesting, remains largely qualitative/heururistic (e.g., Euclidean hidden-state distances). A more quantitative treatment (e.g., probing classifiers, frequency response, or spectral analyses of recurrent Jacobians) would strengthen the claims.
- Reproducibility could be improved: code is not provided; some key hyperparameters (e.g., gradient normalization/clipping specifics and thresholds) are only briefly described.

Quality
- Technically sound overall, with appropriate use of BPTT and large-scale training. However, the empirical study is under-powered (few runs, minimal hyperparameter exploration), and several comparisons are not apples-to-apples. Claims about time-scale hierarchies are plausible but not rigorously established.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. The exposition of the two output designs is clear, and the analyses are easy to follow. Still, more precise reporting would help: exact gradient normalization procedure (is it always normalized to unit norm? relation to norm clipping), truncation length and its effect, validation protocol, and how many epochs each model sees. Some references are incomplete.

Originality
- Incremental. Stacking RNNs and attaching outputs at multiple layers are known ideas; the contribution lies in pulling these together at scale for character LM and providing a careful empirical/diagnostic study.

Significance
- Moderate. Showing that SGD-trained stacked RNNs can approach MRNN/HF on this task may impact practice. The interpretability analyses are useful but not transformative. Without stronger baselines and clearer SOTA gains, the work advances understanding more than performance.

Arguments for acceptance
- Careful empirical investigation that gives insight into temporal hierarchies in stacked RNNs.
- Competitive performance with a simple optimizer on a demanding benchmark.
- Practical training tips (all-layers outputs; incremental training) that could benefit the community.

Arguments for rejection
- Limited novelty relative to existing hierarchical/stacked RNN literature; missing strong contemporary baselines (stacked LSTM).
- Incomplete and partially non-comparable empirical evaluation; few configurations and no variance reporting.
- Key conclusions about time scales confounded by training procedure and supported by heuristic measures.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines with stacked LSTMs (and/or GRUs) and single-layer RNNs with matched parameter counts; report results with and without “all-layers output.”
- Control for training procedure: train DRNN-1O without incremental stages (using gradient clipping) to disentangle architecture from curriculum. Quantify gradient flow to lower layers to support the “AO alleviates vanishing gradients” claim.
- Provide multiple random seeds, a validation set, and standard regularization; report mean ± std BPC and learning curves.
- Strengthen the analysis: probe what each layer encodes using linear classifiers for n-gram features, word boundaries, or parentheses state; analyze recurrent Jacobians/spectral radii to link time scales to dynamics; measure mutual information decay with lag.
- Clarify dataset comparability (95 vs 86 characters) and, if possible, re-run with the original character set for a direct comparison.
- Release code and precise training configs to improve reproducibility; detail the gradient normalization vs clipping choice and its effect.

Recommendation
- Leaning reject (borderline). The paper is carefully executed and informative, but the architectural novelty is modest and the empirical evaluation lacks key baselines and comparability needed for a NeurIPS-standard contribution. With stronger baselines, tighter experimental methodology, and more rigorous analyses, this could become a solid paper.
