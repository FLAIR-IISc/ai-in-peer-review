Summary
The paper studies “deep recurrent neural networks” (DRNNs), i.e., stacked Elman RNN layers with per-layer temporal recurrences, for character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus. Two variants are compared: DRNN-1O (only top layer connects to softmax) with incremental layer-wise training, and DRNN-AO (all layers connect to softmax). Using SGD with gradient-norm normalization and a linear learning-rate decay, models with ~4.9M parameters achieve BPC comparable to prior MRNNs trained with Hessian-free methods. The paper further analyzes emergent time scales via perturbation experiments, context-switch tests, and a qualitative “parentheses closing” analysis, arguing that stacking induces a temporal hierarchy and that DRNNs capture long-range dependencies.

Strengths
- Timely topic; stacked recurrent architectures are important in sequence modeling.
- Empirical performance competitive with HF-trained MRNNs at similar parameter counts using simpler SGD.
- Interesting analyses: layer ablations (zeroing layer outputs), typo perturbation and context-switch effects on hidden states and accuracy, and a targeted long-range dependency probe (parentheses).
- The incremental training of DRNN-1O is pragmatic and shows clear impact on emergent time scales.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality/Methodology
- Comparability of BPC numbers is compromised:
  - Vocabulary differs (95 vs. 86 chars in [19]) and splits are not strictly matched; authors state the effect is minimal but provide no quantitative ablation to support this. This undermines “state-of-the-art for recurrent networks” claims.
  - Only three model instantiations, no multiple random seeds, no variance reporting. The claim that “initializations don’t matter” is anecdotal.
- Training protocol is underspecified:
  - Hidden state handling across sequences (reset vs. carry) not stated; this affects effective context length and comparability.
  - Truncated BPTT length appears to be 200 (250 sequence, first 50 ignored), but not clearly stated.
  - No validation set or hyperparameter selection procedure. Learning rates and layer counts are described as chosen ad hoc (“results not shown”).
- Equation (3) seems incorrect as written: θ(j+1)=θ(j)−η0(−j/T)∇/||∇|| suggests a negative factor; presumably it should be η0(1−j/T) with clipping/normalization. The current form is ambiguous and possibly wrong.
- Gradient handling is unconventional: always normalizing to unit norm rather than clipping to a threshold is known to distort optimization dynamics; no ablation vs. standard gradient clipping is provided, and no reference is given.
- Output weight initialization at zero is risky (symmetry) and generally discouraged; needs justification and/or a reference. At minimum, demonstrate it does not harm.
- Parameter accounting used to equalize model sizes is not shown; for AO there are extra output matrices. Provide explicit formulas to confirm parity.
- Layer-importance via zeroing a layer’s output at test time is only a crude diagnostic and confounded by interactions (acknowledged). Consider more principled measures (e.g., Shapley-style attributions, gradient-based contribution, or retraining ablations).
- Time-scale analyses:
  - Hidden-state Euclidean distances are reported without normalization by layer dimensionality; compare across layers fairly.
  - No statistical summaries (confidence intervals) on the curves; figures are missing in the submission.
  - The emergence of the same long time scale in the top three layers of DRNN-1O is confounded by the incremental training schedule; the paper notes this but then still interprets “hierarchy”. A controlled experiment (same schedule for AO, or AO trained incrementally) is needed.
- Parentheses experiment mixes qualitative examples with a single scalar failure rate; no breakdown by span length, punctuation, or nesting depth (though some qualitative failure modes are noted). Include quantitative curves vs. span length; otherwise conclusions about “several hundred characters” remain anecdotal.
- No regularization is discussed (weight decay, dropout, zoneout, L2 on recurrent weights). Overfitting risk is not assessed (no train vs. test loss).
- Efficiency claims (“roughly 4x cost”) are speculative; wall-clock vs. data passes are not clearly matched to baselines.

Clarity/Presentation
- Numerous references to figures and tables (Fig. 1–4, Table 1–2) are not present. This severely impedes assessment; the analysis sections cannot be evaluated without them.
- Several footnotes and URLs are inline and poorly formatted; superscripts (1–4) do not render as footnotes.
- Typos and style:
  - “wellsuited”, “exists”→“exist”, “Zi>1”, “characters2”, “In To appear…”, inconsistent hyphenation, missing spaces around punctuation in some places.
  - Claims like “unlimited memory depth” are imprecise; standard RNNs have fading memory unless specially parameterized.
- Notation: augmenting vectors with 1 for bias (x̄) is fine, but biases are “not explicitly written” while they are used; clarify shapes and where bias enters.

Originality
- The architecture is essentially a stacked Elman RNN (deep RNN), which has clear precedents (e.g., hierarchical/stacked RNNs, stacked LSTMs). The novelty rests mainly in the training recipe (incremental top-only outputs) and the analysis suite. Related work is cited but the contribution relative to stacked RNNs/LSTMs is under-differentiated; comparisons to stacked LSTMs (beyond [8]) are missing.

Significance
- Competitive results with simple SGD are useful, but the lack of strict comparability and missing figures reduce confidence.
- The analyses are potentially insightful but need stronger controls and complete presentation to be impactful.

Questions for authors
- Please correct Eq. (3) and clarify whether you normalize or clip gradients; provide ablations.
- How are hidden states initialized and handled across sequences/batches? Do you reset at sequence boundaries?
- Provide a validation set protocol and sensitivity of results to η0, layer count, truncation length.
- Quantify the impact of using 95 vs. 86 characters on BPC on the same split.
- Justify zero initialization of output weights; show that small random init yields similar results.
- Include parameter-count formulas to verify parity across models.
- Provide the missing figures/tables and, if possible, code or sufficient details to reproduce.

Pros
- Simple, scalable training; competitive BPC with SGD.
- Thoughtful attempts at analyzing temporal hierarchy and long-range behavior.
- Relevant to the community; bridges performance and interpretability.

Cons
- Major presentation gaps (missing figures/tables); multiple methodological ambiguities.
- Weak experimental rigor (no variance, limited baselines, non-comparable datasets).
- Limited novelty in architecture; some claims confounded by training schedule.
- Several technical inaccuracies/typos (notably Eq. (3)) and formatting issues.

Recommendation: Reject
While the topic is appropriate and the direction promising, the submission in its current form has too many methodological and presentation deficiencies to meet NeurIPS standards. A revised version with corrected equations, complete figures/tables, stricter experimental controls, and clearer positioning versus prior stacked RNN/LSTM work could be much stronger.
