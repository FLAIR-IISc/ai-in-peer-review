

# Review of "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks"

This paper proposes a Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN) architecture that combines hierarchical processing of deep neural networks with the temporal memory capabilities of recurrent neural networks. The authors argue that standard RNNs lack explicit temporal hierarchies, and their DRNN architecture automatically creates different time scales at different layers. The paper claims state-of-the-art performance on character-level language modeling using only SGD, previously requiring Hessian-free optimization.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work
The paper builds on existing work on hierarchical RNNs [3,5,6] and stacked LSTMs [8], but introduces a novel DRNN architecture with temporal feedback loops in each layer. The authors claim to demonstrate a temporal hierarchy in the DRNN layers, which they verify through various experiments including parenthesis closing tasks and time-scale analysis.

## Strengths
The paper makes a clear contribution to the understanding of temporal hierarchies in neural networks. The experimental results showing different time scales across layers in DRNNs are interesting and provide evidence for the authors' claims. The comparison to standard RNNs on the Wikipedia text corpus is relevant, and the parenthesis-closing task provides a concrete example of long-term dependency learning.

## Weaknesses

**Technical Inconsistencies:**
1. The learning rate schedule in equation (3) is mathematically incorrect: η0(-j/T) would produce negative learning rates as j increases, which is nonsensical. The authors likely meant η0(1 - j/T) or similar. This fundamental error undermines the training methodology description.

2. The paper claims to use "stochastic gradient descent" but describes a modified version with gradient normalization (||∇θ(j)|| in the denominator) and a linearly decreasing learning rate. This should be clearly defined as a variant of SGD, not standard SGD.

3. The training setup states "the DRNN-AO sees the full training set about 7 times in total, and the DRNN-1O about 10 times" without explaining how this difference affects the comparison. This is a significant methodological flaw in the experimental design.

**Clarity and Detail Issues:**
1. The paper uses "DRNN" as an acronym without defining it at first use. The term "deep recurrent neural network" should be spelled out before using the acronym.

2. The paper references Figure 1 multiple times but does not include the figure, making it impossible to understand the proposed architecture.

3. The paper states "we used 95 of the most common characters" but provides no justification for this specific number, and footnote 2's explanation about the 95 vs. 86 characters from [19] is insufficiently detailed.

4. The paper claims to use "only SGD" to achieve state-of-the-art results, but doesn't provide a fair comparison to [19] in terms of training time or computational resources. The claim that "this was previously only obtained using the far more advanced Hessian-free training algorithm" is misleading without context on the computational cost.

**Methodological Flaws:**
1. The paper states "We need time in the order of 10 days to train a single network, largely due to the difficulty of exploiting massively parallel computing for SGD" - this is factually incorrect as SGD is highly parallelizable, and the difficulty likely stems from other factors.

2. The paper claims "the DRNN-AO has very short time-scales in the three bottom layers and longer memory only appears for the two top ones" but this contradicts their layer-wise training approach for DRNN-1O, where lower layers would have shorter time scales.

3. The paper lacks error bars or confidence intervals for the results, which is essential for scientific rigor in machine learning.

4. The paper claims "we only tested three network instantiations" without justification for why this limited number of experiments is sufficient to draw conclusions about the DRNN architecture.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting architectural concept and provides some evidence for temporal hierarchies in DRNNs, the numerous technical inconsistencies, methodological flaws, and lack of clarity in the experimental setup significantly undermine the paper's claims. The mathematical error in the learning rate schedule alone is sufficient to question the validity of the reported results. The comparison to prior work is not properly contextualized, and the paper fails to provide enough detail to reproduce the experiments.

The paper's main contribution is interesting but not sufficiently supported by the presented evidence. The claims about "state-of-the-art performance" using only SGD are misleading without proper comparison to [19] in terms of computational resources and training time. The paper would benefit from addressing these fundamental issues before it can be considered for publication at NeurIPS.

## Recommendation

**Reject**

The paper contains multiple technical errors and methodological inconsistencies that prevent it from being considered a solid scientific contribution. The mathematical error in equation (3) is particularly concerning as it directly affects the training procedure described. While the concept of DRNNs is interesting, the paper's execution fails to adequately support its claims with rigorous methodology and clear presentation. The minor flaws identified here are substantial enough to warrant rejection, as they undermine the core claims of the paper.
