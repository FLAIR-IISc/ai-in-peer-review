Summary
This paper studies stacked recurrent neural networks (“deep RNNs,” DRNNs) for character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus. Each layer is a tanh RNN; higher layers receive the previous layer’s hidden state as input. Two output variants are considered: DRNN-1O (only the top layer connects to the softmax) and DRNN-AO (all layers connect to the same softmax via separate linear projections). Training uses SGD with backpropagation through time (truncated to 250 steps, first 50 ignored for BPTT), gradient normalization to unit norm, and a linear learning-rate decay to zero. For DRNN-1O, layers are added incrementally so each layer is directly connected to the output during a stage of training.

Empirically, with roughly 4.9M parameters, both DRNNs outperform a single-layer RNN baseline and approach the performance of multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free optimization on the same corpus. The authors analyze time scales via perturbation experiments (typo injection and context swapping), layer ablations in DRNN-AO, and a “parentheses closing” probe, concluding that stacking induces a temporal hierarchy and supports long-range dependencies, sometimes beyond the truncated BPTT horizon.

Relation to prior work
The work is related to earlier hierarchical RNNs [El Hihi & Bengio; Fernández et al.], multi-timescale RNNs, and stacked LSTMs (e.g., Graves et al.). The performance comparison to MRNNs trained with HF optimization (Sutskever et al.) is appropriate. The architectural idea is essentially stacked (deep) RNNs; the AO variant resembles deep supervision/skip connections to the output, and the incremental training for DRNN-1O is a supervised pretraining-like scheme. The paper’s contribution is primarily empirical and analytical at scale on a realistic corpus with simple tanh RNNs trained by SGD.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated problem: character-level modeling with both short- and long-term dependencies.
- Solid engineering and scale: large dataset, careful parameter budgeting across models, and consistent training setup.
- Empirical result: stacked vanilla RNNs trained with straightforward SGD achieve competitive BPC with HF-trained MRNNs of similar size.
- Insightful analysis: perturbation-based timescale probes, layer-output ablations, and a targeted “parentheses closing” test provide concrete evidence for temporal hierarchies and long-range memory.
- The incremental training regime for DRNN-1O is practical and seems effective at overcoming optimization difficulties in deep recurrent stacks.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited baselines: no comparison to LSTM (or gated RNNs) and, crucially, no stacked LSTM baseline as in [Graves et al.]. Given the focus on long-term dependencies, this omission makes it hard to assess the real value of stacking vanilla RNNs relative to standard gated architectures.
- Small ablation surface and few runs: only three models with single seeds (authors state little variance but do not provide evidence). Uncertainty remains about robustness across initializations/hyperparameters.
- Optimization method: always-normalizing the gradient to unit norm is unusual; it discards magnitude information rather than clipping. More justification/ablation (vs classic clipping, RMSProp/Adagrad) would strengthen the claims that “plain SGD” suffices.
- Data protocol and model selection: no mention of a validation split; hyperparameter choices and training schedules appear to be decided without validation. Several analyses are performed directly on the test set. This risks mild test leakage for model/hyperparameter selection and complicates fair comparison.
- Interpretation limits: AO ablation (zeroing one layer’s contribution to the softmax) measures direct contribution but not preprocessing effects; the paper acknowledges this but still leans on these results. The observed “automatic hierarchy” is not universal: in DRNN-1O, top three layers share similar time scales, driven by the training curriculum.
- Reporting and clarity: exact BPC numbers for the main models are not clearly enumerated in the text excerpt (only a number for a larger model is given). Reproducibility would benefit from more explicit hyperparameters (e.g., how T relates to passes over data per model and per stage in DRNN-1O).
- Missing regularization and calibration: no discussion of overfitting, weight decay, or regularization; output weights initialized to zero is unconventional and might slow convergence (though it works here).

Quality
Technically sound overall, with careful experiments and thoughtful analyses. Claims about temporal hierarchies are supported empirically but remain qualitative; no theoretical analysis of why stacking induces specific time scales. The comparison to HF-trained MRNNs is reasonable given parameter budget, but stronger baselines (LSTM/stacked LSTM) are needed.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized; figures/tables (not visible here) seem to play an important role in supporting the claims. Some reference formatting is incomplete. Training details are mostly present, but a validation protocol and exact BPC numbers would improve clarity and reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. Stacking RNNs is not new; connecting all layers to the same output for improved gradient flow is a straightforward design. The main novelty is the scale, the simple training (SGD with gradient normalization), and the careful empirical analyses of time scales and long-range behavior.

Significance
Moderate. Demonstrating competitive performance with simple tanh DRNNs and SGD on a large corpus is useful, and the analyses offer insights likely of interest to practitioners. However, without gated baselines, it is difficult to judge the practical impact relative to the then-standard LSTM approaches.

Questions for the authors
- Please report exact test BPCs for all three 4.9M-parameter models, and specify the validation protocol used for hyperparameter selection. Was any tuning done on the test set?
- How sensitive are results to the gradient normalization scheme? Could you compare to gradient clipping at a threshold and to RMSProp/Adagrad?
- How many seeds were tried per configuration? Please provide mean±std if available.
- Can you provide a stacked LSTM baseline of comparable parameter count, or at least cite numbers from [8] on the same dataset/metric?
- What is the effect of the BPTT truncation length on the long-range behaviors (e.g., parentheses)? Any evidence the model truly generalizes beyond the truncation or relies on slow-changing hidden states?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a stacked LSTM baseline; if infeasible, provide a careful discussion with available results and differences in data/protocol.
- Include a validation split and tune hyperparameters without peeking at the test set; report final test results once.
- Compare gradient normalization vs clipping and adaptive optimizers; report training stability and speed.
- Provide multiple runs to quantify variance; add parameter-count vs performance curves for depth vs width trade-offs.
- Extend analysis to per-layer gradient norms and information flow to substantiate the optimization benefits of AO connections.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a solid empirical study and insightful analyses of deep RNNs that would interest the community, but the lack of strong gated baselines and limited ablations weaken the case. With clarified reporting and, ideally, a stacked LSTM comparison, it would be a clear accept.

Confidence
High.
