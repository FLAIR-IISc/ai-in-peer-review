Summary
This paper studies stacked recurrent neural networks (“deep RNNs”, DRNNs) for character-level language modeling. Each layer is a simple tanh RNN receiving the lower layer’s hidden state; two output designs are considered: top-only (DRNN-1O) and all-layers-to-output (DRNN-AO). Models are trained with SGD using gradient normalization and a linearly decaying learning rate; DRNN-1O is trained incrementally by adding layers. On a large Wikipedia corpus, the authors report bits-per-character comparable to the multiplicative RNN (MRNN) trained with Hessian-free optimization, at similar parameter counts. They further analyze learned time scales via perturbation experiments, layer ablations, and a qualitative/quantitative study of long-range dependencies (parentheses closure), arguing the stacked architecture induces a temporal hierarchy and better long context use than a plain RNN.

Relation to prior work
Stacked/hierarchical RNNs have a long history (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio 1996; Fernández/Graves/Schmidhuber; more recently, stacked LSTMs). The multiplicative RNN and LSTM/GRU baselines are standard for character modeling. The idea of using outputs from multiple layers is a modest architectural variant; incremental supervised layer-wise training has analogs in deep nets. Prior work has focused more on performance (e.g., stacked bi-LSTM for TIMIT); here the main additional contribution is empirical analysis of time scales and long-range behaviors in DRNNs.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant, difficult task (character-level modeling on a large corpus) with nontrivial scale (~5M parameters).
- Parameter-matched comparisons between a plain RNN and two DRNN variants help isolate the effect of depth (to some extent).
- Interesting analytical experiments: perturbation-based time-scale measures, layer-output ablations, and a focused evaluation of parentheses closure in both discriminative and generative modes.
- Practical training insights (gradient normalization, linear LR decay, incremental training) that may be of interest.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/methodological soundness
- Baselines are insufficient. There is no comparison to LSTM or GRU (single-layer or stacked), which are the established baselines for long-term dependencies on character-level text. The claim of “state-of-the-art for recurrent networks” is not compelling without such comparisons.
- The comparison to MRNN/Hessian-free is indirect and not strictly comparable (different character set sizes; unclear total data passes; different training hardware). The paper concedes the difficulty but still anchors claims on it.
- Only three model instantiations were trained (due to compute), with no variance across random seeds. The decision for 5 layers is based on “results not shown.” There is no systematic exploration of depth vs. width trade-offs beyond matching parameters.
- Training procedure differences confound architectural conclusions: DRNN-1O uses incremental layer addition and different training durations per stage, which likely explains its long time scales in upper layers. Thus, some “temporal hierarchy” observations may be artifacts of the schedule rather than intrinsic to the architecture.
- Gradient handling is unusual: always normalizing the gradient to unit norm combined with a linearly decaying LR to zero. This is nonstandard compared to clipping; implications for convergence and generalization are not discussed, and ablations are missing.
- Output weights initialized to zero can block gradient flow to hidden layers initially (since dL/dh includes U^T(y−t)); training then relies on early updates to U before hidden layers receive any signal. This design choice should be justified and tested.
- Data protocol is under-specified: there is no description of a validation split, hyperparameter selection, or early stopping. It appears the test set is used repeatedly for reporting during model development, which risks bias.

Clarity
- Generally readable, but some critical details are missing (validation setup, exact BPC numbers for all methods, training runtime per model, batch definition). Equation (3) appears to have a sign typo and an unclear LR schedule (likely η = η0(1 − j/T)).
- Several claims rely on qualitative samples; while useful, stronger quantitative evidence would be preferable.

Originality
- Stacked RNNs and multiscale hierarchical RNNs are well-established; using output connections from all layers is a mild variation. The incremental supervised training stage is also not novel. The most original part is the empirical analysis of time scales and the specific long-range phenomena (parentheses), though similar analyses have appeared in different forms.

Significance
- The reported performance, if fairly benchmarked, would be interesting due to using plain SGD. However, without LSTM/GRU baselines and stronger controls, it is hard to conclude that DRNNs advance the SOTA. The analytical findings are suggestive but not definitive, given confounds.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include strong, parameter-matched baselines: at least single-layer and stacked LSTM (and/or GRU). Ideally also compare against MRNN under the same dataset and character set.
- Use a proper validation split for hyperparameter tuning; report variance across seeds.
- Provide ablations: number of layers, width per layer, with/without multi-layer outputs, with/without incremental training; compare gradient normalization versus standard clipping; non-zero output weight initialization.
- Ensure comparability: same character set as prior work; report exact BPCs and training budgets (updates, epochs, wall-clock).
- Clarify and correct training equation and schedule; provide code or sufficient details to reproduce.
- Strengthen quantitative analysis of long-range dependencies beyond parentheses (e.g., bracket/quote matching, indentation, code-like structures) with controlled synthetic and real tests.

Pros
- Tackles an important problem at scale.
- Clear empirical evidence that stacking helps over a plain RNN at fixed parameter count.
- Thoughtful analysis of temporal behavior and long-range effects.

Cons
- Limited and outdated baselines; no LSTM/GRU comparisons.
- Confounded training procedures undermine causal claims about architecture.
- Nonstandard training choices not justified by ablations.
- Comparisons to prior SOTA not strictly comparable.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The analysis is interesting and the empirical observations are potentially useful, but the experimental evidence is not yet rigorous enough against established baselines to substantiate the paper’s claims. With stronger baselines, cleaner protocols, and ablations, this work could become a solid contribution. Confidence: moderate.
