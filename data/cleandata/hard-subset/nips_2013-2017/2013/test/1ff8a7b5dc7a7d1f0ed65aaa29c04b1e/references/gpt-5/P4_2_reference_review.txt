Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) formed by stacking simple recurrent layers, each with its own recurrent weights, and explores two output variants: (i) DRNN-1O, where only the top layer connects to the softmax, and (ii) DRNN-AO, where all layers connect to the output (“all outputs”). The authors argue that stacking induces a temporal hierarchy: lower layers capture shorter temporal dependencies, higher layers longer ones. They evaluate on character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus (1.4B chars, 10M test), comparing a single-layer RNN (≈2.1k units) to 5-layer DRNNs with ≈4.9M parameters total. Training uses SGD with gradient normalization (update direction normalized by its norm) and a linearly decaying learning rate. DRNN-1O is trained incrementally by adding layers, each time retraining the full stack with the new top layer as the only output. They report bits-per-character (BPC) competitive with the multiplicative RNN (MRNN) trained with Hessian-free optimization [18], and provide analyses: (a) layer ablations for DRNN-AO, (b) perturbation-based time-scale measurements via hidden state distances after a single-character typo, (c) context-length sensitivity via replacing initial context, and (d) qualitative/quantitative tests for closing parentheses during generation.

The work relates to hierarchical RNNs [5,6], stacked bidirectional LSTMs for speech [8], and MRNNs with Hessian-free training [18]. Its architectural idea—stacking recurrent layers with per-layer recurrence—is known; the main additions are a systematic comparison of top-only vs all-output training, an incremental supervised pretraining for DRNN-1O, and empirical analyses of emergent time scales on a large real-world corpus.

Strengths
- Empirical contribution: On a widely used character-level benchmark, DRNNs trained with plain SGD and gradient normalization achieve BPC comparable to MRNNs trained with second-order methods and similar parameter counts, supporting the viability of first-order training at scale.
- Architectural clarity: The DRNN formulation and the two output variants are precisely defined. Training details (BPTT length, batch construction, initialization, learning rate schedule) are mostly specified.
- Analysis of temporal hierarchy: The perturbation-based measurements (hidden-state divergence after a typo; performance drop after context scrambling) are carefully averaged over many instances and demonstrate that different layers exhibit distinct time scales—particularly in DRNN-AO—and that DRNNs exploit longer contexts than a single-layer RNN.
- Interpretability via ablation: Layer-wise output ablations in DRNN-AO show the top layer contributes most to predictive performance while intermediate layers act more as preprocessors, consistent with the intended hierarchy.
- Task-focused probe: The parentheses-closing experiment provides a concrete long-term dependency test, with DRNNs outperforming a vanilla RNN.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty relative to prior work: Stacked RNNs and temporal hierarchies have been explored (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio; Graves et al. for stacked LSTMs). The architectural contribution is incremental. The “all outputs” idea is a form of deep supervision; the paper does not relate to prior work on auxiliary outputs.
- Limited baselines: No comparisons to gated architectures (LSTM/GRU) that are widely used for long-term dependencies and stacked hierarchies, despite [8] being a direct precedent. This omission limits the significance of the architectural conclusions and the strength of the state-of-the-art claim.
- Experimental breadth and rigor: Only three model instantiations are reported, with a single random seed per model and no variance measures. The character set differs from [18] (96 vs 86), with the effect argued negligible but not empirically controlled. The choice of five layers is justified by “results not shown,” and the compute budget prevents ablations over layer count or width per layer.
- Training protocol questions: The DRNN-1O incremental scheme likely induces the observed long time scales in intermediate layers (as the authors note), confounding the claim that hierarchical time scales arise “automatically” from stacking. The update rule in Eq. (3) appears to contain a sign/parenthesis typo (η0(1 − j/T) would be expected). There is no mention of a validation set or early stopping; it is unclear how hyperparameters were selected without test leakage.
- Reproducibility: While many hyperparameters are given and Theano is cited, code availability is not stated. Some referenced results (e.g., Table 1/2 values) are not fully detailed in the text; exact BPC numbers for each model are not provided here.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The methodology is generally sound. Gradient normalization (direction-only updates) is a reasonable stabilization heuristic. The analyses average over many samples and align with the qualitative claims. However, the lack of stronger baselines (e.g., stacked LSTM) and limited ablations weaken causal conclusions about the advantages of DRNNs per se.
- Completeness: The work presents a complete training/evaluation cycle on a large dataset, plus multiple analyses. Yet, key comparisons and statistical robustness are missing.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear and well organized. Suggestions: correct Eq. (3) notation; explicitly list BPC numbers; detail validation protocol; clarify whether output-weight zero initialization had any impact; discuss computational throughput and total tokens seen vs MRNN for fair comparison.

Originality
- Moderate. Stacking simple RNNs and analyzing time scales is not new, but the combination of large-scale character modeling with systematic perturbation-based analyses and the “all outputs” vs “top only” comparison adds incremental value.

Significance
- Moderate. Demonstrating competitive performance with SGD on this corpus is useful. The time-scale analyses may inform practitioners. Without comparisons to stacked LSTMs/GRUs, the broader impact on sequence modeling practice is limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include strong baselines: stacked LSTM/GRU with comparable parameter counts and training budgets.
- Provide multiple seeds and report mean±std BPC; add a validation set and describe model selection.
- Control for character set differences when comparing to [18], or re-evaluate under identical preprocessing.
- Expand ablations: number of layers, per-layer widths, presence/absence of all-output connections, effect of gradient normalization, BPTT length.
- Deepen the analysis: measure gradient norms per layer over time; quantify mutual information between layers’ states and past inputs; analyze error types (e.g., bracket closure, quotes, capitalization).
- Clarify computational cost: total tokens processed, wall-clock time, hardware, to contextualize efficiency claims.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid, large-scale empirical study showing stacked simple RNNs trained with SGD can match prior HF-trained MRNNs on character-level modeling.
- Thoughtful analyses of emergent time scales and long-term dependencies with clear, reproducible probes.
- Practical training insights (incremental supervised pretraining; all-outputs deep supervision) that may be useful.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental novelty; architecture and claims overlap with prior stacked RNN/LSTM work.
- Missing comparisons to modern gated baselines; limited experimental breadth and no statistical robustness.
- Some methodological ambiguities (validation, equation typo, effect of differing character sets) reduce confidence in the strength of claims.

Overall, the paper offers a useful empirical and analytical perspective on stacked RNNs, but its impact would be strengthened by broader baselines, more rigorous experimental methodology, and clearer reporting.
