Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies stacked recurrent neural networks (“deep RNNs,” DRNNs) for character-level language modeling. Each layer is a tanh RNN; higher layers receive the previous layer’s hidden state as input. Two output variants are considered: DRNN-1O (only top layer to softmax) and DRNN-AO (all layers connect to softmax, i.e., deep supervision). Training is done with SGD and backpropagation through time (BPTT), using gradient normalization (rescaling to unit norm) and a linearly decaying learning rate. For DRNN-1O, the authors use incremental supervised layer-wise training (adding layers and reinitializing the output head each stage). Experiments on a Wikipedia character dataset (~1.4B chars) compare a single-layer RNN, 5-layer DRNN-1O, and 5-layer DRNN-AO under a fixed parameter budget (~4.9M). DRNNs match prior state-of-the-art BPC for similarly sized recurrent models previously trained with Hessian-free optimization on a multiplicative RNN (MRNN). The paper further analyzes: (i) leave-one-layer-out contributions to the output; (ii) layer-wise time scales via a “typo perturbation” and context-switch tests; and (iii) the ability to model long-range dependencies like closing parentheses.

Stacked/hierarchical RNNs have been explored before (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio, NIPS; Fernández et al.; Graves et al., ICASSP’13 on stacked bidirectional LSTMs for TIMIT). This paper’s novel contribution is a focused analysis of time scales and layer roles in stacked vanilla RNNs for character modeling, and a demonstration that SGD with simple tricks can be competitive with HF on a large corpus.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architecture and training procedure; practical observations (deep supervision and incremental layer-wise training help).
- Empirical evidence that deeper RNNs exhibit emergent temporal hierarchies; well-designed perturbation experiments to probe time scales and context usage.
- Qualitative and quantitative analysis (layer ablations, generated samples, parentheses tracking) that goes beyond headline numbers and provides insight.
- Demonstrates that strong performance is attainable with plain SGD, challenging the perceived need for second-order methods.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: stacked RNNs and multi-output connections are not new; the architectural delta is modest. The main contribution is analysis rather than a new model or algorithm.
- Baselines are insufficient: no LSTM/GRU comparisons, despite their centrality to modeling long-term dependencies and their widespread use in char-level LM. No MRNN reproduction under the same training budget. The single-layer RNN baseline is too weak for a NeurIPS-level claim.
- Experimental scope is narrow: only three model instantiations due to compute constraints; no variability across random seeds; limited hyperparameter exploration; five layers chosen based on unreported prior experiments.
- Comparability concerns: different character vocabularies vs. cited work (86 vs. 96); unclear whether train/valid/test splits and preprocessing match prior benchmarks (text8/enwik8); lack of validation set and early stopping. This makes SOTA claims hard to verify.
- Methodological choices need justification: rescaling gradients to unit norm (vs. clipping to a threshold) discards magnitude information; zero initialization of output weights; linear LR decay to zero without validation; no regularization.
- Some analyses could be confounded: leave-one-layer-out importance without retraining the output head may reflect interactions rather than true contribution. Euclidean distances in hidden space can be coordinate-dependent.

Quality
- The work is technically competent, and the analyses are thoughtfully designed. However, claims about matching state of the art are undercut by missing/unequal baselines and dataset differences. The absence of LSTM/GRU comparisons is a major gap for a paper about long-term dependencies. Reproducibility is hindered by missing details (e.g., exact BPC numbers table; validation splits; gradient norm statistics; code).

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized; the motivation and experimental design are clear. Some references are incomplete. Provide precise dataset description (tokenization, casing, Unicode handling), all hyperparameters, and figures/tables with exact numbers. Clarify gradient normalization (global vs. per-parameter, per-batch vs. per-sequence), BPTT truncation, and LR schedule per stage.

Originality
- Incremental. Stacked RNNs and deep supervision are established ideas; the main novelty lies in the careful layer-time-scale analyses and training insights (e.g., supervised layer-wise for DRNN-1O).

Significance
- Moderate. Insights into temporal hierarchies in stacked RNNs are interesting and likely useful. However, without stronger baselines and broader evaluation, the impact on the state of the art is limited. The community would benefit more if the analyses were presented alongside competitive LSTM/GRU results and more rigorous comparisons.

Specific suggestions for improvement
- Add strong baselines under the same parameter/compute budget: 1–2 layer LSTM/GRU; stacked LSTM; MRNN if feasible. Report multiple runs with mean±std.
- Evaluate on standard char-level benchmarks (text8/enwik8) with identical preprocessing to prior work; or clearly detail differences and report controlled comparisons.
- Provide ablations: depth vs. width under a fixed parameter budget; effect of deep supervision; effect of incremental training; BPTT length sensitivity; gradient clipping threshold vs. unit normalization; non-zero output init.
- For layer contribution, retrain the output head after ablating a layer to decouple synergy effects. Consider probing classifiers on hidden states to quantify which n-gram/linguistic cues each layer captures.
- Strengthen the long-range tests: nested parentheses, quotes/brackets of different types, indentation, section headers; quantify BPC changes around such structures.
- Improve bibliographic completeness; release code and trained models for reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Interesting and well-executed analysis of temporal hierarchies in deep RNNs.
- Practical training insights (deep supervision, incremental supervised layer-wise training) with large-scale results using SGD.
- Empirical evidence of long-range dependency handling in a realistic corpus.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty of the model; insufficient and unequal baselines (no LSTM/GRU).
- Narrow experimental scope; comparability issues with prior SOTA; lack of statistical rigor and ablation breadth.
- Some methodological choices insufficiently justified; reproducibility concerns.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper presents useful analyses and practical insights, but the experimental evidence relative to strong baselines is not yet sufficient for NeurIPS. With stronger comparative results, broader ablations, and clearer reproducibility, this would be a compelling submission.
