Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), i.e., stacked layers of vanilla tanh RNNs where each layer receives the previous layer’s hidden state as input. Two output strategies are tested: a single output from the top layer (DRNN-1O) and a single shared softmax receiving summed affine contributions from all layers (DRNN-AO). The work targets character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus and reports bits-per-character (BPC) comparable to prior recurrent models of similar parameter count trained with Hessian-free optimization. Beyond accuracy, the paper analyzes emergent time scales via perturbation experiments and illustrates long-range effects such as closing parentheses.

This sits among earlier hierarchical/stacked RNNs (El Hihi & Bengio 1996; Graves et al. deep LSTM), MRNNs (Sutskever et al. 2011), and stacked bidirectional LSTMs for speech (Graves et al.). The “all-layers-to-output” variant resembles deep supervision/skip connections into the classifier, albeit with a single loss.

Strengths
- Clear empirical question: do stacked vanilla RNNs yield temporal hierarchies and competitive language modeling performance?
- Novel analyses for the era: perturb-and-measure hidden state distances and context-switch impact; qualitative and quantitative examination of parenthesis closing.
- Simplicity of training: SGD with gradient normalization, no second-order methods, yet competitive BPC for comparable parameter budgets.
- Useful engineering observation: incremental layer-wise training helps DRNN-1O.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Missing or incomplete key results. Table 1/2 are referenced but BPC numbers for the main 4.9M-parameter models are not reported in text; only a 9.6M model (1.472 BPC) is mentioned. Without exact numbers and variance across runs, claims of state-of-the-art are hard to verify.
- Unfair comparisons and mismatched budgets. DRNN-1O is trained for more total updates (about 7.5e5) and more dataset passes (~10) than DRNN-AO (~7) and the plain RNN (5e5). Claims that DRNN-1O “performs best” are confounded by training budget and schedule.
- No validation protocol. Hyperparameters (learning rates, depth, hidden size) were set without a stated validation set; final test performance could be overfit or suboptimal.
- Equation (3) appears incorrect. The decay factor is shown as (− j/T) rather than (1 − j/T). As written, the step size becomes negative and grows in magnitude—likely a typographical but serious error in a core method equation.
- Gradient normalization always to unit norm discards magnitude information, can destabilize convergence, and differs from standard gradient clipping. No ablation vs simple clipping or Adam/momentum. No constraints on recurrent spectral radius or stability analysis beyond heuristic.
- Truncated BPTT length and long-term claims. Backprop is truncated to 200 steps (first 50 of 250 ignored); yet the paper infers memory “several hundred characters” and beyond. Without training signal beyond 200, such claims require stronger evidence (e.g., controlled synthetic tasks, systematic length generalization).
- Experimental design details missing/ambiguous:
  - Are hidden states reset between randomly sampled sequences? If so, that undermines training for very long contexts; if not, how are boundaries handled?
  - Data preprocessing, exact train/valid/test split (a validation set is not described), and tokenization specifics (95 vs 86-char alphabets) impact comparability.
  - Output weights initialized to zero for all layers; this may slow learning and biases early training; rationale and ablation are missing.
  - Batch size, optimizer details (no momentum/weight decay), and regularization (dropout/L2) are absent.
- Analysis methodology concerns:
  - Hidden-state distance comparisons use Euclidean norms without per-layer normalization; different layer scales make cross-layer time-scale comparisons questionable.
  - “Layer contribution” pre-softmax logits are not directly comparable across layers and can exhibit cancellations; the paper notes limited interpretability but then draws qualitative conclusions.
- Baselines lacking. No comparison to LSTM/GRU or to stacked LSTM results on the same corpus; given known advantages of gating for long-term dependencies, this limits significance. No reproduction or reimplementation of MRNN under the same data setup.
- Reproducibility: Only three runs due to compute; no code, no seeds, no full hyperparameter sweep. Claims that “initialization matters little” are anecdotal.
- Writing/formatting issues:
  - Numerous footnotes appear inline; figures/tables referenced but not included; several typos and grammar (e.g., “longterm”), and inconsistent hyphenations.
  - References largely incomplete (years/pages missing; “To appear”; inconsistent formatting).
  - Notation: bias handling via augmented vectors is stated but then output weights initialized to zero implies zero bias; clarify.

Questions for the authors
- Provide exact BPC numbers (with at least two seeds) for all three 4.9M models on the stated test set, plus the validation protocol.
- Clarify whether hidden states are reset between training sequences; if not, how are boundaries managed?
- Correct Eq. (3) and detail the learning-rate schedule used in code.
- Report matched-budget comparisons (same updates/passes and wall-clock) between RNN, DRNN-AO, and DRNN-1O.
- Normalize hidden states per layer and redo the time-scale analysis; add ablations with different BPTT lengths.
- Compare to a stacked LSTM baseline of similar parameter count on the same data.

Pros (for acceptance)
- Simple architecture; insightful analyses of temporal hierarchy in stacked RNNs.
- Competitive performance with basic SGD on a large, realistic dataset.
- Interesting qualitative demonstrations (parentheses behavior).

Cons (for acceptance)
- Missing core experimental numbers and mismatched training budgets undermine claims.
- No strong baselines (LSTM/GRU) or ablations; limited runs and no validation set.
- Methodological and analytical weaknesses (Eq. 3 error, hidden-state distance comparability, truncated BPTT vs long-term claims).
- Reproducibility and formatting/reference quality below NeurIPS standards.

Recommendation
Reject. While the idea and analyses are interesting, the accumulation of methodological issues, missing or ambiguous results, and presentation/formatting problems prevent confident assessment of the contribution. With corrected methodology, matched comparisons, stronger baselines, and clearer reporting, this could become a solid submission.

Confidence
High.
