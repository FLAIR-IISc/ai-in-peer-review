This paper investigates deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), essentially stacking RNNs to create a hierarchical temporal model. While the motivation – capturing multi-scale temporal dependencies – is reasonable, and the empirical results are promising, I find the paper lacking in sufficient novelty and rigor to warrant acceptance to NIPS at this time. The core idea of stacking RNNs isn’t fundamentally new (as acknowledged by the authors referencing [3, 5, 6, 8]), and the analysis provided doesn't convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over established methods or provide deep insight into *why* these DRNNs perform well.

The primary strength lies in achieving state-of-the-art performance on a character-level language modeling task using only SGD, a feat previously accomplished with more sophisticated optimization techniques like Hessian-free optimization [19]. However, this result, while encouraging, is presented without a truly comprehensive comparison. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their limited experimentation (only three network instantiations). A more thorough ablation study, varying network depth, layer size, and output connection schemes, would be essential to solidify these claims.

My major concerns center around the lack of a compelling theoretical justification for the observed benefits. The authors suggest that stacking RNNs automatically creates different time scales, but this is largely asserted rather than demonstrated. The analysis of time scales (Figure 3) is interesting, but its interpretation feels somewhat post-hoc. The distinction between the DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO time scale behavior is attributed to the training method, but a deeper investigation is needed to understand why this difference arises. Moreover, the connection between these observed time scales and the hierarchical nature of language remains tenuous.

The discussion regarding the contribution of individual layers in the DRNN-AO (Figure 2) is also somewhat superficial. While it identifies that the top layer is most important for direct output, the interpretation of intermediate layers as primarily “preprocessing” is speculative. A more rigorous analysis, perhaps examining the learned representations within each layer, would be necessary to support this claim.

Clarity is generally good, and the paper is reasonably well-organized. However, the notation can be slightly dense, and a more detailed explanation of the training procedure would be helpful. The inclusion of more implementation details – beyond just the GPU model – would also enhance reproducibility.

Originality is a significant weakness. Stacking RNNs has been explored previously. While this work adds a specific instantiation (DNN-like structure with temporal feedback) and focuses on character-level language modeling, the conceptual leap isn’t substantial enough to warrant strong consideration. The paper’s novelty hinges on the empirical results, but these are insufficiently supported by rigorous analysis.

Significance is moderate. Character-level language modeling is a relevant task, and improved performance is always welcome. However, the practical impact of this work is unclear. It's not immediately obvious how the insights gained from DRNNs on this task would generalize to other domains. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate that DRNNs offer a fundamentally new approach to sequence modeling or address a critical limitation of existing techniques.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging task with a relatively simple training algorithm.
*   Provides some empirical evidence for the emergence of hierarchical time scales in DRNNs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty; stacking RNNs is not a fundamentally new idea.
*   Insufficient theoretical justification for the observed benefits.
*   Lack of a comprehensive ablation study and comparison to established baselines.
*   Superficial analysis of layer contributions and time scale behavior.
*   Unclear practical significance and limited generalizability.



**Overall, I recommend rejection.** The paper presents a reasonable idea and promising results, but lacks the rigor, novelty, and depth of analysis expected of a NIPS publication. A significantly expanded experimental evaluation, coupled with a more robust theoretical justification, would be needed to address my concerns.
