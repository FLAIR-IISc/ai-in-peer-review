- Paper proposes Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs): multi-layer RNNs where each layer is recurrent and receives the previous layer’s hidden state, claiming an automatic temporal hierarchy.  
- Empirical claim: DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art recurrent-network performance on character-level language modeling using only SGD, avoiding Hessian-free optimization.  
- Provides evidence for temporal hierarchy: typo-perturbation experiment shows lower layers operate at shorter time scales and higher layers retain longer memory.  
- Parentheses-closing test demonstrates top-layer ability to model long-term dependencies over hundreds of characters.  
- Methodological insight: DRNN-AO (outputs from all layers) helps mitigate vanishing gradients in deep RNNs.  
- Diagnostic tool: analysis of layer importance via removing output contributions offers useful interpretability methods.  
- Novelty is questionable: DRNN closely resembles stacked LSTMs/GRUs and isn’t clearly distinguished from existing architectures.  
- Theoretical justification for the claimed temporal hierarchy is weak and absent.  
- Empirical evidence is limited—results come from a single dataset/task and lack exploration across modalities or tasks.  
- Comparison to prior work is superficial; the “state-of-the-art” claim ignores more recent advances and insufficiently situates against related methods.  
- Computational-cost claims are unclear and potentially misleading, lacking exact hardware/training-time comparisons.  
- Layer-removal analysis is limited because synergistic inter-layer effects make results hard to interpret.  
- Overall significance is modest and primarily methodological rather than transformative.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject; needs clearer novelty, stronger theoretical grounding, broader/rigorous experiments, and better comparisons to prior work.
