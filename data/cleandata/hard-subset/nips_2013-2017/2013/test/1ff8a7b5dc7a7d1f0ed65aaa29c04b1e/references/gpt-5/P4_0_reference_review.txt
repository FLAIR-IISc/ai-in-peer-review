Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies stacked recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) for character-level language modeling on the Hutter Wikipedia corpus. Each layer is a tanh RNN; stacking creates a hierarchy where higher layers receive the previous layer’s hidden states as inputs. Two output designs are considered: DRNN-1O (only the top layer connects to the softmax output) and DRNN-AO (all layers connect to the output and their contributions are summed before the softmax). Training uses SGD with gradient normalization (unit-norm scaling) and a linearly decaying learning rate. For DRNN-1O, the authors use incremental layer-wise training: add a layer, reinitialize the output head on the new top layer, and train the full network at each stage.

On the Hutter corpus (≈1.4B chars; last 10M for test), the authors report bits-per-character (BPC) comparable to the multiplicative RNN trained with Hessian-free optimization [19] at similar parameter budgets (~4.9M). They analyze the temporal behavior: (i) ablations removing a single layer’s output, (ii) “typo perturbation” to measure how long differences persist in hidden states by layer, (iii) context-switch experiments to gauge effective context length, and (iv) bracket-closing behavior to probe long-term dependencies. They find evidence that stacking induces a temporal hierarchy (shorter time scales at lower layers, longer at higher ones), with DRNNs using longer contexts for prediction than a single-layer RNN and demonstrating robust closing of parentheses in both discriminative and generative modes.

This work builds on hierarchical/stacked RNNs (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio [5], Fernández et al. [6], and stacked bi-LSTMs for speech [8]) and text RNNs trained with HF [18,19]. The main contributions relative to prior work are: (a) strong character-level performance with vanilla tanh stacked RNNs trained by SGD, (b) a clear empirical analysis of layer-wise time scales and their role in prediction, and (c) a practical training recipe (gradient normalization and incremental layer-wise training for the 1O variant).

Strengths
- Clear empirical evidence that stacking vanilla RNNs yields temporal hierarchies and longer effective context than a single-layer RNN, supported by multiple diagnostics (perturbation, context switch, bracket tests).
- Competitive BPC with simpler training (SGD) compared to prior HF-trained MRNNs at similar parameter counts; practical and relevant given training complexity concerns.
- Thoughtful ablations (zeroing per-layer outputs) to assess layer contributions; generative examples that qualitatively align with quantitative findings.
- Training details are reasonably specified (BPTT length, batch handling, initialization, LR schedule), aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental; stacked RNNs and multi-timescale RNNs have prior art. The architectural components (stacking, multi-output heads to aid gradient flow) are not fundamentally new; the main novelty is the analysis and training recipe.
- Comparisons omit strong baselines such as LSTM/GRU (including stacked LSTM) on the same dataset/preprocessing, which limits claims about state of the art more broadly and about modeling long-range dependencies.
- Dataset/protocol comparability: the character set differs from [19] (95+UNK vs 86), and the exact preprocessing may affect BPC; the fairness of the quantitative comparison would benefit from matched settings or re-evaluation of baselines.
- Limited experimental breadth: only three model instantiations, one seed per setting, and limited hyperparameter exploration. No variance estimates or statistical tests.
- Training for DRNN-1O differs (incremental layer-wise) from DRNN-AO; performance differences may reflect procedure rather than architecture. The analysis notes this but does not control for it.
- Initializing output weights to zero implies zero gradient to lower layers on the first step; harmless but suboptimal; could be avoided.
- No validation set/early stopping is described; risk of over/underfitting is not assessed.

Quality
- Methodologically sound and carefully executed within the chosen scope. The analyses of time scales and long-term behaviors are compelling and appropriately averaged (50k instances).
- Some design choices could be strengthened: matched-baseline comparisons (e.g., stacked LSTM with SGD/HF), reporting of training/validation curves, multiple runs to assess variance.
- Claims about “state-of-the-art” should be qualified to “among RNNs with similar parameter counts on this corpus under near-matched conditions,” given character-set differences.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. The motivation for DRNN-AO (gradient flow and interpretability) is clear. The perturbation/context experiments are explained clearly.
- Some details could be made more explicit: exact BPC numbers (Table 1 not visible here), preprocessing differences vs. prior work, rationale for 5 layers (cited but not shown), and the practical effect of zero-initialized output weights.

Originality
- Architectural novelty is limited; stacked RNNs and hierarchical time scales are established ideas. The multi-output head and the analysis suite add incremental methodological novelty and insights.

Significance
- The pragmatic result that a stacked vanilla RNN trained with SGD can match HF-trained MRNN performance at similar scale is valuable to practitioners.
- The temporal analysis is informative and likely to influence how researchers think about depth in RNNs beyond LSTM gating.
- Broader impact would increase with stronger baselines and ablations (e.g., depth/width tradeoffs, effect of gradient normalization, AO vs 1O under identical training regimes).

Suggestions for improvement
- Include matched baselines: single-layer LSTM, stacked LSTM, and/or GRU, all trained with the same SGD setup on the same character set.
- Report exact BPC values with training compute, and preferably re-run MRNN or report adjusted comparisons with the same character vocabulary.
- Provide results across multiple seeds and report mean±std.
- Control for training procedure: train DRNN-1O without incremental layer-wise pretraining (or pretrain DRNN-AO similarly) to isolate architecture effects.
- Add a validation split and learning-curve diagnostics; justify hyperparameters such as depth.
- Consider non-zero initialization for output layers; evaluate the effect of gradient normalization vs clipping thresholds.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid empirical analysis demonstrating learned temporal hierarchies in stacked RNNs.
- Competitive performance with a simple training procedure on a challenging corpus.
- Clear, instructive experiments (perturbation/context/bracket tests) that provide insights beyond raw metrics.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty over prior hierarchical/stacked RNNs; main contribution is analytical rather than architectural.
- Missing strong modern baselines (LSTM/GRU) and limited experimental breadth/variance reporting.
- Comparison to prior SOTA not strictly controlled due to differing character sets/protocols.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers useful empirical insights into deep RNNs’ temporal behavior and demonstrates a practical training recipe achieving strong results. With strengthened baselines and more controlled comparisons, it would be even more compelling.
