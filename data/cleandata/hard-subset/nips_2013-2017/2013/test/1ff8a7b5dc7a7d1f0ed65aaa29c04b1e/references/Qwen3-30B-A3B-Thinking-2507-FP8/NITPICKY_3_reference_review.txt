

# Review of "Training and Analyzing Deep Recurrent Neural Networks"

This paper proposes Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), which combine hierarchical processing of Deep Neural Networks with temporal feedback of RNNs. The authors claim DRNNs naturally create temporal hierarchies and outperform standard RNNs on character-level language modeling. The work builds on prior hierarchical RNN approaches [3,5,6] and the stacked LSTM work [8], but presents a novel architecture with theoretical claims about time-scale hierarchies.

## Strengths
The paper provides a clear architecture definition and conducts interesting experiments on time-scale hierarchies through perturbation tests and parenthesis-closing tasks. The results on Wikipedia text corpus show DRNNs achieve competitive performance with state-of-the-art recurrent networks. The analysis of layer contributions through output perturbation is a valuable contribution.

## Critical Weaknesses

1. **Technical Inconsistencies**: 
   - Equation (3) contains a critical typo: "η0 (− j T )" should be "η0(1 - j/T)" or similar. As written, the learning rate becomes negative for j > 0, which would cause divergence.
   - The paper states "All our networks have approximately 4.9 million trainable parameters" but the specified neuron counts don't match: 5 layers × 727 units = 3635 units, which is far fewer than the claimed parameter count. The parameter count calculation appears inconsistent with the architecture description.
   - The paper claims DRNN-1O sees the training set "about 10 times" while DRNN-AO sees it "about 7 times," but the training details don't clarify how this count was computed.

2. **Terminology and Notation Issues**:
   - The paper uses "x̄ = [x; 1]" without defining the semicolon notation for concatenation, which is non-standard in ML literature.
   - "1O" and "AO" are never defined (presumably "1 output" and "all outputs"), creating confusion for readers.
   - The phrase "wellsuited" (Section 1) is misspelled as one word.

3. **Methodological Flaws**:
   - The claim that "DRNNs suffer from a pathological curvature" is inaccurate; the issue with deep networks is vanishing gradients, not pathological curvature.
   - The paper states "we add layers one by one" but then claims "we always train the full network after each layer is added," which contradicts the description of incremental layer-wise training.
   - The statement "the same authors also used the PAQ compression algorithm [14]" is incorrect; [14] is Mahoney's "Adaptive weighing of context models," not PAQ compression.

4. **Experimental Analysis**:
   - The paper claims "the DRNN-AO sees the full training set about 7 times," but the training details don't provide sufficient information to verify this calculation.
   - The claim "This similarity might reflect limitations caused by the network size" lacks evidence; the authors don't compare to larger DRNNs to test this hypothesis.
   - The paper states "the networks are so large that there is very little difference in performance for different initialisations" without providing empirical evidence.

5. **Citation Errors**:
   - The paper cites [8] as using "stacked bi-directional LSTM networks" on TIMIT, but Graves et al.'s [8] actually uses standard (not bi-directional) LSTMs.
   - The paper references "the same authors" when discussing [19] without clearly identifying who "the authors" are.

## Overall Assessment

While the DRNN concept is interesting and the paper presents some valuable experimental analysis of temporal hierarchies, the numerous technical inconsistencies, notation errors, and methodological flaws undermine the paper's credibility. The critical typo in Equation (3) would cause the training procedure to fail, and the parameter count discrepancy suggests fundamental misunderstandings of the architecture's implementation. These are not trivial errors but rather substantial technical flaws that would prevent reproduction of the results.

The paper's claims about DRNNs creating "temporal hierarchies" are supported by the perturbation experiments, but the methodological issues make it difficult to assess whether the observed phenomena are truly due to the architecture or artifacts of the training procedure. The paper also fails to clearly distinguish its contributions from prior hierarchical RNN work [5,6].

Given the paper's technical flaws, I recommend rejection. The authors should address these serious methodological issues before the work can be considered for publication. The paper presents a potentially interesting idea, but the current version contains too many technical inconsistencies to be considered scientifically sound.
