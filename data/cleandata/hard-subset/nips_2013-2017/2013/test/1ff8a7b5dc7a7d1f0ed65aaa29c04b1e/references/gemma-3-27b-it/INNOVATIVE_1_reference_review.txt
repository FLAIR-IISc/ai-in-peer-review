This paper introduces Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), essentially stacking RNNs to create a hierarchy of timescales, and demonstrates competitive performance on character-level language modeling. The core idea – combining the hierarchical processing of DNNs with the temporal memory of RNNs – is genuinely novel and intuitively appealing. While the experimental validation isn’t exhaustive, the authors achieve state-of-the-art results *without* relying on computationally expensive Hessian-free optimization, a significant feat. The analysis of timescale emergence and the closing parenthesis experiment are particularly insightful, providing initial evidence supporting the theoretical motivation.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the DRNN architecture. The mathematical formulation is concise, and the experimental setup is described sufficiently for reproducibility. The inclusion of ablation studies (removing layer outputs) and the analysis of parenthesis completion demonstrate a commendable effort towards understanding the network’s behavior, beyond simply reporting performance numbers.  The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, accurately positioning the contribution relative to existing hierarchical RNN approaches.

However, the paper does have weaknesses.  The experimental evaluation is limited. Only three network instantiations are tested, making it difficult to assess the robustness of the findings. The comparison to other methods (particularly the Hessian-free MRNN) isn’t entirely fair, as the authors acknowledge differences in computational resources. The layer-wise training procedure for DRNN-1O feels somewhat ad-hoc, and a more principled approach could be beneficial.  Furthermore, while the timescale analysis is intriguing, it lacks quantitative rigor. Simply showing a decay in correlation after a perturbation doesn’t provide a precise measure of the timescale. The discussion of limitations regarding nested parentheses and sentences interrupting parenthesis closure is honest, but further investigation into why the DRNNs struggle with these scenarios would be valuable. The justification for choosing 5 layers isn't fully clear beyond a previous, unreported experiment.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The DRNN architecture is a conceptually new way to combine DNNs and RNNs, offering a fresh perspective on modeling temporal hierarchies.
*   **Strong Performance:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on a challenging task (character-level language modeling) without relying on sophisticated optimization techniques is impressive.
*   **Insightful Analysis:** The timescale analysis and the parenthesis completion experiments provide preliminary support for the underlying theoretical motivation.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Experimentation:** The small number of network instantiations and the lack of a comprehensive hyperparameter search raise concerns about the robustness of the findings.
*   **Ad-hoc Training:** The layer-wise training procedure for DRNN-1O isn’t fully justified.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** The timescale analysis lacks quantitative precision.
*   **Incomplete Validation:**  The paper doesn’t explore the DRNN’s performance on tasks specifically designed to test long-term memory, such as those cited from Hochreiter’s work.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations in experimental validation, the novelty and potential significance of the DRNN architecture are compelling. The authors have identified a promising direction for research, and the initial results are highly encouraging. I believe the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, even if some aspects require further refinement.  I lean towards acceptance, prioritizing the novelty and conceptual strength over methodological perfection.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding experimental validation and timescale analysis. Specifically, the authors should consider performing a more thorough hyperparameter search and providing more quantitative measures of the timescales learned by the network.
