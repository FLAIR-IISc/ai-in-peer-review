

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving the consensus problem, establishing convergence rates that are subdiffusive (O(D log(D/ε))) compared to the diffusive rates (O(D² log(1/ε))) of classical methods. The authors demonstrate that Min-Sum Splitting can be interpreted as a process on a lifted space (directed edges) and as a multi-step method on the original graph, connecting it to two major lines of accelerated consensus methods. The main contribution is the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, with a new proof technique using an auxiliary process on the nodes.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper identifies an important connection between Min-Sum message-passing and accelerated consensus methods, bridging two previously separate lines of research.
2. The theoretical analysis is sound for the quadratic case, with Theorem 1 establishing a square-root improvement in convergence rates over diffusive methods.
3. The paper provides clear algorithmic descriptions and demonstrates the equivalence between Min-Sum Splitting and shift-register methods, which is a significant theoretical contribution.
4. The paper is well-organized, with clear progression from background to problem statement to results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Technical Inconsistencies:** The paper contains several critical mathematical notational inconsistencies that undermine its technical soundness. In Section 5, the matrix definition K̂(δ,Γ) uses ambiguous indexing (e.g., "K̂(δ,Γ)wv,zu := δΓzw if u = w, z ∈ N(w) \ {v}"), which is unclear without additional context about the indexing of directed edges. The paper also incorrectly writes "φv(z) := 12z − bvz" instead of the expected "φv(z) := (1/2)z² - bvz" (p. 4, Section 3).

2. **Mathematical Errors:** Theorem 1 contains a serious mathematical error in the expression for ρK: "ρK := ρ(K −K∞) = limt→∞ ‖(K −K∞)t‖1/t = √(1−√1−ρ2W)/(1+√1−ρ2W)" is missing closing parentheses, making the expression mathematically ambiguous and incorrect. This is a fundamental error that should be corrected.

3. **Notational Confusion:** The paper inconsistently uses "D" for graph diameter but also uses "d" for graph degree without clear distinction (e.g., "d-regular graphs" vs. "D ∼ n in a cycle"). This confusion appears multiple times throughout the paper.

4. **Algorithmic Clarity:** In Algorithm 1, the output line "xtv = arg minz∈R µtv(z), v ∈ V" is problematic because µtv is defined as "φv + δ ∑ w∈N(v) Γwvµ̂ t wv", which is a function, not a value. This should be clarified as "xtv = arg minz∈R µtv(z)" where µtv is the belief function.

5. **Formatting Issues:** The paper inconsistently writes "log 1/ε" versus "log(1/ε)" throughout, which is a minor but distracting formatting issue. There are also missing spaces in mathematical expressions (e.g., "φv/δ − µ̂s−1wv" should be "φv/δ − µ̂^{s-1}_{wv}").

6. **Theoretical Assumptions:** The paper claims "the choice of splitting parameters that yields subdiffusive rates involves global type of information," but this is not a weakness of the paper but rather a characteristic of accelerated methods. However, the paper does not adequately explain why this global information is necessary or how it can be practically obtained in a distributed setting.

7. **References:** The reference list contains several entries with incomplete information (e.g., "Randomized gossip algorithms, S. Boyd, A. Ghosh, B. Prabhakar, D. Shah, IEEE Transactions on Information Theory,, , , " has extra commas).

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents an interesting connection between Min-Sum message-passing and accelerated consensus methods, and establishes a theoretically sound convergence rate analysis for the quadratic case, the numerous technical inconsistencies and mathematical errors significantly undermine its quality. The notational errors and missing parentheses in Theorem 1 are particularly concerning as they could mislead readers about the actual convergence rate. The paper would benefit from a thorough technical review to correct these fundamental issues.

The theoretical contribution is novel and potentially significant, but the paper's execution falls short of the standards expected for NIPS. The technical flaws are too numerous and serious to ignore, especially considering the paper's claim of establishing convergence rates. These issues would require substantial revision before the paper could be considered for publication.

**Recommendation:** Reject

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The connection between Min-Sum and accelerated consensus methods is novel and significant.
- The convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting is the first of its kind.
- The paper provides a new proof technique using an auxiliary process.

*Con:*
- Multiple serious mathematical errors and notational inconsistencies.
- The paper fails to properly format mathematical expressions.
- The technical details are not sufficiently rigorous or clear.
- The paper contains several distracting formatting inconsistencies.
