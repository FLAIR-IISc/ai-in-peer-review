

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum Splitting to the consensus problem, establishing convergence rates that achieve a square-root improvement over traditional diffusive methods. The authors demonstrate that by properly tuning the splitting parameters, Min-Sum Splitting can achieve convergence rates of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, compared to the O(D² log(1/ε)) rates of classical algorithms. The work connects Min-Sum schemes to two established lines of research: lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step accelerated optimization methods. The authors provide a novel proof technique using an auxiliary process to track the algorithm's evolution on the nodes rather than the directed edges, which simplifies the convergence analysis.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of Min-Sum-based distributed algorithms. The first analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting in the consensus problem is valuable.
2. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods is insightful and well-motivated, providing a unified perspective on accelerated consensus methods.
3. The proof technique using an auxiliary process is elegant and potentially applicable to other distributed optimization problems.
4. The paper provides a thorough comparison with prior work, particularly [27], showing how the proposed approach improves upon previous Min-Sum methods for consensus.
5. The theoretical results are technically sound, with clear theorems and proofs in the supplementary material.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper focuses exclusively on the quadratic consensus problem (network averaging), which is a special case. The authors should discuss how their approach might generalize to non-quadratic objective functions, which would strengthen the paper's significance.
2. The theoretical results depend on global information (the spectral gap of a matrix associated with the graph) for parameter tuning. The paper does not address how this limitation might affect practical implementation where global information is unavailable.
3. The paper lacks empirical validation. While theoretical results are important, simulations showing the convergence rates in practice would significantly strengthen the contribution and provide readers with intuition about the practical benefits.
4. The connection to lifted Markov chains is mentioned but not deeply explored. The paper could benefit from more detailed discussion of how Min-Sum Splitting compares to specific lifted chain constructions.
5. The paper does not discuss the communication or computational overhead of Min-Sum Splitting compared to other accelerated methods, which is crucial for practical deployment.

**Recommendation:**
The paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution to the field of distributed optimization with a clear connection to established research areas. While the theoretical results are sound, the paper would be significantly strengthened by empirical validation, discussion of generalization beyond quadratic objectives, and a more thorough exploration of the practical implications of the global information requirement.

**Specific suggestions for improvement:**
1. Add a section with simulations comparing the convergence rates of Min-Sum Splitting with classical methods and other accelerated approaches (e.g., lifted Markov chains, Nesterov acceleration) for various graph topologies.
2. Discuss how the global information requirement for parameter tuning might be addressed in practice, perhaps through adaptive methods or approximations.
3. Extend the analysis to non-quadratic objective functions, at least in a theoretical discussion, to demonstrate broader applicability.
4. Provide a more detailed comparison with lifted Markov chain methods, explaining how Min-Sum Splitting avoids the need for constructing lifted graphs while achieving similar acceleration.
5. Include a brief discussion of the computational complexity and communication requirements of the proposed method.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding Min-Sum-based distributed algorithms, but would benefit from addressing the limitations mentioned above. The core theoretical results are strong and novel, but the practical impact would be significantly enhanced with empirical validation and discussion of generalization beyond quadratic objectives.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**
*Pro:* Novel theoretical results on convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, first analysis of this algorithm for consensus problems, insightful connections to two established lines of research, elegant proof technique using auxiliary process.

*Con:* Focuses only on quadratic consensus problem, lacks empirical validation, depends on global information for parameter tuning, limited discussion of practical implementation considerations.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that would be significantly improved with empirical validation and discussion of broader applicability. The paper meets the quality standard for NeurIPS, but the limited scope and lack of empirical results prevent it from being a top-tier submission. I recommend rejection with the understanding that the authors could address these limitations in a revised version.
