

# Review: Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting

This paper proposes an analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for the consensus problem, claiming to establish convergence rates that achieve a square-root improvement over diffusive methods. The authors connect Min-Sum Splitting to lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods, showing that proper tuning of splitting parameters yields accelerated convergence.

The paper's main contributions include: (1) establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, (2) showing connections between Min-Sum Splitting and two established accelerated methods (lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods), and (3) demonstrating that proper parameter tuning recovers the same matrix as shift-register methods used in numerical analysis.

The theoretical analysis appears technically sound, with the authors providing rigorous proofs for their claims about convergence rates. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and existing accelerated methods is interesting and potentially valuable, as it bridges two previously separate lines of research. The paper properly cites relevant prior work, including [27] which investigated Min-Sum for consensus but did not establish convergence rates, and [30, 36] for Nesterov-based methods.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's quality and significance. The most serious issue is the complete absence of empirical validation. The paper claims a "square-root improvement" in convergence rates and that Min-Sum Splitting "gets close to the optimal rate O(D log(1/ε))," but provides no experimental evidence to support these claims. NIPS typically expects both theoretical and empirical validation for papers claiming performance improvements, especially in the context of distributed optimization where practical performance is crucial.

The paper's theoretical claims are substantial, but they're presented without any supporting experiments. For instance, the authors state that Min-Sum Splitting "achieves better convergence rates than the ones established (and conjectured) for the Min-Sum method in [27]," but provide no numerical comparison to validate this claim. Without empirical results, it's impossible to assess whether the theoretical improvements translate to practical benefits.

The paper also overstates its novelty in places. While it correctly identifies that [27] didn't establish convergence rates, it claims to be "the first" to analyze Min-Sum Splitting, but [35] (cited in the paper) had previously shown that Min-Sum with splitting converges where ordinary Min-Sum does not. The paper doesn't sufficiently clarify how their analysis differs from or extends [35].

Furthermore, the paper's connection to shift-register methods (claiming "a proper choice of the splitting parameters recovers the same matrix that support the evolution of shift-register methods") is a strong claim that would benefit from more explicit comparison to existing shift-register approaches, rather than just citing [45, 4, 24].

As a skeptical reviewer who prefers established methods and requires thorough validation, I find the lack of empirical evidence particularly concerning. The paper presents a theoretically interesting analysis but fails to demonstrate that the proposed method delivers tangible benefits in practice. For a NIPS paper claiming to "accelerate" consensus algorithms, this omission is critical.

I also note that the paper's theoretical approach relies on specific assumptions about the graph structure and problem formulation (e.g., quadratic objective functions, specific initial conditions), which limits its applicability to more general settings. The paper doesn't adequately address how these assumptions affect the practical utility of the method.

In summary, while the paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the analysis of Min-Sum Splitting, it falls short of NIPS standards by lacking empirical validation, overclaiming novelty in places, and not demonstrating practical benefits. The absence of experiments to validate the claimed performance improvements is a major weakness for a paper making such strong claims.

Recommendation: Reject. The paper's theoretical contribution is potentially valuable, but the lack of empirical validation renders it incomplete as a scientific contribution. NIPS requires both theoretical rigor and empirical validation for papers claiming performance improvements, and this paper fails to meet the latter requirement. The authors should provide experimental results comparing Min-Sum Splitting to existing methods before resubmitting.
