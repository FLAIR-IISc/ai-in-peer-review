

This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for distributed consensus problems, establishing convergence rates that demonstrate a square-root improvement over classical diffusive methods. The authors focus on the network averaging problem, where each node in a graph holds a value and the goal is to reach consensus on the average value across the network. The paper builds on prior work by [27], which showed that ordinary Min-Sum does not converge on graphs with cycles, and proposed a modified version using soft barriers. The authors demonstrate that Min-Sum Splitting, which involves a reparametrization of the objective function, guarantees convergence for any graph topology and achieves accelerated convergence rates.

Strengths:
- Theoretical contribution: The paper provides the first proof of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting (Theorem 1), showing that with proper parameter tuning, the algorithm achieves O(D log(D/ε)) convergence time for cycle graphs and tori, compared to the classical Θ(D² log(1/ε)). This represents a square-root improvement over diffusive methods.
- Novel connections: The paper establishes the first connection between Min-Sum schemes and two established approaches in the literature: lifted Markov chain techniques (e.g., [8, 5, 37]) and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization (e.g., [13, 24]). This insight bridges different research threads and provides a unified perspective on accelerated consensus algorithms.
- Technical soundness: The proofs are rigorous, with a novel analytical technique involving an auxiliary process to track Min-Sum Splitting evolution. The paper clearly distinguishes its approach from [27] by demonstrating superior convergence rates.
- Clarity: The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions, consistent notation, and logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results. The connection to prior work is well-explained, and the manuscript provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results.

Weaknesses:
- The focus on quadratic objective functions (network averaging) limits the paper's applicability to a specific case of the consensus problem. The paper does not sufficiently address how the results might extend to more general objective functions.
- The parameter choice in Theorem 1 (δ = 1 and Γ = γW) depends on global information about the graph (the spectral gap of W), which may be impractical in fully distributed settings where nodes lack global graph knowledge.
- The paper lacks empirical validation of the theoretical results, which would strengthen the claims about convergence rates and practical benefits.
- The paper could better contextualize its contributions within the broader literature on accelerated consensus algorithms, particularly in relation to other methods that achieve similar convergence rates.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
1. The paper establishes the first theoretical analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, representing a significant contribution to the literature.
2. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two different accelerated methods (lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods) is novel and insightful.
3. The theoretical results demonstrate a meaningful improvement over classical diffusive methods, achieving rates close to the optimal O(D log(1/ε)) for relevant graph topologies.
4. The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs and clear exposition.

Con arguments for rejection:
1. The focus on quadratic objectives limits the paper's broader impact, as many distributed optimization problems involve non-quadratic objectives.
2. The dependence on global information for parameter tuning may limit practical applicability in fully distributed settings.
3. The lack of empirical validation weakens the practical significance of the theoretical results.
4. The paper does not fully address how the approach might extend to more general optimization problems beyond consensus.

The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to understanding Min-Sum algorithms for consensus problems. The connection to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods is particularly insightful, and the convergence rate analysis is rigorous and meaningful. While the focus on quadratic objectives and lack of empirical results are limitations, these do not undermine the paper's theoretical contributions. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a significant problem in distributed optimization.

The paper is within NIPS scope, as it addresses distributed optimization problems with connections to machine learning and signal processing applications. The theoretical contribution is substantial and advances the state of the art in understanding convergence rates for distributed consensus algorithms. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the potential extension to non-quadratic objectives and discuss the practical implications of the global information requirement.
