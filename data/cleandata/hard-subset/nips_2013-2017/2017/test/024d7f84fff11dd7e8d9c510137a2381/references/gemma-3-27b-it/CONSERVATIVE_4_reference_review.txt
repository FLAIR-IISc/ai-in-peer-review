This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors establish convergence and demonstrate a square-root improvement in convergence time compared to diffusive methods, achieving rates competitive with recently established accelerated algorithms. The work connects Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, offering a unified perspective.

The paper is technically sound, presenting a rigorous analysis based on auxiliary linear processes. The proofs, while deferred to the supplementary material, are presumably solid given the detailed explanation of the approach. The choice to focus on quadratic consensus is reasonable, allowing for analytical tractability. However, the immediate applicability of these results to more general, non-quadratic consensus problems is unclear and warrants further discussion. The authors acknowledge this limitation but could benefit from speculating on potential extensions.

The clarity is generally good, though the paper is dense with technical details and requires a strong background in distributed optimization and message passing. The notation is consistent, and the organization is logical, progressing from problem formulation to analysis and conclusions. A more intuitive explanation of the “splitting” process itself, beyond the mathematical formalization, would be beneficial for a broader audience. While the connection to existing work is acknowledged, the discussion feels somewhat fragmented, with numerous citations without a clear synthesis of the landscape. 

The originality lies in the convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, which appears to be the first of its kind. The demonstration of a connection to both lifted Markov chains *and* accelerated first-order methods is a notable contribution, offering a fresh perspective on message-passing algorithms. However, the core idea of splitting—reparameterizing the objective function—is not entirely novel and builds on prior work by Ruozzi and Tatikonda [34]. The paper’s originality is therefore incremental rather than groundbreaking. The empirical evaluation is notably absent. While the theoretical analysis is strong, demonstrating the performance gains on real-world graphs or datasets would significantly strengthen the paper.

The significance is moderate. While the theoretical results are compelling, the practical impact remains uncertain without empirical validation. The square-root improvement in convergence time is valuable, but its relevance to specific applications depends on the graph structure and problem scale. The connection to existing accelerated methods is interesting, but it doesn't immediately suggest a fundamentally new algorithmic paradigm.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Rigorous theoretical analysis establishing convergence and rates for Min-Sum Splitting.
*   Novel connection to lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods.
*   Demonstrated improvement over diffusive methods.
*   Well-written and technically sound.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of empirical validation.
*   Incremental originality – builds on existing work rather than presenting a revolutionary approach.
*   Limited discussion of applicability to non-quadratic problems.
*   Dense technical presentation may limit accessibility.
*   The core idea of “splitting” is a reparameterization and not a fundamentally new algorithmic idea.

**Overall Assessment:**

I am leaning towards **reject** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after including extensive experimental results. While the paper presents a technically sound and potentially valuable theoretical contribution, the lack of empirical validation significantly weakens its impact. The authors need to demonstrate the practical benefits of Min-Sum Splitting on real-world graphs and datasets to convince the community of its significance. Furthermore, a more accessible presentation and a deeper discussion of the algorithm's limitations would enhance its overall quality. The theoretical results, while impressive, are not sufficient justification for acceptance in the absence of practical demonstration. I am skeptical of the claim of a significant contribution without evidence of improved performance in practice.
