Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting (a reparameterized variant of Min-Sum/Belief Propagation) for the consensus/averaging problem on graphs. The main contributions are:
- A specialization of Min-Sum Splitting to quadratic consensus that yields a linear message-passing protocol on directed edges.
- An auxiliary node-based, two-step linear system that exactly tracks the algorithm’s output, enabling spectral analysis.
- A proof that with a specific tuning (δ = 1, Γ = γW with γ depending on the spectral gap of a symmetric weight matrix W), Min-Sum Splitting converges at an accelerated, “subdiffusive” rate. On cycles and tori, the convergence time scales as O(D log(D/ε)) rather than Θ(D^2 log(1/ε)).
- Conceptual connections between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted nonreversible Markov chains, and multi-step accelerated first-order methods (shift-register/heavy-ball style).

This fills a gap: while Min-Sum Splitting had been observed to converge in settings where ordinary Min-Sum fails, no prior rate analysis existed for consensus. The accelerated rate matches the shift-register accelerated consensus literature and approaches the O(D log(1/ε)) best-known results (e.g., Nesterov-style methods) while providing a principled Min-Sum-based route.

Quality (technical correctness and support)
- The technical path—deriving a linear edge process for quadratic consensus, then proving equivalence with a node-based 2n-dimensional linear system—is plausible and well aligned with known analyses of quadratic BP/Gaussian BP and message reparameterizations. The equivalence propositions (edge-to-node) are the key step that makes the spectral argument tractable.
- The main theorem’s rate ρK = sqrt((1−√(1−ρW^2))/(1+√(1−ρW^2))) and the square-root improvement over ρW match known second-order acceleration behavior (Chebyshev/shift-register). The need for γ tuned via ρW is standard in accelerated consensus.
- Assumptions: symmetric W with W1=1 and ρ(W−11^T/n)<1; synchronous updates; quadratic objectives; exact knowledge (or a good bound) of ρW to set γ. These are reasonable but limit generality.
- Some technical clarity issues: the output formula in Algorithm 2 appears malformed (a ratio is intended, consistent with Algorithm 3’s xtv = r^t_v/R^t_v); the positivity condition in Proposition 1 could be stated with a proof that the chosen initialization/parameters maintain it. These are fixable editorial points, but I would like explicit statements ensuring denominators don’t vanish under the recommended tuning.

Clarity (organization and readability)
- The paper is generally well organized: it motivates the problem, situates it among diffusive, lifting, and multi-step methods, and clearly states the main theorem.
- Notation is heavy; a brief notational table or a cleaner separation between edge- and node-level quantities would help. Several typesetting issues (missing fractions, superscripts, minor typos) distract and should be corrected (especially Algorithm 2’s output).
- The conceptual insights—“splitting induces directionality akin to nonreversible lifting” and “directionality manifests as memory in the auxiliary node process”—are insightful and clearly explained.

Originality (novelty and relation to prior work)
- To my knowledge, this is the first rate analysis of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus and the first to expose its concrete connection both to lifted Markov chains and to shift-register acceleration. Prior work either analyzed non-splitting Min-Sum for consensus with weaker or topology-specific results or obtained acceleration through other algorithmic families (Chebyshev/Nesterov, ADMM variants, lifted walks).
- The auxiliary-node reduction is a new analytical tool for this setting, and the identification of the exact K-matrix matching shift-registers is crisp.

Significance (impact and usefulness)
- For researchers in message passing, distributed optimization, and spectral graph methods, the result is significant: it demonstrates that a widely used message-passing paradigm (Min-Sum) can be tuned to achieve near-best-known consensus rates and provides a reusable analysis technique.
- Practical impact depends on parameter tuning (needs ρW or a proxy) and lack of empirical validation. Nonetheless, the theoretical guarantee and bridging perspective are valuable, and could inspire extensions to broader convex objectives or to ADMM/BP hybrids.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical advance: first accelerated rates for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus.
- Elegant reduction from edge-wise BP to a node-wise two-step linear system enabling spectral analysis.
- Conceptual unification across BP, lifted chains, and multi-step acceleration.
- Near-optimal rates on geometric graphs (O(D log(D/ε))) and general spectral characterization.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope: restricted to quadratic consensus with symmetric W and synchronous updates; extensions to general strongly convex consensus, constraints beyond equality, directed/asynchronous settings would broaden impact.
- Tuning: requires global spectral information (ρW). Discuss practical estimation strategies or robust parameter choices based on graph bounds (e.g., conductance/diameter) and the degradation in rate if γ is misspecified.
- Clarity/typos: fix Algorithm 2 output (use explicit ratio), state and prove conditions ensuring positivity/denominator bounded away from zero under the recommended initialization and parameters.
- Empirics: even small-scale experiments on cycles/grids/random geometric graphs would illustrate acceleration and robustness relative to Metropolis, Chebyshev acceleration, and ADMM/EXTRA.
- Complexity: discuss per-iteration message/compute complexity versus standard consensus, accelerated multi-step, and lifted-walk methods; memory cost and initialization overhead.

Questions for authors
- Can γ be chosen adaptively online (e.g., via spectral estimation) without losing the acceleration guarantee?
- Does the auxiliary-process approach extend to general strongly convex φv or to constraints beyond equality (e.g., Laplacian regularization)?
- How sensitive is convergence to small model mismatches or asynchronous updates?

Overall assessment
- The paper meets the NeurIPS criteria well: technically sound with a novel analysis technique, clear positioning relative to prior work, and a meaningful theoretical advance for a central distributed primitive. The lack of experiments and some presentation issues are the main drawbacks but do not negate the contribution.
- Recommendation: accept (borderline-to-strong), contingent on fixing clarity issues and, ideally, adding brief empirical validation.

Per-criterion evaluation
- Quality: high (theory solid and plausible; minor clarifications needed)
- Clarity: medium-high (well structured but notation/typos should be improved)
- Originality: high
- Significance: medium-high (important for theory of distributed optimization/message passing)
