This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, a generalized message-passing scheme, for solving the canonical network averaging consensus problem. The authors' primary goal is to establish, for the first time, the rates of convergence for this algorithm and to show that with proper parameter tuning, it can achieve accelerated ("subdiffusive") convergence.

The paper begins by situating the consensus problem within the broader context of distributed optimization. It reviews classical "diffusive" algorithms, which are known to have suboptimal convergence rates (e.g., Θ(D^2 log(1/ε)) on tori, where D is the graph diameter), and the two primary lines of research for acceleration: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods from convex optimization. The core contribution is to demonstrate that Min-Sum Splitting, a belief propagation method, can be tuned to achieve accelerated rates comparable to these state-of-the-art approaches. The key technical innovation is the introduction of an "auxiliary message-passing scheme." This scheme allows the analysis of the algorithm, which is naturally defined by message-passing on the graph's directed edges, to be mapped to a simpler linear system with memory on the graph's nodes. This analytical tool enables the authors to connect the Min-Sum Splitting dynamics directly to well-understood multi-step methods. Specifically, they show that a particular choice of the splitting parameters recovers the exact dynamics of a shift-register method, thereby inheriting its known accelerated convergence rate. This provides a square-root improvement over diffusive methods, achieving a convergence time of O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori. A significant conceptual contribution is the unification of Min-Sum schemes with the two major families of accelerated methods, showing how the "splitting" reparametrization induces a directionality and memory analogous to non-reversible lifted walks and momentum terms, respectively.

### Quality and Technical Soundness

The paper is technically sound. The analysis is presented for the network averaging problem, which involves a quadratic objective function. In this setting, the authors demonstrate that the Min-Sum Splitting messages remain in the quadratic family, allowing the updates to be expressed as a linear system on the message parameters (Proposition 1). The central analytical device, the auxiliary message-passing scheme on the nodes (Proposition 2), is a clever construction that simplifies the analysis from a system of size 2m (where m is the number of edges) to one of size 2n (where n is the number of nodes). The main result, Theorem 1, then follows by leveraging the existing spectral analysis of the resulting 2n x 2n matrix, which is known from the literature on shift-register methods for linear solvers. This approach of reducing the problem to a previously analyzed one is solid. The claims are well-supported by the theoretical derivations presented. The work is a complete piece, starting from the general algorithm and systematically leading to the main convergence rate theorem. The authors are careful to state the conditions for their results and acknowledge that the optimal parameter choice depends on global graph properties (the spectral radius of a related matrix W), which is a common feature of such accelerated methods.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The introduction and background sections (Sections 1, 3, 4) provide a thorough and clear review of the consensus problem, standard algorithms, and existing accelerated methods, making the paper accessible and effectively contextualizing the contributions. The logical flow is excellent, guiding the reader from the general Min-Sum Splitting algorithm (Section 2), to its specialization for consensus (Section 5), to the introduction of the auxiliary process for analysis (Section 6), and finally to the main convergence result (Section 7). The main contributions are explicitly enumerated in the introduction, which helps frame the reader's expectations. The connection drawn between Min-Sum Splitting and other accelerated methods is explained intuitively and effectively. The mathematical notation is consistent and clearly defined.

### Originality

The paper makes several original contributions. While Min-Sum Splitting was proposed in [34, 35], this is the first work to provide a convergence rate analysis for it. The analytical technique based on the auxiliary node-based process is novel and could potentially be applied to analyze other edge-based message-passing algorithms.

The most significant and original contribution is the conceptual bridge built between three previously distinct areas: belief propagation (via Min-Sum), lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order optimization methods. Showing that a reparametrization trick from the belief propagation literature can be tuned to reproduce the dynamics of an accelerated optimization method (shift-register) is a new and powerful insight. This work also significantly advances the state of the art for Min-Sum-based consensus compared to [27], providing a more general analysis (for any graph topology), a faster convergence rate, and a clearer connection to mainstream acceleration techniques. While the final algorithm's dynamics are equivalent to a known method, the originality lies in demonstrating that this behavior can emerge from a principled message-passing framework, thereby unifying these perspectives.

### Significance

The results presented are significant for the machine learning and distributed optimization communities. Consensus is a foundational building block in many large-scale learning algorithms. This work demonstrates that Min-Sum, a canonical distributed inference algorithm, is not only viable for consensus but can be made highly competitive with state-of-the-art accelerated methods. This could encourage the use and further study of message-passing schemes for a broader class of distributed optimization problems.

The conceptual connection between splitting, directionality (as in lifted chains), and memory (as in momentum methods) is particularly impactful. It provides a new lens through which to understand and potentially design accelerated distributed algorithms. Researchers and practitioners working on distributed systems, sensor networks, and large-scale ML are likely to find these ideas useful and build upon them. The primary limitation on immediate practical impact is the requirement of global graph information (spectral properties) to optimally tune the splitting parameters, a common challenge for many accelerated distributed methods. Nonetheless, the theoretical and conceptual advances are substantial.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  Provides the first convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.
2.  Establishes a novel and insightful connection between belief propagation and mainstream accelerated optimization methods, unifying disparate lines of research.
3.  Introduces a novel and elegant proof technique (the auxiliary process) that simplifies the analysis of a complex edge-based algorithm.
4.  Demonstrates that a properly tuned Min-Sum scheme achieves a significant, square-root speedup over standard consensus algorithms, making it competitive with state-of-the-art methods.
5.  The paper is very clearly written, well-motivated, and places the contributions in excellent context.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
1.  The analysis is restricted to the network averaging problem (quadratic objective). Generalization to other convex functions is left as future work.
2.  The optimal parameter setting requires knowledge of global spectral properties of the graph, which may be difficult to obtain in a fully decentralized setting.
