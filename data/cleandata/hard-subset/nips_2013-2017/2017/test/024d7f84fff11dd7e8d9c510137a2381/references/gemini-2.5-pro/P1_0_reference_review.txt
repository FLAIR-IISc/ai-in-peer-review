### **Summary**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, a generalization of the standard Min-Sum message-passing protocol, when applied to the network averaging consensus problem. The authors establish that while ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge on graphs with cycles, Min-Sum Splitting can not only guarantee convergence but also achieve accelerated rates.

The core technical contribution is a novel proof technique that introduces an auxiliary message-passing scheme. This auxiliary process runs on the nodes of the graph and tracks the output of the original Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, which operates on the directed edges. This simplifies the analysis by transforming the problem into studying a multi-step linear system on the nodes, which is more tractable.

The main result (Theorem 1) shows that by carefully selecting the splitting parameters (δ and Γ), the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm recovers the exact dynamics of accelerated first-order methods, specifically the shift-register method. This leads to an asymptotic convergence time that scales like O(D log(D/ε)) on graphs like cycles and tori, a square-root improvement over the O(D² log(1/ε)) rate of standard "diffusive" consensus algorithms. A key conceptual contribution of the work is connecting the reparametrization in Min-Sum schemes to acceleration mechanisms found in both lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** This paper provides the first convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. The results are strong, demonstrating not just convergence but a provable acceleration that matches the performance of well-known accelerated consensus methods. This elevates Min-Sum Splitting from a heuristic for ensuring convergence to a principled tool for designing fast distributed algorithms.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Proof Technique:** The introduction of the auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) is a clever and effective analytical tool. It bridges the gap between the complex dynamics on the 2m-dimensional edge space and a much more structured 2n-dimensional node space, allowing the authors to leverage existing spectral analysis of accelerated methods. This technique may be of independent interest for analyzing other message-passing algorithms.

3.  **Excellent Conceptual Connections:** The paper does a superb job of synthesizing ideas from three distinct areas: graphical models (Min-Sum/Belief Propagation), accelerated convex optimization (multi-step/momentum methods), and theoretical computer science (lifted/non-reversible Markov chains). It convincingly argues that the "splitting" parameters introduce a form of directionality or memory that is analogous to the acceleration mechanisms in the other two fields. This provides a new and valuable perspective on why and how message-passing can be accelerated.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. It begins with a clear motivation and a thorough review of related work on both standard and accelerated consensus. The technical sections build the argument logically, first specializing the algorithm, then introducing the analytical tool (the auxiliary process), and culminating in the main theorem. The contributions are clearly articulated.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Experimental Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical results are compelling, the inclusion of even simple numerical simulations would have significantly strengthened the paper. For example, a plot comparing the convergence of the proposed Min-Sum Splitting scheme against a standard diffusive method (e.g., using W_MH) and the prior Min-Sum work of [27] on a cycle or torus graph would provide a clear, visual confirmation of the claimed acceleration and its practical relevance.

2.  **Practicality of Parameter Selection:** The accelerated rate in Theorem 1 is contingent on a specific choice of the parameter γ, which in turn depends on ρ_W, the second largest eigenvalue magnitude of the matrix W. This is a global property of the network graph. The paper does not discuss how this parameter might be chosen or estimated in a distributed setting, which is a key challenge for implementing such accelerated methods in practice. While this is a common issue for many accelerated algorithms, a brief discussion of this limitation or potential workarounds would be beneficial.

3.  **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is performed for the quadratic network averaging problem. While this is a canonical and important instance of consensus, Min-Sum is a general framework for problems with separable objectives. The paper could be improved by briefly discussing the potential for extending these results. Is the linearity of the updates in the quadratic case essential for the entire analytical framework, or could the core ideas be applied to more general convex objectives? A short discussion in the conclusion would help frame the scope and future directions of this work.

### **Detailed Comments & Questions for Authors**

*   The connection to shift-register methods is very clear. Could you comment on the connection to Nesterov's accelerated method? The updates are slightly different, but both achieve similar acceleration. Does a different choice of δ and Γ perhaps recover Nesterov-style dynamics?
*   In Proposition 1, the condition `1 + δ ∑ w∈N (v) ΓwvR̂ t wv > 0` is required. Is this condition naturally satisfied under the parameter choices of Theorem 1, especially given the initialization R̂0 = 0? A brief justification would be helpful.
*   The comparison with [27] is a strong point. You mention they achieve O(D/ε log 1/ε) for cycles. Your O(D log(D/ε)) appears worse due to the log(D) term. Could you clarify this comparison? Is it possible the constants or the exact error metric definition makes your result superior? Or is the main advantage of your work the generality beyond d-regular graphs and the tighter analysis for tori?

### **Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality, well-written theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on distributed optimization and message-passing algorithms. Its primary strengths are the novel rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and the insightful conceptual bridge it builds between belief propagation and accelerated optimization methods. The main weakness is the absence of empirical results to complement the theory. Despite this, the theoretical contribution is strong and original enough to be of great interest to the NIPS community.

**Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by the theoretical analysis.
**Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is very well-organized and clearly written.
**Originality:** High. This is the first work to provide a rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and to formally connect it to accelerated first-order methods.
**Significance:** High. The results provide a new understanding of message-passing algorithms as a potential source for accelerated distributed methods, likely inspiring future work in this direction.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
