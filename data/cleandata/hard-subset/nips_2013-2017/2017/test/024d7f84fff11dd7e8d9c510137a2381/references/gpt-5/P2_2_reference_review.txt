Summary
The paper studies the Min-Sum Splitting variant of message passing and establishes convergence rates when applied to the equality-constrained consensus (network averaging) problem. The authors (i) specialize Min-Sum Splitting to quadratic consensus, showing the messages remain quadratic and the algorithm reduces to a linear iteration on directed edges; (ii) construct an auxiliary node-based, two-step linear iteration that exactly tracks the edge-based dynamics; and (iii) prove that with a specific choice of splitting parameters (δ = 1, Γ = γW with γ tuned from the spectrum of a symmetric W with W1 = 1), the method converges with a rate matching classical shift-register/Chebyshev-accelerated consensus, yielding a square-root improvement over diffusive methods. They connect the acceleration mechanism to both lifted, non-reversible Markov chains and to multi-step momentum methods in convex optimization. On grids and tori the convergence time scales as O(D log(D/ε)) versus O(D^2 log(1/ε)).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The paper’s main result (Theorem 1) is precise: it identifies a parameterization under which the auxiliary node-level matrix coincides with the shift-register iteration, so the rate follows from known spectral analysis. The derivation of linear message dynamics (Proposition 1) and the node-based auxiliary reduction (Proposition 2) are plausible and valuable. The bounds are clean and standard (spectral radius of K − K∞).
- Assumptions and limitations are stated: quadratic objectives, synchronous updates, fixed graph, symmetric W with W1=1, and an initialization that enables the mapping (R̂0 = r̂0 = 0). The method needs global spectral information (ρW) to choose γ.
- Completeness: The theoretical story is coherent, but the paper lacks empirical validation (even small synthetic graphs) and does not discuss robustness to asynchrony, noise, time-variation, or quantization. These are common in consensus literature and would strengthen practical relevance.
- Correctness/consistency: One point to clarify is the sparsity constraint on Γ versus the usual presence of nonzero diagonals in W (e.g., Metropolis weights). The paper assumes Γvw = 0 if {v,w} is not an edge; for simple graphs, {v,v} is not an edge, implying Γvv=0. Since Theorem 1 sets Γ = γW, this suggests either W must have zero diagonal or the diagonal of Γ is ignored by the algorithm. Please clarify whether W is required to be zero-diagonal (allowing negative off-diagonal entries) or whether the framework permits nonzero Γvv and how it is used in Algorithms 1–3 (sums currently run over neighbors only).

Clarity
- The paper is well organized and mostly clear. The motivation and positioning relative to diffusive consensus, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step methods are well articulated. The auxiliary process is a key conceptual contribution and is clearly presented.
- Notational density is high; a brief table or reminder of symbols (W, Γ, A, K̂, K, etc.) would help. Check equation indexing (e.g., K̂ definition references) and ensure consistency between neighbor sums and any diagonal entries in Γ/W.
- A short proof sketch of Theorem 1 in the main text (beyond citing known spectral results) would further aid readers.

Originality
- Establishing the first convergence-rate guarantees for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, and the explicit equivalence to node-level multi-step/shift-register iterations, appears novel. The auxiliary-process proof technique is new in this context.
- The acceleration rate achieved matches known Chebyshev/shift-register rates; while the rate itself is not new, showing it can be obtained via properly tuned Min-Sum Splitting and relating the mechanism to both lifting and momentum methods is an original and insightful unification.
- Related work is broadly covered (GaBP/Min-Sum convergence conditions, consensus propagation, lifted chains, accelerated consensus, ADMM). A more direct comparison to modern decentralized accelerated methods (e.g., EXTRA/Exact diffusion/ADMM rate constants) would help position the contribution quantitatively.

Significance
- Conceptual significance is high: it bridges communities (BP/splitting, accelerated consensus, lifting) and provides a reusable analytical tool (auxiliary node-level reduction). This can influence future work on accelerating message passing beyond consensus.
- Practical impact is tempered by the need for global spectral information to tune γ, synchronous operation, and the restriction to quadratics. Discussing distributed or adaptive tuning (e.g., Chebyshev polynomials with estimated bounds, or continuation schemes), and any inherent robustness of Min-Sum Splitting to model mismatch, would increase applicability.
- Compared to prior Min-Sum for consensus that relied on edge-space spectral gaps and provided partial results, the present analysis is more general and yields better, near-optimal rates on geometric graphs.

Pros
- Clear theoretical contribution: first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting; square-root acceleration over diffusive consensus.
- Elegant auxiliary-process reduction from edge- to node-space that exposes equivalence to multi-step methods.
- Thoughtful connections to lifting and momentum that clarify the acceleration mechanism.
- General statement for arbitrary graphs under mild spectral assumptions.

Cons
- Requires global spectral knowledge to set γ; no distributed/adaptive tuning strategy is provided.
- Restricted to quadratic consensus, synchronous updates; no experiments or robustness analysis.
- Possible ambiguity on whether Γ can have nonzero diagonal and how this interacts with W used in practice.
- Main asymptotic rate follows from known shift-register analysis; novelty is chiefly in the mapping and application to Min-Sum Splitting.

Suggestions/questions
- Clarify the diagonal issue for Γ and the class of allowable W in Theorem 1.
- Can the result be extended to time-varying or asynchronous settings, or to strongly convex separable objectives beyond pure averaging?
- Discuss how to choose γ with only local information (e.g., bounds on spectral interval, Chebyshev acceleration with estimates).
- Provide small-scale experiments comparing Min-Sum Splitting vs. Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, and ADMM on rings and grids to illustrate constants and transient behavior.

Overall assessment
This is a solid theoretical paper that advances understanding of Min-Sum Splitting by providing the first convergence-rate guarantees on consensus and by unveiling deep connections to accelerated consensus and lifted chains. Despite practical limitations and lack of experiments, the conceptual contribution and clean analysis merit publication. I recommend acceptance, contingent on clarifying the Γ/W diagonal issue and, if space permits, adding brief empirical illustrations or a discussion of practical tuning. Confidence: high.
