Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting (a reparameterized variant of Min-Sum/GBP due to Ruozzi & Tatikonda) on the consensus/averaging problem. It shows that with an appropriate choice of splitting parameters, the algorithm (i) becomes a linear message-passing process on directed edges; (ii) can be tracked by an auxiliary two-step linear recursion on nodes; and (iii) achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence rates. The core result (Theorem 1) selects δ=1 and Γ=γW (with W symmetric, W1=1, and γ depending on ρ(W)) so that the induced 2n×2n matrix K coincides with the shift-register/Chebyshev acceleration operator known in numerical analysis and accelerated gossip. This yields a square-root improvement in asymptotic convergence time over diffusive methods, e.g., O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori, approaching the O(D log(1/ε)) best-known rates from Nesterov-type methods.

Conceptually, the paper connects three lines: (a) message-passing/BP—where splitting induces directionality; (b) lifted Markov chains—where non-reversibility accelerates mixing; and (c) multi-step first-order methods—where the edge-level directionality becomes a node-level memory term. It improves on prior Min-Sum approaches to consensus (e.g., consensus propagation) by providing convergence rates applicable to general graphs via a node-based auxiliary process rather than analyzing a 2m-dimensional edge process. It relates to accelerated gossip (Chebyshev/shift-register, heavy ball, Nesterov) and to recent views of ADMM as lifting.

Strengths
- Novel synthesis: A clear and, to my knowledge, first bridge between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted non-reversible walks, and multi-step acceleration. The auxiliary node-based process is an elegant analytical tool that reduces the spectral analysis from edges to nodes.
- First rate guarantees for Min-Sum Splitting: Theorem 1 provides explicit asymptotic rates and parameter tuning that match shift-register acceleration, with a clean spectral characterization ρK < ρW and square-root speedup.
- Broad applicability within quadratic consensus: The result holds for arbitrary graphs (symmetric W with W1=1), clarifies when ordinary Min-Sum fails, and shows how splitting fixes both correctness and rate.
- Clarity of high-level narrative and related work coverage, including connections to both lifted chains and accelerated optimization.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practicality of parameter choice: The accelerating γ requires ρ(W), a global quantity. How can γ be estimated/learned in a fully distributed manner? Can the method be made robust to mis-specification? Any adaptive tuning rules?
- Algorithmic delta vs reinterpretation: With δ=1 and Γ=γW, the auxiliary process reduces to a known shift-register method. From an algorithm-design standpoint, is Min-Sum Splitting delivering a new implementable protocol or a new interpretation/analysis of a known accelerated update? Are there regimes where the edge-based Min-Sum formulation is advantageous (e.g., compositional objectives, constraints) beyond quadratic consensus?
- Scope: Results are for quadratic objectives (averaging). How far do they extend to general convex φv or constrained instances (where Min-Sum is often attractive)? Even partial extensions or obstacles would strengthen impact.
- Missing experiments: No empirical validation or comparison with Chebyshev/EXTRA/ADMM/Nesterov-based gossip on representative graphs (cycles, grids, random geometric, expanders). Plots of error vs iterations and sensitivity to γ would make the case more compelling.
- Communication/memory complexity: The Min-Sum process exchanges edge-level messages, whereas the auxiliary two-step node-level method needs only O(1) memory per node. If practitioners can run the auxiliary recursion directly, does Min-Sum Splitting offer any practical benefit? Please discuss per-iteration cost, storage, and robustness (asynchrony, time-varying graphs).
- Assumptions/initialization: Theorem 1 uses R̂0=r̂0=0 and δ=1. How sensitive are guarantees to other initializations and δ≠1? Proposition 1 requires positivity conditions—can these fail in practice?

Quality
- The theoretical development appears sound and well-motivated; the auxiliary-process trick is technically neat. Proofs are deferred; the main statements are precise. However, the lack of experiments and limited discussion of practical tuning leave some claims about competitiveness unsubstantiated.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized and readable. Some notational slips/formatting issues (indices in K̂, superscripts, signs) should be cleaned. Clarify the mapping between [27] and “consensus via Min-Sum” as described (regularity assumptions, initialization at fixed points).

Originality
- High conceptually: first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and a new conceptual bridge to lifting/multi-step methods. Algorithmically, the accelerated rate matches known shift-register behavior; the novelty is in showing Min-Sum Splitting recovers it and in the analysis technique.

Significance
- For communities working on BP/Min-Sum, distributed optimization, and accelerated consensus, this provides a new lens and tools. If extended beyond quadratic consensus, impact could be substantial. Even within averaging, the unification is valuable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide experiments comparing to Metropolis-Hastings, optimal W, Chebyshev/shift-register, Nesterov, EXTRA/ADMM on cycles, grids, random geometric graphs.
- Discuss distributed/online estimation of ρ(W) and robustness to γ mismatch.
- Elaborate on extensions beyond quadratic objectives and on asynchrony/time-varying graphs.
- Clarify communication/memory trade-offs between edge-based Min-Sum and node-based auxiliary updates.

Recommendation: Accept (7/10). The paper offers a novel and insightful connection with a clean theoretical result—exactly the kind of bold conceptual advance I value—even if experiments and broader extensions are left for future work.

Confidence: 0.7
