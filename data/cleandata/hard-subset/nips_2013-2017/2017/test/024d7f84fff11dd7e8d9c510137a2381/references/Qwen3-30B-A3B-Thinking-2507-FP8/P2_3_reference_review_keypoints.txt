- Summary: Analyzes Min-Sum Splitting for distributed consensus and proves a square-root improvement in convergence (O(D log(D/ε)) vs O(D² log(1/ε))) for graphs like cycles and tori.  
- Key insight: Connects Min-Sum Splitting to lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order optimization, unifying accelerated consensus perspectives.  
- Technical novelty: Introduces an auxiliary message-passing scheme enabling spectral analysis and rigorous convergence-rate proofs.  
- Theoretical quality: Convergence analysis is rigorous with clear proofs and careful treatment of prior limitations.  
- Originality: First convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and the first explicit connection to both lifted chains and multi-step methods.  
- Practical significance: Square-root speedup is important for large-scale/geometric networks and suggests broader impact on distributed algorithms.  
- Clarity: Well-written, organized, consistent notation, and a well-motivated auxiliary algorithm (Algorithm 3).  
- Scope limitation: Analysis is restricted to quadratic (network averaging) consensus and does not extend to general/non-quadratic or constrained objectives.  
- Parameter tuning practicality: Acceleration depends on globally-tuned parameters (δ, Γ) tied to spectral graph properties, which is impractical in distributed settings.  
- Lack of empirical validation: No experiments are provided to corroborate theoretical claims or illustrate practical performance.  
- Comparison gap: Does not explicitly compare performance to recent accelerated consensus methods (e.g., [30, 36, 13]).  
- Intellectual honesty: Authors acknowledge global-information tuning limitation but do not propose distributed estimation methods.  
- Overall assessment: High-quality, novel theoretical contribution that meets NIPS standards despite practical limitations.  
- Recommendation: Accept, with suggestion to discuss how to address global information requirement and ideally add empirical comparisons.
