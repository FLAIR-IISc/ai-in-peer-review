

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper introduces a novel approach to accelerating consensus algorithms in distributed networks through a clever modification of the Min-Sum message-passing algorithm, which they term "Min-Sum Splitting." The authors establish theoretical convergence rates for this approach and make a significant connection between message-passing algorithms and two established lines of research in optimization: lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step accelerated methods.

The paper's main contribution is establishing that Min-Sum Splitting, with proper parameter tuning, achieves subdiffusive convergence rates (O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori) compared to the classical diffusive rates (Θ(D² log(1/ε))). This represents a square-root improvement over conventional methods. The key insight is that the splitting parameters introduce directionality in message passing, analogous to the directionality in non-reversible Markov chains, while simultaneously introducing memory terms that relate to multi-step acceleration methods. The authors prove this connection through Theorem 1, which shows that with proper parameter choices, Min-Sum Splitting achieves the same convergence rates as shift-register methods.

The novelty of this work lies in three key aspects: (1) the first proof of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting; (2) the first connection between Min-Sum schemes and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods; and (3) the demonstration that directionality in message passing can be tuned to accelerate convergence. This bridges two previously disconnected areas of research in distributed optimization and message passing, offering a unified perspective on acceleration mechanisms.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a thorough literature review that situates their work within the context of existing consensus algorithms. The theoretical analysis is sound, with the authors carefully establishing the connection between the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm and the auxiliary process that enables their convergence analysis. The paper's focus on the quadratic consensus problem (network averaging) is appropriate for a theoretical contribution, and the authors acknowledge limitations in generalizing to other problem instances.

The significance of this work is substantial. Consensus is a fundamental primitive in distributed systems, and the slow convergence of classical methods on graphs with geometry (like sensor networks) has been a known limitation. This paper provides a theoretically grounded approach to overcoming this limitation with a clean, principled method. The connection to existing acceleration techniques also offers valuable theoretical insights that could inspire further research in distributed optimization.

The paper's main weakness is the narrow scope of its analysis (limited to quadratic objective functions), though this is common for theoretical works establishing new convergence rates. The authors appropriately acknowledge this limitation and suggest generalization to other problems as future work. There are no significant methodological flaws in the presented analysis, and the theoretical contributions are substantial.

What makes this paper particularly compelling for NIPS is its bold theoretical connection between seemingly disparate areas of research (message passing, Markov chains, and accelerated optimization). The authors demonstrate that Min-Sum Splitting isn't just another algorithm but rather a unified framework that incorporates and explains acceleration mechanisms from two established lines of research. This represents a genuine conceptual advance in understanding distributed optimization algorithms.

The paper's contribution is clearly novel compared to recent NIPS work, as it establishes a previously unknown connection between message-passing algorithms and acceleration techniques. While there has been work on accelerated consensus (e.g., lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods), the paper demonstrates how Min-Sum Splitting unifies these approaches under a single framework.

In conclusion, this paper presents a significant theoretical advance with strong novelty. The connection between Min-Sum message passing and acceleration techniques is insightful and well-supported, and the convergence rate improvements are substantial. Despite the narrow scope of the quadratic case, the theoretical contribution is substantial and would likely inspire further research in distributed optimization. The paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel theoretical contribution that NIPS values.

**Recommendation: Accept**
