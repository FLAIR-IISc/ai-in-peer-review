Summary
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting (a reparameterized min-sum/belief propagation scheme) applied to the equality-constrained consensus (averaging) problem on a graph. For quadratic objectives, the authors (i) show that Min-Sum Splitting induces a linear message-passing dynamics on directed edges; (ii) construct an auxiliary node-based, two-step linear system that tracks the evolution of the algorithm; and (iii) prove an asymptotic rate result (Theorem 1) showing a square-root acceleration over diffusive consensus for a specific parameter choice δ = 1 and Γ = γW, with γ tuned using the spectral gap of a symmetric W. In particular, on cycles and tori, the scheme achieves O(D log(D/ε)) iterations to ε-accuracy, compared to Θ(D^2 log(1/ε)) for diffusive methods. The paper also emphasizes conceptual connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step/shift-register accelerated methods.

Relation to prior work
- Classical averaging/consensus and optimal linear iterations (e.g., Metropolis, Chebyshev acceleration, Xiao & Boyd) are extensively studied. Multi-step momentum/shift-register/Nesterov-type accelerations are known to achieve subdiffusive rates and, in some cases, near-optimal O(D log(1/ε)).
- Prior work on Min-Sum for consensus (e.g., Moallemi & Van Roy) showed issues with convergence on loopy graphs and analyzed a soft-constraint variant via a 2m-dimensional linear system on directed edges; rates were characterized only in special cases.
- This paper’s key novelty is to re-derive accelerated rates for consensus specifically through the lens of Min-Sum Splitting, providing the first explicit rate analysis for this variant and a clean auxiliary node-based two-step representation. It also clarifies the analogy between splitting-induced directionality and both lifting and multi-step memory.

Strengths
- A clear theoretical bridge: the auxiliary node-based recursion K(δ,Γ) neatly relates Min-Sum Splitting to known two-step accelerated schemes. This is technically tidy and potentially reusable.
- Theorem 1 rigorously quantifies an accelerated spectral rate ρK that matches the known shift-register behavior and yields O(D log(D/ε)) on geometric graphs, improving upon diffusive consensus.
- The work situates Min-Sum within the broader landscape (lifted chains, accelerated first-order methods, ADMM), which increases conceptual clarity of where splitting “fits.”
- The edge-to-node reduction (Proposition 2) is a useful analytical device, avoiding harder 2m-dimensional spectral analyses on directed edges.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited scope: All results are restricted to quadratic consensus (averaging). There is no extension to general convex objectives or inequality constraints, despite the introductory motivation.
- Practicality and tuning: The accelerated choice Γ = γW requires γ = 2/(1+√(1−ρW^2)), i.e., global spectral information. The paper does not address how to estimate ρW in a distributed/robust way, nor the consequences of mis-specification. This limits deployability.
- Essentially recovers known accelerated behavior: The main rate and matrix K coincide with classical shift-register/Chebyshev-accelerated consensus. The novelty is that this is achieved via Min-Sum Splitting, but the algorithmic advantage over directly running an established accelerated consensus seems limited. The paper is largely a reinterpretation rather than a new algorithm with superior properties.
- No empirical validation: There are no experiments comparing Min-Sum Splitting to Chebyshev, heavy-ball, Nesterov, ADMM, EXTRA, or lifted-chain gossip. Given the reliance on idealized assumptions and global parameter tuning, empirical evidence would be valuable.
- Asymptotic emphasis: The bounds are asymptotic spectral rates. Finite-time, non-asymptotic constants (and dependence on initializations) are not developed. For example, Theorem 1 assumes R̂0 = r̂0 = 0 and relies on an implicit positivity condition; these choices and their robustness need clearer justification.
- Clarity/presentation: The manuscript is dense and occasionally rough (e.g., typos, formatting; K∞’s scaling could be better motivated). Some derivations appear only in the supplement; more intuition in the main text would help.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive is performance to errors in γ? Can you characterize rates under approximate ρW or propose an adaptive/distributed tuning?
2. Can your auxiliary-process technique extend to non-quadratic convex φv or to constraints beyond equality, yielding provable acceleration?
3. How does Min-Sum Splitting compare empirically (iterations, computation, message complexity) to Chebyshev-accelerated consensus, Nesterov, and distributed ADMM on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs?
4. Can you provide finite-time non-asymptotic bounds with explicit constants under your initialization?
5. Please clarify the conditions ensuring positivity/convexity of the beliefs over iterations when starting from R̂0 = 0, and check the scaling in K∞.

Pros
- First rigorous rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting in consensus.
- Conceptually unifies Min-Sum with lifting and multi-step acceleration.
- Clean technical reduction from edge-based to node-based two-step dynamics.
- Achieves provable square-root acceleration on geometric graphs.

Cons
- Narrow scope (quadratic consensus only); no experiments.
- Depends on global spectral information; no adaptive/distributed tuning.
- Largely recovers known accelerated behavior; limited algorithmic novelty/advantage over established methods.
- Asymptotic focus with limited discussion of constants and robustness.

Recommendation
Lean toward rejection. While the theoretical connection is neat and the auxiliary-process analysis is technically sound and of some interest, the contribution feels incremental relative to established accelerated consensus literature. The lack of empirical validation, dependence on global tuning, and restriction to quadratic consensus reduce significance for NeurIPS. Strengthening the paper with experiments, adaptive/robust tuning strategies, and extensions beyond quadratic objectives would improve its case.
