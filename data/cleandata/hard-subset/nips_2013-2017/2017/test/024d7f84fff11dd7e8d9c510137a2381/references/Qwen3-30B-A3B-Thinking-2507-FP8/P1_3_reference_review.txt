

# Peer Review: "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving consensus problems in distributed networks. The authors establish convergence rates for this algorithm, showing that with proper parameter tuning, it achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence rates compared to traditional diffusive methods. Specifically, for cycle graphs and tori, the convergence time scales as O(D log(D/ε)) where D is the graph diameter, which represents a square-root improvement over the standard diffusive rate of O(D² log(1/ε)). 

The paper makes a significant connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two streams of prior work: lifted Markov chain techniques (e.g., [8, 5, 37]) and multi-step accelerated optimization methods (e.g., [13, 24]). The authors show that Min-Sum Splitting can be viewed as a process on a "lifted space" (the space of directed edges) that introduces directionality similar to non-reversible Markov chains, while simultaneously exhibiting memory terms that relate to multi-step optimization methods. This connection had not been established in previous literature.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides the first rigorous convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The authors establish a square-root improvement in convergence rates for consensus problems, which is a substantial theoretical advance.

2. **Novel Connection**: The paper's most important contribution is establishing the connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods. This insight provides a unified perspective on different accelerated consensus approaches and opens new avenues for research.

3. **Technical Rigor**: The proofs are mathematically sound, with a new proof technique involving an auxiliary process on the nodes of the graph. Theorem 1 provides a clear characterization of the convergence rate and its dependence on global graph properties.

4. **Clear Exposition**: The paper is well-organized and presents complex ideas in a logical, accessible manner. The relationship between Min-Sum Splitting and other methods is clearly articulated.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope**: The analysis is restricted to the quadratic consensus problem (network averaging), which is a special case. The authors should more thoroughly discuss how their results might generalize to non-quadratic problems or other constrained optimization settings.

2. **Parameter Tuning Dependency**: The optimal parameters (e.g., γ in Theorem 1) depend on global information about the graph (ρW, the second largest eigenvalue of W), which may be difficult to obtain in large-scale distributed systems without centralized knowledge. The paper should discuss practical implementation challenges.

3. **Lack of Numerical Validation**: Despite the strong theoretical results, the paper lacks numerical experiments to validate the theoretical convergence rates. This is a notable omission for a NIPS paper, as it would help demonstrate practical relevance.

4. **Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: While the paper mentions connections to other methods, it could provide a more detailed comparison with the most recent accelerated consensus algorithms to better establish the relative advantages of Min-Sum Splitting.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs that properly support the claims. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., the need for global information for parameter tuning). The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and addresses the key challenges in the convergence analysis of Min-Sum Splitting. The paper represents a complete piece of work rather than a position paper.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the key insights are well-explained. The paper provides sufficient information for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the results, though the lack of numerical experiments slightly reduces reproducibility.

## Originality Assessment

The paper makes a significant original contribution by establishing the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and by connecting it to two distinct lines of research (lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization). While Min-Sum has been studied previously, the "Splitting" variant and its convergence properties have not been analyzed in depth before. The connection between these different accelerated methods represents a novel theoretical insight.

## Significance Assessment

The results are highly significant for distributed systems, as consensus is a fundamental primitive in many applications including sensor networks, multi-agent coordination, and distributed machine learning. The square-root improvement in convergence rates represents a meaningful practical advancement. The connection to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods provides new theoretical insights that could inspire future research.

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and accelerated consensus methods is novel and insightful, and the convergence rate analysis is rigorous and significant. While the limited scope (quadratic consensus only) and lack of numerical experiments are notable weaknesses, these do not detract from the paper's core theoretical contribution.

The paper would be significantly strengthened with:
1. Numerical experiments validating the theoretical convergence rates
2. A more thorough discussion of generalization to non-quadratic problems
3. A detailed comparison with the most recent accelerated consensus algorithms

Given the paper's strong theoretical contribution and its potential to influence future research in distributed optimization, I recommend acceptance at NIPS.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro:**
- First rigorous convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting
- Novel theoretical connection between Min-Sum and two distinct accelerated methods
- Square-root improvement in convergence rates for important graph topologies
- Well-written and technically sound paper

**Con:**
- Limited to quadratic consensus problem (lacks generalization discussion)
- No numerical experiments to validate theoretical results
- Parameter tuning requires global graph information, which may be impractical

The strengths of this paper substantially outweigh the weaknesses, and I believe it represents a significant contribution to the field that would be valuable to the NIPS community.
