This paper presents a novel analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem, establishing convergence rates and demonstrating a square-root improvement over diffusive methods. The core idea – strategically re-parametrizing the objective function via “splitting” – is a compelling and potentially broadly applicable technique. The connection drawn between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods is particularly insightful and elevates the work beyond a purely algorithmic contribution. The mathematical analysis appears rigorous, building on auxiliary message-passing schemes to provide a tractable framework for convergence proofs.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The primary strength lies in the originality of the approach. While Min-Sum splitting has been previously introduced, this paper provides the *first* analytical convergence guarantees and rates. The connection to shift-register methods and the interplay with lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient descent are particularly novel observations.
*   **Significance:** Achieving a square-root improvement in convergence speed over established diffusive methods is a significant result, especially for large-scale distributed systems. This has practical implications across various domains, including sensor networks, distributed machine learning, and multi-agent coordination.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper's theoretical foundations seem strong, with a clear derivation of convergence rates and a well-defined auxiliary process for analysis.
*   **Clarity:** While dense, the paper is generally well-structured, and the key ideas are presented logically. The extensive background provides sufficient context for understanding the contributions.
*   **Connections to Existing Literature:** The authors do a commendable job of situating their work within the broader landscape of distributed optimization and consensus algorithms, drawing connections to relevant work on lifting, accelerated methods, and message passing.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks any experimental validation. While the theoretical results are compelling, it would be beneficial to see simulations demonstrating the performance gains in practice, even on relatively simple graph topologies. Demonstrating the dependence of convergence on graph diameter and comparing it against established methods would strengthen the paper significantly.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The optimal choice of splitting parameters (δ and Γ) is crucial for achieving the accelerated rates, and it depends on the spectral properties of the graph (specifically, the second largest eigenvalue of a related matrix). The practical implications of estimating these parameters in real-world scenarios are not discussed.
*   **Scope of Applicability:** The analysis is focused specifically on the consensus problem with a quadratic objective function. The extent to which these results can be generalized to other optimization problems or objective function classes is unclear.
*   **Dense Notation:** While generally clear, the mathematical notation is quite dense and can be challenging to follow at times. A more liberal use of explanatory text alongside the equations could improve readability.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a fundamentally novel and theoretically sound approach to accelerating consensus algorithms.
*   The convergence guarantees and the demonstrated square-root speedup are significant contributions to the field.
*   The connections to existing literature in lifting and accelerated optimization provide valuable insights.
*   The paper opens up new avenues for research in Min-Sum-based algorithms and distributed optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical validation weakens the practical impact of the results.
*   The parameter sensitivity and the need for spectral knowledge might limit the applicability of the algorithm in certain scenarios.
*   The focus on a specific problem instance (consensus with a quadratic objective) may limit the broader relevance of the work.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly lean towards accepting this paper. Despite the lack of empirical validation and some concerns regarding parameter sensitivity, the novelty and theoretical significance of the work are compelling. The paper introduces a new perspective on Min-Sum splitting and provides a solid foundation for future research. The insights regarding the connection to other acceleration techniques are particularly valuable. The absence of experiments is a drawback, but I believe the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance, particularly given NIPS’s tendency to favor novel ideas over methodological completeness. I would encourage the authors to prioritize experimental validation as a follow-up to this work.



**Rating:** 8.5/10 (Strong Accept)
