- Studies Min-Sum Splitting for consensus, showing messages remain quadratic and reduce to a linear edge-based iteration.
- Constructs an exact auxiliary node-based two-step linear iteration tracking edge dynamics.
- Proves a parameter choice (δ=1, Γ=γW with γ tuned from W’s spectrum) yields shift-register/Chebyshev convergence, giving square-root acceleration over diffusive methods.
- Shows on grids/tori convergence time scales as O(D log(D/ε)) vs O(D^2 log(1/ε)) for diffusive consensus.
- Theorem 1 and Propositions 1–2 are technically sound and derive clean spectral-radius bounds.
- Assumptions include quadratics, synchronous updates, fixed graph, symmetric W with W1=1, and a specific initialization (R̂0 = r̂0 = 0).
- Method requires global spectral information (ρ(W)) to select γ.
- Lacks empirical validation and does not address robustness to asynchrony, noise, time-variation, or quantization.
- Potential inconsistency/ambiguity about sparsity: whether Γ (and W) may have nonzero diagonals and how neighbor-sum operations handle Γvv.
- Paper is well organized with clear motivation and positioning relative to diffusive consensus, lifted chains, and momentum methods.
- Notational density is high; a symbol table or reminders would help and some equation indexing needs checking.
- A short proof sketch of Theorem 1 in the main text would improve readability.
- Novelty: first convergence-rate guarantees for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus and a new auxiliary-process proof technique.
- Rate itself matches known Chebyshev/shift-register results; novelty lies in showing Min-Sum Splitting attains this via the mapping to multi-step methods.
- Related work coverage is good but should include more direct quantitative comparisons to modern decentralized accelerated methods (e.g., EXTRA, Exact diffusion, ADMM).
- Conceptually significant for bridging BP/splitting, accelerated consensus, and lifting; provides a reusable analytical tool.
- Practical impact is limited by global tuning requirement, synchrony assumption, and restriction to quadratics; adaptive/distributed tuning not addressed.
- Pros: clear theoretical contribution, elegant reduction to node-space, insightful connections to lifting/momentum, general applicability across graphs.
- Cons: needs global spectral knowledge, restricted problem setting, no experiments or robustness analysis, ambiguity on Γ diagonal handling.
- Suggested clarifications/extensions: clarify Γ diagonal issue and allowable W, consider extensions to time-varying/asynchronous or strongly convex separable objectives, discuss distributed/adaptive γ selection, and add small-scale experiments on rings/grids comparing to Metropolis/Chebyshev/ADMM.
- Overall: solid theoretical advance that merits acceptance pending clarification of the Γ/W diagonal issue and, if possible, brief empirical illustrations or practical tuning discussion.
