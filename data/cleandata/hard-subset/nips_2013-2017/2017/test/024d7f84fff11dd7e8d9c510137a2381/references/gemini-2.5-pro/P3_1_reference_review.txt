**Summary**

This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for the distributed consensus problem, specifically network averaging. The authors demonstrate that this message-passing framework, which is a generalization of the standard Min-Sum algorithm, can achieve accelerated convergence rates. The key contributions are threefold. First, the paper establishes a novel and insightful connection between Min-Sum schemes, lifted Markov chain methods, and multi-step accelerated first-order methods from convex optimization. Second, it provides the first formal convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. The main analytical tool is a clever reduction of the algorithm's dynamics on the graph's directed edges to an equivalent, more tractable "auxiliary" multi-step linear process on the graph's nodes. Third, by appropriately tuning the algorithm's "splitting" parameters, the authors show that the resulting process recovers the dynamics of shift-register methods, achieving a convergence time of O(D log(D/ε)) on graphs like cycles and tori. This constitutes a square-root improvement over standard "diffusive" consensus algorithms and improves upon prior Min-Sum-based approaches for consensus.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance of Connections:** The paper's primary strength lies in the conceptual bridge it builds between three seemingly disparate areas: Belief Propagation (Min-Sum), non-reversible Markov chains (liftings), and accelerated optimization (multi-step methods). It elegantly shows that the "splitting" reparametrization in Min-Sum acts as a mechanism to introduce momentum or directionality, which is precisely the principle behind acceleration in the other two fields. This is a fundamental insight that could inspire further research into accelerating other message-passing algorithms.

2.  **Technical Quality and Rigor:** The theoretical analysis is sound and well-executed. The introduction of the auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) is a powerful and elegant proof technique. It successfully transforms the analysis of a complex linear system on 2m variables (on the edges) into a well-studied 2n-variable system on the nodes, which directly maps to the state-space representation of accelerated linear iterations. Theorem 1 provides a clear, quantitative result, rigorously establishing the accelerated convergence rate and its dependence on the graph's spectral properties.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. It provides a comprehensive yet concise review of the relevant background on consensus, diffusive methods, and various acceleration techniques. The narrative flows logically, starting from the general Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, specializing it to consensus, introducing the auxiliary process for analysis, and culminating in the main theorem on accelerated rates. The contributions are clearly articulated and well-supported by the analysis.

4.  **Advancement over Prior Work:** The work significantly advances the understanding of Min-Sum for consensus. While Moallemi and Van Roy [27] showed that ordinary Min-Sum fails for graphs with cycles and proposed a modified version, their analysis was limited to regular graphs and provided slower rates (or conjectures) than what is established here. This paper demonstrates that the splitting framework is not just a fix, but a powerful tool for acceleration, providing a general analysis and achieving near-optimal rates.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Practicality of Parameter Selection:** The accelerated rate demonstrated in Theorem 1 hinges on a specific choice of the parameter γ, which depends on ρW, the spectral radius of the normalized consensus matrix W. This ρW is a global property of the graph, and computing it can be as challenging as the consensus problem itself. While this is a common requirement for many accelerated distributed methods, the paper would benefit from a discussion on the practical implications. How sensitive is the algorithm to a suboptimal γ? Could γ be adapted or learned in a distributed fashion?

2.  **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is focused on a specific parameter setting (δ = 1, Γ = γW) that recovers the shift-register method. It remains an open question how other choices of the splitting parameters δ and Γ might perform. This choice, while fruitful, may not be the only way to achieve acceleration within this framework. A brief discussion on the potential role of δ or other forms of Γ could broaden the paper's scope.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contribution is strong and stands on its own, the inclusion of even simple numerical experiments would have significantly strengthened the claims. Simulations on a cycle graph or a 2D torus comparing the convergence of Min-Sum Splitting against a diffusive method (e.g., using WMH) and the method from [27] would provide a compelling visual confirmation of the O(D) vs. O(D²) scaling difference promised by the theory.

**Recommendation**

I strongly recommend acceptance. This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the fields of distributed optimization and message-passing algorithms. The novel connection it establishes between Belief Propagation and accelerated optimization is a major conceptual advance. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, elegant, and leads to strong, state-of-the-art results for the consensus problem. While the weaknesses noted above (practicality of parameter tuning and lack of experiments) are valid points for improvement, they do not detract from the core scientific merit of the work. They are better viewed as interesting avenues for future research that this paper opens up. This work is likely to be influential and to stimulate further investigation into the design and analysis of accelerated message-passing algorithms.
