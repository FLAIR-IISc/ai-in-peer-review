

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper proposes "Min-Sum Splitting" as an accelerated method for solving consensus problems in distributed optimization. The authors claim to establish convergence rates for their method that outperform standard diffusive approaches, achieving a square-root improvement in convergence time (O(D log(D/ε)) vs. O(D² log(1/ε)) for cycle and torus graphs). They position their work as connecting Min-Sum schemes to two established lines of research: lifted Markov chains and multi-step accelerated methods in convex optimization.

The paper makes several theoretical contributions, including establishing the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, introducing an auxiliary message-passing scheme to analyze convergence, and showing that their method achieves rates matching shift-register methods. The theoretical analysis is mathematically rigorous, with Theorem 1 providing a clear connection between Min-Sum Splitting and existing accelerated methods.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's quality and significance. While the theoretical analysis is technically sound, the paper lacks essential empirical validation. The authors focus exclusively on the quadratic consensus problem (network averaging) and provide no experimental results demonstrating that their method actually converges faster than established baselines in practice. This is particularly concerning given the authors' claim of "accelerated" convergence. Without empirical evidence, it's impossible to assess whether the theoretical improvements translate to practical benefits.

The paper also makes a critical assumption that the optimal parameter choice (γ in Theorem 1) depends on global information about the graph (specifically, the spectral properties of a matrix W). In distributed optimization settings, nodes typically have only local information about the graph structure, not global spectral properties. The paper fails to address how this global information would be obtained in practice, which severely limits the practical applicability of the proposed method.

Furthermore, the paper's connection to previous work is somewhat superficial. While they cite [27] as having investigated Min-Sum for consensus, they don't adequately compare their method to the state-of-the-art accelerated methods for consensus (e.g., those in [13, 24, 30]). The paper claims to outperform [27], but doesn't establish how their method compares to more recent and effective accelerated approaches.

The focus on quadratic objectives is another limitation. Consensus problems in machine learning often involve non-quadratic objectives (e.g., logistic regression, support vector machines). The paper doesn't address how their method would generalize to these more common cases, which significantly limits its relevance to practical machine learning applications.

I'm also concerned about the paper's significance for NIPS. While distributed optimization is related to machine learning, the paper doesn't clearly connect its theoretical results to machine learning applications. The consensus problem is important in distributed learning, but the paper doesn't demonstrate how their method would improve distributed machine learning algorithms in practice.

The paper's strengths lie in its theoretical rigor and clear mathematical presentation. The auxiliary message-passing scheme introduced in Section 6 is a novel technical contribution that could be useful for analyzing other message-passing algorithms. The paper also makes a reasonable effort to situate its work within the broader literature on accelerated consensus methods.

However, these strengths are overshadowed by the lack of empirical validation, the restrictive assumption of quadratic objectives, and the impractical requirement for global graph information. The paper presents a theoretically interesting extension of Min-Sum, but without evidence that it provides practical advantages over existing methods, it doesn't advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

I would have been more convinced if the authors had:
1. Provided experimental results comparing Min-Sum Splitting to state-of-the-art accelerated methods on real-world distributed optimization tasks
2. Demonstrated how their method could be adapted to non-quadratic consensus problems
3. Addressed how to obtain the global graph information required for optimal parameter tuning in distributed settings

Without these elements, the paper remains a theoretical curiosity rather than a substantial contribution to the field. The authors have established a connection between Min-Sum schemes and accelerated methods, but they haven't provided sufficient evidence to support the practical value of this connection.

In conclusion, while the paper makes a technically sound theoretical contribution, it lacks the empirical validation and practical relevance necessary for acceptance at NIPS. The theoretical results are interesting but don't demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods in practice. The paper would benefit from significantly more experimental work to validate their claims before it could be considered for publication.
