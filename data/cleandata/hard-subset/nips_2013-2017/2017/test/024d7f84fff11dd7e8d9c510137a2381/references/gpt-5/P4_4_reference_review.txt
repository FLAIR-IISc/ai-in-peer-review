Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting (a reparameterized form of Min-Sum/Belief Propagation) for the consensus (network averaging) problem on a graph. The core contributions are:

- For quadratic consensus, the Min-Sum Splitting updates reduce to a linear message-passing process on directed edges (Algorithm 2), parameterized by δ and a symmetric weight matrix Γ respecting the graph.
- The authors construct an auxiliary linear process on nodes (Algorithm 3) that exactly tracks the algorithm’s output for appropriate initialization. This node-based process is a two-step linear recursion with memory, governed by a 2n×2n block matrix K(δ,Γ).
- With a specific choice δ=1 and Γ=γW, where W is any symmetric averaging matrix with W1=1 and ρ(W−11ᵀ/n)<1, and γ=2/(1+√(1−ρ(W−11ᵀ/n)²)), they prove convergence to the average at a rate corresponding to the second eigenvalue of a “shift-register” matrix K (Theorem 1). This yields an asymptotic square-root improvement over diffusive methods: 1/(1−ρ_K)=Θ(√(1/(1−ρ_W))).
- For cycles and tori, this implies O(D log(D/ε)) iterations to reach error ε (in ℓ2), improving over O(D² log(1/ε)) of reversible random-walk-based consensus. This is close to the O(D log(1/ε)) optimal rate achieved by certain accelerated first-order methods (e.g., Nesterov-type) and lifted Markov chains in specific settings.

The work connects Min-Sum Splitting to two acceleration paradigms: (i) lifted non-reversible Markov chains (directionality on edges), and (ii) multi-step/shift-register methods in convex optimization (memory on nodes). Prior work [27] analyzed Min-Sum variants for consensus and characterized convergence via edge-based chains in regular graphs, with O(D) mixing on cycles. The present paper provides a general spectral-rate analysis and accelerated rates via parameter tuning. It also complements connections between distributed optimization and acceleration (e.g., [13, 24, 36]) and relates to lifting for ADMM-like methods [12].

Quality (technical soundness)
- The linearization for quadratic objectives is standard, and the derivations of Algorithms 2 and 3 are consistent with Gaussian BP/Min-Sum literature. Propositions 1–2 formalize the reduction and the equivalence between edge- and node-based schemes under specific initializations.
- Theorem 1’s rate matches the classical Chebyshev/second-order acceleration (shift-register) spectrum: ρ_K=√((1−√(1−ρ_W²))/(1+√(1−ρ_W²)))<ρ_W, with square-root improvement in the convergence time. The bound on finite-time error leverages spectral norms of K−K_∞ and matches known analyses for two-step methods.
- Assumptions (symmetric W, W1=1, spectral radius <1, synchronous updates) are clear. The tuning depends on ρ_W, a global spectral quantity, aligning with known requirements for optimal acceleration.
- Proofs are deferred to the supplement; the main statements are plausible and align with established theory.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized: background on consensus, diffusive vs accelerated methods, then specialization of Min-Sum Splitting, auxiliary process, and the main theorem.
- Some presentation issues: minor typographical glitches, footnotes are awkwardly interleaved, and a stray “st Conference…” line appears. The definition of K̂ (eq. (4)) and K(δ,Γ) (eq. (5)) could be streamlined. Notation for norms and spectral radius is clear.
- The relation between Algorithm 2 (edge space) and Algorithm 3 (node space) is a strong expository device.

Originality
- To the best of the provided context, this is the first analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting and the first to show accelerated (subdiffusive) rates for consensus via Min-Sum with tuned splitting parameters.
- The explicit connection to both lifting and multi-step acceleration within the Min-Sum framework appears novel. Prior work linked ADMM and lifting [12] and studied accelerated consensus via multi-step methods [13, 24, 36]; here, the contribution is to realize comparable acceleration in Min-Sum Splitting and formalize the equivalences.

Significance
- The results suggest Min-Sum Splitting can achieve near-optimal consensus rates on geometric graphs and provide a unifying view linking message passing, lifting, and multi-step acceleration.
- Practical impact is somewhat constrained by the need for global parameter tuning (ρ_W), synchronous operation, and restriction to quadratic consensus in the main theorem. Nevertheless, the theoretical insight is valuable and could inform extensions to broader classes of objectives and algorithms.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical advance: spectral-rate analysis yielding square-root acceleration for Min-Sum Splitting on arbitrary graphs (via W).
- Elegant auxiliary node-based reformulation that simplifies analysis and connects to known accelerated methods.
- Situates the result within two previously distinct acceleration paradigms.

Weaknesses
- Scope limited to quadratic consensus; extension to general convex φ_v or constraints is not treated.
- Parameter γ requires global spectral information; no adaptive/distributed tuning or robustness analysis is provided.
- No empirical evaluation to illuminate constants, transient behavior, or compare against ADMM/EXTRA/accelerated gossip.
- Some clarity/polish issues in presentation; a few conditions (e.g., positivity in Proposition 1) are stated but not thoroughly discussed for general initializations.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide numerical experiments on representative graphs (cycle, grid, random geometric, expander), comparing convergence and constants with Metropolis, optimized W, shift-register/Nesterov, ADMM/EXTRA, and lifted chains.
- Discuss practical choices of W and how to estimate/tune γ in a distributed manner (e.g., spectral gap estimation, diameter-based heuristics).
- Clarify initialization requirements, robustness to inexact messages/noise, and behavior under asynchronous or time-varying graphs.
- Consider generalizing the analysis to strongly convex smooth objectives or constrained formulations beyond averaging.

Arguments for acceptance
- Introduces and rigorously analyzes an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting scheme for consensus with provable square-root speedup.
- Bridges message-passing, lifting, and multi-step optimization in a principled way.
- Provides a general spectral framework applicable to arbitrary graphs and connects rates to well-understood accelerated matrices.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited problem class (quadratic consensus) and no experiments.
- Reliance on globally tuned parameters may limit deployability; no adaptive strategy is provided.
- Some presentation polish needed; proofs not included in main text.

Overall, the paper offers a solid theoretical contribution that advances understanding of accelerated message passing for consensus and is relevant to the NIPS community interested in distributed optimization and graphical models.
