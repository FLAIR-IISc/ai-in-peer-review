This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for the distributed consensus problem. The consensus problem, particularly in its network averaging form, is a fundamental primitive in distributed computing and machine learning. Standard distributed algorithms based on linear iterations often exhibit slow, "diffusive" convergence rates, especially on graphs with high diameter like cycles or grids. While the general Min-Sum Splitting algorithm was previously proposed as a generalization of Min-Sum that can ensure convergence on loopy graphs, its convergence *rate* has remained an open question.

The authors' central contribution is to provide the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting applied to the network averaging problem. They demonstrate that by appropriately setting the algorithm's "splitting" parameters (δ and Γ), one can achieve an accelerated, or "subdiffusive," rate of convergence. The key technical innovation is the introduction of an auxiliary message-passing scheme that runs on the nodes of the graph. This auxiliary process tracks the output of the original Min-Sum Splitting algorithm (which operates on the directed edges) and recasts its dynamics as a multi-step linear system on the nodes. This reformulation simplifies the spectral analysis significantly. The authors show that a specific choice of parameters recovers the dynamics of shift-register methods, a known technique for accelerating linear solvers. This allows them to leverage existing spectral analysis to prove that Min-Sum Splitting achieves an asymptotic convergence time of O(D log(D/ε)) on topologies like cycles and tori, which represents a square-root improvement over the O(D^2 log(1/ε)) time of diffusive methods. A further significant contribution is the conceptual bridge the paper builds between Min-Sum schemes and two other families of accelerated algorithms: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Conceptual Contribution:** The paper's most significant strength is the novel and insightful connection it establishes between Min-Sum message-passing, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order optimization methods. By showing that the "splitting" parameters can be tuned to introduce momentum-like terms, the authors provide a new perspective on how message-passing algorithms can achieve acceleration. This unification of ideas from previously disparate research areas is a valuable contribution to the field.

2.  **Technical Quality and Novelty:** The analysis is technically sound and elegant. The introduction of the auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) to analyze the edge-based dynamics of Min-Sum Splitting (Algorithm 2) is a clever and effective technique. It successfully bypasses the difficulties of directly analyzing the larger, more complex system on the edges, a problem that limited prior work [27]. By relating the dynamics to the well-studied shift-register methods, the paper builds upon a solid theoretical foundation.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and logically structured. The introduction and background sections (Sections 1-4) do an excellent job of motivating the problem, reviewing relevant literature from multiple domains, and positioning the paper's contributions. The progression from the general algorithm to the specific analysis is easy to follow.

4.  **Significance and Impact:** The results are significant. The paper demonstrates that a general-purpose, belief-propagation-style framework can be tuned to be competitive with specialized, state-of-the-art algorithms for a fundamental distributed problem. This elevates the status of Min-Sum schemes and suggests a promising avenue for accelerating other distributed optimization tasks where Min-Sum is applicable. The findings are likely to be of interest to researchers in distributed optimization, machine learning, and graphical models.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the absence of any numerical experiments is a notable weakness. The analysis focuses on asymptotic convergence rates, but experiments would be crucial to:
    *   Verify that the asymptotic speed-up translates to meaningful gains in finite time.
    *   Compare the performance against other methods (e.g., standard gossip, Nesterov-accelerated methods) on various graph topologies (cycles, grids, random graphs).
    *   Illustrate the algorithm's behavior and provide a more intuitive understanding of the results.

2.  **Practicality of Parameter Tuning:** The accelerated rate is achieved by setting the parameter Γ based on a matrix W and a scalar γ, where γ depends on ρW, the spectral radius of W minus the rank-one projector onto the consensus subspace. This quantity is a global property of the graph, related to its spectral gap. While the need for global information is common among accelerated methods, the paper would benefit from a discussion on the practical implications. How sensitive is the algorithm to an imperfect estimate of ρW? Can it be estimated efficiently in a distributed manner? Addressing this would strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Inclusion of Experiments:** Even a small set of experiments on canonical graphs (e.g., a path graph, a 2D grid) would substantially strengthen the paper. A plot showing the error `||x_t - b_bar*1||` versus iteration `t` for Min-Sum Splitting, a standard diffusive method (e.g., using W), and perhaps another accelerated method would provide compelling evidence for the theoretical claims.

*   **Discussion on Parameter Selection:** I suggest adding a brief paragraph in Section 7 or 8 discussing the challenge of selecting γ. Acknowledging that this requires global information and briefly mentioning potential approaches for its estimation (e.g., via power iteration) or the impact of its sub-optimal choice would make the work more complete.

*   **Explicit Comparison to [27]:** The paper rightly positions its results as an improvement over Moallemi and Van Roy [27]. This could be made even more striking. For instance, for cycle graphs, [27] achieved a convergence time of O(D/ε), whereas this work achieves O(D log(D/ε)). Explicitly stating that the improvement lies in replacing a polynomial dependence on `1/ε` with a logarithmic one would more clearly highlight the magnitude of the advancement.

### Summary and Recommendation

Overall, this is an excellent paper with significant theoretical contributions. It provides the first rate-of-convergence analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on the consensus problem and, more importantly, uncovers a deep and elegant connection between message-passing schemes and established acceleration techniques from optimization and random walks. The technical approach is novel and the writing is superb. The primary weakness is the lack of experiments to support the theory. However, the strength and novelty of the theoretical and conceptual contributions are high enough to warrant publication at NeurIPS. The suggestions above are intended to further strengthen an already strong manuscript.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Highly original conceptual contribution linking Min-Sum to other acceleration paradigms.
*   First convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, solving an open problem.
*   Technically strong and elegant proof technique using an auxiliary process.
*   Exceptional clarity in presentation and writing.
*   Significant potential impact on the design of distributed algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   Complete lack of empirical validation for the theoretical results.
*   Practical aspects of parameter tuning are not discussed.

**Decision:** Accept.
