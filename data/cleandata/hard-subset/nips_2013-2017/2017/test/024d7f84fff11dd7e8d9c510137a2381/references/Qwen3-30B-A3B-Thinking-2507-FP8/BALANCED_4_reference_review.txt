

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving the consensus problem, establishing novel convergence rates and connecting it to two distinct lines of research: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods. The authors show that Min-Sum Splitting achieves a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods, scaling as O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori (where D is the graph diameter), compared to the O(D² log(1/ε)) scaling of classical methods. The paper establishes the first rigorous convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, which is significant given that previous work [27] only conjectured convergence rates for specific graph topologies.

**Strengths:**
1. **Novel theoretical contribution:** The paper provides the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, filling a significant gap in the literature. The authors establish a clear connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two distinct acceleration techniques (lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods), which is a valuable insight that wasn't previously recognized.

2. **Elegant proof technique:** The introduction of an auxiliary process supported on the nodes (rather than directed edges) to track convergence is a clever and novel analytical approach that simplifies the analysis compared to previous work [27].

3. **Clear theoretical results:** Theorem 1 provides precise convergence guarantees with explicit bounds, demonstrating a meaningful square-root improvement over diffusive methods. The connection to shift-register methods in numerical analysis (via the matrix K) is particularly compelling.

4. **Well-organized and well-written:** The paper is clearly structured, with a thorough introduction that effectively contextualizes the work within the broader literature on distributed consensus.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of empirical validation:** The paper is purely theoretical and lacks any experimental results to support the theoretical claims. While the analysis is rigorous, some empirical validation on specific graph topologies (e.g., cycle graphs, tori) would significantly strengthen the contribution and make the results more tangible for practitioners.

2. **Practical implementation concerns:** The optimal parameter choice (δ=1, Γ=γW with γ depending on ρW) requires global information about the graph (the spectral gap ρW), which is not readily available in distributed settings. The paper does not address how to compute this information in a distributed manner, which is a significant practical limitation.

3. **Limited scope:** The analysis is restricted to the quadratic case (network averaging), which is a specific instance of the consensus problem. The authors should discuss how their results might extend to more general objective functions.

4. **Comparison with existing methods:** While the paper establishes connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods, it doesn't directly compare the practical performance of Min-Sum Splitting with these other accelerated methods on specific graph topologies.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the analysis of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus problems. The novel connection between Min-Sum message-passing and two distinct acceleration techniques is particularly insightful, and the convergence rate analysis provides a meaningful improvement over previous work.

However, the lack of empirical validation and the practical limitation of requiring global information for optimal parameter tuning are significant drawbacks. The paper would be substantially strengthened by adding experimental results demonstrating the theoretical convergence rates on specific graph topologies, and by discussing how to compute the required global information in a distributed manner (e.g., through a pre-processing phase or approximate methods).

The paper is technically sound and makes a significant theoretical contribution, but the limited scope (quadratic case only) and absence of empirical validation reduce its significance for the NIPS community. I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses in the camera-ready version, particularly by adding empirical results and discussing practical implementation considerations.

**Suggestions for improvement:**
1. Add experimental results comparing convergence rates of Min-Sum Splitting with classical diffusive methods and other accelerated approaches on specific graph topologies (cycles, tori).
2. Discuss how to approximate the required global information (ρW) in a distributed manner, or explore parameter choices that don't require global information.
3. Extend the analysis to more general objective functions beyond quadratic (network averaging).
4. Include a more detailed comparison with existing accelerated methods (lifted Markov chains, multi-step methods) in terms of both theoretical convergence rates and practical implementation complexity.
