## Peer Review for "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors establish that, with a specific parameter tuning, Min-Sum Splitting achieves a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods for graphs with geometry (cycles and tori), approaching the optimal rate for consensus. They cleverly connect Min-Sum splitting to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, demonstrating a unifying perspective on accelerated convergence in consensus problems. The key technical contribution is an auxiliary message-passing scheme on the nodes of the graph that allows for a tractable analysis of the algorithm’s convergence.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, establishing clear convergence rates and demonstrating acceleration compared to standard diffusive approaches. The theorem regarding convergence rate is well-defined and provides valuable insights.
* **Novel Connections:** The authors successfully bridge the gap between Min-Sum schemes, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods, offering a unifying view on acceleration techniques for consensus. This is a significant conceptual contribution.
* **Clear Exposition (Mostly):** The paper is generally well-structured and logically flows from introduction to theoretical results.  The motivation for using splitting and its connection to existing work are clearly presented.
* **Practical Relevance:** Consensus algorithms are fundamental in distributed systems and machine learning, making improvements to their convergence rates highly impactful.
* **Well-Defined Auxiliary Problem:** Introducing the auxiliary message-passing scheme is a clever analytical tool. It simplifies the analysis by moving the dynamics to the nodes rather than edges, and allows for a direct connection to existing theory.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Dense and Technical:** The paper is quite dense and requires a strong background in optimization, graph theory, and message-passing algorithms to fully understand.  Certain sections (e.g., the definition of K and K∞) could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper focuses heavily on theoretical analysis and lacks empirical validation.  While rigorous theoretical results are valuable, demonstrating the performance gains on real-world or simulated graphs would strengthen the paper significantly. It would be beneficial to see how the proposed algorithm performs compared to other state-of-the-art consensus algorithms on various graph topologies.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the paper identifies a specific parameter tuning (δ=1, Γ=γW) that yields accelerated convergence, it doesn’t explore the robustness of this tuning to changes in the graph structure or noise.  A discussion of parameter sensitivity and potential adaptation strategies would be valuable.
* **Repetitive Background:** The introduction and sections 3 & 4 provide a lengthy review of related work. While contextualization is important, these sections feel somewhat redundant and could be streamlined to focus more on the unique contributions of the paper.
* **Notational Overhead:** The paper utilizes a large number of symbols and notations, which can sometimes make it difficult to follow the mathematical derivations.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound and provides a rigorous analysis of a challenging problem. The theoretical results are well-supported, and the choice of methods is justified. However, the lack of empirical validation slightly detracts from the overall quality.
* **Clarity (6/10):** While logically structured, the paper suffers from density and technical jargon.  Improved explanations and more intuitive definitions of key concepts would enhance clarity.  The large amount of notation makes it harder to follow the derivations.
* **Originality (9/10):** The combination of techniques and the unifying perspective on acceleration are highly original.  The connection between Min-Sum splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods is a novel contribution.
* **Significance (8/10):** Accelerated consensus algorithms are important for a wide range of applications.  The theoretical results presented in this paper have the potential to significantly impact the design and analysis of distributed systems and machine learning algorithms.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical results demonstrating accelerated convergence.
* Novel connections between different acceleration techniques.
* Addresses a fundamental problem in distributed systems.
* Clever analytical tool in the form of the auxiliary message-passing scheme.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of empirical validation.
* Dense and technical writing style.
* Limited discussion of parameter sensitivity.
* Repetitive background material.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of distributed consensus. While the lack of empirical validation and the density of the writing style are weaknesses, the originality and strength of the theoretical results outweigh these concerns.  I suggest the authors focus on:

1. **Adding Empirical Results:**  Include simulations or experiments demonstrating the performance gains on various graph topologies.
2. **Improving Clarity:**  Streamline the exposition, providing more intuitive explanations of key concepts and reducing the notational overhead.
3. **Discussing Parameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the robustness of the proposed parameter tuning and explore potential adaptation strategies.
4. **Streamlining Background:** Reduce redundancy in the related work sections.
