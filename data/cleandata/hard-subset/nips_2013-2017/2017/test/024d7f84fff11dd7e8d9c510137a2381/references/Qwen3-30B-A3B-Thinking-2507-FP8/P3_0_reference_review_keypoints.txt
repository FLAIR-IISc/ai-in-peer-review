- Studies Min-Sum Splitting for consensus (network averaging) and shows proper tuning of splitting parameters yields accelerated convergence.
- Main theoretical claim: square-root improvement in convergence time—O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles/tori versus O(D² log(1/ε)) for diffusive methods.
- Establishes connections between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chain techniques, and multi-step gradient acceleration.
- Introduces an auxiliary node-based message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) to reduce edge-based dynamics to a spectral analysis of a matrix K.
- Generalizes and improves on prior work [27] by providing convergence-rate analysis for arbitrary graph topologies.

- Provides the first rigorous convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and proves a substantial theoretical speedup (Theorem 1).
- Formally unifies Min-Sum Splitting with two major acceleration paradigms, offering insightful theoretical connections.
- Technical innovation: auxiliary message-passing simplifies analysis and makes spectral characterization tractable.
- Clearly positions contributions relative to prior literature, especially [27], and explains extensions.
- Paper is well-organized with precise and consistent mathematical presentation.

- Analysis is limited to the quadratic (network averaging) case and does not address extension to general convex or non-quadratic problems.
- Optimal parameter choice (γ = 2/(1 + √(1-ρ²_W))) requires global spectral knowledge (ρ_W), which is impractical in distributed settings.
- No empirical evaluation or experiments to demonstrate practical convergence speedups on real or synthetic networks.
- Lacks direct quantitative comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art accelerated methods (e.g., [36], [13]).
- Assumes simple undirected graphs and does not discuss extensions to directed graphs or self-loops common in practice.

- Theoretical analysis and proofs appear sound; Algorithm 3 is an elegant technical contribution.
- Relating convergence to spectral properties of K is appropriate, but the rationale for the specific parameter choice γ could be explained more thoroughly in terms of eigenstructure.

- Significance: substantial theoretical contribution to distributed optimization with potential practical impact if extended and validated.
- Practical impact would be strengthened by experiments, extensions to non-quadratic problems, and addressing parameter-estimation in distributed settings.

- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions (theoretical novelty outweighs lack of experiments and limited scope).

- Suggestion: add discussion on potential extensions to non-quadratic consensus problems as future work.
- Suggestion: include small experiments (e.g., cycles and tori) to empirically demonstrate convergence speedup.
- Suggestion: discuss how γ might be estimated or approximated in a distributed manner.
- Suggestion: clarify quantitative comparison with recent state-of-the-art accelerated consensus methods.
- Suggestion: comment on adapting the method to directed graphs or graphs with self-loops.

- Final verdict: strong theoretical paper; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
