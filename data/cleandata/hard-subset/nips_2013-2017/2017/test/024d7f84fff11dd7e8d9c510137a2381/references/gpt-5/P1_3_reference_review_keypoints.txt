- Paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for equality-constrained quadratic consensus and shows it can yield accelerated convergence versus standard diffusive consensus.  
- Main contribution: specialization of Min-Sum Splitting giving linear edge-message updates (Algorithm 2) parameterized by δ and Γ.  
- Main contribution: construction of a node-based 2n×2n linear multi-step auxiliary process (Algorithm 3) that exactly tracks the edge dynamics.  
- Main contribution: specific parameter choice (δ=1, Γ=γW with γ tuned to W’s spectral radius) yields accelerated spectral factor ρ_K and Õ(D) convergence on cycles/tori, a square-root improvement over diffusive O(D^2).  
- Conceptual insight: splitting induces directionality similar to non-reversible lifted Markov chains and yields a two-step node method related to shift-register/Polyak/Nesterov acceleration.  
- Relation to prior work: connects and formalizes links between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted non-reversible chains, and multi-step/Chebyshev acceleration; provides first general-graph rate guarantees for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus.  
- Theory quality: core results are well-motivated, technically sound, and align with known second-order/shift-register consensus analyses.  
- Completeness concern: proofs are in supplement but the paper lacks empirical validation and robustness/asynchrony analysis.  
- Claim framing: acceleration claim is asymptotic and matches best-known accelerated consensus rather than improving upon it.  
- Clarity: paper is organized and explicit, but notation is heavy and explanations (splitting intuition, role of δ, positivity/existence conditions for R̂t) need tightening.  
- Clarity suggestion: include a small worked example (e.g., path or cycle) to illustrate Algorithm 2 and mapping to Algorithm 3.  
- Originality: novelty in first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus and the auxiliary node-process reduction; obtaining known accelerated rates via Min-Sum Splitting appears new.  
- Significance (conceptual): bridges BP/Min-Sum with accelerated consensus and non-reversible lifting, advancing theoretical understanding of message-passing.  
- Significance (practical): requires global spectral parameter γ and offers interpretive rather than clear practical advantage over known node-based accelerated methods.  
- Strength: provides a clear, rigorous spectral rate (Theorem 1) with explicit constants and links to optimal accelerated rates on geometric graphs.  
- Strength: elegant reduction from edge-level Min-Sum to node-level multi-step iteration (Algorithm 3) as a new analytical tool.  
- Strength: situates results well within lifted Markov chain and accelerated first-order method literatures.  
- Weakness: limited to quadratic consensus; no extension to general convex φ_v or other constraints.  
- Weakness: depends on global spectral information (γ) with no robustness analysis or distributed estimation strategy.  
- Weakness: no experiments to validate constants, transient behavior, or compare overhead versus Chebyshev/ADMM.  
- Weakness: essentially recovers known accelerated rates, so practical algorithmic advantage of Min-Sum Splitting is not demonstrated.  
- Questions for authors: how sensitive is convergence to γ errors, and can you give non-asymptotic bounds or distributed estimators for ρ_W?  
- Questions for authors: do δ≠1 choices offer advantages in rates, robustness, or stability?  
- Questions for authors: can the auxiliary-process technique extend to strongly convex non-quadratic φ_v or to inequality constraints via penalties?  
- Questions for authors: how do per-iteration cost and memory compare to Chebyshev/shift-register consensus and distributed ADMM?  
- Questions for authors: any implications for time-varying or asynchronous networks?  
- Suggestions: add experiments (cycles, grids, random geometric graphs) comparing Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, and Min-Sum Splitting with true/approximate γ, reporting iteration/error and wall-clock/communication.  
- Suggestions: discuss robustness to γ and initialization and provide heuristic γ rules when ρ_W is unknown.  
- Suggestions: clarify W selection and whether Γ=γW assumes a pre-chosen sparse W (e.g., WMH) and whether optimizing W is beneficial.  
- Suggestions: expand discussion on generalizing the auxiliary-process viewpoint beyond consensus.  
- Recommendation: borderline accept — strong theoretical/conceptual contribution but limited practical impact due to lack of experiments and scope restricted to quadratics.
