- Overall: Significant, well-executed contribution showing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting with square-root improvement over diffusive methods; strongly recommend acceptance.  
- Core idea: Reformulate consensus so Min-Sum Splitting converges where standard Min-Sum fails.  
- Parameterization: Proper choice of splitting parameters (δ, Γ) yields accelerated subdiffusive O(D log(D/ε)) rates on cycles and tori, approaching optimal O(D log(1/ε)).  
- Connections: Authors relate the method to lifted Markov chains and multi-step (shift-register/Nesterov-like) gradient methods, but treatment is brief.  
- Technical innovation: Introduces an auxiliary node-based message-passing algorithm to track and analyze the splitting algorithm.  
- Strength — theory: Rigorous analysis and provable convergence rates with a substantial square-root improvement.  
- Strength — presentation: Clear, well-organized, and accessible writing and structure.  
- Strength — novelty: Novel use of splitting techniques and original analysis of parameter impacts on convergence.  
- Strength — context: Thoughtful connections to established accelerated methods and prior literature.  
- Strength — technical depth: Deep mathematical analysis supporting the results.  
- Weakness — connections shallow: Relationship to lifted Markov chains and Nesterov-like acceleration is mentioned but not deeply analyzed or parameter-mapped.  
- Weakness — notation complexity: Multiple algorithms and notational systems can be initially confusing; streamlining would help.  
- Weakness — empirical validation: Paper is almost entirely theoretical; limited experiments would increase practical confidence.  
- For acceptance: Significant theoretical advance, clear exposition, addresses a fundamental distributed-systems problem, and motivates further research via connections to other methods.  
- Against acceptance: Practical applicability is unclear due to lack of experiments; connections to other accelerated approaches need strengthening.  
- Specific question 1: Clarify which parameter choices in Algorithm 1 recover ordinary Min-Sum.  
- Specific question 2: Provide a concrete example illustrating Theorem 1’s dependence on global graph properties.  
- Future work: Explore generalization to more complex objective functions and broader network topologies.
