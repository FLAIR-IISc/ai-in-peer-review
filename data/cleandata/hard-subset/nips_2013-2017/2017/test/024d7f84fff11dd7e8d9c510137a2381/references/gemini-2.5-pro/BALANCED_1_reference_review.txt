This paper analyzes the convergence rate of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for the distributed consensus problem. The consensus problem, particularly network averaging, is a foundational task in distributed machine learning and multi-agent systems. The authors situate their work in the context of accelerating consensus beyond the "diffusive" rates of standard algorithms (e.g., gossip or first-order gradient methods), which scale poorly (O(D²)) on graphs with large diameters like cycles or grids. The paper correctly identifies the two dominant lines of research for acceleration: lifted Markov chains, which employ non-reversible dynamics on an expanded state space, and multi-step methods from convex optimization (e.g., Polyak's heavy ball, Nesterov acceleration, shift registers), which incorporate memory into the updates.

The core contribution of this work is to connect the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, a generalization of the standard Min-Sum message-passing protocol previously introduced in [34], to these two acceleration paradigms. The authors specialize the algorithm to the network averaging problem, showing that the message-passing updates become linear and operate on the directed edges of the communication graph. The key technical innovation is the introduction of an "auxiliary message-passing scheme" that tracks the evolution of the node estimates. This auxiliary process cleverly recasts the dynamics on the 2m edges into an equivalent 2n-dimensional linear system on the nodes. This reformulation is instrumental, as it reveals that with a specific choice of splitting parameters (δ and Γ), the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm exactly reproduces the dynamics of accelerated shift-register methods. The main theoretical result (Theorem 1) leverages this connection to establish an accelerated asymptotic convergence rate, achieving a square-root improvement over diffusive methods and scaling as O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori, which is competitive with the state-of-the-art for this class of algorithms.

### Strengths:

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The primary strength of the paper is the novel and insightful connection it establishes between message-passing algorithms (specifically, a variant of Belief Propagation), lifted non-reversible processes, and accelerated multi-step optimization methods. By showing that tuning the splitting parameters in Min-Sum can induce a momentum-like effect equivalent to a shift-register, the paper provides a new perspective on acceleration in distributed systems. This unification of seemingly disparate concepts is a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The analysis is rigorous and the proof technique is elegant. The introduction of the auxiliary process (Algorithm 3) to analyze the original edge-based dynamics (Algorithm 2) is a clever methodological contribution that simplifies a complex system and makes the connection to existing literature on accelerated methods transparent. The derivation appears sound and leads to the strong quantitative results in Theorem 1.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a comprehensive and clear overview of the problem landscape, existing methods, and their limitations, effectively motivating the work. The logical flow from the general algorithm to its specific application and subsequent analysis via the auxiliary process is easy to follow.

4.  **Important Results:** The paper provides the first convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. More importantly, it demonstrates that this message-passing framework can not only be made to converge on cyclic graphs (where standard Min-Sum fails) but can also achieve an accelerated rate competitive with specialized algorithms designed for this purpose. This significantly advances our understanding of the capabilities of Belief Propagation-style algorithms in distributed optimization.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:

1.  **Practicality of Parameter Tuning:** The main result in Theorem 1 requires setting the splitting matrix Γ as a function of γ = 2 / (1 + sqrt(1 - ρ²_W)), where ρ_W is the spectral radius of the matrix (W - 11ᵀ/n). This ρ_W is a global property of the network topology. A central challenge in designing distributed algorithms is to avoid reliance on such global information. The paper does not discuss how this parameter γ could be computed or estimated in a distributed fashion. While many other accelerated methods share this requirement, a discussion of the practical implications and potential workarounds (e.g., using distributed power iteration to estimate ρ_W) would significantly strengthen the paper and address a key implementation concern.

2.  **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is restricted to the network averaging problem, where the objective functions are quadratic. This allows for the updates to remain linear and tractable. However, the Min-Sum framework is applicable to much more general optimization problems. While the conclusion rightly suggests that these results motivate further studies, the paper would benefit from a short discussion on the potential challenges in extending this acceleration analysis beyond the quadratic case. For instance, would the updates become nonlinear? Would the connection to linear multi-step methods still hold, or would a different analytical approach be required?

3.  **Comparison with ADMM:** The authors briefly mention the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) as another relevant algorithm. Given the popularity and effectiveness of ADMM for distributed consensus, a more detailed comparison would be valuable. Specifically, it would be interesting to compare the structure of the Min-Sum Splitting updates with those of consensus ADMM and discuss the relationship between the splitting parameters (δ, Γ) and the ADMM penalty parameter.

4.  **Minor Typo:** There appears to be a small typo in the text. In Section 5, when introducing Algorithm 2, the text refers to "K̂(δ,Γ) defined in (5)", but the definition is in equation (4). Similarly, in Section 6, Algorithm 3 uses "K(δ,Γ) defined in (5)", which is correct. This minor inconsistency could be easily fixed.

### Summary and Recommendation:

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the field of distributed optimization. It elegantly connects Min-Sum message-passing to established techniques for acceleration, providing both a new understanding of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm and a powerful new tool for designing fast consensus protocols. The technical analysis is sound, the results are strong, and the paper is written with outstanding clarity. While the practical issue of setting the acceleration parameter based on global information could be discussed more thoroughly, this does not detract from the importance of the core theoretical contribution. The paper's strengths far outweigh its minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
