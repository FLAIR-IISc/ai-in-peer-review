- Paper analyzes Min-Sum Splitting for averaging, showing it induces linear edge message-passing and an exact auxiliary two-step node recursion that enables spectral analysis.
- Main result: with δ=1 and Γ=γW (W symmetric, W1=1, γ tuned to spectrum of W−11^T/n) the method converges to the average with accelerated, subdiffusive rate matching shift-register/Chebyshev methods.
- Obtains O(D log(D/ε)) convergence on cycles and tori, a square-root improvement over diffusive consensus.
- Technical device: auxiliary node-based recursion (Algorithm 3) and a 2n×2n matrix K whose spectrum follows shift-register analysis.
- Extends prior Min-Sum/Belief Propagation work by providing a rate proof for splitting on general graphs and relating splitting to lifting and multi-step momentum.
- Clarifies how splitting’s directionality induces memory and connects BP, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step acceleration.
- Novelty: first rate analysis of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus and the auxiliary-process technique linking to known accelerated rates.
- Generality: analysis applies to arbitrary graphs given W and suggests parameter selection via spectral quantities.
- Strength: elegant propositions linking edge-based Min-Sum Splitting to a tractable node-based linear system useful beyond this paper.
- Strength: main theorem gives explicit spectral-rate characterization and recovers expected optimality on geometric graphs.
- Weakness: results limited to quadratic averaging; unclear which parts generalize to broader objectives.
- Weakness: accelerated tuning requires global spectral knowledge (ρW); no practical local estimation or robust tuning rules provided.
- Weakness: no experiments — unclear practical advantages over directly implementing known accelerated consensus methods.
- Weakness: assumptions/initialization details (positivity of denominators, sensitivity to other initializations) are not fully addressed.
- Weakness: analysis is synchronous and noiseless; robustness to asynchrony, noise, quantization, or link failures is not discussed.
- Presentation issues: dense notation, minor typos, and some unnecessary conditions; a compact main-text proof sketch and illustrative example would help.
- Quality: technical derivations appear sound and match classical Chebyshev/shift-register rates; proofs in supplement are complete but main text needs more intuition.
- Originality: high in combining Min-Sum Splitting with acceleration literature and proving rates, though the rate itself is known.
- Significance: moderate-to-high for theory/algorithms community; practical impact would improve with experiments and extensions beyond quadratics.
- Pros for acceptance: rigorous, novel theoretical contribution that bridges literatures and attains accelerated rates.
- Cons for acceptance: restricted to averaging, needs global spectral info, and lacks empirical validation demonstrating practical gains.
- Suggestions: add experiments comparing to Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, Nesterov, EXTRA/ADMM; discuss spectral estimation/tuning, positivity conditions, asynchrony, and extensions beyond quadratics.
- Recommendation: Weak accept — solid theoretical work suitable for the track but would benefit from empirical validation and broader scope.
