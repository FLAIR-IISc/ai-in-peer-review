This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors establish convergence and derive rates, demonstrating a square-root improvement over diffusive methods for cycle and toroidal graphs, approaching the optimal rate for some accelerated algorithms. The work builds upon previous research on Min-Sum and splitting techniques [34, 35] and connects it to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods [37, 13].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting is a significant contribution, filling a gap in the existing literature. The connection to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods provides a valuable theoretical framework.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous mathematical analysis, building upon established techniques in graph theory and optimization. The introduction of the auxiliary process (Algorithm 3) appears to be a clever way to track the evolution of the Min-Sum scheme and derive convergence results.
* **Significance:** The improved convergence rates demonstrated have the potential to impact various applications leveraging distributed consensus, such as sensor networks, distributed inference, and large-scale machine learning [20, 21, 9, 19, 31]. The practical implications of a square-root improvement in convergence speed are substantial.
* **Clarity (Mostly):**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear introduction of the problem and related work.  The mathematical derivations are presented logically.
* **Thoroughness:** The paper carefully compares the results to existing literature on diffusive methods [44, 2] and other accelerated approaches [37, 13]. The authors acknowledge the connection to and differences from prior work in a detailed manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity (Details):** While the overall structure is good, some sections, particularly those with heavy mathematical notation (Sections 5 & 6), could benefit from more detailed explanations. The jump between Algorithms 1 & 2, and then to Algorithm 3, could be smoothed out with more guiding text explaining the motivation behind each step.  For example, the reasoning for specific choices of parameters (δ, Γ) could be expanded.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses entirely on theoretical analysis. While the analysis is strong, the absence of even simple simulations to corroborate the theoretical results is a significant drawback. Empirical evaluation, even on small graphs, would greatly strengthen the paper’s claims and demonstrate the practical benefit of the proposed algorithm.
* **Repetitive Introduction:** The introduction is quite lengthy and, at times, repetitive. While a thorough literature review is appreciated, it could be more concise, focusing on the most relevant prior work and highlighting the specific contributions of this paper more clearly.
* **Notation:** The notation is dense and occasionally overwhelming. While standard in the field, consistent and careful labeling of equations and variables could improve readability.
* **Connection to ADMM:** The paper briefly mentions a connection to ADMM [3, 43, 38], but this connection is not explored in sufficient detail. Expanding on this link could be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.
* The convergence rates established represent a significant improvement over existing methods for certain graph topologies.
* The work connects Min-Sum Splitting to well-established techniques in lifted Markov chains and accelerated optimization.
* The theoretical results are potentially impactful for a wide range of applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation is a major concern.
* Some sections are difficult to follow due to dense notation and limited explanation.
* The lengthy introduction could be more concise.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The analysis is rigorous and the results are promising. However, the lack of empirical validation significantly weakens the paper. While the theoretical results are strong, without experimental evidence to support them, it is difficult to assess their practical relevance.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit.**

The authors should address the following points in a revised version:

1. **Add Empirical Evaluation:** Include simulations to validate the theoretical results and demonstrate the performance of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm on various graph topologies.
2. **Improve Clarity:**  Expand explanations in sections 5 & 6, clarify the transitions between algorithms, and simplify the notation where possible.
3. **Concise Introduction:** Streamline the introduction to focus on the core contributions of the paper.
4. **Explore ADMM Connection:** Expand on the connection to ADMM and discuss potential implications.
5. **Consider a discussion of computational complexity:** The runtime of the proposed algorithm relative to other distributed consensus methods would be a valuable addition.

Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its chances of acceptance in a future submission. The theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant reconsideration if these revisions are made.
