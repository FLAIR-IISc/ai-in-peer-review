## NIPS 2017 Review: Accelerated Consensus via Min-Sum Splitting

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant contribution to the understanding of Min-Sum algorithms for consensus problems. It establishes convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, demonstrating a square-root improvement over diffusive methods and approaching optimality. The work is technically sound, clearly written, and connects nicely to existing literature in distributed optimization, network averaging, and message passing. While the theoretical focus may limit immediate practical impact, the insights are valuable and the connections drawn between different approaches are compelling. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses the problem of distributed consensus, specifically network averaging, using a Min-Sum Splitting approach. Traditional Min-Sum struggles with convergence on graphs with cycles, and prior work ([27]) offered improvements but lacked comprehensive convergence rate analysis. This paper fills that gap by proving convergence for Min-Sum Splitting on *any* graph topology and establishing asymptotic convergence rates. Importantly, it connects this work to two established lines of research: lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, revealing deeper underlying principles. The paper highlights the role of “splitting” in introducing directionality that accelerates convergence, a connection not previously explored. The authors position their work relative to existing diffusive methods ([44, 2]) and recent accelerated algorithms ([30, 36]), demonstrating a tangible improvement in convergence speed.

**2. Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The proof of convergence and the derivation of the convergence rates are solid and well-motivated. The auxiliary process introduced to track the algorithm’s evolution is a clever technique, simplifying the analysis.
*   **Generality:** The result holds for *any* graph topology, a significant advantage over previous approaches limited to specific graph structures.
*   **Connections to Existing Literature:** The paper expertly connects Min-Sum Splitting to lifted Markov chains and accelerated optimization methods, offering a unified perspective. This is a particularly strong aspect of the work.
*   **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the contributions.
*   **Significant Result:** Achieving a square-root improvement in convergence rate is a substantial advance, bringing Min-Sum closer to optimal performance.

**3. Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Considerations:** The complexity of implementing the proposed Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, especially determining the optimal splitting parameters, isn't thoroughly discussed. While theoretically advantageous, the overhead could outweigh the benefits in certain practical scenarios. More discussion on the trade-offs would be valuable.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The dependence of the convergence rate on the spectral gap of the underlying graph (and the tuning of γ) raises questions about robustness. How sensitive is the performance to inaccuracies in estimating the spectral gap?
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper relies heavily on theoretical analysis. While this is appropriate for a NIPS paper, including some simple simulations demonstrating the convergence speed on representative graph topologies (e.g., cycle, grid) would strengthen the results and build intuition.
* **Repetitive sections**: The introductory sections (1-4) contain significant overlap in describing existing work and feel slightly repetitive. A more concise presentation would improve readability.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

*   **Section 3:** The discussion of diffusive rates and the optimality results for certain graph types is excellent context.
*   **Section 5 & 6:** The introduction of Algorithm 2 and 3 and Propositions 1 & 2 are well explained and build a clear understanding of the reparametrization.
*   **Theorem 1:** The statement is clear and the result is strong.  However, the practical implications of determining the optimal γ based on the spectral gap need further discussion.  Could the authors briefly mention practical methods for estimating the spectral gap?
*   **Conclusion:** The suggestion of generalizing the acceleration to other problems is a good direction for future work.

**5. Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to accelerating consensus algorithms.
*   The convergence rate improvement is significant and demonstrated rigorously.
*   The connection to existing literature provides valuable insights and strengthens the contribution.
*   The clarity of the writing and organization is commendable.

**Against Acceptance:**

*   The practical implementation challenges and sensitivity to parameter tuning are not fully addressed.
*   The lack of empirical validation limits the assessment of real-world performance.
*   The introductory sections feel slightly repetitive.

**6. Final Recommendation:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. I recommend the authors address the concerns regarding practical considerations, parameter sensitivity, and the repetitive introduction. Adding some simple simulations to illustrate the convergence speed would further strengthen the paper. Despite these minor points, the theoretical contribution is significant and the paper is well-deserving of acceptance at NIPS.
