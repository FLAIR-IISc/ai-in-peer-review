## NIPS 2017 Review: Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant contribution to the theory of distributed consensus algorithms, specifically analyzing the convergence rates of Min-Sum Splitting. The authors establish a theoretical speedup – a square-root improvement over diffusive methods – for cycle and toroidal graphs, bringing the convergence rate closer to the optimal bound. The connection drawn between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods is insightful and strengthens the contribution. While the technical details are dense, the paper is generally well-written and the results are compelling. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Summary:**

The paper investigates the convergence of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for the consensus problem.  Traditional Min-Sum struggles with convergence on graphs with cycles, while splitting provides a workaround. This work provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, demonstrating a subdiffusive convergence rate – specifically, O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles and tori –  achieved by carefully tuning the splitting parameters. A key innovation is the introduction of an auxiliary linear process on the nodes of the graph, facilitating the analysis and providing a link to shift-register methods, resulting in a convergence rate comparable to recently established optimal bounds.  The authors also highlight intriguing connections to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated optimization techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Results:** The core contribution is the rigorous proof of a subdiffusive convergence rate for Min-Sum Splitting, a significant advancement in the field. The convergence rate of O(D log(D/ε)) is a notable improvement over the diffusive rate for graphs with geometry.
* **Novel Analytical Technique:** The introduction of the auxiliary linear process is a clever way to analyze the algorithm’s convergence. It sidesteps the complexities of analyzing the message-passing directly on the edges, offering a more tractable approach.
* **Connection to Existing Work:**  The paper successfully connects Min-Sum Splitting to two seemingly disparate research areas: lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order optimization. This bridging of concepts adds significant value and provides a broader perspective. The explicit link to shift-register methods is particularly insightful.
* **Clear Motivation & Problem Setup:** The introduction provides a solid background on Min-Sum, consensus problems, and the limitations of existing algorithms. The problem statement is clearly defined.
* **Well-Organized:** The paper follows a logical flow, building from the basic Min-Sum framework to the specific results for consensus, and then exploring the connections to related work.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Density:** The mathematical derivations and notation are dense and require a strong background in distributed optimization and graph theory to fully grasp. While this is common for theoretical papers, it could limit accessibility to a broader audience.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses almost entirely on theoretical analysis. While the theoretical results are strong, some limited empirical validation (even on small graphs) could further bolster the claims and demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach.
* **Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The paper does not discuss the practical implications of choosing the optimal splitting parameters (γW in Theorem 1). Calculating the spectral radius of W might be computationally expensive for large graphs.  Addressing this practical challenge would be beneficial.
* **Repetitive Background:** The repeated review of existing work in Sections 3 & 4, especially regarding diffusive algorithms and accelerated methods, feels slightly redundant. It could be streamlined.



**Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Page 2, lines 28-30:**  The statement about constraints not applying to existing sufficient conditions seems somewhat vague. Can the authors elaborate on why the existing conditions are not applicable when constraints are incorporated as hard barriers?
* **Page 4, lines 25-27:**  The claim that Min-Sum Splitting introduces a directionality analogous to lifted graphs is a crucial connection.  A more intuitive explanation of *how* this directionality arises from the splitting process would be helpful.
* **Page 7, Algorithm 2:** The notation K̂(δ,Γ) is not immediately clear. A brief explanation of its construction or a reference to its derivation would be beneficial.
* **Page 9, Algorithm 3:** The same comment applies to K(δ,Γ). Providing some intuition behind the matrix’s structure would be useful.
* **Theorem 1:** The statement about achieving rates comparable to shift-register methods is strong.  A more explicit comparison, detailing the specific parameters that need to be matched, would strengthen this claim.
* **Future Work:** The authors mention generalizing the acceleration to other problems. Can they speculate on which types of problems might be most amenable to this approach, and what challenges might arise?




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Significant theoretical advancement in understanding the convergence of Min-Sum Splitting.
* Novel analytical technique with a clear connection to existing literature.
*  Demonstrated subdiffusive convergence rate is a valuable contribution.
* Strong connections to related fields (lifted Markov chains, accelerated optimization).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Technical density may limit accessibility.
* Lack of empirical validation.
* Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges.



**Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The theoretical results are compelling, and the connections to related work are insightful. The minor weaknesses are easily addressable with revisions. I confidently recommend acceptance.**
