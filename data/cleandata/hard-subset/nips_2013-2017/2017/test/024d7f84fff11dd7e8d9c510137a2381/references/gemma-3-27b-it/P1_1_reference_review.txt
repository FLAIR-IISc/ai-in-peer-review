## NIPS 2017 Review: Accelerated Consensus via Min-Sum Splitting

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm when applied to the consensus problem (network averaging).  The authors demonstrate that, unlike the standard Min-Sum algorithm which can fail to converge on graphs with cycles, Min-Sum Splitting *does* converge for any graph topology.  Crucially, they establish that properly tuned splitting parameters lead to *accelerated* convergence, achieving a square-root improvement over diffusive methods and approaching the optimal convergence rate for consensus. They achieve this by introducing an auxiliary linear process on the nodes of the graph to track the evolution of the algorithm, simplifying the analysis compared to previous work.  The paper also highlights connections between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods in convex optimization.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds on prior work by [34] introducing Min-Sum Splitting, and [27] which investigated Min-Sum for consensus, demonstrating non-convergence of the standard algorithm and proposing a modified version with improved, but not optimal, convergence rates. This work surpasses [27] by providing *analytical* convergence rate guarantees for Min-Sum Splitting across *all* graph topologies, a significant step forward. It also draws parallels and contrasts with the burgeoning literature on accelerated consensus methods based on lifted Markov chains (e.g., [8, 5, 37]) and accelerated gradient descent (e.g., [13, 24]), effectively positioning Min-Sum Splitting within a broader landscape of approaches.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper rigorously proves convergence and establishes a novel convergence rate for Min-Sum Splitting, a previously open question. The square-root improvement over diffusive methods is a significant theoretical advance.
*   **Generality:** The convergence guarantees hold for *any* graph topology, making the results widely applicable.
*   **Novel Analytical Technique:** The introduction of the auxiliary message-passing scheme is a clever and effective technique for analyzing the dynamics of Min-Sum Splitting, simplifying the analysis compared to approaches used in prior work.
*   **Connection to Existing Literature:**  The paper effectively connects Min-Sum Splitting to related research areas, providing valuable context and highlighting the contribution of this work. The analogies to lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods are insightful.
*   **Clear Presentation:** While dense with mathematical notation, the paper is generally well-organized and the core ideas are clearly presented.  The gradual introduction of concepts is appreciated.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **High Mathematical Density:**  The paper is quite mathematically demanding.  While the derivations seem correct, they require significant effort from the reader, potentially limiting its accessibility. More intuitive explanations of key steps would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses entirely on theoretical analysis. While this is justifiable given the complexity of the analysis, some limited simulations demonstrating the improved convergence rate on representative graph topologies (e.g., cycles, tori) would strengthen the practical impact of the work.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The choice of the splitting parameter (γ in Theorem 1) depends on knowing the spectral gap of the graph. Determining this in practice can be computationally expensive, potentially negating some of the benefits of the accelerated convergence. This trade-off isn’t discussed in detail.
*   **Repetitive Background Material:** Sections 3 and 4 provide fairly extensive background on existing consensus algorithms and accelerated methods. While helpful for completeness, this material could be somewhat condensed to focus more on the core contributions of the paper.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical result: establishing convergence and accelerated rates for Min-Sum Splitting applied to consensus.
*   The analytical technique introduced (the auxiliary message-passing scheme) is clever and represents a genuine contribution.
*   The work addresses a challenging problem and advances the state of the art in distributed consensus algorithms.
*   The clear connections made to related literature place the work in a broader context and highlight its importance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The high mathematical density and lack of empirical validation might limit the paper’s impact and accessibility.
*   The practical considerations related to determining the optimal splitting parameter are not fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions to the field of distributed consensus algorithms. While the mathematical complexity and lack of empirical results are minor drawbacks, the novelty, generality, and analytical rigor of the work outweigh these concerns. The paper meets the NIPS criteria for quality, clarity (despite its density), originality, and significance. 

**Rating:** **Accept (Strongly)**

**Confidence:** 8/10 (High confidence given the clear theoretical arguments, but would be higher with some supporting empirical results).
