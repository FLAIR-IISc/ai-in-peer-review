- Paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for average consensus, specializing it to equality-constrained quadratic consensus and showing quadratic messages reduce to a linear edge recursion.
- Introduces a 2n×2n auxiliary node-based linear process K(δ, Γ) whose evolution exactly tracks Min-Sum estimates, enabling spectral analysis.
- Shows that with δ=1 and Γ=γW (W symmetric averaging matrix, γ tuned from ρ(W−11^T/n)) the rate matches shift-register/Chebyshev acceleration, giving a square-root improvement and O(D log(D/ε)) scaling on grids.
- Conceptually connects Min-Sum Splitting to lifting/non-reversible Markov chains (directionality on edges) and to multi-step accelerated first-order methods (memory on nodes).
- Places work relative to prior literature: extends Splitting analyses [Ruozzi & Tatikonda], relates to lifting and momentum-based acceleration, and ties into distributed ADMM/lifting discussions.
- Reduction from Algorithm 1 to linear edge-based Algorithm 2 for quadratics is standard but carefully executed; quadratic messages remain quadratic (Proposition 1).
- The auxiliary node-based recursion (Algorithm 3) and Proposition 2 are the core technical contributions, moving spectral analysis from 2m×2m to 2n×2n matrices.
- Theorem 1 gives closed-form γ = 2/(1+sqrt(1−ρ_W^2)), proves convergence, and derives the accelerated spectral radius matching classical rates.
- Assumptions and caveats include restriction to quadratic objectives, symmetric W with W1=1, specific initialization R̂0=r̂0=0, positivity conditions for beliefs, and need for global spectral info (ρ_W).
- Paper is dense but mostly clear; separation of three algorithmic forms is helpful though proofs are deferred to supplement.
- A brief proof sketch for Proposition 2 and Theorem 1 in the main text would improve clarity; there are minor notational issues and a missing Figure 1.
- Originality: novel auxiliary node-based tracking and explicit parameterization recovering shift-register matrix; the achieved rate itself is known.
- Significance: first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting within GBP community and a potentially reusable auxiliary-process technique; less novel for consensus community.
- Strength: rigorous specialization and linear-quadratic representation tying Γ directly to spectral analysis.
- Strength: elegant auxiliary process enabling spectral analysis and closed-form tuning of γ.
- Strength: insightful conceptual unification of Splitting, lifting, and multi-step acceleration.
- Weakness: limited scope to quadratic consensus; no extension to general convex node costs or constrained variants.
- Weakness: γ requires global spectral knowledge; no robustness analysis to mis-specification or adaptive schedules discussed.
- Weakness: communication/computation overhead comparison missing—edge-based messages send two scalars versus node-based one scalar per neighbor.
- Weakness: positivity condition for denominators needs explicit guarantee under proposed initialization and tuning.
- Weakness: no experiments provided to compare constants and transient behavior versus other accelerated methods.
- Weakness: asynchronous/noisy/time-varying graph settings not discussed though relevant for Min-Sum applications.
- Suggestions: add proof sketches for key propositions/theorems, robustness analysis for γ mis-tuning, or propose adaptive proxies.
- Suggestions: include complexity comparison (messages/memory) against node-based accelerations and lifted walks.
- Suggestions: provide simulations on canonical graphs and discuss constants and transients.
- Suggestions: clarify role of δ and whether δ≠1 can offer benefits.
- Minor comments: remove unnecessary assumption 0<b_v<1, ensure Figure 1 and references are present, unify edge notation, and fix footnote/typo issues.
- Recommendation: Weak accept — technically interesting bridge between Min-Sum Splitting and known accelerated consensus, but limited by scope and lack of experiments/robustness analysis.
