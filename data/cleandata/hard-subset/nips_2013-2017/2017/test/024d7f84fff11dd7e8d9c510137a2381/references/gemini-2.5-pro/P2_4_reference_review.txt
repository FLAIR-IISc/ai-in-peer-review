### Summary and Contributions

This paper analyzes the rate of convergence for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, a generalization of the standard Min-Sum message-passing protocol, applied to the canonical distributed consensus problem. The main contribution is a theoretical one: the authors are the first to establish convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting. Specifically, for the network averaging problem, they demonstrate that with a proper choice of the algorithm's tuning parameters (δ and Γ), Min-Sum Splitting achieves an accelerated, "subdiffusive" rate of convergence. This rate provides a square-root improvement over classical "diffusive" methods (e.g., gossip algorithms based on reversible Markov chains) and matches the performance of well-known accelerated first-order methods, such as the shift-register algorithm.

To achieve this, the paper makes two other key contributions. First, it establishes a novel and insightful connection between the Min-Sum framework (a belief propagation method) and two other major families of accelerated consensus algorithms: lifted Markov chains and multi-step momentum methods from convex optimization. The "splitting" parameters are shown to introduce a directionality analogous to non-reversible lifted walks, which translates into a memory term in the node updates, similar to momentum methods. Second, the authors introduce a powerful analytical technique. They derive an "auxiliary message-passing scheme" on the nodes of the graph that exactly tracks the output of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, which naturally operates on the directed edges. This simplifies the analysis significantly by reducing the dynamics from a 2m x 2m system (where m is the number of edges) to a more tractable 2n x 2n system (where n is the number of nodes), allowing for a direct connection to the spectral properties of the graph Laplacian.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
*   **(Strength)** The paper is technically sound and the theoretical results are well-supported. The derivation of the auxiliary message-passing scheme in Section 6 (Proposition 2) is a clever and elegant step that forms the cornerstone of the analysis. By showing that a specific parametrization of Min-Sum Splitting recovers the exact same linear operator as the shift-register method (Theorem 1), the authors are able to leverage existing spectral analysis to prove their claims about the accelerated convergence rate.
*   **(Strength)** The work is a complete piece of theoretical analysis for the problem it sets out to solve (network averaging). The connection between the Min-Sum parameters (δ, Γ) and the matrix `W` used in classical methods is made explicit, providing a clear prescription for achieving acceleration.
*   **(Weakness)** The analysis is confined to the quadratic case, i.e., the network averaging problem. While this is a foundational problem in the field, the title "Accelerated consensus" suggests a broader scope. The paper does not address how or if this acceleration extends to consensus problems with more general convex functions `φ_v`, where the message updates would no longer be linear. This limitation should be acknowledged more directly as a direction for future work.

**Clarity:**
*   **(Strength)** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors provide an excellent and concise review of diffusive and accelerated consensus methods (Sections 3 & 4), which effectively situates their contribution within the existing literature. The logical flow is clear: from the general Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, to its specialization for consensus, to the introduction of the auxiliary process, and finally to the main theorem on convergence rates. The notation is consistent and the propositions and theorem are stated precisely.

**Originality:**
*   **(Strength)** The primary originality of this paper lies not in discovering a new, faster algorithm, but in revealing that an existing, general-purpose message-passing framework (Min-Sum Splitting) can be tuned to become one of the state-of-the-art accelerated algorithms for consensus. The connection established between belief propagation, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step optimization methods is highly novel and insightful. To my knowledge, this link has not been established before and bridges concepts from distinct research communities.
*   **(Strength)** The analytical technique based on the auxiliary process is also novel and could potentially be applied to analyze other message-passing schemes.
*   **(Minor Weakness)** As acknowledged, the final convergence rate itself is not new; it is the known rate for shift-register methods [45, 24]. The contribution is showing a new path to achieve it via a principled message-passing framework.

**Significance:**
*   **(Strength)** The results are highly significant. They elevate Min-Sum from a heuristic (on loopy graphs) or a specialized tool into a competitive algorithm for a fundamental distributed optimization problem. This is important because Min-Sum is a generic framework applicable to a wide range of problems expressed as factor graphs. This work demonstrates that the reparameterization offered by "splitting" is not just a trick to ensure convergence, but a powerful mechanism for controlling and accelerating it.
*   **(Strength)** The conceptual bridge built by the paper is likely to inspire further research. For example, can splitting parameters be optimized to accelerate convergence for other problems beyond consensus? Can insights from accelerated optimization be used to design better message-passing schedulings or updates?
*   **(Weakness)** A practical limitation, common to many accelerated methods, is that the optimal choice of the parameter γ (and thus Γ) depends on `ρ_W`, a global property of the graph (the spectral gap of `W`). The paper does not discuss how this parameter might be estimated or set in a distributed setting. This affects the immediate practical applicability of the optimally-tuned algorithm, although sub-optimal choices may still provide benefits.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Conceptual Contribution:** The paper establishes a powerful and previously unknown connection between belief propagation (Min-Sum) and accelerated convex optimization methods. This is a significant intellectual contribution that bridges two important fields.
2.  **Strong Technical Results:** It provides the first-ever convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, resolving an open question. The proof technique via the auxiliary process is elegant and effective.
3.  **High Clarity and Impact:** The paper is very well-written and clearly articulates its contributions and their context. The findings are significant, suggesting that message-passing algorithms can be systematically accelerated and are competitive with specialized state-of-the-art methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The theoretical results are restricted to the quadratic network averaging problem, and the generalization to broader classes of consensus problems is not discussed.
2.  **Practical Implementation Details Omitted:** The requirement of global graph information (`ρ_W`) for optimal parameter tuning is a practical hurdle that is not addressed.

### Conclusion

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. The novel connection it forges between Min-Sum and accelerated optimization is a major contribution that is both surprising and insightful. The technical analysis is sound, and the paper is written with exceptional clarity. While the analysis is limited to the quadratic case, this is a standard and important first step for a new line of inquiry. This work significantly advances our understanding of message-passing algorithms and their relationship to mainstream optimization, and is very likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
