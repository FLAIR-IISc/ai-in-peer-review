Summary
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting, a reparameterized version of the Min-Sum (a.k.a. Gaussian belief propagation for quadratics) message-passing algorithm, and establishes convergence rates for the consensus (network averaging) problem. The authors show: (i) with a suitable parametrization, Min-Sum Splitting induces a linear message-passing process on directed edges; (ii) via a reparametrization, the edge-based process can be tracked by an auxiliary two-state linear system on nodes (a multi-step update with memory); (iii) picking δ = 1 and Γ = γW with γ tuned from the spectral gap of a symmetric matrix W (with W1 = 1 and ρ(W − 11T/n) < 1) yields an accelerated, subdiffusive rate matching shift-register (second-order consensus) methods. The resulting convergence time is O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori, improving by a square-root factor over diffusive methods (O(D2 log(1/ε))) and approaching the recent O(D log(1/ε)) optimum from accelerated first-order methods.

Relation to prior work
- Min-Sum/GBP convergence on quadratics and conditions (diagonal dominance) are classical; Min-Sum Splitting (Ruozzi & Tatikonda) has been known to help convergence but lacked rate analysis. The paper fills this gap for consensus.
- Prior consensus acceleration includes: lifted nonreversible Markov chains; multi-step (heavy-ball, Nesterov), Chebyshev/shift-register methods; and ADMM-based schemes. The authors connect Min-Sum Splitting to both lifting (edge-space dynamics induce directionality) and multi-step methods (auxiliary node process with memory), and recover shift-register rates via spectral analysis of a 2n×2n matrix K, aligning with Golub–Varga, Young, Xiao–Boyd, and more recent surveys.
- Prior Min-Sum for consensus (e.g., “Consensus Propagation”) analyzed modified Min-Sum on regular graphs via edge-based chains and provided rates on cycles; this work generalizes to arbitrary graphs and provides explicit rates via node-space analysis.

Quality
- The main theorem is precise: for quadratic consensus, with specific δ, Γ, and initialization R̂0 = r̂0 = 0, the estimates converge to the average with asymptotic rate ρK = sqrt((1 − √(1 − ρW^2))/(1 + √(1 − ρW^2))) < ρW, matching shift-register methods. The auxiliary node-space formulation (Algorithm 3) is the key technical tool.
- Claims are well-supported by analysis (proofs deferred to the supplement). The translation from edge-space dynamics (Algorithm 2) to node-space multi-step dynamics (Algorithm 3) is clear and seems technically sound.
- Limitations: focus on the quadratic consensus objective; synchronous, undirected, fixed graphs; tuning depends on global spectral information; no experiments; and some stability/initialization conditions are only stated (e.g., positivity conditions in Prop. 1) without intuition in the main text.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized: background on Min-Sum Splitting, consensus, accelerated methods; specialization to consensus; auxiliary algorithm; main theorem. The algorithms are clearly stated.
- Some notation/layout issues (e.g., footnotes/typesetting, minor typos) and sparse intuitive explanation of the positivity condition and why R̂0 = r̂0 = 0 is the natural initialization. A compact proof sketch in the main text for Theorem 1 would help.

Originality
- The acceleration mechanism per se is not new for consensus, but the paper’s novelty lies in: (i) showing Min-Sum Splitting can be tuned to recover the shift-register accelerated rate; (ii) the auxiliary node-based linear system analysis enabling spectral-rate bounds; (iii) drawing conceptual connections between belief propagation with splitting, lifted nonreversible chains, and multi-step optimization. This appears to be a new angle.

Significance
- Consensus is a core primitive in distributed optimization and learning. Establishing that a BP-style scheme with splitting can attain near-optimal rates strengthens the bridge between message-passing and optimization. The technique (auxiliary node process) could be useful beyond consensus.
- Practical impact is limited by the need for global spectral information to set γ and by absence of empirical validation, but the theoretical contribution is nontrivial.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical advance: first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus with accelerated rates and explicit dependence on spectral quantities.
- Conceptual unification: connects Min-Sum Splitting to lifted chains and multi-step methods.
- General graph topologies and clean asymptotic bounds; square-root improvement over diffusive methods.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope restricted to quadratic consensus; no extension to general convex φv or to constraints beyond equalities.
- Requires knowledge (or a good bound) of ρ(W − 11T/n) to set γ. Is there a distributed way to estimate or adapt γ? How sensitive is performance to mis-specification?
- Synchronous, undirected, static graphs; no discussion of asynchrony, time-variation, or directed graphs.
- No experiments to assess constants, robustness, or communication cost vs. state-of-the-art (Chebyshev, Nesterov, ADMM).
- Communication/state overhead: edge-based messages vs. node-based two-state updates—what is the practical overhead compared to standard accelerated consensus?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add numerical experiments on cycles, grids, random geometric graphs comparing to Metropolis, optimal symmetric W, Chebyshev/shift-register, Nesterov, and ADMM; report wall-clock iterations and sensitivity to γ mis-tuning.
- Provide guidance for γ when ρW is unknown (e.g., using degree/diameter bounds, polynomial filtering/chebyshev approximations, or adaptive schemes).
- Discuss and, if possible, extend to asynchronous/time-varying graphs and directed graphs.
- Clarify the positivity condition in Prop. 1 and why the chosen initialization guarantees it.
- Include a brief proof sketch of Theorem 1 and intuition for the spectrum of K − K∞.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid and novel theoretical contribution; bridges communities (BP/message-passing, Markov chain lifting, accelerated optimization).
- Clear improvement over prior Min-Sum consensus analyses and generality across graph topologies.
- Potential to inspire extensions to broader distributed optimization problems.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theory-only with no empirical validation.
- Requires global spectral information; practicality unclear without tuning guidance.
- Narrow problem class (quadratic consensus), leaving open general convex settings central to ML.

Recommendation
Weak Accept. The paper offers a clean and interesting theoretical result connecting Min-Sum Splitting to accelerated consensus with near-optimal rates and introduces a useful analytical tool. Adding experiments and discussion on tuning/robustness would strengthen the contribution. Confidence: high.
