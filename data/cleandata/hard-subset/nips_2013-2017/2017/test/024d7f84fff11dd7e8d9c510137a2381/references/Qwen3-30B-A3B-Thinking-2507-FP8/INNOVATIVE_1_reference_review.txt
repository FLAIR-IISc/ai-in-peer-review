

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper makes a significant contribution by establishing the first convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem, and by revealing a profound theoretical connection between Min-Sum message-passing algorithms and two distinct lines of research on accelerated distributed optimization: lifted Markov chains and multi-step gradient methods.

The paper begins by contextualizing the consensus problem, noting that classical diffusive algorithms suffer from suboptimal convergence rates (O(D² log(1/ε))) for graphs with geometry, while recent accelerated methods have achieved better rates (O(D log(1/ε))). The authors then demonstrate that Min-Sum Splitting, a variant of the Min-Sum algorithm that incorporates "splitting" parameters, can achieve a square-root improvement in convergence rate (O(D log(D/ε))). The key insight is that proper tuning of the splitting parameters allows Min-Sum Splitting to recover the same accelerated rates as shift-register methods, which are known to achieve optimal convergence for consensus problems.

The most novel contribution is the theoretical connection the authors establish between Min-Sum Splitting and two different acceleration techniques. First, they show Min-Sum Splitting can be interpreted as a process on a "lifted space" (the space of directed edges), analogous to lifted Markov chains that break the diffusive nature of reversible chains. Second, they demonstrate that the splitting parameters introduce a memory term that makes Min-Sum Splitting resemble multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization. This dual connection is particularly valuable because it bridges two previously disconnected lines of research.

The paper's main technical contribution is Theorem 1, which establishes the asymptotic convergence rate of Min-Sum Splitting with optimally tuned parameters. The authors prove that the convergence rate ρK satisfies 1/2√(1/(1-ρW)) ≤ 1/(1-ρK) ≤ √(1/(1-ρW)), where ρW is the spectral radius of the diffusive method. This shows a square-root improvement over diffusive methods, which is significant for practical applications in sensor networks and distributed machine learning.

The paper's novelty is substantial. Prior work on Min-Sum for consensus ([27]) only established convergence for d-regular graphs and did not analyze convergence rates, while the current work provides a general convergence rate analysis for any graph topology. The connection to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods is also novel, as the authors note this relationship "seems to have not been established in the literature" prior to their work.

The paper is well-written, with a clear organization that builds from the problem statement to the main contributions. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and situates it within the broader context of distributed optimization. The theoretical analysis is sound, with the main proof technique (using an auxiliary process on the nodes rather than the directed edges) being both elegant and insightful.

The paper's significance is high. Consensus is a fundamental primitive in distributed systems, and the square-root improvement in convergence rate is practically meaningful. The connection to two different acceleration techniques not only provides theoretical insight but also suggests new research directions for applying Min-Sum to other problems beyond consensus. The authors rightly note that their findings "motivate further studies to generalize the acceleration due to splittings to other problem instances."

Minor weaknesses include the focus on quadratic objective functions (though this is standard for consensus analysis) and the reliance on spectral properties of the graph (which may require global knowledge). However, as a reviewer prioritizing novelty over methodological completeness, I view these as minor limitations rather than significant flaws.

This paper represents a significant theoretical advance in distributed optimization. The connection between Min-Sum message-passing and two distinct acceleration techniques is novel and insightful, and the convergence rate analysis provides a concrete, significant improvement over existing methods. The paper establishes a new theoretical framework that bridges different areas of research, which is exactly the kind of bold, novel contribution that NIPS should prioritize.

In summary, this paper makes a clear, significant contribution by:
1. Establishing the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting
2. Demonstrating a square-root improvement in convergence rate over diffusive methods
3. Revealing a novel connection between Min-Sum and two different acceleration techniques
4. Providing a new proof technique using an auxiliary process on the nodes

These contributions are substantial and advance the state of the art in distributed optimization. The paper is theoretically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field. Given the reviewer's emphasis on novelty and bold approaches, this paper should be accepted for NIPS.
