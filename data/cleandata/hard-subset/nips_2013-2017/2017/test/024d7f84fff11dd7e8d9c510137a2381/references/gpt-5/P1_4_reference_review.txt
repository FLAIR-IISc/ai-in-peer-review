Summary
This paper analyzes Min-Sum Splitting (a reparameterized variant of Min-Sum/Belief Propagation) for the consensus/averaging problem. The authors: (i) specialize Min-Sum Splitting to network averaging and show it induces a linear message-passing process on directed edges; (ii) construct an auxiliary linear process on nodes with memory (two-step dynamics) that exactly tracks the Min-Sum outputs; (iii) prove that with a particular choice of splitting parameters (δ = 1, Γ = γW with W symmetric, W1=1, and γ chosen from the spectral radius of W − 11^T/n), the method converges to the average with an accelerated, subdiffusive rate matching shift-register (Chebyshev) methods; (iv) obtain O(D log(D/ε)) convergence time on cycles and tori, a square-root improvement over diffusive consensus; and (v) conceptually relate Min-Sum Splitting to (non-reversible) lifted Markov chains and to accelerated multi-step first-order methods. The main technical device is the auxiliary node-based recursion (Algorithm 3) that allows a spectral analysis via a 2n×2n matrix K whose spectrum is known from shift-register methods.

Relation to prior work
- Ordinary Min-Sum can diverge on loopy graphs; sufficient conditions exist for unconstrained strictly convex problems (e.g., scaled diagonal dominance) but not for equality constraints. The consensus-specific Min-Sum variants in [27] required special initialization and led to mixing-time characterizations on the 2m edge space; rates were established for cycles and conjectured for tori.
- Accelerated consensus via lifted Markov chains [8,5,18,37] and via multi-step gradient methods/shift-registers [14,24,45] is well studied; the authors’ theorem reproduces the shift-register rate via Min-Sum Splitting and clarifies the role of directionality/memory.
- The link between Splitting, lifting, and multi-step momentum, and a rate proof for Min-Sum Splitting on general graphs, appear to be new.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: Propositions linking edge-based Min-Sum Splitting to a tractable auxiliary node-based linear system are elegant and broadly useful for analysis.
- Main theorem: clear spectral-rate characterization ρK = sqrt((1−√(1−ρW^2))/(1+√(1−ρW^2))) and convergence bound; recovers the accelerated square-root improvement vs diffusive consensus and aligns with shift-register optimality on geometric graphs.
- Conceptual contribution: bridges three lines of work—BP with splitting, lifted chains, and multi-step acceleration—clarifying how splitting’s directionality induces memory on nodes.
- Generality: the analysis covers arbitrary graphs (given W) and provides a pathway to parameter selection via spectral quantities.
- Novelty relative to [27]: general topologies; no need for d-regularity or edge-level mixing-time analysis.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope: Results are limited to quadratic consensus. The introduction emphasizes Min-Sum Splitting for general objectives, but the main theorem and guarantees do not extend beyond averaging. It would help to delineate which parts might generalize (e.g., strongly convex separable objectives with equality constraints) and what obstacles arise.
- Parameter knowledge: The accelerated choice uses γ depending on ρW, a global spectral quantity. This mirrors known limitations of lifted and multi-step methods; nonetheless, practical protocols to estimate or upper-bound ρW locally (or robust tuning rules) are not discussed.
- Practical significance: No experiments. Since the method reproduces shift-register dynamics on nodes via an auxiliary mapping, it is unclear when one would prefer implementing Min-Sum Splitting over directly implementing known accelerated consensus. Empirical comparisons (iterations and wall-clock time, message sizes, robustness) with Metropolis, optimally tuned W, Chebyshev/shift-register, Nesterov-type, EXTRA/ADMM, etc., would strengthen the case.
- Initialization and positivity: The theorem uses R̂0 = r̂0 = 0, yielding R0 = 1, Q0 = 0. The paper assumes denominators remain positive; sufficient conditions ensuring R^t_v > 0 (or handling sign changes) should be stated. How sensitive is convergence to other feasible initializations?
- Asynchrony, noise, quantization, link failures: The analysis is synchronous and noiseless. Given the BP pedigree, a discussion of robustness and possible asynchronous extensions would be valuable.
- Clarity/minor issues: Some notation is dense and occasionally inconsistent (typos in quadratic objective, formatting of Algorithm 2/3, conditions like 0 < b_v < 1 which are unnecessary). A compact proof sketch of Theorem 1 in the main text would aid readability.

Quality
The technical core appears sound: the derivations of the linear edge recursion and auxiliary node recursion are carefully specified; the spectral rate matches classical Chebyshev/shift-register analysis. The work is a complete theoretical contribution, albeit focused on a special but important case. Proofs are deferred to the supplement; adding intuition and intermediate lemmas in the main text would help.

Clarity
Overall well organized, but quite dense. The algorithms and matrices (K̂, K) are clearly defined. Suggested improvements: streamline notation; fix typos; briefly justify positivity of denominators; add a small illustrative example (e.g., path graph) to build intuition; provide a short proof sketch of the main theorem.

Originality
High in the specific combination: first rate analysis of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus; novel auxiliary-process technique; explicit connection to acceleration mechanisms in two disparate literatures. While the achieved rate is not new per se, demonstrating that Min-Sum Splitting can attain it and explaining why is original.

Significance
Moderate to high for the theory/algorithms community interested in BP, distributed optimization, and consensus. The results could stimulate further accelerated BP-style methods beyond averaging. Practical impact would be clearer with experiments and extensions beyond quadratics.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, rigorous theoretical contribution with new analysis tools.
- Bridges important literatures; clarifies acceleration via Splitting.
- Achieves near-optimal accelerated rates on broad graph classes.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited to averaging; no empirical validation.
- Requires global spectral information for tuning.
- Advantages over directly using known accelerated consensus methods are conceptual more than practical at present.

Suggestions
- Add experiments comparing to Metropolis, optimally tuned W, Chebyshev/shift-register, Nesterov, EXTRA/ADMM on cycles, grids, random geometric graphs.
- Discuss parameter estimation/tuning without global knowledge (e.g., Chebyshev with bounds on spectrum, adaptive schemes).
- Clarify conditions ensuring positivity of R^t and sensitivity to initialization; comment on asynchrony/robustness.
- Expand discussion on extending beyond quadratics and on relations to ADMM-based message passing.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper provides a solid and novel theoretical bridge with a clean rate result, appropriate for NeurIPS’ theory track. Empirical validation and broader scope would strengthen it further.
