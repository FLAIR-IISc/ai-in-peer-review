

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving the consensus problem in distributed networks. The authors establish convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, showing it achieves subdiffusive convergence (O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori) compared to the diffusive rates (O(D² log(1/ε))) of classical methods. The work makes two key contributions: (1) establishing the first proof of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, and (2) revealing novel connections between Min-Sum Splitting and two types of accelerated methods: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization.

The paper builds on prior work in the field. Min-Sum has been studied for distributed optimization (e.g., [28, 25, 34, 35]), and the consensus problem has been extensively investigated in the literature (e.g., [42, 41, 7, 9, 20, 21]). Previous work on Min-Sum for consensus [27] showed that the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm does not converge in graphs with cycles, and proposed a modified version with convergence time O(D/ε log 1/ε) for cycle graphs. The current paper extends this by showing that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve faster convergence rates.

The paper's main technical contribution is the introduction of an auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) that allows tracking the evolution of Min-Sum Splitting via a linear process with memory on the nodes of the graph. This construction is crucial for establishing the convergence rate analysis. The authors prove that a proper choice of splitting parameters (δ = 1 and Γ = γW, where γ = 2/(1 + √(1-ρ²_W))) recovers the same matrix that supports shift-register methods for consensus [45, 4, 24], achieving the asymptotic rate ρ_K = √((1-√(1-ρ²_W))/(1+√(1-ρ²_W))) < ρ_W < 1. This results in a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods.

The paper makes a strong connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two established lines of research: lifted Markov chains (which introduce directionality to break diffusive behavior) and multi-step first-order methods (which add memory terms). This connection is novel and well-articulated, showing how Min-Sum Splitting combines aspects of both approaches without requiring lifted graph constructions.

Strengths:
1. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured, with clear proofs of the main results.
2. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and accelerated methods is novel and significant.
3. The paper provides a thorough literature review that properly contextualizes the work.
4. The auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) is a valuable technical contribution that simplifies the analysis.
5. The paper acknowledges limitations, particularly the need for global information to choose parameters, which is an important practical consideration.

Weaknesses:
1. The analysis is limited to the quadratic consensus problem (network averaging), which is a specific case. The paper would benefit from discussing how the results might generalize to other consensus problems.
2. The paper lacks empirical validation of the theoretical results; the convergence rates are only theoretically established without experimental verification.
3. The requirement for global information (the spectral gap ρ_W) to choose parameters might limit practical applicability, though the authors acknowledge this limitation.
4. The paper could provide more intuition about why the specific parameter choice (δ = 1, Γ = γW) yields accelerated convergence.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The theoretical results are significant as they establish that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve convergence rates close to the optimal O(D log(1/ε)), matching results from Nesterov's acceleration [30, 36]. The connection to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods is novel and could inspire future work in accelerated distributed algorithms.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to analysis to results. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the notation is consistent throughout. The authors provide sufficient background to understand the context of their work, and they properly reference related contributions.

In terms of significance, the results have potential implications for distributed machine learning, sensor networks, and multi-agent systems where consensus is a fundamental primitive. The accelerated convergence rates could lead to faster distributed computation in large-scale networks.

Pro: Theoretical contribution is strong, with rigorous proofs of convergence rates and novel connections to established accelerated methods. The auxiliary process technique is a valuable technical contribution. The paper is well-written and properly contextualized within the literature.

Con: Limited to quadratic consensus problem without empirical validation. Requires global information for parameter selection, which may limit practical applicability. Lacks discussion of generalizations to non-quadratic problems.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of Min-Sum algorithms for distributed optimization. The novelty of the connections to accelerated methods and the rigorous convergence rate analysis are particularly strong aspects. While the lack of empirical results and limitation to the quadratic case are weaknesses, the theoretical contribution is substantial and would be valuable to the NIPS community. The paper meets the standards of quality, clarity, originality, and significance expected for NIPS.
