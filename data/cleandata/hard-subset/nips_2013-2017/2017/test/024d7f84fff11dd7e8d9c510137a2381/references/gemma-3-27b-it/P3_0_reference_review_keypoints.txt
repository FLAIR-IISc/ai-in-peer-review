- Main result: Shows Min-Sum Splitting can achieve accelerated convergence (O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles and tori) improving over diffusive O(D^2 log(1/ε)) rates.  
- Technical approach: Derives a linear message-passing update and an auxiliary node process, linking convergence to the spectral gap of a derived matrix.  
- Connections: Bridges Min-Sum splitting with lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, including links to shift-register techniques.  
- Novelty over prior work: Extends [27] by providing general convergence-rate analysis and situating Min-Sum splitting within broader accelerated-consensus literature.  
- Strength — theoretical rigor: Provides rigorous proofs and a novel proof technique via the auxiliary process.  
- Strength — interdisciplinary insight: Successfully connects message-passing, spectral graph theory, and optimization perspectives.  
- Strength — parameter clarity: Clearly identifies splitting parameters (δ and Γ) and how to tune them for acceleration.  
- Strength — presentation: Well-organized and generally well-written with useful background material.  
- Strength — literature coverage: Comprehensive review of related work in consensus, lifted chains, and acceleration.  
- Weakness — accessibility: Mathematically dense and may be hard to follow for readers unfamiliar with message passing or spectral graph theory.  
- Weakness — no experiments: Entirely theoretical with no simulations to empirically validate the claimed speedups.  
- Weakness — practical tuning: Optimal parameter choice relies on the graph spectral gap, which is impractical to obtain in real distributed settings.  
- Weakness — limited topology scope: Accelerated results shown for specific topologies (cycles, tori); practical impact vs. algorithm overhead is unclear.  
- Weakness — objective limitation: Analysis is tied to quadratic objectives; generalization to other convex objectives is uncertain.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, theoretically sound, bridges multiple fields, and advances understanding of distributed consensus.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Lacks empirical validation, may be inaccessible due to density, limited practical applicability due to spectral gap requirement, and limited generality beyond quadratics.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Required revisions: add simulations (even small-scale), discuss sensitivity to spectral-gap estimation, comment on generalization to other convex objectives, and improve intuitive explanations and clarity.
