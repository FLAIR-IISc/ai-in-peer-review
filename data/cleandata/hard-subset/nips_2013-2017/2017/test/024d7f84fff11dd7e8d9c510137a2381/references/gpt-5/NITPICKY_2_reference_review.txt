Summary
The paper studies the Min-Sum Splitting (Ruozzi–Tatikonda) message-passing algorithm for consensus/averaging on graphs. It (i) specializes Min-Sum Splitting to quadratic consensus, (ii) shows that with a specific parametrization the edge-based algorithm becomes linear, (iii) constructs an auxiliary node-based 2n-dimensional linear iteration with memory that exactly tracks the edge-based dynamics, and (iv) proves an accelerated convergence rate for consensus by tuning Γ = γW with γ depending on the spectral gap of a symmetric, stochastic W. The main theorem shows that, with δ = 1 and Γ = γW, the induced node-level iteration K matches the shift-register/Chebyshev-accelerated iteration and yields a “square-root improvement” in mixing time over diffusive methods: for cycles/tori, O(D log(D/ε)) iterations versus Θ(D^2 log(1/ε)). The paper also argues conceptual connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step acceleration.

Relation to previous work
- Classical consensus (Xiao–Boyd, SCL’04; many others) optimizes W and obtains diffusive scaling Θ(1/(1−ρW)). Chebyshev/shift-register acceleration and heavy-ball/Nesterov multi-step methods for consensus and distributed optimization are well-known (e.g., Young ’71; Golub–Varga ’61; Liu et al. Automatica; Ghadimi et al. TSP’15; Scaman et al. ICML’17).
- Consensus propagation and GaBP/Min-Sum for consensus were analyzed in [27], with edge-lifted linear dynamics and mixing-time characterizations in special cases.
- Lifting for accelerated gossip (Diaconis–Holmes–Neal; Jung–Shah–Shin) offers subdiffusive rates via directionality.

The paper’s technical novelty is the exact derivation of an auxiliary node-space linear system tracking Min-Sum Splitting and the demonstration that, with an appropriate Γ, this recovers the shift-register matrix and its rate, thereby giving the first rigorous rate guarantees for Min-Sum Splitting in consensus.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual bridge: shows Min-Sum Splitting induces directionality on edges and corresponds, in node-space, to a multi-step method with memory.
- The auxiliary node process K(δ,Γ) is elegant and analytically convenient: for δ = 1 and Γ = γW with W1 = 1, K reduces to the classic 2×2 block shift-register form.
- Theorem 1’s rate matches known accelerated rates, improving over diffusive bounds; the dependence on ρW and the parameter choice γ = 2/(1+√(1−ρW^2)) aligns with Chebyshev optimality.
- Addresses prior gap: [27] analyzed rates for specific regular graphs via an edge-space spectral argument; here the analysis is general and simpler via node-space tracking.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Over-claiming/novelty: The accelerated rate and the K matrix in Theorem 1 reproduce known shift-register/Chebyshev acceleration. The new part is linking that to Min-Sum Splitting via a particular Γ, but the algorithmic effect at nodes is exactly the established shift-register iteration. The claim “first proof of how directionality in BP can be tuned to accelerate” seems strong; damping/reweighting and walk-sum perspectives on GaBP have explored related ideas.
- Parameter knowledge and practicality: γ depends on ρW (global spectral information). The paper acknowledges this but does not discuss how to compute/approximate ρW distributively, robustness to misspecification, or adaptive schemes. For NeurIPS, some guidance or analysis of robustness would be valuable.
- Assumptions and consistency:
  - δ = 1 in Theorem 1 (no node splitting); acceleration comes from choosing Γ ≠ A (scaled W). This slightly undermines the narrative that “splitting” (δ,Γ) induces acceleration via directionality; here δ plays no role.
  - Proposition 1 requires positivity: 1 + δ∑w Γwv R̂t_wv > 0 to ensure a proper quadratic minimizer for µ_t^v. This is not verified under the proposed initialization beyond t = 1; the main theorem sidesteps this by working with r_t/R_t in the auxiliary scheme, but the equivalence of outputs then implicitly relies on that positivity (or a precise argument that the ratio formula equals the argmin without it). This requires clarification/proof.
  - Γ sparsity vs W’s diagonal: Algorithm 1 assumes Γ respects G’s sparsity (off-diagonal). Theorem 1 sets Γ = γW with W1 = 1 (W typically has nonzero diagonal). The sums in the algorithm exclude self-loops via N(v), and K(δ,Γ) uses diag(Γ1), so the construction can work, but the paper should explicitly state that Γvv is unused/irrelevant and that Γ1 = γ1 suffices for K to match the shift-register form.
- Missing empirical validation: No experiments (cycles, grids, random geometric graphs) are presented to confirm the theory, illustrate constants/transients, or compare to (i) Metropolis, (ii) Chebyshev, (iii) Nesterov/Heavy-ball consensus, (iv) consensus propagation. Even simple plots on cycles/tori would strengthen the case.
- Limited scope: Only quadratic consensus is treated. While natural for consensus, claims about “generalizing to other problems” are speculative without evidence.
- Clarity and organization issues:
  - Several equation/algorithm cross-references are inconsistent: K̂ is “defined in (5)” in Algorithm 2 but earlier appears as (4); K(δ,Γ) is also (5). Please fix numbering.
  - The “st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)” line appears in the middle of the introduction—formatting artifact.
  - Footnotes appear inline and partially garbled (e.g., “2 φv(z) := 12z − bvz”), and many 1/2 factors are typeset as “12”.
  - Reference list is incomplete and inconsistently formatted (missing years, venues, pages). Some citations in text (e.g., [33]) are ambiguous in the provided bibliography.
  - Algorithm statements mix hat/unhat variables and edge/node indices densely; a small illustrative example (path or cycle) would aid readability.
- Comparison to state of the art: The paper cites O(D log(1/ε)) optimal results but does not precisely reconcile the extra log(D) factor in its bound or discuss whether tighter constants/analysis can remove it (Chebyshev acceleration often achieves O(D log(1/ε))).
- Reproducibility: All proofs are deferred; some key steps (equivalence conditions, positivity, spectral mapping) deserve at least sketches in the main text.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you rigorously ensure the positivity condition in Proposition 1 for the recommended initialization, or justify that the ratio r_t/R_t equals the argmin even if the quadratic coefficient is nonpositive?
2. Is the extra log(D) in the convergence time intrinsic to your analysis, or can you tighten to O(D log(1/ε))?
3. How sensitive is the method to γ? Can you provide bounds for γ-mismatch or a practical heuristic to estimate ρW?
4. Could you include numerical experiments comparing to Chebyshev, heavy-ball, Nesterov, and consensus propagation on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs?

Pros
- Clean theoretical link between Min-Sum Splitting and multi-step acceleration.
- General rate result via node-space auxiliary dynamics; simpler than prior edge-space analyses.
- Demonstrates subdiffusive rates for consensus using a BP-style method.

Cons
- Significant presentation/formatting issues and inconsistent cross-references.
- Novelty is incremental relative to known Chebyshev acceleration; no experiments.
- Practical aspects (parameter selection, robustness) not addressed.
- Some technical conditions unclear (positivity, exact equivalence of outputs).

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The core idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper in its current form suffers from clarity and polish issues, lacks empirical validation, and somewhat overstates novelty. With tightened analysis (especially regarding equivalence/positivity), cleaned presentation, and experiments, it could be a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
