Summary
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting (a reparameterized variant of min-sum/Gaussian BP) for the consensus/averaging problem on graphs. The authors (i) show that, for quadratic objectives encoding consensus, Min-Sum Splitting reduces to a linear message-passing scheme on directed edges; (ii) introduce an auxiliary linear process on nodes (with memory) that exactly tracks the edge-based dynamics; and (iii) with a specific choice of splitting parameters (δ = 1, Γ = γW where W is a symmetric averaging matrix and γ depends on the spectral radius of W − 11^T/n), establish an accelerated convergence rate matching shift-register/Chebyshev semi-iterative methods. They derive asymptotic convergence O(ρ_K^t) with 1/(1−ρ_K) ≈ √(1/(1−ρ_W)), implying O(D log(D/ε)) iterations on cycles and tori, improving over diffusive O(D^2 log(1/ε)). Conceptually, the work connects Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chains (via edge-based directionality) and multi-step acceleration (via node-level memory).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivations appear careful. Proposition 1 shows quadratic closure of messages and converts Algorithm 1 to a linear update (Algorithm 2). Proposition 2 rigorously connects the edge and node recursions via initialization. Theorem 1 leverages known spectral analysis for shift-register methods to obtain the accelerated rate. The bounds and choice γ = 2/(1+√(1−ρ_W^2)) are consistent with Chebyshev-type acceleration.
- Scope: Results are for synchronous, static, undirected graphs and quadratic consensus (averaging). The need for γ tuned to ρ_W is clearly acknowledged.
- Missing pieces: No empirical validation; finite-time constants and robustness are not discussed. The positivity condition in Prop. 1 and feasibility of Γ with potentially negative entries merit more discussion.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized, with a clear line from Min-Sum Splitting to the auxiliary node process and to the final rate. The explanation of how equality constraints are encoded in φ_vw (as hard barriers vs. penalties) could be made more explicit in Section 5 to reassure readers about well-defined minimizations and the origin of linearity.
- Algorithm notation is heavy; adding a brief intuitive explanation of K(δ,Γ) and the role of each block would help. The assumption W may have negative entries (optimal W) should be stated earlier and reconciled with message updates.

Originality
- The accelerated rate itself is not new—Chebyshev/shift-register acceleration for consensus is well established (e.g., Xiao & Boyd 2004; Ghadimi et al. 2013; Liu et al. 2015; Scaman et al. 2017). The novelty lies in: (a) showing Min-Sum Splitting can be tuned to recover the same accelerated rate; (b) introducing an auxiliary node-level linear system that makes the analysis of an edge-supported BP-like algorithm tractable; and (c) drawing conceptual links to lifted non-reversible walks. This is a meaningful and original connection between communities (BP/message passing and accelerated consensus).

Significance
- Conceptual significance is solid: it unifies perspectives (BP, lifting, multi-step methods) and may inspire analogous acceleration for other Min-Sum tasks beyond consensus.
- Practical significance is limited by the need for global spectral information to choose γ, and by a lack of experiments or implementation guidance. Still, the result narrows the perceived gap between BP and state-of-the-art accelerated consensus.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution: reduction to node-level multi-step linear system and spectral analysis.
- First rate guarantees for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus; accelerated asymptotics with O(D log(D/ε)) on geometric graphs.
- Insightful connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods; recovers known optimal K matrix structure.
- Improves upon prior Min-Sum work [27], which had topology-specific results and conjectures.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Applicability limited to quadratic consensus; no discussion of general convex φ_v or constrained variants with hard barriers beyond quadratic case.
- Requires global knowledge (ρ_W) to set γ; no decentralized estimation or robust tuning strategy is provided.
- No empirical evaluation comparing to Chebyshev acceleration, Nesterov-like schemes, EXTRA/Exact Diffusion, or distributed ADMM—making it hard to assess constants and pre-asymptotic behavior.
- The initialization and positivity condition in Proposition 1 deserve a proof sketch and practical guidance; with δ = 1, ensuring 1 + Σ Γ R̂ stays positive for all t is nontrivial.
- The choice W is assumed symmetric with W1=1; joint design of W and γ is not discussed, nor is the cost of using potentially negative weights.
- Communication/computation overhead: the edge-based algorithm maintains parameters per directed edge; a complexity comparison with node-based accelerated schemes is missing.

Requests for clarification
- How exactly are the equality constraints encoded in φ_vw to yield the stated linear-quadratic updates? Please present the explicit φ_vw used.
- Under the recommended initialization R̂0 = r̂0 = 0, can you guarantee the positivity condition in Prop. 1 for all t? If so, include a lemma.
- Practical tuning: Can one use upper bounds on ρ_W (e.g., via degrees, diameter) to set a conservative γ with guaranteed acceleration? Any robust range for γ?
- Can the auxiliary K(δ,Γ) approach extend to strongly convex smooth objectives beyond averaging (e.g., distributed least squares with edge penalties)? What breaks?
- How does the proposed method compare empirically to Chebyshev acceleration and to EXTRA/Exact Diffusion or distributed ADMM on grids and geometric graphs?

Minor comments and suggestions
- Tie the result more explicitly to the Chebyshev semi-iterative method and cite foundational works (Golub & Varga 1961; Young 1971) alongside consensus papers.
- Discuss the trade-off between using W = WMH vs. optimized W (SDP-based), and its impact on ρ_W and ρ_K.
- Consider adding a short numerical section to validate the rate and constants on cycles and grids.

Overall assessment
- Pros: Solid theory; clear novelty in bridging BP/Splitting with accelerated consensus; improves over prior Min-Sum analyses; likely to interest both ML and control/distributed optimization communities.
- Cons: Narrow problem class; requires global tuning; missing experiments; practical implications not fully explored.

Score: Weak accept, conditional on clarifying the constraint encoding and positivity, and ideally adding a brief empirical validation.

Reviewer confidence: High.
