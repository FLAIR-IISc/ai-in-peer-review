This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors establish convergence and derive rates, demonstrating a square-root improvement over diffusive methods and achieving competitive performance with recently established optimal rates. The work builds upon prior research on Min-Sum and Splitting schemes [34, 35] and connects the analysis to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, offering a unified perspective.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first theoretical analysis of the convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting applied to consensus, a significant contribution. Establishing a square-root improvement over diffusive methods is a noteworthy result.
* **Connections to Existing Work:**  The authors effectively connect Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, providing a broader context and highlighting its relationship to established techniques. This connection is valuable for researchers in both fields.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The analysis utilizes an auxiliary process to track the algorithm’s evolution, a clever technique that simplifies the analysis and leads to a clear understanding of the convergence behavior. The link to shift-register methods is also well-established.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The paper generally presents the ideas in a clear and organized manner. The introduction provides sufficient background and motivation.
* **Well-Referenced:** The related work section is comprehensive, covering relevant literature on consensus algorithms, Min-Sum schemes, and accelerated methods.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Notational Density & Accessibility:** While the theoretical analysis is rigorous, the notation is very dense and can be difficult to follow, particularly in Sections 5 and 6. More explanation of the variables and matrices would improve readability.  The reader unfamiliar with the specific notation from [27] will struggle.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses entirely on theoretical analysis. While the analysis is strong, a small-scale numerical example demonstrating the improved convergence rates would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Overly Detailed Background:** The extensive review of existing algorithms (Sections 3 & 4) feels somewhat lengthy and detracts from the core contribution. It’s valuable, but could be more concise. The literature review sometimes reads like a survey rather than focused justification of novelty.
* **Motivation for Parameter Choices:** The specific choice of parameters (δ = 1, Γ = γW) is justified through the convergence analysis, but a more intuitive explanation of *why* this particular choice leads to acceleration would be beneficial.
* **Practical Considerations:** The practical implications of the results aren't fully discussed.  How easy is it to estimate the parameters needed for optimal performance in real-world scenarios? What are the computational costs associated with the splitting procedure?



**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

* **Section 2:** The explanation of splitting and the connection to the original problem formulation could be more intuitive.
* **Section 5 & 6:** These sections are the most technically challenging.  Adding intermediate steps in derivations and providing more intuitive explanations of the variables and matrices would be extremely helpful. A visual representation of the auxiliary algorithm could also improve understanding.  Perhaps a simple example with a small graph would help illustrate how the transformation works.
* **Theorem 1:** The statement of the theorem is dense. Breaking it down into smaller, more digestible parts would improve clarity.
* **Discussion:** The discussion could benefit from a more nuanced comparison with existing methods. Specifically, discussing the trade-offs between Min-Sum Splitting and other accelerated methods in terms of computational complexity and implementation effort would be valuable.
* **Future Work:** Expanding on potential generalizations of the acceleration due to splitting to other problems, as suggested, would be useful.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of distributed optimization.
* The convergence analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the behavior of Min-Sum Splitting.
* The connection to existing work is well-established and demonstrates the significance of the results.
* The potential for improved convergence rates is substantial.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper's density and complex notation may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
* The lack of empirical validation weakens the overall impact of the results.
* The extensive background material could be streamlined to focus more on the core contribution.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of Min-Sum Splitting and its convergence properties. The core ideas are novel and the analysis is rigorous. However, the paper’s density and lack of empirical validation are weaknesses. I believe that with some revisions to improve clarity and accessibility, and the addition of a simple numerical example, this paper could be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

**Recommendation:**  **Weak Accept**.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon addressing the concerns regarding clarity, accessibility, and empirical validation. The authors should prioritize improving the readability of Sections 5 and 6 and providing a small-scale numerical demonstration of the improved convergence rates.
