This paper presents a significant and novel advancement in the analysis of Min-Sum Splitting algorithms for consensus, establishing convergence rates and demonstrating an acceleration over traditional diffusive methods. The core idea – connecting Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods – is a strong contribution. The mathematical analysis appears rigorous, and the result of achieving a square-root improvement in convergence time (O(D log(D/ε)) vs. O(D² log(1/ε)) for cycles/tori) is impressive. While the presentation is dense, it is ultimately clear and logically structured. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The connection between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated optimization is a genuinely novel insight.  This provides a fresh perspective on distributed consensus algorithms.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper delivers a solid theoretical analysis, culminating in a proven convergence rate improvement. The auxiliary process construction is clever and enables tractable analysis.
* **Significance:**  The acceleration achieved is practically meaningful, particularly for graphs with geometry where diffusive methods struggle.  This has potential implications for distributed machine learning, sensor networks, and multi-agent systems.
* **Clear Contribution:** The authors clearly articulate their contributions in the introduction and throughout the paper. The connection to existing work (lifted chains, Nesterov’s acceleration) is well explained.
* **Well-defined problem setting**: The application to the consensus problem provides a concrete and tractable setting for the analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Density of Presentation:** The paper is mathematically dense and requires significant effort from the reader to follow all the details. More intuitive explanations could be helpful.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper is primarily theoretical. While the analysis is strong, some limited empirical results, even on small graphs, would strengthen the claims and build confidence. Specifically, demonstrating the asymptotic rate improvement in practice would be valuable.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The optimal choice of the splitting parameter (γ) depends on the spectral gap of the graph. In practice, estimating this spectral gap accurately may be challenging.  A discussion of practical methods for parameter tuning would be beneficial.
* **Scope of Applicability**: The analysis appears focused on quadratic consensus problems.  It's unclear how well the results generalize to other consensus formulations or more complex objective functions.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The novelty and theoretical significance of the work are high. The insights regarding the connection between Min-Sum Splitting and other acceleration techniques are substantial.
* The achieved convergence rate improvement is a meaningful contribution to the field of distributed optimization.
* The mathematical analysis appears sound and the proofs are well-structured (though detailed and requiring careful reading).
* The paper addresses a fundamental problem in distributed systems.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of empirical validation weakens the practical impact of the results. While not essential for a theoretical paper, even limited experiments would enhance credibility.
* The density of the presentation might limit accessibility to a wider audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend **acceptance**. Despite the density of the presentation and the lack of extensive empirical validation, the paper’s novelty, theoretical rigor, and potential significance are compelling. The demonstrated convergence rate improvement is a significant achievement. The weaknesses are not critical and can be addressed in future work. This paper introduces a novel framework for analyzing and accelerating distributed consensus algorithms, and is a valuable contribution to the field. I would place this paper in the top 10% of submissions.
