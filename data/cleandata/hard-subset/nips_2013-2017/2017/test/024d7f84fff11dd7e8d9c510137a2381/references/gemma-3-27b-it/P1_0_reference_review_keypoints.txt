- Summary: Shows Min-Sum Splitting converges for general graphs and gives accelerated rates (square-root improvement), e.g., O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles/tori, using an auxiliary linear process to track evolution.  
- Key technical contribution: Introduces an auxiliary node-level linear process that simplifies convergence analysis and links Min-Sum Splitting to lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on earlier Min-Sum consensus results (which showed non-convergence), situates results among lifted Markov chain and accelerated gradient literature, and connects to shift-register methods.  
- Strength: Strong theoretical results with explicit convergence rates previously absent in the literature.  
- Strength: Novel application of Min-Sum Splitting to achieve accelerated consensus, unifying several existing approaches.  
- Strength: Clear exposition and well-organized presentation that motivates contributions.  
- Strength: The auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) is a clever analytical tool.  
- Strength: Demonstrates concrete connections to shift-register and other existing methods, aiding interpretation.  
- Weakness: Heavy and sometimes confusing notation; readability could improve with a streamlined presentation.  
- Weakness: No empirical validation; limited experiments would help verify practical speedups.  
- Weakness: Convergence proofs rely on specific initial conditions—sensitivity to other initializations is not discussed.  
- Weakness: Parameter choices (γ and W) depend on spectral gap and may be hard to compute in practice; practical selection strategies are missing.  
- Quality score: 9/10 (technically sound and rigorous).  
- Clarity score: 7/10 (generally clear but dense notation hinders readability).  
- Originality score: 9/10 (novel approach and useful connections).  
- Significance score: 8/10 (theoretically important with potential practical impact).  
- Arguments for acceptance: Rigorous, novel theoretical advances with clear connections to established methods and significant potential impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Lack of experiments, accessibility issues from notation, and practical parameter-tuning challenges.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong Accept, with encouragement to add limited empirical results and streamline notation in future revisions.
