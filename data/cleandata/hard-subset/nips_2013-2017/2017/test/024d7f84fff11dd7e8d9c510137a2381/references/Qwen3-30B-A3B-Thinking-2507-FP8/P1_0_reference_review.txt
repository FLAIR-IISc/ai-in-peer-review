

# Peer Review: "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving consensus problems in distributed networks, establishing novel convergence rate guarantees. The authors demonstrate that with proper parameter tuning, Min-Sum Splitting achieves subdiffusive convergence rates (O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, where D is the graph diameter), representing a square-root improvement over traditional diffusive methods (O(D² log(1/ε))). 

The paper makes several key contributions: (1) It establishes the first connection between Min-Sum schemes and both lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step methods in convex optimization; (2) It provides the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting; (3) It introduces a novel proof technique using an auxiliary process to track the algorithm's evolution on nodes rather than on directed edges; and (4) It shows that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve convergence rates matching shift-register methods by properly tuning the splitting parameters.

The authors establish that Min-Sum Splitting can be interpreted as a process on a lifted space (directed edges), analogous to lifted Markov chains, while simultaneously exhibiting memory terms characteristic of multi-step accelerated methods. This dual perspective provides new theoretical insights into why Min-Sum Splitting can achieve accelerated convergence.

This work builds on prior research by [27], which showed that ordinary Min-Sum does not converge for graphs with cycles, and investigated a modified version with soft barriers. The current paper extends this work by showing that Min-Sum Splitting (with proper parameters) converges for all graph topologies and achieves better convergence rates than the methods in [27].

## Strengths

1. **Strong Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides rigorous convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, which has been lacking in the literature. The authors establish the first convergence rate guarantees for this algorithm, which is a significant theoretical contribution.

2. **Novel Connections**: The paper makes a compelling connection between Min-Sum schemes and two distinct lines of accelerated methods (lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods). This theoretical insight is valuable for understanding why Min-Sum Splitting works and how it can be improved.

3. **Clear Technical Presentation**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical flow from problem statement to algorithm to analysis. The auxiliary process construction (Algorithm 3) is particularly elegant and provides a powerful tool for analysis.

4. **Significant Practical Impact**: The results demonstrate that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve convergence rates that are competitive with the best-known methods for consensus problems, with a clear square-root improvement over traditional diffusive methods. This has important implications for distributed machine learning and sensor networks.

5. **Honest Assessment of Limitations**: The authors correctly note that the optimal parameter choices depend on global information about the graph (the spectral gap), which is a limitation for fully decentralized implementations. This demonstrates intellectual honesty about the practical constraints of their approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Validation**: While the paper provides strong theoretical results, it lacks experimental validation to support the theoretical claims. It would be valuable to see empirical results demonstrating the convergence rates in practice for various graph topologies.

2. **Parameter Tuning Complexity**: The optimal parameter choice (Γ = γW, where γ depends on ρW) requires knowledge of the spectral properties of the graph. For large-scale networks, computing this globally might be challenging. The paper could discuss potential decentralized approaches to estimate these parameters.

3. **Assumptions on Graph Structure**: The analysis focuses on graphs with polynomial growth or finite doubling dimension (as in [7]), but it's not clear how the results extend to arbitrary graph topologies. The paper might benefit from discussing this limitation.

4. **Comparison to Recent Methods**: While the paper references some accelerated methods, it could provide a more detailed comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art methods for consensus, such as those in [30, 36, 45] that have recently achieved optimal rates.

## Assessment of Criteria

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The proofs in the supplementary material (referred to in the main text) appear to be well-constructed. The authors carefully distinguish their approach from prior work and provide clear theoretical guarantees. The paper is a complete piece of work rather than a position paper. However, the lack of experimental validation slightly limits the quality assessment.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the key concepts (Min-Sum Splitting, auxiliary process, etc.) are clearly explained. The flow from problem statement to algorithm to analysis is logical. The supplementary material would likely provide additional clarity for readers interested in the technical details.

**Originality**: This work is highly original in its theoretical contribution. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods is novel, as acknowledged by the authors. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work on Min-Sum (e.g., [27]) and establishes new theoretical ground. The convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting is a significant original contribution.

**Significance**: The results have significant theoretical and practical importance. The square-root improvement in convergence rates over diffusive methods is substantial for large-scale distributed systems. The insights connecting different areas of research (belief propagation, Markov chains, optimization) provide new theoretical perspectives that could inspire future work. The findings have clear applications in distributed machine learning and sensor networks, making this a significant contribution to the field.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- Provides the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting
- Establishes novel theoretical connections between belief propagation and two major lines of accelerated methods
- Achieves a square-root improvement in convergence rates over diffusive methods
- Introduces a novel proof technique using an auxiliary process
- Demonstrates theoretical competitiveness with state-of-the-art accelerated methods
- Well-written with clear mathematical presentation
- Makes important contributions to understanding distributed algorithms

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- Lacks experimental validation of the theoretical results
- Parameter tuning requires global knowledge of the graph, which might limit practical applicability
- Analysis limited to graphs with polynomial growth or finite doubling dimension
- Does not fully address how to estimate the required spectral properties in a distributed setting

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contributions are significant, novel, and well-supported. While the lack of experimental validation is a minor weakness, the paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of distributed consensus algorithms. The connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods provide valuable theoretical insights that will likely inspire future research.

The paper is a strong fit for NIPS, as it addresses an important problem in distributed optimization with theoretical depth and significance. The results contribute meaningfully to the state of the art in distributed algorithms for machine learning.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include experimental results demonstrating the convergence rates for various graph topologies (cycles, tori, etc.) to validate the theoretical claims.

2. Discuss potential decentralized approaches to estimate the spectral properties needed for optimal parameter tuning.

3. Provide a more detailed comparison with the most recent accelerated consensus methods, particularly those achieving optimal O(D log(1/ε)) rates.

4. Consider extending the analysis to more general graph topologies beyond those with polynomial growth.

5. Clarify the computational complexity of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm compared to other accelerated methods.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes a significant advance in understanding distributed consensus algorithms. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
