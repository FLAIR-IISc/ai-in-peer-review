

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates Min-Sum Splitting, a message-passing algorithm for distributed optimization, and establishes convergence rates for its application to the consensus problem (network averaging). The authors demonstrate that Min-Sum Splitting achieves subdiffusive convergence rates (O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori), representing a square-root improvement over diffusive methods (O(D² log(1/ε))). The paper establishes connections between Min-Sum Splitting and two established approaches to accelerated consensus: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods. The key technical contribution is an auxiliary process that tracks Min-Sum Splitting's evolution on graph nodes, enabling the convergence analysis.

The paper relates well to prior work. It correctly cites [27] as the previous study on Min-Sum for consensus, which only analyzed diffusive rates for d-regular graphs. The authors acknowledge [34] as introducing Min-Sum Splitting but note the lack of convergence rate analysis. The paper also properly situates itself within the broader literature on accelerated consensus methods, distinguishing between lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods as described in [7, 13, 37].

Quality: The paper presents a technically sound theoretical analysis with rigorous proofs for convergence rates. The authors carefully establish the connection between Min-Sum Splitting and existing accelerated methods, and they appropriately address both strengths (subdiffusive rates) and limitations (dependence on global graph information) of their approach. The analysis is complete for the quadratic consensus problem, a standard starting point for such studies. However, the paper lacks empirical validation of the theoretical claims, which would strengthen the quality assessment.

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The main contributions are explicitly stated, and the technical details follow a logical flow from problem formulation to analysis. The auxiliary process construction is particularly well-explained, making the complex analysis accessible. The notation is consistent throughout, and the paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to follow the arguments. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of the practical implications of requiring global graph information for parameter tuning.

Originality: This is the first paper to establish convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting applied to the consensus problem. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods is novel. The paper clearly differentiates itself from [27] by providing a convergence rate analysis and demonstrating the square-root improvement over diffusive methods. The auxiliary process construction represents a novel technical contribution to the field.

Significance: The square-root improvement in convergence rate for graphs with geometry (cycles, tori) is significant for distributed systems where these topologies are common (e.g., sensor networks). The unifying perspective connecting Min-Sum Splitting to existing accelerated methods provides valuable theoretical insight. The paper's results motivate further study of Min-Sum Splitting for other optimization problems beyond consensus. While the dependence on global graph information for parameter tuning is a limitation, this is acknowledged by the authors and is a common challenge in accelerated distributed methods.

Strengths:
- First convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting applied to consensus
- Establishes a square-root improvement over diffusive methods for common graph topologies
- Novel connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two established accelerated methods
- Introduces an auxiliary process that enables the convergence analysis
- Well-structured and clearly written

Weaknesses:
- Focuses only on quadratic consensus problem (a special case)
- Requires global graph information for parameter tuning (may not be practical)
- Lacks empirical validation of theoretical claims
- The connection to existing methods is structural rather than performance-based

Overall, this paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to distributed optimization. It establishes the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on the consensus problem and demonstrates a meaningful square-root improvement in convergence rate for common graph topologies. The connection to existing accelerated methods is novel and insightful. While the reliance on global graph information for parameter tuning is a limitation, this is acknowledged by the authors and is a common challenge in accelerated distributed methods. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
