

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper proposes a "Min-Sum Splitting" algorithm for solving the consensus problem in distributed networks, claiming to establish convergence rates that achieve a square-root improvement over traditional diffusive methods. The authors argue that their method, which modifies the standard Min-Sum algorithm by introducing splitting parameters, achieves convergence rates of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, compared to the Θ(D² log(1/ε)) rates of standard diffusive methods.

The paper makes several theoretical claims: (1) Min-Sum Splitting converges for any graph topology; (2) proper tuning of splitting parameters yields accelerated convergence; (3) the method connects to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods. The authors present a theoretical analysis establishing these claims, with proofs in supplementary material.

**Strengths:**
- The paper identifies an important gap in the literature regarding convergence rates for Min-Sum schemes applied to consensus problems.
- The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with careful construction of an auxiliary process to track convergence.
- The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and existing accelerated methods (lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods) is potentially insightful.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of empirical validation:** The most significant weakness is the absence of any experimental results. The paper claims to establish accelerated convergence rates, but without empirical validation on real or synthetic graphs, it's impossible to assess whether the theoretical improvements translate to practical benefits. For a paper claiming to "accelerate consensus," empirical results showing concrete speedups would be essential.

2. **Overstatement of novelty:** The paper claims "first proof of how the directionality embedded in Belief Propagation protocols can be tuned and exploited to accelerate the convergence rate," but the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm was previously introduced in [34] and its convergence for quadratic objectives was shown in [35]. The paper doesn't sufficiently clarify how its theoretical analysis differs from these prior works.

3. **Unrealistic assumptions for practical implementation:** Theorem 1 requires knowledge of ρ_W (the second largest eigenvalue in magnitude of a matrix W) to choose the splitting parameter γ. This is a global property of the graph, which would be difficult to obtain in a distributed setting without additional communication overhead. The paper doesn't address this practical limitation.

4. **Limited scope:** The analysis is restricted to the quadratic consensus problem (network averaging), which is a specific case. The paper doesn't discuss how the results would extend to more general objective functions or constrained problems.

5. **Questionable significance claim:** The paper claims to "get close to the optimal rate O(D log(1/ε)) recently established for some algorithms based on Nesterov's acceleration." However, the paper doesn't provide a comparison of convergence times with these established accelerated methods, making it difficult to assess whether the claimed rate is actually competitive.

6. **Lack of comparison with prior Min-Sum approaches:** While the paper cites [27] which investigated Min-Sum for consensus, it doesn't provide a clear comparison of the convergence rates achieved by their method versus the method in [27], particularly for the cases where [27] made conjectures.

**Overall assessment:**
While the theoretical analysis is technically sound and the paper identifies a legitimate gap in the literature, the lack of empirical validation is a serious flaw for a paper claiming to demonstrate accelerated convergence. The field of distributed optimization has been heavily focused on practical implementation and empirical validation, and a theoretical paper without any experimental results is difficult to justify for NIPS.

The paper's contributions are incremental rather than groundbreaking. The connection to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods is interesting but not novel enough to warrant acceptance without empirical support. The theoretical results are limited to a specific quadratic case of the consensus problem, and the practical implementation challenges are not addressed.

I recommend rejection for the following reasons:
1. The paper lacks empirical validation, which is essential for a claim about convergence rates in distributed systems.
2. The novelty is overstated, with key contributions building on prior work without sufficient differentiation.
3. The practical implementation challenges are not addressed, making it difficult to assess the real-world value of the proposed method.

While the theoretical analysis is sound, NIPS prioritizes work that either makes significant theoretical advances with clear practical implications or provides strong empirical evidence for new methods. This paper falls short on both fronts. A more appropriate venue for this work would be a more theoretically focused journal or conference where empirical validation is not required.
