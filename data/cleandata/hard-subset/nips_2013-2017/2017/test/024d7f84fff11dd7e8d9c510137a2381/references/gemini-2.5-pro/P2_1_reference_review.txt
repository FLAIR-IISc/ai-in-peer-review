### **Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"**

**Summary**

This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, a generalization of the standard Min-Sum message-passing protocol, for solving the distributed consensus problem (specifically, network averaging). The authors demonstrate that while the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge on graphs with cycles, Min-Sum Splitting can be configured not only to converge but to achieve an accelerated rate. The core contribution is a novel theoretical analysis that establishes a deep connection between Min-Sum Splitting and accelerated first-order methods from convex optimization.

The key technical steps are:
1.  Specializing the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm to the quadratic consensus problem, showing it reduces to a linear message-passing system on the graph's directed edges.
2.  Introducing an equivalent "auxiliary" linear process that tracks the algorithm's evolution on the graph's nodes. This auxiliary process takes the form of a two-step linear recurrence, which is characteristic of momentum-based optimization methods.
3.  Proving (Theorem 1) that with a specific choice of the splitting parameters (δ and Γ), the auxiliary process becomes identical to the well-known shift-register method for accelerated consensus.
4.  By leveraging the existing analysis of shift-register methods, the authors establish an accelerated, "subdiffusive" convergence rate for Min-Sum Splitting. For graphs like cycles and tori, this yields a convergence time of O(D log(D/ε)), a square-root improvement over the O(D² log(1/ε)) time of standard "diffusive" methods.

The paper frames this result as bridging the gap between belief propagation methods, lifted Markov chain techniques, and accelerated gradient methods, showing that the "splitting" mechanism can be interpreted as introducing a form of momentum or directionality that speeds up convergence.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the linear updates for the quadratic case (Proposition 1) and the construction of the equivalent auxiliary process on the nodes (Proposition 2) are clear and correct. This auxiliary process is a clever analytical tool that successfully simplifies the analysis by moving from a system on 2m edges to a 2n-dimensional system on the nodes. The main result, Theorem 1, rests on connecting this system to a known result in the literature on accelerated consensus, which is a solid and effective proof strategy. The claims about the accelerated convergence rate are well-supported by this theoretical connection.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides an excellent overview of the consensus problem, diffusive vs. accelerated rates, and relevant prior work, including a clear positioning relative to Moallemi and Van Roy [27]. The logical flow is easy to follow: the paper introduces the general algorithm, specializes it to the problem of interest, develops the necessary analytical tools (the auxiliary process), and then presents the main convergence result. The notation is consistent and the propositions and theorem are stated precisely.

**Originality:** The primary originality of this work lies in the conceptual connection it establishes. While accelerated consensus algorithms are not new, and the final algorithm derived is equivalent to the existing shift-register method, the discovery that Min-Sum Splitting can *embody* this algorithm through a specific parameterization is highly novel. This provides a new perspective on message-passing algorithms, suggesting that mechanisms like splitting are not just heuristics for ensuring convergence but can be tuned to embed sophisticated acceleration principles like momentum. This bridge between the belief propagation community and the accelerated convex optimization community is a significant contribution. It also provides a novel and more powerful analysis of Min-Sum Splitting, a topic for which convergence rates were previously not established.

**Significance:** The results are significant for several reasons:
1.  It demonstrates that message-passing schemes like Min-Sum can be competitive with state-of-the-art specialized algorithms for consensus. This could motivate the use and analysis of such schemes for other distributed optimization problems.
2.  It provides a deeper theoretical understanding of the Min-Sum Splitting reparameterization. The insight that splitting can be used to introduce momentum could inspire new designs for distributed algorithms.
3.  The work improves significantly upon prior attempts to use Min-Sum for consensus [27], providing better rates for a wider class of graphs and with a more general proof technique.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The entire analysis is specific to the quadratic objective function of the network averaging problem. While this is a fundamental problem, it is unclear how or if these acceleration results would extend to consensus problems with more general convex functions φ_v(x_v). The linearity of the updates is crucial to the analysis, and this property is lost in the general case. A discussion on potential challenges or pathways for generalization would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Requirement of Global Information:** The accelerated rate is achieved by setting the splitting parameter Γ as a function of γ, which in turn depends on ρ_W, the second largest eigenvalue modulus of a consensus matrix W. This is a global property of the graph. This requirement is common to many accelerated first-order methods, but it does limit the "fully distributed" nature of the algorithm's setup phase. The authors acknowledge this connection but could be more explicit about this practical limitation.

3.  **No Experimental Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While the theoretical argument is strong, the inclusion of even simple numerical experiments would have been beneficial. For instance, a simulation on a cycle or grid graph comparing the convergence of a standard diffusive method, the proposed Min-Sum Splitting scheme, and perhaps the method from [27] would have provided a powerful visual confirmation of the theoretical speedup.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novel Conceptual Contribution:** Establishes a previously unknown and insightful connection between Min-Sum Splitting and accelerated first-order optimization methods.
*   **Strong Technical Results:** Provides the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and shows it can achieve near-optimal rates for the consensus problem.
*   **High Clarity and Quality:** The paper is very well-written, easy to follow, and the claims are rigorously supported.
*   **Potential for Impact:** The ideas could inspire new research at the intersection of belief propagation and distributed optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The derived algorithm is ultimately equivalent to an existing one (shift-registers), so the paper does not propose a fundamentally new, faster algorithm, but rather a new perspective on an existing one.
*   The analysis is confined to the specific case of network averaging (quadratic loss).
*   The practical implementation requires global graph information to set the parameters for acceleration.

**Overall Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The weaknesses are relatively minor compared to the strength of its main contribution. The novel conceptual bridge built between Min-Sum message-passing and classical acceleration techniques is a significant and elegant result. It provides a new and powerful way to think about the design and analysis of distributed algorithms. This work is likely to be influential and to spur further research in this direction.
