Summary
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting (a reparameterized version of Min-Sum/Belief Propagation) for the consensus/averaging problem. It specializes the algorithm to quadratic consensus, shows the induced message passing on directed edges is linear, and, crucially, constructs an auxiliary node-based 2-step linear system that exactly tracks the edge-based evolution. With a specific parameter choice (δ = 1 and Γ = γW, where W is a symmetric averaging matrix with W1 = 1 and γ depends on the spectral gap ρW), the resulting dynamics coincide with shift-register/second-order methods. The main theorem proves convergence to the average with an accelerated rate ρK < ρW, yielding an O(D log(D/ε)) convergence time on cycles and tori (a square-root improvement over diffusive O(D2 log(1/ε))), while being close to the O(D log(1/ε)) best-known results from Nesterov-type acceleration. The paper also makes conceptual connections between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted non-reversible Markov chains, and multi-step first-order methods.

Relation to prior work
- Ordinary Min-Sum can diverge on graphs with cycles; prior work [27] proposed a related variant and analyzed a 2m-dimensional edge system for regular graphs, proving O(D/ε log(1/ε)) on cycles and conjecturing rates on tori. This paper provides general-graph guarantees and a different proof route via a node-based auxiliary process.
- Accelerated consensus via multi-step, Chebyshev, Nesterov, and lifted Markov chains is well studied. This work’s novelty lies not in proposing a new accelerated iteration per se, but in showing that Min-Sum Splitting, appropriately tuned, recovers the same accelerated behavior, providing the first convergence-rate theory for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus and formal links to both acceleration paradigms.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivations reducing Min-Sum Splitting to linear quadratic updates (Algorithm 2) and then to a node-based auxiliary 2-step system (Algorithm 3) are well motivated and consistent with the structure of consensus constraints. The main theorem’s rate bound builds on known spectral analyses of shift-register/Chebyshev-like iterations; the mapping appears correct and yields clear asymptotic rates.
- Assumptions: The key result requires δ = 1, Γ = γW with W symmetric and ρ(W − 11T/n) < 1, and uses zero initialization of edge messages. The parameter γ depends on the global quantity ρW. The analysis is for quadratic consensus (averaging). These are reasonable for a first rigorous rate analysis but should be stated as limitations.
- Completeness: The theoretical contribution is complete for the considered setting. There are no experiments; while not strictly necessary for a theory paper, small-scale empirical validation (cycles, grids, random geometric graphs) would strengthen the paper and illustrate constants/transients.
- Rigor: Proofs are deferred; the statements and intermediate constructions are precise. Some conditions (e.g., positivity in Proposition 1) could be emphasized and discussed for general initializations.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized (background → specialization → auxiliary system → main theorem). Notation is dense; a small example (e.g., a path or cycle) illustrating Algorithms 2 and 3 would help intuition.
- Minor presentation issues: a few typos/footnotes appear embedded awkwardly; references are inconsistently formatted. Clarify the role of δ (since the main theorem sets δ = 1, yet “splitting” still matters via Γ ≠ A).

Originality
- Novel theoretical contribution: first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, and a new proof technique via a node-based auxiliary process linking edge-based message passing to accelerated multi-step iterations.
- Conceptual novelty: clear connections to lifted non-reversible chains and to momentum/shift-register methods, with an explicit parameter mapping.

Significance
- Consensus/averaging is a core primitive in distributed optimization and ML systems. Showing that Min-Sum Splitting achieves subdiffusive rates and nearly matches state-of-the-art accelerations is valuable, especially for communities using BP-style methods.
- Practical impact depends on (i) ability to tune γ without global spectral knowledge, (ii) robustness to asynchrony, noise, and time-varying graphs, and (iii) extensions beyond quadratic consensus. Even with these caveats, the conceptual bridge and rate guarantees are likely to be cited and built upon.

Pros
- Clear, rigorous rate result for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus with square-root improvement over diffusive methods.
- New auxiliary-process analysis that simplifies spectral arguments by working on nodes rather than edges.
- Bridges BP/Min-Sum with lifted chains and multi-step optimization, unifying perspectives.

Cons
- Limited to quadratic consensus and synchronous updates; no results for general convex objectives or constraints.
- Requires global spectral information to set γ; no discussion of distributed/online tuning.
- No empirical validation; constants and transient behavior are not illustrated.
- Main acceleration essentially recovers known shift-register/Chebyshev behavior; novelty is in the mapping rather than a new algorithmic rate.

Questions for authors
- Can γ be estimated or adapted in a decentralized way (e.g., via local spectral estimation or line search) while retaining acceleration?
- How sensitive is performance to misspecification of γ?
- Do the results extend to strongly convex smooth objectives beyond averaging, or to ADMM-like Min-Sum variants?
- What about asynchronous or randomized message schedules?
- Can you provide finite-time, non-asymptotic bounds with explicit constants for standard graphs?

Suggestions
- Add a brief empirical section comparing Min-Sum Splitting to Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, and ADMM on cycles/grids/RGGs.
- Clarify initialization requirements and discuss robustness.
- Tighten presentation and references; include a small illustrative example.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper is theoretically solid, provides a clear and novel link between Min-Sum Splitting and accelerated consensus, and establishes the first rates for this class. While scope and practical aspects are limited, the contribution is of interest to the NeurIPS community working on distributed optimization, message passing, and accelerated methods.
