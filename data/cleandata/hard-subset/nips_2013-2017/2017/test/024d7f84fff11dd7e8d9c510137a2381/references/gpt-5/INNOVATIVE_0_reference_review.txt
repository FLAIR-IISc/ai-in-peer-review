Summary
This paper studies the Min-Sum Splitting (a reparameterized variant of Min-Sum / Gaussian BP) for the equality-constrained consensus problem, with a focus on the network averaging setting. The authors:

- Show that, for quadratic objectives, Min-Sum Splitting induces a linear message-passing process on directed edges (Algorithm 2) and, via a new reparameterization, an equivalent auxiliary linear multi-step process on nodes (Algorithm 3).
- Prove that an appropriate choice of splitting parameters recovers the same iteration matrix as classical shift-register/chebyshev-type accelerated consensus methods, yielding a square-root improvement over diffusive rates: O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori, compared to Θ(D^2 log(1/ε)).
- Clarify conceptual links between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted non-reversible Markov chains, and multi-step accelerated first-order methods.
- Provide spectral convergence guarantees (Theorem 1) under symmetric W with W1=1, for δ=1 and Γ=γW, where γ depends on ρ(W−11^T/n).

Contributions relative to prior work: Ordinary Min-Sum is known to diverge on loopy graphs; previous Min-Sum variants for consensus [27] analyzed edge-supported linear dynamics (with soft constraints) and characterized rates in specific cases (cycle) and conjectured others (tori). This paper gives the first general-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, introduces a node-based auxiliary process that simplifies spectral analysis, and unifies Min-Sum with accelerated consensus (shift-register/Chebyshev) and lifting perspectives.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual boldness: The reparameterization that reduces an edge-based Min-Sum to a node-based multi-step linear system is elegant and, to my knowledge, new for analyzing Min-Sum Splitting. The explicit equivalence to shift-register acceleration via tuning Γ=γW is a clean and impactful bridge between two communities (BP/message passing and accelerated consensus).
- Theory with clear asymptotic improvement: Theorem 1 establishes a square-root improvement over diffusive methods on geometric graphs and recovers optimal scalings (up to logs) on cycles and tori. The spectral characterization of the rate ρK and its relation to ρW is crisp and aligns with known accelerated consensus results.
- Scope: The analysis applies to arbitrary graphs (under the symmetric W assumption) and clarifies how acceleration depends on global spectral information, akin to lifting and multi-step methods.
- Positioning: The paper situates Min-Sum Splitting among lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, which is both original and informative.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practicality and parameter selection: The accelerated choice γ=2/(1+√(1−ρW^2)) requires knowledge of ρW, a global quantity. The paper acknowledges this but does not offer distributed estimation or robust tuning strategies (e.g., bounds-based γ, adaptive schemes, or power-method approximations). Even brief guidance would improve applicability.
- Focus on quadratic consensus only: While consensus is an important primitive, the promise of Min-Sum Splitting is broader. The paper does not extend rates beyond quadratic least-squares or provide a roadmap for general convex constraints/objectives, beyond motivation. A discussion of which proof components rely on quadraticity and what breaks for general convex problems would strengthen impact.
- Algorithmic overhead and comparison to alternatives: Since the accelerated rate matches that of shift-register/Chebyshev methods, it would help to position Min-Sum Splitting in terms of per-iteration cost, memory, and communication versus implementing shift-register directly. The edge-based Min-Sum iteration (2m states) versus a node-based shift-register (2n states) merits a quantitative comparison.
- Role of δ: While the setup emphasizes Splitting parameters (δ, Γ), the main result fixes δ=1 and tunes Γ. Some discussion of whether δ affords any further acceleration or robustness (or a negative result) would be useful.
- Completeness of presentation: Proofs are in the supplement; that is acceptable, but a small number of key lemmas (e.g., the equivalence underpinning Proposition 2, the spectral decomposition leading to ρK) summarized in the main text would aid readability for NeurIPS’ broad audience.
- Minor clarity issue: Proposition 1 assumes initial quadratic messages with R̂0vw>0, while Theorem 1 sets R̂0=r̂0=0. Although positivity emerges after one step for δ=1, please reconcile assumptions explicitly to avoid confusion.

Quality
Technically sound as far as can be assessed from the main text. The reduction to a node-based 2n-system and the spectral bound in Theorem 1 are consistent with known accelerated consensus analyses. The novelty lies in proving these properties for Min-Sum Splitting and showing exact correspondence to shift-register dynamics.

Clarity
The paper is dense but generally well-organized. Algorithms and matrices are precisely specified. However, the exposition could be improved by:
- Streamlining notation around Γ, diag(Γ1), and the various K, K̂ matrices, and adding a schematic of the relationships among Algorithms 1–3.
- Providing a short worked example on a path/cycle to illustrate initialization and updates.
- Explicitly stating distributed implementability of Algorithm 3 and whether one can implement the accelerated update on nodes without maintaining edge messages.

Originality
High. Establishing accelerated rates for Min-Sum Splitting in consensus, introducing the auxiliary node-based analysis, and drawing precise connections to lifting and multi-step methods are new and likely to stimulate follow-on work.

Significance
Moderate to high. Beyond consensus, the analysis framework may transfer to other quadratic (and perhaps strongly convex) problems in distributed inference and learning. The conceptual bridge between BP and accelerated consensus is valuable for both communities.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a short section on choosing γ without exact ρW: e.g., bounds using degrees/diameter, Gershgorin-based intervals, or adaptive tuning via a few rounds of a distributed power/Chebyshev method.
- Provide a small empirical illustration (cycles and grids) to corroborate constants and compare to Metropolis-Hastings, Chebyshev/shift-register, and the method of [27].
- Discuss extensions beyond quadratics: what parts of the analysis might survive for strongly convex separable φv with equality constraints?
- Clarify computational/communication tradeoffs versus direct accelerated consensus.

Recommendation
Accept. This paper introduces a compelling and novel viewpoint on Min-Sum Splitting, provides the first convergence-rate analysis with accelerated guarantees for consensus, and forges meaningful conceptual links to lifting and multi-step optimization. While largely theoretical and focused on quadratics, the ideas are bold and have the potential to influence future work on message-passing and distributed optimization. Confidence: high.
