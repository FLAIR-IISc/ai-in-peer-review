## Peer Review for "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors demonstrate that, unlike the standard Min-Sum algorithm, Min-Sum Splitting *does* converge for general graph topologies and achieves accelerated convergence rates—specifically, a square-root improvement over diffusive methods. This improvement manifests as a convergence time of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles and tori, bringing the algorithm closer to the optimal O(D log(1/ε)) rate. A key contribution is the introduction of an auxiliary linear process on the nodes of the graph to track the algorithm's evolution, simplifying the analysis. The authors also establish connections between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chain techniques and accelerated first-order methods, offering a unifying perspective.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon prior work on Min-Sum algorithms for consensus [27], which showed non-convergence of the standard Min-Sum in cyclic graphs. It significantly advances this work by proving convergence and characterizing the rate for Min-Sum Splitting.  The paper also clearly situates its work within the broader landscape of accelerated consensus algorithms, referencing both lifted Markov chains [8, 5, 37] and accelerated gradient methods [13, 24].  The connection drawn to shift-register methods [45, 4] is particularly insightful. The authors appropriately acknowledge and contrast their approach with existing methods, highlighting the novel contributions of their work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing explicit convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, a result previously lacking in the literature.
*   **Novel Approach:**  The use of Min-Sum Splitting to achieve accelerated consensus is a novel approach. The connection to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods provides a valuable unifying perspective.
*   **Clear Exposition:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear progression of ideas. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the contributions.
*   **Auxiliary Process:** The introduction of the auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) is a clever and effective analytical tool, simplifying the convergence analysis.
*   **Connection to Existing Methods:** Demonstrating the link to shift-register methods is a strong result, providing a concrete comparison point and highlighting the practical implications of the theoretical findings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of Notation:** The paper uses a significant amount of notation, which can be challenging to follow at times. While the notation is generally consistent, a more streamlined presentation could improve readability.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:**  The paper focuses exclusively on theoretical analysis. While the theoretical results are strong, some limited empirical validation (even on small graphs) would strengthen the work and demonstrate practical performance. It would be helpful to see if the theoretical speedup translates to real-world performance gains.
*   **Assumptions on Initial Conditions:** The convergence result relies on specific initial conditions for Algorithm 2.  A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to different initializations would be valuable.
*  **Parameter Tuning:** The choice of parameters γ and W based on the spectral gap can be difficult to compute in practice. Discussing practical ways to estimate these parameters or a more robust way of selecting them would be helpful.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

*   **Quality (9/10):** The paper is technically sound, with a rigorous theoretical analysis and well-supported claims. The proofs seem carefully constructed, and the overall work is complete.
*   **Clarity (7/10):** The paper is generally clear, but the density of notation and technical details occasionally hinders readability.
*   **Originality (9/10):** The approach of applying Min-Sum Splitting to achieve accelerated consensus is novel, and the connections to existing methods provide a fresh perspective.
*   **Significance (8/10):** The results are significant, as they provide a theoretically grounded approach to accelerating consensus algorithms. The potential impact on distributed optimization and machine learning applications is substantial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical results with a clear convergence rate improvement.
*   Novel approach with connections to well-established areas.
*   Potential to impact distributed optimization and learning.
*   Rigorous analysis and well-defined contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of empirical validation.
*   Complexity of notation could hinder accessibility.
*   Parameter tuning is potentially challenging.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** 

Despite the minor weaknesses related to notation and lack of empirical validation, the paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of distributed consensus algorithms. The rigorous analysis, novel approach, and potential for real-world impact make this a valuable addition to the NIPS program.  I recommend acceptance with the encouragement to consider including some limited empirical results and potentially streamlining the notation for improved clarity in future versions.
