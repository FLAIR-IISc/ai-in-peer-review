Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm (a reparameterized form of Min-Sum/Belief Propagation) for the consensus/averaging problem on graphs. The main technical development specializes Min-Sum Splitting to quadratic consensus and shows:

- For consensus with quadratic local costs, Min-Sum Splitting reduces to linear message passing on directed edges (Algorithm 2), with parameters evolving linearly via a matrix K̂(δ, Γ).
- An auxiliary node-based, two-step linear iteration (Algorithm 3) tracks the output of the edge-based Min-Sum iteration exactly, for appropriate initialization. This iteration is governed by a 2n×2n matrix K(δ, Γ).
- With a specific tuning (δ = 1 and Γ = γW, where W is a symmetric averaging matrix with W1 = 1 and ρ(W − 11^T/n) < 1, and γ = 2/(1 + √(1 − ρ^2_W))), the method attains an asymptotic rate controlled by ρK = √[(1 − √(1 − ρ^2_W))/(1 + √(1 − ρ^2_W))], i.e., a square-root improvement over diffusive rates. This yields O(D log(D/ε)) iterations on cycles and tori, close to the O(D log(1/ε)) optimal bounds achieved by some Nesterov-based methods.

The paper positions these results relative to:
- Classical consensus via linear iterations (Boyd et al., Xiao-Boyd), which are diffusive and mix in Θ(D^2 log(1/ε)) on geometric graphs; Metropolis-Hastings achieves this rate.
- Min-Sum for consensus with soft constraints (Moallemi-Van Roy), whose convergence on regular graphs is tied to a 2m-dimensional linear system on directed edges with mixing times O(D) on cycles; conjectures for higher-dimensional tori were open.
- Min-Sum Splitting (Ruozzi-Tatikonda) previously shown to aid convergence in Gaussian BP without established rates.
- Accelerated consensus via lifted non-reversible Markov chains and via multi-step (heavy-ball, Nesterov, shift-register/Chebyshev) methods; the paper shows Min-Sum Splitting induces directionality akin to non-reversibility and a memory term akin to multi-step methods, and with the stated tuning recovers the same K matrix and rates as shift-registers.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The quadratic specialization is consistent: the Min-Sum messages remain quadratic, leading to linear recursions (Proposition 1). The auxiliary node-based reduction (Proposition 2) is plausible and reduces a 2m-dimensional edge dynamics to coupled n-dimensional node dynamics, enabling spectral analysis in terms of Γ (and hence W).
- The main rate theorem (Theorem 1) matches classical shift-register/Chebyshev acceleration results: the spectral contraction factor ρK is the well-known Chebyshev improvement over ρW, and the square-root relationship 1/(1 − ρK) ≈ √(1/(1 − ρW)) is standard. The bound ‖xt − b̄1‖ ≤ const · ‖(K − K∞)^t‖ is explicit.
- Assumptions and scope are clear: synchronous, static, undirected graph; symmetric W; quadratic objective; particular initialization (R̂0 = r̂0 = 0) in the theorem; and conditions ensuring positivity of denominators in the reconstruction. The dependence of γ on global spectral information is explicit.

Potential technical gaps/clarifications:
- The effect of nonzero or arbitrary initial messages is only partly addressed (positivity conditions are stated, but Theorem 1 uses zero initialization). It would help to state whether the rate is initialization-independent (beyond transient constants).
- Γ = γW may have negative entries when optimal W includes negative weights; while quadratics remain convex globally, clarifying convexity conditions and the positivity condition 1 + δ∑ Γ R > 0 would improve rigor.
- Robustness to mis-specified γ (inevitable in decentralized settings) is not analyzed.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized: sections progress from background to algorithms and the main theorem. The two algorithmic forms (edge-based and node-based) and their equivalence are clearly motivated.
- Some presentation issues could be improved: minor indexing typos, equation numbering mismatch (e.g., piecewise K̂), and dense piecewise definitions that could be simplified or accompanied by intuition. A brief complexity/communication discussion would help (edge vs node iterations).

Originality
- To the best of my assessment, this is the first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, and the first formal connection tying Min-Sum Splitting both to lifted non-reversible chains and to multi-step acceleration with an explicit recovery of shift-register/Chebyshev rates via tuning.
- While the attained rates are known in the consensus literature, the novelty lies in deriving them through Min-Sum Splitting, the auxiliary reduction from edges to nodes, and clarifying the mechanism by which splitting induces acceleration (directionality/memory). This is an incremental but nontrivial bridge between message-passing and accelerated consensus.

Significance
- Conceptual significance: unifies perspectives across BP/Min-Sum, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step methods; may inform design/analysis of message-passing for other problems.
- Practical significance may be limited: the accelerated tuning requires global spectral information; no experiments or finite-sample constant comparisons are provided; and the scope is limited to quadratic consensus under synchrony. Nonetheless, the theoretical insight is valuable for the community.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and rigorous theoretical contribution: first-rate bound for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus with an explicit acceleration factor.
- Elegant auxiliary reduction enabling spectral analysis on nodes rather than edges.
- Useful conceptual connections to established acceleration frameworks; clarifies the role of splitting parameters.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical validation and practical considerations are absent (constants, communication/computation cost vs standard accelerated consensus).
- Requires global spectral parameters; no robustness/distributed tuning analysis.
- Scope restricted to quadratic consensus; no extension to general convex consensus with constraints or to asynchrony/time-variation.
- Some clarity/notation issues and minor presentation polish needed.

Questions/suggestions to authors
- Can the auxiliary reduction extend to more general convex separable objectives with equality constraints? If not, what obstacles arise?
- How sensitive is convergence to misspecification of γ? Are there safe tunings that still guarantee subdiffusive improvement with only rough bounds on ρW?
- Provide per-iteration cost and message complexity comparisons to shift-register/Nesterov consensus and, if possible, empirical illustrations on cycles/grids.
- Clarify conditions ensuring convexity and positivity of denominators with Γ = γW when W has negative entries.

Overall assessment
A technically sound and conceptually insightful paper that advances understanding of Min-Sum Splitting and its relation to accelerated consensus. Theoretical contributions are solid; practical relevance would benefit from empirical support and robustness analysis. I lean toward acceptance conditional on improved clarity and a brief empirical or complexity comparison section.
