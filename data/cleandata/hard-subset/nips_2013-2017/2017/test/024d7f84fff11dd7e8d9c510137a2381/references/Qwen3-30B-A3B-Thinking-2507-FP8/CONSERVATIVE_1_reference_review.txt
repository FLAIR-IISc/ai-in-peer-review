

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum Splitting to the consensus problem, claiming to establish accelerated convergence rates compared to traditional diffusive methods. The authors present theoretical analysis showing that with proper parameter tuning, their method achieves convergence time of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, which they claim is a square-root improvement over the O(D² log(1/ε)) of diffusive methods.

The paper makes several key claims: (1) Min-Sum Splitting is the first to analyze convergence rates for Min-Sum applied to consensus; (2) the method connects to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods; (3) proper parameter tuning yields accelerated rates that match shift-register methods. The authors provide a theoretical proof using an auxiliary process to track convergence behavior.

**Strengths:** The paper demonstrates a clear theoretical connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two established lines of research (lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods). The mathematical analysis is technically sound, and the paper correctly situates its contribution within the literature on consensus algorithms. The proof technique using the auxiliary process is novel and well-executed for the specific quadratic case considered.

**Weaknesses:** The most significant limitation is the complete absence of empirical validation. The paper makes strong claims about "accelerated" convergence but provides no experimental results demonstrating either the claimed theoretical improvements or practical benefits in real-world scenarios. The convergence time claims are limited to the specific quadratic network averaging problem, not general consensus problems. More critically, the parameter tuning (γ = 2/(1 + √(1-ρW²))) requires knowledge of ρW, which depends on global spectral properties of the graph. This is highly problematic for distributed systems where nodes typically lack global graph knowledge, making the approach impractical for most real-world applications.

The paper also fails to adequately compare with state-of-the-art accelerated consensus methods. While they reference multi-step methods and lifted Markov chains, they don't provide a clear comparison of computational complexity, communication requirements, or practical performance with existing approaches like those in [13, 24, 45]. The claimed "square-root improvement" is theoretical but doesn't translate to practical speedups without addressing the global information requirement.

**Significance concerns:** The paper's contribution appears to be primarily theoretical with limited practical significance. The accelerated convergence rate is interesting mathematically, but the requirement for global spectral information renders the method impractical for distributed settings where the paper claims to operate. The authors don't demonstrate how their method would perform in real networks with limited communication or how it would compare to existing practical accelerated methods.

**Comparison to related work:** The paper cites [27] which investigated Min-Sum for consensus but only for d-regular graphs. However, the authors don't properly address how their approach differs from or improves upon the practical methods in [27] or the more recent work on accelerated consensus like [13, 24]. The connection to lifted Markov chains is interesting but doesn't provide a practical advantage over existing methods that have been successfully implemented.

**Recommendation:** While the theoretical analysis is sound for the specific case studied, the paper lacks the empirical validation expected at NIPS for a method claiming practical improvements. The requirement for global information to tune parameters fundamentally undermines the distributed nature of the problem. I recommend rejection unless the authors provide empirical results demonstrating practical speedups in realistic distributed settings, address the global information requirement, and provide a thorough comparison with existing accelerated consensus methods.

**Specific suggestions for improvement:**
1. Conduct experiments comparing Min-Sum Splitting with state-of-the-art accelerated consensus methods on standard graph topologies (cycles, grids, random geometric graphs).
2. Investigate whether the global parameter tuning requirement can be replaced with a distributed approximation.
3. Expand the analysis to non-quadratic objective functions to demonstrate broader applicability.
4. Provide a clear comparison of computational complexity and communication requirements with existing methods.

Without these improvements, the paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution but fails to demonstrate the practical significance required for NIPS. The claim of "accelerated" convergence is mathematically valid but impractical for the distributed setting it purports to address.
