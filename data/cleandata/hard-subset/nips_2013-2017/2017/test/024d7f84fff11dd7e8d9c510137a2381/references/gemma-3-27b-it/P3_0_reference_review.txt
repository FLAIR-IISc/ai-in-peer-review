## Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors establish that, with a careful parameter tuning, Min-Sum Splitting achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence rates – specifically, O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle and toroidal graphs – which is an improvement over classical diffusive methods (O(D^2 log(1/ε))). They achieve this by demonstrating a linear message-passing update for any graph and then introducing an auxiliary process on the nodes to track the algorithm's evolution, relating the convergence time to the spectral gap of a related matrix. The paper also draws connections between Min-Sum splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods, highlighting its position within broader theoretical frameworks.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the work of [27], which examined Min-Sum for consensus but didn’t provide general convergence rate analysis. It also connects to a large body of work on accelerated consensus algorithms, including those based on lifted Markov chains [8, 5, 37] and accelerated gradient methods [13, 24].  The authors correctly position their work as bridging the gap between these two seemingly independent lines of research.  The connection to shift-register methods, and the resulting convergence rate, is a particularly interesting link to numerical analysis techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing a quantifiable improvement in convergence rate over existing methods for specific graph topologies. The proof technique, leveraging an auxiliary process, appears novel and insightful.
* **Bridging Different Fields:** The authors successfully connect Min-Sum splitting to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated optimization, providing a broader understanding of the algorithm's properties and origins. This is a significant contribution.
* **Clear Identification of Key Parameters:** The paper clearly identifies the importance of the splitting parameters (δ and Γ) and provides guidance on how to tune them to achieve the accelerated rates.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written, although dense, and the organization is logical. The inclusion of relevant background material is helpful.
* **Comprehensive Literature Review:**  The authors provide a thorough overview of related work in consensus algorithms, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity and Density:** The paper is mathematically dense and may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with message passing algorithms and spectral graph theory. While rigor is good, greater intuition and simplification of key concepts could broaden accessibility.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the analysis is compelling, the lack of even simple simulations to demonstrate the improved convergence in practice weakens the argument. It would be valuable to see empirical confirmation of the theoretical results, even on small-scale networks.
* **Practical Considerations:**  The optimal parameter setting (γW) requires knowledge of the spectral gap of the graph, which may not be readily available in real-world distributed systems.  Discussing the sensitivity of the performance to inaccuracies in estimating this spectral gap would be beneficial.
* **Scope of Accelerated Convergence:** The paper focuses on achieving a *square root* improvement over diffusive methods for specific graph topologies (cycles, tori). While significant, the practical impact of this improvement should be discussed in relation to the computational overhead of the Min-Sum splitting algorithm itself.
* **Assumption of Quadratic Objective:** The analysis is specifically tied to a quadratic objective function. How well do the results generalize to other convex objective functions commonly used in consensus problems?




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound analysis of Min-Sum splitting.
* Significant contribution to understanding the convergence properties of distributed consensus algorithms.
* Bridges the gap between multiple research areas (message passing, spectral graph theory, optimization).
* Strong potential for impacting the design of efficient distributed algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of empirical validation.
* Mathematical density might limit accessibility.
* Practical limitations related to spectral gap estimation.
* Limited generalization beyond quadratic objectives.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper with significant potential to advance our understanding of distributed consensus algorithms. The theoretical results are compelling, and the connection to other fields is insightful. However, the lack of empirical validation and the potential practical challenges related to parameter tuning are concerns.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend acceptance contingent on the following:

1. **Inclusion of Simulations:** Add simulations, even on small-scale networks, to demonstrate the improved convergence rates in practice.
2. **Discussion of Parameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to inaccuracies in estimating the spectral gap.
3. **Consideration of Generalization:** Briefly discuss the potential for extending the results to other convex objective functions.
4. **Clarity Improvements:** While maintaining rigor, consider adding more intuitive explanations of key concepts and simplifying the presentation where possible.
