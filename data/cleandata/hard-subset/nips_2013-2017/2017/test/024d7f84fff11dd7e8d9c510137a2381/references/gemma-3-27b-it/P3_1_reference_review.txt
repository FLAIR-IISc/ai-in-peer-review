## Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant and well-executed contribution to the theory of distributed consensus algorithms. It establishes convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem, demonstrating a square-root improvement over diffusive methods for graphs with geometry (cycles and tori). The work is theoretically sound, clearly written, and builds upon existing literature in a meaningful way. While the connection to existing accelerated methods (lifted Markov chains, Nesterov's acceleration) is highlighted, it feels somewhat cursory and could be deepened. Despite this minor point, I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm – a generalization of the traditional Min-Sum scheme – for solving the consensus problem.  The core idea is to reformulate the consensus problem in a way that allows Min-Sum splitting to guarantee convergence even on graphs where the standard Min-Sum fails. The authors demonstrate that a proper parametrization of the splitting parameters (δ and Γ) leads to accelerated convergence rates, achieving a subdiffusive scaling of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles and tori, approaching the optimal O(D log(1/ε)) rate. They establish a connection to existing accelerated methods by showing a relationship with lifted Markov chains and multi-step gradient methods (specifically, shift-register methods). A key technical contribution is the introduction of an auxiliary message-passing algorithm on the nodes of the graph to track the evolution of the splitting algorithm, allowing for a more tractable analysis.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting.  The square-root improvement over diffusive methods is a substantial result.
* **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:**  The paper is remarkably well-written and logically structured. The introduction motivates the problem effectively, the methodology is clearly explained, and the connections to related work are articulated.
* **Novelty and Originality:** While building on existing Min-Sum and consensus literature, this paper introduces a novel application of splitting techniques and provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of its convergence properties.  The analysis of the splitting parameters and their impact on convergence is original.
* **Connection to Established Methods:** The authors intelligently connect Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chain techniques and accelerated first-order methods, providing a broader context for their work.
* **Technical Depth:** The introduction of the auxiliary message-passing algorithm and the subsequent analysis demonstrate a deep understanding of the underlying mathematical principles.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Superficial Connection to Accelerated Methods:** While the authors *mention* the connection to lifted Markov chains and Nesterov-like acceleration, the discussion feels somewhat shallow. A more detailed analysis of the relationship, for example, explicitly mapping the splitting parameters to parameters in those existing methods, would strengthen the paper.  The benefits and trade-offs between Min-Sum Splitting and these other approaches are not fully explored.
* **Complexity of Notation:** While the paper is generally clear, the introduction of several different algorithms (1, 2, 3) and associated notation can be initially confusing. A more streamlined presentation of these algorithms could enhance readability.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses almost entirely on theoretical analysis. While the theoretical results are strong, some limited empirical validation (even on small-scale graphs) would bolster confidence in the practical relevance of the proposed algorithm.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The theoretical results are significant and provide a substantial advance in the understanding of Min-Sum Splitting.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to a broad audience.
* The work addresses a fundamental problem in distributed systems and provides a promising approach to achieving faster convergence.
* The connection to existing accelerated methods, even if not fully developed, provides valuable context and motivates further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation leaves some question about the practical applicability of the algorithm.
* The connection to existing accelerated methods could be strengthened with a more detailed analysis.



**Specific Comments/Questions:**

* **Page 6, Paragraph 2:** "Algorithm 1 can be seen to correspond to the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm..." - This statement could be clarified.  What specific choice of parameters leads to the ordinary Min-Sum?
* **Page 8:** "Theorem 1 shows that the choice of splitting parameters... depends on global properties of the graph G." - While true, a more concrete example illustrating this dependence would be helpful.
* **Future Work:** It would be interesting to explore how the results generalize to more complex objective functions or network topologies.



**Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical results are compelling, the presentation is clear, and the originality is high. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above, particularly deepening the connection to existing accelerated methods and considering some limited empirical validation.**
