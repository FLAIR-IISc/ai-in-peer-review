- Summary: Analyzes Min-Sum Splitting for quadratic consensus, introduces an edge-based linearization (Alg.2) and an equivalent node-based two-step linear system (Alg.3), and proves an accelerated convergence rate matching Chebyshev/shift-register methods.

- Main theoretical claim: With δ=1 and Γ=γW (symmetric W), tuning γ=2/(1+√(1−ρW^2)) yields asymptotic rate ρK that improves over diffusive rate ρW, giving a square-root improvement in convergence time.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on Min-Sum/GBP and consensus literature, improves on [27] by providing a general spectral-rate analysis for arbitrary graphs via the auxiliary process.

- Technical soundness: Linearization and quadratic message invariance are standard and formalized (Prop.1); Prop.2 enables a clean spectral analysis via a 2n×2n recursion; Theorem 1 matches known accelerated rates and is consistent.

- Assumptions/limitations: Analysis restricted to quadratic consensus, synchronous static undirected graphs, symmetric W; γ requires global spectral info (ρW); specialized initialization (R̂0=r̂0=0); positivity condition in Prop.1 not fully discussed.

- Rigor/completeness: Proofs mainly deferred to supplement; main statements and mappings are correct; error-bound constants explicit but loose; asymptotic rate is the main value.

- Clarity: Well organized overall but notation is dense; minor typos (e.g., missing division in Alg.2 output) and some formula transcription errors should be fixed.

- Intuition: The paper would benefit from a brief illustrative example (e.g., path/cycle) to explain the “directionality” induced by splitting.

- Originality: Novelty is in showing Min-Sum Splitting implements accelerated dynamics and in providing the first convergence-rate analysis for this splitting; auxiliary-process technique is elegant and new in this context.

- Conceptual contribution: Useful unification with lifted non-reversible Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods.

- Significance for BP/Min-Sum community: Valuable result showing Splitting can be tuned for accelerated consensus and may inspire extensions to other objectives.

- Practical significance: Modest, since resulting rate/matrix coincide with known accelerated schemes; requirement for global spectral knowledge limits decentralization unless additional estimation is used.

- Strengths: Clear mapping from Min-Sum Splitting to a tractable node process, clean spectral-rate bound, conceptual bridges to other acceleration paradigms, analysis for general graph topologies, improves prior Min-Sum consensus results.

- Weakness — scope: Limited to quadratic consensus and synchronous symmetric settings; would be stronger if extended to broader quadratic or composite objectives.

- Weakness — practicality: Needs discussion on choosing W (e.g., Metropolis), distributed estimation of ρW, robustness to γ mis-specification, and communication/memory trade-offs (2m edge states vs 2n node states).

- Weakness — experiments: No empirical validation; small-scale simulations comparing Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, Min-Sum Splitting, and ADMM would substantiate theory and constants.

- Weakness — presentation: Fix Alg.2 output, equation numbering and K̂ transcription errors, and add a proof sketch for Theorem 1 in the main text.

- Weakness — relation to prior acceleration: Explicit comparison to Chebyshev polynomial acceleration and recent optimal rates (e.g., Scaman et al.) is needed to clarify practical advantages.

- Pros for acceptance: Solid theoretical contribution, elegant auxiliary analysis, potential to bridge communities and generalize.

- Cons for acceptance: Limited scope, dependence on global spectral information, lack of experiments, and some presentation issues.

- Recommendation: Borderline accept — technically sound and interesting but would benefit from experiments and clearer discussion of practical deployment.
