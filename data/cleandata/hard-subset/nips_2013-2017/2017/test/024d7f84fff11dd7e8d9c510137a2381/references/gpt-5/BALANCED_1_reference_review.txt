Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting (a reparameterized Min-Sum/Belief Propagation scheme) for the equality-constrained consensus problem, focusing on the quadratic “network averaging” case. The authors (i) show that, with appropriate parameters, Min-Sum Splitting reduces to a linear message-passing process on directed edges and, crucially, can be tracked by an auxiliary linear process with memory on nodes; (ii) prove that a specific choice of splitting parameters achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence rates; and (iii) connect the mechanism of acceleration to both lifted, non-reversible Markov chains and multi-step (shift-register) methods for consensus/first-order optimization.

The main theorem establishes that choosing δ=1 and Γ=γW (with W symmetric, W1=1, and γ tuned via ρW, the spectral radius of W−11^T/n) yields asymptotic contraction rate ρK=√((1−√(1−ρW^2))/(1+√(1−ρW^2)))<ρW, matching the rate of shift-register/Chebyshev-like methods. Consequently, for geometric graphs (cycles/tori), the iteration complexity improves from Θ(D^2 log(1/ε)) to O(D log(D/ε)), close to the O(D log(1/ε)) lower bound/optimal rates known for certain accelerated schemes. Prior work includes: (a) Min-Sum for consensus with soft barriers and edge-based linearizations in [Moallemi & Van Roy, “Consensus Propagation,” and follow-ups], which did not provide general rates and where ordinary Min-Sum can fail on loopy graphs; (b) lifted Markov chains for accelerated gossip [Chen–Lovász–Pak; Diaconis–Holmes–Neal; Jung–Shah–Shin; Li–Dai], with graph-specific constructions and overhead; and (c) accelerated multi-step consensus (Chebyshev, heavy-ball, Nesterov/shift-register) [Xiao–Boyd; Ghosh–Muthukrishnan–Schultz; Golub–Varga; Ghadimi–Shames–Johansson; Olshevsky; Scaman et al.]. The novelty here is a rigorous convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, and an explicit bridge between BP-style reparameterizations, lifted mixing, and multi-step acceleration.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The theorem reduces Min-Sum Splitting to a 2n-dimensional linear system whose spectrum is analyzable, and recovers known accelerated rates (shift-register). Claims are carefully tied to spectral quantities; proofs are deferred to the supplement. The need for global tuning (γ depending on ρW) is stated.
- Scope: The results are for quadratic (averaging) consensus, with synchronous updates and specific initialization (zero messages) and δ=1. Convergence hinges on spectral assumptions for W and proper positiveness conditions.
- Empirics: The paper is purely theoretical; no simulations are provided to illustrate constants, robustness, or comparisons.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and the high-level narrative is clear: edge-based Min-Sum Splitting → auxiliary node-based process → spectral analysis → accelerated rates. Some presentation issues remain: footnote markers and minor typographical glitches; dense notation for K̂, K; the role of initialization and the positivity condition could be clarified. A brief intuitive explanation of why splitting induces directionality/memory would help readers unfamiliar with BP reparameterizations.

Originality
- First convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting.
- New conceptual connections between BP splitting, non-reversible lifting, and multi-step acceleration.
- While the asymptotic rate matches classic shift-register/Chebyshev methods, establishing that a BP-style algorithm realizes the same dynamics and rates is novel and potentially influential.

Significance
- The results show Min-Sum Splitting can be tuned to attain near-optimal consensus rates on geometric graphs and improve over diffusive methods on any graph (ρK<ρW).
- The conceptual bridge may stimulate cross-fertilization: using message-passing reparameterizations to realize accelerated schemes without explicit lifting or global polynomial filters.
- Practical impact is somewhat limited by global parameter tuning (γ needs ρW), the restriction to quadratics, and lack of asynchronous/noisy/ time-varying analysis.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution with rigorous rate guarantees.
- Elegant reduction to a node-based 2n-dimensional linear system and clean spectral characterization.
- Unifies viewpoints (BP/lifting/multi-step), which is intellectually appealing and may inspire extensions.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited scope: quadratic objective (averaging) only; synchronous, static graphs; specific initialization.
- Requires global spectral information to set γ; no adaptive/local tuning strategy or robustness bounds for mis-specified γ.
- No experiments to illustrate constants, finite-time behavior, or comparisons against Chebyshev/Nesterov/ADMM-based consensus.
- Comparison to existing accelerated consensus in both theory and practice could be sharper (e.g., how do constants and per-iteration costs compare to Chebyshev filtering or Nesterov schemes?).
- Practical constraints: memory and per-iteration communication overhead vs. classical methods are not discussed.

Questions for authors
1. How sensitive is convergence to mis-specification of γ? Can you provide bounds when γ is chosen using an upper bound on ρW?
2. Can the auxiliary node-based process suggest an adaptive scheme that estimates spectral gap online (e.g., via local variance reduction) to set γ?
3. Does the analysis extend to time-varying/asynchronous settings, or to mildly non-quadratic strongly convex φv?
4. What are the per-iteration computational and communication costs relative to Chebyshev/Nesterov methods and to consensus propagation in [27]?
5. Can you comment on the necessity of zero initialization and the positivity condition on R̂t for broader initializations?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a brief experimental section on cycles/grids/random geometric graphs comparing convergence curves with Metropolis, Chebyshev, shift-register, and lifted-walk baselines.
- Discuss implementation details: message sizes, storage, and complexity per iteration; provide guidance for choosing W.
- Provide robustness analysis to γ mis-tuning and possible adaptive tuning heuristics.
- Clarify presentation (fix footnotes/typos; restate key assumptions near Theorem 1; explain positivity condition).
- Outline prospects and obstacles for extending beyond quadratics (e.g., strongly convex smooth objectives; ADMM connections).

Arguments for acceptance
- First rigorous rate for Min-Sum Splitting; clean, general spectral analysis.
- Conceptually unifies disparate acceleration paradigms.
- Demonstrates that BP-style algorithms can realize state-of-the-art consensus rates without explicit lifting.

Arguments against acceptance
- Narrow problem class (quadratic consensus), synchronous/static setting.
- Dependence on global spectral information without adaptive remedies.
- No empirical validation; practical benefits vs. established accelerated methods not demonstrated.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contribution and conceptual connections are novel and solid; adding empirical support and clarifying practical aspects would further strengthen the paper.
