Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the Min-Sum (a.k.a. belief propagation for optimization) algorithm with “splitting” reparameterization [34,35] and provides the first convergence-rate analysis for the consensus problem, focusing on network averaging (quadratic objective). The authors show:

- The Min-Sum Splitting protocol becomes a linear message-passing algorithm on directed edges (Algorithm 2) with quadratic messages preserved over time (Proposition 1).
- They construct an auxiliary node-based, two-step linear iteration with memory (Algorithm 3) that exactly tracks the edge-based dynamics (Proposition 2). This reduces analysis from a 2m-dimensional edge process to two coupled n-dimensional node processes, with behavior governed by the spectrum of Γ.
- With a particular parameter choice δ=1 and Γ=γW (W symmetric, W1=1, ρ(W−11T/n)<1) and γ tuned as γ=2/(1+√(1−ρW^2)), they prove convergence to the average with an accelerated asymptotic rate (Theorem 1). The spectral radius ρK of the induced 2n×2n update matrix K satisfies 1/(1−ρK) ≈ √(1/(1−ρW)), yielding a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods. On cycles and tori this implies O(D log(D/ε)) iterations vs. Θ(D^2 log(1/ε)).
- They interpret the mechanism as introducing directionality in edge updates (akin to nonreversible lifted Markov chains [5,8,18,37]) and, via the auxiliary process, as a multi-step memory term analogous to shift-register/Polyak/Nesterov-style accelerations [13,24,45].

This connects strands of prior work: failures and fixes of ordinary Min-Sum on loopy graphs [27,28,25,35]; accelerated consensus via lifted Markov chains [5,8,18,22,23,37]; multi-step accelerated methods and shift-register schemes for consensus [4,14,24,45]; and related distributed optimization approaches (ADMM, gradient/subgradient) [3,12,16,29,32,36,38–40].

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The derivation of linearity and quadratic invariance (Prop. 1) is standard for Gaussian BP; the edge-to-node auxiliary reduction (Prop. 2) is the main analytical device and appears well-posed. Theorem 1 relies on spectral analysis of the 2n×2n companion matrix K and recovers known accelerated rates of shift-register methods. Assumptions are explicit (W symmetric, doubly stochastic with spectral gap). The constants in the nonasymptotic bound are given, and the asymptotic factor ρK is tight due to existing theory [13,15,24].
- Scope and limitations: The analysis is restricted to quadratic objectives (network averaging), synchronous updates, and exact knowledge of a global spectral quantity (ρW) to tune γ. The convergence condition in Prop. 1 (positivity of 1+δ∑ΓR̂) should be discussed more (why ensured by initialization R̂0=0, and whether it holds for all t). No robustness analysis (mis-specified γ, noise, asynchrony) or experiments are provided.
- Completeness: Proofs are deferred to the supplement; the main text states clear results, but empirical validation or illustrative simulations across topologies would help assess constants and practical performance.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-organized: introduction and related work, algorithmic development (Algorithms 1–3), and the main theorem. The auxiliary-process viewpoint clarifies why rates depend on Γ’s spectrum. The exposition could benefit from:
  - Cleaning typographical issues (e.g., misplaced footnotes, “st Conference...”, reference formatting, and the mention of a Figure 1 not included).
  - Explicitly stating all standing assumptions early (e.g., on Γ sparsity, symmetry, δ choices).
  - Briefly summarizing the proof idea for Theorem 1 in the main text (link to Chebyshev/second-order methods).
  - Clarifying the condition ensuring the minima in Algorithm 1/2 are well-defined at all iterations.

Originality:
- The work appears to provide the first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and the first explicit equivalence between Min-Sum Splitting and accelerated multi-step/shift-register updates for consensus. While the achieved rates match known accelerated methods, attributing them to a principled tuning of Min-Sum Splitting is novel. The auxiliary reduction from edge to node dynamics is a clean analytical contribution.

Significance:
- For consensus/averaging, accelerated rates are well known; thus, the practical impact on this specific problem is incremental. However, the conceptual bridge—showing how reparameterized Min-Sum achieves acceleration via directionality and memory—could inform the design and analysis of message-passing algorithms beyond quadratic consensus (e.g., other convex objectives, constraints). The result also clarifies when Min-Sum can be competitive with optimization-based accelerations and lifting, without explicit construction of a lifted graph.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical contribution: first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus.
- Elegant auxiliary-process reduction and spectral analysis linking to shift-register methods.
- Conceptual unification with lifted Markov chains and multi-step acceleration.
- General graph applicability via spectral quantities.

Weaknesses:
- Limited to quadratic objectives and synchronous settings; no discussion of extensions or robustness (asynchrony, time-varying graphs, noise).
- Requires global tuning (γ depends on ρW); no local/adaptive scheme or sensitivity analysis.
- No experimental validation; constants and finite-time behavior on practical graphs are not illustrated.
- Some presentation issues (typos, missing figure, reference formatting).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and technically solid analysis of Min-Sum Splitting with clear acceleration guarantees.
- Bridges communities (BP, Markov chain lifting, accelerated optimization) with a unifying perspective.
- Methods and proof techniques (auxiliary node process) may generalize to other problems.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical validation absent; practical implications and robustness remain unclear.
- Acceleration for consensus is not new per se; contribution is mainly interpretive and theoretical within a well-studied problem.
- Global parameter dependence reduces deployability; lack of discussion on estimating ρW in distributed settings.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add numerical experiments on representative graphs (cycle, grid, expander, random geometric) comparing to Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, and lifted schemes.
- Discuss robustness to γ mis-specification and provide guidelines or distributed estimation schemes for ρW.
- Clarify the positivity/minimization conditions and any constraints on Γ, δ ensuring well-defined iterates.
- Fix typos, include the referenced figure, and standardize references.

Overall, this is a solid theoretical paper with a clear and interesting connection that could motivate further work beyond quadratic consensus.
