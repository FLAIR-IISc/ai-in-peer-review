

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

## Summary
This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving the consensus problem in distributed networks. The authors establish convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting applied to the network averaging problem (a specific case of consensus), showing that proper tuning of splitting parameters yields a square-root improvement in convergence time over traditional diffusive methods. They prove that Min-Sum Splitting achieves O(D log(D/ε)) convergence time for cycle graphs and tori (where D is the graph diameter), compared to the O(D² log(1/ε)) of diffusive methods. The paper establishes connections between Min-Sum Splitting and two other acceleration approaches: lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization. This work extends prior results on Min-Sum for consensus (e.g., [27]), which only established convergence for specific graph topologies without providing convergence rates.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides the first rigorous analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, which is a significant gap in the literature. The proof technique using an auxiliary process to track the algorithm's evolution on the nodes (rather than the edges) is novel and well-executed.

2. **Important Connection**: The paper establishes a meaningful connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two other acceleration techniques (lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods), which is a valuable theoretical contribution that enriches our understanding of distributed optimization.

3. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a good balance between technical details and high-level intuition. The distinction between the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm and Min-Sum Splitting is well-motivated.

4. **Significant Improvement**: The square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods is substantial for problems where D is large (e.g., sensor networks with grid topologies), making the theoretical results highly relevant for practical applications.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Scope**: The paper focuses exclusively on the quadratic consensus problem (network averaging), which is a specific case of the broader consensus problem. The significance would be stronger if the results could be extended to more general consensus problems or other distributed optimization problems. The authors briefly mention generalization to other problems in the conclusion, but this remains speculative without concrete results.

2. **Practical Parameter Selection**: The optimal splitting parameters (δ = 1 and Γ = γW) depend on global graph properties (specifically, the spectral gap ρW of the matrix W). This is problematic for truly distributed settings where global information is unavailable, and the authors do not address how to estimate these parameters in a distributed manner.

3. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: While the paper shows Min-Sum Splitting achieves O(D log(D/ε)) convergence, it does not provide a direct comparison to other methods that achieve similar rates (e.g., Nesterov-accelerated methods [30, 36] or lifted Markov chain methods [37]). A more detailed comparison would strengthen the significance claim.

4. **Algorithmic Complexity**: The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm compared to other methods. For example, how does the number of messages exchanged per iteration compare to standard diffusive methods or multi-step methods?

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by establishing the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting in the consensus problem. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two other acceleration techniques is insightful and well-explained. The results are technically sound, and the paper is clearly written.

However, the limited scope (quadratic consensus only) and the dependence on global parameters for optimal performance reduce the practical impact of the results. The paper would be significantly stronger with:
1) A more detailed comparison to state-of-the-art accelerated methods
2) Discussion of how to select parameters in distributed settings without global information
3) Extension of results to more general consensus problems

The paper's theoretical contribution is strong, but its practical significance would be enhanced by addressing these limitations. The authors should consider expanding the scope of their analysis to more general problems and addressing the parameter selection challenge.

## Recommendation
The paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution with significant insights into the connection between Min-Sum Splitting and other acceleration techniques. However, the limited scope and lack of discussion on practical implementation challenges prevent it from being a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. I recommend **weak rejection** with the following suggestions for revision:

1. Extend the analysis to more general consensus problems (e.g., with non-quadratic objectives) or discuss the challenges of such extension.
2. Provide a more detailed comparison to other accelerated methods that achieve O(D log(1/ε)) rates.
3. Address the practical issue of parameter selection in distributed settings, perhaps by proposing a heuristic or distributed method to estimate the required spectral properties.
4. Discuss the computational complexity and message complexity of Min-Sum Splitting compared to other methods.

With these revisions, the paper would become a strong contribution to the field.
