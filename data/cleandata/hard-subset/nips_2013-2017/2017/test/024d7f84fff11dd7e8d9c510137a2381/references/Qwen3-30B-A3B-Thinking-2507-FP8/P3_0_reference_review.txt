

# NIPS Peer Review: "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

## Summary

This paper investigates the convergence properties of Min-Sum Splitting for solving the consensus problem (specifically network averaging) in distributed systems. The authors establish that by properly tuning the splitting parameters (δ and Γ), Min-Sum Splitting achieves accelerated convergence rates compared to traditional diffusive methods. The key contribution is showing that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods, scaling as O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori instead of the O(D² log(1/ε)) of standard approaches. The authors connect Min-Sum Splitting to two major lines of accelerated methods: lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step gradient methods in convex optimization. They introduce an auxiliary message-passing scheme that allows them to analyze convergence rates by relating them to the spectral properties of a matrix associated with the graph, rather than directly analyzing the more complex edge-based dynamics.

The paper builds on previous work by [27], which investigated Min-Sum for consensus but only established convergence for d-regular graphs with a specific initialization, and did not analyze convergence rates. The authors show that their approach overcomes the limitations of [27] by providing a general convergence rate analysis for any graph topology.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides the first rigorous analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The main result (Theorem 1) establishes a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods, which is a substantial theoretical advance.

2. **Novel Connection**: The paper successfully identifies and formalizes the connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two major lines of accelerated methods (lifted Markov chains and multi-step gradient methods). This unification is insightful and demonstrates a deeper understanding of the acceleration mechanisms across different domains.

3. **Technical Innovation**: The auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) is a clever technical innovation that simplifies the analysis by transforming the edge-based dynamics into a node-based multi-step process. This approach makes the spectral analysis tractable and provides a clear pathway for understanding the acceleration mechanism.

4. **Clear Positioning**: The paper effectively situates its contributions relative to prior work, particularly [27] and the literature on accelerated consensus methods. The discussion in Sections 4 and 7 clearly articulates how their work differs from and extends previous approaches.

5. **Well-Structured**: The paper is well-organized, with a logical flow from problem setup to theoretical results. The mathematical presentation is precise and consistent.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Analysis**: The paper focuses exclusively on the quadratic case (network averaging problem), which is a special case of the consensus problem. While this is a common starting point, the authors do not address whether their results extend to more general convex problems, which would significantly broaden the paper's impact. The connection to the broader Min-Sum literature (which is often applied to non-quadratic problems) is not sufficiently developed.

2. **Practical Implementation Concerns**: The optimal choice of parameters (γ = 2/(1 + √(1-ρ²_W))) requires global knowledge of the graph's spectral properties (specifically ρ_W), which is not available in distributed settings. The paper does not discuss how this parameter could be estimated or approximated in practice, limiting the practical applicability of the results. In contrast, many practical accelerated methods (like Nesterov's acceleration) can be implemented with local information.

3. **Lack of Experimental Validation**: The paper is purely theoretical with no empirical evaluation. While theoretical analysis is valuable, for a NIPS paper, demonstrating the practical impact through experiments on synthetic or real networks would strengthen the contribution significantly. The authors should consider adding experiments showing the convergence speedup on specific graph topologies.

4. **Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper mentions that their method approaches the optimal rate O(D log(1/ε)) but does not directly compare their convergence time with the most recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., [36] or [13]). A quantitative comparison would better demonstrate the practical significance of their result.

5. **Assumptions**: The paper assumes the graph is simple and undirected. It would be valuable to discuss how the approach might extend to directed graphs or graphs with self-loops, which are common in practical distributed systems.

## Technical Assessment

The theoretical analysis appears sound, with the main results (Theorems 1 and the supporting propositions) being well-motivated and carefully stated. The auxiliary process construction (Algorithm 3) is particularly elegant and represents a significant technical contribution. The proof technique of relating the convergence rate to the spectral properties of a matrix (K) is standard in the literature on accelerated methods, but the application to Min-Sum Splitting is novel.

However, the connection to the specific choice of parameters (γ = 2/(1 + √(1-ρ²_W))) could be more thoroughly explained. Why does this specific choice yield the optimal acceleration? A brief discussion of how this relates to the eigenvalue structure of the transition matrix would strengthen the paper.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial for the distributed optimization community. The square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods is a meaningful theoretical advance with potential practical implications for large-scale distributed systems. The connection to two major lines of accelerated methods suggests that the insights gained could be broadly applicable beyond consensus problems.

However, the significance would be greatly enhanced by:
1. Demonstrating the practical impact through experiments
2. Extending the analysis to non-quadratic problems
3. Addressing the practical implementation challenges of the parameter choice

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of Min-Sum message-passing algorithms and their connection to accelerated methods. While the limited scope (quadratic case only) and lack of experimental validation are weaknesses, the theoretical novelty and significance outweigh these limitations. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the field.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Add a brief discussion** of how the results might extend to non-quadratic consensus problems, even if just as a future work direction.

2. **Include a small set of experiments** (even synthetic ones) demonstrating the convergence speedup on cycle graphs and tori, to complement the theoretical analysis.

3. **Address the practical implementation challenge** of the parameter γ more thoroughly, perhaps by discussing how it might be estimated in a distributed manner.

4. **Clarify the comparison** with the most recent state-of-the-art accelerated methods (e.g., [36]) in terms of convergence time for specific graph topologies.

5. **Consider a brief discussion** of how the method might be adapted to directed graphs or graphs with self-loops, which are common in practical distributed systems.

## Final Verdict

This is a strong theoretical contribution that makes a novel connection between Min-Sum message-passing and two major lines of accelerated methods. The paper fills an important theoretical gap in the literature on Min-Sum algorithms and provides a clear path to understanding their acceleration mechanisms. With the suggested minor revisions addressing the practical implementation concerns and adding experimental validation, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

**Quality**: 9/10 (Theoretical analysis is sound, but lacks empirical validation)
**Clarity**: 8/10 (Well-organized, but the transition between algorithms could be smoother)
**Originality**: 9/10 (Novel connection to two different accelerated methods)
**Significance**: 8/10 (Theoretical significance is high, practical significance could be strengthened)

**Overall**: Strong acceptance. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of Min-Sum algorithms and their convergence properties.
