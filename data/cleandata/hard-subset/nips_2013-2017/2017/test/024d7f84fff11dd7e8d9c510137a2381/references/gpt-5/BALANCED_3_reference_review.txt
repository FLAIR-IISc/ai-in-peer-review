Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting, a reparameterized variant of the Min-Sum (a.k.a. Gaussian BP in the quadratic case) message passing algorithm, for the consensus/averaging problem. The authors (i) specialize Min-Sum Splitting to equality-constrained quadratic consensus, (ii) show that under appropriate parametrization, the edge-based messages evolve linearly, and (iii) construct an auxiliary node-based linear multi-step iteration that exactly tracks the algorithm’s output. Using this auxiliary system, they prove that with δ=1 and Γ=γW (W symmetric with W1=1), and a specific γ that depends on the spectral gap of W, the method converges to average consensus at an accelerated rate: the asymptotic contraction factor matches that of shift-register/Chebyshev-type second-order methods, yielding a square-root improvement over standard diffusive methods. For cycles and tori, this gives O(D log(D/ε)) iterations versus the Θ(D^2 log(1/ε)) for reversible random-walk-based gossip, approaching the O(D log(1/ε)) lower bound up to a log D term.

The paper positions the result relative to: (a) classical gossip/consensus (e.g., Metropolis-Hastings, Boyd et al.), which are diffusive; (b) accelerated consensus via polynomial/shift-register or heavy-ball/Nesterov methods (Xiao & Boyd; Ghadimi et al.; Golub & Varga; Scaman et al.); and (c) lifted, non-reversible Markov chains (Diaconis et al.; Jung–Shah–Shin), which achieve subdiffusive mixing at the cost of a lifted state space. The work is also related to Min-Sum Splitting (Ruozzi & Tatikonda), consensus propagation (Moallemi & Van Roy), and recent connections between ADMM and lifted dynamics. The novelty asserted here is the first convergence-rate analysis of Min-Sum Splitting, and the demonstration that appropriate splitting induces directionality/memory analogous to liftings and multi-step acceleration, without explicitly constructing a lifted graph.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The main theorem is plausible and well-aligned with known spectral analyses of second-order consensus (shift-register/Chebyshev). The reduction from edge-based BP-like dynamics to a node-based 2n-dimensional linear system K, and the spectral bound ρK < ρW, match classical results. The proof is relegated to the supplement; the mapping is credible and the constants are reasonable.
- Assumptions: The result requires W symmetric with W1=1 and knowledge of ρ(W−11^T/n) to set γ. Practicality of obtaining/estimating this global spectral information in a distributed way is not addressed. The analysis focuses on quadratic consensus with synchronous updates and no noise/asynchrony.
- Completeness: The theoretical development is self-contained for the stated setting. However, positivity/regularity conditions (e.g., ensuring denominators R_t stay positive in the ratio r_t/R_t) are assumed rather than characterized. No empirical validation is provided.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but suffers from presentation issues: several typos/formatting artifacts; occasionally overloaded notation (E vs E for directed edges; diag(Γ1); K vs K̂); missing equation references; and an abrupt assumption 0<b_v<1. The algorithmic description would benefit from an explicit node-based implementation (derived from the auxiliary system) to clarify communication/computation per iteration.

Originality
While accelerated consensus via second-order/polynomial methods and lifted chains is well-known, the contribution here is to show that Min-Sum Splitting, a message-passing scheme from graphical models, can be tuned to recover the same accelerated behavior and rates, and to provide the first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus. The conceptual bridge—splitting induces directionality on edges, which becomes a memory term on nodes—is interesting and, to my knowledge, not previously formalized for Min-Sum.

Significance
The result is a neat conceptual unification that could broaden the applicability of message-passing to accelerated distributed optimization. For consensus, the practical value hinges on whether one prefers an edge-based Min-Sum implementation over existing node-based second-order methods, given that the rate matches known shift-register/Chebyshev acceleration and requires similar global spectral tuning. The auxiliary-process proof technique may transfer to other Min-Sum instances, but this is not demonstrated.

Pros
- First convergence-rate analysis of Min-Sum Splitting; clear square-root speedup over diffusive gossip.
- Clean mapping from edge-based Min-Sum Splitting to a node-based 2n-dimensional linear system with known spectral properties.
- Conceptual links to non-reversible lifting and multi-step acceleration are insightful.
- Improvement over prior Min-Sum consensus propagation analyses (e.g., [27]), with topology-agnostic rates.

Cons
- Scope limited to synchronous, quadratic consensus; no experiments or empirical comparisons to accelerated gossip, Chebyshev, EXTRA/ADMM variants, or lifted chains.
- Requires global spectral information (ρW) to set γ; no discussion of distributed estimation/tuning or robustness to mismatch.
- Practical implementation details (communication/memory vs. node-based accelerations) and complexity are not compared.
- Some technical conditions (e.g., positivity of denominators; choice/design of W beyond symmetry and sparsity) are assumed but not characterized.
- Presentation/notation issues reduce clarity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a node-based algorithm derived from the auxiliary system (explicit update of a two-state variable per node) and compare per-iteration communication/computation to standard accelerated consensus (Chebyshev, heavy-ball, Nesterov).
- Add simulations on cycles, grids, random geometric and Erdos–Rényi graphs, comparing convergence rates and constants against Metropolis, optimally tuned Chebyshev/shift-register, EXTRA/ADMM, and lifted chains.
- Discuss how to choose γ without exact ρW: distributed power iterations, safeguarded tuning, or adaptive schemes; analyze robustness to γ-mismatch.
- Clarify conditions ensuring R_t>0 and the effect of different initializations beyond R̂0=r̂0=0.
- Comment on extensions: time-varying/asynchronous settings, quantization/noise, and beyond-quadratic objectives (e.g., strongly convex separable losses).
- Improve notation consistency and fix typos.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical result is solid and the conceptual connection is interesting, but the contribution is narrow (quadratic consensus), largely recovers known accelerated rates via an equivalence, and lacks empirical validation or a compelling practical advantage over established accelerated consensus methods. With experiments, clearer implementation guidance, and some robustness/adaptation analysis, this work would be significantly strengthened.
