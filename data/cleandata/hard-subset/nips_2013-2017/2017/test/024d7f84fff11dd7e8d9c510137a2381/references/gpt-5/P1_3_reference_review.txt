Summary
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting, a reparameterized variant of Min-Sum (a.k.a. Gaussian BP for quadratics), for distributed consensus/averaging. The authors focus on equality-constrained quadratic consensus, where standard linear consensus iterations x^{t}=Wx^{t-1} have diffusive convergence rates (mixing time Θ(D^2) on geometric graphs). The main contributions are:

- A specialization of Min-Sum Splitting to consensus that yields linear message updates on directed edges (Algorithm 2) with parameters δ and Γ.
- An auxiliary node-based linear multi-step process (Algorithm 3) that exactly tracks the edge-based Min-Sum Splitting output via a 2n×2n matrix K(δ,Γ).
- A choice of parameters (δ=1, Γ=γW with γ depending on the spectral radius of W−11^T/n) that ensures convergence at a “subdiffusive” rate matching the best-known shift-register/Chebyshev-type accelerated consensus: spectral factor ρ_K=√((1−√(1−ρ_W^2))/(1+√(1−ρ_W^2))) and asymptotic time O(1/(1−ρ_K) log((1/(1−ρ_K))/ε)) = Õ(D) on cycles and tori. This is a square-root improvement over diffusive O(D^2).
- Conceptual connections: splitting introduces directionality akin to non-reversible lifted Markov chains on a lifted state space (directed edges), and the node-level auxiliary system is a two-step method closely related to shift-register/Polyak/Nesterov-style acceleration.

Relative to prior work: Ordinary Min-Sum can diverge on loopy graphs; [27] studied a modified Min-Sum for consensus and linked rates to a 2m-state chain but only fully analyzed cycles. Splitting was proposed in [34,35] but rates were not established. Accelerated consensus via multi-step/Lanczos/Chebyshev methods and via lifted non-reversible chains are well known; the present work links Min-Sum Splitting to both and proves rates on general graphs.

Quality
- Theory: The core results are well-motivated and technically sound given the provided statements. The derivation of a linear edge-based iteration (Algorithm 2) for quadratic consensus, the construction of the node-based auxiliary process (Algorithm 3), and the spectral analysis culminating in Theorem 1 are coherent and align with known results on second-order/shift-register consensus (e.g., [24,45]). The equivalence propositions (edge-to-node mapping) are the key technical device and look plausible.
- Completeness: Proofs are in the supplement; the main text states results clearly but lacks empirical validation. No robustness analysis to parameter misspecification or to asynchrony/noise is given.
- Claims: The acceleration claim is asymptotic and matches best-known accelerated linear consensus; the paper does not claim to outperform those methods but to obtain them via Min-Sum Splitting with a clean analysis.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized. Algorithms and matrices K̂ and K are explicit. However, notation is heavy and some explanations could be tightened:
  - Improve intuition for how splitting induces directionality on edges and memory on nodes.
  - Clarify the role of δ beyond δ=1 (Theorem 1 uses δ=1, but δ is introduced as a degree of freedom).
  - Discuss existence/positivity conditions for R̂t (Proposition 1) and when the output is well-defined.
  - Minor typos and formatting (e.g., “st Conference …”).
- A small worked example (e.g., a path/cycle) illustrating Algorithm 2 and the mapping to Algorithm 3 would aid comprehension.

Originality
- Novelty lies in: (i) first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus; (ii) the auxiliary node process that reduces the 2m-dimensional edge dynamics to a 2n-dimensional multi-step iteration linked to Γ; (iii) explicit parameter tuning yielding accelerated rates and formal connections to lifted chains and multi-step optimization.
- While the accelerated rates are not new per se, obtaining them via tuned Min-Sum Splitting and proving correctness on general graphs appears new.

Significance
- Conceptual: Bridges BP/Min-Sum with accelerated consensus and non-reversible lifting. This is useful for theory and for understanding message-passing algorithms in distributed optimization.
- Practical: The method requires a global parameter γ depending on ρ_W, similar to existing accelerated consensus. In practice, one could use W=WMH and approximate γ, but guidance on robustness is missing. Since the auxiliary process is essentially the known shift-register method, the practical benefit over directly running node-based accelerated consensus is mainly interpretive rather than algorithmic. Nonetheless, the result advances theoretical understanding of Min-Sum Splitting.

Strengths
- Clear, rigorous spectral rate result (Theorem 1) with explicit constants and links to known optimal accelerated rates on geometric graphs.
- Elegant reduction from edge-level Min-Sum to node-level multi-step iteration; new analytical tool (Algorithm 3).
- Situates the work well in two literatures (lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods).
- Improves over [27] by providing general-graph guarantees and accelerated rates.

Weaknesses
- Limited scope: quadratic consensus only; no results for general convex φ_v or constraints beyond equality, despite Min-Sum’s broader applicability.
- Parameter dependence on global spectral information; no analysis of robustness to γ misspecification or strategies to estimate γ in a distributed way.
- No experiments to validate constants, transient behavior, or compare communication/compute overhead versus standard accelerated consensus or ADMM.
- It essentially recovers known accelerated rates; the practical algorithmic advantage of Min-Sum Splitting over established node-based methods is not demonstrated.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is convergence to errors in γ? Can you provide non-asymptotic bounds under γ-misspecification and simple distributed estimators of ρ_W?
- Do δ≠1 choices offer any advantage (rates, robustness, stability margins)?
- Can the auxiliary-process technique extend to strongly convex but non-quadratic φ_v or to inequality constraints via soft penalties?
- How does the per-iteration cost and memory compare to Chebyshev/shift-register consensus and to distributed ADMM?
- Any implications for time-varying or asynchronous networks?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments (cycles, grids, random geometric graphs) comparing: Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, Min-Sum Splitting with true/approximate γ. Report iteration/error and wall-clock/communication.
- Discuss robustness to γ and to initialization; provide heuristic γ rules when ρ_W is unknown.
- Clarify the relation to W selection: if Γ=γW must respect sparsity, do you assume W is pre-chosen (e.g., WMH)? Is optimizing W beneficial here?
- Expand on how the auxiliary-process viewpoint might generalize beyond consensus.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a clean and insightful theoretical contribution—first rate guarantees for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, with a novel analytical reduction and clear connections to two important acceleration paradigms. The lack of experiments and the focus on quadratics limit immediate practical impact, but the conceptual bridge and analysis should interest the NeurIPS community working on message passing and distributed optimization. Adding empirical validation and a robustness discussion would strengthen the paper.
