- Paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for quadratic consensus and proves the first convergence-rate results for averaging.  
- Main technical contributions: edge-based linearization, an exact auxiliary node-based two-step iteration, splitting-parameter choice yielding accelerated rates, and links to lifted Markov chains and multi-step acceleration.  
- Main theorem: with δ=1 and Γ=γW (γ depends on spectral gap) the error contracts at rate ρK < ρW, giving a square-root improvement in convergence time on cycles/tori.  
- Builds on Moallemi–Van Roy, Ruozzi–Tatikonda, and consensus propagation; connects to shift-register/Chebyshev acceleration and lifted Markov chains.  
- The derived rate matches classical shift-register/Chebyshev acceleration and is close to Nesterov-type O(D log(1/ε)).  
- Strength: provides a clear conceptual bridge between splitting-induced directionality (nonreversibility/lifting) and memory-based multi-step acceleration.  
- Strength: Theorem 1 gives an explicit spectral-rate analysis with constants and specialization to cycles/tori.  
- Strength: The auxiliary node-based two-step iteration (Propositions 1–2) simplifies analysis and may be reusable beyond consensus.  
- Strength: Shows that appropriately split BP converges on loopy graphs and attains near-optimal accelerated rates.  
- Weakness: Results are limited to quadratic averaging; unclear extension to general convex or constrained objectives.  
- Weakness: Acceleration rate itself is not new—main novelty is the BP/splitting interpretation and the auxiliary reduction.  
- Weakness: Choice of γ requires global spectral-gap knowledge; no guidance on estimation, robustness, or decentralized tuning.  
- Weakness: No experiments or empirical comparisons to Chebyshev, Nesterov, ADMM/EXTRA, or other accelerated methods.  
- Weakness: Practical implementation details (recommended edge vs node implementation, per-iteration communication/memory, positivity of denominators) are not fully discussed.  
- Weakness: Relation to ADMM-based acceleration and recent linear-time consensus methods could be expanded and compared.  
- Quality: Core theorem and reductions appear technically sound, but some assumptions (e.g., positivity/convexity and R^t>0) should be stated more explicitly.  
- Clarity: Paper is dense but organized; algorithm definitions are clear though there are minor editorial issues and missing summaries of costs/assumptions.  
- Originality: Moderate—first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and a novel interpretive connection, but rate itself aligns with existing methods.  
- Significance: Moderate—valuable for BP/message-passing researchers; limited broader impact since rates match known accelerations and are slightly suboptimal by a log D factor.  
- Pros for acceptance: first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, insightful conceptual links, and a reusable analytical tool.  
- Cons for acceptance: no experiments, limited empirical/implementation guidance, and novelty mainly interpretive rather than algorithmic.  
- Suggestions: add empirical benchmarks, discuss decentralized γ estimation and robustness, clarify positivity/complexity and recommended implementation, and outline extensions beyond quadratics.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept (high reviewer confidence).
