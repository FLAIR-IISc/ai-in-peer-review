

# Peer Review: "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces a novel theoretical analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving the consensus problem in distributed networks. The authors establish convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, showing that proper tuning of splitting parameters yields accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence compared to traditional diffusive methods. The key insight is that Min-Sum Splitting can be viewed as a process on a lifted space (directed edges) that, when properly parameterized, exhibits acceleration analogous to both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step gradient methods in convex optimization. The authors establish a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods, achieving O(D log(D/ε)) convergence for cycle graphs and tori (where D is the graph diameter), compared to the Θ(D² log(1/ε)) rate of standard methods.

The paper connects to previous work in several important ways: it builds upon the Min-Sum algorithm [28, 34] and its variants, relates to the network averaging problem studied in [42, 41], and connects to the two main lines of research on accelerated consensus algorithms: lifted Markov chains [8, 5, 37] and accelerated first-order methods [13, 24]. The paper also extends the work of [27], which investigated Min-Sum for consensus but did not establish convergence rates.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution with rigorous proofs of convergence rates. The authors carefully establish the connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two different acceleration techniques, and provide a novel proof technique using an auxiliary process on the nodes of the graph. The paper is complete and well-structured, with clear definitions and theorems. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, noting that "it remains to be investigated how choices of splitting parameters different than the ones investigated in Theorem 1 affect the convergence behavior."

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the main contributions are clearly stated. The connections to previous work are well-explained, and the paper provides sufficient background to understand the context of the problem. The supplementary material (mentioned in the paper) should contain additional proofs that would help readers fully understand the technical details.

**Originality:** This paper makes a significant contribution by establishing the first theoretical analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting. The key novelty is the connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two different acceleration techniques (lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods), which had previously been developed independently. The paper shows that the directionality introduced by splitting in Min-Sum is analogous to the directionality in lifted Markov chains, while the memory term introduced by splitting corresponds to multi-step methods. This connection is novel and well-justified.

**Significance:** The consensus problem is fundamental in distributed systems, and the paper demonstrates a meaningful improvement in convergence rates (square-root improvement over diffusive methods) for a wide class of graphs. The results show that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve rates close to the optimal O(D log(1/ε)) established for some Nesterov-accelerated methods [30, 36]. The paper opens new research directions by suggesting that the acceleration mechanism via splitting could be generalized to other distributed optimization problems beyond consensus.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Scope of Applications:** The paper focuses exclusively on the quadratic case (network averaging problem), which is a specific instance of the consensus problem. While this is a natural starting point, the paper doesn't address how the approach might generalize to non-quadratic objectives, which would significantly broaden its applicability. The authors note in Section 8 that "further studies to generalize the acceleration due to splittings to other problem instances" are needed, but this limitation should be more prominently discussed in the main paper.

**Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical with no experimental results to validate the claimed convergence rates. While the theoretical analysis is strong, including some empirical comparisons (e.g., convergence time on cycle graphs or tori) would strengthen the paper's claims and help readers better understand the practical implications.

**Superficial Connection to Lifted Markov Chains:** The paper mentions connections to lifted Markov chains but doesn't deeply explore the similarities. The connection seems to be primarily structural (both involve directionality), but the paper doesn't discuss how the specific lifting parameters in Min-Sum Splitting relate to the optimal lifting parameters in previous work. A deeper exploration of this connection would strengthen the paper's contribution.

**Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of implementing the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. While the theoretical convergence rate is improved, the overhead of maintaining the auxiliary process or the splitting parameters might offset some of the gains in practice. A brief discussion of computational complexity would be valuable.

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- First theoretical analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting
- Novel connection between Min-Sum message-passing and two different lines of research (lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods)
- Significant theoretical contribution showing square-root improvement in convergence rates
- Well-structured, technically sound paper with rigorous proofs
- Clear statement of contributions and connections to previous work

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Focuses only on the quadratic case, limiting applicability
- Lacks empirical validation to support theoretical claims
- Connection to lifted Markov chains is somewhat superficial
- Doesn't discuss computational complexity of the algorithm
- Limited to the consensus problem, with no indication of broader applicability

## Specific Comments and Suggestions

1. **Empirical Validation:** I strongly recommend adding a section with empirical results comparing the convergence rates of Min-Sum Splitting with traditional diffusive methods (e.g., Metropolis-Hastings) and accelerated methods (e.g., Nesterov's acceleration) on cycle graphs and tori. This would greatly strengthen the paper's claims.

2. **Non-quadratic Objectives:** The paper should discuss how the approach might extend to non-quadratic objectives. The authors could mention specific challenges or potential modifications needed for more general consensus problems.

3. **Computational Complexity:** A brief discussion of the computational complexity of implementing Min-Sum Splitting (compared to standard methods) would be valuable. How many additional operations does the splitting parameterization require?

4. **Connection to Lifted Markov Chains:** The paper mentions the connection to lifted Markov chains in Section 7, but this could be strengthened by more explicitly comparing the directionality parameters in Min-Sum Splitting to those in lifted Markov chains.

5. **Supplementary Material:** I recommend that the supplementary material include detailed proofs of all claims and potentially additional examples to help readers better understand the auxiliary process.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that makes a significant connection between two previously separate lines of research on accelerated consensus algorithms. The paper establishes a novel proof technique using an auxiliary process on the nodes, and demonstrates a square-root improvement in convergence rates over diffusive methods. While the focus on the quadratic case and lack of empirical validation are notable limitations, the theoretical contribution is substantial and advances the state of the art in distributed optimization.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in distributed systems with a novel theoretical approach that connects to multiple areas of machine learning and optimization. The results have clear potential for practical applications in sensor networks, distributed machine learning, and multi-agent systems.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limitations noted above, particularly adding empirical validation and expanding the discussion of the algorithm's applicability beyond the quadratic case.

## Final Recommendation

**Accept** (with minor revisions)
