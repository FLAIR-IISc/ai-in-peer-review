

# Review: "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

## Summary

This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving the consensus problem in distributed networks. The consensus problem involves nodes in a graph exchanging information to reach agreement on the average value across the network. The authors establish that Min-Sum Splitting, a generalization of the traditional Min-Sum algorithm, achieves accelerated convergence rates (O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori) compared to classical diffusive methods (O(D² log(1/ε))). The paper makes several key contributions: (1) first analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, (2) connection between Min-Sum schemes and both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step methods in convex optimization, and (3) proof that proper tuning of splitting parameters yields a square-root improvement in convergence rate.

The work builds on previous research on Min-Sum for consensus in [27], which showed that ordinary Min-Sum does not converge in graphs with cycles, and proposed a modified version that achieves O(D/ε log(1/ε)) convergence for cycle graphs. The current paper significantly advances this by providing a general theoretical analysis for any graph topology and establishing superior convergence rates.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with a rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors establish Theorem 1 with a clear proof technique using an auxiliary linear process to track the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. This approach is well-motivated and provides a significant improvement over the analysis in [27], which only characterized convergence for specific graph topologies (like cycle graphs) and presented conjectures for others.

The paper carefully distinguishes between ordinary Min-Sum and Min-Sum Splitting, and properly acknowledges the limitations of the approach (e.g., the need for global information to set the optimal parameters). The authors are honest about the scope of their results, focusing on the quadratic case for the consensus problem, and correctly note that generalization to non-quadratic problems remains an open question.

The mathematical presentation is precise, with clear definitions of the algorithm (Algorithm 1), its application to consensus (Algorithm 2), and the auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3). The connection to shift-register methods in numerical analysis is particularly well-established through Theorem 1.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and establishes the context within the literature. The organization of the paper (from general Min-Sum Splitting to specific application to consensus) follows a logical progression that is easy to follow.

The authors do an excellent job of explaining the connections between their work and prior research. They clearly articulate how Min-Sum Splitting relates to lifted Markov chain techniques (by introducing directionality in message exchange) and to multi-step methods (by introducing memory terms for the auxiliary process). The notation is consistent throughout, and the key theorems and propositions are well-explained.

The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of the practical implications of the global information requirement (ρW), but otherwise, the exposition is clear and accessible to experts in distributed optimization.

## Originality

This paper makes several significant original contributions. First, it provides the first rigorous analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, which has been previously observed to yield convergence where ordinary Min-Sum fails [35]. Second, the paper establishes the first connection between Min-Sum schemes and both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step accelerated methods in convex optimization. This theoretical connection is novel and insightful.

The paper distinguishes itself from previous work by [27] in several important ways. While [27] characterized the convergence behavior for specific graph topologies (cycles) and presented conjectures for others (tori), this paper provides a general analysis for any graph topology. Moreover, [27] achieved O(D/ε log(1/ε)) convergence for cycles, while the current paper achieves O(D log(D/ε))—a square-root improvement in the convergence rate.

The paper is substantially different from previous NIPS papers on consensus (e.g., [30, 36] which focus on Nesterov's acceleration) and from papers on Min-Sum (e.g., [27, 34, 35]) by providing a convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting that establishes its relationship to two major lines of accelerated methods.

## Significance

The results have significant implications for distributed systems where consensus is a fundamental primitive operation. The square-root improvement in convergence rate (from O(D² log(1/ε)) to O(D log(D/ε))) is substantial, particularly for large-scale distributed systems where the graph diameter D can be large.

The connection to both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step methods provides theoretical insights that could guide the development of future accelerated algorithms. This work bridges two previously separate lines of research in distributed optimization, which is valuable for the field.

The paper's significance is further enhanced by its potential to motivate further research on generalizing the acceleration due to splittings to other problem instances beyond consensus. The authors correctly identify this as an important direction for future work.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear proof techniques
- Novel connections between Min-Sum Splitting and two major lines of accelerated methods
- First convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting
- Clear exposition and organization
- Proper comparison with previous work (including [27])

**Weaknesses:**
- The need for global information (ρW) to set optimal parameters may be challenging in fully distributed settings where nodes have limited knowledge
- Focus on quadratic objective functions (network averaging) without clear discussion of generalization to non-quadratic problems
- No empirical validation of the theoretical claims (would strengthen the paper significantly)
- Limited discussion of communication complexity or computational overhead compared to simpler methods

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The paper makes significant theoretical advances in understanding the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, establishes novel connections to two major lines of accelerated methods, and provides a rigorous convergence rate analysis that improves upon previous work.

The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), well-written and organized (Clarity), makes important new contributions (Originality), and addresses a significant problem with meaningful results (Significance).

The main weakness is the lack of empirical validation, which would have strengthened the paper considerably. However, for a theoretical contribution focused on convergence rate analysis, the absence of experiments is not necessarily a fatal flaw, especially given the paper's strong theoretical foundation.

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of distributed optimization algorithms and their convergence properties. The connections to existing lines of research are insightful and well-established, and the results have clear implications for the design of efficient distributed algorithms.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in distributed systems. It is substantially different from previous NIPS papers on consensus and distributed optimization, and it advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

The minor weaknesses (lack of empirical results, focus on quadratic case) are common in theoretical papers and do not detract significantly from the paper's overall contribution.
