- Investigates convergence rates of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus and shows careful parameter tuning yields acceleration comparable to Nesterov methods.  
- Establishes a square-root improvement in convergence time, with O(D log(D/ε)) scaling for cycles and tori.  
- Introduces a novel auxiliary linear process to track the algorithm’s evolution and derive convergence rates.  
- Connects Min-Sum Splitting to lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order/shift-register methods.  
- Novel contribution: fills a theoretical gap by analyzing Min-Sum Splitting’s convergence properties.  
- Technical soundness: rigorous proofs, well-motivated analysis, and clear proof outline.  
- Significance: practically important for large-scale distributed systems, especially on graphs with geometry.  
- Clarity: generally well-written and organized; derivations and theory are logically presented.  
- Complexity: mathematically dense and may be inaccessible without strong optimization and graph theory background.  
- Limited empirical validation: no experiments provided, weakening practical impact.  
- Scope concerns: theoretical claims for any graph but analysis focuses mainly on cycles and tori; behavior on other graph classes unclear.  
- Potential overhead: splitting parameters introduce additional computational cost; trade-offs not discussed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, rigorous, well-connected to related areas, and potentially high practical impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: accessibility issues, lack of empirical validation, and limited practical discussion.  
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical paper deserving acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions; suggest adding discussion of practical considerations and preliminary experiments.
