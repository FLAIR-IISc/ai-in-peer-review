## Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. It establishes that, with proper parameter tuning, Min-Sum Splitting achieves accelerated convergence (subdiffusive rates) compared to traditional diffusive methods, achieving a square-root improvement in convergence time.  The authors connect their work to both lifted Markov chain techniques and accelerated first-order methods, framing Min-Sum splitting as bridging these two approaches. They introduce an auxiliary linear process to track the algorithm’s evolution, facilitating the analysis and proving convergence rates.  The key result demonstrates convergence rates approaching the optimal O(D log(1/ε)) for certain graph topologies.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Significance:** The analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting is a novel contribution. Establishing accelerated convergence for this algorithm is significant, offering a potentially valuable tool for distributed optimization.  The connection to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods provides a broader theoretical understanding.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical analysis appears rigorous, with a clear outline of the auxiliary process used for analysis. The paper builds upon existing work in consensus algorithms and message passing, correctly referencing relevant literature (e.g., [27], [37]). The proofs are deferred to supplementary material, which is standard practice.
* **Clarity:** The introduction is well-written and provides a solid background. The paper clearly defines the problem, the algorithm, and the main results. The notation is generally consistent.
* **Well-motivated:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing diffusive methods and the motivation for exploring accelerated alternatives.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity & Accessibility:**  The paper is dense with mathematical notation and requires significant background knowledge in optimization, graph theory, and distributed algorithms. This could limit its accessibility to a broader audience. While the introduction provides context, the later sections delve deeply into technical details without always providing sufficient intuition.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the analysis is compelling, the absence of any experimental validation weakens the practical impact of the results.  Demonstrating the benefits of Min-Sum Splitting on realistic graph topologies would greatly strengthen the paper.
* **Parameter Tuning Complexity:** While the paper identifies the optimal parameters (δ = 1, Γ = γW), determining 'γ' requires knowledge of the spectral gap (ρW) which, in itself, can be challenging to compute for arbitrary graphs. This practical hurdle isn’t fully addressed.
* **Incremental Gain:** While the result of subdiffusive convergence is significant, the gains (square-root improvement) are perhaps incremental compared to existing accelerated methods, and this comparison isn’t always clearly highlighted in the results.



**Qualitative Evaluation (Addressing the NIPS Criteria):**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a rigorous analysis and well-supported claims. However, the lack of experimental validation is a concern.
* **Clarity:** The writing is generally clear, but the density of mathematical notation and technical details could make it difficult for some readers to follow.
* **Originality:** The analysis of Min-Sum Splitting's convergence rates is original. The connection to lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods provides a novel perspective.
* **Significance:**  The results are potentially significant, offering an alternative algorithm for distributed consensus. The subdiffusive convergence rates are a valuable contribution, though the practical implications need further investigation.



**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating accelerated convergence.
* Novel connection between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated gradient methods.
* Addresses a challenging problem in distributed optimization.

**Con:**

* Lack of experimental validation.
* High complexity and potential accessibility issues.
* Parameter tuning requires knowledge of graph spectral properties.
* Incremental improvement over existing accelerated methods.


**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of Min-Sum Splitting and its convergence properties. However, the lack of experimental validation is a major weakness. The authors should consider including some simulations, even on simple graph topologies, to demonstrate the practical benefits of their approach.  Furthermore, improving the clarity of certain sections and providing more intuition behind the mathematical derivations would enhance the paper's accessibility. Addressing the parameter tuning complexity issue (e.g., providing heuristics for estimating γ) would also be beneficial.
