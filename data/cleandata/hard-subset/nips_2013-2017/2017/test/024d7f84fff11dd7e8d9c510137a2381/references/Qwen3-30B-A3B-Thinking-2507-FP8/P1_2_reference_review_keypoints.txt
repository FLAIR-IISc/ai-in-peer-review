- Investigates Min-Sum Splitting for distributed consensus and shows proper parameter tuning yields accelerated convergence.  
- Demonstrates Min-Sum Splitting is equivalent to a lifted process on directed edges and to a multi-step method on nodes, linking lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization.  
- Theorem 1: a specific parameter choice gives roughly a square-root improvement over diffusive methods, yielding O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles and tori.  
- Builds on prior Min-Sum literature and improves on [27] by establishing convergence rates for general graph topologies.  
- Introduces a novel auxiliary linear-process proof technique to track Min-Sum Splitting dynamics.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and technically sound, with well-supported proofs.  
- First paper to provide convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, resolving prior empirical observations.  
- Lacks experimental results; simulations on simple graphs would strengthen the theoretical claims.  
- Well-written and organized with consistent notation and clear definitions.  
- Assumes reader familiarity with message-passing and graph theory; a brief glossary or expanded Min-Sum intro could help.  
- The dual interpretation (lifted + multi-step) is an original and insightful conceptual advance.  
- Results are significant: square-root improvement and near-optimal rates for graphs with geometry.  
- Connects to distributed ADMM and shift-register methods, indicating broader applicability.  
- Practical limitation: optimal parameter selection depends on global graph information (e.g., spectral gap), which may be impractical in distributed settings.  
- Scope limited to quadratic (network averaging) objectives; extension to more general problems remains open.  
- Reviewer recommends acceptance due to the strong theoretical contribution.  
- Suggests adding small numerical experiments (e.g., cycles and 2D tori) to validate convergence rates.  
- Additional improvement suggestions: discuss practical challenges of computing the optimal parameter, state constant factors in O(D log(D/ε)), clarify why standard Min-Sum conditions fail for constrained problems, and compare to algorithms like Extra or D-ADMM.
