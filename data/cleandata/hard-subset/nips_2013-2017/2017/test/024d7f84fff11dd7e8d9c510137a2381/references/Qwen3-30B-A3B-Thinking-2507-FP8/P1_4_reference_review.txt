

# Peer Review: "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

## Summary
This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving consensus problems in distributed networks. The authors establish convergence rates for this algorithm, demonstrating that proper tuning of splitting parameters yields a square-root improvement in convergence time over classical diffusive methods. They show that Min-Sum Splitting connects to both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first-order optimization methods. The main technical contribution is an auxiliary message-passing scheme that allows the authors to analyze Min-Sum Splitting by reducing it to a linear process with memory on the graph nodes.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound and rigorously supported by theoretical analysis. The authors provide a complete convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting applied to the consensus problem, which fills a critical gap in the literature (previous work [27] had only established convergence for specific graph topologies like cycle graphs, not the general case). The proof technique using an auxiliary process is novel and well-executed. The authors establish that with proper parameter tuning, Min-Sum Splitting achieves rates of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, representing a square-root improvement over diffusive methods (O(D² log(1/ε))). The theoretical analysis is thorough, with proofs provided in the supplementary material, and the authors carefully acknowledge limitations (e.g., "It remains to be investigated how choices of splitting parameters different than the ones investigated in Theorem 1 affect the convergence behavior").

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized with a logical flow from background to problem statement to technical contributions. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The connection to previous work is clearly explained, and the authors effectively differentiate their approach from related work (e.g., [27]). The paper might be somewhat dense for readers unfamiliar with message-passing algorithms, but the authors provide sufficient background to follow the main arguments. A minor suggestion for improvement would be to include a simple example illustrating the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm in action, which could help readers grasp the intuition more quickly.

## Originality
The paper makes several significant original contributions. First, it provides the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, which has been observed to converge in settings where ordinary Min-Sum does not [35]. Second, it establishes the first connection between Min-Sum schemes and both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first-order optimization methods, which have previously evolved independently. Third, it introduces a novel proof technique using an auxiliary process to track the evolution of Min-Sum Splitting. The authors carefully differentiate their work from previous contributions [27, 28, 34, 35] and provide comprehensive references to related work.

## Significance
The results have substantial practical implications for distributed computing, where faster consensus is crucial for large-scale systems. The paper demonstrates that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve convergence rates close to the theoretical optimum (O(D log(1/ε)) for some algorithms [30, 36]), which is a major improvement over previous methods. By connecting Min-Sum to two different lines of research on accelerated consensus algorithms, the paper opens new avenues for future work. This is particularly valuable for applications in sensor networks, distributed machine learning, and multi-agent systems where consensus is a fundamental primitive. The paper makes a demonstrable advancement in the state of the art by providing the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and establishing its relationship to existing accelerated methods.

## Strengths
1. First convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting algorithm
2. Novel connection between Min-Sum and two different lines of research on accelerated consensus
3. Square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods
4. Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear proof technique
5. Careful differentiation from previous work and acknowledgment of limitations

## Weaknesses
1. The paper might be somewhat inaccessible to readers unfamiliar with message-passing algorithms
2. Practical implementation details and computational complexity are not discussed in depth
3. The authors do not provide empirical results to complement their theoretical analysis (though this may be acceptable for a theoretical paper)

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of distributed optimization, with a clear connection to both theoretical and practical aspects of consensus problems. The quality of the analysis is high, the originality is strong, and the significance is clear. While the paper could benefit from a bit more practical context and a simple example to illustrate the algorithm, these are minor points that do not detract from the main contribution.

The paper aligns well with NIPS topics, particularly in the areas of distributed optimization, theoretical machine learning, and statistical learning. It provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for a practical distributed algorithm with clear connections to existing work in the field. The results are likely to be of interest to researchers working on distributed systems, optimization, and machine learning.

This is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes important contributions to our understanding of distributed optimization algorithms. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and accelerated methods in both Markov chain theory and optimization is particularly insightful and likely to inspire future work. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a significant contribution to the theory of distributed algorithms, which is a relevant topic for the conference.
