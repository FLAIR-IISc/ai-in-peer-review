Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the Min-Sum (a.k.a. belief propagation for MAP) algorithm with splitting/reparametrization (Ruozzi & Tatikonda) and establishes convergence rates when applied to the consensus/averaging problem. The main results are: (i) a parametrization of Min-Sum Splitting for quadratic consensus that induces a linear message passing on directed edges; (ii) an auxiliary node-based linear two-step process that exactly tracks the output of the edge-based algorithm; and (iii) a tuning of the splitting parameters (δ = 1, Γ = γW with γ depending on the spectral gap of a symmetric W) that yields an accelerated “subdiffusive” rate matching shift-register/Chebyshev-type acceleration known in numerical analysis and accelerated consensus (Xiao & Boyd; Golub & Varga; Ghadimi et al.; Young). For cycles and tori this gives O(D log(D/ε)) iterations to reach ε error, a square-root improvement over diffusive methods O(D^2 log(1/ε)), but short of the O(D log(1/ε)) achieved by some Nesterov-based schemes.

The work connects Min-Sum Splitting to two strands: (a) lifting and non-reversible Markov chains for faster mixing (Diaconis et al.; Jung–Shah–Shin), and (b) multi-step first-order/shift-register methods for consensus and distributed optimization (Xiao & Boyd; Ghadimi et al.; Olshevsky; Scaman et al.). It improves on prior Min-Sum analyses for consensus (Moallemi & Van Roy; Moallemi & Van Roy “Consensus Propagation”; Ruozzi & Tatikonda on splitting) by providing convergence rates and a principled parameter choice, and by clarifying when/why directionality (via splitting) accelerates convergence.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual bridge: elegant equivalence between edge-based Min-Sum Splitting and a node-based two-step linear iteration, making spectral analysis tractable and linking BP-style message passing to accelerated linear solvers.
- Theoretical guarantees: a clean spectral-rate result (Theorem 1) with explicit dependence on ρ(W), showing square-root acceleration over diffusive consensus on geometric graphs and recovering shift-register rates within a BP framework.
- Broad contextualization: situates the contribution amid lifted chains and multi-step optimization, and clarifies how global information (eigenvalues, diameter) enters parameter tuning similarly across methods.
- Addresses a known gap: prior Min-Sum/consensus work established correctness/mixing properties in special cases; here the rate analysis is general across graph topologies (given a symmetric W).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in rate: the accelerated rate matches existing shift-register/Chebyshev acceleration; the novelty lies in the mapping to Min-Sum Splitting rather than a new acceleration mechanism or improved rate.
- Strong assumptions/scope: results are for quadratic consensus (averaging), synchronous updates, and require global spectral information to set γ; extension to general convex φv or constraints is left open.
- Lack of empirical validation: no experiments comparing Min-Sum Splitting with Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, Nesterov-accelerated gossip, lifted chains, or ADMM-based methods on representative graphs; no robustness tests to mis-specified γ or asynchronous updates.
- Practical aspects underdeveloped: communication/storage complexity per iteration vs standard methods, sensitivity to initialization (e.g., positivity condition for beliefs), and guidance on choosing W in practice are not fully articulated.
- Presentation issues: several typos and footnote artifacts; some notation heavy sections could be streamlined; figures are referenced but not present here.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The technical development is sound and well-motivated; the auxiliary process and spectral analysis appear correct given the literature they build on. However, proofs are deferred to the supplement, and practical performance claims lack empirical support.
- Clarity: Generally clear, with helpful high-level narrative. The paper would benefit from tighter notation management, explicit statements of conditions (e.g., positivity in Proposition 1), and a short proof sketch of Theorem 1 in the main text.
- Originality: The primary novelty is the theoretical connection and rate analysis of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus via an auxiliary node-based process. The achieved rate is not new per se, but the route and insight for BP-style algorithms are original.
- Significance: Conceptually valuable for unifying message passing, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods; useful to researchers who use BP variants in distributed optimization. Practical impact would be strengthened by experiments, robustness analyses, and extensions beyond quadratic objectives.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant and insightful theoretical connection; clarifies the role of splitting/directionality in accelerating BP.
- General rate result across graph topologies; square-root improvement over diffusive consensus.
- Bridges communities (BP, Markov chain lifting, accelerated optimization) in a principled way.

Cons for acceptance
- Accelerated rate is already known for other methods; contribution is mainly reframing rather than advancing state-of-the-art performance.
- No empirical validation or practical guidance; unclear advantages in practice over simpler accelerated consensus schemes.
- Scope restricted to quadratic consensus with global parameter tuning and synchronous updates.

Questions and suggestions for improvement
- Provide experimental evaluation on cycles, grids, random geometric graphs, and expanders. Compare to Metropolis, optimal symmetric W, Chebyshev/shift-register, Nesterov-accelerated gossip, lifted chains, and decentralized ADMM. Report iteration counts and wall-clock/communication costs.
- Discuss robustness: how sensitive is performance to mis-specifying γ or using local approximations to ρ(W)? Can γ be tuned adaptively from local measurements (e.g., polynomial filtering/Lanczos-on-the-fly)?
- Clarify computational/communication complexity and memory vs standard consensus and shift-register methods. Any advantage of the edge-based BP implementation vs purely node-based multi-step methods?
- Extend or at least outline extensions to strongly convex/non-quadratic φv (e.g., smooth + strongly convex), possibly via quadratic approximations or proximal variants. What breaks in the analysis?
- Consider asynchronous/time-varying communication and mild message delays.
- Improve presentation: fix typos, unify notation, state all technical conditions in the main text, and add an intuitive explanation/diagram of the auxiliary mapping.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper offers a neat and potentially influential conceptual link and a solid rate analysis within the Min-Sum Splitting framework, but the absence of empirical validation and the fact that the accelerated rate matches existing methods limit its immediate impact for NeurIPS. With experimental evidence, practical guidance, and an exploration beyond the quadratic case, this work would be much stronger.
