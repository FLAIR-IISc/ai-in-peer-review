

# NIPS Review: "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

## Summary

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum Splitting to the consensus problem in distributed networks, establishing convergence rates that significantly outperform traditional diffusive methods. The authors show that Min-Sum Splitting, a generalization of the standard Min-Sum algorithm, can be tuned to achieve a square-root improvement in convergence time over classical approaches. Specifically, while diffusive methods scale as O(D² log(1/ε)) (where D is the graph diameter), Min-Sum Splitting achieves O(D log(D/ε)) convergence. The paper makes a novel connection between Min-Sum message-passing algorithms and two established approaches for accelerating consensus: lifted Markov chains and multi-step gradient methods. The authors introduce a new proof technique using an auxiliary process supported on the nodes of the graph to track the evolution of the algorithm, which they argue is more analytically tractable than the edge-based formulation used in prior work.

The paper builds upon the foundational work of [27] (which investigated Min-Sum for consensus but showed only suboptimal convergence rates) and [34] (which introduced Min-Sum Splitting but lacked convergence analysis). It connects to the broader literature on distributed optimization, including the work of [30, 36] on Nesterov's acceleration for consensus.

## Strengths

### Quality
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide a clear proof of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting applied to the consensus problem, which is a significant contribution since "a theoretical investigation of the rates of convergence of Min-Sum Splitting has not been established" as stated in the paper. The key result (Theorem 1) establishes the asymptotic convergence rate and shows how it relates to the spectral properties of the underlying graph. The authors carefully distinguish between the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm (which may not converge for graphs with cycles) and Min-Sum Splitting (which does converge with proper parameter tuning). The connection to shift-register methods in numerical analysis is particularly compelling, as it demonstrates that the same matrix governing the convergence rate appears in multiple domains.

### Originality
The paper makes several important original contributions:
1. It establishes the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, addressing a clear gap in the literature.
2. It shows a novel connection between Min-Sum message-passing algorithms and two distinct approaches for accelerating consensus (lifted Markov chains and multi-step gradient methods).
3. It provides a new proof technique using an auxiliary process on the nodes of the graph, which is more analytically tractable than the edge-based formulation in prior work.
4. It demonstrates that the acceleration mechanism in Min-Sum Splitting is analogous to mechanisms in both lifted Markov chains and multi-step gradient methods, providing a unified perspective on these approaches.

### Significance
The consensus problem is fundamental to distributed systems, with applications in sensor networks, multi-agent systems, and distributed machine learning. The paper's contribution of a square-root improvement in convergence time (from O(D² log(1/ε)) to O(D log(D/ε))) is significant, as it allows Min-Sum Splitting to approach the optimal rate O(D log(1/ε)) established for some accelerated methods. This makes the approach competitive with the state-of-the-art in distributed optimization. The paper also motivates further research into generalizing the acceleration due to splittings to other problem instances beyond consensus.

## Weaknesses

### Clarity
While the paper is well-organized and technically sound, some sections are dense and may be challenging for readers without a strong background in distributed optimization. The connection to machine learning applications could be strengthened. The paper mentions distributed machine learning in the introduction but doesn't explicitly demonstrate how Min-Sum Splitting could be applied to specific machine learning tasks (e.g., distributed training of large-scale models), which would better position it for NIPS. The paper could also benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implementation challenges (e.g., how the global information required for parameter tuning might be obtained in practice).

### Empirical Validation
The paper is purely theoretical with no empirical results to validate the theoretical claims. While theoretical analysis is important, NIPS typically values a combination of theoretical and empirical results. The absence of experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical impact of the proposed method. The authors could have included small-scale simulations to illustrate the convergence behavior of Min-Sum Splitting compared to other methods.

### Scope for NIPS
The paper is more closely aligned with the distributed systems and optimization communities than with the machine learning community that dominates NIPS. While distributed optimization is relevant to machine learning, the paper doesn't sufficiently bridge this gap. A stronger connection to machine learning applications (e.g., distributed stochastic optimization, federated learning) would better position it for NIPS.

## Final Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the analysis of Min-Sum Splitting for the consensus problem, establishing convergence rates that outperform previous methods. The connection to lifted Markov chains and multi-step gradient methods is novel and insightful, and the new proof technique using an auxiliary process is a valuable contribution to the analysis of distributed algorithms.

However, the paper would be stronger with empirical validation and a clearer connection to machine learning applications. While the theoretical contribution is substantial, the lack of empirical results and the somewhat narrow focus on distributed optimization (without sufficient machine learning context) limits its impact for a NIPS audience.

## Recommendation

**Accept with minor revisions** (or **Accept** if the authors can address the empirical validation and machine learning connection in the camera-ready version)

**Justification**: The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution that advances the understanding of distributed optimization algorithms. The quality of the theoretical analysis is high, and the results are significant for the distributed optimization community. The main weaknesses (lack of empirical validation and limited machine learning context) are important but not critical for the theoretical contribution. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of how the results could be applied to machine learning problems and potentially some small-scale simulations to illustrate the convergence behavior. If the authors can incorporate these elements, the paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS.

**Pro for acceptance**: Strong theoretical contribution, novel connections to existing methods, rigorous proof of convergence rates, significant improvement over previous methods.

**Con for acceptance**: Limited empirical validation, somewhat narrow scope for NIPS, limited discussion of machine learning applications.

The paper is technically sound, original, and significant within the distributed optimization community, and with minor revisions to strengthen the connection to machine learning, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
