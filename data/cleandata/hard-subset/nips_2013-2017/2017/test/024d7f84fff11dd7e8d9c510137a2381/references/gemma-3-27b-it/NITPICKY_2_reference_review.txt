This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors establish convergence and demonstrate a square-root improvement over diffusive methods, achieving rates comparable to recent advancements using Nesterov's acceleration. The paper connects Min-Sum splitting to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, providing a unifying perspective.

While the paper presents a significant theoretical contribution, establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum splitting—something previously lacking—the presentation and level of detail in several areas are insufficient and contain inconsistencies, raising concerns about the rigor of the results. The writing is often dense and lacks clarity, making it difficult to follow the technical arguments.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum splitting is a genuine contribution. The connection to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated optimization methods provides valuable insight.
* **Theoretical Rigor (Potential):** The core ideas seem sound, and the approach of using an auxiliary linear process is clever.
* **Significance:** Accelerating consensus algorithms is important for various distributed applications. A square-root improvement in convergence rate is a meaningful result.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceedingly dense. Key definitions (e.g., K̂(δ,Γ) in Section 5, K in Section 7) are presented without sufficient motivation or explanation. The jump between the algorithm descriptions (Algorithm 1, 2, 3) and the associated analysis is abrupt and difficult to follow. Equations are often presented without sufficient context.
* **Inconsistent Notation:** The definition of the adjacency matrix A is introduced without formalizing its connection to the graph G. Furthermore, the graph G is introduced as having *n* nodes and *m* edges, yet the later equations operate with dimensions referencing *n* without explicitly relating it to *m*. The change from lowercase to uppercase for matrices (e.g. W in Section 4, then K in Section 7) without explanation is confusing.
* **Missing Details in Proof Sketches:** The paper states "proofs are given in the supplementary material," but the supplementary material was not provided for review. Therefore, the correctness of the claims cannot be verified. The proofs *mentioned* in the main text (e.g., Proposition 1 and 2) are not provided, and simply asserting their existence is insufficient.
* **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses exclusively on theoretical analysis. While theoretical guarantees are valuable, the lack of any experimental evaluation limits the practical impact of the work. It is unclear how sensitive the results are to the choice of parameters or the specific graph structure.
* **Overclaiming Connections:** The connection to shifted register methods, while interesting, is presented as a direct recovery of the same rate. However, the conditions for achieving this rate (Theorem 1) are quite specific and may not be easily met in practice. The claim of a similar relationship to ADMM is presented vaguely, without detailed comparison.
* **Redundancy and Verbosity:** The introduction is excessively long and repetitive. The literature review, while extensive, is not always focused and includes lengthy descriptions of established concepts.
* **Minor Technical Issues:** The assertion in Section 3 that “φv(z) := 12z 2−bvz” is equivalent to φv(z) := 12(z−bv)2 is only true up to a constant term. While not crucial, it’s a minor imprecision.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The theoretical result establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum splitting is a valuable addition to the literature.
* The paper identifies a connection between different approaches to accelerating consensus algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of clarity and the numerous inconsistencies significantly hinder understanding and make it difficult to assess the correctness of the claims.
* The absence of the supplementary material prevents verification of the proofs.
* The lack of empirical validation limits the practical impact of the work.
* The presentation is overly dense and verbose, obscuring the core ideas.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially significant contribution, the current state of the paper is unacceptable for NIPS. The lack of clarity, the missing supplementary material, and the absence of empirical validation outweigh the novelty of the theoretical result. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should significantly revise the paper, addressing the clarity and consistency issues, providing a complete and verifiable proof in the supplementary material, and including some empirical validation to demonstrate the practical relevance of their findings. The paper should be rewritten with a more concise and focused presentation. The connections to other works should be more thoroughly investigated and explained.
