This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors establish convergence and derive accelerated rates, achieving a square-root improvement over diffusive methods for cycle and tori graphs. The work connects Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, providing a unified perspective on acceleration techniques in consensus.

The paper is generally well-structured, with clear definitions and a logical flow of ideas. However, it suffers from a degree of verbosity, particularly in the introduction, which could be streamlined. The technical details are largely present, but frequently obscured by dense writing and a lack of explicit connections between equations and their interpretations. The notation, while consistent, is also quite heavy and sometimes makes following the derivations difficult. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting is a significant contribution, filling a gap in the literature. Establishing a square-root improvement over diffusive methods is a noteworthy result.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper employs a solid theoretical framework, building on existing work in Markov chains and optimization. The auxiliary process introduced for analysis is a clever approach.
* **Connections to Existing Literature:** The paper successfully connects Min-Sum Splitting to related areas like lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, offering valuable insights.
* **Potential Impact:** The results could be relevant to various applications of distributed consensus, particularly in large-scale machine learning and sensor networks.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Excessive Verbosity:** The introduction, in particular, is overly long and contains redundant statements.  The summary of existing work on consensus algorithms feels exhaustive to the point of being distracting.
* **Lack of Clarity in Derivations:** While the mathematical derivations appear correct, they are often presented without sufficient explanation.  The jump between equations is sometimes abrupt, and the reader is left to infer the reasoning. For example, the rationale behind defining the matrix K and K∞ in Section 7 is not adequately explained before the equation is presented.
* **Heavy Notation:** The extensive use of symbols and indices makes the paper difficult to read and understand.  Simplifying the notation would significantly improve clarity.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks any empirical evaluation. While the focus is on theoretical analysis, some simulations, even on small graphs, would help validate the theoretical results and provide practical insights.
* **Minor Technical Issues:** Equation (4) lacks a clear definition of *u* and *z*. The role of *u* is never clearly defined; it appears to index the edges, but this is never explicitly stated. Also, the statement of Theorem 1 could be more precise, particularly regarding the conditions for the applicability of the results (e.g., assumptions on the graph structure).
* **Formatting inconsistencies:** "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)" feels out of place within the introduction. It would be appropriate in the full paper header, but breaks the flow here.
* **Unclear Motivation for Choices:** The choice of δ=1 and Γ=γW feels somewhat arbitrary without a deeper justification. The authors state it yields subdiffusive rates, but a more intuitive explanation would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel theoretical result with potential significance for distributed optimization.
* The analysis is rigorous and builds upon existing work in the field.
* The connections to related areas are insightful.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper suffers from significant clarity and conciseness issues.
* The lack of empirical evaluation weakens the claims.
* The density of notation and the lack of detailed explanations make the paper difficult to follow.
* The numerous minor flaws, while individually small, collectively detract from the overall quality of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a novel result, its lack of clarity, verbosity, and absence of empirical validation raise serious concerns. The numerous minor flaws also contribute to a negative overall impression. I believe the paper, in its current form, does not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS. A major revision addressing the clarity, conciseness, and empirical validation aspects would be necessary for reconsideration. I recommend **rejection**. The potential of the work is evident, but it is currently obscured by its presentation.
