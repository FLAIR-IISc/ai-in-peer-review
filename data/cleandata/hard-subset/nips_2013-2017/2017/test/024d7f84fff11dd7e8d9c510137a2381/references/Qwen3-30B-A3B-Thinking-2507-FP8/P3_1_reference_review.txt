

# Peer Review: "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving the consensus problem (specifically network averaging) in distributed systems. The authors establish convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, demonstrating that proper tuning of splitting parameters yields accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence rates compared to classical diffusive methods. The key theoretical contribution is showing that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve convergence time O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, representing a square-root improvement over diffusive methods that scale like Θ(D² log(1/ε)).

The paper makes two significant conceptual contributions:
1. It establishes the first connection between Min-Sum schemes and both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first order methods in convex optimization
2. It introduces a novel proof technique using an auxiliary linear process on the nodes of the graph, rather than the directed edges, to analyze convergence

This work builds on previous literature on consensus algorithms (e.g., [27] which studied ordinary Min-Sum for consensus), lifted Markov chains [5, 8, 37], and accelerated first order methods [13, 24]. While prior work has explored accelerated consensus algorithms through either lifted Markov chains or multi-step methods, this paper uniquely identifies a connection between these two approaches through the lens of Min-Sum Splitting.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical novelty**: The paper successfully establishes convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, which has been an open question in the literature. This is a significant theoretical contribution to the field.

2. **Innovative proof technique**: The introduction of the auxiliary process in Section 6 is elegant and potentially useful for analyzing other message-passing algorithms. This approach avoids the complexity of analyzing the dynamics on directed edges (as done in [27]) and instead relates convergence to the spectral properties of a matrix on the nodes.

3. **Conceptual connections**: The paper successfully demonstrates how Min-Sum Splitting bridges two separate lines of research (lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods), providing a unifying perspective that wasn't previously apparent.

4. **Clear technical presentation**: The paper is well-organized and the mathematical derivations are carefully presented, with clear propositions and theorems.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Lack of experimental validation**: The paper is entirely theoretical with no empirical results to validate the claimed convergence rates. Given the practical nature of consensus algorithms, this is a significant omission. A comparison with state-of-the-art accelerated methods on real graph topologies would strengthen the claims.

2. **Limited scope of analysis**: The paper focuses exclusively on the quadratic case (network averaging) with specific parameter choices. While this is a standard problem, the authors claim "further studies to generalize the acceleration due to splittings to other problems" but provide no evidence that the approach extends beyond this special case.

3. **Practical implementation concerns**: The parameter choice γ = 2/(1 + √(1-ρ_W²)) requires global knowledge of the graph's spectral properties (specifically ρ_W), which is typically unavailable in distributed settings. The paper doesn't address how to estimate ρ_W in a distributed manner, which is a major practical limitation.

4. **Overstated significance**: The claimed "square-root improvement" is somewhat misleading. The bound 1/(1-ρ_K) ≤ √(1/(1-ρ_W)) means the convergence rate improves by a square root factor, but this doesn't translate to a square-root improvement in convergence time when considering the logarithmic dependence on ε. The convergence time is still O(D log(1/ε)), not O(√D log(1/ε)) as might be implied by the "square-root improvement" claim.

5. **Comparison with recent work**: The paper doesn't adequately compare with the most recent accelerated consensus methods. For example, Scaman et al. (NIPS 2017) showed optimal convergence rates for distributed optimization on networks, and their approach might be more practical than the Min-Sum Splitting method proposed here.

## Technical Concerns

1. **Theorem 1 interpretation**: The paper states "the convergence time is O(D log(D/ε)" but the bound in Theorem 1 is actually O(1/(1-ρ_K) log(1/ε)), and 1/(1-ρ_K) = Θ(D) for cycle graphs, so the convergence time is O(D log(1/ε)), not O(D log(D/ε)). The authors should clarify this distinction.

2. **Parameter selection**: The choice of δ = 1 and Γ = γW requires knowing ρ_W, which is a global property. In distributed settings, computing ρ_W typically requires global knowledge or additional communication, which undermines the distributed nature of the algorithm. The paper doesn't address this practical limitation.

3. **Connection to shift-register methods**: The paper claims to recover the same matrix as shift-register methods, but the connection isn't clearly explained. A more detailed comparison with the specific shift-register methods (e.g., [45, 4, 24]) would strengthen this claim.

4. **Assumptions**: The paper assumes quadratic objective functions, which limits applicability. It would be valuable to discuss how the approach might extend to non-quadratic problems.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting and by identifying important conceptual connections between different lines of research on accelerated consensus algorithms. The proof technique using the auxiliary process is particularly elegant and may find applications beyond the specific problem considered.

However, the paper's significance is limited by its theoretical nature and lack of experimental validation. The practical implementation challenges (requiring global knowledge of the graph for parameter selection) are not addressed, which is a significant drawback for a distributed algorithm. The claimed "square-root improvement" is also somewhat misleading in the context of the convergence time.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The theoretical contribution is sound and novel, but the lack of experimental validation and the practical limitations of the parameter selection significantly weaken the paper's impact. The paper would be much stronger with:

1. Empirical results showing convergence rates on specific graph topologies
2. A discussion of how to estimate the required global parameters in a distributed manner
3. A clearer comparison with recent accelerated consensus methods (e.g., Scaman et al. NIPS 2017)

Without these additions, the paper's contribution is primarily theoretical and somewhat narrow in scope. It would be more compelling if it demonstrated the practical viability of the approach.

## Specific Recommendations for Revision

1. **Add experimental results**: Include simulations showing convergence rates on cycle graphs and tori, comparing Min-Sum Splitting with classical diffusive methods and other accelerated methods.

2. **Clarify the "square-root improvement" claim**: Be precise about the relationship between convergence rates and convergence times, and avoid potentially misleading phrasing.

3. **Address practical parameter selection**: Discuss how to estimate ρ_W in a distributed setting, or explore parameter choices that don't require global information.

4. **Expand comparison with recent work**: Compare with Scaman et al. (NIPS 2017) and other state-of-the-art distributed optimization methods.

5. **Consider broader applicability**: Briefly discuss how the approach might extend beyond the quadratic case, even if a full analysis isn't included.

This paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field, but requires more evidence of practical relevance and better contextualization within the current state of the art in distributed optimization.
