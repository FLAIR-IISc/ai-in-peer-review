Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting, a reparametrized form of Min-Sum (a.k.a. Gaussian BP in the quadratic case), for the equality-constrained consensus/averaging problem. The authors show that: (i) for quadratic consensus, Min-Sum Splitting reduces to a linear, scalar message-passing algorithm on directed edges; (ii) via a carefully constructed auxiliary process on nodes, the edge dynamics can be tracked by a 2n-dimensional linear recursion with memory; (iii) with a specific tuning (δ = 1, Γ = γW where W is a symmetric consensus matrix and γ depends on ρ(W−11^T/n)), the algorithm converges at an accelerated “subdiffusive” rate, matching shift-register/Chebyshev-type accelerated consensus: asymptotic convergence time O(1/(1−ρ_K) log([1/(1−ρ_K)]/ε)) with 1/(1−ρ_K) ≈ √(1/(1−ρ_W)). Hence, on cycles and tori this yields O(D log(D/ε)) vs O(D^2 log(1/ε)) for diffusive methods.

This connects and contrasts with prior work: (a) ordinary Min-Sum for consensus may diverge on loopy graphs (Moallemi & Van Roy); (b) “consensus propagation” variants and soft penalties (Moallemi & Van Roy) were linked to Cesàro mixing on edge-based chains and analyzed primarily for regular graphs; (c) accelerated consensus via multi-step methods (e.g., shift-register/Chebyshev, heavy-ball, Nesterov) and lifted, non-reversible Markov chains are known to break the diffusive barrier. The novelty here is a rigorous rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, the auxiliary node-based representation, and an explicit parameter tuning that recovers the same accelerated rate as shift-register methods, while clarifying how “directionality” introduced by splitting relates to lifting and momentum.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual bridge: The paper convincingly relates Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step accelerated optimization, with a concrete auxiliary linear system on nodes that simplifies spectral analysis.
- Theoretical contribution: First rigorous convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, with precise asymptotics matching known accelerated methods and improving over diffusive rates on graphs with geometry.
- General topology: Results are stated for arbitrary graphs (via spectral properties of W), not restricted to regular graphs.
- Technically neat: The derivation of the node-level 2n-state recursion K(δ,Γ) and the equivalence between edge-based and node-based processes are elegant and likely reusable.

Weaknesses
- Scope limited to quadratic (averaging) consensus with synchronous updates and exact arithmetic; no empirical validation to assess constants, robustness, or practical gains vs known accelerated methods.
- Dependence on global information: The optimal γ uses ρ(W−11^T/n). This mirrors lifted chains and accelerated methods but challenges decentralized implementability; no discussion of distributed/adaptive tuning or robustness to γ mis-specification.
- Incremental in rate: The achieved rate matches existing shift-register/Chebyshev methods; the main novelty is the Min-Sum Splitting perspective and analysis, rather than a new acceleration regime or improved constants.
- Clarity and notation: Some notational overload (e.g., K used for both K(δ,Γ) and the 2n×2n matrix in Theorem 1), and conditions for positivity in Proposition 1 are assumed rather than verified under the chosen initialization (R̂0=r̂0=0). The paper defers key proofs to the supplementary with limited intuition in the main text.
- Practical costs: Communication/computation per iteration (edge vs node updates), memory, and convergence constants are not compared to standard accelerated consensus, ADMM-based methods, or lifted gossip.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level: the spectral analysis and mapping to shift-register dynamics are plausible and consistent with known results. However, important technical details (ensuring convexity/positivity of quadratic beliefs for the specific initialization, stability under small perturbations of γ, and explicit constant bounds) are deferred. Adding proof sketches or sanity-check lemmas in the main text would improve confidence.

Clarity
Generally well-organized, with a helpful narrative. Some notation is dense; carefully distinguishing K(δ,Γ) (auxiliary) from the shift-register matrix K in Theorem 1 would improve readability. A figure or pseudocode showing how the edge-based updates collapse to the 2n recursion would aid understanding. There are minor typographical/formatting issues and incomplete references.

Originality
Moderate to good. The rate itself is not new, but the analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, the auxiliary node-level representation, and the explicit conceptual tie to lifting and multi-step acceleration appear novel and could influence subsequent work on message-passing acceleration.

Significance
Moderate. For researchers who use message passing beyond consensus, the results suggest a pathway to accelerate Min-Sum via splitting. However, the current contribution is limited to averaging and requires global tuning. Demonstrating extensions (e.g., to broader convex objectives, constraints, or to ADMM connections) would raise impact.

Pros for acceptance
- First rigorous rate analysis of Min-Sum Splitting; clean auxiliary-process technique.
- Clarifies relationships among three literatures (BP/min-sum, lifted chains, accelerated gradient).
- Meaningful theoretical improvement over diffusive rates on geometric graphs.

Cons for acceptance
- Scope limited to quadratic consensus; no experiments.
- Requires global spectrum information; no adaptivity/robustness study.
- Achieves known best rates rather than surpassing them; practical advantages over existing accelerated consensus are unclear.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide empirical results on cycles, grids, random geometric graphs, and expanders comparing convergence constants, communication/memory costs, and robustness to mis-tuned γ against Chebyshev/shift-register, Nesterov, EXTRA/Exact Diffusion, and ADMM-based consensus.
- Discuss how to estimate or track ρ(W) in a decentralized fashion (e.g., power iterations, polynomial filtering), and analyze sensitivity when γ is mis-specified.
- Clarify conditions ensuring positivity/convexity of quadratic beliefs under the stated initialization; include a proof sketch in the main paper.
- Expand discussion on extending the approach to general strongly convex separable objectives and to asynchronous or time-varying graphs.
- Improve notation (disambiguate K matrices), fix minor typos, and standardize references.

Recommendation
Marginal reject. The paper offers a neat and potentially useful theoretical connection and rate analysis, but its scope is narrow, it lacks empirical validation, and the acceleration matches existing methods without clear practical advantages. Strengthening with experiments, robustness/adaptivity, and a clearer path to broader objectives would likely make it competitive.

Confidence: 4/5
