## Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors demonstrate that, with a proper parameter tuning, Min-Sum Splitting achieves faster convergence than traditional diffusive methods (and improves upon previous Min-Sum-based approaches) achieving rates comparable to those obtained with Nesterov’s acceleration. They establish a square-root improvement in convergence time, scaling as O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles and tori, and present a novel analysis technique based on an auxiliary linear process to track the algorithm’s evolution. Importantly, they connect Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chain techniques and accelerated first-order methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting is a significant contribution. While Min-Sum Splitting itself was introduced earlier, a theoretical understanding of its convergence properties was missing.  The connection to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated optimization methods is insightful and provides a broader context for the work.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous mathematical analysis. The use of the auxiliary process is a clever way to track the dynamics of the algorithm and derive convergence rates. The proof outline is clearly stated, and the connection to shift-register methods is well-motivated.  The established convergence rate of O(D log(D/ε)) is a strong result.
* **Significance:** Accelerated consensus algorithms are crucial for large-scale distributed systems.  A square-root improvement in convergence time is practically significant, especially for graphs with geometry where diffusive methods perform poorly.  The connections made to other areas of research (lifted Markov chains, accelerated optimization) highlight the broader applicability of the findings.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction provides sufficient background and motivation. The derivations, while complex, are presented in a logical manner.  The connection between the theory and the auxiliary algorithm is clearly explained.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The paper is mathematically dense and requires a strong background in optimization and graph theory to fully understand. While the organization is good, the numerous algorithms and propositions can be overwhelming.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:**  The paper relies entirely on theoretical analysis. While the analysis is rigorous, the lack of experimental validation weakens the impact.  Demonstrating the improvements empirically, even on relatively small-scale examples, would strengthen the claims.
* **Scope of Graphs:**  The theoretical results are presented for "any graph topology," but the specific analysis appears to focus primarily on cycles and tori, and the benefits compared to existing methods are most clearly articulated for these cases.  More discussion of the algorithm's behavior on other graph classes would be beneficial.
* **Potential for Overhead:** The splitting parameters introduce additional computational overhead. While the asymptotic convergence rate is improved, a discussion of the practical trade-offs between convergence speed and computational cost would be valuable.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical contribution to the field of distributed optimization.
* The convergence rate analysis is rigorous and well-motivated.
* The connections to other areas of research provide valuable insights.
* The potential for practical impact is high, particularly in applications requiring fast consensus.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is mathematically challenging and may be inaccessible to some readers.
* The lack of empirical validation limits the practical impact of the results.
* The discussion of practical considerations (e.g., computational overhead) is limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. The rigorous analysis and the connection to established methods in optimization and distributed systems make it a valuable addition to the NIPS program.  The lack of empirical validation is a concern, but the theoretical results are compelling enough to justify acceptance.  I believe the paper meets the NIPS criteria of quality, clarity, originality, and significance, despite its mathematical complexity.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions.  I suggest the authors consider adding a section discussing potential practical considerations (e.g., computational overhead) and exploring the possibility of including some preliminary experimental results to support their theoretical findings.
