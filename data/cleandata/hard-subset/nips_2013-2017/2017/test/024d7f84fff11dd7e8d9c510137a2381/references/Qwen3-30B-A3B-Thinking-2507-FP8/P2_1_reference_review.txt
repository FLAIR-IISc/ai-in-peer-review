

# NIPS Review: "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

## Summary

This paper introduces and analyzes a novel distributed algorithm called "Min-Sum Splitting" for solving the consensus problem (specifically, the network averaging problem) on arbitrary graph topologies. The authors build upon the Min-Sum message-passing algorithm, which is known to converge to the correct solution for tree-structured problems but may fail for graphs with cycles. They introduce "splitting" parameters that, when properly tuned, allow the Min-Sum protocol to achieve accelerated convergence rates compared to traditional diffusive methods. The key insight is that Min-Sum Splitting can be interpreted as a process on a lifted space (directed edges of the graph) that achieves subdiffusive convergence rates, and also as a multi-step method with memory on the original graph nodes. The authors establish a square-root improvement in convergence time from Θ(D² log(1/ε)) for diffusive methods to Θ(D log(D/ε)) for cycles and tori, where D is the graph diameter. They connect their approach to two independent lines of research: lifted Markov chains and multi-step accelerated first-order methods.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS literature on distributed optimization and consensus algorithms, building on prior work in message-passing algorithms (e.g., [28, 34, 35]) and accelerated methods ([13, 24, 30, 36]).

## Strengths

### Quality
The paper is technically sound and makes a strong theoretical contribution. The authors establish rigorous convergence rate guarantees for their proposed algorithm, which is a significant advancement over previous work. They provide a new proof technique based on constructing an auxiliary linear process to track the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, which is a novel contribution. The mathematical analysis is thorough and well-supported, with the authors carefully addressing the conditions under which their results hold. The paper demonstrates a clear understanding of the limitations of previous methods (e.g., the failure of ordinary Min-Sum on cyclic graphs) and provides a principled way to overcome these limitations.

### Originality
This paper makes several important original contributions:
1. It establishes the first connection between Min-Sum schemes and lifted Markov chains techniques as well as multi-step methods in convex optimization.
2. It provides the first proof of how directionality embedded in Belief Propagation protocols can be tuned to accelerate convergence.
3. It offers the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting.
4. It introduces a new proof technique using an auxiliary process to track the algorithm's evolution.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work on Min-Sum (e.g., [27]) and the broader literature on accelerated consensus algorithms (e.g., [7, 37, 45]). The authors appropriately cite related work and position their contributions within the existing literature.

### Significance
The consensus problem is a fundamental distributed optimization problem with wide applications in sensor networks, multi-agent systems, and distributed machine learning. The paper's results are significant because they provide a method that achieves convergence rates close to the optimal O(D log(1/ε)) for certain graph topologies (cycles and tori), which is a substantial improvement over the standard Θ(D² log(1/ε)) rate of diffusive methods. This square-root improvement in convergence time is particularly valuable for large-scale distributed systems where communication is expensive. The paper's connection to two independent lines of research (lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods) opens up new avenues for future work in distributed optimization.

### Clarity
The paper is well-written, well-organized, and clearly communicates its main contributions. The structure follows a logical flow from background to problem statement, algorithm description, theoretical analysis, and conclusions. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The authors clearly state their main contributions in the introduction and provide a helpful roadmap for the rest of the paper. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and other approaches is explained clearly, which helps readers understand the significance of their results.

## Weaknesses

### Limited Empirical Validation
While the paper provides a strong theoretical analysis, it lacks empirical validation. NIPS typically values both theoretical and empirical contributions, and the absence of experimental results to corroborate the theoretical claims is a notable weakness. It would have been valuable to include simulations demonstrating the convergence rates on various graph topologies (e.g., cycle graphs, tori) compared to baseline methods.

### Global Information Dependency
The paper shows that achieving the accelerated convergence rate requires tuning the splitting parameters based on global information about the graph (specifically, the spectral gap of a matrix associated with the graph). This is a limitation for practical distributed implementations, where global information is often unavailable. The paper doesn't discuss how this dependency could be addressed in a distributed setting, which would be important for real-world applications.

### Focus on Quadratic Case
The paper focuses exclusively on the quadratic case of the consensus problem (network averaging), which is a common setting but limits the generality of the results. The authors don't discuss how their approach might extend to more general objective functions, which would be important for broader applicability.

### Limited Discussion of Communication Complexity
The paper doesn't analyze the communication complexity of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm compared to other methods. In distributed systems, communication is often the bottleneck, so understanding the communication requirements of the proposed algorithm would be important for practical implementation.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of distributed optimization, establishing a significant improvement in convergence rates for the consensus problem. The work is technically sound, original, and addresses an important problem with clear significance for distributed systems. While the lack of empirical validation and the dependency on global information are notable weaknesses, the paper's theoretical advances are substantial and likely to have a lasting impact on the field.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- Strong theoretical contribution with rigorous convergence rate analysis
- First connection between Min-Sum schemes and two independent lines of research (lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods)
- First proof of how directionality in Belief Propagation can be tuned for acceleration
- Square-root improvement in convergence rates over diffusive methods
- Well-written and clearly organized

## Arguments against Acceptance

- Lack of empirical validation to support theoretical claims
- Requires global information for parameter tuning, limiting practical applicability
- Focuses only on the quadratic case of the consensus problem
- Doesn't analyze communication complexity of the proposed algorithm

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contribution is significant and well-executed, making a clear advance in the state of the art for distributed consensus algorithms. While the lack of empirical validation is a weakness, the paper's theoretical rigor and novelty more than compensate for this limitation. The connection to two independent lines of research is particularly valuable, as it opens up new avenues for future work that could lead to practical improvements in distributed optimization.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) at a high level, with only minor weaknesses that don't detract from its overall contribution to the field. The authors have made a clear and important advance in understanding how to accelerate distributed consensus algorithms, and their work is likely to influence future research in distributed optimization.

I would recommend minor revisions to address the lack of empirical validation (e.g., adding a small set of simulation results) and to discuss the practical implications of the global information dependency, but these are not necessary for acceptance. The paper is already of high quality and makes a substantial contribution to the field.
