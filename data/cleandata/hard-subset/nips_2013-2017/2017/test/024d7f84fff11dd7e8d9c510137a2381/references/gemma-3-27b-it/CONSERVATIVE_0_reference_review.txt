This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors establish rates of convergence for any graph topology, achieving a square-root improvement over diffusive methods in certain cases. The work connects Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, providing a valuable theoretical analysis and highlighting the algorithm's potential.

However, despite the interesting theoretical results, I find the presentation somewhat dense and the novelty, while present, feels incremental given the substantial body of work already existing on accelerated consensus algorithms. The paper relies heavily on established concepts (Min-Sum, consensus, accelerated methods) and, while demonstrating a connection between them in this specific context, doesn’t offer a genuinely disruptive new idea. The theoretical results, while mathematically rigorous, lack a compelling demonstration of practical impact. The gains over existing methods, while theoretically sound, may be marginal in real-world applications given the complexity added by splitting.

**Strengths:**

*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed and mathematically sound analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, establishing convergence rates for general graph topologies.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors effectively connect Min-Sum Splitting to established techniques like lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods, providing valuable insights into its theoretical underpinnings.
*   **Improved Convergence Rate:** The claimed square-root improvement over diffusive methods, particularly for cycle graphs and tori, is a positive contribution, albeit incremental.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The consensus problem is well-defined, and the motivation for seeking faster convergence algorithms is clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Novelty:** The primary contribution – analyzing the convergence of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus – feels incremental. Similar acceleration strategies exist within both the lifted Markov chain and accelerated optimization frameworks.
*   **Complexity & Practicality:** The benefit of splitting, while theoretically demonstrable, isn’t clearly translated into practical advantages. The algorithm's increased complexity compared to simpler methods needs stronger justification.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks empirical validation. While rigorous theoretical analysis is appreciated, demonstrating the performance gains on real-world graph datasets is crucial for convincing the community of the method's usefulness. The lack of runtime comparisons to established methods (e.g., Nesterov’s acceleration-based algorithms mentioned in the introduction) is a significant omission.
*   **Dense Presentation:** The paper is mathematically dense and can be difficult to follow without a strong background in optimization and graph theory. A more accessible presentation, perhaps with illustrative examples, would improve its readability.
*   **Extensive Background:** The introduction spends a considerable amount of space reviewing existing work. While thorough, it detracts from the focus on the novel contributions.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a theoretically sound analysis of a relatively unexplored aspect of the Min-Sum algorithm.
*   The connection to established acceleration techniques provides valuable insights.
*   The work could stimulate further research into applying splitting techniques to other optimization problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty is incremental, and the practical impact is unclear without empirical validation.
*   The dense presentation and lack of experimental results limit the paper's accessibility and impact.
*   The gains over existing methods may be marginal in practice.

**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the theoretical contributions are valuable, the lack of empirical validation and the incremental nature of the novelty raise concerns. The paper's density and length further detract from its overall impact. If the authors can address the empirical validation concerns and improve the presentation, the paper would be a stronger candidate for acceptance. Without these improvements, the paper remains a theoretically interesting but practically unconvincing contribution. I encourage the authors to consider adding a section with preliminary experiments to demonstrate the practical benefits of Min-Sum Splitting.
