This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving the consensus problem. The authors establish that, with appropriate parameter tuning, the algorithm achieves accelerated convergence, specifically a square-root improvement over diffusive methods, and rates approaching those of recently established algorithms based on Nesterov’s acceleration. The key contribution lies in demonstrating that the splitting parameters, when properly chosen, induce a directionality in the message exchange analogous to techniques used in lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods. The analysis relies on constructing an auxiliary linear process on the nodes of the graph to track the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical results are well-supported by a detailed analysis, and the connection to existing accelerated methods is clearly articulated. The use of an auxiliary process is a clever technique that simplifies the analysis. The authors carefully state their assumptions and provide a clear roadmap of their contributions. However, the proofs are relegated to supplementary material, making it challenging for the reader to fully verify the technical details without substantial effort. While the paper highlights similarities to lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods, a more in-depth discussion comparing the computational complexities and practical implications of each approach would strengthen the analysis. The assumption of quadratic objective functions limits the generalizability of the results, and a discussion on how the approach might be extended to other scenarios would be valuable.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, although it is dense and requires significant background knowledge in distributed optimization and message passing. The introduction provides a good overview of the problem and the paper’s contributions. However, the sheer number of citations and related works can be overwhelming. The notation is consistent, but the numerous variables and indices require careful attention.  The connection between the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm (Algorithm 1) and the auxiliary algorithm (Algorithm 3) could be made more explicit. A visual representation illustrating the relationship between the two algorithms would improve clarity. The paper also assumes a high degree of familiarity with the related literature, and while many references are provided, briefly summarizing the key concepts from these works within the main text would be helpful.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, demonstrating its connection to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods – a connection not previously established in the literature. The derivation of accelerated convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting is a significant contribution. While the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm itself is not new, the theoretical analysis of its convergence properties, particularly the achievement of subdiffusive rates, is original.  The paper builds upon existing work on consensus and distributed optimization but offers a unique perspective by leveraging the structure of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

**Significance:** The results are significant as they provide a theoretical foundation for understanding the convergence behavior of Min-Sum Splitting, a widely used algorithm in distributed optimization. The demonstration of accelerated convergence rates makes the algorithm more attractive for large-scale applications. The connection to other accelerated methods suggests potential avenues for further research and the development of new algorithms. The work addresses a challenging problem in distributed optimization and contributes to the ongoing effort to design efficient and scalable algorithms for large-scale machine learning and data processing. The practical implications of the derived rates and the conditions for achieving them, however, are not fully explored. A discussion of the limitations and potential challenges of implementing the algorithm in real-world scenarios would enhance the significance of the findings.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical analysis of Min-Sum Splitting.
*   Demonstration of accelerated convergence rates.
*   Establishment of connections to other accelerated methods.
*   Well-supported results with a rigorous mathematical analysis.
*   Addresses a significant problem in distributed optimization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper is dense and requires significant background knowledge.
*   Proofs are relegated to supplementary material, hindering verification.
*   Limited discussion of practical implications and implementation challenges.
*   The scope is limited to quadratic objective functions.
*   The sheer number of references can be overwhelming.
