Summary
The paper studies the Min-Sum Splitting variant of message passing and applies it to average consensus. It shows that, with a specific parameterization (δ, Γ), the algorithm reduces to a linear message-passing scheme on directed edges, and—via an auxiliary reparametrization—to a 2-step linear process on nodes. The main theorem picks δ = 1 and Γ = γW for a symmetric W with W1 = 1 and tuning γ = 2/(1 + sqrt(1 − ρW^2)), and proves convergence to the average with an asymptotic rate ρK strictly smaller than ρW, yielding a square-root improvement over diffusive rates (O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori). The paper positions Min-Sum Splitting as a bridge between lifted Markov chains and multi-step (shift-register/Chebyshev) accelerations.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual connection between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted (nonreversible) chains, and multi-step/shift-register acceleration.
- Reduction from edge-based dynamics to an auxiliary node-based 2-step linear system is elegant and useful for analysis.
- Theorem 1 recovers known accelerated rates for consensus (Chebyshev/shift-register), giving a principled tuning of splitting parameters and a neat spectral characterization.
- The work clarifies when ordinary Min-Sum fails and how splitting can fix convergence for consensus.

Weaknesses (technical, methodological, and presentation issues)
- Novelty/significance: The acceleration achieved is essentially that of classical Chebyshev/shift-register methods; the main theorem reproduces known rates by mapping Min-Sum Splitting to a standard 2-step process K. Beyond establishing the equivalence, there is little new theory on rates or tuning beyond what is known in accelerated consensus.
- Practicality/tuning: The method requires global spectral information (ρW) to choose γ; there is no discussion of how to estimate it distributedly or robustly (adaptive/online tuning), which limits practical impact.
- Scope: Only synchronous, undirected, quadratic consensus is covered. No results for directed/asynchronous/ noisy/ time-varying graphs, or for constrained non-quadratic consensus where Min-Sum is often attractive.
- Missing empirical validation: No experiments comparing Min-Sum Splitting with MH gossip, optimal-weight gossip, Chebyshev filtering, Nesterov-accelerated consensus, or lifted-chain methods on standard topologies. This is a serious omission for NeurIPS.
- Finite-time guarantees: Emphasis is “asymptotic.” No clean, explicit finite-time bounds with constants; the norm bound in Theorem 1 depends on ‖(K − K∞)t‖ without turning it into a transparent explicit inequality in t, ε, and graph parameters.
- Ambiguity about W, Γ, and sparsity: Earlier the paper “assumes” Γ respects graph sparsity. Theorem 1 chooses Γ = γW; it should be stated explicitly that W must respect G (including self-loops), and what default W to use (e.g., Metropolis-Hastings) and the consequences for ρW.
- Incomplete discussion of computational/communication complexity: The edge-based scheme uses two scalars per directed edge; the auxiliary node-based update implies a 2-state node memory. It would help to state clearly the implementable node-based update (eliminating edge messages) and compare per-iteration costs to standard accelerated consensus.
- Numerous notational/formatting errors (see below) make the paper difficult to follow and raise concerns about care in the derivations.

Relation to prior work
- Please situate more precisely with Chebyshev/shift-register acceleration for consensus/gossip (e.g., Xiao & Boyd 2004; Cao-Spielman-Yeh; Ghadimi et al.; Olshevsky; Scaman et al.), and make the novelty claim modest: the rate is inherited, the contribution is the Min-Sum Splitting equivalence and analysis technique (auxiliary process).
- Compare to Consensus Propagation (Moallemi & Van Roy) and to Ruozzi & Tatikonda’s splitting more directly, beyond stating that ordinary Min-Sum may diverge.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide an implementable node-based update (from Algorithm 3) with exact per-iteration operations and communication, and compare complexity to Chebyshev-accelerated consensus and lifted-chain methods.
- Add experiments on cycles, grids, random geometric graphs, expanders; report convergence time vs D and ε; compare with MH, optimal W, Chebyshev/Nesterov gossip, and lifted random walks.
- Offer guidance for choosing W (default choice) and estimating ρW in a distributed manner (or provide a robust range of γ).
- Clean up all notation, references, and formatting; add a concise proof sketch of Propositions 1–2 and Theorem 1 in the main text.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually neat bridge between Min-Sum Splitting and known accelerated consensus methods.
- Auxiliary node-based reparametrization is technically useful and may generalize.
- Could spur further work on Min-Sum for other objectives.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty over established accelerated consensus/Chebyshev methods.
- No experiments; practical impact unclear.
- Numerous presentation errors; some statements are imprecise or potentially incorrect as written.
- Heavy reliance on global spectral knowledge without proposed remedies.

Recommendation
- Reject (borderline). With substantial revisions (clarity, correctness, experiments, practical guidance), this could be a solid theory paper, but in its current form the accumulation of technical/presentation flaws and limited incremental novelty do not meet NeurIPS standards.

Confidence
- High.
