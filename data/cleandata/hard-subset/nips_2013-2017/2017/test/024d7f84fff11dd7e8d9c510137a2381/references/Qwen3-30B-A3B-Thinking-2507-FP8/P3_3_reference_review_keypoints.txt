- Provides the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, filling a theoretical gap in distributed optimization.  
- Shows Min-Sum Splitting achieves subdiffusive O(D log(D/ε)) rates on cycles and tori, a square-root improvement over classical diffusive methods.  
- Introduces an elegant proof technique using an auxiliary process tracking evolution on nodes rather than directed edges.  
- Establishes novel connections between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step gradient methods, unifying two acceleration paradigms.  
- Offers rigorous spectral-based theoretical analysis with strong results (Theorem 1 matches shift-register methods).  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly organized from problem setup to analysis and literature connections.  
- Lacks empirical validation to demonstrate practical benefits and verify theoretical rates on real or synthetic graphs.  
- Analysis is limited to quadratic objectives (network averaging); extensions to general convex or non-quadratic cases are not addressed.  
- Does not clearly articulate improvements over prior Min-Sum work [27] or explicitly resolve the conjecture in [27].  
- Practical implementation issues are unaddressed, notably reliance on global information (eigenvalues of W) to set parameter γ.  
- Overall assessment: strong, substantial theoretical contribution highly relevant to distributed optimization and NIPS.  
- Recommendation: Accept — theoretical novelty and insights into acceleration mechanisms outweigh the lack of experiments.  
- Suggestion/future direction: include brief empirical demonstrations and explore generalization of the acceleration mechanism to broader distributed optimization problems.
