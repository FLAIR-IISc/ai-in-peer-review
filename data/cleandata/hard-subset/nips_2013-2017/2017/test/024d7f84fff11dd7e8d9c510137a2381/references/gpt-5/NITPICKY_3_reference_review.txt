Summary
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting (a reparametrized variant of min-sum / GaBP) for the consensus/averaging problem. The main idea is to show that, with a suitable choice of splitting parameters, the algorithm reduces to a linear process on directed edges, which can be tracked by an auxiliary two-step linear iteration on nodes. By selecting Γ = γW for a symmetric W with W1 = 1 and γ = 2/(1 + sqrt(1 − ρW^2)), the resulting auxiliary matrix K matches the shift-register/Chebyshev-accelerated consensus iteration, yielding a square-root improvement over diffusive rates: O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori. The authors claim (i) the first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, (ii) a conceptual connection between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step accelerated methods, and (iii) a unified proof technique via an auxiliary node-level process.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on: (a) Min-Sum/GaBP and convergence conditions (Moallemi & Van Roy; Ruozzi & Tatikonda); (b) consensus and accelerated consensus via lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods (Boyd et al., Cao-Spielman-Yeh, Ghadimi et al., Xiao-Boyd, Golub-Varga); (c) consensus propagation via BP (Moallemi & Van Roy) and its limitations on loopy graphs; and (d) recent optimal rates via Nesterov-type acceleration (Scaman et al.). The paper’s technical result essentially recovers the classical Chebyshev/shift-register rate by showing Min-Sum Splitting can emulate that iteration.

Strengths
- Conceptual bridge: a clear mapping from Min-Sum Splitting on edges to a two-step linear iteration on nodes. This auxiliary representation (Algorithm 3) is technically clean and could be useful more broadly.
- Theorem 1: a precise rate statement in terms of ρ(W), recovering the shift-register spectral bound ρK and the √-speedup over diffusive consensus. The generality with respect to graph topology follows from spectral arguments.
- Addresses a gap: to my knowledge, explicit convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus is not in the literature; this paper fills that niche.
- Discussion relates the directionality/memory aspects to lifting and multi-step acceleration, which may help unify perspectives across communities.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness/completeness:
- Algorithm 2 output appears incorrect. It currently reads xtv := bv + δ Σ Γ r̂ + δ Σ Γ R̂ with no denominator. By quadratic completion, the minimizer should be xtv = (bv + δ Σ Γ r̂)/(1 + δ Σ Γ R̂). This is consistent with Algorithm 3’s output xtv = rtv/Rtv and with Proposition 1. Please fix.
- Equation/label inconsistencies: K̂(δ,Γ) is defined in (4) but Algorithm 2 says “K̂(δ,Γ) defined in (5).” Several other numbering mismatches occur.
- Multiple typographical/math errors hinder reproducibility. Examples: “φv(z) := 12z − bvz” (missing square), a later “φv(z) := 1/2 ∑v∈V (z − bv)^2” (sum is nonsensical inside a per-node definition), scattered mismatched parentheses and indices, and an inserted “st Conference on NIPS 2017” line. These should be cleaned thoroughly.
- Positivity/convexity condition: Proposition 1 requires 1 + δ ΣΓ R̂t > 0 to ensure a strictly convex belief. With R̂0 = 0 and δ = 1 this holds at t = 0, but the recursion R̂s = (2 − 1/δ)1 + K̂R̂s−1 with potentially negative K̂ entries does not immediately guarantee positivity for all t. The paper should provide a proof or sufficient conditions ensuring this holds under the chosen parameters, or state the assumption explicitly in Theorem 1.
- K∞ in (6): the scaling (2−γ)n in the denominator of a block matrix of 11T terms is unusual. A derivation or reference would help confirm this is the correct limit projector for K.
- The main proofs are deferred to the supplement. For a theory-only paper, including at least the core steps that establish the equivalence (Proposition 2) and the spectral bound would be helpful.

Novelty/significance:
- The rate achieved is exactly that of classical shift-register/Chebyshev acceleration; the contribution is mainly a reinterpretation of that method as Min-Sum Splitting via a specific parameter choice and initialization. While this is interesting, it is incremental from a performance standpoint.
- Claims such as “first connection” to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods seem overstated. Prior works have connected GaBP/min-sum to linear solvers and Krylov/Chebyshev-type acceleration (e.g., Malioutov-Johnson-Willsky; classical GaBP-as-linear-solver viewpoints). The novelty here is the particular Splitting formulation and the precise auxiliary mapping; please nuance the claims.
- Practical limitations: the accelerated parameters depend on ρ(W), a global spectral quantity. The paper acknowledges this but offers no discussion of how to estimate ρ(W) distributedly, robustness to mis-specification, or adaptive schemes. This substantially limits applicability.

Clarity/presentation:
- The manuscript suffers from many formatting and citation issues (incomplete references, inconsistent numbering, missing figure). The heavy notation (two coupled edge/node processes) makes precision crucial; the current state impedes independent reproduction.
- No empirical results. Even a small set of experiments (cycles/grids/random geometric graphs) comparing (i) Min-Sum Splitting, (ii) Chebyshev/shift-register, (iii) Metropolis, and (iv) ADMM would substantiate finite-time behavior and constants, and verify the equivalence.

Scope/assumptions:
- Synchronous, undirected, symmetric W; no discussion of asynchrony, time-variation, directed graphs, or noise. 
- Only quadratic φv are treated; general convex consensus is left open.
- Complexity per iteration and message sizes vs. lifted Markov chains and standard accelerated consensus are not quantified.

Pros
- Clear, useful auxiliary node-level representation of Min-Sum Splitting.
- Rigorous spectral rate bound matching known accelerated consensus.
- Bridges perspectives between BP/Min-Sum and accelerated consensus.

Cons
- Numerous technical/typographical errors and equation inconsistencies.
- No experiments; proofs not in main text; positivity condition unresolved.
- Practical parameter selection requires global spectral information; no robustness analysis.
- Performance matches known methods; novelty is mainly interpretive.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The conceptual contribution is interesting, but the paper is not yet up to NeurIPS standards due to the accumulation of technical/formatting issues, lack of empirical validation, and limited novelty beyond reinterpreting known accelerated iterations. I encourage the authors to: fix all mathematical/numbering errors; include core proofs; add experiments; discuss distributed/robust parameter selection; and temper novelty claims with precise citations. With these improvements, the work could be a solid contribution.
