## NIPS 2017 Review: Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant contribution to the theory of distributed consensus algorithms, specifically concerning the Min-Sum Splitting framework. The authors establish convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting applied to the consensus problem, demonstrating a square-root improvement over classical diffusive methods and achieving rates competitive with more recent accelerated approaches. The technical analysis is rigorous, the connection to existing work is well-articulated, and the paper is generally well-written. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of achieving consensus in a distributed network, focusing on the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. Min-Sum is a fundamental message-passing technique for distributed optimization, but its convergence isn't guaranteed on general graphs.  Min-Sum Splitting, introduced in [34], offers a remedy by re-parameterizing the problem, effectively creating a modified graph representation. This work provides the *first* theoretical analysis of Min-Sum Splitting’s convergence rate, specifically for the consensus problem. 

The authors carefully situate their work within the broader landscape of distributed consensus algorithms. They contrast their approach with classical diffusive methods ([42, 41]) and acknowledge recent advances in accelerated methods – both lifted Markov chains ([37]) and accelerated first-order methods ([13]). Critically, they demonstrate connections between Min-Sum Splitting and *both* of these existing lines of research, highlighting the algorithm's versatility and theoretical depth. The referencing is thorough and appropriate, covering key works in the field. 

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting is a genuinely novel contribution. This fills a significant gap in the understanding of this algorithm.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The analysis is mathematically sound, employing a clever auxiliary process to track the algorithm's evolution and relate it to spectral properties of relevant matrices. The proofs (detailed in the supplementary material) are likely to be robust.
* **Significant Results:** The proven square-root improvement in convergence rate over diffusive methods is a substantial advance.  Achieving rates competitive with Nesterov-acceleration-based algorithms is a particularly noteworthy achievement.
* **Connections to Existing Work:**  The insightful links drawn between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods are valuable. This contextualization elevates the work beyond a purely technical result.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and the contribution.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Density:** The paper is mathematically demanding. While the core ideas are clearly presented, a reader unfamiliar with the specific tools of spectral graph theory and message-passing algorithms might struggle to follow all the details.  A slightly more intuitive explanation of the auxiliary process in Section 6 could be beneficial.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper focuses on theoretical convergence rates. While these are important, a discussion of the practical implications of the tuning parameters (δ and Γ) and their sensitivity to graph structure would be valuable.  How robust is the acceleration in practice, and are there guidelines for choosing these parameters in real-world scenarios?
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:**  The paper lacks empirical validation. While theoretical guarantees are strong, demonstrating the improved performance of Min-Sum Splitting on synthetic or real-world graphs would strengthen the findings.
* **Slightly Repetitive:**  Sections 3 and 4 contain substantial background material. While useful for context, some of this information feels repetitive given the target audience of NIPS.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 5, Proposition 1:** The condition “1 + δ ∑ w∈N (v) ΓwvR̂ t wv > 0” seems critical for guaranteeing the output equivalence. Is there a practical way to ensure this condition holds, or could it lead to instability in certain graph configurations?
* **Section 7, Theorem 1:** While the asymptotic rate is presented, a clearer illustration of how this rate translates into actual convergence time (number of iterations) for different graph topologies (e.g., cycles, tori) would be helpful.
* **The choice of γ:** The connection between γ and the spectral radius ρ(W) is crucial. Is there a way to characterize the optimal γ for graphs where determining ρ(W) is computationally expensive?
* **Future Work:** The authors mention generalizing the acceleration due to splitting to other problems. Specific examples of problems where this approach might be beneficial would be welcome.

**5. Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Significant theoretical contribution with strong results.
* Novel approach to accelerating distributed consensus.
* Well-connected to existing literature.
* Rigorous mathematical analysis.

**Con:**

*  High technical density may limit accessibility.
*  Lack of empirical evaluation.
*  Some sections are slightly repetitive.




**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should consider:

* Adding a brief discussion of practical considerations related to parameter tuning.
* Including a small-scale empirical evaluation to demonstrate the algorithm's performance.
* Streamlining Sections 3 and 4 to reduce redundancy.
* Providing a slightly more intuitive explanation of the auxiliary process in Section 6.



This paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of distributed optimization and has the potential to inspire further research in this area. The strengths of the work significantly outweigh the weaknesses.
