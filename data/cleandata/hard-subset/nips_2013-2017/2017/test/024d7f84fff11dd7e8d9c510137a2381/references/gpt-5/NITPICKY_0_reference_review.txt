Summary
This paper studies Min-Sum Splitting (a reparametrized min-sum message-passing scheme) for the equality-constrained consensus/averaging problem. The authors: (i) specialize Min-Sum Splitting to quadratic consensus, (ii) show the resulting messages remain quadratic and reduce to a linear update on directed edges (Algorithm 2), (iii) introduce an auxiliary node-based linear multi-step system (Algorithm 3) that tracks the edge-based process, and (iv) prove an accelerated convergence rate (Theorem 1) by selecting splitting parameters that recover the shift-register/Chebyshev-type acceleration, yielding O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori. They highlight conceptual links between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted non-reversible Markov chains, and multi-step accelerated methods.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on Min-Sum Splitting [Ruozzi & Tatikonda], prior negative/partial results for min-sum on consensus [Moallemi & Van Roy], accelerated consensus via multi-step methods (shift-register, heavy-ball, Nesterov), and lifted chains. The claimed novelty is the first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, plus explicit connections to both lifted chains and multi-step methods.

Strengths
- Attempts the first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus.
- Bridges two threads (lifting and acceleration) via a message-passing interpretation; the auxiliary node-based linear system is insightful.
- The rate statement (Theorem 1) matches known shift-register acceleration and near-optimal O(D log(D/ε)) on geometric graphs.
- Clear high-level motivation about directionality/memory mechanisms.

Major weaknesses (technical correctness and completeness)
1) Algorithmic inconsistencies and likely errors:
- Algorithm 2 output appears incorrect: “xtv := bv + δ Σ Γ r̂t_wv + δ Σ Γ R̂t_wv” (no denominator). Proposition 2 and Algorithm 3 imply xtv should be a ratio, xtv = (bv + δ Σ Γ r̂t_wv) / (1 + δ Σ Γ R̂t_wv). As written it is dimensionally wrong and contradicts Proposition 2.
- Algorithm 2 references “K̂(δ,Γ) defined in (5)” while K̂ was defined in (4). There is also a clash with K(δ,Γ) defined later in (5).
- Indices in Algorithm 1/2 are frequently inconsistent (e.g., ξ̂swv vs ξ̂svw, φvw(·,z)/Γvw terms), making the updates ambiguous.
- Algorithm 3 updates (r,q) and (R,Q) with the same K, but q and Q never enter the output. Their role is unclear and appears superfluous as presented.

2) Conditions for correctness not established under the theorem’s initialization:
- Proposition 1 requires 1 + δ Σ Γ R̂t_wv > 0 for all t to equate Algorithm 1 and 2 outputs; the paper never proves this holds for the parameter choice of Theorem 1, especially with R̂0 = 0 and δ = 1. This is critical because it ensures the objective remains strongly convex along message updates and that the “ratio” output is well-defined. Theorem 1 uses R̂0 = r̂0 = 0 but does not justify the positivity condition over all iterations.

3) Missing specification of W and distributed implementability:
- Theorem 1 assumes a symmetric W with W1 = 1 and spectral radius ρ(W − 11T/n) known, then sets Γ = γ W with γ = 2/(1+√(1−ρW^2)). It is not explained how W is chosen in practice nor how γ is computed in a distributed way (requires global spectral information). This undermines the practical value of the claimed acceleration and should at least be discussed quantitatively (e.g., estimating ρW, robustness to misspecification).
- It is unclear whether using the “optimal” W from [Xiao & Boyd] (possibly with negative weights) is intended and how that interacts with Γ in Algorithm 1.

4) Proof sketch and constants:
- All proofs are deferred; however, the key nontrivial parts (edge-to-node reduction equivalence, positivity/convexity preservation, spectral bound constants like 4√2 n/(2−γ)) are critical to trustworthiness. The presented inequalities even include apparent typos (e.g., “12 √ 1/(1−ρW)” likely meant “(1/2)√(1/(1−ρW))”).

Clarity and presentation issues (numerous and significant)
- Many equations are malformed or contain typos; indices flip between (w,v) and (v,w); summation sets are ambiguous (incoming vs outgoing edges).
- Equation numbering mismatches ((4) vs (5)); references to definitions are incorrect.
- Footnotes and asides are interleaved into the main text (e.g., “st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)…” inserted mid-paragraph).
- Notation is overloaded: Γ is both a tuning matrix and tied to graph sparsity; diag(Γ1) introduced without careful motivation; 1 is used for vector and sometimes scalar.
- References are incomplete and inconsistent (missing years, venues; mixed numeric and author-year styles; unclear mapping of [numbers] to the bibliography block at the end).
- Several claims use imprecise language (e.g., “square-root improvement” without stating the precise regime and dependency assumptions).

Originality and significance
- The main rate matches known shift-register acceleration; novelty is the derivation that Min-Sum Splitting (with a specific parameterization) reproduces that dynamic. This conceptual unification is interesting but, in its current form, feels incremental without rigorous and clean derivations and without empirical support.
- The global-parameter requirement (needing ρW) limits practical impact unless addressed.
- No experiments or numerical illustrations are provided to validate the theory or demonstrate robustness.

Pros
- Addresses an active topic (accelerated consensus) with a message-passing lens.
- Potentially useful conceptual bridge between BP-type methods, lifting, and multi-step methods.
- If fixed and completed, the auxiliary process could be a reusable analysis tool.

Cons
- Multiple serious notation/equation errors (Algorithm 2 output, numbering, indices).
- Key technical condition for correctness (positivity of aggregate curvatures) is unproven under the main theorem’s initialization.
- Lack of clarity on how W and γ are chosen and implemented in a distributed setting.
- No experiments; proofs deferred; several rate constants and inequalities are suspect/typoed.
- Poor formatting and referencing quality hinders reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1) Please correct Algorithm 2 output and clarify the exact formulas for xt and the updates. Provide a complete, self-contained derivation from Algorithm 1 to Algorithm 2.
2) Under Theorem 1’s initialization (R̂0 = r̂0 = 0, δ = 1), can you prove 1 + δ Σ Γ R̂t_wv > 0 for all t (or at least that denominators never vanish)? If not, restrict parameters/initializations accordingly.
3) How should W be chosen in practice? What is the cost of estimating ρW and the sensitivity of performance to γ misspecification?
4) What are the per-iteration communication and computation costs vs. standard accelerated consensus?
5) Can you provide numerical experiments on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs to corroborate the theory?

Recommendation: Reject (in current form)
While the core idea is promising, the manuscript contains numerous technical and presentation flaws that preclude reliable evaluation. The equivalence claims hinge on conditions not established under the main theorem; critical equations are incorrect as written; and the exposition is hard to follow. With substantial revision—fixing algorithms/equations, proving required positivity, clarifying the role/choice of W and γ, and adding empirical validation—the work could become a solid contribution. My decision is driven by the accumulation of issues rather than the underlying idea, which I find interesting.
