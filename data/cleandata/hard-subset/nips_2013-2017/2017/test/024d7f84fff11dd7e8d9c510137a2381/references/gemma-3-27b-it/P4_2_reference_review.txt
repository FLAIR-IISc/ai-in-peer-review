This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving the consensus problem. The core contribution lies in establishing a square-root improvement in convergence speed compared to traditional diffusive methods, achieving a rate of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle and torus graphs, where D is the graph diameter and ε is the desired accuracy. The authors connect Min-Sum splitting to both lifted Markov chain techniques and accelerated first-order methods, providing a unifying perspective. The analysis hinges on constructing an auxiliary linear process to track the algorithm’s evolution, relating convergence to the spectral gap of a related matrix.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, building upon existing literature on consensus algorithms and spectral graph theory. The introduction of an auxiliary process is a clever technique to simplify the analysis. The presentation of Theorem 1 and its implications are clear. However, the paper could be improved by providing more intuition behind the choice of the specific splitting parameter (γ) and its relation to the graph’s spectral properties. While the authors mention the connection to shift-register methods, a more detailed explanation of this connection would be beneficial. The claim of relating Min-Sum to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated methods is intriguing, but the specific mapping and differences could be made more explicit. The paper relies heavily on prior work in the analysis of consensus algorithms; establishing the novelty of the analytical techniques used is crucial.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, but dense. The extensive background material (sections 3 and 4) is necessary to contextualize the work, but could potentially be streamlined. The notation is consistent, but the multiple algorithms (1, 2, 3) and their associated variables require careful tracking.  The flow between different concepts—Min-Sum splitting, auxiliary process, spectral gap, and convergence rate—is logically sound, but could benefit from more transitional explanations. Defining more variables in equations would enhance readability.  The connection between the parameters of the splitting algorithm and their effect on the convergence rate is not always immediately apparent. 

**Originality:** The primary novelty of the paper lies in establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting and demonstrating an acceleration compared to diffusive methods. While the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm itself has been proposed previously [34], a theoretical analysis of its convergence rate, particularly the improved rate achieved by careful parameter tuning, is new. The connection to shift-register methods and accelerated gradient techniques also offers a novel perspective. However, the paper would benefit from explicitly contrasting its results with the conjecture in [27] regarding convergence on tori. A deeper discussion of how the proposed method differs from existing accelerated algorithms in terms of computational complexity and implementation would also enhance the originality argument. The use of an auxiliary process is a useful technique, but it's important to position it in the landscape of existing analysis techniques for consensus algorithms.

**Significance:** The results are significant because they provide a theoretical justification for using Min-Sum Splitting to accelerate consensus algorithms. The demonstrated improvement in convergence rate—from diffusive to subdiffusive—is important, especially for large-scale networks with high diameter. The connection to accelerated first-order methods and lifted Markov chains may open up new avenues for algorithm design and analysis. The findings are relevant to a wide range of applications, including sensor networks, distributed optimization, and machine learning.  However, the practical implications of the proposed method need to be discussed. For example, how does the computational cost of implementing Min-Sum Splitting compare to existing algorithms? Is the improved convergence rate worth the added complexity? Investigating these aspects would further strengthen the significance of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides theoretical guarantees for the convergence of Min-Sum Splitting in a challenging problem (consensus).
*   Demonstrates a convergence rate improvement over classical diffusive methods.
*   Establishes a connection between Min-Sum splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated gradient methods.
*   The auxiliary process provides a novel and insightful approach to analyze the algorithm’s behavior.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is dense and requires a strong background in consensus algorithms and spectral graph theory.
*   The explanation of the parameter tuning process could be more intuitive.
*   The practical implications and computational cost of the proposed method are not fully explored.
*   The relationship to prior work and existing conjectures could be more thoroughly discussed.



Overall, this is a strong technical paper with significant results. Addressing the weaknesses mentioned above would further enhance its quality and impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
