- Paper shows Min-Sum Splitting with proper tuning attains subdiffusive consensus rates O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori, improving over diffusive O(D² log(1/ε)).  
- Establishes first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and proves it converges for all graph topologies with appropriate parameters.  
- Reveals novel connections between Min-Sum schemes, lifted Markov chains (lifted space on directed edges), and multi-step accelerated optimization methods.  
- Introduces an auxiliary-process proof technique that tracks algorithm evolution on nodes instead of directed edges.  
- Demonstrates Min-Sum Splitting can match shift-register accelerated methods by tuning splitting parameters.  
- Strong theoretical contribution with rigorous proofs and the first rate guarantees for this algorithm.  
- Novel theoretical insight linking belief propagation-style algorithms to two distinct acceleration paradigms.  
- Clear, well-organized technical presentation; Algorithm 3 (auxiliary process) is elegant and effective for analysis.  
- Significant practical impact: square-root improvement over diffusive methods relevant to distributed ML and sensor networks.  
- Authors honestly note practical limitation that optimal parameters depend on global spectral information.  
- Lacks experimental validation to empirically confirm the theoretical convergence rates across topologies.  
- Optimal parameter tuning requires knowledge of graph spectral properties, which is challenging for large decentralized networks.  
- Analysis mainly assumes graphs with polynomial growth or finite doubling dimension; extension to arbitrary topologies is unclear.  
- Paper could provide more detailed comparisons to the latest accelerated consensus methods that claim optimal rates.  
- Technically sound and complete work, though absence of experiments slightly reduces the overall assessment.  
- Writing and notation are clear and consistent, with logical flow from problem to algorithm to analysis.  
- Highly original: first to link Min-Sum Splitting to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods and to give convergence rates.  
- Significant: provides theoretical and potential practical advances, with clear applications and inspiration for future research.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel convergence analysis, theoretical connections, square-root rate improvement, new proof technique, strong presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing experiments, reliance on global spectral knowledge, limited topology assumptions, lack of distributed estimation strategy.  
- Recommendation: accept — paper is a substantial theoretical contribution suitable for NIPS.  
- Suggested improvements: include empirical results on various graphs, discuss decentralized spectral estimation, compare to recent optimal-rate methods, extend analysis beyond polynomial-growth graphs, and clarify computational complexity versus other accelerated methods.
