This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors establish rates of convergence for any graph topology, showing a square-root improvement over diffusive methods and achieving rates competitive with Nesterov’s acceleration. The paper builds upon previous work on Min-Sum schemes [27] and relates the proposed approach to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods in convex optimization, providing a valuable connection between these different lines of research.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting is a novel contribution. Establishing a square-root improvement over diffusive methods is significant, particularly for graphs with geometry where diffusive methods perform poorly. The connection to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods is insightful and expands the understanding of Min-Sum schemes.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, deriving convergence rates and establishing the conditions under which the algorithm achieves acceleration. The introduction of the auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) appears to be a clever technique for simplifying the analysis.
* **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The paper carefully situates its contribution within the existing literature on consensus algorithms, referencing relevant work on diffusive methods, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated optimization techniques. It clearly explains how the proposed approach relates to and improves upon previous results, especially those in [27].
* **Well-Motivated Problem:** The consensus problem is a fundamental problem in distributed optimization and machine learning, making the investigation of improved algorithms highly relevant.
* **Detailed Exposition:** The paper is quite detailed in its presentation, laying out the background, problem formulation, and algorithmic details meticulously.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity and Readability:** The paper is dense and mathematically demanding. While the rigor is commendable, the sheer amount of notation and technical detail can make it challenging to follow the main ideas. The extensive background material, while valuable, could potentially be streamlined to improve readability.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses exclusively on theoretical analysis. While rigorous theoretical results are essential, the lack of empirical validation leaves the reader wondering how well the theoretical gains translate into practical performance.  Including some simulations, even on relatively simple graph topologies, would strengthen the paper considerably.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The optimal choice of splitting parameters (δ and Γ) depends on the spectral gap of the graph. Determining this spectral gap can be computationally expensive for large graphs, potentially offsetting some of the benefits of the accelerated algorithm. This practical consideration should be discussed.
* **Assumptions:**  The theoretical results rely on specific assumptions about the graph structure and the initial conditions. A discussion of the robustness of the results to violations of these assumptions would be beneficial.
* **Repetitive Structure:** The paper is somewhat repetitive in its presentation. The introduction and subsequent sections revisit similar concepts and arguments multiple times.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical result with the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in distributed consensus algorithms.
* The analysis is rigorous and well-supported by mathematical derivations.
* The connection to related areas of research (lifted Markov chains and accelerated optimization) is insightful and valuable.
* The problem addressed is important and relevant to a wide range of applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The paper is dense and challenging to read, potentially limiting its accessibility to a wider audience.
* The lack of empirical validation makes it difficult to assess the practical impact of the theoretical results.
* The parameter sensitivity and the computational cost of determining the optimal splitting parameters could limit the algorithm's applicability.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong theoretical contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The results are novel, significant, and rigorously established. However, the lack of empirical validation and the complexity of the presentation are significant weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should address the weaknesses by:

1. **Adding empirical results:** Conduct simulations on a variety of graph topologies to demonstrate the practical benefits of Min-Sum Splitting.
2. **Improving readability:** Streamline the presentation, reducing redundancy and clarifying the key concepts. Consider adding more intuitive explanations alongside the mathematical derivations.
3. **Discussing practical considerations:** Analyze the computational cost of determining the optimal splitting parameters and discuss the robustness of the results to violations of the assumptions.
4. **Consider a more focused presentation:** Perhaps simplifying the extensive connections to lifted Markov Chains and accelerated methods to streamline the focus.

Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. The potential benefits of this work are substantial, and I believe that a revised version could be a valuable contribution to NIPS.
