

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving the consensus problem, claiming to establish convergence rates that improve upon classical diffusive methods. The authors connect Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step accelerated optimization methods, which is an interesting conceptual contribution. However, the paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies and unclear formulations that undermine its credibility.

## Major Technical Issues

1. **Ambiguous notation and notation inconsistencies**: The paper uses inconsistent mathematical notation throughout. For example, "φv/δ" appears in Algorithm 1 without clarification of whether this represents a function or value. The paper states "For an arbitrary choice of strictly positive integer parameters, Algorithm 1 can be seen to correspond to the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm..." yet later claims "Algorithm 1 is defined for any real choice of parameters δ and Γ." This contradiction creates fundamental confusion about the algorithm's domain.

2. **Critical definition missing**: The paper refers to "splitting" as a key concept but fails to provide a clear definition of what constitutes a "split" in the context of the graph. The phrase "splitting the nodes of the original graph" is vague and not elaborated upon, making it difficult to understand how the algorithm actually operates.

3. **Mathematical errors in key equations**: In Theorem 1, the authors define γ = 2/(1 + √(1-ρ²W)), but ρW is defined as ρ(W - 11^T/n) < 1. The expression √(1-ρ²W) is mathematically valid since ρW < 1, but the paper should explicitly state this to avoid confusion. More critically, the matrix K∞ is defined as "(2-γ)n (11^T 11^T (1-γ)11^T (1-γ)11^T)", which is an unusual and confusing matrix notation that should be clarified.

4. **Unjustified claims**: The paper claims "the convergence time is O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles and tori" but doesn't provide the derivation for this specific case, merely referencing [33, 1]. For a paper claiming to establish convergence rates, this is a significant omission.

## Formatting and Presentation Issues

1. **Inconsistent terminology**: The paper alternates between "Min-Sum" and "Min-Sum" without clear justification, and inconsistently uses "Min-Sum Splitting" versus "Min-Sum scheme."

2. **Typos and formatting errors**: The paper contains multiple mathematical typos, such as "φv(z) := 12z^2−bvz" (should be (1/2)z^2 - bvz), "φv(z) := 12 ∑ v∈V (z − bv)^2" (should be (1/2)∑(z-bv)^2), and "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems" (should be "The 31st Conference" or simply "NIPS 2017").

3. **Incomplete proofs**: The paper states that proofs are in the supplementary material but fails to provide the necessary details in the main text for key claims. For example, the proof that "the output of Algorithm 2 coincides with the output of Algorithm 1" is not provided for real δ values.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper identifies an interesting connection between Min-Sum Splitting and accelerated methods, the numerous technical inconsistencies, ambiguous formulations, and mathematical errors prevent it from being a solid scientific contribution. The paper's claim of establishing convergence rates is undermined by the lack of clear definitions, inconsistent notation, and missing derivations. 

The paper's main contribution - connecting Min-Sum Splitting to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods - is potentially valuable, but the execution is marred by the aforementioned issues. The authors fail to clearly define critical concepts, provide sufficient mathematical rigor, or properly justify their claims.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection due to multiple technical inconsistencies and unclear formulations that undermine the paper's core claims. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to clarify definitions, fix notation, and provide complete mathematical derivations. As it stands, the paper fails to meet the technical standards expected of NIPS publications.

The authors should address the following before resubmission:
1. Provide a clear, consistent definition of "splitting"
2. Resolve the contradiction between integer and real parameter choices
3. Fix all mathematical notation inconsistencies and typos
4. Provide complete derivations for key claims in the main text
5. Clarify the definition of K∞ and other ambiguous matrix expressions

The paper has potential but currently lacks the technical rigor required for NIPS.
