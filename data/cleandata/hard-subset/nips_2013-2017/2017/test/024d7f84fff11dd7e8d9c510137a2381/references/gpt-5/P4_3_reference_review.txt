Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the Min-Sum Splitting variant of belief propagation (BP) for distributed consensus/averaging. It makes three main contributions: (i) it specializes Min-Sum Splitting to the consensus problem and shows that, with quadratic objectives, the resulting message updates on directed edges become linear; (ii) it introduces an auxiliary node-based linear process with memory whose evolution exactly tracks the BP estimates, enabling spectral analysis; (iii) it proves that with a specific tuning of the splitting parameters, the method achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence rates that match those of shift-register/multi-step consensus schemes.

The work connects three lines of literature: Min-Sum/BP and its “splitting” reparameterization [34, 35], accelerated consensus via multi-step first-order methods (e.g., heavy-ball, Chebyshev, shift registers) [14, 24, 41, 45], and accelerated mixing via lifted, non-reversible Markov chains [5, 8, 18, 37]. Prior work [27] showed ordinary Min-Sum does not converge on loopy graphs for consensus and proposed a soft-constraint variant with rates characterized via a 2m-dimensional edge process for regular graphs, with explicit results for cycles and conjectures for tori. This paper instead provides convergence guarantees and rates for Min-Sum Splitting on arbitrary graphs, and shows a square-root improvement vs diffusive schemes, obtaining O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori (D is the diameter), getting close to the O(D log(1/ε)) rates of recent Nesterov-type methods [30, 36].

Technical content and quality
- Formalization: The paper presents Min-Sum Splitting (Algorithm 1) with parameters δ and Γ, and for quadratic consensus it derives a linear edge-based iteration (Algorithm 2) with parameters (R̂, r̂), and an auxiliary 2n-dimensional node-based linear iteration (Algorithm 3) with state (r, q) and (R, Q). Propositions 1–2 establish the quadratic invariance and the exact correspondence between Algorithms 2 and 3.
- Main theorem: Theorem 1 fixes δ=1, Γ=γW for a symmetric W with W1=1 and ρW:=ρ(W−11T/n)<1, and chooses γ=2/(1+√(1−ρW^2)). It proves convergence to the average and an asymptotic rate governed by ρK=ρ(K−K∞)=√((1−√(1−ρW^2))/(1+√(1−ρW^2)))<ρW, i.e., a Chebyshev/shift-register acceleration yielding 1/(1−ρK)≈√(1/(1−ρW)). This recovers the square-root speedup familiar from second-order/shift-register methods [15, 24, 45].
- Soundness: The derivations for Algorithms 2–3 and the spectral argument for Theorem 1 are consistent with known analyses of second-order consensus and Chebyshev semi-iterative methods [15, 24, 45]. The reliance on symmetric W and global γ calibrated by ρW is standard for accelerated consensus [13, 24]. The positivity condition for the quadratic coefficients in Proposition 1 is noted and appears satisfied under the chosen initialization (R̂0=r̂0=0) and δ=1, though an explicit statement verifying positivity for all t would improve completeness.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally well-organized: background on consensus and accelerated methods, specialization of Min-Sum Splitting, the auxiliary process, and the main theorem. The exposition of the edge-to-node reduction (Proposition 2) is a key clarity contribution. There are minor typos/formatting issues (e.g., stray footnote markers, truncated header text), and a few definitions could be streamlined (e.g., K̂(δ,Γ) in (4) could be compacted using incidence matrices). The relationship to existing accelerated methods is explained clearly.

Originality
- The analysis appears to be the first to establish accelerated convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, and to formally connect BP splitting parameters to both lifted Markov chain directionality and multi-step momentum/shift-register dynamics.
- The auxiliary node-based representation enabling a spectral analysis in terms of Γ (and ultimately W) is a novel and useful technique within the BP context.

Significance
- Conceptual: Establishes a substantive bridge between BP/Splitting and the two major acceleration paradigms (lifting and multi-step methods). This can influence how message passing is designed for other distributed optimization problems.
- Practical: The resulting rates are competitive with best-known accelerated consensus schemes, though the need for global spectral information to set γ matches the status quo for accelerated consensus.
- Scope: The results are currently limited to quadratic consensus; generalization to broader convex objectives remains open.

Strengths
- Clear, rigorous reduction from edge-based BP to a node-based linear system with memory (Propositions 1–2).
- A clean spectral proof of acceleration (Theorem 1) with explicit parameter choice, matching known optimal second-order rates.
- Broad applicability across graph topologies, with explicit implications for cycles and tori (O(D log(D/ε))).
- Insightful conceptual connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Global parameter dependence: γ requires knowledge (or accurate estimation) of ρW, which is not locally available and is nontrivial to estimate in a fully decentralized way.
- Restricted problem class: Results cover only quadratic objectives (averaging). Extension to general convex φv or constrained consensus is not provided.
- Synchrony and stability: Assumes synchronous updates and exact arithmetic; robustness to asynchrony, delays, noise, or time-varying graphs is not analyzed.
- Implementation overhead: While the edge-based BP interpretation is interesting, the auxiliary node-based form is essentially a standard shift-register scheme; practical benefit over directly using known accelerated consensus may be limited without additional advantages (e.g., flexibility to more general objectives).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a brief proof or lemma ensuring the positivity condition in Proposition 1 under the chosen parameters and initialization.
- Discuss practical strategies to set or adapt γ in a distributed manner (e.g., local spectral bounds, Chebyshev polynomials based on degree/diameter proxies, or adaptive tuning).
- Include numerical experiments on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs to empirically validate the predicted O(D log(D/ε)) behavior and compare to [27], Metropolis-Hastings, and shift-register baselines.
- Clarify communication and computation costs per iteration relative to standard node-based second-order methods, to make the trade-offs explicit.
- Outline a path (or counterexamples) for extending the analysis beyond quadratic objectives, and comment on potential connections to ADMM-based lifting [12] in this context.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and rigorous analysis linking Min-Sum Splitting to accelerated consensus with provable square-root speedup.
- Conceptually unifies BP, lifting, and multi-step methods; likely to be of interest to the NeurIPS community.
- General graph applicability with clear rate statements.

Arguments against acceptance
- Dependence on global spectral information without a distributed selection mechanism for γ.
- Limited to quadratic consensus; lack of empirical validation and robustness analysis.
- Practical advantage over existing accelerated consensus methods may be primarily conceptual rather than algorithmic.

Overall, the paper provides a solid theoretical contribution with clear novelty and significance for the study of message-passing and distributed optimization.
