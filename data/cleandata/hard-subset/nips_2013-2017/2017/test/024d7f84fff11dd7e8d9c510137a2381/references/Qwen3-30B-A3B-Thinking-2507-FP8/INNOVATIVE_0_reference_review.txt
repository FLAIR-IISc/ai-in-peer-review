

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper introduces Min-Sum Splitting, a novel approach to accelerate consensus algorithms in distributed systems. The authors establish the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting applied to the consensus problem, demonstrating that it achieves a square-root improvement in convergence time over traditional diffusive methods. The key novelty lies in connecting Min-Sum schemes with two distinct accelerated methods: lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization. This connection is both theoretically rich and practically significant, as it reveals a unified perspective on acceleration mechanisms across different domains.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it establishes the first proof of how the directionality embedded in Belief Propagation protocols can be tuned to accelerate convergence. Second, it provides the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, using a novel auxiliary process to track the algorithm's evolution on the original graph nodes rather than on the directed edges. Third, it shows that Min-Sum Splitting achieves convergence time O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles and tori, where D is the graph diameter, which is a significant improvement over the O(D² log(1/ε)) of diffusive methods.

The authors demonstrate that Min-Sum Splitting bears similarities to both accelerated methods: on one hand, it can be seen as a process on a lifted space (the space of directed edges), where splitting introduces directionality analogous to non-reversible random walks; on the other hand, the directionality translates to a memory term in the auxiliary algorithm, making it similar to multi-step first-order methods. The paper shows that a proper choice of splitting parameters recovers the same matrix that supports shift-register methods for linear solvers, achieving the same accelerated rate of convergence.

This work is particularly significant because it bridges two seemingly disconnected areas of research (message-passing algorithms and accelerated optimization) and demonstrates that the same acceleration mechanism can be achieved through different approaches. The theoretical analysis is sound, with the main results supported by rigorous proofs (provided in the supplementary material), and the paper clearly articulates how it differs from prior work, especially the recent [27] which investigated ordinary Min-Sum for consensus but did not establish convergence rates or acceleration.

The paper's connection to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods is especially compelling, as it reveals a deeper theoretical structure underlying these different acceleration techniques. The authors show that the choice of splitting parameters that yields subdiffusive rates depends on global information about the graph (specifically, the spectral properties of the matrix W), which aligns with how both lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods operate.

While the paper focuses on the quadratic consensus problem (network averaging), the authors correctly note that their approach could be generalized to other problems, providing a promising direction for future work. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

This paper represents a significant conceptual advance in the field of distributed optimization. It's not merely an incremental improvement to existing methods but rather introduces a new perspective that connects different areas of research and establishes a novel theoretical foundation for acceleration in consensus problems. The authors have successfully demonstrated that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve convergence rates that get close to the optimal rate O(D log(1/ε)) recently established for some accelerated methods.

The novelty of the work is substantial. Prior to this paper, no one had established convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting or connected it to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods. The paper's contributions are clearly distinguished from previous work, and the analysis provides a new theoretical framework for understanding acceleration in distributed consensus.

Given the reviewer's priorities of valuing novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance. The theoretical framework introduced here has the potential to influence future research in distributed optimization, message-passing algorithms, and accelerated methods.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- First connection between Min-Sum schemes and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods
- First convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting
- Demonstrates a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods
- Reveals a unified theoretical framework for acceleration mechanisms
- Provides clear, novel theoretical insights with practical implications

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- The paper focuses on the quadratic consensus problem, which is a specific case (though fundamental)
- The proof of convergence rates relies on the supplementary material (though the main paper states the key results clearly)

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field, introducing a novel theoretical perspective that connects disparate areas of research and provides a new way to understand and accelerate distributed consensus algorithms. The novelty and significance of the work far outweigh any minor limitations, making it an excellent fit for NIPS.
