This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, a generalization of the standard Min-Sum message-passing protocol, for solving the network averaging consensus problem. The authors demonstrate that while the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge on graphs with cycles, Min-Sum Splitting can be tuned to not only converge but also achieve an accelerated rate. The main contribution is a formal analysis establishing that a specific parametrization of Min-Sum Splitting matches the dynamics and accelerated convergence rate of shift-register methods, a known technique from numerical analysis and multi-step optimization. This yields a square-root improvement over standard "diffusive" consensus algorithms on graphs with geometric structure, such as cycles and tori. The analysis relies on a novel and elegant proof technique, where the edge-based message-passing updates are tracked by an equivalent, simpler auxiliary linear process on the nodes. The paper also provides a valuable conceptual contribution by connecting the "splitting" parameters in a belief propagation framework to the momentum/memory terms in accelerated first-order methods.

### Strengths

*   **Novel Conceptual Connection:** The paper draws a compelling and previously unestablished link between Min-Sum Splitting, a message-passing algorithm from the graphical models community, and the well-studied field of accelerated first-order methods for convex optimization. Showing that the splitting parameters can be tuned to mimic the behavior of shift-register methods is a significant insight.
*   **First Convergence Rate Analysis for Min-Sum Splitting:** To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a formal analysis of the convergence *rate* of Min-Sum Splitting. This transforms the algorithm from a heuristic with empirical evidence of improved convergence [35] into a principled method with theoretical guarantees for an important class of problems.
*   **Elegant Proof Technique:** The introduction of the auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) is a key methodological contribution. It cleverly transforms the analysis of a complex 2m x 2m linear system on the edges into a much more tractable 2n x 2n system on the nodes, which has a known structure that can be analyzed spectrally.
*   **Significant Result:** The paper demonstrates that a Min-Sum-based protocol can achieve accelerated "subdiffusive" convergence rates for consensus, scaling as `O(D log(D/ε))` on tori. This makes the method competitive with other state-of-the-art accelerated consensus algorithms and is a substantial improvement over the `O(D^2)` scaling of standard methods.

### Weaknesses

Despite the strong core ideas, the manuscript is marred by a significant number of minor errors, notational inconsistencies, and technical gaps. This lack of meticulousness undermines the quality of the presentation and the rigor of the claims.

**1. Presentation and Formatting Issues:**
*   A leftover template artifact is present in the first page's footnote: "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)...". This is a sign of carelessness.
*   In Algorithm 2, the reference to the definition of `K̂(δ,Γ)` is incorrect. It states it is defined in (5), but it is actually defined in (4).
*   The `l_2` norm is written with a backtick (` `2 norm`) in the introduction, which is improper typesetting.
*   The asymptotic behavior in Section 7 is described with an informal dot notation (`.`), e.g., `‖xt − b̄1‖/ √ n . 4 √ 2ρtK/(2− γ)`. Standard `O()` or `Θ()` notation would be more precise.

**2. Notational and Definitional Imprecision:**
*   Footnote 2 defines `φv(z)` for the least-squares case as `1/2 ∑v∈V (z − bv)^2`. This is incorrect; the summation should not be inside the definition of the function for a single node `v`. It should be `φv(z) := 1/2 (z − bv)^2`.
*   In Algorithm 1, the update for `µ̂swv` uses the ambiguous notation `φvw( · , z)`. It is unclear what the first argument is or which variable is being minimized over. A more precise formulation, such as `min_y { ... }`, is required for clarity.
*   Similarly, the definition of the output `xtv` in Algorithm 1 uses `µtv(z)`, but `µtv` is defined as a sum of functions without specifying its argument. It should likely be `µtv(z) = φv(z) + δ ∑w Γwvµ̂twv(z)`.

**3. Technical Gaps and Unclear Statements:**
*   **Positivity of Quadratic Terms:** Proposition 1 relies on the fact that the quadratic messages remain well-defined, which requires the quadratic coefficients `R̂svw` and the final denominators `Rtv = 1 + δ ∑w ΓwvR̂twv` to be positive. The paper states this as a condition (`If 1 + δ ... > 0`) but never proves that it holds for the choice of parameters in Theorem 1. This is a critical technical gap, as a non-positive denominator would make the output undefined.
*   **Assumptions on W:** Theorem 1 relies on a matrix `W`. The properties of `W` (e.g., connected, non-bipartite, which ensure `ρW < 1`) are not explicitly stated as prerequisites. Furthermore, the error bound contains a `(2-γ)` term in the denominator. Since `γ → 2` as `ρW → 1`, this bound blows up. The behavior in this limit case should be discussed.
*   **Citation of Prior Work [27]:** The paper claims the convergence time in [27] for cycle graphs is `O(D/ε log 1/ε)`. A linear dependence on `1/ε` is highly atypical for linear systems and suggests a misunderstanding or misstatement of the result in [27]. A correct comparison to prior work is essential, and this error calls into question the authors' diligence.
*   **Derivation of Convergence Time:** The final convergence time is stated as `O(D log(D/ε))`. The standard analysis yields `O(1/(1-ρK) log(1/ε))`, which for tori is `O(D log(1/ε))`. The origin of the extra `log D` factor is not explained and seems to appear without justification from the stated bounds.

### Summary of recommendation

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Presents a novel and important connection between Min-Sum Splitting and accelerated optimization.
2.  Provides the first, to my knowledge, rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting.
3.  The use of an auxiliary process is an elegant and powerful analysis technique.
4.  The main result—achieving accelerated consensus—is significant and of interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The paper suffers from a large number of minor but distracting presentation errors (typos, incorrect references, formatting issues).
2.  Several key notations and algorithm steps are defined imprecisely, hindering reproducibility and clarity.
3.  There are significant technical gaps, most notably the lack of a proof for the positivity of the quadratic terms, which is essential for the algorithm's validity.
4.  A key result from prior work [27] appears to be cited incorrectly, undermining confidence in the paper's positioning within the literature.

While the core ideas of this paper are strong and potentially of high impact, the execution falls short of the standards expected for NIPS. The sheer volume of minor flaws, combined with several non-trivial technical omissions, makes the paper difficult to endorse in its current form. The work is promising, but it requires a thorough revision to address the issues of clarity, precision, and technical rigor. Therefore, I recommend rejection.
