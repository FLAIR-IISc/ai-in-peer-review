

# Peer Review: "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

## Summary

This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving the consensus problem (specifically the network averaging problem) in distributed systems. The authors demonstrate that by properly tuning the splitting parameters, the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm can achieve subdiffusive convergence rates (O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori), which represents a square-root improvement over the diffusive rates (Θ(D² log(1/ε))) of classical consensus algorithms. The key contributions include: (1) establishing the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting; (2) introducing a novel proof technique using an auxiliary process to track the algorithm's evolution on nodes rather than directed edges; (3) connecting Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step accelerated methods in convex optimization.

The paper builds on prior work by [27], which investigated Min-Sum for consensus but only showed convergence for certain graph structures with suboptimal rates. The authors demonstrate that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve rates matching shift-register methods, and they establish how the directionality embedded in Min-Sum protocols can be tuned for acceleration.

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound theoretical analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for the quadratic consensus problem. The mathematical derivations are rigorous and the proof technique using the auxiliary process (Algorithm 3) is novel and well-justified. Theorem 1 provides a clear convergence rate analysis with appropriate bounds.

However, there are several quality concerns:

1. **Limited scope of analysis**: The paper focuses exclusively on quadratic objective functions (network averaging), which is a specific case of the consensus problem. The authors do not address how their results would generalize to more general convex objective functions, which is a significant limitation for a paper claiming to advance distributed optimization methods.

2. **Lack of empirical validation**: The paper provides no experimental results to validate the theoretical claims. For a NeurIPS paper, this is a notable omission, as it's difficult to assess the practical relevance of the theoretical improvements without empirical evidence, even on small-scale graphs.

3. **Global parameter dependency**: The optimal parameter choice in Theorem 1 (γ = 2/(1 + √(1-ρW²))) requires knowledge of ρW, which is a global property of the graph. For large-scale distributed systems, computing ρW is impractical, which significantly limits the algorithm's applicability.

4. **Connection to other methods**: While the paper mentions connections to lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods, these connections are superficial and not deeply explored. The paper could have provided more insight into how these connections lead to new algorithmic insights beyond the specific convergence rate results.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, and the key concepts (Min-Sum Splitting, consensus problem, auxiliary process) are properly explained.

The proof technique using the auxiliary process is particularly well-explained, and the transition from the edge-based formulation (Algorithm 2) to the node-based formulation (Algorithm 3) is clearly justified. The paper also provides good context by comparing to previous work, particularly [27].

Some sections could be improved for clarity:
- The intuition behind the parameter choice in Theorem 1 is not sufficiently explained. The paper states that γ depends on global properties of the graph, but doesn't provide much intuition for why this choice leads to the improved convergence rate.
- The connection to shift-register methods (mentioned as "the same matrix that support the evolution of shift-register methods") could be more explicitly developed.

## Originality

The paper makes several original contributions:

1. It establishes the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, which is a significant gap in the literature (as noted by the authors).

2. The auxiliary process proof technique (Algorithm 3) is novel and represents a new way to analyze Min-Sum-based algorithms.

3. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two different approaches to accelerated consensus (lifted Markov chains and multi-step accelerated methods) is original and valuable.

4. The specific convergence rate results (O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori) represent a clear theoretical improvement over previous work.

The paper effectively distinguishes its contributions from prior work, particularly [27], by providing convergence rates rather than just convergence guarantees.

## Significance

The consensus problem is fundamental to distributed systems, with applications in sensor networks, distributed machine learning, and multi-agent systems. The square-root improvement in convergence rate (from Θ(D²) to Θ(D)) is significant for large-scale distributed systems, especially for graphs with geometry (like sensor networks). This represents a meaningful advance in the theoretical understanding of distributed consensus algorithms.

However, the significance is somewhat limited by:
- The focus on quadratic objectives rather than general convex problems
- The lack of empirical validation to demonstrate practical impact
- The global parameter dependency that may limit practical applicability

The connection to lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods provides theoretical insights that could potentially lead to new algorithmic developments, but the paper doesn't fully explore these possibilities.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of Min-Sum-based algorithms for distributed consensus. The convergence rate analysis and auxiliary process proof technique are novel and well-executed. The connection to lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods provides interesting theoretical insights.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses that prevent it from being a strong candidate for NeurIPS:

1. The exclusive focus on quadratic objectives limits the general applicability of the results.
2. The complete absence of empirical results is a major drawback for a NeurIPS paper.
3. The global parameter dependency (requiring knowledge of ρW) may severely limit practical implementation.

## Recommendation

**Weak Reject**

While the theoretical contribution is sound and the paper makes an important step in understanding Min-Sum Splitting, the limitations in scope (only quadratic objectives) and the lack of empirical validation make the paper less impactful for a NeurIPS audience. A stronger paper would have included empirical results to validate the theoretical claims and explored extensions to more general convex problems.

## Strengths
- First convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting
- Novel auxiliary process proof technique
- Clear theoretical improvement over previous work (O(D log(D/ε)) vs. Θ(D² log(1/ε)))
- Valuable connection between Min-Sum and two different lines of accelerated consensus research

## Weaknesses
- Limited to quadratic objective functions (network averaging)
- No empirical results to validate theoretical claims
- Global parameter dependency (requires knowledge of ρW) limits practical applicability
- Superficial exploration of connections to lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods
- Doesn't address practical implementation challenges (e.g., communication noise, asynchronous updates)

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Extend the analysis to more general convex objective functions, or at least discuss the challenges in doing so.
2. Include empirical results on small-scale graphs to validate the theoretical convergence rates.
3. Explore how to compute the required parameters (like ρW) in a distributed manner, or provide alternative parameter choices that don't require global knowledge.
4. Deepen the connection to lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods, showing how these insights can lead to new algorithmic developments.
5. Discuss practical implementation considerations for large-scale distributed systems.

## Final Comments

The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to the understanding of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus, but the narrow scope and lack of empirical validation significantly reduce its impact for NeurIPS. With the suggested improvements, particularly adding empirical results and expanding the analysis to more general problems, this could become a strong NeurIPS paper. As it stands, the paper would be better suited for a more theoretically focused venue like SODA or a more specialized journal in distributed systems.
