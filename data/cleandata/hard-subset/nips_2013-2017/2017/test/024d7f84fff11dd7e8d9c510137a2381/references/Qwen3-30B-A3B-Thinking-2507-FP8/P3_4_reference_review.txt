

# Peer Review: "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

## Summary
This paper investigates the convergence properties of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for the consensus problem (network averaging) in distributed optimization. The authors establish that Min-Sum Splitting, when properly parameterized, achieves a square-root improvement in convergence rate over classical diffusive methods, scaling as O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori (where D is the graph diameter), compared to the standard O(D² log(1/ε)) rate. The key contribution is establishing the first theoretical convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting and demonstrating its connection to two distinct acceleration techniques: lifted Markov chains (via directionality in message passing) and multi-step accelerated methods (via memory terms). The paper builds upon prior work [27, 34, 35] but provides the first rate analysis for this algorithm.

## Strengths

**Quality & Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting. The main result (Theorem 1) is well-supported with a clean proof technique using an auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) that tracks the algorithm's behavior on the original graph nodes rather than on the directed edges. This approach elegantly connects the convergence to the spectral properties of the underlying graph. The authors correctly acknowledge that parameter tuning requires global information (spectral gap), which is consistent with prior work on accelerated methods.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by establishing the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains (via the directionality introduced by splitting) and multi-step methods (via the memory term) is novel and insightful. This bridges two previously separate lines of research in distributed optimization, as the authors correctly note.

**Significance:** The convergence rate improvement is substantial for networks with geometry (cycle graphs, tori), which are common in applications like sensor networks. The result shows Min-Sum Splitting can achieve rates approaching the theoretical lower bound O(D log(1/ε)), which is a significant advancement over diffusive methods. This provides a theoretically grounded alternative to existing accelerated consensus algorithms.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The key technical concepts (Min-Sum Splitting, auxiliary process) are introduced carefully with precise mathematical formulations. The connection to prior work is well-articulated, and the paper's contributions are clearly stated.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Scope:** The analysis is restricted to the quadratic consensus problem (network averaging), which is a specific case of the general consensus problem. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not explore how their results might extend to non-quadratic objective functions, which would significantly broaden the applicability of their results.

**Practical Parameter Tuning:** The parameter choice (γ = 2/(1+√(1-ρ_W²))) requires knowledge of the spectral gap ρ_W of a graph matrix, which is a global property. In distributed settings where nodes have limited global information, this could be challenging to implement without additional communication overhead. The paper does not address how this limitation might be overcome in practice.

**Lack of Experimental Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical with no empirical validation of the convergence rates. While theoretical analysis is appropriate for NIPS, including even a small set of experiments would strengthen the claims and provide intuition for the practical behavior of the algorithm.

**Superficial Connection to Multi-Step Methods:** While the paper shows Min-Sum Splitting can be written in the same form as shift-register methods, the connection is primarily structural rather than functional. A deeper comparison of practical performance and implementation complexity would have strengthened this aspect of the contribution.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to distributed optimization by establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting and connecting it to two different acceleration techniques. The result is significant for the consensus problem, particularly for graphs with geometry. However, the limited scope to quadratic objectives and lack of experimental validation are notable weaknesses.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in distributed optimization. It represents a clear advancement over prior work on Min-Sum for consensus [27] and provides new theoretical insights. The connection to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods is novel and potentially impactful for the broader distributed optimization community.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the following: (1) Explicitly state the limitations of the quadratic assumption in the conclusion, (2) Add a brief discussion of how global parameter tuning might be implemented in practice, and (3) Include a small set of experiments (even synthetic ones) to validate the theoretical convergence rates.

The paper advances the state of the art in distributed optimization and offers valuable theoretical insights that will likely stimulate further research in this area. The results are particularly relevant to applications in sensor networks, distributed machine learning, and multi-agent systems where consensus is a fundamental primitive.
