

# Peer Review for "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for distributed consensus problems, establishing convergence rates and demonstrating how proper parameter tuning can achieve accelerated convergence compared to traditional diffusive methods. The authors show that Min-Sum Splitting can be viewed as a process on a lifted space (the directed edges of the graph), while simultaneously being equivalent to a multi-step method on the original graph nodes. This dual interpretation connects Min-Sum Splitting to two major lines of research in accelerated consensus: lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods. The main theoretical contribution is Theorem 1, which establishes that a proper choice of splitting parameters yields a convergence rate improvement of roughly a square root over diffusive methods, achieving O(D log(D/ε)) convergence for cycle graphs and tori.

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere by building upon the Min-Sum message-passing framework introduced in [28, 34, 35] and connecting it to accelerated methods for consensus that have been studied in [7, 27, 37]. The authors explicitly compare their work to [27], which investigated Min-Sum for consensus but did not establish convergence rates for general graph topologies or achieve accelerated rates.

## Evaluation

### Quality

The paper is technically sound with a rigorous theoretical analysis. The main result (Theorem 1) is well-supported by a detailed proof technique that introduces an auxiliary linear process on the nodes of the graph to track the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. The authors carefully establish connections between their method and existing approaches, and they acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., the need for global information about the graph structure to choose the optimal parameters).

The paper makes a significant contribution by being the first to establish convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, which has previously been observed to converge in settings where ordinary Min-Sum fails [35]. The proof technique using the auxiliary linear process is particularly novel and well-executed.

*Weakness:* The paper does not provide experimental results to validate the theoretical findings. While the focus is on theoretical analysis, including even a small set of simulations on simple graphs would strengthen the claims about convergence rates.

### Clarity

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with a clear flow from problem statement to theoretical development to results. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the authors carefully define all terms and concepts before using them. The connections to previous work are well-explained, and the main contributions are clearly summarized.

*Minor weakness:* The paper assumes familiarity with message-passing algorithms and graph theory. While this is appropriate for a NIPS audience, some readers might benefit from a brief glossary of key terms or a more detailed explanation of the Min-Sum algorithm in the introduction.

### Originality

The paper makes a significant original contribution by establishing the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains techniques (by viewing the algorithm as a process on directed edges) and multi-step first-order methods (by showing the algorithm's equivalence to shift-register methods) is novel and insightful.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, particularly [27], which investigated Min-Sum for consensus but did not establish convergence rates for general graph topologies or achieve accelerated rates. The authors also note that their results connect to a recent line of work on distributed ADMM [12], suggesting further research directions.

*Originality strength:* The dual interpretation of Min-Sum Splitting as both a lifted process and a multi-step method represents a significant conceptual advance in understanding message-passing algorithms for consensus.

### Significance

The results are highly significant for the field of distributed optimization. The square-root improvement in convergence rates over diffusive methods is a substantial theoretical advance, particularly for graphs with geometry (like cycles and tori) where diffusive methods are known to be suboptimal. The paper demonstrates that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve convergence rates close to the optimal O(D log(1/ε)) bound recently established for some accelerated algorithms [30, 36].

The connection to multiple areas (lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods) broadens the paper's impact and suggests potential applications beyond consensus problems. The authors' observation that Min-Sum Splitting can recover the same matrix as shift-register methods used in numerical analysis for linear solvers [45, 4, 24] is particularly compelling.

*Significance strength:* The paper provides a unified theoretical framework that connects disparate lines of research in distributed optimization, potentially inspiring new methods that combine the best features of both lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization.

## Pros and Cons for Acceptance

### Pros
- First theoretical analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting
- Establishes a square-root improvement over diffusive methods for consensus
- Makes a novel connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two major lines of accelerated consensus research
- Introduces a new proof technique using an auxiliary linear process on the graph nodes
- Clearly demonstrates how Min-Sum Splitting can be tuned to achieve accelerated convergence
- Well-organized and well-written with careful comparison to prior work

### Cons
- Lacks experimental validation of the theoretical results
- The choice of optimal parameters depends on global graph information (e.g., spectral gap), which may be impractical in some distributed settings
- The paper focuses on quadratic objective functions (network averaging), leaving open the question of applicability to more general problems
- The connection to distributed ADMM [12] is mentioned but not deeply explored

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of distributed consensus algorithms. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear, novel contribution by establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting and connecting it to two major lines of research in accelerated consensus. While the lack of experimental validation is a minor drawback, the theoretical results are compelling and significant for the field.

The paper addresses an important problem in distributed optimization with a novel theoretical approach that has clear implications for practical applications. The results represent a meaningful step toward achieving optimal convergence rates for distributed consensus algorithms.

## Recommendations

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contribution is significant, well-executed, and well-positioned within the literature. The paper makes a clear, novel contribution to our understanding of message-passing algorithms for distributed optimization.

To strengthen the paper, I would recommend including a small set of numerical experiments to validate the theoretical convergence rates on simple graph topologies (e.g., cycle graphs and 2D tori). This would help bridge the gap between theory and practice and provide readers with a more concrete understanding of the algorithm's performance.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a significant theoretical contribution to a problem of broad interest in distributed optimization and machine learning. The connections to both Markov chain theory and optimization methods are particularly relevant to the NIPS community.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Consider adding a brief discussion of the practical challenges in implementing the parameter choice (γ = 2/(1 + √(1-ρ²_W))), particularly for large-scale networks where computing ρ_W may be difficult.

2. The paper mentions "the convergence time is O(D log(D/ε)" in Section 7 but does not explicitly state the constant factors. A brief discussion of how these constants compare to other methods would be helpful.

3. In the introduction, the paper states that "the known sufficient conditions do not apply in this case" for constrained problems. It might be helpful to briefly clarify why the standard conditions for Min-Sum don't apply to constrained problems (even if it's not the focus of the paper).

4. Consider adding a brief comparison to the "Extra" algorithm [36] or "D-ADMM" [38], which have also achieved near-optimal rates for distributed optimization, to better position the contribution.

This is an excellent paper that makes a clear, significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
