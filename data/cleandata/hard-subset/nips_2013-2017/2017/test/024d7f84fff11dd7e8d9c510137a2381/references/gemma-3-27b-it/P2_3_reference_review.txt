## Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors demonstrate that, with a proper parameter tuning, Min-Sum Splitting achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence rates compared to classical diffusive methods. Specifically, they establish a square-root improvement in convergence time for cycle and tori graphs, achieving rates close to the optimal bounds recently established for algorithms based on Nesterov’s acceleration. A key contribution is the introduction of an auxiliary linear process to track the algorithm’s evolution, simplifying the analysis and relating Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting is novel. While the algorithm itself was introduced previously, this work provides the first theoretical guarantees and rate analysis.
* **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis establishing a square-root speedup over classical diffusive methods. The connection to shift-register methods and Nesterov’s acceleration is a strong result.
* **Technical Ingenuity:** The introduction of the auxiliary process is a clever technique that simplifies the analysis significantly. It allows relating the complex Min-Sum splitting dynamics to a more tractable linear system.
* **Connections to Existing Literature:** The paper effectively positions the work within the broader landscape of consensus algorithms, explicitly drawing connections to lifted Markov chains and accelerated optimization methods. The detailed literature review is helpful.
* **Significance:** Improved convergence rates for distributed consensus algorithms have practical implications for many applications in networked systems, distributed machine learning, and sensor networks.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The paper is dense and technically demanding. While the theoretical results are strong, they are presented with a high degree of mathematical formalism, making it challenging for readers not deeply familiar with the field to fully grasp the nuances.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses almost entirely on theoretical analysis. While the theoretical results are compelling, a lack of empirical validation (even on simple graph structures) weakens the practical impact. Demonstrating the acceleration in practice, even in a limited setting, would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Parameter Tuning Dependence:** The achieved acceleration is contingent on a specific parameter tuning (δ and Γ). The sensitivity of the performance to deviations from this optimal tuning is not discussed. A discussion on robustness would be valuable.
* **Overly Detailed Introduction:** The introduction is quite lengthy and contains a very detailed literature review. While comprehensive, it could be streamlined to focus more directly on the specific contribution of the paper.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by mathematical analysis. The proofs, while not included in the main text, are stated to be available in the supplementary material, which is a positive aspect.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the density of mathematical notation and the complexity of the concepts make it challenging to follow. More intuitive explanations and examples would improve clarity.
* **Originality:** The work is original in its analysis of Min-Sum Splitting and its connections to other acceleration techniques.
* **Significance:** The potential impact of achieving faster consensus rates is significant, particularly for large-scale distributed systems.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical guarantees of accelerated convergence.
* Novel connection between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated optimization.
* Clever use of an auxiliary process to simplify the analysis.
* Addresses a relevant and important problem in distributed systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of empirical validation.
* High technical complexity may limit accessibility.
* Potential sensitivity of performance to parameter tuning is not discussed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of Min-Sum Splitting and its potential for accelerated consensus. While the lack of empirical validation and the high technical complexity are weaknesses, the novelty and strength of the theoretical results outweigh these concerns.  I believe the paper is of sufficient quality and significance for acceptance to NIPS.  

**Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend that the authors consider adding a small-scale empirical evaluation to demonstrate the practical benefits of their approach. Additionally, a discussion of the robustness of the performance to deviations from the optimal parameter tuning would be beneficial. Streamlining the introduction could also improve clarity.
