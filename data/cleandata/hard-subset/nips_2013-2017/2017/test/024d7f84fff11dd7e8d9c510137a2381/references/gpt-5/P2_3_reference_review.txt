Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for distributed optimization and establishes the first convergence-rate analysis of this algorithm on the consensus (network averaging) problem. The authors show: (i) a specific parametrization of Min-Sum Splitting yields a linear message-passing process on directed edges; (ii) by constructing an auxiliary node-based linear process with memory, they can track the algorithm’s evolution and analyze its rate; (iii) with a proper choice of splitting parameters, the method enjoys a square-root improvement in asymptotic convergence time over diffusive consensus (O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori), approaching the O(D log(1/ε)) optimal rates known for Nesterov-type accelerations.

The work connects three lines of research: belief propagation/min-sum on graphs (including splitting/reparametrization [Ruozzi & Tatikonda]), accelerated consensus via lifted non-reversible Markov chains [Diaconis-Holmes-Neal; Chen-Lovász-Pak; Jung-Shah-Shin], and multi-step/shift-register accelerations in distributed optimization [Golub-Varga; Xiao & Boyd; Ghadimi-Shames-Johansson]. Prior work showed ordinary Min-Sum can fail on loopy graphs and analyzed a softened Min-Sum variant in special cases [Moallemi & Van Roy; Moallemi & Van Roy (consensus); Ruozzi & Tatikonda], but did not provide general convergence-rate guarantees for Min-Sum Splitting. This paper fills that gap for quadratic consensus.

Technical content and main claims:
- The authors specialize Min-Sum Splitting to consensus with quadratic local objectives and equality constraints. Messages remain quadratic; their parameters evolve linearly on directed edges (Algorithm 2).
- They construct an auxiliary node-based linear “multi-step” scheme (Algorithm 3) described by a 2n×2n matrix K(δ,Γ), and prove equivalence in outputs (Proposition 2).
- For δ=1 and Γ=γW, where W is any symmetric averaging matrix (W1=1) and γ=2/(1+√(1−ρW^2)), they show the algorithm converges to the global mean with rate controlled by the second eigenvalue magnitude of a shift-register matrix K = [[γW, I]; [(1−γ)I, 0]]. The spectral radius ρK = √((1−√(1−ρW^2))/(1+√(1−ρW^2))) leads to the well-known square-root improvement: 1/(1−ρK) ≈ √(1/(1−ρW)).
- Hence, for graphs with geometry, the convergence time becomes O(D log(D/ε)), improving over diffusive O(D^2 log(1/ε)). The result holds for any graph topology, given W and its spectral gap.

Quality:
- The analysis is technically sound and well grounded in known spectral results for second-order (Chebyshev/shift-register) iterations. The equivalence between the edge-based Min-Sum Splitting dynamics and the node-based multi-step auxiliary system is a neat reduction that makes the rate analysis tractable.
- Claims are precise and supported by theorems. Proofs are said to be in the supplement; from the main text, the steps and dependencies are plausible and consistent with the literature.
- One limitation: no empirical validation is provided (finite-time behavior, constants, robustness), which is unusual for NeurIPS but not disqualifying for a strongly theoretical contribution.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized and reads clearly for experts. The step from Algorithm 2 to Algorithm 3 and the role of parameters δ, Γ are explained. The connection to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods is articulated and helpful conceptually.
- Notation is heavy; a concrete running example (e.g., a cycle) or a small illustrative figure for the auxiliary process would improve readability. Minor typos and cross-references (e.g., missing figure in the excerpt) should be checked.

Originality:
- Novel contributions include: first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting; the auxiliary node-based tracking method; and an explicit bridge showing that appropriately tuned Min-Sum Splitting recovers the same accelerated matrix as shift-register consensus.
- While the asymptotic rate matches known shift-register accelerations, deriving it via Min-Sum Splitting and exposing the “directionality” mechanism in BP as akin to non-reversibility/momentum is new and insightful.

Significance:
- The results clarify when and how Min-Sum Splitting can be accelerated, connecting message-passing inference and accelerated optimization/gossip. This is likely to influence both the theoretical understanding of BP-like algorithms and the design of distributed optimization methods.
- Practical impact is constrained by the need for global information (γ depends on ρW) and synchronous operation; nonetheless, this is a common limitation of accelerated consensus, and the authors acknowledge it.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical advance: first convergence-rate guarantees for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus.
- Elegant auxiliary-process technique reducing edge dynamics to a 2n-dimensional node process.
- Conceptual unification: links splitting/BP directionality, lifted non-reversible chains, and multi-step momentum.
- Square-root acceleration on broad graph classes; general graph topology coverage.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Dependence on global spectral information: How can γ (and potentially W) be chosen or estimated in a decentralized manner? Robustness to γ-mismatch?
- Scope restricted to quadratic consensus. Can the auxiliary-process approach extend to general strongly convex objectives or constrained problems beyond averaging?
- No experiments. Even small-scale simulations comparing to Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, ADMM-based consensus, and lifted gossip would strengthen the paper.
- Assumptions: synchronous updates, fixed graph, symmetric W (possibly with negative weights). How sensitive is the method to asynchrony, time-varying topology, or quantization?
- Computational/communication overhead: Algorithm 2 operates on 2m edge messages; Algorithm 3 requires two states per node (like shift-register). A discussion of per-iteration cost and memory vs. baselines would help.

Suggestions:
- Add a brief decentralized estimation procedure for ρW (or a bound-based choice of γ) and discuss performance under uncertainty.
- Provide numerical experiments on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs illustrating O(D log(D/ε)) scaling and constants.
- Clarify the role of δ; since the key theorem sets δ=1, explain more directly how “directionality” arises via Γ, and whether δ≠1 offers benefits.

Overall evaluation:
- Quality: high (theoretical)
- Clarity: good for experts; could benefit from examples/experiments
- Originality: solid
- Significance: moderate to high (conceptual bridge + first rate guarantees)

Pros for acceptance:
- First rigorous rate analysis of Min-Sum Splitting with compelling connections and improved rates.
- General, graph-agnostic theoretical results.

Cons against acceptance:
- No empirical validation; practical deployment issues (global parameter knowledge) not resolved.
- Restricted to quadratic consensus.

Verdict: I lean toward acceptance for the strong theoretical contribution and insightful connections, contingent on adding basic experiments and an expanded discussion on parameter selection and robustness.
