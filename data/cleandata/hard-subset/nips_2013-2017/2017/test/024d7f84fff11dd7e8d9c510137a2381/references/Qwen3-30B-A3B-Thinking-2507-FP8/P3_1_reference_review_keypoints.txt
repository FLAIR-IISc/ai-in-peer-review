- Paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for distributed consensus (network averaging) and derives convergence rates.  
- Claims accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence with properly tuned splitting parameters versus classical diffusive methods.  
- Main theoretical result: Min-Sum Splitting can achieve improved scaling (claimed O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles/tori), a square-root improvement over Θ(D² log(1/ε)).  
- Conceptual contribution 1: first connection established between Min-Sum schemes, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step first-order methods.  
- Conceptual contribution 2: introduces an auxiliary linear process on nodes to analyze convergence instead of directed-edge dynamics.  
- Builds on prior work in consensus, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods, uniquely bridging those lines.  
- Strength: significant theoretical novelty by establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting.  
- Strength: elegant and potentially broadly useful proof technique (auxiliary node-process).  
- Strength: clarifies conceptual connections between disparate accelerated-consensus approaches.  
- Strength: clear technical presentation with careful propositions and theorems.  
- Weakness: no experimental validation—entirely theoretical with no simulations or empirical comparisons.  
- Weakness: limited scope—analysis restricted to the quadratic (network averaging) case and specific parameter choices.  
- Weakness: practical implementation issue—parameter γ = 2/(1+√(1−ρ_W²)) needs global spectral knowledge (ρ_W), not addressed.  
- Weakness: “square-root improvement” claim is misleading—logarithmic ε-dependence means time scaling isn’t a literal sqrt improvement in D.  
- Weakness: insufficient comparison with recent accelerated consensus methods (e.g., Scaman et al. NIPS 2017).  
- Technical concern: Theorem 1 is misinterpreted—bound is O(1/(1−ρ_K) log(1/ε)) which for cycles gives O(D log(1/ε)), not O(D log(D/ε)).  
- Technical concern: chosen parameters δ=1 and Γ=γW rely on ρ_W, requiring global computations and undermining distributed applicability.  
- Technical concern: unclear and underexplained connection to shift-register methods; needs detailed comparison to specific works.  
- Technical concern: strong assumption of quadratic objectives limits applicability; extension to non-quadratic cases is not demonstrated.  
- Overall assessment: valuable and novel theoretical contribution, especially the proof technique and conceptual links.  
- Overall limitation: practical impact limited by lack of experiments and global-knowledge parameter requirements.  
- Recommendation: weak accept—novel theory but needs empirical validation and practical fixes.  
- Revision suggestions: add experiments (cycles/tori comparisons), clarify the square-root claim, address distributed estimation of ρ_W or alternative parameters, expand comparisons with recent work (e.g., Scaman et al.), and discuss extensions beyond quadratic problems.
