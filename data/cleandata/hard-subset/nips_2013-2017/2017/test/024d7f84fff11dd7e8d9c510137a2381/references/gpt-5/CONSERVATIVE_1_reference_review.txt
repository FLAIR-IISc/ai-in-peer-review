Summary
The paper studies the Min-Sum Splitting variant of belief propagation and applies it to the consensus (network averaging) problem. The authors (i) show that, for quadratic consensus, Min-Sum Splitting induces linear message updates on directed edges and, via a reparametrization, an auxiliary linear two-step recursion on nodes; (ii) choose splitting parameters so that the auxiliary recursion matches a shift-register (Chebyshev-type) accelerated consensus method; and (iii) derive asymptotic convergence rates that improve over diffusive methods by a square-root factor, yielding O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori. They emphasize conceptual links to lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods, and claim this is the first analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus and a neat auxiliary node-level recursion that exposes the algorithm’s structure and simplifies spectral analysis.
- Theoretical convergence guarantee (Theorem 1) with explicit spectral-radius characterization that recovers classic shift-register/Chebyshev acceleration rates.
- Conceptual unification: the work connects Min-Sum Splitting to both nonreversible (lifted) Markov chains and multi-step momentum methods, which is insightful.
- The results hold for general graphs (not only regular), and the derivations are consistent with well-known behavior on cycles/tori.

Weaknesses
- Practicality: The accelerated regime requires choosing Γ = γW with γ a function of ρ(W − 11ᵀ/n), i.e., global spectral information that is not locally available. No method is given for distributed or robust estimation/tuning, nor is adaptivity discussed. This substantially limits real-world applicability.
- Novelty/significance: The main rate result essentially re-derives the well-known shift-register/Chebyshev acceleration by showing equivalence via the auxiliary recursion. Thus, while the connection is interesting, the achieved rate is not new and is not optimal compared to existing O(D log(1/ε)) methods known for certain graph families.
- Validation: There are no experiments or numerical illustrations. Given the emphasis on acceleration and nontrivial constants, even small-scale simulations comparing Min-Sum Splitting to Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, EXTRA/ADMM, and prior Min-Sum variants would be important to substantiate claims and clarify constants/transients.
- Assumptions/technical gaps: The specialization to consensus relies on quadratic forms and on initializations (R̂⁰ = r̂⁰ = 0). Proposition 1 requires positivity conditions (e.g., 1 + δ Σ Γ R̂ᵗ > 0) for the outputs to coincide, but these are not verified in the main text. It remains unclear whether Γ may contain negative weights (as in optimal W) without compromising convexity or stability of the underlying Min-Sum updates, since Algorithm 1 divides by Γvw in φvw/Γvw. The paper does not discuss robustness to asynchrony, delays, or time-varying graphs.
- Scope/positioning: The work targets only consensus, whereas much of the paper’s motivation is about broader optimization via Min-Sum. Claims about generalization are left as future work. The relationship to recent accelerated decentralized optimization (e.g., EXTRA, gradient tracking, optimal algorithms of Scaman et al.) is not quantitatively compared.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide a simple, distributed procedure (even approximate) to tune γ without global spectral knowledge, and discuss its impact on the rate?
2. Under your specialization to consensus, are negative Γvw allowed? If yes, how do you avoid nonconvexity issues arising from φvw/Γvw in Algorithm 1? If no, what are the implications when the best W has negative weights?
3. Can you verify the required positivity condition in Proposition 1 for your initialization and parameter choice?
4. How do the constants and transients compare empirically against Chebyshev acceleration, EXTRA, and ADMM on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs?
5. What is the per-iteration communication/memory cost versus standard accelerated consensus? Is there any advantage of implementing acceleration via Min-Sum Splitting rather than directly using node-based two-step methods?

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Theoretical development appears sound and aligns with known spectral analyses for two-step methods. However, key assumptions (global parameter tuning, positivity conditions) are not fully justified in the main text, and there is no empirical corroboration.
- Clarity: Generally well-organized, though notation is heavy and some conditions are deferred to the supplement. A clearer statement of assumptions (signs of Γ, initialization, convexity conditions) would help.
- Originality: The auxiliary-process viewpoint for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus is interesting and, to my knowledge, new. However, the resulting rate matches classical Chebyshev/shift-register results, limiting novelty of the performance claim.
- Significance: The conceptual connection may spur future work, but as presented, the practical impact is limited: the method recovers known accelerated behavior, requires global tuning, and lacks experiments. The contribution feels more incremental than transformative.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a clean analytical tool (auxiliary node process) that may be reusable.
- Clarifies the relationship between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted chains, and multi-step acceleration.
- Provides the first explicit rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus.

Cons for acceptance
- Accelerated rate is not new; it mirrors classic shift-register acceleration.
- Strong reliance on global spectral information; no adaptive/tunable variant provided.
- No empirical results; practical benefits and constants remain unclear.
- Technical conditions (e.g., positivity, sign of Γ) insufficiently addressed in the main text.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper offers a nice conceptual bridge and a technically competent analysis, but the performance guarantees largely rephrase known accelerated consensus behavior, rely on unrealistic global tuning, and are unvalidated experimentally. Strengthening with clear assumptions, a discussion of Γ’s feasibility, adaptive tuning, and numerical comparisons would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
