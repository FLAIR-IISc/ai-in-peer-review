Summary
This paper analyzes the Min-Sum Splitting variant of message passing for the consensus/averaging problem. The authors (i) show that, with a suitable parametrization, Min-Sum Splitting induces a linear message-passing process on directed edges; (ii) introduce an auxiliary node-based, two-step linear system that exactly tracks the Min-Sum evolution and is easier to analyze; and (iii) prove accelerated convergence rates by tuning the splitting parameters using spectral information of a symmetric weight matrix W. The main theorem shows a square-root improvement in asymptotic rate over classical diffusive methods, yielding O(D log(D/ε)) iterations on cycles and tori, approaching the O(D log(1/ε)) best-known rates achieved by accelerated first-order methods. The paper also draws conceptual connections between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted non-reversible Markov chains, and multi-step (shift-register/heavy-ball) methods.

Relation to prior work: Ordinary Min-Sum (Gaussian BP) often fails on loopy graphs; sufficient conditions exist but are restrictive. Ruozzi–Tatikonda introduced splitting, and empirical/partial convergence results exist. For consensus, previous Min-Sum variants with soft constraints [Moallemi–Van Roy] led to diffusive behavior and analysis via edge-space dynamics; rates were characterized only in special topologies. Accelerated gossip via Nesterov/Chebyshev and lifted chains is well-studied, but those lines have not been tied to Min-Sum. This work provides the first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus and a clean bridge to both acceleration paradigms.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual contribution: The auxiliary node-based process that exactly tracks edge-based Min-Sum Splitting is elegant and, to my knowledge, new. It unlocks a spectral analysis that recovers shift-register convergence factors from a BP-like algorithm. The paper clearly situates Min-Sum Splitting at the intersection of lifted chains and multi-step optimization.
- Theoretical significance: Theorem 1 gives explicit spectral-rate bounds and exhibits a square-root improvement 1/(1−ρK) ≍ √(1/(1−ρW)). For geometric graphs, this yields O(D log(D/ε)), substantially better than diffusive O(D^2 log(1/ε)). The result is topology-agnostic once W is fixed and uses standard norms, making it comparable to existing literature.
- Clarity of the main idea: The transition from a 2m-dimensional edge process to a 2n-dimensional node process, and the identification of the K matrix matching shift-register methods, makes the acceleration mechanism transparent.
- Relevance: Consensus/averaging is a core primitive in distributed optimization and ML systems. Connecting BP-style methods to accelerated consensus broadens the algorithmic toolbox.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Scope: Results are restricted to the quadratic averaging case (unconstrained, synchronous, undirected graphs, symmetric W). The paper motivates broader impact but does not provide extensions or partial results beyond quadratics.
- Parameter selection requires global information: The accelerated choice Γ=γW with γ depending on ρ(W) requires knowledge (or a sharp bound) on the spectral gap. This mirrors known trade-offs for lifted chains and multi-step methods, but it limits plug-and-play deployability. No adaptive/distributed tuning strategy is provided.
- Practical overhead and comparisons: There are no experiments. It would help to quantify per-iteration communication and memory costs (edge-based messages vs node-based two-step state), and to compare wall-clock convergence with Chebyshev/Nesterov, lifted gossip, and ADMM-based consensus on standard graphs.
- Minor tension in narrative: The theorem’s optimal setting uses δ=1, Γ=γW; yet earlier the text emphasizes the role of both δ and Γ and “directionality” induced by splitting. It would help to clarify where the directionality arises when δ=1 (i.e., it is the reparameterization via Γ, not δ, that matters here).
- Robustness: No discussion of asynchrony, time-varying graphs, noise, or quantization. BP-style methods can be sensitive to such factors; even a brief discussion would improve the paper.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The reduction to the auxiliary process and spectral analysis appear correct and leverage well-known results on second-order methods. Assumptions are clearly stated (symmetric W, ρ(W−11^T/n)<1, initialization R̂0=r̂0=0). The proof outlines are deferred, which is acceptable given space, but the main intuitions are conveyed. The lack of empirical validation is a gap but does not undermine the theoretical claims.

Clarity
Well organized, with a thorough related work section that fairly acknowledges prior lines of research. However, notation is heavy; the definitions of K̂(δ,Γ) and K(δ,Γ) could benefit from a brief worked example (e.g., a path graph) to aid intuition. There are some formatting artifacts (e.g., “st Conference…”, footnote placement). Explicitly restating the constraints on Γ (sparsity matching G) and the positivity condition for Algorithm 2’s output would help reproducibility.

Originality
High. This is the first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus and the first explicit equivalence to a node-based multi-step process yielding accelerated rates. The cross-pollination between BP, lifted chains, and multi-step methods is fresh and likely to inspire follow-ups.

Significance
Moderate to high. Consensus remains central in decentralized ML/optimization. Providing an accelerated BP-like method, with a clean spectral rationale, is valuable. While confined to quadratics here, the techniques (auxiliary process, parametrization) could extend to other convex objectives and message-passing schemes.

Questions for the authors
- Can γ be tuned adaptively in a distributed way (e.g., via power iterations, Chebyshev bounds, or spectral proxy such as degree/diameter) without losing acceleration?
- How does the per-iteration communication/memory compare to Chebyshev-accelerated consensus? Is the edge-based Min-Sum form necessary, or can one implement directly the auxiliary node-based two-step update?
- Robustness: what happens under mild asynchrony or link failures? Does the auxiliary analysis extend?
- Beyond quadratics: can your auxiliary-process approach handle strongly convex smooth φv, or consensus with soft constraints/penalties?
- Directed or time-varying graphs: any obstacles to extending the analysis?

Minor comments/typos
- “st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems …” appears as a leftover; fix.
- Several footnotes are inline and disrupt flow; consider moving to appendix.
- Define all symbols on first use (e.g., 1, diag(·), norms).
- In Algorithm 2 output, check the sign and denominators in xtv expression for readability.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and elegant theoretical bridge yielding accelerated rates for Min-Sum Splitting.
- Clear spectral characterization; square-root improvement over diffusive consensus on geometric graphs.
- Potential to catalyze new BP-based accelerated methods beyond consensus.

Cons for acceptance
- No experiments; practicality and overheads remain unquantified.
- Requires global spectral information; no adaptive tuning.
- Limited to quadratic, synchronous, undirected settings.

Recommendation
Accept (7/10). Bold and conceptually new theory with clear acceleration, despite limited scope and lack of empirical validation.

Confidence
4/5.
