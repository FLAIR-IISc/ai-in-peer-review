This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors establish convergence and derive rates, showing a square-root improvement over diffusive methods, achieving a rate comparable to Nesterov-accelerated methods. The core contribution lies in demonstrating how proper tuning of splitting parameters leads to acceleration, connecting Min-Sum schemes to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous mathematical analysis supporting the claims. The introduction of an auxiliary process to track the algorithm's evolution is a clever technique that simplifies the analysis. The theoretical results appear complete and well-justified, although the proofs are relegated to supplementary material. The authors acknowledge limitations and relate their work to prior research. The analysis appears careful, but the density of the mathematical notation might hinder thorough verification without access to the proofs. The connection to shift-register methods and the resulting rate is a strong result. 

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but dense with mathematical notation. The organization is logical, building from background on Min-Sum and consensus to the main results. The motivations for the Min-Sum splitting algorithm, and the challenges of analyzing it, are clearly stated. However, understanding the nuances of the algorithm and its connection to lifted Markov chains and accelerated methods requires careful reading. The extensive background material, while necessary, could potentially be trimmed to improve flow.  Algorithm 2 and 3 are well-presented but could benefit from a more intuitive explanation alongside their formal definition.

**Originality:** The application of Min-Sum splitting to consensus, and the subsequent analysis of its convergence rate, represents a novel contribution.  While Min-Sum and consensus are well-studied areas, the specific analysis and acceleration achieved through splitting are new. The connection drawn between Min-Sum, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods offers a fresh perspective on these techniques.  The paper appropriately cites relevant work, demonstrating awareness of the existing literature. The authors effectively position their work relative to [27] and highlight the improvements they achieve.

**Significance:** The results are significant, offering a faster convergence rate for consensus problems, particularly on graphs with geometry. This has potential implications for distributed optimization, sensor networks, and machine learning applications. The connection to existing accelerated methods could inspire further research into combining these techniques. The insight that splitting parameters can be tuned to achieve acceleration is a valuable contribution that extends beyond the specific context of consensus. The theoretical results provide a foundation for understanding and improving Min-Sum-based algorithms in other domains. 

**Strengths:**
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis of Min-Sum Splitting convergence rates.
*   Establishment of a square-root speedup compared to diffusive methods.
*   Novel connection between Min-Sum, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods.
*   Clear identification of the role of splitting parameters in achieving acceleration.
*   Well-defined auxiliary algorithm for tracking the Min-Sum evolution.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The paper is dense with mathematical notation, potentially hindering accessibility.
*   The proofs are in the supplementary material, making thorough verification difficult.
*   The extensive background material might benefit from streamlining.
*   The practical implications of the results, beyond theoretical improvements, are not fully explored.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound analysis of a relevant algorithm.
*   The achieved convergence rate improvement is significant.
*   The connections drawn between different algorithmic techniques are insightful.
*   The work advances the state of the art in distributed consensus algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper's density and mathematical complexity might limit its impact.
*   The lack of empirical validation or discussion of practical considerations is a drawback.
*   While the connections to other algorithms are highlighted, a more in-depth exploration of their synergies could strengthen the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**  Strong Accept.  The paper's theoretical contributions are substantial, and the results are significant for the field. While the density of the presentation and lack of empirical validation are minor drawbacks, the novelty and rigor of the analysis outweigh these concerns. This paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS conference.
