**Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, a variant of the canonical Min-Sum message-passing protocol, when applied to the distributed consensus problem. The authors focus on the network averaging case, a fundamental primitive in distributed computing and machine learning. The paper correctly positions this work within the extensive literature on consensus, noting that classical methods based on reversible Markov chains (e.g., gossip algorithms) suffer from slow "diffusive" convergence on graphs with geometric structure, such as cycles and tori.

The authors connect their work to two primary families of accelerated consensus algorithms that have been developed to overcome this limitation: (1) lifted Markov chain methods, which use non-reversible dynamics on an expanded graph to speed up mixing, and (2) accelerated first-order optimization methods, which incorporate momentum or memory terms (e.g., Polyak's heavy ball, Nesterov's method, shift-registers). The core contribution of this paper is to demonstrate that Min-Sum Splitting, a general message-passing framework, can be understood through the lens of both approaches. By specializing the algorithm to the quadratic consensus objective, the authors show it becomes a linear process on the directed edges of the graph. More importantly, they introduce a novel "auxiliary message-passing scheme" that tracks the algorithm's output via a process on the graph's nodes. This auxiliary process reveals that, with a proper choice of the "splitting" parameters, the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm's dynamics are identical to those of accelerated shift-register methods. This connection allows the authors to provide the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, showing it achieves a square-root improvement over diffusive methods, matching the state-of-the-art asymptotic rates for this problem class.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength lies in the novel and significant conceptual bridge it builds between belief propagation-style algorithms and accelerated convex optimization. While Min-Sum is a well-known heuristic, its precise connection to momentum-based acceleration has not been formally established. By showing that the "splitting" reparameterization can be tuned to exactly recover the dynamics of an accelerated first-order method (shift-registers), this work provides a new and powerful perspective on how message-passing can achieve acceleration. This finding is likely to inspire further research into using reparameterization and splitting as a general principle for designing fast distributed algorithms.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical contributions are solid. The introduction of the auxiliary process (Algorithm 3) to analyze the edge-based dynamics of Min-Sum Splitting (Algorithm 2) is an elegant and effective proof technique. It simplifies the analysis from a system of size 2m (number of directed edges) to a system of size 2n (number of nodes), which directly connects to a well-studied body of literature. The main result, Theorem 1, is clearly stated and leverages existing spectral analysis of shift-register matrices to establish a strong asymptotic convergence rate, achieving a O(D log(D/ε)) time on tori, a significant improvement over the O(D^2 log(1/ε)) of standard methods.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It provides a clear background on the consensus problem and existing accelerated methods, which helps frame the contributions effectively. The authors clearly articulate how their work connects to and differs from prior work on Min-Sum for consensus [27], highlighting their more general analysis and improved rates.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the absence of any numerical experiments is a notable weakness. Simple simulations on representative graph topologies (e.g., a cycle or a 2D torus) would have greatly strengthened the paper by: (a) empirically verifying that the derived asymptotic rates hold in practice, (b) visually demonstrating the performance gap between the proposed method and standard diffusive algorithms, and (c) providing a more complete and convincing piece of work.

2.  **Practicality of Parameter Selection:** The accelerated rate proven in Theorem 1 requires setting the splitting parameter Γ = γW, where the optimal choice of γ depends on ρW, the second-largest eigenvalue magnitude of the matrix W. This is a global property of the graph, and the paper does not discuss the practical challenge of how this parameter would be computed or estimated in a distributed setting. While this is a common issue for many accelerated methods, acknowledging this limitation and briefly discussing potential approaches (e.g., using power iteration or known bounds for specific graph classes) would enhance the paper's completeness.

3.  **Connection to Lifted Markov Chains:** The connection to lifted Markov chains is presented as a high-level intuition. The authors compellingly argue that Min-Sum Splitting operates on a lifted space (directed edges) and that the splitting parameters induce a beneficial "directionality." However, unlike the rigorous connection to multi-step methods, this link is not formalized. For instance, it is not shown whether the update matrix K̂ can be interpreted as the transition matrix of a specific non-reversible random walk. While not a central flaw, strengthening this connection would make the narrative more complete.

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

*   For the final version, I strongly encourage the authors to include a small experimental section. Plotting the convergence error `||x_t - b_bar*1||` versus iteration `t` for your method against a standard gossip algorithm on a cycle graph would be highly effective and straightforward to implement.
*   Please add a brief discussion on the practical implications of choosing the parameter γ, which depends on global graph information (ρW). This would demonstrate an awareness of the practical challenges and make the work more grounded.
*   In Section 5, it would be helpful to add a sentence or two of intuition explaining *why* the `min` operation in the general Min-Sum algorithm (Algorithm 1) becomes a simple linear update for the parameters (R̂, r̂) in Algorithm 2. This is due to the objective and messages being quadratic, which makes the minimization step analytically tractable, but explicitly stating this would improve clarity for a broader audience.

**Decision**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a significant and original theoretical contribution. It elegantly connects two important areas of research and provides the first, and very favorable, convergence analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. The weaknesses, primarily the lack of experiments and discussion of parameter selection, are addressable and do not detract from the importance of the core theoretical insight. The paper is a clear advancement of the state of the art in the theory of distributed optimization.
