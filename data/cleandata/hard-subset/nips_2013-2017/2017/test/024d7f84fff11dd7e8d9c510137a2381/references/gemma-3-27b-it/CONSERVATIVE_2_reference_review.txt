This paper investigates the convergence rates of Min-Sum Splitting applied to the consensus problem. The authors establish accelerated convergence rates, specifically a square-root improvement over diffusive methods, and connect their work to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods. While the problem of accelerating consensus is important, and the theoretical results are presented with a degree of rigor, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling empirical validation and relies heavily on theoretical analysis without demonstrating practical impact.

The core idea – leveraging splitting to achieve faster convergence in Min-Sum – is interesting, and the connection to existing work in lifted Markov chains and accelerated optimization is well-articulated. The theoretical analysis appears sound, building on previous work by [27] and extending it to demonstrate convergence for arbitrary graph topologies. The authors' careful tracking of the evolution of the algorithm via an auxiliary message-passing scheme is a clever analytical tool.

However, the paper's weaknesses are significant. First, the lack of experimental results is a major drawback. While the authors focus on theoretical convergence rates, there is no demonstration of these rates in practice. Simulations on various graph topologies (cycles, tori, random graphs) would be crucial to validate the theoretical claims and demonstrate the practical benefit of Min-Sum Splitting. Without such validation, the results remain largely academic. The authors claim a square-root improvement, which, even if correct theoretically, needs to be substantiated with evidence showing this improvement translates to reduced runtime in realistic scenarios.

Second, the notation is dense and, at times, difficult to follow. While mathematical rigor is appreciated, clarity suffers, making it challenging for a reader unfamiliar with the specific nuances of Min-Sum and related fields to fully grasp the details. The numerous references to previous work, while demonstrating awareness of the existing literature, can also overwhelm the reader and obscure the core contributions of this paper.

Third, the motivation for the specific choice of parameters (δ=1, Γ=γW) isn’t fully explained in terms of practical considerations.  The derivation seems driven by mathematical convenience to achieve the theoretical result, without discussing whether these parameters are easily tunable or have a clear interpretation in a distributed setting.

Finally, the connection to existing accelerated methods, while stated, feels somewhat superficial.  The authors highlight similarities to shift-register methods, but don’t explore the implications of these connections for practical algorithm design or implementation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Theoretical novelty: The paper presents a non-trivial theoretical analysis of Min-Sum Splitting, demonstrating accelerated convergence rates.
*   Connection to existing work: The authors successfully link their work to relevant research in lifted Markov chains and accelerated optimization.
*   Rigor: The analysis is mathematically rigorous and builds upon established results in the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of experimental validation: The absence of simulations significantly weakens the paper’s claims.
*   Clarity and readability: The dense notation and complex presentation make the paper difficult to follow.
*   Limited practical implications: The theoretical results are not accompanied by a discussion of practical considerations or implementation details.
*   Incremental contribution: While the theoretical result is positive, the lack of empirical evidence makes it difficult to assess its true significance.



**Overall assessment:** I recommend **rejecting** this paper. While the theoretical contributions are potentially interesting, the lack of empirical validation and the difficulties in understanding the presentation significantly detract from its overall quality. The authors need to provide substantial experimental results to demonstrate the practical benefits of Min-Sum Splitting before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field. The paper currently feels incomplete and lacks the necessary evidence to justify its claims. The theoretical results, while interesting, are insufficient to warrant acceptance at NIPS.
