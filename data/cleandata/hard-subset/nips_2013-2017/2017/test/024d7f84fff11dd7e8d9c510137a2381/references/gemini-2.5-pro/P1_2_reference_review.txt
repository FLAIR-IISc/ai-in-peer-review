### **Summary**

This paper analyzes the convergence rate of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, a generalization of the standard Min-Sum message-passing protocol, when applied to the consensus problem. The authors focus on the network averaging case, a fundamental instance of consensus with a quadratic objective. The paper's core contribution is to demonstrate that, unlike the standard Min-Sum algorithm which fails to converge on graphs with cycles, the Min-Sum Splitting variant can be tuned to not only converge but also achieve an accelerated, "subdiffusive" convergence rate.

The authors establish this result through a series of elegant steps. First, they show that for the quadratic case, the Min-Sum Splitting updates become a linear system on the directed edges of the graph. The key innovation is the introduction of an "auxiliary message-passing scheme" which tracks the evolution of the algorithm's output via a simpler multi-step linear process on the *nodes* of the graph. By analyzing the spectral properties of the matrix governing this auxiliary process, the authors demonstrate that a specific choice of the splitting parameters recovers the exact dynamics of accelerated shift-register methods. This leads to their main result (Theorem 1), which establishes an asymptotic convergence time of O(D log(D/ε)) for graphs like cycles and tori, a square-root improvement over the O(D² log(1/ε)) time of standard "diffusive" consensus methods.

Conceptually, the paper provides a novel and insightful bridge between three distinct areas: belief propagation (Min-Sum), accelerated convex optimization (multi-step methods like shift-registers), and fast-mixing Markov chains (lifted/non-reversible walks). It shows how the "splitting" parameters introduce a form of momentum or directionality that is crucial for acceleration, a mechanism shared by the other two accelerated approaches. This work significantly improves upon prior analysis of Min-Sum for consensus [27] and provides the first theoretical rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm introduced in [34, 35].

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis appears sound and is well-structured. The derivation showing the equivalence between the edge-based Min-Sum Splitting process (Algorithm 2) and the node-based auxiliary process (Algorithm 3) is the technical highlight. This reduction is clever and makes the spectral analysis tractable by connecting it to a well-understood class of accelerated linear systems. The main result, Theorem 1, is a direct and powerful application of this reduction, leveraging existing analysis of shift-register methods to prove acceleration. The claims are well-supported by the theoretical derivations.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly situates the work within the broader context of consensus algorithms, outlining the limitations of standard methods and the existence of accelerated approaches. The progression from the general Min-Sum Splitting algorithm to its specialization for consensus, and finally to the auxiliary process, is logical and easy to follow. The notation is consistent and the definitions are precise. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the intuition behind their approach, particularly in connecting the splitting parameters to the concepts of "directionality" from lifted Markov chains and "memory" from multi-step methods.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original and makes several novel contributions:
1.  **First Rate Analysis for Min-Sum Splitting:** To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a formal convergence rate for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for any problem class.
2.  **Unifying Conceptual Framework:** The connection established between a belief propagation variant, multi-step optimization methods, and lifted Markov chains is a significant conceptual insight. It suggests that the "splitting" reparameterization is a powerful mechanism for embedding acceleration principles directly into message-passing frameworks.
3.  **Novel Proof Technique:** The introduction of the auxiliary process on the nodes to analyze a more complex process on the edges is a novel and effective analytical tool that may be applicable to other message-passing algorithms.
4.  **State-of-the-Art Rates for a BP-style Algorithm:** The paper demonstrates that a Min-Sum-based method can achieve convergence rates competitive with state-of-the-art specialized consensus algorithms, improving significantly on previous attempts [27].

**Significance:** The results are significant for several reasons.
1.  They elevate Min-Sum Splitting from a heuristic that was observed to work [35] to a principled algorithm with provably accelerated performance for an important class of problems.
2.  The insights could inspire new research into using reparameterization and splitting to design accelerated distributed algorithms for other optimization problems beyond consensus, potentially impacting fields like distributed machine learning and signal processing.
3.  It provides a new perspective on acceleration in distributed systems, showing that momentum-like effects can be achieved implicitly through the message-passing structure itself, rather than through explicit multi-step formulas.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  **Practicality of Parameter Tuning:** The optimal choice of the parameter `γ` in Theorem 1 depends on `ρ_W`, the second largest eigenvalue magnitude of the matrix `W`. This is a global property of the graph and is generally not available locally at the nodes, which presents a practical challenge for a distributed algorithm. While this is a common issue for many accelerated methods, the paper would be strengthened by acknowledging this limitation and briefly discussing potential strategies for estimating or adapting this parameter in a distributed setting.
2.  **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is restricted to the quadratic objective of the network averaging problem. This is where the message-passing updates become linear, which is critical for the entire analytical framework. A short discussion on the challenges of extending this analysis to more general (non-quadratic) convex consensus problems would be valuable. Does the intuition of acceleration via splitting still hold, even if a formal rate analysis is much harder?
3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While the theory is strong and convincing, the inclusion of even a small set of numerical experiments would significantly bolster the claims. For instance, a simulation on a cycle or grid graph comparing the convergence of a standard diffusive method, the method from [27], and the proposed Min-Sum Splitting algorithm would provide a clear and compelling visualization of the theoretical O(D²) vs. O(D) scaling.

### **Summary of Pros and Cons**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents the first convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.
*   Establishes a novel and insightful connection between belief propagation, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated optimization methods.
*   Proves that a Min-Sum based algorithm can achieve competitive, accelerated rates for the fundamental consensus problem, a significant improvement over prior work.
*   The paper is technically sound, exceptionally clear, and well-organized.
*   The proposed proof technique using an auxiliary process is elegant and original.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis is limited to the quadratic case (network averaging).
*   The optimal tuning of the algorithm requires global graph information (`ρ_W`), which may be impractical in a fully distributed setting.
*   The paper lacks any experimental results to empirically validate the theoretical findings.

### **Recommendation**

Despite the minor weaknesses, which are primarily related to practical implementation and scope, the theoretical contributions of this paper are substantial, novel, and significant. The unification of ideas from different communities is a major strength, and the rigorous analysis provides a solid foundation for a new perspective on accelerated distributed optimization. The paper is a very strong contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance. The authors are encouraged to add a discussion on parameter tuning and consider including empirical results in the final version if space permits.
