

# Review: Accelerated Consensus via Min-Sum Splitting

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving consensus problems in distributed networks. The authors establish convergence rates for this algorithm, demonstrating a square-root improvement over diffusive methods. They show that Min-Sum Splitting can be interpreted as a process on a lifted space (similar to lifted Markov chains) and as a multi-step method (similar to accelerated first-order methods), bridging two distinct streams of research in distributed optimization. This connects to prior work by [27] (which showed ordinary Min-Sum fails on cyclic graphs) and [34] (which introduced Min-Sum Splitting), but provides the first convergence rate analysis for this variant.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with a rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors establish Theorem 1, which provides a precise characterization of the convergence rate, showing it achieves ρK = √((1-√(1-ρW²))/(1+√(1-ρW²))) < ρW, where ρW is the convergence rate of diffusive methods. This square-root improvement is significant. The proof technique using an auxiliary process (Algorithm 3) to track the algorithm's evolution on the original graph rather than the lifted edge space is novel and well-executed. The paper carefully addresses the limitations of their approach, noting that "it remains to be investigated how choices of splitting parameters different than the ones investigated in Theorem 1 affect the convergence behavior." The theoretical claims are well-supported, and the connection to existing literature (lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods) is properly contextualized.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized with clear sections that logically build the argument. The introduction effectively frames the problem and motivation, and the technical presentation is generally clear. The authors use a consistent notation system, and key contributions are well-summarized. The main theorem (Theorem 1) is stated precisely, and the proof sketch provides sufficient intuition. However, some readers unfamiliar with message-passing algorithms might find the mathematical details in Section 5-6 challenging. The paper would benefit from a brief intuitive explanation of why the auxiliary process (Algorithm 3) successfully tracks the algorithm's behavior, though this is a minor issue given the paper's technical nature.

## Originality

This paper makes several important original contributions. First, it establishes the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, addressing a gap noted in the literature. Second, it makes the novel connection between Min-Sum schemes and two distinct accelerated methods (lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods), showing how the directionality in Min-Sum Splitting is analogous to directionality in non-reversible Markov chains and memory terms in accelerated gradient methods. Third, the paper shows that proper tuning of splitting parameters recovers the same matrix as shift-register methods, establishing a concrete link between these approaches. This connection appears to be genuinely new and bridges two previously separate research streams.

## Significance

The results are highly significant for distributed optimization. The paper demonstrates that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve convergence times of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori (where D is the graph diameter), matching the accelerated rates recently established for some Nesterov-based methods [30, 36]. This represents a substantial improvement over the Θ(D² log(1/ε)) rates of classical diffusive methods. The practical implications are considerable, as consensus is a fundamental primitive in distributed machine learning, sensor networks, and multi-agent systems. The paper's theoretical insights also open new research directions for generalizing this acceleration to other problems beyond consensus.

## Strengths

- First convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting
- Novel connection between Min-Sum message-passing and two distinct accelerated methods
- Square-root improvement in convergence rates over diffusive methods
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with well-justified assumptions
- Clear demonstration of how global information (spectral properties) enables acceleration

## Weaknesses

- The paper focuses on quadratic objective functions, which limits immediate applicability to non-quadratic problems (though the authors acknowledge this as a starting point)
- The optimal parameter choice (γ = 2/(1+√(1-ρW²))) requires global knowledge of the graph's spectral properties, which may not be practical in some distributed settings
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with the state-of-the-art accelerated consensus algorithms (e.g., those in [30, 36])

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that makes significant progress in understanding and accelerating distributed consensus algorithms. The paper addresses an important gap in the literature with rigorous analysis, establishes novel connections to two distinct research areas, and demonstrates practical improvements in convergence rates. The quality of the technical content is excellent, and the paper is well-written and well-organized.

The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria at a high level: it is of high technical quality, clearly written, original in its connections and analysis, and significant for the field. The minor weaknesses noted do not detract from the paper's overall contribution.

## Recommendation

Accept. This paper makes a clear, significant contribution to distributed optimization and consensus algorithms. The theoretical results are rigorous, the connections to existing literature are insightful, and the practical implications are substantial. The paper represents a meaningful advancement over previous work on Min-Sum algorithms and distributed consensus.
