Summary
The paper studies the Min-Sum Splitting variant of the Min-Sum (a.k.a. Gaussian BP for quadratics) message passing algorithm for distributed optimization, and establishes the first convergence-rate guarantees for consensus/averaging. The main contributions are:

- Specialization of Min-Sum Splitting to equality-constrained quadratic consensus, showing the algorithm reduces to a linear message-passing scheme on directed edges with parameters (δ, Γ).
- Construction of an auxiliary node-based, two-step linear process with memory whose dynamics track the output of Min-Sum Splitting, enabling spectral analysis via an n×n matrix instead of a 2m×2m edge system.
- A main theorem proving that with δ=1 and Γ=γW, for any symmetric W with W1=1 and spectral radius ρ(W−11^T/n)<1, and with γ chosen as γ=2/(1+√(1−ρ_W^2)), the algorithm converges to the average with an accelerated “subdiffusive” rate. The asymptotic contraction factor ρ_K equals the shift-register/second-order Chebyshev rate and yields a square-root improvement over diffusive methods. On cycles and tori, this gives O(D log(D/ε)) iterations vs. the Θ(D^2 log(1/ε)) diffusive time.
- Conceptual connections: (i) Min-Sum Splitting acts like a process on a lifted space (directed edges), reminiscent of lifted non-reversible Markov chains; (ii) via the auxiliary node process, it manifests as a two-step method with memory akin to shift-register or heavy-ball/Nesterov-style acceleration.

Relation to prior work: Ordinary Min-Sum may diverge on loopy graphs; sufficient conditions (scaled diagonal dominance) fail under hard constraints. Ruozzi & Tatikonda analyzed Splitting for quadratics but did not give rates. Moallemi & Van Roy (consensus propagation) obtained rates linked to an edge-based chain; for cycles they showed O(D/ε log(1/ε)) and conjectured rates on tori. Accelerated consensus via multi-step/Chebyshev filtering (Xiao & Boyd; Golub & Varga; Ghadimi et al.) and via lifted Markov chains (Diaconis et al.; Jung-Shah-Shin) are well-known. This work is novel in proving Min-Sum Splitting attains the same accelerated asymptotics as shift-register acceleration and in providing an analysis tool (the auxiliary node system) that relates the rate to Γ directly.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution: first rigorous convergence rate for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, matching best-known two-step accelerated rates.
- Elegant analysis: reduction from edge to node dynamics with memory; explicit spectral link to W and Γ.
- Broad graph coverage: the result holds for any symmetric W with a spectral gap; yields concrete iteration complexity on graphs with geometry.
- Conceptual unification: believable and useful connections to lifted chains and multi-step optimization methods.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Scope: restricted to quadratic consensus (averaging). It remains unclear whether analogous rates hold for general (even strongly convex) objectives or for constrained, non-quadratic penalties.
- Global tuning: acceleration requires γ depending on ρ_W. While standard in accelerated consensus, this limits practicality. No discussion of distributedly estimating ρ_W or robustness to misspecification.
- Implementation details: the proposed Min-Sum Splitting is framed on directed edges (2m messages). The auxiliary node process is used for analysis; is there a recommended node-only implementation that preserves equivalence and communication costs?
- Assumptions/initialization: Theorem 1 uses δ=1, Γ=γW, symmetric W, and zero initial messages. Proposition 1 requires positivity conditions (1+δΣΓ R̂^t>0); the main text does not establish these hold under the prescribed parameters.
- Empirical validation: no experiments demonstrating constants, finite-time behavior, or comparison to Chebyshev-accelerated gossip, Nesterov-type methods, ADMM/EXTRA/Exact Diffusion on representative graphs.
- Presentation: some notation/numbering issues (e.g., K̂ defined in eq. (4) but referenced as (5); reuse of (5) for K). Footnotes and minor typos distract.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivations and reductions are plausible and consistent with known linear-iteration analyses. The main theorem’s rate matches classical second-order Chebyshev/shift-register behavior. However, key proofs and the positivity condition are deferred; bringing a brief proof sketch or a lemma guaranteeing positivity under the chosen γ, W would strengthen confidence.
- Completeness: For the quadratic consensus setting, the work is complete in asymptotic rates. Finite-time, constant-sensitive bounds and robustness to parameter errors are not addressed.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized and readable for an expert audience, with helpful motivation linking to lifted chains and multi-step methods. However, notation is heavy; tighten the exposition in Sections 5–6, fix equation numbering, and explicitly state all assumptions needed for Propositions 1–2 and Theorem 1. A schematic showing the relation among Algorithms 1–3 would help.

Originality
- The primary novelty is not a new rate per se (it matches known accelerated rates), but the first demonstration that Min-Sum Splitting can be tuned to attain such rates, plus a new analytical pathway (auxiliary node process) and the conceptual bridge to lifted chains and multi-step acceleration. This is a meaningful and original connection in the BP/min-sum literature.

Significance
- For researchers using message passing, the result clarifies when and how Splitting yields acceleration and relates it to well-studied accelerations. Potentially impactful if extended beyond quadratics. As is, it advances the theoretical understanding of Min-Sum and may guide practical algorithm design.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you guarantee the positivity condition in Proposition 1 under δ=1, Γ=γW, R̂^0=r̂^0=0? If so, please state it explicitly.
- Is there a node-only implementation equivalent to Algorithm 2 that uses O(n) memory and O(|E|) messages per iteration, leveraging Algorithm 3 constructively?
- How sensitive is the rate to γ misspecification? Provide bounds or experiments with over/under-estimated ρ_W.
- Compare empirically against Chebyshev-accelerated gossip, Nesterov/shift-register, and ADMM/EXTRA on cycles, grids, expanders, and random geometric graphs.
- Discuss how to choose W and estimate ρ_W in a distributed way (e.g., power method, Lanczos, degree-based bounds), and whether time-varying/asynchronous variants preserve acceleration.
- Any paths to extend to strongly convex smooth objectives (beyond pure averaging), or to handle constraints without reducing to averaging?

Minor comments
- Fix equation/algorithm references (e.g., K̂(δ,Γ) definition and references).
- Clean up footnotes and punctuation; ensure all references are complete and correctly formatted.

Decision
Weak accept. The paper offers a solid and novel theoretical contribution by establishing accelerated rates for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus and by introducing an insightful analytical framework. With improved clarity and at least some empirical illustration, it would be a strong addition to the NeurIPS program.
