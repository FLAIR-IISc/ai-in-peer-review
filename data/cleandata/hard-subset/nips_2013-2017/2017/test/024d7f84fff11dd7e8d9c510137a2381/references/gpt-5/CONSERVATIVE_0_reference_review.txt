Summary
This paper studies Min-Sum Splitting, a reparameterized variant of Min-Sum (a.k.a. Gaussian BP in the quadratic case), for the distributed consensus/averaging problem on graphs. For quadratic objectives, the authors show that Min-Sum Splitting induces a linear message-passing process on directed edges. They introduce an auxiliary linear process on nodes with memory that tracks the edge-based dynamics. With a specific tuning of the splitting parameters (δ = 1 and Γ = γW, where W is a symmetric averaging matrix and γ depends on ρW), they prove convergence to consensus with an asymptotic rate matching the “shift-register” (Chebyshev-type) acceleration, yielding a square-root improvement over diffusive methods on graphs with geometry (e.g., O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori). They position the result as a bridge between lifted Markov chain acceleration and multi-step first-order methods, arguing Min-Sum Splitting adds directionality/memory akin to those approaches.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical connection between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step/shift-register accelerations; the auxiliary node-based system is technically neat and may be of independent interest.
- Rigorous spectral-rate result (Theorem 1) that recovers known accelerated rates for consensus within the Min-Sum Splitting framework.
- Addresses a known shortcoming of ordinary Min-Sum (lack of convergence on cycles) with a principled parameterization.
- Good coverage of related literature and contextualization within consensus/acceleration work.

Weaknesses
- Narrow scope: results are for quadratic consensus (averaging) with specific initialization; no general convex objectives, constraints, or nonquadratic cases are handled beyond high-level discussion.
- Practicality concerns: the required tuning depends on global spectral information (ρW) and on selecting a specific W; computing/approximating these in a distributed setting is nontrivial. The paper does not provide a viable distributed procedure for parameter selection.
- No empirical validation. Given the wealth of established accelerated consensus methods (Chebyshev, heavy-ball/Nesterov, Olshevsky’s linear-time algorithms, ADMM variants), the absence of experiments comparing convergence, communication/memory costs, and robustness (asynchrony, noise, quantization) is a significant gap.
- Incremental novelty: the accelerated rate matches classical shift-register/Chebyshev acceleration, and the proof leans on known spectral results. The contribution is largely a reinterpretation: showing Min-Sum Splitting can be tuned to emulate these known accelerations.
- Complexity not assessed. Min-Sum Splitting operates on messages on directed edges (2m variables), effectively a lifted space. The paper does not quantify per-iteration computation/communication and memory relative to competing accelerated node-based methods, nor does it discuss convergence wall-clock/bit complexity.
- Some technical assumptions are awkward or underexplained (e.g., 0 < bv < 1, positivity conditions in Proposition 1, reliance on R̂0 = r̂0 = 0), and robustness to other initializations is unclear.

Quality
- Theoretical development appears mathematically sound and consistent with prior spectral analyses, though proofs are relegated to the supplement. However, the lack of experiments weakens the validation of practical claims (e.g., “competitive and close to optimal”).
- Important implementation aspects (parameter selection, complexity, robustness) are not addressed.

Clarity
- Generally well written and well organized, with a helpful narrative connecting to two acceleration paradigms. Notation is dense; some footnote integration and minor typos aside, the exposition is understandable for experts.
- It would help to more explicitly state the conditions under which the auxiliary process exactly tracks Min-Sum and discuss when the positivity constraints may fail.

Originality
- Conceptual connection of Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods is interesting, and the auxiliary process is a nice device. That said, the achieved rates and underlying mechanisms are well known; novelty is primarily in reinterpretation rather than new algorithms or improved rates.

Significance
- The main value is theoretical unification: it may inform analysis and design of message-passing schemes through known acceleration theory. From an algorithmic standpoint, significance is limited without demonstrating practical advantages over established accelerated consensus methods. Requiring global spectral tuning and operating on edge messages detract from applicability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide empirical comparisons on cycles, grids, random geometric graphs, and expanders versus Metropolis, optimized W, Chebyshev/shift-register, heavy-ball/Nesterov, ADMM/EXTRA, and lifted-walk methods. Report both iteration counts and communication/bit complexity.
- Discuss and, if possible, implement distributed/robust parameter selection (estimating ρW or diameter), and sensitivity to misspecification.
- Quantify per-iteration costs (memory, messages per node) and contrast with node-based accelerated methods.
- Extend theory or experiments beyond quadratic to strongly convex objectives, and consider asynchronous/time-varying/quantized settings.
- Clarify initialization requirements and the positivity condition in Proposition 1; discuss general initializations.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical link unifying message passing, lifting, and multi-step acceleration.
- Clean spectral analysis and auxiliary-node construction may be useful to the community.

Cons for acceptance
- No experiments; limited practical validation.
- Acceleration and rates are not new; contribution is mainly a reinterpretation.
- Requires global information and exhibits edge-level overhead; unclear practical advantage.
- Scope restricted to quadratic consensus.

Overall recommendation
While the theoretical connections are interesting, the work feels incremental relative to established accelerated consensus methods and lacks empirical validation. Given NeurIPS’s emphasis on rigor plus impact, I lean toward rejection in its current form. A strengthened version with thorough experiments, complexity analysis, and broader applicability would be more compelling.
