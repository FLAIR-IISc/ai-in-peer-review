

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of distributed optimization by establishing convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to consensus problems. The authors demonstrate that proper tuning of the splitting parameters enables Min-Sum to achieve subdiffusive convergence rates, specifically O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, which represents a square-root improvement over the O(D² log(1/ε)) rates of classical diffusive methods. The paper establishes novel connections between Min-Sum Splitting and two major lines of accelerated consensus research: lifted Markov chains and multi-step gradient methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper fills a critical gap in the literature by providing the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, which has been observed to converge where ordinary Min-Sum fails but lacked theoretical guarantees. The proof technique using an auxiliary process to track the algorithm's evolution on the nodes (rather than on directed edges) is elegant and insightful.

2. **Novel Connections:** The paper makes a compelling case for how Min-Sum Splitting bridges two distinct approaches to accelerated consensus: it can be viewed as a process on a lifted space (analogous to lifted Markov chains) while simultaneously incorporating memory terms (analogous to multi-step gradient methods). This unification is particularly valuable for understanding the acceleration mechanism.

3. **Technical Rigor:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing precise convergence rates and relating them to the spectral properties of matrices associated with the graph. Theorem 1 is particularly strong, showing that the proposed parameters yield convergence rates matching those of shift-register methods.

4. **Clear Organization:** The paper is well-structured, with a logical flow from problem definition to related work, algorithm description, theoretical analysis, and connections to broader literature.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation:** As a machine learning conference paper, the absence of empirical results to validate the theoretical findings is a notable gap. While the paper focuses on theoretical analysis, NIPS typically expects some empirical demonstration of the method's practical benefits, especially given the practical applications of consensus algorithms.

2. **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The paper focuses on quadratic objective functions (network averaging), which is a specific case of the consensus problem. It would strengthen the paper to briefly discuss how the results might extend to more general convex objectives or non-quadratic problems.

3. **Comparison to Recent Work:** While the paper references [27] as previous work on Min-Sum for consensus, it doesn't clearly articulate how the current work improves upon it beyond convergence rates. The connection to the conjecture in [27] could be more explicitly addressed.

4. **Practical Implementation Concerns:** The paper doesn't discuss the practical implementation challenges of the proposed method, such as the need for global information (eigenvalues of W) to set the parameter γ, which may be difficult to obtain in distributed settings.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of distributed optimization, establishing a novel connection between message-passing algorithms and accelerated consensus methods. The convergence rate analysis is rigorous and the paper provides valuable insights into the acceleration mechanism. The work is highly relevant to NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in distributed machine learning and optimization.

The primary weakness is the lack of empirical validation, which is somewhat unusual for a NIPS paper. However, the theoretical contribution is substantial and fills a clear gap in the literature. The paper would be significantly strengthened with a brief empirical demonstration of the convergence rate improvement on simple graph topologies.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

**Justification:** The paper's theoretical contribution is significant, well-executed, and addresses an important problem in distributed optimization. The novel connections to two major lines of research in accelerated consensus are particularly valuable. While empirical validation would have strengthened the paper, the theoretical results are sufficiently compelling for a top-tier machine learning conference. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, providing a new perspective on how to accelerate distributed algorithms through message-passing techniques.

The work opens promising avenues for future research on generalizing the acceleration mechanism to other distributed optimization problems beyond consensus, which could have significant impact on large-scale machine learning systems. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field.
