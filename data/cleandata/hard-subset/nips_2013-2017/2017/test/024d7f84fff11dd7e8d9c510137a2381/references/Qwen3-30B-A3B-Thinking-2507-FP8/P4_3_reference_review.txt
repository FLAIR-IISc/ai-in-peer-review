

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

The paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving the consensus problem in distributed networks, establishing convergence rates that achieve a square-root improvement over traditional diffusive methods. The consensus problem involves nodes in a graph reaching agreement on an average value, with applications in sensor networks, distributed optimization, and machine learning. The authors demonstrate that Min-Sum Splitting, when properly parameterized, achieves convergence times of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori (where D is the graph diameter), compared to the Θ(D² log(1/ε)) of classical diffusive methods. The main technical contribution is an auxiliary message-passing scheme that allows the authors to analyze the convergence behavior by relating it to the spectral properties of a matrix, rather than the more complex dynamics on directed edges. The paper establishes novel connections between Min-Sum Splitting and two established approaches for accelerated consensus: lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization.

**Quality:** The paper provides technically sound theoretical analysis. The convergence rate proof (Theorem 1) is rigorous, with the auxiliary process construction being a significant technical contribution that enables the spectral analysis. The authors carefully address both the strengths (accelerated convergence) and limitations (requires global information for parameter tuning) of their approach. The theoretical results are well-supported by the analysis in the supplementary material, and the paper properly contextualizes its contributions relative to prior work, including the earlier study by [27] that investigated Min-Sum for consensus but did not establish convergence rates.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and situates the work within the broader context of distributed consensus algorithms. The connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods are clearly articulated. The paper defines all necessary terms and provides sufficient background on Min-Sum and consensus problems. The structure of the paper follows a logical progression from problem formulation to analysis to results. The supplementary material appears to contain the necessary details for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The paper makes several significant novel contributions. It establishes the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, which has been previously observed to converge where ordinary Min-Sum fails. The auxiliary process construction is a novel technical contribution. Most importantly, the paper establishes the first connection between Min-Sum schemes and both lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization. The paper demonstrates how Min-Sum Splitting achieves acceleration through a mechanism similar to these established approaches but without the overhead of constructing lifted graphs. This connection appears to be novel to the literature.

**Significance:** The results have clear significance for distributed computing applications. The square-root improvement in convergence rate (from Θ(D² log(1/ε)) to O(D log(D/ε))) represents a substantial theoretical advance for graphs with geometric structure (cycle graphs, tori, random geometric graphs). The paper demonstrates that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve rates close to the optimal O(D log(1/ε)) recently established for some accelerated methods. The connection to two established approaches suggests potential for cross-fertilization of ideas in distributed optimization. The paper opens up new directions for generalizing the acceleration due to splitting to other optimization problems beyond consensus.

**Strengths:**
- First convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting algorithm
- Novel auxiliary process construction for convergence analysis
- Clear theoretical connections to two established accelerated consensus approaches
- Demonstrated square-root improvement in convergence rates for important graph topologies
- Careful theoretical analysis of both acceleration mechanism and limitations

**Weaknesses:**
- Parameter tuning requires global information about the graph (spectral gap), which may be impractical in fully distributed settings
- Focuses solely on quadratic objective functions (network averaging), limiting generalizability to non-quadratic problems
- Lacks empirical validation to complement the theoretical results
- The practical implementation challenges of the algorithm in real distributed systems are not thoroughly discussed

**Conclusion:** This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of Min-Sum Splitting for distributed consensus. The convergence rate analysis and the novel connections to established accelerated methods represent substantial advances. While the requirement for global information in parameter tuning is a limitation, the theoretical results are compelling and the paper establishes a solid foundation for future work on accelerating distributed algorithms. The work is well-executed, clearly written, and addresses a meaningful problem in distributed optimization. The paper's contributions are sufficiently novel and significant for NIPS.
