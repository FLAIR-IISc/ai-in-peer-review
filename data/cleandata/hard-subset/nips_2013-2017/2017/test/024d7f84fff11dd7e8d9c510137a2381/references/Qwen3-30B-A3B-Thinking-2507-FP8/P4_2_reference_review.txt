

This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving the consensus problem in distributed networks, establishing convergence rates that significantly outperform classical diffusive methods. The authors analyze Min-Sum Splitting applied to the network averaging problem (a specific case of the consensus problem with quadratic objectives), proving that with proper parameter tuning, the algorithm achieves accelerated convergence rates of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, where D is the graph diameter. This represents a square-root improvement over the standard Θ(D² log(1/ε)) convergence rate of diffusive methods. The paper establishes novel connections between Min-Sum Splitting and two established accelerated methods: lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first-order optimization methods. The key technical contribution is the introduction of an auxiliary message-passing scheme that enables analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm by relating its convergence to the spectral properties of a matrix supported on the nodes of the original graph.

The paper builds on prior work in several ways. It extends the Min-Sum Splitting framework introduced in [34] and [35] to the consensus problem, addressing limitations of the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm noted in [27] (which showed convergence only for certain graph topologies with a different formulation). The authors position their work within the broader context of accelerated consensus algorithms, distinguishing their approach from both lifted Markov chain techniques [8, 5, 37] and multi-step gradient methods [13, 24]. Their theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) demonstrates that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve the same asymptotic convergence rate as shift-register methods, with the same matrix K described in [45, 4, 24].

Strengths:
Quality: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis with a complete proof of the convergence rate (Theorem 1), including bounds on the asymptotic convergence time. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach (requiring global information for parameter tuning) and provide clear connections to established results in the literature. The auxiliary process technique for analysis is technically sound and well-motivated.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The problem definition, related work, and technical contributions are presented with precision. Algorithm 3 (the auxiliary message-passing scheme) is particularly well-explained as the key tool for the analysis. The paper avoids unnecessary jargon and provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with Min-Sum protocols.

Originality: This work makes several novel contributions. It is the first to establish convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, the first to connect Min-Sum schemes with both lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods, and the first to demonstrate how directionality in message-passing protocols can be tuned to accelerate convergence. The auxiliary process technique represents a significant methodological contribution.

Significance: The results have important implications for distributed computing, as they establish a practical method to achieve near-optimal convergence rates for consensus problems. The square-root improvement in convergence time for common graph topologies (cycles, tori) is substantial and directly addresses a well-known limitation of classical consensus algorithms. The connections to established accelerated methods suggest broader applicability to other distributed optimization problems.

Weaknesses:
Quality: The analysis is limited to the quadratic case of the consensus problem (network averaging), which is a specific but common case. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not thoroughly discuss how their approach might extend to non-quadratic problems or more general constraints. The paper lacks experimental validation of the theoretical results, which would strengthen the claims about practical performance.

Clarity: While the paper is generally clear, the connection between the auxiliary process and the original Min-Sum Splitting algorithm (Proposition 2) could be more explicitly motivated for readers unfamiliar with message-passing algorithms. Some technical details in the supplementary proofs might benefit from additional explanation.

Originality: The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from recent work on distributed ADMM [3, 43, 38, 12], which also connects message-passing to lifting techniques. While the authors note this connection in Remark 1, they could more thoroughly compare their results to these ADMM-based approaches.

Significance: The requirement for global information (ρ_W) to tune the splitting parameters is a practical limitation that might hinder implementation in fully decentralized settings. The paper could better address how this limitation might be overcome in practice or how it compares to similar requirements in other accelerated methods.

Pro and Con Arguments:
Pro: The paper makes significant theoretical contributions to understanding Min-Sum Splitting, establishes the first convergence rate analysis for this algorithm, demonstrates a practical square-root improvement in convergence time, and establishes novel connections to two major lines of accelerated consensus research. The auxiliary process technique is a valuable methodological contribution.

Con: The analysis is limited to quadratic objectives, the parameter tuning requires global information, and the paper lacks experimental validation. The connection to ADMM-based approaches is not sufficiently explored.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the theory of distributed optimization, establishing a new framework for understanding and accelerating Min-Sum protocols. While the focus on quadratic objectives and the need for global information are limitations, the theoretical results are sound and significant. The paper meets all four NIPS criteria with strong quality, clarity, originality, and significance. The theoretical advances have clear potential to influence future work in distributed optimization and message-passing algorithms.
