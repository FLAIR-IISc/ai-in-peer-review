

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper proposes a Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for the consensus problem and claims to establish accelerated convergence rates compared to classical diffusive methods. While the paper addresses an interesting problem and makes some connections to existing literature, it suffers from multiple technical inconsistencies, ambiguous formulations, and insufficient experimental validation that undermine its scientific contribution.

## Major Technical Issues

1. **Inconsistent Notation and Ambiguous Definitions**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout, particularly regarding the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. For example, it refers to "Min-Sum Splitting" in the title but uses "Min-Sum scheme based on splitting" in the introduction. The algorithm parameters δ and Γ are defined in Section 2 but the paper doesn't clarify that δ must be positive for the quadratic messages to be well-defined (a critical requirement for the algorithm to work as claimed).

2. **Theoretical Ambiguities**: Theorem 1 contains critical ambiguities. The matrix K is defined as K := [[γW I], [(1-γ)I 0]], which is unclear without proper block matrix notation. More significantly, the paper states "γ = 2/(1 + √(1-ρ_W^2))" but ρ_W is defined as "ρ(W - 11^T/n)" (the spectral radius of the matrix W minus the all-ones matrix). This is confusing because ρ(W - 11^T/n) is not the same as the second largest eigenvalue of W (which is what one would expect for convergence analysis of consensus algorithms).

3. **Missing Justification for Key Assumptions**: Proposition 1 states a condition "1 + δ ∑ w∈N(v) ΓwvR̂^t_wv > 0" must hold, but the paper doesn't explain why this condition is necessary or how to ensure it in practice. For the algorithm to be useful, the authors should provide conditions under which this holds for practical choices of parameters.

4. **Inconsistent References**: The paper references [35] as evidence that "the algorithm with splitting has been shown to yield convergence in settings where the ordinary Min-Sum does not converge," but [35] (Walk-sums and belief propagation in gaussian graphical models) is about Gaussian graphical models, not convergence properties of Min-Sum with splitting. Similarly, [7] is cited for "the mixing time of any reversible Markov chain scales at least like D^2," but [7] (Moderate growth and random walk on finite groups) is about random walks on finite groups, not graphs with geometry.

5. **Lack of Experimental Validation**: As a NIPS paper, the absence of any experimental results to validate the theoretical claims is a significant omission. The paper makes strong claims about convergence rates but provides no empirical evidence to support them.

## Minor but Significant Issues

1. **Formatting Inconsistencies**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for "min" vs "Min-Sum" throughout. In the abstract, it says "Min-Sum Splitting" but in the introduction it's "Min-Sum scheme based on splitting." This inconsistency makes it difficult to follow the paper.

2. **Ambiguous Terminology**: The paper states "the convergence time is O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles and tori" but doesn't specify whether this is for the number of iterations or computational complexity. For a distributed algorithm, both are important, but the paper fails to clarify.

3. **Incorrect Mathematical Notation**: In Section 7, the paper writes "1/(1-ρ_K) ≤ √1/(1-ρ_W)" which is ambiguous without proper parentheses. It should be written as "1/(1-ρ_K) ≤ √(1/(1-ρ_W))" to be mathematically clear.

4. **Overstated Contributions**: The paper claims "first analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting" but doesn't clearly explain why previous work (like [27]) doesn't cover this. [27] already investigates Min-Sum for consensus, so the paper needs to explicitly state what's novel in their analysis.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths**:
- The paper makes a valuable connection between Min-Sum schemes and both lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first-order methods.
- The auxiliary process introduced in Section 6 is a clever technical contribution.

**Weaknesses**:
- The theoretical claims are not rigorously supported due to ambiguous formulations.
- The paper lacks experimental validation, which is expected for a NIPS submission.
- The references to prior work are inconsistent and sometimes misleading.

## Recommendation

While the paper touches on an interesting problem and makes some novel connections, the technical inconsistencies, ambiguous formulations, and lack of experimental validation prevent it from meeting NIPS's standards for scientific rigor. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to clarify the mathematical formulations, provide proper experimental validation, and ensure all references are accurately cited.

The paper is currently not suitable for acceptance at NIPS due to these significant technical issues, despite the potential interest of the topic. The authors should address the specific concerns raised above before resubmission.

Overall, the paper's technical flaws outweigh its potential contribution, and I recommend rejection.
