This paper analyzes the convergence rate of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, a generalization of the standard Min-Sum (or Belief Propagation) algorithm, when applied to the consensus problem. The consensus problem, particularly network averaging, is a fundamental building block in distributed optimization and machine learning. Standard consensus algorithms based on linear iterations, such as gossip methods, are known to have a "diffusive" convergence rate which can be slow on graphs with certain geometries (e.g., cycles or grids). The standard Min-Sum algorithm is known to fail to converge on such loopy graphs for this problem.

The authors show that the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, which introduces tunable parameters through a reparametrization of the objective function, not only converges but can be tuned to achieve an accelerated, or "subdiffusive," rate of convergence. The main contribution is a formal proof establishing this accelerated rate. By specializing the algorithm to the quadratic objective of network averaging, the authors show the message-passing updates become linear. The core of their analysis is the introduction of an auxiliary message-passing scheme that tracks the algorithm's output on the nodes of the graph, rather than on the directed edges. This transforms the analysis of a complex 2m-dimensional system (where m is the number of edges) into that of a more tractable 2n-dimensional system (where n is the number of nodes).

This auxiliary process reveals a deep and novel connection to existing accelerated consensus methods. The authors demonstrate that with a specific choice of splitting parameters, the Min-Sum Splitting dynamics on the nodes are mathematically equivalent to those of accelerated multi-step methods, specifically the shift-register method. By leveraging the known spectral analysis of these methods, they prove that Min-Sum Splitting achieves a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods for graph families like cycles and tori, matching the state-of-the-art rate for such algorithms. This work is the first to provide a convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and, more broadly, the first to formally connect the reparametrization of belief propagation schemes to the acceleration mechanisms of lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis. The derivation of the linear update for the quadratic case (Proposition 1) and the subsequent construction of the equivalent auxiliary node-based process (Proposition 2) are clear and correct. The main result, Theorem 1, correctly leverages established spectral analysis of shift-register dynamics to prove the accelerated convergence rate. This is a complete piece of work that provides a definitive answer to the question of Min-Sum Splitting's convergence rate for the consensus problem.

A minor weakness is the lack of empirical validation. While the theoretical contribution is strong and stands on its own, numerical experiments on various graph topologies would have complemented the analysis, demonstrating the practical performance of the algorithm and confirming the asymptotic rates. Furthermore, the analysis is limited to the network averaging problem (quadratic objectives). While this is a critical and widely-studied case, a discussion of potential challenges or pathways to extending the analysis to more general convex functions would have broadened the paper's scope.

**Clarity:**
The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively situates the work within the broader context of consensus algorithms and message-passing methods, clearly stating the contributions. The logical progression from the general algorithm to its specific application and the novel analysis technique is easy to follow. The explanation of how the auxiliary process simplifies the analysis is particularly well done.

A small point for improvement is the presentation of the matrix `K̂` in Equation (4), which is quite dense. A block-matrix representation or a more descriptive explanation could enhance readability. There also appears to be a minor inconsistency or potential typo in the description of the output of Algorithm 2. The output is defined as `xtv := (bv + δ Σ...) / (δ Σ...)`, but Proposition 1 states a condition `1 + δ Σ... > 0`, and the auxiliary algorithm's output is `rtv / Rtv` where `R0v` is initialized with a `1+...` term. This suggests the denominator in Algorithm 2's output should also contain a `1+...` term, which should be clarified.

**Originality:**
The paper is highly original. To our knowledge, this is the first work to provide a formal convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. The most significant and novel contribution is the conceptual bridge it builds between three distinct areas: belief propagation-style message passing, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order optimization methods. The paper shows that the "splitting" reparametrization in Min-Sum acts as a mechanism to induce directionality and memory, which are the key ingredients for acceleration in the other two families of algorithms. The proof technique, using an auxiliary process on the nodes to analyze the dynamics on the edges, is also novel and could be applicable to the analysis of other message-passing schemes.

**Significance:**
The results are significant for several reasons. First, they elevate Min-Sum Splitting from a heuristic with observed empirical benefits [35] to a principled algorithm with provably state-of-the-art acceleration for a fundamental distributed problem. Second, the unification of ideas from seemingly disparate fields provides a new and powerful lens through which to understand and design accelerated distributed algorithms. This work could spur further research into using reparametrization and splitting to accelerate message-passing algorithms for other problems in optimization and machine learning.

A practical limitation, common to many accelerated methods, is that the optimal choice of the tuning parameter (γ) depends on global information about the graph (the spectral gap ρW). The paper acknowledges this connection but does not discuss how this parameter could be set in a distributed setting. This tempers the immediate practical applicability but does not diminish the theoretical importance of the findings.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  Provides the first-ever convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.
2.  Establishes a novel and insightful connection between message-passing schemes and the two primary classes of accelerated consensus algorithms (lifting and multi-step methods).
3.  Demonstrates that a belief propagation-style algorithm can be tuned to achieve state-of-the-art accelerated rates for the canonical consensus problem.
4.  The proof technique based on an auxiliary process is elegant and potentially of broader interest.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
1.  The analysis is restricted to the quadratic case (network averaging) without discussion of extension to more general problems.
2.  The paper lacks experimental results to support the theoretical analysis.
3.  The optimal parameter choice requires global graph information, limiting immediate practical deployment.

Overall, the strengths, particularly the novelty of the connections established and the technical quality of the results, significantly outweigh the weaknesses. The paper makes a fundamental contribution to the understanding of message-passing algorithms and their relationship to accelerated optimization.
