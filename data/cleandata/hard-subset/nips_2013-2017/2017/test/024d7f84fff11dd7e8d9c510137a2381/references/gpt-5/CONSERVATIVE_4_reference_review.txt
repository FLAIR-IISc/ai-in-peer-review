Summary
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting, a reparametrized version of Min-Sum (a.k.a. Gaussian BP in the quadratic case), for the consensus/averaging problem on graphs. The authors (i) show that for quadratic consensus, Min-Sum Splitting induces a linear message update on directed edges; (ii) construct an auxiliary 2n-dimensional linear iteration on nodes that tracks the algorithm’s output; and (iii) prove a convergence-rate result (Theorem 1) showing a square-root improvement over classical diffusive consensus when the splitting parameters are tuned using global spectral information. With δ=1 and Γ=γW, γ=2/(1+sqrt(1−ρW^2)), the iteration recovers the same rate as second-order/shift-register methods, yielding O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori, compared to O(D^2 log(1/ε)) for reversible walks. The paper also draws conceptual connections between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step accelerated first-order methods.

Relation to prior work
- Ordinary Min-Sum may diverge on loopy graphs; previous work [27] analyzed a variant and tied rates to a 2m-dimensional edge process. Splitting was proposed in [34,35] but without rate guarantees. The present paper provides the first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus.
- The achieved rate matches that of classical Chebyshev/shift-register methods (e.g., [15,24,45]) and is “close” to O(D log(1/ε)) rates obtained by certain Nesterov-type schemes [30,36].
- The auxiliary linear system offers a cleaner node-level spectral analysis than prior edge-based analyses.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of Min-Sum Splitting with tunable parameters; the quadratic case is worked out to linear recursions.
- The auxiliary node-based iteration (Algorithm 3) is a useful analytical device that relates BP-style message passing to standard multi-step linear iterations; this connection may be of independent interest.
- Theorem 1 gives an explicit rate and parameter choice and recovers known accelerated rates, providing the first rigorous rate characterization for Min-Sum Splitting in this setting.
- The paper builds bridges between three lines of work (BP/splitting, lifting, and multi-step acceleration), which could spur cross-fertilization.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited scope: all results are for quadratic consensus. Despite claims of broader relevance, no extension (or even partial result) is provided for general convex objectives or constrained formulations–precisely where Min-Sum is often used. As such, the contribution is a reinterpretation of known accelerated consensus within Min-Sum Splitting.
- Practicality of tuning: the accelerated choice requires γ as a function of ρW, a global spectral quantity. The paper does not provide distributed estimation/tuning or robustness analysis to mis-specification. This substantially limits applicability.
- Lack of empirical validation: no experiments on cycles, grids, random geometric graphs, or general graphs. There is no comparison to established baselines (Metropolis, optimal symmetric W, Chebyshev/shift-register, Nesterov-accelerated consensus, ADMM-based methods, and lifted Markov chain schemes). Without empirical constants, communication/computation costs, and robustness tests, it is hard to assess practical significance at NeurIPS.
- Algorithmic overhead: the Min-Sum implementation exchanges edge-based messages, whereas the auxiliary method suggests an equivalent node-based 2-step iteration. It is unclear if there is any advantage to using Min-Sum Splitting over simply running a known shift-register method; costs per iteration, memory, and numerical stability are not discussed.
- Theoretical novelty: the main rate matches classical second-order methods; the novelty is primarily the derivation of Min-Sum Splitting that replicates those rates. For NeurIPS, this is incremental unless backed by new insights that enable broader classes of problems or practical gains.
- Assumptions and technical details: Theorem 1 assumes symmetric W with W1=1, and uses initial conditions R̂0=r̂0=0. The positivity condition in Proposition 1 (to ensure well-defined beliefs) is not guaranteed by the provided dynamics; this should be proved under the recommended parameter choice. Some claims (bounds relating 1/(1−ρK) and 1/(1−ρW)) are deferred to the supplement and not sketched. The manuscript contains typos/formatting issues and references to figures not present, which detract from clarity.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide experimental results comparing wall-clock/communication complexity vs Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, Nesterov-accelerated consensus, ADMM variants, and lifted-chain methods on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs?
2. How can γ be tuned in a distributed way without global spectral knowledge? Is the method robust to over/under-estimation? Any adaptive scheme?
3. Do your techniques extend to strongly convex (non-quadratic) φv or pairwise couplings beyond equality constraints? Even partial results or obstacles would be valuable.
4. What conditions ensure the positivity required in Proposition 1 under your recommended parameters and initialization?
5. Is there any concrete advantage in using the edge-based Min-Sum Splitting over a direct node-based second-order scheme, given the auxiliary equivalence?

Minor comments
- Improve clarity/notation and fix typos (e.g., footnotes formatting, “st Conference”).
- Precisely define K̂ (equation numbering consistency) and ensure all referenced figures are included.
- Discuss the sign pattern of Γ=γW when W has negative entries (as in optimal symmetric W) and implications for implementability.

Recommendation
While the theoretical bridge is interesting and the analysis appears technically sound (pending supplemental proofs), the contribution is narrow (quadratic consensus), relies on global tuning, and lacks empirical validation or demonstrated practical benefits over established accelerated consensus methods. Given NeurIPS standards and my emphasis on rigorous validation against strong baselines, I lean to reject. Confidence: high.
