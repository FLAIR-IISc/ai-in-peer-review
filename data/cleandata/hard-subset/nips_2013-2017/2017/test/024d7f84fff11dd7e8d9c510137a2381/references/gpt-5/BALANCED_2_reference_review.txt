Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting (a reparameterized Min-Sum/Belief Propagation scheme) for the consensus/averaging problem on graphs. The authors:

- Formulate Min-Sum Splitting for quadratic consensus and show that, with an appropriate parametrization, the message updates on directed edges are linear in scalar parameters (Algorithm 2).
- Introduce an auxiliary node-based, two-step linear iteration with memory (Algorithm 3) that exactly tracks the Min-Sum outputs under suitable initialization (Proposition 2). This makes the analysis depend on the spectrum of an n×n matrix Γ rather than a 2m×2m edge matrix.
- Prove that choosing δ=1 and Γ=γW (with W symmetric, W1=1, and γ tuned from ρW) yields convergence with an accelerated rate governed by the second eigenvalue of a shift-register matrix K (Theorem 1). This gives a square-root improvement over diffusive rates and O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori, close to the O(D log(1/ε)) achievable by optimal accelerated methods.
- Conceptually connect Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chains (non-reversibility/directionality) and multi-step first-order methods (momentum/shift-register).

This builds on prior work showing Min-Sum may fail or be slow on loopy graphs; splitting improves convergence for quadratics [34,35]. For consensus-specific Min-Sum, [27] studied a variant with soft constraints and characterized rates via a 2m-dimensional Markov chain for certain graphs. The present work provides general convergence rates for any graph via the auxiliary node process and recovers the same asymptotic rates as classical shift-register/Chebyshev acceleration [4,15,24,45]. The connection to lifted Markov chains aligns with results in [5,8,18,37], and the optimization view aligns with accelerated consensus literature [13,14,24,32,41,44], as well as distributed ADMM links [3,12,38,43].

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical contribution: first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, with a clean spectral bound and explicit tuning.
- Useful technical reduction: the auxiliary node-based process (Algorithm 3) both clarifies the mechanism (memory/directionality) and simplifies analysis compared to edge-based dynamics.
- Solid positioning: relates Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step gradient methods, showing that acceleration in Min-Sum arises from the same mechanisms.
- Generality of the rate claim across graph topologies; matches known accelerated rates on cycles/tori and improves upon [27].

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Scope limited to quadratic consensus (averaging) with synchronous updates and exact arithmetic. No results for general convex objectives, constraints beyond equalities, or non-quadratic penalties.
- Requires global spectral information (ρW) to set γ; this is common in accelerated methods but undermines fully decentralized implementability. No discussion of how to estimate or adaptively tune γ.
- Implementation/overhead not fully clarified. While Min-Sum messages live on directed edges, the auxiliary scheme suggests an equivalent node-based 2n-state implementation. A detailed comparison of per-iteration communication/memory versus standard accelerated consensus (e.g., Chebyshev/shift-register, Nesterov, EXTRA, accelerated gossip) is missing.
- Experimental validation is absent. Given that the asymptotic rate is known/expected to match shift-register methods, empirical results would help quantify constants, robustness, and finite-time behavior versus baselines (Metropolis, optimal W from SDP, Chebyshev acceleration, Nesterov/heavy-ball, lifted walks, ADMM/EXTRA).
- Some choices raise questions: The main theorem uses δ=1 (no node splitting), placing all “splitting” in Γ. Clarifying when δ≠1 helps or hurts would strengthen the narrative about directionality from splitting.

Quality:
- The main theorem and equivalences are plausible and consistent with known results for Chebyshev/shift-register methods; the spectral formula for ρK is standard. The proofs are deferred; assuming correctness, the technical contribution is sound. However, the paper would benefit from more detailed proof sketches in the main text to highlight the crucial steps (e.g., the construction and properties of K(δ,Γ), and the equivalence between edge- and node-based dynamics).

Clarity:
- Generally well organized with a good literature overview and discussion of intuition. Some definitions and equations (e.g., K̂(δ,Γ) and the conditions in Proposition 1) could be streamlined with clearer notation and intuition about positivity conditions. It would help to explicitly state the communication/computation per iteration for Algorithms 2 and 3 and to summarize the practical recipe (how to choose W, how to set γ, how to initialize).

Originality:
- The acceleration mechanism and resulting rates per se are not new (they match shift-register/Chebyshev acceleration), but applying Min-Sum Splitting to recover them and establishing the precise equivalence via the auxiliary process seems new. The paper provides a conceptual bridge linking Min-Sum Splitting, lifted chains, and multi-step optimization, which is a nice contribution.

Significance:
- For researchers using message passing and distributed optimization, showing that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve accelerated consensus rates and is intimately connected to known accelerations is valuable. However, the practical advantage over existing accelerated consensus methods is unclear without experiments or complexity comparisons. The focus on averaging only limits immediate impact.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide simulations on cycles, grids, expanders, and random geometric graphs comparing Min-Sum Splitting (edge vs auxiliary node implementation) to Metropolis, optimal W, Chebyshev/shift-register, Nesterov/heavy-ball, EXTRA, and distributed ADMM.
- Discuss how to choose W in practice and how to estimate ρW (e.g., distributed power methods) or use robust parameter choices when only bounds are known. Consider adaptive/robust tuning of γ.
- Clarify implementability: present a node-level implementation (Algorithm 3) with exact message/compute costs; compare to standard accelerated consensus iterations with two states per node.
- Explore δ≠1 and whether additional splitting yields benefits or robustness.
- Extend the analysis beyond quadratics (e.g., strongly convex smooth functions via local quadratization) or at least discuss prospects and obstacles.
- Comment on asynchrony, time-varying graphs, and quantization/noise robustness.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear theoretical contribution and conceptual connections.
- Novel analysis technique for Min-Sum Splitting and first rates on consensus.
- Results align with and help unify disparate literatures (BP, lifting, accelerated optimization).

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited scope (quadratic consensus only), no empirical validation, and unclear practical advantages over established accelerated consensus methods.
- Requires global spectral information and provides limited guidance for robust tuning.
- Acceleration achieved matches known methods; novelty is mainly the connection rather than new algorithms or better rates.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper offers a nice theoretical bridge and a clean analysis, but without empirical evidence and with limited scope beyond averaging, the contribution feels incremental relative to established accelerated consensus methods. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying practical implementability and tuning would significantly improve the paper.
