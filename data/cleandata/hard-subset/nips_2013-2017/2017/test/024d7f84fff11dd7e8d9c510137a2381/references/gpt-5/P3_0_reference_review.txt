Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the Min-Sum Splitting message-passing scheme for distributed optimization and applies it to the classical consensus/averaging problem. The central contributions are: (i) a linearization of Min-Sum Splitting for quadratic consensus that operates on directed edges (Algorithm 2) and an equivalent auxiliary, node-based, two-step linear system (Algorithm 3); (ii) a convergence-rate analysis showing that, with δ=1 and Γ=γW for a symmetric W with W1=1, and γ tuned as γ=2/(1+√(1−ρW^2)), Min-Sum Splitting converges to the average with asymptotic rate ρK strictly better than the diffusive rate ρW, yielding a square-root improvement in convergence time; (iii) conceptual connections to two acceleration paradigms: non-reversible lifted Markov chains (directionality on edges) and multi-step first-order methods (a shift-register memory on nodes). The main result (Theorem 1) recovers the same rate as Chebyshev/shift-register-type accelerations and implies O(D log(D/ε)) iterations on cycles and tori, improving over O(D^2 log(1/ε)) of reversible diffusion-based consensus.

The paper builds on the Min-Sum/GBP literature (e.g., [28,25,34,35]) and on consensus/averaging (e.g., [44,2,7,33,41,42]) and accelerated consensus (e.g., [4,14,24,45,30,36,37]). It improves upon [27], which analyzed a modified Min-Sum with soft constraints and obtained partial results (exact for cycles, conjectural for tori), by giving a general and clean spectral-rate analysis for arbitrary graphs via the node-based auxiliary process.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The linearization for quadratics is standard; Proposition 1 formalizes quadratic message invariance and recurrences on directed edges. Proposition 2 is the key step enabling a cleaner spectral analysis through the 2n×2n node-based recursion with matrix K(δ,Γ). Theorem 1’s rate matches known shift-register/Chebyshev rates; the choice Γ=γW and δ=1 leads to K=[γW I; (1−γ)I 0], exactly the standard second-order consensus form. This is consistent and compelling.
- Assumptions/limitations: The analysis is restricted to quadratic consensus with synchronous, static, undirected graphs and symmetric W. The parameter γ requires knowledge of ρW, a global spectral quantity. Initialization is specialized (R̂0=r̂0=0). The positivity condition in Proposition 1 is mentioned but not discussed in depth; the auxiliary formulation partly circumvents that, but it would be useful to state conditions ensuring R^t>0 explicitly.
- Rigor and completeness: Most proofs are deferred to the supplement; the main text provides correct statements and clear mappings between the edge- and node-based systems. Constants in the error bound are explicit but somewhat loose; the main value is the asymptotic rate.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized, with clear articulation of background, algorithms, and main theorem.
- Notational density is high; some equations appear to have minor typos in the displayed output formulas (e.g., the expression for x^t_v in Algorithm 2 seems to be missing an obvious division; later, Algorithm 3 clarifies x^t_v=r^t_v/R^t_v). A careful pass to fix such slips would improve readability.
- The intuition behind “directionality” induced by splitting would benefit from a brief illustrative example on a path/cycle to connect the edge updates to non-reversibility.

Originality
- Novelty lies less in achieving a new best-possible rate (the rate matches classic Chebyshev/shift-register acceleration) and more in establishing that Min-Sum Splitting, when properly parametrized, implements the same accelerated dynamics and in providing the first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting. The auxiliary-process technique is elegant and, to my knowledge, new in this specific Min-Sum Splitting context.
- The conceptual unification with lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods is insightful and well motivated.

Significance
- For the BP/Min-Sum community, showing that Splitting can be tuned to achieve accelerated consensus rates is valuable and may catalyze similar analyses for other objectives.
- For consensus per se, the practical novelty is modest, since the resulting rate and iteration matrix coincide with known accelerated schemes; however, the route via Min-Sum Splitting may be advantageous in settings where message-passing formulations are already in place or where one wants to reason about constraints via factor graphs.
- The requirement for global spectral information to set γ mirrors other accelerated methods and limits full decentralization unless one adopts estimation schemes; discussing this would increase practical impact.

Strengths
- Clear mapping from Min-Sum Splitting to a tractable linear two-step node process; clean spectral-rate bound.
- Conceptual bridges to non-reversible lifting and multi-step methods.
- General graph topology analysis (not limited to regular graphs).
- Improves upon prior Min-Sum consensus analyses and corrects non-convergence of ordinary Min-Sum on loopy graphs.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope: Restricted to quadratic consensus, synchronous updates, symmetric W. It would strengthen the paper to discuss whether the auxiliary-process technique can extend to strongly convex quadratic objectives with constraints beyond equality, or to composite objectives.
- Practicality: Requires γ tuned with ρW. Please discuss:
  - How to choose W (e.g., Metropolis) and estimate ρW in a distributed manner (e.g., power method, Lanczos, local degree bounds), and robustness to mis-specification.
  - Comparison of communication/memory complexity: Min-Sum Splitting updates per directed edge (2m states) vs shift-register methods per node (2n states). What is the per-iteration message size and cost? When is Min-Sum preferable?
- Empirics: No experiments are provided. Even small-scale simulations on cycles, grids, random geometric graphs, and expanders comparing Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, the proposed Min-Sum Splitting, and ADMM-based consensus would substantiate the theory, illustrate constants, and show robustness to parameter mismatch.
- Clarity/typos: Fix the output expression in Algorithm 2, check equation numbering and ensure K̂ definition is free of transcription errors. Provide a short proof sketch for Theorem 1 in the main text to guide readers.
- Relation to prior acceleration: Please compare explicitly to Chebyshev polynomial acceleration (Xiao & Boyd 2004) and to recent optimal rates (e.g., Scaman et al. 2017) in terms of rates and parameter knowledge, and clarify in what sense Min-Sum Splitting offers advantages (easier to derive from factorization? flexibility with additional constraints?).

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution tying Min-Sum Splitting to accelerated consensus with rigorous rates.
- Elegant auxiliary-process analysis with potential to generalize.
- Bridges communities (BP/GBP, Markov-chain lifting, first-order acceleration).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited scope (quadratic, synchronous, symmetric) and dependence on global spectral information.
- No experimental validation or complexity comparison; the accelerated behavior coincides with known methods.
- Some presentation issues (minor typos, dense notation).

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper provides a clear and technically sound theoretical advance on Min-Sum Splitting with interesting conceptual connections. Adding experimental validation and clarifying practical deployment aspects would make it stronger. Confidence: high.
