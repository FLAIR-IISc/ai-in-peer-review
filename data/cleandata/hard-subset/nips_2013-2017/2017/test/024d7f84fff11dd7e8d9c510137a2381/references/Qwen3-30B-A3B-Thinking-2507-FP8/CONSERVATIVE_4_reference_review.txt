

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

## Summary
This paper proposes "Min-Sum Splitting" as a method to accelerate consensus algorithms in distributed optimization. The authors claim their method achieves subdiffusive convergence rates (O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle and torus graphs) compared to the standard diffusive rates (O(D^2 log(1/ε))). They establish theoretical convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting applied to the consensus problem, showing it can be related to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order optimization methods. The paper presents a theoretical analysis without empirical validation, claiming to be the first to establish convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting in the consensus setting.

## Strengths
1. The paper presents a well-structured theoretical analysis of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus problems.
2. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two well-established approaches (lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods) is interesting and potentially valuable.
3. The paper is clearly written with precise mathematical formulations.
4. The authors properly contextualize their work within the literature on distributed consensus.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of empirical validation**: This is the most significant weakness. The paper makes strong claims about convergence rate improvements but provides no experimental results to support these claims. Without empirical validation, it's impossible to assess whether the theoretical improvements translate to practical benefits. For a paper claiming to establish "accelerated" methods, this is a critical omission.

2. **Questionable novelty**: The paper claims to be "the first" to establish convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting in consensus, but [27] already investigated Min-Sum for consensus (though with a different formulation) and provided convergence results for cycle graphs. The authors don't adequately distinguish their analysis from [27]'s work, particularly since [27] already conjectured similar results for tori. The connection to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods is interesting but not entirely novel—these connections have been explored in other works (e.g., [18, 37] for lifting, [13, 24] for multi-step methods).

3. **Practical limitations**: The proposed method requires global knowledge of the graph (specifically, the spectral radius ρW) to choose the optimal splitting parameters. As noted in Theorem 1, "γ depends on the quantity ρW, the second largest eigenvalue in magnitude of the matrix W." In distributed settings, this global information is typically unavailable, making the proposed method impractical for real-world deployment without significant additional communication overhead. The paper fails to address how this parameter selection could be performed in a distributed manner.

4. **Overstated significance**: The paper claims their method "gets close to the optimal rate O(D log(1/ε)) recently established for some algorithms based on Nesterov's acceleration" but doesn't provide a meaningful comparison with these methods. The theoretical improvements are framed as a "square-root improvement" but without empirical evidence of actual speedup in practice.

5. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper mentions "accelerated first-order methods" but doesn't compare Min-Sum Splitting with the best-performing methods in the literature (e.g., [13, 24, 30]). Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess the practical relevance of the claimed improvements.

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents an interesting theoretical analysis of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus problems. However, the lack of empirical validation, questionable novelty, and significant practical limitations (requiring global graph knowledge) substantially undermine the paper's contribution. While the theoretical analysis appears rigorous, the absence of any experimental results to validate the claimed improvements makes it difficult to assess the practical value of the method.

The paper would be significantly stronger with:
1. Empirical validation showing actual convergence speed improvements on benchmark graphs
2. A discussion of how the required global information could be obtained in a distributed setting
3. A more thorough comparison with existing accelerated consensus methods

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the theoretical claims compelling but insufficiently validated. The paper does not provide enough evidence to support the claim that Min-Sum Splitting is a practical improvement over existing methods. The absence of empirical results is particularly concerning for a paper making strong claims about convergence rates.

## Recommendation
Reject. The paper lacks empirical validation, which is essential for a claim of "accelerated" convergence. The theoretical contribution appears to be incremental rather than transformative, and the practical limitations significantly reduce the paper's impact. Without experimental results demonstrating the claimed improvements, the paper does not meet NIPS standards for a high-quality scientific contribution.
