

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates the convergence properties of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving the consensus problem in distributed networks. The authors establish that Min-Sum Splitting, when properly parameterized, achieves accelerated convergence rates compared to classical diffusive methods. Specifically, they show that with appropriate splitting parameters, the algorithm achieves convergence time O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, representing a square-root improvement over the O(D² log(1/ε)) of traditional methods. The paper makes a novel connection between Min-Sum schemes, lifted Markov chain techniques, and multi-step first-order optimization methods, establishing that the acceleration mechanism in Min-Sum Splitting is analogous to both approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides the first rigorous analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, which is a significant theoretical contribution. The authors establish a clear connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two established approaches in the literature (lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods).

2. **Novel Proof Technique**: The introduction of an auxiliary process to track the evolution of Min-Sum Splitting on the nodes of the graph is elegant and potentially applicable to other problems. This technique allows the authors to relate convergence rates to the spectral properties of matrices, which is more tractable than analyzing the edge-based process directly.

3. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper effectively frames the consensus problem, reviews existing approaches, and clearly articulates how Min-Sum Splitting differs from prior work. The connections to related work are well-explained and properly contextualized.

4. **Significant Theoretical Result**: Theorem 1 establishes a concrete square-root improvement in convergence rate, which is a meaningful theoretical advance. The paper correctly identifies that this rate matches the asymptotic rates of shift-register methods for consensus.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: The paper is purely theoretical and lacks any empirical evaluation. While the theoretical results are compelling, the absence of experiments showing the practical performance improvement over existing methods (particularly in comparison to the lifted Markov chain and multi-step methods the paper claims to relate to) significantly weakens the impact of the contribution.

2. **Limited Scope of Results**: The results are restricted to quadratic objective functions in the consensus problem. The paper doesn't address whether the acceleration can be extended to more general non-quadratic problems, which would be important for broader applicability.

3. **Practical Implementation Concerns**: The paper requires global information (ρW, the second largest eigenvalue in magnitude of W) to properly tune the splitting parameters. The paper doesn't address how this information could be obtained in a distributed setting without incurring significant overhead, which is a critical practical consideration.

4. **Comparison to Existing Methods**: While the paper establishes theoretical connections, it lacks a detailed comparison of the computational complexity and practical implementation requirements of Min-Sum Splitting versus the alternative accelerated methods it references. A more thorough comparison would strengthen the significance claim.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the understanding of Min-Sum algorithms for consensus. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods is novel and potentially significant. However, the lack of empirical validation and limited scope of the results (quadratic objectives only) prevent the paper from making a strong contribution to the NIPS community.

The authors should address the following concerns before reconsideration:

1. Add empirical results comparing Min-Sum Splitting to existing methods (both diffusive and accelerated) on standard consensus problems. This should include convergence speed comparisons for cycle graphs and tori.

2. Extend the analysis to non-quadratic objective functions or at least discuss the limitations of the quadratic restriction.

3. Address how the required global information (ρW) can be obtained in a distributed setting without excessive overhead.

4. Provide a more detailed comparison of computational complexity and practical implementation requirements versus alternative accelerated methods.

The theoretical contribution is strong, but the paper's impact would be significantly enhanced by empirical validation and a more thorough discussion of practical implementation considerations. Without these additions, the paper is primarily of theoretical interest without clear evidence of practical utility.

## Final Assessment

**Quality**: 7/10 (Theoretical results are sound but lack empirical validation)
**Clarity**: 8/10 (Well-structured and clearly written)
**Originality**: 8/10 (Novel connections established)
**Significance**: 6/10 (Theoretical contribution is significant, but practical impact is unverified)

**Recommendation**: Weak Reject (with strong potential for revision). The paper has a strong theoretical foundation but requires empirical validation and a more thorough discussion of practical implementation to justify its significance for the NIPS community.
