

# Review: Accelerated Consensus via Min-Sum Splitting

## Summary
This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving the consensus problem in distributed networks. The authors establish theoretical convergence rates for this algorithm, showing that with proper parameter tuning, it achieves a square-root improvement over diffusive methods (O(D log(D/ε)) versus O(D² log(1/ε)) for graphs like cycles and tori). The key insight is that Min-Sum Splitting can be connected to both lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first-order optimization methods, providing a unified perspective on accelerated consensus. The paper introduces a novel auxiliary message-passing scheme to track the algorithm's evolution, which enables the spectral analysis required for their convergence rate proofs.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theoretical understanding of distributed optimization. The convergence rate analysis is rigorous, with clear proofs of the main theorems. The authors carefully address the limitations of previous work (notably [27]) and demonstrate that their method achieves superior convergence rates. The paper acknowledges the need for global information to tune parameters (a limitation they discuss), showing intellectual honesty about the work's scope.

**Originality:** This paper makes several novel contributions. First, it provides the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, a gap identified in the literature. Second, it establishes the first connection between Min-Sum schemes and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods. Third, it demonstrates how directionality in message passing can be tuned for acceleration—a novel insight for distributed algorithms. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work, particularly [27], which only conjectured improved rates for specific topologies.

**Significance:** The consensus problem is a fundamental distributed optimization primitive with wide-ranging applications. The square-root improvement in convergence rate is significant for large-scale distributed systems, especially those with geometric constraints (e.g., sensor networks). The paper's connection to established accelerated methods suggests potential for broader impact beyond consensus, potentially inspiring new distributed algorithms. The theoretical advances represent a meaningful step toward the optimal convergence rate O(D log(1/ε)) recently established for some accelerated methods.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem, the limitations of existing methods, and the paper's contributions. The notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and other accelerated methods is explained with sufficient mathematical precision. The auxiliary algorithm (Algorithm 3) is particularly elegant and well-motivated.

## Weaknesses

**Scope Limitation:** The analysis is restricted to the quadratic consensus problem (network averaging), which is a specific case of the broader consensus problem. While this is a standard benchmark, the paper doesn't address how the results might extend to more general objective functions (e.g., non-quadratic or constrained problems), which would strengthen the significance.

**Practicality of Parameter Tuning:** The accelerated convergence requires parameters (δ and Γ) that depend on global graph properties (e.g., ρ_W, the second largest eigenvalue of a matrix associated with the graph). In distributed settings, nodes typically only have local information, making this global tuning impractical. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't propose a distributed method to estimate these parameters, which is a significant barrier to practical implementation.

**Lack of Experimental Validation:** While the paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, it lacks empirical results to validate the theoretical claims. A small set of experiments showing convergence rates on specific graph topologies would greatly strengthen the paper's contribution, especially for the NIPS audience which often values both theoretical and empirical validation.

**Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper cites [30, 36] regarding optimal rates, but doesn't explicitly compare its method to other recent accelerated consensus methods like those in [30, 36] or [13]. A more detailed comparison would better contextualize the paper's contribution.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes significant advances in understanding distributed optimization algorithms. The paper is clearly written, makes novel connections between different lines of research, and establishes meaningful theoretical improvements over existing methods. While the restriction to quadratic objectives and the need for global parameter tuning are limitations, the theoretical insights are substantial and could inspire future work on distributed algorithms.

The paper meets the NIPS standards for quality, originality, and significance. The theoretical results are well-supported, and the connections to established methods in both Markov chain theory and optimization provide a compelling narrative. The lack of empirical validation is a minor drawback for NIPS, but the theoretical contribution is strong enough to overcome this limitation.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear, well-executed theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of distributed optimization algorithms. The insights about the connection between Min-Sum message passing and accelerated methods are novel and valuable, and the convergence rate analysis provides a meaningful improvement over existing methods. While the practical limitations of global parameter tuning should be acknowledged, the theoretical insights are substantial and would be of interest to the NIPS community. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of how to address the global information requirement in practice, but this doesn't diminish the strength of the theoretical contribution.

**Pro:** Strong theoretical contribution, first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, novel connection to two lines of research, clear writing.

**Con:** Limited to quadratic consensus problem, requires global parameter tuning, lacks empirical validation.

This paper represents a meaningful advance in the theoretical understanding of distributed optimization algorithms and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
