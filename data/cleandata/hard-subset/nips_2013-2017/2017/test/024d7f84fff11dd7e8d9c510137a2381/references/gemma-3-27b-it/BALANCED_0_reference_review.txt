This paper investigates accelerated convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors establish rates of convergence for any graph topology, achieving a square-root improvement over diffusive methods, and showing competitiveness with optimal rates recently established using Nesterov’s acceleration. The work connects Min-Sum splitting to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, providing valuable insights into the underlying mechanisms driving the acceleration.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem – accelerating consensus algorithms – and provides a significant contribution by establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, a previously underexplored algorithm in this context. The square-root improvement over diffusive methods is a notable result.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The analysis is mathematically sound, building on a solid foundation of existing literature on message passing, graph theory, and optimization. The use of an auxiliary process to track the algorithm’s evolution is a clever technique that simplifies the analysis.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper skillfully connects Min-Sum Splitting to two distinct areas of research – lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods – providing a broader perspective on the algorithm's properties and potential applications.  The authors correctly identify the parallels to shift-register methods.
* **Clarity of Presentation (in parts):** The motivation for the work is clearly presented, and the connection to previous work is well-established. The introduction effectively outlines the problem and the contributions of the paper.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Accessibility:** While the introduction is well-written, the technical sections (particularly sections 2, 5, and 6) are dense and difficult to follow.  The notation is heavy, and the flow of the argument is sometimes obscured by the level of detail. A more gradual introduction of the mathematical tools and concepts would significantly improve readability.
* **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses exclusively on theoretical results. While this is acceptable for a NIPS submission, the inclusion of even limited simulations to demonstrate the practical performance of the algorithm and validate the theoretical findings would strengthen the paper considerably.
* **Repetitive Background:** The background material on diffusive algorithms and accelerated methods (Sections 3 and 4) is somewhat repetitive and lengthy. The authors could streamline these sections by focusing on the most relevant prior work and highlighting the specific gaps that their work addresses.
* **Algorithm 2 and 3 introduction:** The sudden introduction of Algorithms 2 and 3, with minimal motivating explanation, creates a jarring transition. The link between these algorithms and the original Min-Sum Splitting is not immediately obvious and requires careful reading.
* **Assumptions and Generalizability:** The current analysis is focused on the specific case of the quadratic consensus problem. It would be valuable to discuss the potential for generalizing the results to other objective functions and constraints.

**Specific Comments and Suggestions:**

* **Section 2:** The discussion of splitting parameters δ and Γ could be more intuitive. Providing a concrete example of how these parameters affect the objective function would be helpful.
* **Section 5:** The explanation of Proposition 1 feels rushed. The reader is left to infer the connection between Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2.
* **Section 6:** The motivation for introducing Algorithm 3 is unclear.  More explicitly state *why* tracking the evolution via this auxiliary algorithm simplifies the analysis.
* **Theorem 1:** The statement of Theorem 1 is quite technical. A more intuitive explanation of the convergence rate and its implications would be appreciated.
* **Consider adding a table summarizing the convergence rates of different consensus algorithms:**  This would provide a clear comparison of the performance of Min-Sum Splitting with existing methods.
* **In the conclusions, mention future work:** Addressing the limitations, such as generalizing to non-quadratic objective functions.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical contribution to the field of distributed optimization.
* The analysis is rigorous and well-supported by mathematical arguments.
* The connections to existing work are insightful and demonstrate a deep understanding of the relevant literature.
* The results have the potential to impact a wide range of applications, including sensor networks, distributed machine learning, and multi-agent coordination.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in the technical sections.
* The lack of empirical validation weakens the impact of the theoretical results.
* The repetitive background material detracts from the overall flow of the paper.
* The abrupt introduction of Algorithms 2 and 3 hinders readability.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field, but its clarity and accessibility need improvement. The theoretical results are significant, and the connections to existing work are well-established. However, the lack of empirical validation and the density of the technical sections are drawbacks. 

**Recommendation:**

**Reject with Major Revisions.** 

I recommend rejecting the paper in its current form, but encourage the authors to revise it based on the feedback provided above. Specifically, they should focus on improving the clarity of the technical sections, adding empirical validation, streamlining the background material, and providing more intuitive explanations of the key concepts. If these revisions are made, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
