- Paper analyzes Min-Sum Splitting for distributed consensus and proves convergence rates, showing a square-root improvement over diffusive methods.  
- Establishes first convergence rate analysis for this variant of Min-Sum, filling a gap in the literature.  
- Connects Min-Sum Splitting to lifted Markov chains and multi-step (accelerated) first-order methods, bridging two research streams.  
- Theorem 1 precisely characterizes the improved convergence rate (strictly better than diffusive rate ρW).  
- Proof technique uses a novel auxiliary process (Algorithm 3) to track behavior on the original graph rather than the lifted edge space.  
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with well-justified assumptions and clear technical presentation.  
- Results show accelerated rates O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori, matching other accelerated consensus methods.  
- Demonstrates how proper tuning of splitting parameters recovers shift-register matrices, linking to existing methods.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written, though Sections 5–6 may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with message-passing.  
- Limited to quadratic objective functions, restricting immediate applicability to non-quadratic problems.  
- Optimal parameter choice requires global spectral knowledge of the graph, which may be impractical in some distributed settings.  
- Would benefit from a more detailed empirical/theoretical comparison with other state-of-the-art accelerated consensus algorithms.  
- Overall assessment: strong, original theoretical contribution with significant practical implications; recommendation: accept.
