

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

## Summary
The paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving the consensus problem in distributed networks, claiming to establish accelerated convergence rates compared to classical diffusive methods. The authors connect Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first-order optimization methods, arguing that proper tuning of splitting parameters yields subdiffusive convergence rates. The key contribution is a theoretical analysis showing that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve convergence time O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, improving upon the Θ(D² log(1/ε)) of diffusive methods.

## Strengths
The paper makes a valuable connection between two distinct lines of research: lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods. The theoretical analysis is well-motivated, and the paper correctly identifies the need for a new proof technique to analyze Min-Sum Splitting, avoiding the spectral gap analysis of the edge-based process used in [27]. The paper's claim that Min-Sum Splitting is competitive with recent accelerated algorithms based on Nesterov's acceleration is substantiated through the theoretical analysis.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Notational Inconsistencies
The paper contains numerous minor but persistent formatting and notational errors that undermine its professionalism and clarity:

1. Inconsistent notation: The paper uses both "W" and "W" for the matrix (e.g., "W t → 11T /n" vs "W^t → 11^T/n"). The mathematical notation should be consistently formatted.

2. Typos in key equations: "φv(z) := 12z − bvz" should be "(1/2)z² - b_v z" (missing square). The paper also states "12 √ 1/(1− ρW )" which should be "1/2 √ 1/(1− ρW )".

3. Formatting errors in algorithms: Algorithm 1 uses inconsistent spacing ("φv/δ − µ̂s−1wv") and incorrect notation ("N (v)" should be "N(v)").

4. Reference formatting: The citation "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)" contains an erroneous "st" (should be "Conference").

5. Inconsistent mathematical notation: The paper uses "log(D/ε)" in some places and "log D/ε" in others, creating ambiguity.

### Theoretical Gaps and Presentation Issues

1. Theorem 1 contains a mathematically messy expression: "ρK := ρ(K −K∞) = limt→∞ ‖(K −K∞)t‖1/t = √ (1− √ 1−ρ2W )/(1+ √ 1−ρ2W )" which could be simplified for clarity. The paper doesn't explain why this expression is preferable to the more standard form.

2. The paper claims "a square-root improvement" but doesn't explicitly show the comparison to the diffusive case in the main text. The comparison is buried in the asymptotic convergence time discussion.

3. The paper states "the convergence time isO(D logD/ε)" which should be "O(D log(D/ε))" - the missing parentheses make the expression ambiguous.

4. The paper refers to "the supplementary material" for proofs, but NIPS expects the core technical contributions to be in the main paper, not relegated to supplementary material.

5. The connection to shift-register methods is claimed to be "the same matrix," but the paper doesn't explicitly show the equivalence in the main text.

### Minor but Significant Technical Issues

1. The paper states "the convergence time, i.e., the number of iterations required to reach a prescribed level of error accuracy ε > 0 in the `2 norm relative to the initial condition, scales independently of the dimension of the problem, as Θ(log 1/ε)" but this is only true for graphs with good expansion properties (e.g., complete graphs or expanders), not for all graphs.

2. In Proposition 2, the initialization "Q0v := − δ ∑ w∈N (v) ΓwvR̂ 0 wv" contains inconsistent summation notation ("w∈N (v)" vs "w∈N(v)").

3. The paper claims "the auxiliary process is supported on the nodes of G" but the matrix K(δ,Γ) is 2n×2n, not n×n as suggested in the text.

4. The paper cites [7] for "the mixing time of any reversible Markov chain scales at least like D²" but [7] actually establishes a lower bound for specific graphs, not for "any" reversible Markov chain.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical connection between Min-Sum Splitting and accelerated consensus algorithms, the numerous minor formatting and notational errors, combined with the lack of complete presentation of the core proof in the main paper, significantly undermine its quality. The paper's main contribution would be valuable if presented with the precision and attention to detail expected at NIPS.

The paper's claim of "a square-root improvement" is not sufficiently highlighted or explained in the main text, and the theoretical analysis contains several minor but persistent inconsistencies that would make it difficult for readers to follow the mathematical reasoning. These issues are particularly concerning for a theoretical paper where precision is paramount.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper has interesting theoretical connections and potential significance, the numerous minor but persistent formatting and notational errors, combined with the lack of complete presentation of the core proof in the main paper, are unacceptable for a NIPS submission. The paper should be revised to address these issues before being considered for publication at NIPS.

The authors should:
1. Consistently format all mathematical notation
2. Correct all typos in equations
3. Present the core proof in the main paper (not in supplementary material)
4. Clarify the claimed "square-root improvement" explicitly
5. Fix the ambiguous expression "log D/ε" to "log(D/ε)"

Without these revisions, the paper fails to meet the quality standards expected at NIPS.
