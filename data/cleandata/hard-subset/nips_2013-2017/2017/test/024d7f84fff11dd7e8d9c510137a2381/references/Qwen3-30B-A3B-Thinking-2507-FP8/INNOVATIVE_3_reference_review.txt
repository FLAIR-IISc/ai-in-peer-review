

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper introduces Min-Sum Splitting, a novel variant of the Min-Sum message-passing algorithm for distributed consensus problems. The authors establish convergence rates for this algorithm, showing it achieves a square-root improvement over traditional diffusive methods, scaling as O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles and tori (where D is the graph diameter), compared to the O(D² log(1/ε)) of standard methods. The key contribution is demonstrating that proper tuning of splitting parameters in Min-Sum yields accelerated convergence, while simultaneously establishing a theoretical connection between Min-Sum message-passing and two distinct accelerated methods: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) It provides the first analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting; (2) It reveals the first connection between Min-Sum schemes and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods; (3) It introduces a novel proof technique using an auxiliary process on the graph nodes rather than the directed edges, which simplifies the spectral analysis; (4) It designs a Min-Sum protocol that achieves better convergence rates than previous Min-Sum approaches for consensus.

The paper's novelty is particularly compelling. While Min-Sum has been studied for decades in distributed optimization and belief propagation, the authors identify a previously unexplored connection between the directionality in Min-Sum message exchange and the acceleration mechanisms in lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization. This is not merely a technical improvement but a conceptual bridge between two distinct areas of research. The authors demonstrate that the splitting parameters in Min-Sum can be tuned to recover the same matrix that supports shift-register methods in numerical analysis, achieving the same accelerated rates.

The theoretical analysis is sound, with rigorous proofs establishing the convergence rates and the connection to existing accelerated methods. The paper provides a clear mathematical framework that relates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm to both lifted Markov chain techniques (which typically require constructing new graph topologies) and multi-step gradient methods (which incorporate momentum terms). The authors convincingly show that Min-Sum Splitting achieves the best of both worlds: it uses the directionality of lifted Markov chains without requiring the construction of lifted graphs, and it incorporates the memory effects of multi-step methods without requiring additional storage.

The significance of this work is substantial. Consensus is a fundamental primitive in distributed systems, with applications in sensor networks, multi-agent coordination, and distributed machine learning. The paper demonstrates that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve near-optimal rates (O(D log(1/ε))), matching the best-known results for accelerated consensus algorithms. The authors further establish that the acceleration mechanism in Min-Sum Splitting is fundamentally similar to those in other accelerated methods, suggesting a unified perspective on acceleration in distributed optimization.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a strong connection to prior work. The authors carefully situate their contribution within the literature on distributed consensus, diffusive methods, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step optimization, making it clear how their work differs from and extends previous research.

Minor weaknesses: The paper focuses primarily on quadratic objective functions for the consensus problem, and while the authors acknowledge this limitation, a more detailed discussion of potential extensions to non-quadratic problems would strengthen the work. Additionally, while the paper shows how to achieve accelerated rates, it does not provide a detailed comparison of computational complexity with other accelerated methods.

Despite these minor limitations, the paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that establishes a new connection between different areas of research. The authors have identified a novel mechanism for acceleration in distributed algorithms that is both theoretically elegant and practically promising. The work represents a bold conceptual advance that goes beyond incremental improvements in convergence rates to provide a new perspective on acceleration in distributed optimization.

Given the high novelty, theoretical significance, and the paper's ability to connect disparate areas of research, I recommend acceptance. The paper introduces a significant new concept that could inspire future work in distributed optimization and message-passing algorithms. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the results are clearly presented with a strong connection to existing literature.

This work stands out as a genuinely novel contribution that advances the state of the art in distributed optimization. It is exactly the kind of bold, conceptual advance that NIPS aims to publish. The connection between Min-Sum message-passing and two different acceleration techniques is particularly noteworthy and represents a significant contribution to the field.
