## Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors demonstrate that, with a proper parametrization, Min-Sum Splitting converges to the consensus solution for any graph topology, achieving a square-root improvement in convergence time over classical diffusive methods. They establish a connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chain techniques and accelerated first-order methods, showcasing its versatility and theoretical grounding. The core technical contribution lies in an auxiliary process that facilitates the analysis of the algorithm’s convergence and recovers rates comparable to shift-register methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, proving convergence and establishing specific convergence rates (O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles/tori) which represent a significant improvement over existing diffusive methods.  The link to shift-register methods is a particularly strong result.
* **Novel Connection of Techniques:**  The authors successfully bridge the gap between Min-Sum schemes, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods.  This unification provides a deeper understanding of the algorithm's underlying principles and potential for generalization.
* **Well-Motivated Problem:** The consensus problem is a fundamental primitive in distributed optimization and machine learning, making the work highly relevant.
* **Clear Presentation (Generally):** The paper is generally well-structured. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and contributions. The connection to prior work is well-established.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The development of the auxiliary process and its subsequent analysis appear sound and provide a novel approach to analyzing Min-Sum convergence.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Density:** The paper is very technically dense, particularly in the sections describing the auxiliary process and the proof of Theorem 1 (which is relegated to supplementary material).  This makes it challenging for readers not deeply familiar with the specific techniques involved.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:**  While the theoretical results are strong, the paper lacks any experimental evaluation.  Demonstrating the practical benefits of the accelerated convergence on real-world or simulated graphs would significantly strengthen the work.
* **Clarity in Parameter Tuning:** While the paper claims a "proper" tuning of parameters achieves acceleration, the specific conditions for optimality (γ and W) are somewhat buried in the theorem statement and might be difficult for a practitioner to easily apply.  A more explicit guide to parameter selection would be beneficial.
* **Repetitive Background:** The extensive background sections (especially the review of accelerated algorithms) feel somewhat lengthy and could be condensed. While thorough, they occasionally detract from the core contribution.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (9/10):**  The paper is technically sound, with well-supported claims and a complete analysis of the theoretical aspects. The core result regarding convergence rates is a substantial contribution.
* **Clarity (7/10):** While generally well-written, the technical depth and density sometimes hinder clarity.  More intuitive explanations and potentially visual aids could improve understanding.
* **Originality (9/10):**  The combination of Min-Sum splitting, analysis via an auxiliary process, and the connection to diverse acceleration techniques is highly original.
* **Significance (8/10):** The results are important for the field of distributed optimization.  The improved convergence rates could have practical implications for applications like sensor networks and distributed machine learning.  However, the lack of empirical validation somewhat limits the demonstrable impact.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*  Strong theoretical guarantees of accelerated convergence for Min-Sum Splitting.
*  Novel connection between different acceleration techniques (lifted Markov chains, first-order methods) and Min-Sum schemes.
*  Addresses a fundamental problem (consensus) with a potentially impactful solution.
*  High quality technical analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  Lack of experimental validation to demonstrate practical benefits.
*  High technical density may limit accessibility.
*  Parameter tuning conditions could be more clearly articulated.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions to the field of distributed optimization. The rigorous analysis and novel connection of ideas make it a valuable addition to the literature. While the lack of empirical validation and the technical density are drawbacks, the strengths of the paper outweigh these weaknesses.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with encouragement to authors to include some empirical results and improve clarity where possible).  A brief discussion of potential empirical evaluation strategies would be beneficial even if full-scale experiments are not feasible for the final version.
