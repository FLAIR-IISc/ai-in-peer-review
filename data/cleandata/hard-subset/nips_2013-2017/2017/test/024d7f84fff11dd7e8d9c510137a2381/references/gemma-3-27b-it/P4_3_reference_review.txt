This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors establish convergence and demonstrate a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods, achieving rates competitive with recently established optimal algorithms based on Nesterov’s acceleration. The work connects Min-Sum splitting to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, providing a unified perspective.

**Summary of Related Work:** The paper appropriately positions itself within the existing literature on consensus algorithms. It accurately describes the limitations of classical diffusive methods and the emergence of accelerated techniques based on lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods. The references to key works in these areas (e.g., [27], [37], [13]) are pertinent. The authors highlight that their work provides a novel connection between these separate lines of research.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting is a novel contribution, as acknowledged by the authors. Establishing the square-root improvement over diffusive methods is significant.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous mathematical analysis, building on the auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) to derive convergence rates. The connection to shift-register methods and the spectral analysis appear sound.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and highlights the contributions. The notation is consistent. The connection to existing methods is clearly explained.
*   **Significance:** The results are potentially impactful. Achieving convergence rates close to optimal is valuable for large-scale distributed optimization problems, especially in scenarios where communication is constrained. The unification of different acceleration techniques is also a positive contribution.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The explicit connection drawn to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods provides valuable context and positions the work within a broader research landscape.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** The mathematical analysis is dense and requires a strong background in optimization and graph theory. While rigorous, the sheer volume of equations and technical details could hinder accessibility for some readers.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks any empirical evaluation. While theoretical results are valuable, demonstrating the practical performance of Min-Sum Splitting on real-world graphs would strengthen the contribution.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The analysis hinges on the proper choice of the splitting parameters (δ and Γ). The paper establishes convergence with a specific parameterization (δ=1, Γ=γW), but doesn’t extensively explore the robustness of the algorithm to different parameter settings.
*   **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on several assumptions, such as the symmetry of Γ and the quadratic objective function. The impact of relaxing these assumptions is not discussed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental problem in distributed optimization.
*   The theoretical results are novel and significant, demonstrating an improvement over existing methods.
*   The technical analysis is rigorous and well-supported.
*   The connection to existing techniques provides a valuable perspective.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical validation limits the practical impact.
*   The complexity of the analysis may hinder wider adoption.
*   The focus on a specific parameter setting and the lack of discussion of parameter sensitivity are drawbacks.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical contribution with the potential to advance the field of distributed optimization. While the lack of empirical validation and the technical complexity are weaknesses, the novelty, rigor, and significance of the results outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors consider adding some empirical results to future work. It would also be helpful to briefly discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm to variations in the splitting parameters.
