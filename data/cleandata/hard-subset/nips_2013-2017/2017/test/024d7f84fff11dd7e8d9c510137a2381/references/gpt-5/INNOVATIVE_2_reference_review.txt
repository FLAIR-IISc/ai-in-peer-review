Summary
This paper studies Min-Sum Splitting (a reparameterized Min-Sum/Belief Propagation scheme) for the consensus/averaging problem and provides the first convergence-rate analysis for this variant. The authors show:
- A parametrization under which Min-Sum Splitting becomes a linear message-passing process on directed edges.
- An auxiliary node-based linear, two-step process that exactly tracks the output of the edge-based Min-Sum scheme.
- With a particular tuning (δ=1, Γ=γW with γ chosen from the spectral gap of W), the method achieves an accelerated, subdiffusive rate with convergence time O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori—matching shift-register/Chebyshev-type accelerations and improving over classical diffusive consensus.
- Conceptual connections between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted non-reversible Markov chains (directionality/lifting on edges), and multi-step accelerated methods (momentum/shift registers on nodes).

Relation to prior work
Classical consensus is diffusive even with optimal symmetric weights (Xiao–Boyd). Accelerated consensus via multi-step methods and Chebyshev/shift-register schemes is well studied, as are lifted non-reversible chains. Prior Min-Sum consensus work (e.g., Moallemi–Van Roy; Johnson et al.) either lacks convergence on loopy graphs or uses soft constraints with rates linked to a 2m-dimensional edge process and limited topology-specific results. This paper appears to be the first to establish accelerated rates for Min-Sum Splitting, and to clearly relate its acceleration mechanism to both lifting and multi-step optimization.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: Establishing an explicit bridge from Min-Sum Splitting to (i) lifted Markov chains via edge directionality and (ii) multi-step acceleration via a node-based two-step recursion is elegant and, to my knowledge, new.
- Technical contribution: The auxiliary node-based linear system (Algorithm 3) is a powerful analytical tool that reduces a 2m-dimensional edge dynamics to spectral properties of an n×n matrix Γ. This seems broadly useful and may extend beyond consensus.
- Main result: Theorem 1 recovers the square-root acceleration in terms of spectral gap (1−ρK ≈ √(1−ρW)), yielding O(D log(D/ε)) on geometric graphs—close to known lower bounds and recent Nesterov-type algorithms—while showing that a BP-style scheme can be tuned to match shift-register rates.
- Scope and positioning: The paper situates Min-Sum Splitting within two independent acceleration lines (lifting and multi-step), which can catalyze cross-pollination.

Weaknesses
- Scope of theory: Results are restricted to quadratic consensus (network averaging) in a synchronous setting with a specific initialization (R̂0=r̂0=0) and δ=1; robustness to other initializations, δ≠1, constraints, or general convex φv is left open.
- Global tuning: The accelerating γ depends on ρ(W−11ᵀ/n), a global spectral quantity; the paper does not provide a distributed estimation/tuning scheme nor sensitivity analysis to γ mismatch.
- Empirical validation: No simulations are presented. Even a few experiments on cycles/grids/random geometric graphs comparing to Metropolis-Hastings, shift-register, Nesterov-type consensus, ADMM/EXTRA would strengthen the case and illuminate constants/transients.
- Connection to lifting: While the edge-based process resembles a lift with directionality, the precise relationship to standard lifting frameworks (e.g., optimal lifts on cycles/grids) remains mostly conceptual; formal equivalence or differences could be clarified.
- Clarity/notation: Some indexing in K̂(δ,Γ) is dense; a schematic figure or block form would aid readability. Minor typos and footnote formatting appear.

Quality
The technical claims are plausible and consistent with known accelerated consensus theory; the reduction to a node-based two-step system is clean, and the spectral-rate calculation aligns with Chebyshev/shift-register analyses. Proofs are deferred to the supplement; the main text is careful, though empirical corroboration and robustness analyses are missing.

Clarity
Well organized and readable for an expert audience. Some algebraic definitions (e.g., K̂, initialization mappings between Algorithms 2 and 3) could be streamlined. A small worked example (path or cycle) would help.

Originality
High. The auxiliary-process technique and the explicit unification of Min-Sum Splitting with lifting and multi-step acceleration appear new. Providing the first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting is a clear contribution.

Significance
Moderate-to-high. Showing that BP-style splitting can be tuned to achieve near-optimal consensus rates opens a path to bringing BP machinery to accelerated distributed optimization. The auxiliary analysis tool could transfer to other problems where BP is used with splitting.

Suggestions/questions for authors
- Can γ be tuned in a distributed/adaptive manner (e.g., spectral gap estimation, line search, Chebyshev polynomials with bounds on ρ)? How sensitive is performance to γ mis-specification?
- Do analogous results hold for general strongly convex φv or for ADMM-like quadratic constraints? Any partial extensions?
- Provide finite-time, non-asymptotic error bounds with explicit constants; discuss transients and initialization effects.
- Add experiments on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs, and compare against MH, shift-register/Chebyshev, Nesterov-type consensus, EXTRA, and distributed ADMM.
- Discuss per-iteration communication/memory cost relative to multi-step node-only methods; are there practical advantages to the edge-based BP implementation?

Recommendation: Accept
This paper introduces a genuinely new angle—accelerating consensus via Min-Sum Splitting—with a clean theoretical tool (auxiliary node process) and a provable square-root speedup. Despite limited scope (quadratic/synchronous, global tuning) and lack of experiments, the conceptual novelty and potential to spur further work justify acceptance.

Confidence: High.
