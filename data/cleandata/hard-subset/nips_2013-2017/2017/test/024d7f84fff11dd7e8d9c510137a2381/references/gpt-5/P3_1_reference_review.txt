Summary and main contributions
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting (a reparameterized variant of Min-Sum / Gaussian BP) for the consensus/averaging problem. The authors (i) show that with an appropriate parametrization, the Min-Sum Splitting messages remain quadratic and the algorithm reduces to a linear update on directed edges; (ii) introduce an auxiliary node-based linear system with memory that exactly tracks the edge-based process; (iii) prove a convergence-rate theorem (Theorem 1) showing that with δ=1 and Γ=γW (W symmetric with W1=1 and spectral radius ρ(W−11ᵀ/n)<1, and γ tuned as in the theorem), the method converges to the average with an accelerated, subdiffusive rate: the spectral gap improves from 1−ρW to 1−ρK, with 1/(1−ρK) ≈ √(1/(1−ρW)) up to constants; consequently, on cycles/tori the iteration complexity becomes O(D log(D/ε)). Conceptually, the paper connects Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chains (directionality on edges) and multi-step/shift-register accelerated first-order methods (a 2n×2n state-space K identical to classic second-order/shift-register accelerations).

Relation to prior work
- Diffusive consensus and optimal choice of W are classical (Metropolis-Hastings and SDP-optimal W) yet limited by diffusion on geometric graphs (Boyd et al.; Xiao & Boyd).
- Acceleration via lifted nonreversible Markov chains is well-known (Diaconis et al., Chen–Lovász–Pak, Jung–Shah–Shin) but entails graph lifting; acceleration via multi-step methods (heavy-ball, Nesterov, Chebyshev/shift-register) on the original graph is also well-developed (Xiao & Boyd; Golub–Varga; Ghadimi et al.; Olshevsky; Scaman et al.).
- For Min-Sum/BP, previous work showed convergence under diagonal dominance or for trees; consensus propagation (Moallemi–Van Roy) and Min-Sum for consensus with soft constraints (Moallemi–Van Roy 2006; 2010; Ruozzi–Tatikonda; Johnson et al.) characterized behavior but did not give accelerated rates for Min-Sum Splitting. The closest is [27], which analyzes an edge-space linear process for a soft-constrained variant and ties rates to an edge-based Markov chain; this paper provides topology-agnostic rates and an auxiliary node-space factorization that simplifies spectral analysis. The apparent novelty is the first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and the first explicit equivalence to shift-register acceleration.

Strengths
- Clear and nontrivial theoretical contribution: the auxiliary node-based linear system (Algorithm 3) that tracks Min-Sum Splitting on edges and exposes the 2n×2n K matrix with known spectral properties.
- Theorem 1 rigorously establishes accelerated (square-root) improvement versus diffusion for any symmetric W with W1=1, recovering O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori; constants and the exact spectral formula for ρK are provided.
- Conceptual unification: ties Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chains (directionality) and multi-step/shift-register methods (memory), clarifying how splitting induces acceleration.
- Improves upon [27] by giving general-topology rates and by avoiding edge-space spectral gaps that are hard to analyze.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practicality: the accelerated regime requires γ tuned as a function of ρW, a global spectral quantity. The paper acknowledges this but does not offer distributed estimation or robust tuning strategies. Sensitivity of convergence and rate to mis-specified γ (over/under-estimation) should be analyzed or at least illustrated empirically.
- Scope: results are for the quadratic (averaging) case on undirected graphs with symmetric W. It is unclear how the analysis extends to general convex φv or to directed/time-varying graphs; the introduction emphasizes general Min-Sum Splitting, but the main theorem is quite specialized (δ=1, Γ=γW, symmetric W).
- Initial conditions: Theorem 1 uses R̂0=r̂0=0; Propositions 1–2 introduce nontrivial positivity conditions for denominators and equivalence between edge- and node-based forms. It would help to state explicitly that with the proposed initialization the denominators remain positive (or that Algorithm 3 defines the output regardless), and to clarify whether the rate is initialization-independent.
- Relation to Chebyshev/second-order Richardson: the matrix K matches shift-register updates; it would be valuable to make the polynomial acceleration viewpoint explicit (i.e., the iterate applies a degree-t Chebyshev polynomial in W to the initial error) and to discuss whether Min-Sum Splitting is doing anything beyond re-deriving that acceleration in an edge-space disguise.
- Empirical validation: no experiments are presented. Given a primarily conceptual contribution, even small-scale simulations on cycles/grids/random geometric graphs comparing Min-Sum Splitting to Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, heavy-ball, and lifted-walk consensus would strengthen the case and illustrate constants/transients and robustness to γ.
- Clarity/presentation: a few typos and formatting issues (e.g., “st Conference…”, footnotes), and some definitions are dense. The role of δ is underplayed in the main theorem (δ=1); either justify this as the optimal choice or discuss whether δ≠1 helps.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a robustness analysis for γ, including bounds or plots for misspecification; discuss distributed estimation of ρW (e.g., power-method style protocols).
- Add experiments contrasting finite-time behavior and communication/memory costs with standard accelerations and with lifted-walk methods.
- Clarify assumptions ensuring the equivalence between Algorithms 2 and 3 and positivity of denominators; state explicitly that with R̂0=0, Algorithm 3 suffices.
- Discuss extensions: nonsymmetric W (directed graphs), time-varying graphs, and nonquadratic convex φv; indicate which parts of the analysis break and what might be possible.
- Make the Chebyshev polynomial connection explicit and relate to known optimality on paths/grids.

Evaluation
- Quality: Solid theoretical development; proofs are deferred but the spectral argument is standard and plausible. Missing empirical support and robustness analysis.
- Clarity: Generally clear, but could benefit from polishing and more intuition; the auxiliary-process insight is a highlight.
- Originality: Novel rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and a clean auxiliary-node formulation; the accelerated rate itself is not new, but the connection to Min-Sum is.
- Significance: Conceptual unification likely of interest to communities working on BP, consensus, and distributed optimization; practical impact depends on addressing tuning/robustness.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a neat and technically sound bridge between BP-style message passing and accelerated consensus, with a new analysis tool (auxiliary process) that may generalize. Strengthening the empirical and robustness aspects would make it clearly above the bar.

Confidence: High (expert in distributed optimization and message passing).
