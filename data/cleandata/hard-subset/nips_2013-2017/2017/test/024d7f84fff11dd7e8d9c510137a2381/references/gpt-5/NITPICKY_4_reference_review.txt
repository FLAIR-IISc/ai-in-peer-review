Summary
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting (a reparameterized min-sum/belief propagation scheme) for distributed consensus, focusing on network averaging with quadratic node potentials. The authors show: (i) with a suitable parametrization, min-sum messages remain quadratic and the algorithm induces a linear iteration on directed edges; (ii) an auxiliary node-based linear two-step system (a “shift-register” form) tracks the min-sum output; (iii) with δ=1 and Γ=γW for a symmetric W with W1=1, choosing γ=2/(1+√(1−ρW^2)) yields a spectral rate ρK strictly smaller than ρW, implying a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods and O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori. The work connects min-sum splitting with both lifted Markov chains (directionality) and accelerated multi-step methods.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual bridge: min-sum splitting is shown to induce directionality on edges and memory (multi-step) on nodes. The auxiliary process K(δ,Γ) is a nice analytical device.
- Theoretical rate guarantee: Theorem 1 recovers the accelerated “shift-register” rate with explicit choice of γ, and argues O(D log(D/ε)) on geometric graphs.
- Addresses known failure of ordinary min-sum on loopy graphs by a principled parameter choice; links to Gaussian BP literature.
- Useful perspective connecting min-sum, lifted chains, and multi-step acceleration, with potential to inspire further generalizations.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scope: results are confined to quadratic consensus (averaging). No guarantees for general convex consensus or constrained settings (despite the broader motivation in Sec. 1).
- Global tuning: the accelerated choice requires knowledge of ρW (global spectral information). The paper does not discuss how to select γ in a decentralized or robust manner, nor sensitivity to mis-specification.
- No experiments or numerical illustrations to corroborate theory, show constants, or compare to Chebyshev, Nesterov, ADMM-based, or lifted Markov chain methods on standard topologies.
- Clarity/formatting issues that impede reproducibility:
  - Algorithm 2 output appears dimensionally wrong; it should be xt_v = (bv + δ∑_w Γ_wv r̂^t_wv)/(1 + δ∑_w Γ_wv R̂^t_wv), consistent with Proposition 1 and the positivity condition. As written, denominators are missing.
  - Equation numbering is inconsistent: K̂(δ,Γ) is defined in (4) but Algorithm 2 cites it as “defined in (5)”.
  - Several footnote markers (e.g., “2” after “we have”) and footnote texts are embedded awkwardly in the main flow.
  - Typos: “st Conference on…” header fragment; “12 √ 1/(1− ρW )” in Theorem 1 likely means (1/2)√(1/(1−ρW)); many punctuation/spacing issues; inconsistent use of bold/italics and math fonts.
  - Some equations omit arguments (e.g., min_z { φ_vw(·,z)/Γ_vw + … }).
- Assumptions and technical gaps:
  - Theorem 1 is restricted to symmetric W; many consensus methods use row/column-stochastic W. The paper does not discuss applicability beyond symmetric/doubly-stochastic matrices.
  - The positivity condition in Proposition 1 is assumed but not ensured generically; the paper should prove it (it holds for R̂0=0 under the proposed parameters, but what about other initializations or time-varying Γ?).
  - K∞ is introduced with block rank-one structure; a clearer derivation/explanation for its form and norm bounds would help. The norm used in the main bound is the spectral norm, but constants are opaque.
  - The “first connection” claims are somewhat strong given existing links between message-passing and lifting/acceleration (e.g., Chebyshev acceleration in gossip [Xiao & Boyd], and ADMM–lifting [França & Bento]). The paper cites these but should more carefully position novelty beyond mapping min-sum splitting to a known shift-register form.
- Related work and comparisons:
  - The relation to Chebyshev semi-iterative methods for consensus (which also achieve the same rate with polynomial filtering) could be sharpened: when does Min-Sum Splitting offer practical advantages in communication, memory, or robustness?
  - No discussion of asynchronous updates, time-varying graphs, quantization, or noise.
  - Bibliography is incomplete and inconsistently formatted (missing years/venues/pages; inconsistent capitalization; duplicate commas).

Questions for the authors
1. Can the accelerated rate be obtained with only bounds on ρW (e.g., via Chebyshev polynomials) rather than exact knowledge? How sensitive is performance to γ mis-tuning?
2. Can you formally guarantee the positivity condition in Proposition 1 for the proposed initialization, and discuss other admissible initializations?
3. How does communication/memory cost compare to shift-register or Chebyshev-accelerated gossip (variables per node/edge, messages per round)?
4. Is there a path to extend the analysis beyond quadratics (e.g., strongly convex smooth φv) or to ADMM-like primal-dual min-sum variants?
5. Could you include numerical experiments on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs comparing against Metropolis, Chebyshev, shift-register, Nesterov-type, and lifted-chain methods?

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant theoretical connection between min-sum splitting and accelerated consensus.
- Provides the first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting (to my knowledge), matching known accelerated rates.
- Potentially influential perspective for extending min-sum to other distributed optimization tasks.

Cons for acceptance
- Narrow scope (quadratic averaging only); reliance on global spectral information.
- No empirical validation; practical benefits vs existing accelerated consensus methods are unclear.
- Numerous clarity and formatting errors, including incorrect equation(s) that hinder reproducibility.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The core idea and main theorem are interesting and potentially valuable, but the manuscript’s current state (lack of experiments, many presentation/formatting issues, narrow scope, and global-parameter requirement without a practical selection strategy) undermines its impact and readability. I encourage revision addressing clarity (fix equations/numbering/typos), adding empirical comparisons, discussing decentralized tuning/robustness, and better positioning relative to Chebyshev/shift-register and ADMM-based accelerations.
