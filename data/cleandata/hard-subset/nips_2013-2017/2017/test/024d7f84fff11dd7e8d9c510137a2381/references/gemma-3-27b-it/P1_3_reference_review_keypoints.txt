- Main contribution: shows Min-Sum Splitting can be tuned to achieve accelerated (sub-diffusive) consensus convergence rates.  
- Concrete improvement: proves a square-root reduction in convergence time for cycle and torus graphs, approaching optimal bounds.  
- Technical novelty: introduces an auxiliary linear process to track algorithm evolution, simplifying analysis.  
- Theoretical rigor: provides a rigorous convergence-rate analysis and connections to known accelerated methods.  
- Connections: links Min-Sum Splitting to lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order optimization methods.  
- Positioning: builds on and correctly situates itself within literature on distributed consensus, lifted chains, and acceleration.  
- Presentation: generally well-written with clear definitions, notation, and logical structure.  
- Practical implication: potential to reduce communication costs in large-scale distributed optimization.  
- Complexity concern: mathematical notation and derivations are dense and may be hard to follow without supplements.  
- Empirical gap: lacks empirical validation; small-scale experiments would strengthen the claims.  
- Parameter tuning issue: optimal splitting parameter depends on graph spectral properties, which may be unavailable in distributed settings.  
- Scope of acceleration: unclear how close results are to optimal rates for all graph topologies; more detailed comparisons needed.  
- Argument for acceptance: novel, rigorous theoretical advance with insightful connections and clever analysis technique.  
- Argument against acceptance: limited empirical evidence, theoretical complexity for broad audiences, and unresolved practical tuning challenges.  
- Overall verdict: strong theoretical paper; recommendation to accept.  
- Reviewer confidence: 8/10.  
- Suggested improvements: add empirical results, discuss practical strategies for estimating the splitting parameter, compare limitations vs. optimal bounds across topologies, and provide more intuitive explanations.
