This paper presents a convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the distributed consensus problem, specifically for network averaging. The core idea is to show that a generalized version of the Min-Sum message-passing algorithm, which previously lacked a convergence rate analysis, can not only be proven to converge but can also be tuned to achieve an accelerated, "subdiffusive" rate of convergence. The authors demonstrate that by selecting specific splitting parameters, the algorithm's performance matches that of accelerated first-order methods like shift-registers, achieving a square-root improvement in convergence time over standard "diffusive" consensus methods on graphs with geometric structure (e.g., cycles, tori).

A key technical contribution is the introduction of an "auxiliary message-passing scheme" on the nodes of the graph to analyze the behavior of the primary Min-Sum algorithm, which operates on the directed edges. This simplifies the analysis considerably. Furthermore, the paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by connecting the Min-Sum/Belief Propagation framework to two other, previously distinct lines of research in accelerated consensus: lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods (e.g., Nesterov's acceleration, Polyak's heavy ball).

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper's most significant contribution is bridging the gap between message-passing algorithms (Min-Sum) and the literature on accelerated first-order optimization methods for consensus. Showing that the "splitting" reparametrization in Min-Sum can be tuned to recover the dynamics of a shift-register method is a novel and powerful insight. This connection is not merely superficial; it explains *how* a local message-passing protocol can achieve acceleration by implicitly incorporating momentum, a mechanism that was previously thought to belong to a different class of algorithms. This is likely to inspire further research into using reparametrization techniques in other message-passing contexts to achieve acceleration.

2.  **Strong Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound and elegant. The introduction of the auxiliary process (Algorithm 3) to analyze the edge-based dynamics (Algorithm 2) is a clever proof technique. It allows the authors to map the complex 2m-dimensional system on edges to a more structured 2n-dimensional system on nodes, whose spectral properties are much easier to analyze in relation to the graph structure (via the matrix Γ). Proposition 2 provides the formal link, and Theorem 1 leverages this to directly import well-established results from the analysis of shift-register methods. The proof appears correct and the claims are well-supported by the analysis.

3.  **Clear Advancement of the State of the Art:** The paper demonstrably improves upon prior work on Min-Sum for consensus. The work of Moallemi and Van Roy [27] showed that ordinary Min-Sum fails on graphs with cycles and proposed a modified version whose convergence analysis was limited to regular graphs and yielded slower rates (e.g., O(D/ε log 1/ε) for cycles). This paper provides a Min-Sum variant that works for *any* graph topology and achieves a much faster O(D log(D/ε)) rate for cycles and tori, which is close to the theoretical lower bound. This is a substantial improvement.

4.  **Good Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It begins with a comprehensive review of the consensus problem, distinguishing between diffusive and accelerated methods. This effectively situates the paper's contributions. The progression from the general Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, to its specialization for consensus, and finally to the auxiliary process for analysis, is clear and easy to follow.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is performed for the quadratic case of network averaging. While this is a canonical and important problem, the Min-Sum framework is applicable to much more general convex optimization problems. The paper concludes by motivating future work in this direction, but as it stands, it is unclear how or if this acceleration technique generalizes beyond quadratic objectives. The linearity of the updates in Algorithm 2 is critical to the analysis, and this property is lost for general functions `φv`.

2.  **Practicality of Parameter Selection:** The accelerated rate in Theorem 1 is achieved by setting the splitting parameter `Γ = γW`, where `γ` depends on `ρW`, the second largest eigenvalue in magnitude of `W`. This is a global property of the graph. The paper does not discuss how a node in a distributed system would obtain this value in order to set its local parameters. While this is a common requirement for many accelerated distributed methods, the lack of discussion on this practical aspect is a minor weakness.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the claims would have been further strengthened by including even simple numerical experiments. A simulation on a cycle or grid graph comparing the convergence of the proposed method against a standard diffusive method (e.g., Metropolis-Hastings) and the method from [27] would have provided a compelling visual confirmation of the claimed square-root speedup.

### Summary of evaluation:

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound with a novel and effective proof technique. The work is complete for the problem it sets out to solve (network averaging).
*   **Clarity:** High. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with adequate background and a logical flow of arguments.
*   **Originality:** High. The central contribution—connecting Min-Sum Splitting to accelerated first-order methods and providing the first rate analysis—is highly original. It synthesizes ideas from different communities in a novel way.
*   **Significance:** High. The results are important for both the message-passing and distributed optimization communities. It establishes Min-Sum as a competitive framework for accelerated consensus and provides a new perspective on how such acceleration can be achieved.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The novel connection it establishes between Belief Propagation-style algorithms and mainstream accelerated optimization methods is insightful and likely to have a lasting impact. The weaknesses, primarily the focus on the quadratic case and the lack of experiments, are outweighed by the strength, novelty, and importance of the theoretical results.
