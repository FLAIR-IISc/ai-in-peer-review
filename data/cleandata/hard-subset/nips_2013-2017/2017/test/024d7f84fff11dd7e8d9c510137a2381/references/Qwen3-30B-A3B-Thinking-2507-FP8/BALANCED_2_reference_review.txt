

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

## Summary
This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for distributed consensus problems, establishing convergence rates that achieve a square-root improvement over traditional diffusive methods. The authors demonstrate that Min-Sum Splitting can be viewed as a process on a lifted space (like lifted Markov chains) and as a multi-step method (like accelerated gradient methods), connecting two previously separate lines of research. The key theoretical contribution is the analysis showing that with proper parameter tuning (δ=1 and Γ=γW), Min-Sum Splitting achieves O(D log(D/ε)) convergence time for cycle graphs and tori, compared to the O(D² log(1/ε)) of diffusive methods.

The paper relates well to NIPS work on distributed optimization, particularly the literature on consensus algorithms (e.g., [7, 27, 33]), lifted Markov chains [37], and accelerated first-order methods [13]. It distinguishes itself from prior Min-Sum work [27] by providing convergence rate analysis for the splitting variant rather than just convergence guarantees.

## Strengths
1. **Novel theoretical connection**: The paper's most significant contribution is establishing the conceptual link between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step accelerated methods. This connection is insightful and opens new theoretical pathways for distributed optimization.

2. **Rigorous convergence analysis**: The paper provides the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, using an elegant auxiliary process technique to transform the analysis from directed edges to nodes. The proof technique is well-motivated and technically sound.

3. **Clear theoretical contribution**: The paper explicitly shows the square-root improvement in convergence time (O(D) vs O(D²)) for important graph topologies like cycles and tori, which is a significant theoretical advance over existing methods.

4. **Strong contextualization**: The paper thoroughly reviews related work, clearly distinguishing itself from previous Min-Sum approaches [27] and connecting to broader literature on accelerated distributed methods.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of empirical validation**: The paper is purely theoretical with no experimental results to demonstrate the practical performance gains. Without empirical validation, it's difficult to assess the practical significance of the theoretical improvements, especially regarding implementation complexity and communication overhead.

2. **Limited scope**: The analysis is restricted to the quadratic consensus problem (network averaging), which is a special case. The paper doesn't address whether the acceleration extends to general convex optimization problems, which would be important for broader applicability.

3. **Global information requirement**: The optimal parameter choice (γ = 2/(1 + √(1-ρ_W²))) depends on the global spectral property ρ_W of the graph, which is impractical to compute in distributed settings without centralized knowledge. This limits the algorithm's real-world deployability.

4. **Implementation details missing**: The paper doesn't discuss communication complexity, storage requirements, or how to handle dynamic networks, which are critical considerations for distributed algorithms.

## Recommendation
The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of distributed optimization algorithms. However, the lack of empirical validation and the limited scope (quadratic case only) represent significant weaknesses for a NIPS submission, which typically values both theoretical and empirical contributions.

**Recommendation: Reject**

## Constructive Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Add empirical validation**: Include experiments on standard graph topologies (cycles, tori, random geometric graphs) comparing Min-Sum Splitting to diffusive methods and other accelerated algorithms. This would validate the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical relevance.

2. **Expand scope**: Extend the analysis to general convex problems beyond quadratic objectives, which would significantly increase the paper's significance.

3. **Address practical implementation**: Discuss how to compute the global parameters (like ρ_W) in a distributed manner or develop a decentralized parameter tuning strategy.

4. **Compare to recent ADMM-based methods**: The paper mentions a connection to ADMM in Remark 1 but doesn't develop it. A comparison with distributed ADMM approaches [3, 43] would strengthen the paper's position in the literature.

5. **Include communication complexity analysis**: Discuss how the communication requirements of Min-Sum Splitting compare to other methods, as this is critical for distributed systems.

The theoretical insights are valuable and could form the basis of a strong paper with empirical validation and broader applicability. The current version is a solid theoretical contribution but lacks the practical dimension that would make it compelling for NIPS.
