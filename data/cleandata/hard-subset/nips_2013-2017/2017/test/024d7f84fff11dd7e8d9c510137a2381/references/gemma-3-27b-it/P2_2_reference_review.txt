## Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors demonstrate that, with a proper parameter tuning, Min-Sum Splitting achieves accelerated convergence—specifically, a square-root improvement over diffusive methods—reaching a rate of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle and toroidal graphs, approaching the optimal rate of O(D log(1/ε)).  They establish a connection between Min-Sum splitting and both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, highlighting its novelty and potential impact. The core of their analysis involves constructing an auxiliary linear process to track the algorithm’s evolution, simplifying the convergence analysis.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents a novel analysis of Min-Sum Splitting, a relatively unexplored algorithm, and establishes its accelerated convergence properties for the consensus problem. The connection drawn between Min-Sum, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated optimization methods is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The analysis is theoretically sound, with a clear mathematical formulation and a rigorous proof strategy using an auxiliary process.  The derivation of convergence rates appears well-justified.
*   **Significance:** Achieving a square-root improvement in convergence rate is significant, particularly for large-scale consensus problems. The results are relevant to a wide range of applications in distributed optimization, sensor networks, and multi-agent systems.
*   **Clarity (mostly):** The paper is generally well-structured. The introduction provides sufficient background and motivation. The connection to existing work is clearly laid out.  The key contributions are summarized effectively.
* **Connection to Prior Work**: The authors carefully place their work within the broader context of convergence analysis for consensus algorithms and highlight how their results build on and improve upon previous approaches, like those in [27].

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity (Technical details):** While the overall structure is good, the technical details in Sections 5 and 6 (introducing the auxiliary algorithms and their corresponding matrices) are dense and difficult to follow without a very strong background in message-passing and graph theory. More intuitive explanations or intermediate steps would enhance understanding.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper relies entirely on theoretical analysis. While rigorous, the lack of experimental validation leaves open the question of practical performance and the sensitivity of the results to the specific graph structures. A small-scale simulation to demonstrate the accelerated convergence would strengthen the paper.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the authors identify a crucial parameter tuning (δ and Γ), the practical implications of choosing these parameters in real-world scenarios are not discussed.  The conditions for achieving the accelerated rate might be difficult to verify in practice.
*   **Repetitive Background:** The lengthy background material in the introduction and Sections 3 & 4 feels somewhat redundant. Condensing this could improve the flow and focus on the core contributions. The extensive literature review, while thorough, can be distracting.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** 8/10 – The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by analysis. The derivation of convergence rates appears rigorous, and the proposed approach is well-motivated.
*   **Clarity:** 6/10 – The overall structure is clear, but the technical details require significant effort to understand. More intuitive explanations and examples would improve readability.
*   **Originality:** 9/10 – The analysis of Min-Sum Splitting and its connection to other accelerated methods is highly original.
*   **Significance:** 8/10 – The accelerated convergence rates are important, and the results have the potential to impact various applications.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel theoretical result with significant implications for distributed consensus algorithms.
*   The analysis is rigorous and well-motivated.
*   The connection to existing work in lifted Markov chains and accelerated optimization is insightful.
*   The potential for performance improvement in large-scale distributed systems is substantial.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of experimental validation.
*   The technical details are challenging to follow.
*   The practical implications of parameter tuning are not discussed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of distributed optimization. While the technical details are dense and the lack of experimental validation is a concern, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and potential significance of the results justify acceptance. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, specifically focusing on improving the clarity of the technical sections and discussing the practical considerations of parameter tuning. Adding even a small simulation would greatly strengthen the paper.
