This paper presents a novel analysis of Min-Sum Splitting applied to the consensus problem, demonstrating accelerated convergence rates compared to traditional diffusive methods. The core idea – tuning splitting parameters to achieve subdiffusive scaling – is genuinely interesting and represents a significant departure from standard analysis of message passing algorithms. The connection drawn to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods provides valuable context and deepens the understanding of the algorithm’s behavior. While the presentation is dense and requires careful reading, the authors successfully establish a theoretical framework for analyzing this algorithm.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The central contribution – demonstrating and analytically establishing accelerated convergence for Min-Sum Splitting – is highly novel.  Existing work (e.g., [27]) highlights the divergence of standard Min-Sum but lacks a rigorous analysis of the convergence rates achievable with splitting.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, including the introduction of an auxiliary process to track algorithm evolution and deriving convergence rates. The connection to shift-register methods is particularly strong.
* **Interdisciplinary Connections:**  Linking Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chains *and* accelerated first-order methods is a significant achievement, offering a broader perspective on the algorithm’s underlying principles.
* **Clear Improvement:** Demonstrating a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods is a tangible and valuable result, particularly for large-scale problems.
* **Well-motivated Problem:** The consensus problem is a fundamental building block in distributed systems and machine learning, increasing the potential impact of this work.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Density & Accessibility:** The paper is mathematically dense. While this is acceptable for a theoretical work, it could benefit from more intuitive explanations of key concepts and a gentler introduction to the auxiliary process.  Readers unfamiliar with message passing or lifted Markov chains may find the initial sections challenging.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses heavily on theoretical analysis. While a full-scale experimental validation may be beyond the scope, some illustrative examples or simulations demonstrating the improved convergence in practice would strengthen the argument. A comparison against established accelerated methods (e.g., those mentioned in Section 4) would be valuable.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** While a convergence rate is established, the sensitivity of this rate to the choice of `γ` (dependent on the spectral radius) isn't fully explored. Practical implications of choosing an optimal `γ` in real-world graph scenarios would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of quadratic objective functions limits the direct applicability of the results. A discussion of potential extensions to other objective function classes would be worthwhile.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **High Novelty:**  The core idea and theoretical results are genuinely novel and represent a significant advance in understanding Min-Sum algorithms.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The analysis is rigorous and provides a solid basis for the claimed convergence rates.
* **Potential Impact:** The results have the potential to improve the performance of distributed optimization algorithms in a variety of applications.
* **Successfully connects disparate fields:** The bridging of message passing, lifted Markov chains and accelerated optimization methods is impressive.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* **Limited Practical Validation:** The lack of experimental results weakens the practical impact of the theoretical findings.
* **Density & Accessibility:** The paper’s complexity may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. Despite some minor weaknesses, the novelty and theoretical depth of the work are exceptional. The authors have identified a significant improvement in the convergence of Min-Sum algorithms and provided a rigorous analysis to support their claims. The theoretical results are compelling, and the connections to other fields are insightful. The lack of extensive empirical validation is a concern, but it is outweighed by the strength of the theoretical contribution.  The paper pushes the boundaries of our understanding of message-passing algorithms and has the potential to inspire further research in this area.  Minor revisions to improve clarity and accessibility would be beneficial but are not essential for acceptance.
