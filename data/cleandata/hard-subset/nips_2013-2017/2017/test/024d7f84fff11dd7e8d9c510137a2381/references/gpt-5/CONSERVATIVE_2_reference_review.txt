Summary
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting (a reparametrized form of Min-Sum/Belief Propagation) for distributed consensus/averaging. For quadratic objectives (network averaging), the authors show:
- With an appropriate parametrization, Min-Sum Splitting reduces to a linear message-passing scheme on directed edges; with quadratic messages, the updates track linear recursions on the edge parameters.
- They construct an auxiliary node-based, multi-step linear process whose evolution matches the edge-based process for suitable initialization. This reduces the analysis to the spectral properties of an n×n matrix, yielding tractable convergence bounds.
- For symmetric W with W1=1, choosing Γ=γW and δ=1 with γ tuned from ρ(W−11^T/n), they prove convergence to the average with an accelerated rate comparable to “shift-register” methods, i.e., a square-root improvement over diffusive consensus on geometric graphs: O(D log(D/ε)) vs. the standard O(D^2 log(1/ε)).
- They emphasize conceptual connections: Min-Sum Splitting as a process on a lifted space (directed edges) akin to lifted, non-reversible Markov chains, and as a multi-step linear method akin to momentum/shift-register acceleration.

Relation to prior work: Ordinary Min-Sum fails on loopy graphs; variants with soft barriers and analyses in regular graphs were studied in prior work (e.g., Moallemi & Van Roy). Accelerated consensus via liftings and multi-step methods (Chebyshev, heavy-ball, Nesterov, shift-register) is well-developed; the paper claims the first convergence-rate result for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus and an explicit link to these accelerations.

Strengths
- The auxiliary node-based formulation (Algorithm 3) that exactly tracks the edge-based Min-Sum Splitting dynamics is technically neat and helps make the analysis tractable.
- Theorem 1 provides a clean spectral-rate characterization and recovers the shift-register acceleration rate for averaging. The square-root improvement on geometric graphs is consistent with known optimal behaviors of multi-step methods.
- Establishing an explicit bridge between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted chains, and multi-step acceleration could be useful for cross-fertilization across communities.
- The paper is largely theoretical and, if proofs check out, provides a self-contained convergence-rate result for this BP variant on consensus.

Weaknesses
- Scope is narrow: results are restricted to quadratic consensus (averaging). No guarantees are provided for general convex φ_v or for equality constraints incorporated as hard barriers (a setting where ordinary Min-Sum conditions fail). The abstract and intro suggest broader implications, but the theorem is for a very special case.
- Reliance on global tuning: accelerated rates require γ as a function of ρ(W−11^T/n). This is global and typically unknown in distributed settings; the paper offers no distributed estimation or adaptive tuning procedure, nor robustness analysis to mis-specification. In practice, this undermines the supposed advantage.
- Limited algorithmic novelty: Theorem 1 essentially shows that with a specific δ, Γ the dynamics coincide with a standard shift-register scheme. If the resulting K matches known accelerated iterations, the gain beyond a conceptual re-derivation via Min-Sum Splitting is modest. The paper does not articulate concrete benefits of using Min-Sum Splitting over directly using shift-register consensus (which already achieves the same rate without the edge-lift).
- No empirical validation: There are no simulations to confirm constants, robustness, or practical performance on various graphs (cycles, grids, random geometric graphs), nor comparisons to strong baselines (Chebyshev acceleration, Nesterov-type methods, gradient tracking/EXTRA/Exact Diffusion, distributed ADMM). Given the incremental nature of the contribution, empirical evidence would help substantiate significance.
- Technical clarity: Some expressions are hard to parse (e.g., definition of K∞, constants in bounds). Important conditions (e.g., positivity conditions on R-iterates) are not demonstrated in the main text for the chosen initialization. The dependence on W (which may have negative entries) is introduced but practical selection of W is not discussed. There are several typographical issues that distract.

Quality
- The core idea and high-level proof strategy are plausible and align with known analyses of multi-step consensus. However, the main proof details are relegated to the supplement; correctness of key identities (e.g., Proposition 2 equivalence, spectral radius formula for K−K∞, and the constants in the error bound) cannot be verified from the main text. The reliance on δ=1 and a carefully tuned Γ limits generality. There is no discussion of asynchronous, time-varying, or noisy settings.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but notation is occasionally overloaded, some matrices are defined tersely, and there are editorial glitches. The presentation would benefit from a streamlined derivation of K and K∞, explicit statement of all assumptions, and a short example on a simple graph.

Originality
- The auxiliary-process reduction for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus and the explicit rate statement appear new. The acceleration mechanism and rates themselves are not new; the main novelty is the connection that Min-Sum Splitting can recover those rates when tuned with global information.

Significance
- For practitioners, the lack of a practical, distributed tuning strategy and absence of experiments limit immediate impact. Conceptually, the connection may spur further theoretical work on BP-like algorithms with acceleration, but the present contribution is incremental relative to established accelerated consensus methods.

Questions and suggestions
- Can the authors provide a distributed or adaptive scheme to set γ (e.g., using local spectral estimates or backtracking) and analyze robustness to tuning errors?
- Clarify the exact relationship between the proposed iteration and standard shift-register/Chebyshev methods: is it algebraically identical after change of variables?
- Provide experiments comparing to Metropolis, Chebyshev, Nesterov, shift-register, and ADMM/EXTRA on cycles, grids, random geometric graphs, and expanders; include sensitivity to γ, communication/memory overhead, and asynchrony robustness.
- Tighten and check the expression for K∞ and constants in the error bound; prove the positivity condition for the given initialization.
- Discuss extension beyond quadratic consensus: do analogous results hold for general strongly convex φ_v or constrained formulations?

Pros for acceptance
- Clean theoretical link between Min-Sum Splitting and accelerated consensus; first rate result for this BP variant on consensus.
- Potential to inspire further work connecting BP, liftings, and multi-step optimization.

Cons for acceptance
- Narrow scope (quadratic averaging only), no experiments, and reliance on global tuning; algorithmic novelty and practical significance are limited.

Recommendation: Weak reject

Confidence: High (familiar with accelerated consensus, BP on graphs, and lifted Markov chains).
