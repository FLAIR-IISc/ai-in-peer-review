This paper tackles a fundamental problem – accelerating consensus – through a novel application and analysis of Min-Sum splitting. The core idea, connecting Min-Sum to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated optimization methods, is genuinely exciting and represents a significant departure from standard approaches. The theoretical result establishing a square-root improvement over diffusive rates is a strong contribution, and the connection to shift-register methods is particularly insightful. The paper is well-motivated, situating the work within the context of both theoretical limitations of existing methods and recent advancements.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core contribution – analyzing and accelerating Min-Sum via splitting for consensus – is highly novel. The bridging of different theoretical frameworks (lifted chains, accelerated optimization) is a standout feature.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The derivation of convergence rates and the identification of a square-root speedup are significant theoretical achievements.
*   **Technical Depth:** The paper demonstrates a strong grasp of relevant theoretical tools, including spectral graph theory and analysis of linear dynamical systems.
*   **Significance:** Achieving subdiffusive rates for consensus has broad implications for distributed optimization and machine learning applications. The potential to avoid the construction of lifted graphs, as highlighted by the authors, is a compelling advantage.
*   **Clarity (Generally Good):** The introduction does a good job of setting the stage and motivating the problem. The exposition is generally clear, although dense in places.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Validation Limited to Theoretical Analysis:** The paper relies exclusively on theoretical analysis. While the analysis is strong, the lack of even simulated experiments to corroborate the derived rates is a significant drawback. A figure illustrating the convergence speed for cycle graphs or tori, compared to existing methods, would greatly strengthen the paper.
*   **Parameter Tuning Complexity:** The optimal choice of parameters (δ and Γ) depends on spectral properties of the graph, which may be difficult to estimate in practice. A discussion of practical considerations for parameter selection would be valuable.
*   **Clarity (Technical Density):** Some sections, particularly those detailing the auxiliary message-passing scheme, are quite dense and require careful reading. More intuitive explanations would improve accessibility.
*   **Related Work Discussion Could Be Expanded:** While related work is mentioned, a more in-depth discussion of recent advances in accelerated consensus algorithms (beyond the cited references) would be beneficial. Specifically, recent work leveraging Nesterov’s acceleration would be relevant.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach to accelerating consensus.
*   The square-root speedup is a significant result with broad implications.
*   The connection to existing theoretical frameworks provides valuable insights.
*   The paper addresses a fundamental problem in distributed optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical validation raises concerns about the practical applicability of the results.
*   The technical complexity might limit the accessibility of the work.
*   The dependence on graph spectral properties for parameter tuning could pose challenges in real-world scenarios.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the lack of empirical validation and some areas where clarity could be improved, I strongly recommend acceptance. The novelty and theoretical depth of this work are exceptional. The square-root speedup represents a significant advance, and the bridging of different theoretical perspectives is particularly insightful. While I would have preferred to see some simulations, the potential impact of this work justifies overlooking this limitation. The paper aligns well with the spirit of NIPS – pushing the boundaries of knowledge with bold, theoretically grounded approaches. I anticipate that this work will stimulate further research in accelerated distributed optimization.



**Rating:** Accept (Strongly)
