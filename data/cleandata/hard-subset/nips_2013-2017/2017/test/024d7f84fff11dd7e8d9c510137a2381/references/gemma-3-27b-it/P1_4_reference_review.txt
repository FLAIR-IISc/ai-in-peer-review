## Peer Review for "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of Min-Sum splitting algorithms, specifically in the context of the consensus problem. It establishes convergence rates and demonstrates acceleration compared to classical diffusive methods. The paper is technically sound, clearly written (though dense), and offers a novel perspective on the interplay between Min-Sum, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated optimization methods. While the theoretical focus limits direct practical demonstration, the results are potentially impactful for distributed optimization and inference. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Summary:** The paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum splitting algorithm for solving the consensus problem. It demonstrates that by carefully tuning splitting parameters, the algorithm achieves convergence rates that are provably faster – specifically, a square-root improvement – compared to traditional diffusive methods. This acceleration is linked to connections with both lifted Markov chain techniques and accelerated first-order optimization methods, framing Min-Sum splitting as a bridge between these fields.  The authors achieve this by introducing an auxiliary linear process to track the algorithm’s evolution, simplifying the analysis.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Results:** The core result – establishing convergence rates and demonstrating acceleration – is a substantial contribution. The mathematical analysis appears rigorous and well-motivated.
*   **Novel Connection of Concepts:** The paper skillfully connects Min-Sum schemes with both lifted Markov chains and accelerated optimization techniques, offering a unified perspective and highlighting the algorithm’s strengths. This is a particularly valuable insight.
*   **Clear Derivation of Auxiliary Process:** Introducing the auxiliary linear process is a clever analytical tool that simplifies the analysis and provides a more tractable way to characterize convergence.
*   **Well-Written and Organized:** Despite the technical depth, the paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The authors provide sufficient background and motivation for each step of the analysis.
*   **Thorough Related Work:** The paper demonstrates a comprehensive understanding of existing literature on consensus algorithms, including diffusive methods, accelerated schemes, and lifted Markov chains.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses entirely on theoretical analysis. While this is justifiable given the complexity of the problem, some limited empirical validation (e.g., on small graphs) could strengthen the claims and provide intuition for the practical impact of the results.
*   **Density of Technical Detail:**  The paper is quite dense with mathematical notation. While necessary for conveying the technical details, this can make it challenging for readers not deeply familiar with the field to fully grasp the nuances. More intuitive explanations alongside the equations would be helpful.
*   **Dependency on Previous Work ([27]):**  The results build significantly on the work in [27]. While properly cited, a more detailed discussion of the differences and advancements over that work would further clarify the contribution.
* **Scope & Generality:** The analysis is performed for a specific quadratic objective function. While a common setting for consensus, it isn't clear how well these results generalize to other objective functions.

**Detailed Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

*   **Quality:** Excellent. The technical analysis appears rigorous, and the claims are well-supported by mathematical derivations. The paper is a complete piece of work in terms of its theoretical contributions. The authors acknowledge limitations.
*   **Clarity:** Good. The writing is clear and logical, but the density of mathematical notation can be a barrier to understanding. Adding more intuitive explanations would improve clarity.
*   **Originality:** High. The connection between Min-Sum splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated optimization is novel and insightful. The convergence rate analysis is also a significant advancement.
*   **Significance:** High. The results have the potential to impact the design and analysis of distributed optimization algorithms, particularly in scenarios where accelerated convergence is crucial. The theoretical framework could inspire new algorithmic developments.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical results establishing convergence rates and demonstrating acceleration.
*   Novel and insightful connection between different areas of research.
*   Well-written and organized presentation.
*   Addresses a challenging problem in distributed optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of empirical validation.
*   Density of technical detail may limit accessibility.
*   Limited discussion of differences from related work [27].



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide any insights into the practical implications of these theoretical results?  Even a small-scale simulation demonstrating the acceleration on a specific graph topology would be valuable.
2.  Can you elaborate on how these results might be extended to other objective functions beyond the quadratic case?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed discussion of the key differences and advancements compared to the work in [27]? Specifically, how does your analysis simplify or extend their findings?

**Overall Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions.** The paper presents a compelling theoretical contribution with significant potential impact. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above (primarily adding some empirical illustration and clarifying the connection to [27]) would further strengthen the paper and enhance its value to the NIPS community.
