Summary
This paper analyzes the Min-Sum Splitting (MSS) message-passing algorithm for distributed consensus. The authors focus on the quadratic averaging case and show: (i) with an appropriate parametrization, MSS reduces to a linear message-passing scheme on directed edges; (ii) by introducing an auxiliary node-based “multi-step” process, the evolution of MSS can be tracked by a 2n-dimensional linear system; (iii) a specific choice of splitting parameters, δ=1 and Γ=γW with γ tuned to the spectrum of a symmetric averaging matrix W, yields accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence, matching the asymptotic rates of shift-register (Chebyshev-type) methods; specifically, the asymptotic contraction factor improves from ρW to ρK = sqrt((1−√(1−ρW^2))/(1+√(1−ρW^2))), leading to O(D log(D/ε)) iterations on cycles and tori; and (iv) conceptual connections are drawn between MSS, lifted non-reversible Markov chains (messages on directed edges) and multi-step first-order methods (memory on nodes). The proof technique leverages a new auxiliary linear process on nodes that simplifies spectral analysis compared to edge-based analyses in prior work.

Relation to prior work
• Ordinary Min-Sum (Gaussian BP) convergence on graphs with cycles is delicate; for convex quadratics, diagonal dominance-type conditions are sufficient, but consensus constraints fall outside standard sufficient conditions. MSS (Ruozzi & Tatikonda) generalizes Min-Sum via reparametrization; prior work showed improved convergence domains but, to date, no rates for MSS.
• For consensus, classical linear iterations xt=Wxt−1 are diffusive; best symmetric choices (including Metropolis-Hastings or SDP-optimized W) yield mixing times Θ(D^2 log(1/ε)) on geometric graphs. Accelerations via (a) lifted non-reversible chains and (b) multi-step/shift-register/Nesterov methods are known and can achieve O(D log(1/ε)) up to constants/log factors.
• Previous Min-Sum consensus (Moallemi & Van Roy, 2006) proposed a soft-constraint variant, analyzed on d-regular graphs with dynamics on directed edges; rates tied to Cesàro mixing on edges. This paper generalizes beyond d-regular graphs and provides explicit accelerated rates via MSS.
• Connections to ADMM/lifting and accelerated first-order methods are noted (e.g., França & Bento; Ghadimi et al.; Xiao & Boyd; Golub & Varga).

Strengths
• Clear, novel contribution: first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, even in the restricted consensus setting.
• Technical novelty: the auxiliary node-based linear system (Algorithm 3) that exactly tracks MSS (Algorithm 2) under suitable initialization. This reduces analysis from 2m to 2n dimensions and ties rate to the spectrum of Γ (and W).
• Acceleration result (Theorem 1) is elegant and sharp: the tuned MSS exactly recovers the shift-register contraction factor; the derived O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori improves over diffusive O(D^2 log(1/ε)) and approaches known lower bounds.
• Conceptual unification: the paper convincingly relates MSS to both lifted Markov chains (directionality on edges) and multi-step methods (memory on nodes).
• Broad relevance: message-passing/BP-inspired acceleration for consensus is of interest to distributed optimization, signal processing, and ML systems.

Weaknesses and questions
• Scope: results are limited to the quadratic averaging problem with synchronous updates on undirected, fixed graphs. No extension to general convex φv, inequality constraints, asynchronous or time-varying networks, or directed graphs.
• Practicality of tuning: acceleration depends on γ determined by ρ(W−11^T/n), a global spectral quantity. The paper does not discuss how to estimate or track this in a distributed fashion, nor robustness to mis-specification.
• Empirical validation: no experiments to corroborate rates, constants, or robustness, or to compare against Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, Nesterov, ADMM, or lifted-chain implementations.
• Clarity/notation: the exposition is dense. There are minor indexing/numbering inconsistencies (e.g., K̂ definition labeled (4) but later cited as (5)), and footnote markers in text. The role of φvw in consensus (where the objective is separable in nodes and constraints are equalities) could be clarified when deriving Algorithm 2. Assumptions ensuring positivity/convexity of beliefs (e.g., conditions on R̂t) should be stated explicitly for the chosen initialization R̂0=r̂0=0.
• Originality vs. reduction: the key accelerated rate rides on the observation that, under a specific parametrization, MSS collapses to a known shift-register dynamic. While valuable, the novelty lies more in the identification/analysis within MSS than in a new accelerated consensus algorithm per se.
• Constants and finite-time bounds: results are asymptotic; the paper provides a bound via ‖(K−K∞)t‖ but does not give explicit finite-time constants or comparisons to best-known polynomial filters/Chebyshev bounds.

Quality
The technical claims appear sound and consistent with known spectral acceleration phenomena. The auxiliary-system derivation and the mapping to shift-register dynamics are plausible and well-motivated; full proofs are deferred to the supplement. The lack of experiments and limited discussion of robustness and implementability are the main quality gaps.

Clarity
Generally clear but compact; several places would benefit from tightening notation, fixing references/equation numbers, and adding intuition. A short proof sketch of Theorem 1 in the main text would help.

Originality
Moderate-to-strong. Establishing rates for MSS and the auxiliary-process analysis are new. The acceleration mechanism itself matches known shift-register results, but casting it within MSS and linking to lifting is original.

Significance
Good. This advances understanding of when and how BP-like splitting can be tuned to achieve near-optimal consensus rates, and introduces tools that may extend to other MSS applications.

Suggestions for improvement
• Add experiments on cycles, grids, random geometric graphs comparing MSS-splitting to Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, Nesterov, ADMM, and lifted chains; include robustness to γ mis-tuning.
• Discuss distributed estimation of ρ(W) (e.g., power/Lanczos or polynomial filters) and adaptive tuning.
• Clarify conditions ensuring convex beliefs and positivity constraints in Propositions 1–2; state precise assumptions under R̂0=r̂0=0.
• Fix minor typos and equation numbering; provide a brief proof sketch of Theorem 1.
• Comment on extensions to general convex objectives, directed/time-varying graphs, and asynchronous updates.

Arguments for acceptance
• First rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting; principled acceleration with clear spectral improvement.
• Conceptual bridge between BP-style message passing, lifted chains, and multi-step methods.
• Simple linear-time, local message updates with minimal per-edge state.

Arguments against acceptance
• Narrow scope (quadratic consensus only), no experiments, and reliance on global spectral tuning.
• Acceleration essentially recovers known shift-register rates; limited algorithmic novelty beyond the MSS mapping.

Recommendation
Weak accept.

Reviewer confidence
High on technical background in consensus/acceleration; moderate on specific MSS literature details pending supplementary proofs.
