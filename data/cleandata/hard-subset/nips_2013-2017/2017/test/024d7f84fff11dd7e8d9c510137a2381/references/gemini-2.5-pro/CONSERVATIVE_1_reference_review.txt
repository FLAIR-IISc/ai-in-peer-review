### Summary

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, a generalization of the standard Min-Sum message-passing protocol, for the specific problem of distributed consensus (network averaging). The authors demonstrate that the standard Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge on graphs with cycles for this problem, but the generalized Splitting version can be tuned to converge. The main contribution is a theoretical analysis showing that with a specific parameterization, Min-Sum Splitting achieves an accelerated, "subdiffusive" convergence rate. The authors establish this by introducing an auxiliary process that tracks the algorithm's evolution on the graph's nodes, rather than its edges. This allows them to show that the algorithm's dynamics can be made equivalent to those of established accelerated methods, specifically shift-register methods, thereby achieving a square-root improvement in convergence time over standard diffusive methods for certain graph topologies. The paper also draws a conceptual link between the acceleration mechanism in Min-Sum Splitting and those in lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order optimization methods.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**

The theoretical analysis appears to be the main strength of this paper. The derivation of the linear message-passing updates for the quadratic case (Algorithm 2) and the subsequent introduction of the auxiliary node-based process (Algorithm 3) is a clever technique that simplifies the spectral analysis. The central result, Theorem 1, rigorously connects the algorithm's convergence rate to that of known shift-register methods, leveraging existing analysis from that domain.

However, the paper's quality is severely undermined by a complete lack of empirical validation. The claims of accelerated convergence are purely asymptotic and theoretical. There are no experiments to demonstrate how the method performs in practice, either on synthetic or real-world graph topologies. Without such evidence, it is impossible to assess the practical relevance of the claimed acceleration. For instance, asymptotic rates can hide large constant factors, and the performance in the pre-asymptotic regime, which is often what matters in practice, remains unknown. Furthermore, the paper does not compare its proposed method against any established baselines for accelerated consensus, such as distributed ADMM or methods based on Nesterov's acceleration [30, 38].

**Clarity:**

The paper is well-structured and the arguments are laid out logically. The introduction and background sections effectively situate the work within the context of both Min-Sum algorithms and the consensus problem. The mathematical derivations are dense but follow a clear path from the general algorithm to the specific result.

**Originality:**

The paper is, to my knowledge, the first to provide a formal convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. The conceptual connection drawn between the reparameterization in a belief propagation framework and acceleration techniques in convex optimization and lifted Markov chains is novel and insightful.

However, the practical originality of the proposed *algorithm* is questionable. The core result of Theorem 1 is that by setting the splitting parameters `δ=1` and `Γ=γW`, the algorithm's dynamics become identical to those of a shift-register method. While this is an interesting theoretical connection, it implies that the paper has not proposed a fundamentally new accelerated algorithm, but rather has shown that a more complex message-passing framework can be configured to emulate a known, and arguably simpler, one. The paper does not demonstrate any benefits of this Min-Sum formulation over a direct implementation of the shift-register update.

**Significance:**

The significance of this work is mixed. On the one hand, the theoretical bridge it builds between message-passing, lifting, and multi-step momentum methods is a valuable conceptual contribution that may inspire future research.

On the other hand, the practical significance is limited. First, the proposed method's acceleration relies on setting the parameter `γ` based on `ρW`, a global spectral property of the graph matrix. This is a strong assumption for a distributed algorithm, as computing this value typically requires centralized knowledge or significant communication overhead. The paper does not address this critical practical limitation, which is a known challenge for many accelerated methods. Second, the analysis is restricted to the simple quadratic network averaging problem. A key appeal of the Min-Sum framework is its applicability to more general optimization problems. The paper provides no evidence that this acceleration mechanism extends beyond the unconstrained quadratic case, which significantly curtails the potential impact. Without a demonstrated advantage over existing, well-understood accelerated methods, it is unclear who would use this approach.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  Provides the first theoretical convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.
2.  Establishes a novel and insightful theoretical connection between a belief propagation-style algorithm and established accelerated optimization techniques.
3.  The proof technique, using an auxiliary process on the nodes to analyze a process on the edges, is elegant and could be of independent interest.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Total lack of experiments:** The paper makes strong claims about accelerated rates without any empirical evidence to support them or to compare against standard, established baselines (e.g., Nesterov-based methods, ADMM). This is a critical omission for a paper on algorithmic performance.
2.  **Limited practical novelty:** The main result shows that the proposed scheme recovers the dynamics of a known accelerated method (shift-registers). It is not clear that a new, practical algorithm is being proposed, but rather a new interpretation of an old one.
3.  **Unaddressed practical limitations:** The algorithm requires global graph information (`ρW`) for optimal tuning, a major barrier to implementation in truly distributed settings, which the authors do not discuss.
4.  **Limited scope:** The analysis is restricted to the basic network averaging problem. The potential for generalizing the acceleration to other problems, which would be a major advantage of the Min-Sum framework, is left entirely as an open question.

### Conclusion

While the theoretical connections presented in this paper are interesting, the work feels incomplete and falls short of the standards expected for a NIPS publication. The primary contribution is showing that a complex message-passing scheme can be tuned to reproduce the behavior of a known accelerated algorithm for consensus. However, the lack of any experimental validation makes it impossible to assess the practical utility of this finding. The unaddressed reliance on global information for parameter tuning further detracts from its applicability. In its current form, the paper is more of a theoretical curiosity than a demonstrable advance in the state of the art for distributed optimization. I recommend rejection, but I would encourage the authors to add a thorough experimental section comparing their method against established accelerated consensus algorithms and to discuss the practical issue of parameter selection.
