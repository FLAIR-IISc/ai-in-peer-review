This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors build upon previous work showing that splitting can lead to convergence in cases where standard Min-Sum fails, and aim to provide a theoretical analysis of its convergence speed. They establish a square-root improvement over diffusive methods and connect their results to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods.

The core idea – analyzing Min-Sum Splitting through an auxiliary linear process on the nodes of the graph – is clever and potentially insightful. The connection to shift-register methods and the resulting convergence rate are the main contributions. However, I find the presentation convoluted and the justification for many claims lacking in rigor.

**Strengths:**

*   **Potentially impactful result:** A square-root improvement in convergence rate is a non-trivial result, particularly for large-scale distributed systems.
*   **Connections to existing literature:** The authors attempt to situate their work within the broader landscape of accelerated consensus algorithms, relating it to lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods.
*   **Auxiliary process:** The introduction of an auxiliary process to analyze the algorithm is a reasonable approach, simplifying the analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of clarity and excessive detail:** The paper is dense and difficult to follow. The introduction spends a significant amount of space reviewing background material, much of which isn’t directly relevant to the core contribution. The notation is heavy, and the logical flow is often obscured by technical details.
*   **Insufficient empirical validation:** Despite claiming significant improvements, the paper lacks any experimental results. Demonstrating the practical benefits of Min-Sum Splitting, even on relatively small graphs, would substantially strengthen the claims. It’s concerning that a purely theoretical analysis is presented without any validation.
*   **Weak comparison to existing work:** The comparison to the work of [27] is superficial. While they claim to improve upon the results of [27], they don’t provide a clear and concise comparison of the convergence rates for specific graph topologies. The conjecture in [27] should be directly addressed with quantitative results, even if simulated.
*   **Overstated claims of novelty:** The connection to lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods feels somewhat forced. The authors claim to provide the "first connection" between these areas, but this feels overstated; the similarities are largely observational rather than deeply integrated into the analysis.
*   **Assumptions and parameter tuning:** The theoretical results rely on a specific choice of parameters (δ = 1 and Γ = γW) and conditions on the graph (W being symmetric). The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions is not adequately discussed. What happens when these assumptions are violated?
*   **Technical rigor:** Certain statements feel hand-wavy, and the proofs are relegated to the supplementary material, making it difficult to assess their correctness. A thorough review would require a deep dive into the supplementary material, which is beyond the scope of this review.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   The lack of experimental validation is a major concern.
*   The paper is overly dense and difficult to follow, hindering understanding and assessment.
*   The claims of novelty are overstated.
*   The technical details and rigor are questionable without a full examination of the supplementary material.

**For:**

*   The potential for a significant theoretical result (square-root improvement in convergence rate) is present.
*   The introduction of the auxiliary process is a reasonable analytical approach.
*   The connections to related work, even if superficial, provide some context.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the theoretical results might be sound, the lack of empirical validation, coupled with the clarity and rigor issues, makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of this work. The paper feels incomplete and relies too heavily on theoretical analysis without demonstrating its practical relevance. The connection to existing work could be strengthened through a more thorough quantitative comparison.  A significant rewrite, accompanied by experimental results, is needed before this work would be suitable for NIPS. The claims are too strong for the evidence presented.
