Summary
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting, a reparameterized variant of Min-Sum (Gaussian BP for quadratics), and establishes the first convergence-rate results for consensus/averaging. The main technical contributions are: (i) a linearization of Min-Sum Splitting for quadratic consensus that runs on directed edges; (ii) an auxiliary node-based two-step linear iteration that exactly tracks the edge-based dynamics’ output; (iii) a choice of splitting parameters (δ, Γ) that yields accelerated (subdiffusive) rates equivalent to classical shift-register/Chebyshev-accelerated consensus methods; and (iv) conceptual links between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted nonreversible Markov chains, and multi-step first-order acceleration. The main theorem shows that with δ=1 and Γ=γW, where W is a symmetric averaging matrix (W1=1) and γ depends on the spectral gap of W, the error contracts at rate ρK strictly smaller than ρW, giving a square-root improvement in the convergence time (O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori), while ordinary Min-Sum may diverge on loopy graphs.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on Moallemi–Van Roy and Ruozzi–Tatikonda’s analyses of (splitting) Min-Sum/BP and on “consensus propagation” [27], which showed standard Min-Sum fails on cycles and analyzed a softened scheme via a Markov chain on directed edges; rates were only characterized for cycles and conjectured for tori. It connects to accelerated consensus via multi-step methods (e.g., Xiao–Boyd, Golub–Varga, Young; and more recent works reviewed in [13,24]) and to lifted Markov chains [5,8,18,37]. The rate derived matches that of shift-register/Chebyshev acceleration and is close to Nesterov-type optimal O(D log(1/ε)) [30,36]. The auxiliary process that reduces an edge-based BP-like iteration to a node-based two-step system is reminiscent of walk-sum interpretations of GaBP but appears novel in this consensus/splitting context.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual bridge: The paper makes a compelling link between (i) directionality introduced by splitting in BP (akin to nonreversibility/lifting) and (ii) memory terms in accelerated multi-step consensus. This perspective is insightful for both communities.
- Technically sound rate: Theorem 1 provides an explicit spectral-rate analysis, including constants, showing square-root acceleration relative to diffusive methods, with clean specialization to cycles/tori.
- Methodological contribution: The auxiliary node-based two-step iteration that exactly tracks the edge-based Min-Sum Splitting outputs (Propositions 1–2) greatly simplifies analysis and may be reusable beyond consensus.
- Complements prior BP work: It demonstrates that, with appropriate splitting, a BP-style scheme not only converges on loopy graphs but attains near-optimal accelerated rates.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope limited to quadratic consensus: The acceleration result is proved only for the averaging (quadratic) case, with δ=1 and Γ=γW. It remains unclear whether similar rates hold for more general convex or constrained objectives where Min-Sum Splitting was originally motivated.
- Novelty vs. known accelerations: The achieved rate matches known shift-register/Chebyshev accelerations. The main novelty is the BP/splitting interpretation and the auxiliary reduction; the rate itself is not new for consensus. The paper could better delineate what is fundamentally new beyond this reinterpretation.
- Dependence on global information: The choice of γ requires knowledge of ρ(W−11^T/n), as in prior lifted/accelerated methods. Practical guidance for estimating γ, robustness to misspecification, or decentralized tuning heuristics is not discussed.
- Comparison and experiments: No empirical results are provided. Given the extensive literature on accelerated consensus (Chebyshev, Nesterov, ADMM/EXTRA/Exact Diffusion), empirical comparisons on cycles, grids, random geometric graphs, and expanders would clarify practical benefits and constants (and whether the edge-based messages add overhead relative to simple two-step node updates).
- Implementation details: While Proposition 2 shows equivalence of outputs between edge-based Algorithm 2 and node-based Algorithm 3 with specific initialization, the paper does not discuss which implementation is recommended in practice, communication/memory costs per iteration, or guarantees (e.g., positivity of denominators R^t) under all suggested parameter choices.
- Relation to ADMM-based acceleration and recent linear-time consensus results could be expanded. In particular, how does the proposed approach compare to EXTRA/Exact Diffusion or distributed ADMM in rate, constants, and information requirements?

Quality
The core theorem and reductions appear technically sound, consistent with classical spectral analyses of two-step methods. The mapping from edge to node iterates is carefully constructed. Some assumptions (e.g., positivity/convexity under division by Γvw, or precise conditions ensuring R^t>0) deserve a more explicit statement in the main text. Proofs are deferred to the supplement.

Clarity
The paper is dense but generally well-organized. Algorithm definitions and matrices K̂, K are clear, and the high-level narrative is coherent. There are minor editorial issues (references to Figure 1 without a figure; footnote markers embedded oddly; “st Conference…” artifact). It would help to explicitly state per-iteration costs and summarize assumptions on W and Γ in one place.

Originality
Moderate. The acceleration rate is not new, but the contribution is the first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and a novel connection to lifted/nonreversible chains and multi-step methods via an exact auxiliary reduction.

Significance
Moderate. For researchers in BP/message passing, the result is valuable: a principled way to obtain accelerated consensus within a Min-Sum framework. For the broader consensus community, the rate matches existing accelerations and is slightly suboptimal by a log D factor, so practical impact hinges on whether the BP/Splitting perspective enables new algorithms beyond consensus.

Pros for acceptance
- First convergence-rate analysis of Min-Sum Splitting with clean, interpretable acceleration.
- Insightful conceptual connections likely to stimulate cross-fertilization between BP and accelerated consensus/lifting.
- Solid, reusable analytical tool (auxiliary node-based process).

Cons for acceptance
- No experiments; limited empirical/implementation guidance.
- Main rate matches known two-step accelerations; novelty is interpretive rather than algorithmic/performance improvement.
- Restricted to quadratic consensus; unclear generality.

Suggestions
- Add experiments benchmarking against Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, Nesterov-style, and ADMM/EXTRA on canonical graphs.
- Discuss decentralized estimation/tuning of γ and robustness to mismatch.
- Clarify positivity/convexity conditions, per-iteration complexity, and recommended implementation (edge vs node).
- Outline extensions beyond quadratics and to asynchronous/time-varying settings.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept
