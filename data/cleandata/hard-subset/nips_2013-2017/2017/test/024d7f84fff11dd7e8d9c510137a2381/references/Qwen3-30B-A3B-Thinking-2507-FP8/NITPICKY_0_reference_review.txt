

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving consensus problems in distributed networks, claiming to establish convergence rates that outperform classical diffusive methods. The authors connect Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first-order methods, showing that proper parameter tuning yields subdiffusive convergence rates.

## Major Technical Concerns

1. **Unjustified Assumptions**: The paper claims convergence for Min-Sum Splitting but relies on the unverified condition "1 + δ ∑ w∈N(v) ΓwvR̂ t wv > 0 for any v ∈ V and t ≥ 1" (Proposition 1). This critical condition is never analyzed or justified, particularly for the parameter choices in Theorem 1. Without proof that this condition holds for the proposed parameter values, the convergence claims are incomplete.

2. **Lack of Practical Implementation Details**: The paper states that the acceleration mechanism depends on "global properties of the graph G" (specifically ρW in Theorem 1), yet fails to address how this global information can be computed in a distributed manner. For a distributed algorithm, all parameters should ideally be computable from local information or with minimal communication overhead, but this crucial implementation detail is missing.

3. **Critical Typo in Algorithm Definition**: In Equation (4) defining K̂(δ,Γ), the entry "δ(Γvw − 1) if u = w, z = v" contains a confusing notation. It should specify whether z = v or z = w, but the text is ambiguous. This is a significant technical error that could undermine the entire algorithm description.

4. **Missing Experimental Validation**: The paper makes strong claims about convergence rates but provides no numerical experiments to validate the theoretical results. For a paper about convergence rates, empirical validation is expected and missing.

## Minor Technical and Presentation Issues

1. **Inconsistent Mathematical Notation**: The paper inconsistently uses "minimize" (lowercase) versus "Min-Sum" (capitalized), and has inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators (e.g., "∑ v∈V" should be "∑_{v∈V}"). These small errors reduce readability and professionalism.

2. **Ambiguous Definitions**: In Section 5, the definition "ĥ(δ)wv := bw + (1− 1/δ)bv" is ambiguous. It doesn't specify the relationship between w, v, and the indices, making it unclear whether this is defined for all (w,v) ∈ E or specific pairs.

3. **Weak Connection to Shift-Register Methods**: The paper claims "a proper choice of the splitting parameters recovers the same matrix that support the evolution of shift-register methods," but fails to clearly connect the mathematical details to the specific shift-register methods cited (e.g., [45, 4, 24]).

4. **Reference Formatting Inconsistency**: References use inconsistent formatting (e.g., some include full journal names while others use abbreviations), which is unprofessional for a NIPS submission.

## Strengths

The paper makes a valuable connection between Min-Sum message-passing algorithms and two major lines of accelerated consensus methods (lifted Markov chains and multi-step gradient methods). The auxiliary process introduced in Section 6 is a novel technical contribution that simplifies the convergence analysis.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical connection between Min-Sum Splitting and accelerated consensus methods, it suffers from multiple critical technical flaws that undermine its main claims. The unverified assumption in Proposition 1, the lack of distributed implementation details for the global parameter ρW, and the critical typo in Algorithm 1 prevent the paper from making a solid technical contribution. The absence of experimental validation for the claimed convergence rates is particularly concerning for a paper focused on convergence analysis.

The paper has potential value in connecting different lines of work in distributed optimization, but the current version lacks the technical rigor required for NIPS. The authors need to address the unverified assumptions, provide a distributed method for computing ρW, correct the algorithm definition, and include experimental validation before the paper can be considered for acceptance.

Given the severity of the technical issues and the fact that they directly impact the main claims of the paper, I recommend rejection. The paper presents an interesting idea but fails to deliver a complete and technically sound analysis. The minor formatting issues are not the primary concern, but the major technical flaws make the paper unsuitable for publication in NIPS at this stage.
