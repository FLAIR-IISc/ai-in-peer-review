Summary and contributions
This paper studies Min-Sum Splitting (a reparametrized min-sum/GBP scheme) for solving average consensus. The authors (i) specialize Min-Sum Splitting to equality-constrained quadratic consensus; (ii) show that with quadratic potentials and a suitable parametrization, messages remain quadratic and the protocol reduces to a linear recursion on directed edges; (iii) introduce an auxiliary linear process on nodes with memory (a 2n×2n system K(δ, Γ)) whose evolution exactly tracks the min-sum estimates; and (iv) prove that with δ = 1 and Γ = γW, where W is a symmetric averaging matrix and γ is tuned as a function of ρ(W − 11^T/n), the resulting rate matches that of classical shift-register/Chebyshev-accelerated consensus: a square-root improvement over diffusive rates, yielding O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori. Conceptually, the paper draws connections between (a) Min-Sum Splitting and lifting/non-reversible Markov chains (directionality on edges), and (b) Min-Sum Splitting and multi-step accelerated first-order methods (memory on nodes).

Relation to prior work
- Ordinary Min-Sum/GBP for quadratic consensus on loopy graphs can diverge; consensus propagation and related modifications were analyzed in [27], with rates linked to mixing on directed edges. Ruozzi & Tatikonda [34,35] introduced Splitting and showed convergence improvements but no rates.
- Classical consensus with nonnegative W achieves diffusive rates; even optimally tuned symmetric W is diffusive [44], consistent with MH [37] and lower bounds on graphs with geometry [7].
- Acceleration via (i) lifting/non-reversibility [5,8,18,37,22,23] and (ii) multi-step momentum/shift-register/Nesterov [14,13,24,45,4] is well-established. The paper shows that Min-Sum Splitting, with proper tuning, recovers the shift-register matrix and rates.
- Connections to distributed ADMM and lifting [3,12,43,38,36] are mentioned.

Technical assessment (quality)
- The reduction from Algorithm 1 to a linear message-passing scheme on directed edges (Algorithm 2) for quadratic objectives is standard for GBP and is executed carefully. Proposition 1 is plausible and aligns with GBP folklore (quadratic messages remain quadratic).
- The auxiliary node-based 2n-dimensional recursion (Algorithm 3) is the core technical device. Proposition 2 (equivalence with appropriate initialization) is interesting and technically valuable: it moves the spectral analysis from a 2m-by-2m edge matrix to a 2n-by-2n node matrix K(δ, Γ) tied directly to Γ. This simplification is a genuine contribution.
- Theorem 1 selects δ = 1 and Γ = γW with γ = 2/(1 + sqrt(1 − ρ^2_W)), shows convergence to b̄, and derives the accelerated spectral radius ρ_K = sqrt((1 − sqrt(1 − ρ^2_W))/(1 + sqrt(1 − ρ^2_W))). This matches the classical shift-register/Chebyshev rate and yields the O(D log(D/ε)) scaling on grids. The constants and asymptotic bounds are reasonable; the dependence on W’s spectrum is standard for such acceleration.
- Assumptions and caveats: (a) Quadratic objectives only; (b) symmetric W with W1 = 1; (c) initialization R̂0 = r̂0 = 0; (d) positivity conditions on denominators for the min-sum beliefs; and (e) γ requires global spectral information (ρ_W). These are consistent with the intended scope but limit generality.

Clarity
The paper is dense but mostly clear. The three algorithmic forms (original Min-Sum Splitting, edge-linearized Algorithm 2, and auxiliary node-based Algorithm 3) are well separated. However, proofs are deferred to the supplement; a brief proof sketch for Proposition 2 and Theorem 1 would help. A few notational issues/typos exist (footnotes, missing Figure 1 referenced in the text).

Originality
- Novel: the auxiliary node-based tracking of Min-Sum Splitting and the explicit parameterization that recovers the shift-register matrix K, yielding a clean spectral analysis and rates. Also, the conceptual unification between Splitting, lifting, and multi-step acceleration in a single message-passing viewpoint.
- Less novel: the achieved rate itself matches known Chebyshev/shift-register acceleration; the dependence on global spectrum is standard; acceleration on cycles/grids is not new. The contribution is thus more in the analysis and bridging than in a new algorithmic rate.

Significance
Within the message-passing/GBP community, the result is significant: it provides the first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and a pathway to tune splitting parameters for acceleration. The auxiliary-process technique may be reusable for other problems. For the consensus community, the rate is not new, but the route via Min-Sum is valuable and may inform extensions to other graphical models beyond consensus.

Strengths
- Clear, rigorous specialization of Min-Sum Splitting to average consensus with a linear-quadratic representation.
- Elegant auxiliary node-based process enabling spectral analysis tied directly to Γ.
- Provable accelerated rates that match shift-register methods, with a clean closed-form tuning of γ.
- Insightful connections to lifting and multi-step acceleration, clarifying the role of directionality/memory in Min-Sum.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope limitation: only quadratic consensus; no extension to general convex φ_v or constrained variants where Min-Sum is often applied.
- Global tuning: γ depends on ρ_W; robustness to mis-specification is not analyzed. Can one use polynomial/time-varying schedules to avoid global spectral knowledge (as in Chebyshev iterations)?
- Practicality/overheads: Min-Sum Splitting operates on directed edges exchanging two scalars per edge per iteration, vs. node-based accelerations that communicate one scalar per neighbor. A communication/computation comparison is missing.
- Initialization/positivity: The output equality (Proposition 1) requires 1 + δ Σ Γ R̂^t_wv > 0. Is this guaranteed under the proposed initialization and tuning? Provide a bound ensuring positivity to avoid division issues.
- No experiments: Even simple simulations on cycles/grids/random geometric graphs to compare constants and transient behavior versus shift-register/Nesterov/EXTRA/ADMM would strengthen the submission.
- Asynchrony/noise/time-varying graphs are not discussed; Min-Sum is often used in such settings.

Suggestions
- Add a short proof sketch of Proposition 2 and Theorem 1 in the main text to convey key ideas (e.g., block-diagonalization, relation to Chebyshev polynomials).
- Provide a robustness analysis for γ mis-tuning, or propose an adaptive/degree-based proxy with provable near-optimality.
- Include a complexity comparison (messages per iteration and memory) versus node-based multi-step methods and lifted walks.
- Present experiments on canonical graphs and a discussion of constants.
- Clarify the role of δ: despite early emphasis, the final accelerated choice sets δ = 1; discuss whether δ ≠ 1 can help and when.

Minor comments
- The assumption 0 < b_v < 1 is unnecessary; state that rescaling ensures bounded norms.
- Ensure Figure 1 and all references are present; unify notation for directed edges E vs. undirected E.
- Fix footnote placement and minor typos.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper provides a solid and technically interesting bridge between Min-Sum Splitting and known accelerated consensus via a new auxiliary-process analysis. While the achieved rates are not novel per se and the scope is limited to quadratic consensus with global tuning, the methodological contribution and clarity of the connection are valuable for the community and may spur extensions beyond consensus. Adding empirical validation and discussing robustness would further strengthen the work.
