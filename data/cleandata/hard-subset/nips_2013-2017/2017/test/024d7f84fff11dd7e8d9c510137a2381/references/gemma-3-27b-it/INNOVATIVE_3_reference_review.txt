This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The core idea – leveraging a splitting technique to accelerate Min-Sum, potentially overcoming limitations of standard approaches – is compelling and novel. The authors establish convergence guarantees and, crucially, demonstrate a square-root improvement in convergence time compared to diffusive methods, nearing optimal rates. The connection drawn to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods is insightful and adds to the theoretical understanding of Min-Sum.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The analysis of Min-Sum Splitting and the establishment of convergence rates are, to the best of my knowledge, entirely new. This isn't simply an incremental improvement; it’s a different approach to accelerating consensus.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, establishing convergence under fairly general conditions. The use of an auxiliary process to track the algorithm’s evolution is a clever analytical technique.
*   **Significant Results:** Achieving a square-root improvement in convergence time is a significant result, especially given the prevalence of the consensus problem in distributed systems. Approaching optimal rates is a strong indicator of the method’s potential.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** Linking Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods provides a valuable broader perspective and highlights the algorithm’s versatility.
*   **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The paper is generally well-structured. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and the authors’ approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Implementation Details:** The paper remains largely theoretical. While the analysis is strong, there’s a lack of discussion regarding the practical implications of the splitting parameters. How sensitive is performance to the choice of γ? What are the computational costs associated with calculating and applying this parameter?
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The lack of experimental results is a substantial weakness. While theoretical guarantees are important, demonstrating the benefits of Min-Sum Splitting on real-world or synthetic graphs would significantly strengthen the paper.  A simulation, even a relatively simple one, demonstrating the improved convergence rate is crucial.
*   **Clarity in Specific Sections:** While the overall structure is good, certain sections (especially the transition between describing Algorithm 2 and Algorithm 3) could benefit from more detailed explanations. The connection between the splitting parameters and the auxiliary process isn’t immediately obvious. A more intuitive explanation would be helpful.
*   **Dependencies on Previous Work:** The paper heavily relies on results from [27]. While properly cited, readers unfamiliar with that work may find it difficult to follow the reasoning. A brief recap of the key findings from [27] would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to accelerating consensus, offering a theoretical guarantee of improved convergence.
*   The connection to established techniques (lifted Markov chains, accelerated gradient methods) provides valuable context.
*   The mathematical analysis appears sound and well-motivated.
*   The potential impact on distributed optimization and machine learning is significant.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of experimental validation raises concerns about the practical relevance of the results.
*   The complexity of the analysis and the reliance on prior work may limit the paper’s accessibility.
*   The discussion of implementation details is absent, leaving important practical questions unanswered.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the lack of empirical validation and some minor clarity issues, I strongly recommend **acceptance**. The novelty and theoretical significance of this work are substantial. The authors tackle a challenging problem with a clever and well-supported approach. The potential for a square-root improvement in convergence time is a compelling result. While practical considerations and experimental verification are important, the foundational contribution of this paper justifies acceptance, particularly given NIPS's emphasis on novel ideas. I encourage the authors to address the empirical validation and clarity concerns in future work.  A brief section outlining potential challenges in implementation and directions for future experimentation would also be very valuable.
