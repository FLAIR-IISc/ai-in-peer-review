Summary
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting (a reparameterized variant of Min-Sum/Belief Propagation) for the equality-constrained consensus/averaging problem. For quadratic objectives, the authors (i) show that Min-Sum Splitting induces a linear message-passing dynamics on directed edges; (ii) construct an auxiliary node-based linear dynamical system with memory that exactly tracks the algorithm’s estimates; and (iii) prove that with a suitable choice of splitting parameters (δ=1 and Γ=γW, with W symmetric, W1=1, and γ tuned via ρ(W−11^T/n)), the method converges with an accelerated (subdiffusive) asymptotic rate. The resulting contraction factor ρK is the classical Chebyshev/shift-register rate, i.e., 1/(1−ρK) ≍ √(1/(1−ρW)), yielding O(D log(D/ε)) iterations on cycles and tori, compared to diffusive O(D^2 log(1/ε)). The work also highlights conceptual connections to lifted Markov chains (directionality via splitting) and to multi-step first-order methods (memory on nodes).

Relation to prior work
- Previous Min-Sum for consensus [Moallemi & Van Roy 2006] showed ordinary Min-Sum may diverge on loopy graphs and analyzed a softened variant via an edge-based linear system; rates were characterized only in special cases (cycles) and conjectured for tori.
- Accelerated consensus via lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods (Chebyshev/shift-register, heavy-ball, Nesterov) is well-studied; optimal O(D log(1/ε)) has been achieved in some settings.
- This paper’s novelty is to bring acceleration to Min-Sum Splitting with provable rates, and to show an exact equivalence (under tuning) to the shift-register dynamics via the auxiliary node process, thereby connecting BP-style splitting to both lifting and multi-step optimization.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The main theorem is precise and its rate matches known best rates for second-order polynomial filtering (shift-register). The linearization for quadratics is standard; the edge-to-node reduction (Algorithm 3) seems carefully derived and provides the key spectral handle. The choice γ = 2/(1+√(1−ρW^2)) yields the classical square-root improvement; the mapping from Γ=γW to the node-based K matrix is consistent.
- Assumptions/limitations are clearly stated: W symmetric with W1=1 and ρ(W−11^T/n)<1; quadratic objectives (averaging); synchronous updates; required knowledge of ρW to set γ; zero initialization for simplicity.
- Completeness: The analysis is asymptotic and focuses on rates; constants are given via norms of powers of K−K∞. No empirical validation is provided. Proofs are deferred to the supplement; given the correspondence to established shift-register analysis, the claims are credible, but the paper would benefit from a brief sketch of the critical steps (e.g., how Algorithm 3 is derived and why it matches the edge process).
- Correctness concerns: None major. One subtlety is that the optimal γ>1 makes certain recursion coefficients negative; stability is nonetheless guaranteed by the spectral analysis. The positivity condition in Proposition 1 is handled by zero initialization.

Clarity
- Overall well-organized: background on consensus, diffusive rates, accelerated methods; derivation of edge and auxiliary node systems; main theorem and discussion of implications.
- The paper is dense and algebra-heavy; some definitions (e.g., K(δ,Γ)) could be motivated more intuitively before presenting full block forms. A small worked example (e.g., a path graph) illustrating Algorithm 2 vs Algorithm 3 could improve readability.
- Typographical issues from the provided text (minor formatting, equation numbering) likely due to rendering, not fundamental clarity problems.

Originality
- New theoretical contribution: first convergence-rate analysis (and acceleration) for Min-Sum Splitting; auxiliary node-based process to analyze an edge-based BP scheme is, to my knowledge, new and elegant.
- The achieved rate itself is not new (it matches shift-register/Chebyshev), but obtaining it through Min-Sum Splitting and establishing the equivalence is novel and bridges distinct literatures (BP, lifted chains, accelerated consensus).

Significance
- Conceptual significance: Strong. It clarifies how “directionality” induced by splitting corresponds to nonreversibility/lifting and to momentum/memory—useful insights for designing accelerated message-passing schemes.
- Practical significance: Mixed. The accelerated tuning requires global spectral information (ρW), a known limitation also in lifted and multi-step methods. The paper does not discuss how to estimate or adapt γ in a distributed manner, nor robustness to mis-specification. No experiments are provided to illustrate constants, finite-time behavior, or comparisons to alternatives (e.g., Chebyshev filtering, ADMM-based consensus, Nesterov-based methods).

Pros
- Clear, nontrivial theoretical advance: first accelerated rate for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus.
- Crisp connection between BP-style splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step acceleration.
- Auxiliary node-centric analysis technique that simplifies spectral reasoning of an edge-based protocol.
- Rates approach near-optimal O(D log(D/ε)) on geometric graphs.

Cons
- Scope restricted to quadratic consensus with symmetric W and synchronous updates.
- Requires global spectral information to tune γ; no adaptive/distributed tuning strategy.
- No empirical evaluation to support constants, robustness, or practical benefits.
- Does not close the gap to O(D log(1/ε)) nor discuss when that gap is fundamental for this approach.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a short proof sketch of the auxiliary process equivalence and the key spectral argument.
- Add numerical experiments on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs comparing to Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, and ADMM/Nesterov, including sensitivity to γ mismatch.
- Discuss strategies to estimate ρW (e.g., distributed power/Lanczos) or adaptive tuning schedules, and robustness to time-varying/async setting.
- Comment on extensions beyond averaging: general strongly convex separable φv, constraints beyond equality, directed graphs, and noisy/asynchronous message passing.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a solid and insightful theoretical contribution by establishing accelerated rates for Min-Sum Splitting and unifying perspectives across BP, lifting, and multi-step methods. While empirical validation and broader scope are lacking, the results are sufficiently novel and of interest to the NeurIPS community working on distributed optimization and message-passing. Confidence: high.
