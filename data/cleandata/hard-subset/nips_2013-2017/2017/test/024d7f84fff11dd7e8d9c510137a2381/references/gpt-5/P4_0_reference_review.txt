Summary
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting (Ruozzi & Tatikonda [34,35]) for distributed consensus, focusing on the averaging problem where φv(z) = 1/2 z^2 − b_v z. The authors show that (i) with an appropriate parametrization, Min-Sum Splitting yields a linear message-passing update on directed edges; (ii) by introducing an auxiliary node-based process, the evolution of Min-Sum Splitting can be tracked by a two-step linear iteration on the nodes; and (iii) with a particular choice of parameters δ=1 and Γ=γW, where W is a symmetric averaging matrix with second eigenvalue ρ_W and γ = 2/(1+√(1−ρ_W^2)), the algorithm converges at a “subdiffusive” (accelerated) rate matching shift-register/Chebyshev acceleration. The main Theorem 1 provides an explicit bound in terms of ρ_K, the spectral radius of the induced 2n×2n iteration matrix K, with 1/(1−ρ_K) ≍ √(1/(1−ρ_W)). This yields O(D log(D/ε)) iterations on cycles and tori, improving over diffusive O(D^2 log(1/ε)) and approaching known near-optimal O(D log(1/ε)) from accelerated methods (e.g., Nesterov-based, [30,36]).

The work situates Min-Sum Splitting between two acceleration paradigms: lifted nonreversible Markov chains (e.g., [8,5,18,37]) and multi-step first-order/shift-register schemes ([13,24,45]). Relative to prior Min-Sum for consensus ([27]), which used soft constraints and analyzed edge-based dynamics with slower or conjectured rates, this paper provides general convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting and a constructive parameter choice.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual bridge: Establishes that Min-Sum Splitting, when tuned, recovers the same iteration matrix as shift-register acceleration. The auxiliary node-based formulation (Algorithm 3) is a clean analytical tool.
- Provable acceleration: Theorem 1 provides explicit spectral-rate guarantees, yielding square-root improvements over diffusive consensus and near-optimal scaling on geometric graphs.
- Relation to prior work: Connects to Gaussian BP/Min-Sum convergence conditions ([28,25,35]) and to acceleration via lifting and multi-step methods ([8,5,13,24,37,45]).
- Generality across graphs: Rates are expressed via ρ_W, covering arbitrary topologies, with explicit implications for cycles and tori.

Weaknesses
- Parameter dependence on global information: Achieving acceleration requires γ tuned from ρ_W, which is global. Practical strategies for estimating/tuning γ are not analyzed.
- Scope limited to quadratic averaging: Results cover consensus with quadratic local costs; extensions to broader convex settings or constrained formulations are only discussed as future work.
- No empirical evaluation: Lacks simulations to corroborate constants, robustness, and performance on finite graphs versus alternatives (Chebyshev-polynomial gossip, ADMM, EXTRA).
- Minor clarity/notation issues: Some equation numbering slips (e.g., K̂ index), stray footnotes, and formatting inconsistencies; conditions ensuring positivity in Proposition 1 could be emphasized.
- Communication/computation costs: While the method is local, per-iteration message/computation and memory overhead versus standard two-step node-based methods are not quantified.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The edge-to-node reduction (Propositions 1–2) is consistent with quadratic Min-Sum/BP algebra. The spectral analysis and parameter choice align with classical Chebyshev/shift-register results ([15,24,45]). Theorem 1’s bounds are in line with known relationships between accelerated and base spectral radii. Assumptions (symmetric W, ρ(W−11^T/n)<1, specific initialization) are standard, but practical robustness (asynchrony, noise, time-variation) is not addressed.
- Completeness: Proofs are stated to be in the supplement; main text provides the needed definitions and statements.

Clarity
- Organization is logical: background on consensus and acceleration, algorithmic specialization, auxiliary process, and main theorem. However, polishing is needed: fix equation references, typos (“st Conference...”), unify symbols (Γ respecting graph sparsity, W properties).
- Reproducibility: For the quadratic case, an expert can reproduce the derivations from the provided recursions and parameter choices; explicit proof steps are deferred.

Originality
- Novel contribution is theoretical: first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, with an explicit accelerated regime; first precise connection of Min-Sum Splitting to lifted chains and multi-step acceleration. While the achieved rates match known accelerations, the algorithmic route (via Min-Sum Splitting) and the auxiliary-process analysis appear new.

Significance
- Theoretical significance: Provides a unifying perspective across inference (Min-Sum/BP) and optimization (accelerated consensus), potentially inspiring extensions to other message-passing settings.
- Practical significance: Moderate; reliance on global spectral information and availability of simpler node-based accelerations may limit direct deployment unless Min-Sum is preferred for modeling reasons.

Arguments for acceptance
- Establishes a rigorous accelerated rate for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus with clean spectral guarantees.
- Bridges previously separate literatures (BP/Min-Sum, lifted chains, multi-step acceleration).
- Provides an analytically tractable auxiliary formulation that could generalize.

Arguments against acceptance
- No empirical results; practical aspects (tuning γ, robustness, overhead) not validated.
- Acceleration achievable by existing simpler methods; novelty is primarily the reinterpretation via Min-Sum.
- Scope limited to quadratic consensus; extensions are not developed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments on cycles, grids, expanders, and random geometric graphs comparing to Metropolis, Chebyshev-accelerated gossip, Nesterov-type, and ADMM/EXTRA, including sensitivity to γ mis-specification.
- Discuss how to estimate ρ_W (e.g., power method, polynomial filtering) and adaptive tuning.
- Quantify communication/computation/memory vs. standard two-step node methods; clarify when Min-Sum Splitting is preferable.
- Clarify assumptions ensuring positivity in Proposition 1 and allowable sign patterns in Γ=γW.
- Fix typos/notation, ensure consistent equation numbering, and improve readability of Algorithms 1–3.
- Comment on potential extensions beyond quadratic objectives and on asynchronous or time-varying settings.
