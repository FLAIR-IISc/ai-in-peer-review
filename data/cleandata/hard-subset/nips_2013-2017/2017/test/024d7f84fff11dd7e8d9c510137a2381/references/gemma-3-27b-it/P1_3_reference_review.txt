## NIPS 2017 Review: Accelerated Consensus via Min-Sum Splitting

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors demonstrate that, with appropriate parameter tuning, Min-Sum Splitting achieves accelerated (sub-diffusive) convergence rates, improving upon the classical diffusive rates observed in standard consensus algorithms and the results of [27]. They establish a square-root improvement in convergence time for cycle and tori graphs, bringing performance closer to optimal bounds. A key contribution is the introduction of an auxiliary linear process to track the algorithm's evolution, simplifying the analysis. The paper also draws connections between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chain techniques and accelerated first-order methods in convex optimization.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds on the existing literature around distributed consensus algorithms. It acknowledges the limitations of classical diffusive approaches (e.g., [44, 2]) and previous Min-Sum based methods for consensus [27].  It positions itself within the broader context of acceleration techniques for consensus, referencing work on lifted Markov chains [37, 18] and accelerated first-order methods [13, 4, 24].  The authors effectively highlight how their work bridges these two previously independent lines of research. They correctly position their contribution relative to the state-of-the-art, particularly the improved bounds achieved by Nesterov’s acceleration based algorithms [30, 36].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting is a novel contribution. Demonstrating that splitting can lead to accelerated convergence for consensus, and providing concrete rates, is a significant result.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing a clear convergence rate and providing a connection to known accelerated methods. The auxiliary process construction is a clever technique for simplifying the analysis.
* **Connections to Existing Literature:** The authors do an excellent job of contextualizing their work, clearly articulating its relationship to prior research in distributed optimization, Markov chain analysis, and accelerated methods.  The parallels drawn between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods are insightful.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear definitions and notations. The structure follows a logical progression, building from background to main results and then to discussion.
* **Practical Implications:** The improved convergence rates offered by Min-Sum Splitting could be beneficial in large-scale distributed optimization problems, particularly in scenarios where communication is a bottleneck.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The mathematical notation and derivations can be dense and challenging to follow for readers not intimately familiar with the field. While the derivations *appear* correct, the supplementary material is essential for full verification.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper focuses primarily on theoretical analysis. While a theoretical result is valuable, some empirical validation of the convergence rates, even on small-scale examples, would significantly strengthen the paper. Demonstrating the practical gains of splitting would be very persuasive.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The optimal choice of the splitting parameter (γ in Theorem 1) depends on the spectral properties of the graph.  This requires knowledge of the graph's structure, which might not always be available in a distributed setting.  A discussion of practical strategies for estimating this parameter would be beneficial.
* **Scope of Acceleration:** While the paper shows acceleration compared to diffusive methods, it remains unclear how close the achieved rates are to the *optimal* achievable rates for all graph topologies. The authors state they get close to O(D log(1/ε)), but a more detailed comparison would be helpful.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound analysis of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus, demonstrating accelerated convergence.
* The connection to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods provides valuable insights into the underlying mechanisms driving the acceleration.
* The auxiliary process construction is a clever technique for simplifying the analysis.
* The paper addresses a challenging problem in distributed optimization with potential practical implications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation limits the paper's impact.
* The theoretical complexity might make it difficult for a broad audience to fully appreciate the results.
* The practical challenges associated with parameter tuning are not fully addressed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper with a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The analysis is rigorous, and the insights gained are valuable. While the lack of empirical validation is a weakness, the theoretical results are compelling enough to warrant acceptance. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Confidence:** 8/10

**Further Suggestions for the Authors:**

* Include some empirical results, even on small-scale examples, to validate the theoretical findings.
* Consider discussing practical strategies for estimating the optimal splitting parameter in distributed settings.
* Briefly discuss the limitations of the acceleration, and how it compares to known optimal bounds for different graph topologies.
* Consider adding more intuitive explanations for key concepts and equations to improve clarity for a wider audience.
