- Overall: Strong theoretical contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
- Summary: First theoretical analysis of Min-Sum Splitting’s convergence rate for consensus, situating it among diffusive and accelerated methods.
- Related work: Thoroughly connected to lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, with appropriate citations.
- Novelty: Establishes previously unknown convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting.
- Theoretical rigor: Analysis is mathematically sound using an auxiliary process linked to spectral properties.
- Performance result: Proves a square-root improvement over diffusive methods and rates competitive with Nesterov-style acceleration.
- Clarity: Paper is generally well written and organized; introduction motivates contributions effectively.
- Technical density: Math-heavy presentation may be hard for readers unfamiliar with spectral graph theory and message-passing.
- Practical tuning: Lacks discussion on tuning parameters (δ and Γ) and sensitivity to graph structure.
- Empirical validation: No experiments; empirical evaluation on synthetic or real graphs is needed to demonstrate practical gains.
- Redundancy: Sections 3 and 4 contain repetitive background that could be streamlined.
- Proposition 1 concern: The positivity condition “1 + δ ∑ … > 0” may be critical and its practical enforceability is unclear.
- Convergence illustration: Need clearer translation of asymptotic rates into iteration counts for specific graph topologies (e.g., cycles, tori).
- Choice of γ: Requires guidance or characterization for selecting γ when computing ρ(W) is expensive.
- Future work: Suggests generalizing splitting-based acceleration to other problems; could list concrete candidate problems.
- Pros for acceptance: Significant theoretical advance, novel acceleration approach, strong literature connections, rigorous proofs.
- Cons against acceptance: High technical density, no empirical results, some repetitive sections.
- Recommended revisions: Add practical parameter-tuning discussion, include small-scale empirical results, streamline background, and clarify the auxiliary process intuitively.
