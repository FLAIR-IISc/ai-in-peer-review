

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for accelerating distributed consensus algorithms through a variant of the Min-Sum message-passing algorithm called "Min-Sum Splitting." The authors establish that by introducing specific splitting parameters, the Min-Sum algorithm can achieve subdiffusive convergence rates (O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori) compared to the classical diffusive rate (Θ(D² log(1/ε))), representing a square-root improvement. The key novelty lies in connecting Min-Sum Splitting to two major lines of research: lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step acceleration methods in convex optimization.

The paper makes several significant theoretical contributions. First, it establishes the first proof of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting applied to consensus problems. Second, it reveals a previously unappreciated connection between Min-Sum protocols and both lifted Markov chains (where splitting introduces directionality in message exchange) and multi-step first-order methods (where splitting induces a memory term in the node-based auxiliary process). Third, it demonstrates that proper tuning of the splitting parameters recovers the same matrix as shift-register methods used in numerical analysis, achieving the same accelerated convergence rates.

The paper builds upon prior work by [27], which showed that ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge on cyclic graphs, and [34], which introduced the splitting framework. However, the authors' key insight is that splitting isn't just a convergence mechanism—it's an acceleration mechanism that can be tuned to achieve optimal rates. The theoretical analysis is elegant, using an auxiliary process on the nodes to track the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm's evolution, which allows the authors to relate convergence rates to the spectral properties of the graph.

The significance of this work is substantial. Consensus problems are fundamental to distributed computing, with applications in sensor networks, multi-agent systems, and distributed machine learning. The paper shows that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve convergence rates approaching the optimal O(D log(1/ε)) bound recently established for some accelerated methods [30, 36]. This represents a meaningful theoretical advance in understanding distributed optimization algorithms.

While the paper is theoretically sound, it has a notable limitation: the optimal parameter choice (γ = 2/(1 + √(1 - ρ²W))) requires global knowledge of the graph's spectral properties, which may not be feasible in practical distributed settings. However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, I find this limitation acceptable given the paper's conceptual breakthrough. The connection between Min-Sum, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step optimization methods is genuinely novel and could inspire new research directions.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical progression from problem formulation to theoretical results. The supplementary material appears to contain the full proofs, which is appropriate for a theoretical contribution. The authors appropriately cite related work, though they could have more explicitly contrasted their approach with [12] (on ADMM and lifting) in the related work section.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by revealing a fundamental connection between seemingly disparate approaches to accelerated consensus. While the practical implementation may require further work to address the global parameter tuning requirement, the theoretical insight is significant. This work not only advances our understanding of Min-Sum algorithms but also provides a new lens through which to view distributed optimization algorithms more broadly.

In terms of NIPS relevance, the paper addresses a core problem in distributed machine learning (consensus) with a novel theoretical approach that connects to multiple areas of interest to the NIPS community (distributed optimization, graphical models, and accelerated methods). The paper's focus on theoretical insights rather than empirical results aligns with the type of work that NIPS values.

Strengths:
- Significant theoretical novelty in connecting Min-Sum to two major lines of research
- First convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting
- Elegant proof technique using auxiliary processes
- Demonstrates a square-root improvement over classical methods
- Clear theoretical contribution to understanding distributed algorithms

Weaknesses:
- Parameter tuning requires global graph knowledge (may limit practical applicability)
- No experimental validation of the theoretical results
- The paper is purely theoretical without empirical evidence

Overall, this paper presents a bold, novel theoretical contribution that significantly advances our understanding of distributed optimization. The connection between Min-Sum, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step optimization is particularly insightful and likely to inspire future work. While the practical implementation details require further investigation, the theoretical novelty and significance of the work justify acceptance. The paper represents a substantial theoretical advance that opens new directions for research in distributed algorithms, which aligns well with NIPS' interest in fundamental advances in machine learning and optimization.
