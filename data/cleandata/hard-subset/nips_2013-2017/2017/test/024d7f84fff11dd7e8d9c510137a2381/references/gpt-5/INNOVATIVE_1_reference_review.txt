Summary
The paper analyzes the Min-Sum Splitting variant of belief propagation for distributed consensus, focusing on quadratic (averaging) objectives. It shows (i) how an appropriate parametrization of Min-Sum Splitting yields a linear message-passing scheme on directed edges, (ii) how to track its evolution via an auxiliary 2n-dimensional linear recursion on nodes with a memory term, and (iii) how a specific tuning (δ = 1, Γ = γW with γ chosen from the spectrum of a symmetric W) achieves a provable square-root acceleration over diffusive methods. The asymptotic contraction factor matches that of classical shift-register/Chebyshev-accelerated consensus methods. The analysis highlights conceptual links between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted nonreversible Markov chains, and multi-step first-order methods. For cycles and tori, the method achieves O(D log(D/ε)) iterations vs. Θ(D^2 log(1/ε)) for reversible walks, approaching the O(D log(1/ε)) lower bound.

Relation to prior work
- Ordinary Min-Sum/Gaussian BP convergence on trees and sufficient conditions on loopy graphs are classical [28,25]. Splitting/reparameterizations in BP were proposed by Ruozzi & Tatikonda [34,35], but without rate guarantees.
- For consensus via Min-Sum, [27] studied a soft-constraint variant and linked its rate to a 2m-edge Markov chain; convergence times were characterized for cycles and conjectured for tori.
- Accelerated consensus via multi-step/shift-register methods and Chebyshev semi-iterative schemes is well established [4,13,24,45]; lifting/nonreversible Markov chains also yield acceleration [5,8,18,37].
- The present paper appears to provide the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, derive an auxiliary node-based recursion enabling spectral analysis, and explicitly connect the splitting-induced “directionality” with both lifting and momentum.

Strengths
- Novelty: Clear, nontrivial conceptual bridge between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step acceleration. To my knowledge, no prior rate analysis exists for splitting in this setting.
- Technical contribution: Elegant auxiliary process on nodes reduces a 2m-edge system to a 2n recursion whose spectral properties are tractable. This is a useful analytical device beyond this problem.
- Performance: Provable square-root improvement over diffusive consensus; matches the asymptotic rate of shift-register methods and yields nearly optimal scaling on geometric graphs.
- Clarity of the main theorem: Theorem 1 is clean, with explicit parameter choice γ = 2/(1+sqrt(1−ρW^2)) and a sharp characterization of ρK.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope: Results are limited to quadratic consensus on undirected, synchronous graphs. The introduction suggests broader implications, but concrete extensions (constraints, nonquadratic φv, time-varying/asynchronous networks) are left open.
- Global tuning: Acceleration requires γ tuned via ρW. While common in accelerated consensus, the paper should discuss how to obtain usable γ from local/computable bounds (e.g., Laplacian spectral gap bounds, Cheeger-type estimates) and robustness to misspecification.
- Practical validation: No experiments. Even small-scale numerical comparisons (cycle, grid, geometric graphs) versus Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, and [27] would help demonstrate constants, robustness, and wall-clock trade-offs.
- Algorithmic distinctiveness: The accelerated rate exactly matches shift-register methods, and the auxiliary recursion is essentially a shift-register with specific K. The added value is the BP/Splitting interpretation and the edge-based implementation, but it would be good to articulate practical advantages (e.g., when Min-Sum is already deployed for larger compositional objectives).
- Assumptions and initialization: Theorem 1 uses R̂0 = r̂0 = 0; Proposition 1 has positivity conditions on beliefs that may be vacuous with this initialization but should be spelled out. Clarify whether W can have negative weights and how this affects message updates.
- Clarity/notation: Some typographical issues (e.g., quadratic φv formatting), and a few definitions are dense (K̂ vs. K, Γ sparsity). A small worked example would aid readers.

Quality
The technical claims are plausible and consistent with known spectral facts for Chebyshev/second-order methods; the auxiliary-process proof strategy is sound in spirit. However, all proofs are deferred; including at least proof sketches of the key spectral identities and the equivalence between Algorithms 2 and 3 would strengthen the main text. Lack of empirical validation is a minus but not fatal given the theoretical nature.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable for an expert audience. Section 6’s auxiliary scheme is a highlight. I recommend: add a brief recipe for choosing W and γ in practice; include a figure or table summarizing rates on canonical graphs; and tighten notation around directed vs. undirected edge sets.

Originality
High. Establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting and the dual connection to lifting and multi-step acceleration is, to my knowledge, new and interesting.

Significance
High for communities at the intersection of BP, distributed optimization, and accelerated consensus. The auxiliary-process technique may catalyze rate analyses for other Min-Sum Splitting instances, which would be quite impactful.

Suggestions for the authors
- Provide guidance for γ when ρW is unknown; analyze sensitivity to over/under-tuning.
- Include numerical experiments on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs; compare to Metropolis, Chebyshev, Nesterov/heavy-ball, and [27].
- Discuss extensions: nonquadratic strongly convex φv, asynchronous/time-varying graphs, and robustness to noise.
- Clarify when W can be chosen locally (e.g., Metropolis) and whether Γ = γW preserves sparsity/nonnegativity constraints needed by messages.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a compelling and novel viewpoint with solid theory and clear potential to influence future work on BP-inspired acceleration. While empirical validation and broader problem classes are missing, the conceptual leap and the auxiliary-process technique justify acceptance.

Confidence
High.
