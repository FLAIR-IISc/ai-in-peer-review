

## Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates the application of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol to solve the consensus problem in distributed optimization, with a focus on establishing convergence rates for a modified version called Min-Sum Splitting. The authors demonstrate that while ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge for graphs with cycles, Min-Sum Splitting yields convergence to the problem solution with accelerated (subdiffusive) rates that match shift-register methods. The key theoretical contribution is establishing a novel connection between Min-Sum algorithms and both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly situates its contribution within the state of the art. It thoroughly reviews relevant literature on consensus algorithms (Section 3), accelerated methods (Section 4), and previous work on Min-Sum for consensus (Section 1). The authors effectively position their work as filling a critical gap in the theoretical understanding of Min-Sum Splitting, which had previously been observed to converge in cases where ordinary Min-Sum fails but without established convergence rates.

The main contributions are clearly articulated: (1) establishing the first connection between Min-Sum schemes and lifted Markov chain techniques, (2) demonstrating how directionality in Belief Propagation can be tuned to accelerate convergence, (3) providing the first analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, and (4) designing a Min-Sum protocol that achieves better convergence rates than previous Min-Sum methods for consensus.

The technical quality is strong, with a well-structured theoretical analysis. The authors establish a novel proof technique by introducing an auxiliary process to track the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm on the nodes rather than the directed edges, which allows them to relate convergence rates to the spectral gap of a simpler matrix. Theorem 1 provides a rigorous characterization of the accelerated convergence rates, showing that proper parameter selection yields a square-root improvement over diffusive methods, scaling like O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori. This result is significant as it approaches the optimal rate O(D log(1/ε)) recently established for some accelerated methods.

The paper's originality is strong, as it identifies a connection between two seemingly disparate areas (Min-Sum message passing and accelerated optimization techniques) that had not been previously established. The authors carefully distinguish their work from prior Min-Sum studies for consensus (notably [27]) and clarify how their approach differs. The results represent a qualitative advance in understanding distributed optimization algorithms.

Regarding clarity, the paper is exceptionally well-organized and written. The introduction clearly frames the problem, motivation, and contributions. The technical sections build logically from background to the main results. One minor issue is the ambiguous phrasing on page 4: "splitting each term φvw into Γvw terms, and each φv into δ terms" is confusing since δ and Γvw were introduced as real numbers (not integers), and the paper later states these can be real. This could be clarified by specifying that this is a conceptual interpretation, not a literal splitting of terms.

The significance of this work is high. Consensus is a fundamental primitive in distributed systems, and the paper provides a theoretically sound approach to accelerating its convergence for graphs with geometry (where diffusive methods are suboptimal). The connection to lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods suggests broader implications for future work. The paper also motivates further research on generalizing the acceleration due to splitting to other problems beyond consensus.

The main weakness is the limited exploration of parameter choices beyond the specific case analyzed in Theorem 1. While the authors note that "it remains to be investigated how choices of splitting parameters different than the ones investigated in Theorem 1 affect the convergence behavior," a more thorough exploration of this aspect would strengthen the paper.

In summary, this paper makes a strong contribution to distributed optimization with clear theoretical advances, novel connections to established techniques, and practical implications for accelerating consensus algorithms. The results are technically sound, well-motivated, and presented with exceptional clarity. The minor issue with the ambiguous phrasing on page 4 is easily fixable and does not detract from the paper's overall quality.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution with novel connections to two established areas (lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization); rigorous analysis of convergence rates; excellent writing and organization; significant practical implications for distributed systems.

Con: Limited exploration of parameter choices beyond the specific case analyzed; minor ambiguity in terminology on page 4.

Overall, this paper represents a significant advance in the field of distributed optimization and should be accepted for presentation at NIPS. The results are both theoretically rigorous and practically relevant, and the paper is clearly written with a strong connection to the existing literature.
