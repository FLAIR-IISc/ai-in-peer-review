- Applies Min-Sum message-passing to distributed consensus and links it to accelerated distributed optimization methods.
- Shows ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge on graphs with cycles, but Min-Sum Splitting converges with accelerated (subdiffusive) rates.
- Demonstrates Min-Sum Splitting achieves rates matching shift-register methods.
- Main contribution: tuning directionality in Min-Sum protocols accelerates convergence.
- Establishes similarity between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step first-order methods.
- Places work within prior Min-Sum literature and two acceleration lines (lifted chains and accelerated first-order methods).
- Interprets Min-Sum Splitting as a process on a lifted space (directed edges) and splitting parameters as memory terms.
- Provides a new proof technique using an auxiliary node-level process linking convergence to graph spectral properties.
- First paper to prove convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting and show parameter tuning yields acceleration.
- Theoretical results are solid and technically sound, with clear exposition and reproducible details.
- Minor clarity issue: ambiguous language on page 4 about "splitting" into δ and Γvw terms when those are real numbers.
- Authors note limitation that optimal parameter choice depends on global graph properties.
- Significant impact: advances distributed optimization and achieves near-optimal rates (e.g., O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles and tori).
- Practical relevance for sensor networks, multi-agent systems, and large-scale ML consensus primitives.
- Weaknesses: minor ambiguity in splitting description and limited exploration of alternative parameter choices.
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, first convergence-rate proof, matching accelerated methods, clear writing, strong theoretical contribution.
- Arguments against acceptance: small clarity issue and incomplete exploration of parameter impacts.
- Overall recommendation: accept — high-quality, significant, and well-presented contribution.
