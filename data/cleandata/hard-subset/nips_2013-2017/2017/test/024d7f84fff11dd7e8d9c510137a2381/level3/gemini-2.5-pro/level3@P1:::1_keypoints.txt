- Studies Min-Sum Splitting for distributed consensus (network averaging) and contrasts with diffusive linear methods.
- Compares two families of accelerated methods: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order optimization methods.
- Main contribution: shows Min-Sum Splitting converges on consensus where ordinary Min-Sum fails and can achieve accelerated, subdiffusive rates.
- For quadratic network-averaging objectives, Min-Sum Splitting updates become linear (Proposition 1).
- Introduces an auxiliary message-passing scheme that maps edge-based dynamics to a multi-step linear node process (Algorithm 3, Proposition 2).
- Relates the auxiliary process to shift-register methods to prove acceleration (Theorem 1).
- Specific tuning of splitting parameters (δ, Γ) recovers accelerated shift-register dynamics and yields a square-root improvement in convergence time on graphs like cycles and tori.
- Conceptual novelty: links belief propagation (Min-Sum), lifted Markov chains (directionality via splitting), and momentum-based acceleration.
- Technical quality is high: rigorous analysis, sound derivations, and a convincing spectral characterization of convergence.
- Paper is very clear and well-organized with a strong, motivating introduction and logical flow.
- Minor clarity issue: presenting δ and Γ as integer "splitting" historically but using them as real-valued tuning parameters could confuse readers.
- Originality: first formal convergence-rate analysis of Min-Sum Splitting and first demonstration of its acceleration on consensus.
- Novel perspective: shows splitting parameters can induce momentum-like effects, opening new research directions for accelerated message-passing.
- Practical significance: provides a provably faster distributed consensus algorithm, though optimal tuning needs global spectral information.
- Theoretical significance: establishes a potentially influential conceptual bridge between message-passing and acceleration techniques.
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a fundamental problem, offers provable acceleration, presents a significant conceptual link, and is technically sound and well-written.
- Arguments against acceptance: acceleration parameter choice depends on global graph info (ρ_W), and exposition on integer vs real interpretation of splitting parameters needs clarification.
