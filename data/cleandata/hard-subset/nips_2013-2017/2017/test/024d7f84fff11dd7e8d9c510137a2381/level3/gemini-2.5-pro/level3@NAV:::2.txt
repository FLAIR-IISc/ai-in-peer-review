This paper proposes using the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for the average consensus problem, demonstrating that with proper parameter tuning, it can achieve accelerated convergence rates. The analysis introduces an auxiliary message-passing scheme to track the algorithm's dynamics on the graph's nodes, drawing connections between the method and established acceleration techniques like lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods such as Nesterov’s.

The paper is well-written and the connection it draws between Min-Sum Splitting and other acceleration frameworks is conceptually interesting. The use of an auxiliary process to analyze the convergence of the edge-based message-passing protocol is a noteworthy analytical technique.

However, the work has several significant weaknesses that limit its contribution.

**Quality and Significance:** The claimed improvement in the convergence rate, O(D log(D/ε)), is not impressive when compared to the existing literature. It offers little advantage over the O(D log(1/ε)) rate established by Olshevsky (2014), and in the worst case where D is on the order of n, it is inferior. The gains appear to stem from standard spectral gap manipulation, and the worst-case bound remains O(n), as D can be O(n).

**Originality and Related Work:** The paper suffers from a serious omission of relevant prior art. It fails to cite or compare its results against several state-of-the-art consensus and distributed optimization algorithms. Crucial missing references include Olshevsky's work on linear time consensus, which is directly competitive. Furthermore, foundational papers on consensus-optimization, such as EXTRA (Shi et al., 2015), fast distributed gradient methods (Jakovetic et al., 2015), and recent analyses of optimal rates (Scaman et al., 2017), are ignored. Related work on Markov chain lifting and ADMM by Franca and Bento is also missing. The novelty of the acceleration itself is unclear, as the auxiliary scheme strongly resembles a reformulation of known momentum-based methods (e.g., Polyak's heavy ball or Nesterov acceleration) applied to the linear system that defines the consensus problem. The connection to memory terms, stated as a finding on page 5, is a long-established fact that should be attributed to prior work (e.g., Rabbat et al., 2005).

**Clarity:** The claim about achieving dimension-independent complexity on "good" graphs is vague. The authors must formally define this class of graphs and clarify misleading statements regarding complete graphs, where consensus converges trivially in one step. Additionally, Proposition 2 appears to be a straightforward reparameterization, representing a supplementary rather than a core contribution.

In summary, while the paper explores an interesting algorithm, its claims of novelty and superior performance are not substantiated against the current state-of-the-art. The contribution is significantly weakened by the lack of engagement with a large body of directly relevant work.
