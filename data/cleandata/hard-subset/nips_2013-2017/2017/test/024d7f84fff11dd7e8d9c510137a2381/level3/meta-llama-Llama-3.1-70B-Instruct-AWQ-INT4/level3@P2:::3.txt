The paper "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting" proposes a novel approach to distributed consensus optimization using the Min-Sum Splitting message-passing algorithm. The authors build upon previous work by Ruozzi and Tatikonda (2013) and analyze the convergence of the algorithm on quadratic consensus problems. The main technical tool used in the paper is an auxiliary linear process that tracks the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, allowing the authors to establish improved convergence rates.

The paper makes several key contributions, including a new proof technique via the auxiliary process, improved convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, and connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods. The manuscript is generally clear and well-written, but contains several typos and grammatical errors throughout.

The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear correct, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm's convergence properties. However, there are several minor technical comments and suggestions for improvement. For example, the authors may have misused the terms "Laplacian" and "Laplace multipliers" on lines 220-221, and the algorithm descriptions are incomplete, with some variables and initializations not explained.

Furthermore, the paper omits relevant literature on (sub)gradient and decentralized optimization, as well as classical acceleration work by Polyak and Nesterov. The authors should consider adding these references to provide a more comprehensive overview of the field.

The paper raises several key questions, including the practicality of the method and when to use it versus other distributed consensus methods. The authors should provide more discussion on the limitations of Min-Sum Splitting and the intuition behind using the auxiliary process. Additionally, it would be interesting to know whether the framework can be extended beyond quadratic objectives to more general consensus problems common in machine learning.

Finally, the authors should state more clearly why the approach is of interest to the machine learning community. While the paper establishes theoretically interesting results, it lacks practical discussion and intuition.

Overall, the paper provides a valuable contribution to the field of distributed consensus optimization, but could benefit from additional discussion and clarification on several key points.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the proofs appear correct. However, there are several minor technical comments and suggestions for improvement. (8/10)
