

This paper presents an interesting application of Min-Sum message-passing to the distributed consensus problem, establishing a novel connection between Min-Sum algorithms and accelerated methods in distributed optimization. The authors show that while ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge for graphs with cycles, a modified version called Min-Sum Splitting achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence rates that match those of shift-register methods. The main contribution is establishing how the directionality embedded in Min-Sum protocols can be tuned to accelerate convergence, and demonstrating that Min-Sum Splitting bears similarities with both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization.

The paper situates its contribution well within the literature. It builds upon previous work on Min-Sum for distributed optimization (e.g., [27, 34, 35]) and connects to two main lines of research on accelerating consensus: lifted Markov chains ([8, 5, 37]) and accelerated first-order methods ([13, 24]). The authors effectively show that Min-Sum Splitting can be interpreted as a process on a lifted space (the directed edges of the graph), analogous to lifted Markov chains, while the tuning of splitting parameters corresponds to adding memory terms similar to multi-step methods. This connection was not previously established in the literature.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear exposition of technical details and a logical flow from problem formulation to results. The theoretical contributions are solid, with the main theorem establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting that match those of shift-register methods. The authors provide a new proof technique using an auxiliary process on the nodes of the graph, which allows them to link convergence rates to spectral properties of the graph. The results are novel, as the paper establishes the first proof of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting and demonstrates how splitting parameters can be tuned for acceleration.

The paper has a minor issue with clarity on page 4, where the description of the splitting mechanism ("splitting each term φvw into Γvw terms, and each φv into δ terms") is ambiguous since δ and Γvw were introduced as real numbers, not integers. This could confuse readers unfamiliar with the paper's prior work. The authors should clarify that this is a conceptual interpretation rather than a literal splitting into integer numbers of terms.

Regarding the four review criteria:

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical results. The authors carefully establish the connection between Min-Sum Splitting and accelerated methods, and provide a clear proof of convergence rates. They acknowledge the limitations of their approach, noting that the optimal parameter choice depends on global graph properties.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written with clear organization. The minor ambiguity mentioned above is the only clarity issue. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results, including the auxiliary process construction that enables their spectral analysis.

Originality: The paper makes a significant original contribution by establishing the novel connection between Min-Sum algorithms and accelerated methods. It's the first to show how Min-Sum schemes can be tuned for accelerated convergence in consensus problems, and to connect them to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods.

Significance: The results advance the state of the art in distributed optimization, providing a new approach to accelerate consensus that achieves near-optimal rates (O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori). This has practical implications for sensor networks, multi-agent systems, and large-scale machine learning where distributed consensus is a fundamental primitive. The connection to established acceleration techniques suggests the approach could inspire further work on accelerating other distributed algorithms.

The main weakness is the minor ambiguity in the description of the splitting mechanism. However, this is a small issue in an otherwise strong paper. The theoretical contribution is substantial and the results are clearly presented.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel connection between Min-Sum algorithms and established acceleration techniques
- First proof of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting
- Demonstrates accelerated convergence matching shift-register methods
- Well-written and clearly situates contribution in literature
- Significant theoretical contribution to distributed optimization

Arguments against acceptance:
- Minor ambiguity in description of splitting mechanism on page 4
- Doesn't fully explore the impact of different parameter choices beyond the main theorem

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The results are novel, well-supported, and have clear significance for distributed optimization. The minor clarity issue is easily addressable in the camera-ready version. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and would be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
