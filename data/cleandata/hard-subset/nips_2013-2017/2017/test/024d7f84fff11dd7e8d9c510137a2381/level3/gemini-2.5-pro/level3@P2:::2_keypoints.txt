- Paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for distributed average consensus and shows Min-Sum Splitting can be parameterized to converge where standard Min-Sum fails on loopy graphs.  
- Main contribution: convergence rate analysis via an auxiliary node-based message-passing scheme that tracks the edge-based algorithm.  
- Shows Min-Sum Splitting can be tuned to emulate shift-register (multi-step) methods, yielding accelerated "subdiffusive" asymptotic convergence O(D log(D/ε)) on graphs like cycles and tori.  
- Draws conceptual links between Min-Sum Splitting acceleration and lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order optimization methods.  
- Technical derivations appear sound; using an auxiliary process (Algorithm 3) to simplify analysis is a clever and valid technique.  
- Evaluation and claims about dimension-independent convergence on "good" graphs are imprecise and potentially misleading (e.g., complete graph consensus is trivial); need clearer definition of "good" graphs.  
- Clarity is generally good and well-structured, but there are minor issues: likely typo ("Laplacian" vs "Lagrangian") and confusing notation about directed edges from an undirected graph.  
- Novelty: first formal convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and an interesting perspective linking belief propagation variants to other acceleration frameworks.  
- However, the acceleration mechanism itself largely restates known momentum/shift-register acceleration principles previously observed in consensus/optimization literature.  
- Significance is limited because the O(D log(D/ε)) rate is not state-of-the-art and can be outperformed (e.g., Olshevsky 2014’s O(D log(1/ε)) result).  
- Literature review is incomplete; paper should compare to key works (Olshevsky 2014; Scaman et al. 2017; Shi et al. 2015/2014; Jakovetić et al. 2014; França & Bento 2016) and contextualize against ADMM and accelerated gradient methods.  
- Recommend revising comparisons to related work, clarifying graph-class claims, fixing minor notation/typos, and de-emphasizing Proposition 2 to streamline the main theoretical argument.
