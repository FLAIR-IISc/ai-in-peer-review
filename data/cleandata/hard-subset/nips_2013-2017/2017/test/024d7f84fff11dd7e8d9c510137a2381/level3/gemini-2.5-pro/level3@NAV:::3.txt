This paper proposes applying the Min-Sum Splitting message-passing algorithm to accelerate distributed consensus optimization. Building on the reparameterization work of Ruozzi & Tatikonda (2013), the authors analyze the algorithm's convergence for quadratic consensus problems. The core technical contribution is a novel proof technique that introduces an auxiliary linear process to track the Min-Sum Splitting dynamics. This approach allows the authors to establish improved convergence rates and, importantly, to draw insightful connections between this belief propagation method and two established areas of accelerated optimization: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods.

The primary strength of this work lies in its theoretical contribution. The use of an auxiliary process is an elegant and novel method for providing convergence rate guarantees for Min-Sum Splitting, which were previously absent. The proofs of the main results appear technically sound. The paper's originality is further enhanced by its successful effort to bridge the conceptual gap between message-passing schemes and classical acceleration frameworks, a connection that could inspire future research.

Despite these strengths, the paper has several weaknesses that need to be addressed.
1.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper's clarity is hampered by incomplete algorithmic descriptions; for instance, key variables (R{vw}, r{vw}) are not defined, and initializations (\hat{R}^0, \hat{r}^0) are missing. The manuscript also contains numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "theirs neighbors," "double-stochastic") and a potential misuse of "Laplacian" for "Lagrangian" (lines 220-221), which undermines its professional quality.
2.  **Related Work:** The submission fails to adequately reference foundational work in decentralized optimization (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989; Wei Shi 2015) and classical acceleration methods (e.g., Polyak, Nesterov), which is essential for properly situating its contributions.
3.  **Significance and Discussion:** The paper's impact is limited by a lack of practical discussion. The authors should clarify the limitations of the method and provide intuition for the auxiliary process. Key questions remain unanswered: When is this method preferable to other distributed optimization algorithms? Can the framework be extended beyond quadratic objectives to more general consensus problems common in machine learning? A more explicit statement on the work's relevance to the machine learning community is needed.

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically interesting analysis with a novel proof technique. However, its significance is currently obscured by issues with clarity, incomplete positioning within the literature, and a lack of discussion regarding practical implications and limitations. Addressing these points is crucial for strengthening the work.
