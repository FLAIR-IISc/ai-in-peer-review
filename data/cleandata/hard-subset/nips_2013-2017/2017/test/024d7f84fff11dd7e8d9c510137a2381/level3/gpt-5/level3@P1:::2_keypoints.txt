- Paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for average consensus, showing ordinary Min-Sum can diverge on loopy graphs while Splitting with tuned parameters converges and accelerates.
- Main technical tool is an auxiliary node-based linear two-step iteration that tracks edge-based Min-Sum Splitting and exposes a spectral link to shift-register (Chebyshev-type) methods.
- With Γ = γW (W symmetric, stochastic) and γ chosen via ρ(W−11^T/n), the method attains Chebyshev-type asymptotic rates, giving O(D log(D/ε)) iterations on cycles and tori.
- Conceptual parallels drawn to lifted/nonreversible Markov chains (directionality via splitting) and to multi-step first-order acceleration (momentum/shift-register).
- Aligns the work with prior acceleration literature (Diaconis–Holmes–Neal, Chen–Lovász–Pak, Jung–Shah–Shin, heavy-ball/Nesterov/Chebyshev, Xiao–Boyd, Ghadimi–Shames–Johansson).
- Result is close in spirit and rate to Olshevsky’s “Linear Time Average Consensus” (O(D log(1/ε))) and other known accelerated consensus methods.
- Strength: clear technical development for quadratic consensus and a neat edge-to-node auxiliary reduction useful for analysis.
- Strength: rigorous spectral analysis tying parameters to optimal shift-register rates; shows Min-Sum Splitting recovers Chebyshev-type acceleration.
- Strength: conceptual unification linking BP-like directionality, lifting, and multi-step momentum; complete proofs and mostly clear exposition.
- Weakness: limited novelty in convergence rates—the guarantees match known methods and the headline O(D log(D/ε)) can be worse than O(D log(1/ε)).
- Weakness: “dimension-independent complexity” claims are misleading without precise regime/graph assumptions (complete graphs trivialize the claim).
- Weakness: missing and incomplete related-work discussion (notably Olshevsky, Shi et al., Scaman et al., França–Bento, EXTRA, Jakovetić et al.).
- Weakness: Proposition 3 appears trivial/incremental and weak as a standalone contribution.
- Weakness: practicality concerns—accelerated parameters depend on global spectral info; no discussion of decentralized estimation of γ or robustness to misspecification.
- Weakness: auxiliary message-passing mainly recovers an already-known two-step linear system; genuine algorithmic novelty over standard accelerated consensus is unclear.
- Quality: main theorem and reduction are technically sound but the practical impact and novelty are modest.
- Completeness: theoretical treatment is complete for quadratics, but empirical validation and comparisons to state-of-the-art accelerated consensus are absent.
- Clarity: generally well written and auxiliary system clarifies structure, but some notation (directed edges on simple graphs) and minor typos confuse readers.
- Originality: interesting conceptual insight that Min-Sum Splitting emulates shift-register acceleration, but rates/mechanisms are not novel relative to existing accelerations.
- Significance: helps bridge BP/splitting, lifting, and multi-step optimization communities, but incremental for consensus compared to existing methods.
- Detailed suggestions: clarify “good” graphs and model constraints, compare quantitatively to Olshevsky, add missing citations, cite early heavy-ball/Nesterov consensus works, and discuss decentralized γ estimation and per-iteration costs.
- Detailed suggestions: fix notation/confusing terminology (directed vs undirected edges; “Laplacian” vs “Lagrangian”) and reconsider the prominence of Proposition 3.
- Pros: solid analysis, clear auxiliary reduction, shows BP/Splitting can match best-known accelerated consensus rates.
- Cons: limited algorithmic novelty/rate improvement, missing key related work, and unaddressed practical deployment issues (global spectral information).
- Overall evaluation: borderline reject — insightful rederivation and unification but not a clear advance over state-of-the-art consensus rates.
