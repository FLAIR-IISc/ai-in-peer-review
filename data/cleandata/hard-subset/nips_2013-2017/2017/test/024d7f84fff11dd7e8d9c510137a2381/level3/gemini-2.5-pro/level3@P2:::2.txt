### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the application of the Min-Sum Splitting message-passing algorithm to the distributed average consensus problem. The authors first show that while the standard Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge on graphs with cycles, the Min-Sum Splitting variant, with appropriate parameterization, does converge to the correct solution. The main contribution is a convergence rate analysis for this algorithm. By introducing an auxiliary message-passing scheme that tracks the algorithm's evolution on the graph's nodes, the authors show that Min-Sum Splitting can be tuned to match the dynamics of shift-register methods. This connection allows them to prove an accelerated, "subdiffusive" asymptotic convergence time of O(D log(D/ε)) for certain graph topologies like cycles and tori, where D is the graph diameter. The paper also draws conceptual parallels between the acceleration mechanism in Min-Sum Splitting and those found in lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper appears technically sound in its derivations. The core analytical contribution is the introduction of an auxiliary process (Algorithm 3) to analyze the dynamics of the edge-based Min-Sum Splitting protocol (Algorithm 2). This is a clever technique that simplifies the analysis by relating the complex edge-based system to a more tractable node-based linear system with memory. The proofs seem to correctly establish the connection to shift-register methods and derive the corresponding convergence rate.

However, the evaluation of the work's contribution is not entirely careful. For instance, the discussion on convergence time (page 2) mentions that for graphs with good expansion properties (like complete graphs), the convergence time is independent of the problem dimension. This is a bit misleading, as consensus on a complete graph is a trivial one-step operation. The authors should clarify precisely which graph classes exhibit this dimension-independent complexity and provide a more rigorous definition of "good" graphs in this context.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and structured. The introduction provides good context on the consensus problem and different algorithmic approaches. The progression from the general Min-Sum Splitting algorithm to its specific application for consensus and the subsequent analysis is logical and easy to follow.

There are minor clarity issues.
-   Line 220: There appears to be a typo; "Laplacian" should likely be "Lagrangian" in the context of optimization.
-   Line 243: The description of message updates involving `K̂(δ,Γ)` uses indices like `wv,zu`. While standard in this literature, it can be dense. More critically, the use of "directed edges" on what is defined as a "simple graph" could be stated more clearly to avoid confusion (e.g., by specifying that each undirected edge {v,w} gives rise to two directed edges (v,w) and (w,v) for message passing).

**Originality:**
The paper presents the first formal convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, which is a novel contribution. The connection it establishes between a belief propagation variant (Min-Sum), lifted Markov chains, and multi-step optimization methods is interesting and potentially provides a new perspective for researchers in the message-passing community.

However, the originality of the acceleration mechanism itself is questionable. The "auxiliary message-passing" scheme effectively recasts the Min-Sum algorithm as a known accelerated linear method (a shift-register, which is closely related to Polyak's heavy ball or Nesterov's momentum). The insight that consensus iterations can be viewed as gradient methods and thus accelerated with momentum is not new; this was discussed, for example, by Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew (2005) and has been the basis for a large body of subsequent work. Thus, the paper seems to re-derive a known acceleration principle within the specific framework of Min-Sum.

**Significance:**
The significance of the results is limited. The primary claim is an accelerated convergence rate of O(D log(D/ε)). While this offers a square-root improvement over diffusive methods (O(D^2 log(1/ε))), it is not state-of-the-art. The paper critically omits comparison with several key works that achieve superior or highly competitive rates. Most notably, Olshevsky (2014, "Linear Time Average Consensus...") established an O(D log(1/ε)) rate, which is strictly better than the rate derived here, especially when D is large (e.g., in a cycle graph where D ~ n, the proposed rate's log(D) term makes it worse).

Furthermore, the paper's literature review is missing a significant portion of the modern distributed optimization literature, which makes it difficult to assess its contribution in proper context. The state-of-the-art has advanced significantly, with algorithms like ADMM and accelerated gradient methods providing provably linear convergence rates.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

The paper would be substantially strengthened by addressing the significant gaps in its comparison to related work. I strongly recommend the authors revise the manuscript to include a thorough discussion and comparison with the following works:

1.  **Directly Competing Consensus Algorithms:**
    *   Olshevsky, A. (2014). "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs...". This paper provides an O(D log(1/ε)) rate, which appears to outperform the results presented here. A direct comparison is essential.

2.  **State-of-the-art in Distributed Optimization:**
    *   Scaman, K., et al. (2017). "Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks." This paper establishes fundamental lower bounds and optimal algorithms, providing a crucial benchmark for any new method.
    *   Shi, W., et al. (2015). "EXTRA: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization."
    *   Jakovetić, D., et al. (2014). "Fast distributed gradient methods."

3.  **ADMM and Related Connections:**
    *   Shi, W., et al. (2014). "On the linear convergence of the ADMM in decentralized consensus optimization."
    *   França, G., & Bento, J. (2016). "Markov chain lifting and distributed admm." This paper explicitly connects Markov chain lifting and ADMM, which is highly relevant to the connections the current paper aims to make.

Finally, Proposition 2 seems to be an intermediate, supplementary result that establishes the equivalence of the two algorithms. Its prominence could be reduced to streamline the main theoretical argument leading to Theorem 1.

In summary, while the paper provides a novel analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm and makes an interesting conceptual connection, its practical significance is undermined by a convergence rate that is not state-of-the-art and a literature review that misses several crucial and more advanced contributions to the field.
