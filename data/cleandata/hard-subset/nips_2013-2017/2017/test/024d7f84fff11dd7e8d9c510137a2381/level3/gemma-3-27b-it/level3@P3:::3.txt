This paper investigates accelerated Min-Sum Splitting for distributed consensus optimization. The authors build upon previous work by Ruozzi & Tatikonda (2013) and leverage an auxiliary linear process to analyze convergence on quadratic consensus problems. The core contribution lies in a novel proof technique, resulting in improved convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting and establishing connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods.

The paper is generally well-written and the core ideas are presented clearly. However, the manuscript suffers from numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "theirs neighbors" should be "their neighbors", "double-stochastic" should be "doubly-stochastic", "casted" should be "cast"). These detract from the reading experience and suggest a lack of careful polishing. The technical analysis appears sound; the proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 seem correct upon inspection. 

A minor technical point: the authors occasionally conflate "Laplacian" with "Lagrangian" and "Laplace multipliers" with "Lagrange multipliers" around lines 220-221. While related, these are distinct concepts, and clarity in terminology is crucial. More significantly, the algorithmic descriptions (particularly Algorithm 1 and the discussion of Algorithm 2) are incomplete. Variables like R{vw} and r{vw} are introduced without sufficient explanation. Critically, the initializations of  \hat{R}^0 and \hat{r}^0 are missing, hindering reproducibility. The connection between the variables in Algorithm 3 and the auxiliary linear process isn’t explicitly stated, making it difficult to follow the analysis.

The paper’s biggest weakness is a lack of contextualization and discussion of practical implications. The authors omit relevant literature on (sub)gradient and decentralized optimization, such as Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis (1989) and Shi (2015), as well as foundational work on acceleration (Polyak, Nesterov). Including these references would better position the contribution within the broader landscape of distributed optimization. Specifically, a discussion of how Min-Sum Splitting compares to, and potentially benefits from, established techniques like ADMM would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The convergence analysis of Min-Sum Splitting and the establishment of its connection to lifted Markov chains and accelerated optimization methods are novel contributions.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The technical analysis appears rigorous and the proofs are well-structured (although the manuscript needs polishing).
*   **Potential Significance:** Accelerated consensus algorithms are critical for large-scale distributed machine learning applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Practical Discussion:** The paper focuses almost exclusively on theoretical results, neglecting practical considerations like implementation complexity, sensitivity to parameter tuning, and scalability.
*   **Incomplete Algorithmic Description:** The incomplete descriptions of the algorithms hinder reproducibility and make it difficult to assess the method's usability.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The omission of key literature on decentralized optimization limits the contextualization of the work.
*   **Polishing Needed:** Numerous typos and grammatical errors detract from clarity.

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  What are the practical considerations for implementing this method? When would one choose Min-Sum Splitting over other distributed consensus methods (e.g., ADMM, gradient descent)?
2.  What are the limitations of Min-Sum Splitting? What types of graphs or problem structures might cause it to perform poorly? What is the intuition behind using the auxiliary process to analyze the algorithm?
3.  Can this framework be extended beyond quadratic objectives to more general consensus problems commonly encountered in machine learning (e.g., those with non-smooth loss functions)?
4.  The introduction mentions applications in machine learning. Could the authors explicitly state *how* these theoretical results translate to improvements in machine learning tasks? What specific ML problems would benefit from this approach?

In summary, this is a theoretically interesting paper that establishes convergence rates and links to established methods. However, the lack of practical discussion, intuitive explanation, and clearer algorithmic exposition significantly limit its impact. Addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly providing a more complete and accessible presentation, would substantially strengthen the manuscript. I recommend acceptance with major revisions.
