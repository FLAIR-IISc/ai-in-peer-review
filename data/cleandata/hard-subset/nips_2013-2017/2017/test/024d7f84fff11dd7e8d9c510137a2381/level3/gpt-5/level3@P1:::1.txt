This paper studies distributed averaging/consensus through a reparameterized Min-Sum (a.k.a. Belief Propagation) scheme, termed Min-Sum Splitting. The authors prove that while ordinary Min-Sum generally fails on loopy graphs for consensus, an appropriate splitting yields a convergent and accelerated algorithm. The key technical contributions are: (i) a linear, edge-based message update specialized to the quadratic consensus objective; (ii) an auxiliary node-based two-step linear process that exactly tracks the algorithm’s output; and (iii) a parameter choice that recovers shift-register (Chebyshev/second-order) accelerated rates, improving the diffusive O(D2) dependence to O(D) (up to logs) on graphs with geometry (e.g., cycles and tori). The work connects Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted, non-reversible Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods.

Relation to prior work: Prior Min-Sum/consensus analyses (e.g., Moallemi and Van Roy [27]) showed non-convergence of ordinary Min-Sum on cycles and introduced a softened formulation, with convergence characterized via a 2m-dimensional edge dynamics and mixing-time arguments on directed-edge chains. This paper instead (Sections 5–6) derives an exact node-based 2n-dimensional auxiliary system whose spectral analysis is substantially easier, and then tunes splitting parameters to match the accelerated shift-register matrix studied in numerical analysis and network optimization (e.g., Golub–Varga [15], Boyd–Xiao [41], and survey [13,24]). Compared to lifted Markov chains [5,8,18,37], the proposed approach achieves the same square-root speedup without constructing an explicit lifted graph; the “directionality” appears implicitly via splitting and manifests as momentum in the auxiliary node dynamics. The rates obtained are competitive with the best known accelerated consensus schemes (e.g., Nesterov-based and Chebyshev-accelerated methods [24,30,36]), and improve on [27] beyond cycles and regular graphs by providing general-graph spectral guarantees.

Technical summary and main results: For quadratic φv(z)=1/2 z2 − b_v z, the authors show Min-Sum Splitting reduces to linear updates on directed edges for quadratic message parameters (R̂, r̂) (Algorithm 2). Crucially, they construct an auxiliary node-based linear recursion (Algorithm 3) for pairs (r,q) and (R,Q), governed by a 2n×2n matrix K(δ,Γ). With the choice δ=1 and Γ=γW, where W is any symmetric averaging matrix with W1=1 and spectral radius ρW=ρ(W−11ᵀ/n)<1, and γ=2/(1+√(1−ρW²)), Theorem 1 shows convergence to the average with asymptotic contraction factor ρK=√((1−√(1−ρW²))/(1+√(1−ρW²)))<ρW, i.e., the shift-register rate. This yields an O(1/(1−ρK) log((1/(1−ρK))/ε)) iteration complexity, which, on cycles/tori where 1/(1−ρW)=Θ(D²), becomes O(D log(D/ε)), a square-root improvement over diffusive schemes. The analysis also clarifies how edge-based splitting induces momentum on nodes and why global spectral information is required to tune γ, paralleling both lifted chains and multi-step acceleration.

Quality: The technical development appears sound and carefully linked to known accelerated linear-system solvers. The equivalence between Algorithms 2 and 3 (Proposition 2) is insightful and enables a clean spectral analysis. The main theorem’s constants and assumptions are explicit (symmetric W, exact γ). Proofs are deferred to the supplement; given the alignment with established shift-register analyses, the claims seem credible. One practical caveat is the reliance on global spectral information (ρW) to set γ; the paper acknowledges this and positions it consistently with other accelerated methods.

Clarity: The paper is generally very well written and situates the contribution clearly across BP/consensus/acceleration literatures. A few presentation issues merit attention:
- On p.4 (Section 2), the wording “splitting each term φ_vw into Γ_vw terms, and each φ_v into δ terms” is ambiguous since δ and Γ_vw are introduced as real numbers. Footnote 1 notes the integer-splitting interpretation but the algorithm allows real parameters. Please clarify that the “splitting” is an algebraic reparameterization valid for real δ,Γ (not literal replication), or restrict the statement to integer parameters and then extend by continuity to reals.
- Algorithm 2’s output expression for x_t,v appears to be missing the division by the effective coefficient; Proposition 1 indicates the correct estimator is x_t,v = r_t,v / R_t,v (after mapping from edge to node parameters). Please fix the formula to avoid confusion.
- Some equation/label misalignments (e.g., K̂ definition numbering) and heavy notation could be streamlined. A brief notational table for Γ, A, W, δ, and the different K/K̂ matrices would help.

Originality: High. This is, to my knowledge, the first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, and the first explicit bridge between BP-style splitting, lifted non-reversible chains, and multi-step acceleration.

Significance: The conceptual bridge is valuable and likely to influence both the BP and distributed optimization communities. The rates are near-optimal on geometric graphs and broadly applicable to arbitrary topologies, albeit for quadratic consensus. Extending the approach to general convex objectives or to ADMM-like message passing would enhance impact.

Practicality and limitations:
- Parameter tuning requires ρW (or a tight bound). Discussion of distributed estimation or robust choices (e.g., Chebyshev schedules using degree-based bounds, or adaptive/backtracking γ) would strengthen applicability.
- No empirical results are presented. Even a modest comparison (e.g., on cycles/grids/geometric graphs) versus Metropolis, Chebyshev, Nesterov, and consensus propagation [27] would illustrate constants, robustness to γ mis-specification, and message complexity.
- Assumptions include synchronous updates and undirected, symmetric W. Extensions to asynchrony, time-varying graphs, or directed graphs are open.
- The analysis is for quadratic objectives; clarifying whether the auxiliary-process technique extends to more general convex separable φ_v with equality constraints would be interesting.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and rigorous convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, with a clean auxiliary-system technique.
- Conceptually unifies BP splitting with accelerated consensus via momentum and with lifting via directionality.
- Competitive accelerated rates on general graphs; near-optimal on geometric graphs.
- Clear writing and thorough positioning within the literature.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Dependence on global spectral information for tuning; limited discussion of robustness or adaptive tuning.
- Lack of experimental validation and practical guidance on choosing W and γ in real networks.
- Scope limited to quadratic consensus; generalization to broader objectives not demonstrated.

Overall recommendation: Leaning accept. The theoretical advances and conceptual connections outweigh the practical limitations; addressing the noted clarity issues and adding a short experimental section (if feasible) would further strengthen the paper.
