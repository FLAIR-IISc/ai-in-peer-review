This paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for average consensus. The authors show that ordinary Min-Sum may diverge on loopy graphs, while a split/reparametrized variant converges. They derive an auxiliary node-space linear system whose transition matrix matches the shift-register form and prove accelerated “subdiffusive” rates; for cycles and tori this yields O(D log(D/ε)). They also highlight conceptual links between splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step first-order methods.

Quality: The analysis is careful and, as far as I can tell, technically sound; the auxiliary node-space formulation is neat and makes the spectral argument transparent. However, the empirical and comparative evaluation is not commensurate with the claims. The claimed improvement is modest, and the discussion of state-of-the-art rates is incomplete.

Clarity: The paper reads well overall. Two issues: (i) p.6, line 243: using directed edges on a “simple graph” without clear orientation/notation is confusing—please rephrase. (ii) p.6, line 220: “Laplacian” should be “Lagrangian.” The statement in the last paragraph of p.5 reiterates a long-known fact; please cite Rabbat–Nowak–Bucklew and acknowledge known connections to Heavy Ball/Polyak and Nesterov.

Originality: The main novelty is framing min-sum splitting so that its auxiliary dynamics coincide with shift-register/Nesterov-like acceleration. Yet, given that consensus reduces to a linear system, the proposed “auxiliary message passing” largely mirrors standard acceleration and seems to add limited algorithmic novelty beyond re-deriving the K-matrix. Proposition 3 appears trivial and better relegated to the supplement.

Significance: The rate O(D log(D/ε)) is close to known accelerated rates, but offers little improvement over O(D log(1/ε)) (e.g., Olshevsky’s “Linear Time Average Consensus…”), and in the worst case (D≈n) can be inferior. The claim about “dimension-independent” complexity on complete graphs is misleading—complete graphs reach consensus in one step with appropriate weights—please clarify which graph classes enjoy dimension-independent complexity and define “good” graphs. Any gains appear to stem from spectral-gap handling; in worst case the bound remains O(n) since O(n)=O(D).

Related work: The paper should cite and compare with: Olshevsky’s linear-time average consensus; ADMM with linear convergence (Shi et al.); Scaman et al. on optimal distributed optimization; EXTRA (Shi et al.); fast distributed gradient (Jakovetić et al.); Markov chain lifting and distributed ADMM (França and Bento). Report their linear rates and discuss competitiveness.

Pros:
- Clear analysis and useful auxiliary formulation
- Bridges BP/splitting with acceleration and lifting

Cons:
- Overstated novelty and misleading claims on complete graphs
- Missing/insufficient comparisons to state-of-the-art linear-time methods
- Limited worst-case improvement; some results are routine

After rebuttal: I appreciate the clarifications, but my overall evaluation remains unchanged.
