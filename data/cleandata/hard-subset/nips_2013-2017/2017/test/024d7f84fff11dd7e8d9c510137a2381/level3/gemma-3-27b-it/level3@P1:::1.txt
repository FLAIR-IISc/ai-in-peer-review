This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum Splitting to the consensus problem in distributed optimization. The authors demonstrate that while the standard Min-Sum algorithm may not converge, a modified version – Splitting – does converge and, importantly, achieves accelerated convergence rates comparable to those obtained with shift-register methods. The work establishes a connection between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step first-order methods, offering a novel perspective on these established techniques.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and provides a thorough contextualization of the work within the existing literature. The authors carefully relate their approach to prior research on distributed consensus algorithms, including diffusive methods, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated gradient methods. The extensive citations demonstrate a clear understanding of the field and position the current work appropriately. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, outlines the challenges with existing approaches, and clearly states the paper's contributions. The detailed exposition and clear notation enhance the readability and accessibility of the complex theoretical results.

The strength of this work lies in its theoretical rigor and the novelty of its findings. The proof of convergence and the establishment of accelerated convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting represent a significant advance. The connection drawn between Min-Sum Splitting and existing acceleration techniques, highlighting the underlying principles shared across different approaches, is a valuable contribution. The paper tackles a challenging problem with a novel solution and rigorously analyzes its performance, demonstrating a strong technical foundation. The authors provide convincing evidence for the potential impact of this method, particularly in large-scale distributed optimization scenarios. 

However, a minor clarity issue exists on page 4. The description of "splitting" the terms φvw and φv using Γvw and δ, respectively, could be more precise. Presenting Γvw and δ as real numbers immediately before describing them as the number of split terms is slightly confusing. While the subsequent explanation clarifies the concept, this initial phrasing could benefit from re-wording.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of Min-Sum Splitting to consensus with a proven convergence rate and acceleration is a significant novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The analysis is rigorous, and the proofs appear well-constructed. The convergence rates are theoretically established and linked to existing methods.
*   **Significance:**  The potential for improved convergence in distributed optimization problems is substantial. The connection to other established techniques offers valuable insights and opens avenues for further research.
*   **Clarity:** Despite the minor issue mentioned above, the paper is generally exceptionally clear and well-organized. The exposition is thorough and accessible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Minor clarity issue:** The wording on page 4 regarding the splitting parameters could be improved for better understanding. (This is a minor point and does not significantly detract from the overall quality of the paper.)
*   **Limited empirical validation:** The paper focuses primarily on theoretical analysis. While the theoretical results are compelling, some empirical validation, even on small-scale examples, could strengthen the claims.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The clarity of the writing, the rigor of the analysis, and the significance of the findings make this a strong candidate for acceptance. The minor clarity issue is easily addressed and does not outweigh the substantial merits of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity concern on page 4.
