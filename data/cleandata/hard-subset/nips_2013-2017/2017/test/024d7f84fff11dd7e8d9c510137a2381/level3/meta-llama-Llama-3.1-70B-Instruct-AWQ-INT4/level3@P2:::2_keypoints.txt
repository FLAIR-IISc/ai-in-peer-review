- Paper proposes Min-Sum Splitting for consensus and proves convergence with tunable parameters achieving subdiffusive accelerated rates.  
- Main contributions: connects Min-Sum with lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex methods; analyzes directionality in Belief Propagation for acceleration; first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting.  
- Paper is well-written, clear, and provides a concise introduction to Min-Sum and consensus.  
- Claimed convergence improvement O(D log(D/ε)) over O(D log(1/ε)) is unimpressive; worst-case bound remains O(n)=O(D).  
- Improvement is not significant given worst-case O(n) dependence across n-node graphs.  
- Missing important citations and comparisons, e.g., Olshevsky’s "Linear Time Average Consensus..." and consensus-optimization works by Shi et al. (ADMM linear rates) and Scaman et al.  
- Claim about complete graphs is misleading (complete graphs converge in one step); authors should clarify which graph classes yield dimension-independent complexity and define “good” graphs.  
- Contribution is unclear; auxiliary message-passing may simply mirror Nesterov acceleration on Min-Sum, with gains likely from spectral-gap handling.  
- Reviewer skeptical that proposed algorithm is substantially different or superior to prior work.  
- Minor errors: typo "Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian" (page 6) and confusing statement about using directed edges on a simple graph.  
- Quality score 7/10: well-written but concerns on claimed rates and missing comparisons.  
- Clarity score 8/10: organized and understandable but some confusing statements remain.  
- Originality score 6/10: interesting ideas but not clearly distinct from prior methods.  
- Significance score 7/10: relevant results but unclear advantage over existing algorithms.  
- Arguments for acceptance: interesting contributions, clear exposition, and relevance to distributed optimization.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unimpressive claimed rate improvement, missing key citations/comparisons, unclear novelty and relation to existing accelerations.
