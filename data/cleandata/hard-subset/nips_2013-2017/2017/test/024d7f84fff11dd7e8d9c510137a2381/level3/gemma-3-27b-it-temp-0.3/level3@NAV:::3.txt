## Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates accelerated convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors leverage a reparameterization from Ruozzi & Tatikonda (2013) and introduce an auxiliary linear process to analyze convergence on quadratic consensus problems. The core contribution lies in establishing a novel proof technique via this auxiliary process, demonstrating improved convergence rates compared to standard Min-Sum, and drawing connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods.

The work builds upon prior research in distributed consensus optimization, notably the work of Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis (1989) and more recent advancements in decentralized optimization like those by Srinivasan et al. (2009) and Shi (2015). It also positions itself within the broader context of acceleration techniques, referencing Polyak and Nesterov’s work. The paper correctly identifies the limitations of standard Min-Sum in cyclic graphs, referencing prior work by [27], and aims to overcome these limitations through the splitting approach. The connection to lifted Markov chains, as highlighted, is a valuable contribution, linking the algorithm to a well-established area of research.

The strengths of the paper are its theoretical rigor and the novelty of the auxiliary process technique. The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appear sound. The paper clearly demonstrates a connection between Min-Sum Splitting and established acceleration methods. However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While generally well-written, numerous typos and grammatical errors detract from readability (e.g., “theirs neighbors,” “double-stochastic,” “casted”). The algorithm descriptions are incomplete; variables like R{vw} and r{vw} are not clearly defined, and initializations are missing. The authors should explicitly map variables to the auxiliary process in Algorithm 3. 

More importantly, the paper lacks a thorough discussion of practicality. The authors should address when this method would be preferred over existing distributed consensus methods and provide insights into its computational complexity. The intuition behind using the auxiliary process could be more clearly explained. Furthermore, the paper doesn’t sufficiently address the limitations of Min-Sum Splitting or explore its potential extension to more general consensus problems common in machine learning, failing to clearly articulate its relevance to the machine learning community.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical contribution with a new proof technique.
*   Improved convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting.
*   Interesting connections to established techniques (lifted Markov chains, multi-step methods).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of practical discussion and analysis.
*   Incomplete algorithm descriptions and unclear variable mapping.
*   Insufficiently addresses limitations and broader applicability.
*   Numerous typos and grammatical errors.



**Overall:** This is a theoretically interesting paper with a solid contribution to the understanding of Min-Sum Splitting. However, the lack of practical considerations and clarity issues prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection with request for major revisions**, contingent on addressing the concerns outlined above. The authors should focus on clarifying the algorithm, discussing its practicality, and expanding on its potential applications.
