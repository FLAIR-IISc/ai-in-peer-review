- Studies accelerated convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus problems, focusing on quadratic consensus.  
- Introduces an auxiliary linear process to track algorithm evolution and prove convergence guarantees.  
- Demonstrates connections between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step convex optimization methods.  
- Paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs, but dense notation makes detailed verification difficult for non-experts.  
- Numerous minor typos and grammatical errors reduce polish (e.g., "theirs neighbors," "double-stochastic," "casted").  
- Algorithm descriptions are incomplete: variables like R{vw}, r{vw} and initializations for \hat{R}^0, \hat{r}^0 are not fully defined.  
- The correspondence between variables in Algorithm 2 and the auxiliary process in Algorithm 3 is not explicitly stated.  
- Introduction assumes high prior knowledge; a more intuitive explanation of the auxiliary process is needed.  
- Novel use of Ruozzi & Tatikonda (2013) reparameterization applied to Min-Sum and insightful cross-domain connections.  
- Related work coverage is incomplete—missing key decentralized optimization and acceleration literature (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis; Srinivasan et al.; Wei Shi; Polyak; Nesterov).  
- Results are potentially significant but lack discussion of practical implications, computational cost, scalability, and when the method is preferable.  
- The connection and relevance to machine learning audiences are not clearly articulated.  
- Arguments for acceptance: rigorous theoretical analysis, novel connections, useful auxiliary-process analysis tool, generally sound proofs.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient practical discussion, clarity and exposition issues, omitted related work, weak ML relevance, and many typos.  
- Questions for authors include practical computational overhead and advantages, intuition/motivation for the auxiliary process, extensions beyond quadratics, limitations/poor graph topologies, and ML relevance.  
- Overall assessment: theoretically interesting but lacking practical discussion and clarity; recommendation—reject but encourage revision and resubmission addressing clarity, related work, and practical relevance.
