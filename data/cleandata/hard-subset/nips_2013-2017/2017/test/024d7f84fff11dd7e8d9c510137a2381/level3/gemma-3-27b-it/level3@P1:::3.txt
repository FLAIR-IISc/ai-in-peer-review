This paper investigates accelerated convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem in distributed optimization. The authors build upon the reparameterization technique introduced by Ruozzi and Tatikonda (2013) and provide a theoretical analysis of convergence for quadratic consensus problems. A central technical contribution is the introduction of an auxiliary linear process designed to track the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, enabling a more tractable analysis. The paper claims several key contributions: a novel proof technique leveraging the auxiliary process, improved convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, and establishing connections to both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step methods from convex optimization.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured, presenting a logical flow from background to contributions. However, it suffers from a notable number of typographical errors and minor grammatical inconsistencies throughout, detracting from the overall polish.  While the proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear correct upon initial inspection, a more detailed review would be necessary for complete validation.  Specific examples of typos include "theirs neighbors" (should be "their neighbors"), "double-stochastic" (should be "doubly-stochastic"), and "casted" (should be "cast").  

The technical content is sound, building upon established concepts in message passing and distributed optimization. The use of an auxiliary process is a clever approach to analyzing the complex dynamics of Min-Sum Splitting.  However, the presentation of Algorithms 2 and 3 is somewhat incomplete. Some variables (e.g., R{vw}, r{vw}) are not clearly defined, and initializations for variables like  \hat{R}^0 and \hat{r}^0 are missing, making it difficult to fully grasp the algorithmic implementation. Explicitly identifying which variables in Algorithm 3 correspond to components of the auxiliary linear process would significantly improve clarity.

The related work section, while extensive, appears to omit some relevant literature. Specifically, seminal works on (sub)gradient methods and decentralized optimization, such as Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis (1989), Srinivasan et al. (2009), and Wei Shi (2015), are not cited. Furthermore, key contributions in classical acceleration methods, like those by Polyak and Nesterov, are also missing. Including these references would contextualize the authors' work more effectively and acknowledge the broader research landscape.

Several questions arise regarding the practical implications of this research.  The authors should comment on the computational overhead and scalability of Min-Sum Splitting compared to other distributed consensus methods. Under what conditions would this approach be preferred, and what are its limitations? A more intuitive explanation of the auxiliary process and its role in achieving acceleration would also be beneficial. Finally, while the theoretical results are interesting, the authors should elaborate on whether the framework can be extended beyond quadratic objectives to address more general consensus problems commonly encountered in machine learning applications.  A clearer statement of the paper’s relevance and potential impact on the machine learning community is needed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical Novelty:** The paper presents a novel analysis of Min-Sum Splitting, establishing convergence rates and demonstrating connections to well-known acceleration techniques.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations and proofs appear rigorous (pending detailed verification).
*   **Auxiliary Process:** The introduction of the auxiliary linear process is a clever and effective analytical tool.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper successfully links Min-Sum Splitting to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Practical Discussion:** The paper lacks a discussion of the practical implications and limitations of the proposed algorithm.
*   **Incomplete Algorithmic Presentation:** Algorithms 2 and 3 are not fully specified, hindering comprehension.
*   **Omitted Related Work:** The related work section is incomplete and omits relevant references.
*   **Limited Intuition:** The motivation and intuition behind certain choices, particularly the auxiliary process, are not fully explained.
*   **Typographical Errors:** The presence of numerous typos detracts from the overall quality of the manuscript.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The analysis of Min-Sum Splitting is rigorous, and the connections to established methods are insightful. However, the lack of practical discussion, incomplete algorithmic details, and omission of related work limit its immediate impact. While the work has merit, it would benefit significantly from addressing these concerns. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent on addressing the identified issues and clarifying the practical relevance of the proposed algorithm.  Specifically, the authors should revise the algorithms for completeness, augment the related work section, provide a more intuitive explanation of the auxiliary process, and discuss the potential applications and limitations of Min-Sum Splitting in a practical setting.  Finally, a careful proofread to eliminate typographical errors is essential.
