### Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

**Summary**

This paper investigates the application of the Min-Sum Splitting message-passing algorithm to the average consensus problem. The authors show that while the standard Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge on graphs with cycles, the "Splitting" variant can be tuned to guarantee convergence. The main contribution is a proof that with a specific choice of parameters, Min-Sum Splitting achieves an accelerated convergence rate of O(D log(D/ε)), where D is the graph diameter. The paper establishes this by introducing an auxiliary message-passing scheme on the graph nodes, which reduces the analysis to that of a multi-step linear system. The authors also draw conceptual connections between their approach, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods in convex optimization, such as shift-register methods.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality & Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and technically coherent. The derivation showing that Min-Sum Splitting for quadratic consensus reduces to a linear system on the directed edges (Proposition 1) and can be tracked by an auxiliary multi-step process on the nodes (Proposition 2) is clear and serves as a useful analytical tool.

However, the paper contains several technical and clarity issues:
*   **Misleading Claims:** The introduction's discussion of convergence time on "graphs with good expansion properties, such as complete graphs" is misleading. Average consensus on a complete graph is trivial and converges in a single communication step. The authors should clarify for which specific graph classes their method yields a dimension-independent complexity and provide a formal definition of "good" graphs in this context.
*   **Trivial Proposition:** Proposition 3, which establishes the equivalence of outputs between Algorithms 2 and 3 under certain initializations, appears to be a straightforward consequence of the reparametrization and offers limited supplementary insight.
*   **Minor Errors:** There is a typo on page 6, line 220, where "Laplacian" should likely be "Lagrangian." Additionally, the phrasing on page 6, line 243, about using directed edges on a "simple graph" is confusing and could be stated more precisely.

**Originality & Significance**

The paper's primary weakness lies in its positioning relative to existing literature. While the connection between Min-Sum and accelerated methods is interesting, the work omits a significant body of highly relevant prior art, which substantially diminishes the perceived originality and significance of its contributions.

1.  **Missing Comparison to State-of-the-Art Consensus Rates:** The paper fails to cite or compare its results with several key works that achieve competitive or superior rates. Most notably, the work of Olshevsky ("Linear Time Average Consensus...", 2014) establishes a convergence rate of O(D log(1/ε)). The rate proposed here, O(D log(D/ε)), offers a marginal improvement at best and is strictly worse in the worst-case scenario where the diameter D is on the order of the number of nodes n. This omission is critical for evaluating the paper's contribution to the state of the art.

2.  **Omission of Key Consensus-Optimization Literature:** The paper is missing crucial references in the broader field of decentralized/consensus optimization.
    *   The linear convergence of ADMM for consensus problems was established by Shi et al. ("On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM...", 2014).
    *   Recent work by Scaman et al. ("Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization...", NIPS 2017) provides tight analyses and establishes optimal rates for this class of problems.
    *   Other foundational first-order methods like EXTRA by Shi et al. (2015) and "Fast distributed gradient methods" by Jakovetic et al. (2014) are also directly relevant and should have been discussed.

3.  **Overstated Novelty of Acceleration Mechanism:** The authors frame the connection between their method and momentum-based acceleration as a novel insight. However, this connection is well-established. The last paragraph of page 5, which discusses the memory term, describes a long-known principle. The authors should cite prior work such as Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew (2005) and explicitly acknowledge the well-known relationship to Polyak's Heavy Ball method and Nesterov acceleration. The proposed "auxiliary message-passing" scheme appears to be a re-derivation of Nesterov-style acceleration within the min-sum framework. Since the consensus analysis ultimately reduces to analyzing the spectral properties of a linear system, the novelty of this mechanism is unclear.

4.  **Ignored Related Work on Lifting:** The paper misses a direct and relevant reference by Franca and Bento ("Markov chain lifting and distributed ADMM," 2016), which explicitly connects Markov chain lifting techniques with distributed ADMM, a topic central to this paper's discussion.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper presents a technically sound analysis of an accelerated Min-Sum algorithm for average consensus. Its strength lies in formally connecting the Min-Sum Splitting framework to established multi-step linear methods. However, the work suffers from a critical lack of engagement with the existing literature. The claimed performance improvement is not significant when compared to uncited state-of-the-art results, and several of the conceptual contributions have been explored previously. To be a strong contribution to the field, the paper must be thoroughly revised to:
1.  Incorporate and provide a detailed comparison with the missing key references, especially Olshevsky (2014) and Scaman et al. (2017).
2.  Accurately position its contributions by acknowledging the well-established links between multi-step methods, momentum, and acceleration in distributed optimization.
3.  Clarify its claims regarding specific graph classes and refine its technical presentation.

Without these revisions, the paper's claims of novelty and performance are overstated, and its overall contribution to the NIPS community is limited.
