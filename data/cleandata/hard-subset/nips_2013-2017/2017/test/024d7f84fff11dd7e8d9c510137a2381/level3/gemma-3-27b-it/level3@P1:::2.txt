This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum Splitting to the consensus problem, aiming to establish convergence rates and demonstrate acceleration compared to traditional diffusive methods. The core idea is to modify the standard Min-Sum algorithm through a reparametrization (splitting) to achieve convergence even in graphs where the ordinary Min-Sum fails, and to potentially improve convergence speed. The authors prove convergence and establish a rate of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles and tori, relating this to shift-register methods and connections to lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order optimization.

The work builds upon prior research in distributed optimization and message-passing algorithms. The authors correctly identify the limitations of standard Min-Sum in non-tree graphs and reference relevant work on sufficient conditions for convergence [28, 25]. They position their contribution within the broader context of overcoming diffusive convergence rates in consensus algorithms, citing early work [42, 41] and more recent advancements [29, 40, 17, 10, 6, 16, 39]. The paper also acknowledges the link between consensus and distributed machine learning [31, 11, 26, 3].

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, and the derivation of convergence rates is well-motivated. The introduction of an auxiliary process to track the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm is a clever approach that simplifies the analysis.
*   **Clear Connections:** The paper successfully draws parallels between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods, highlighting the underlying similarities in their acceleration mechanisms. The connection to shift-register methods is particularly insightful.
*   **Novelty:** While the core idea of Min-Sum splitting isn’t entirely new [34, 35], the detailed theoretical analysis of convergence rates and the explicit link to shift-register methods represent a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Improvement:** The claimed improvement in convergence rate, O(D log(D/ε)), does not represent a substantial leap forward compared to existing methods. As noted in the key points, this is particularly true when considering Olshevsky’s “Linear Time Average Consensus…” [Olshevsky, 2007] which achieves a competitive or better rate of D log(1/ε).  The claim on complete graphs is misleading, as they converge in a single step, and the authors need to more precisely define which graph classes benefit from their approach and exhibit dimension-independent complexity.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The paper omits several important references in the consensus-optimization literature. Crucially, the work of Shi et al. on the linear convergence of ADMM [Shi et al., 2015, 2016] and Scaman et al. [Scaman et al., 2016] should be discussed. The omission of the related work on Markov chain lifting and distributed ADMM by França and Bento [França & Bento, 2017] is also a significant oversight.
*   **Lack of Contextualization:** The discussion on prior work lacks sufficient detail regarding state-of-the-art algorithms and their linear convergence rates. Providing a more comprehensive comparison would better position the contribution of this paper.  The statement on page 5 about accelerated methods being developed independently is a known fact and should be attributed to appropriate sources like Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew [Rabbat et al., 2006] along with connections to Heavy Ball/Nesterov/Polyak methods.
*   **Proposition 3’s Value:** Proposition 3 appears trivial and contributes little to the overall understanding of the algorithm.
*   **Minor Issues:** There are a few minor issues with clarity, such as the confusing use of “directed edges on a simple graph” (p.6, line 243) and a typo ("Laplacian" instead of "Lagrangian" on p.6, line 220).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound analysis of Min-Sum Splitting and provides valuable insights into its convergence properties.
*   The connections drawn between Min-Sum Splitting and other acceleration techniques are well-articulated.
*   The work contributes to a deeper understanding of distributed optimization algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The claimed improvement in convergence rate is modest and does not significantly advance the state-of-the-art.
*   The paper omits important related work, potentially giving a skewed view of the existing landscape.
*   The presentation of the results lacks sufficient contextualization within the broader consensus-optimization literature.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper is a solid technical contribution that provides a rigorous analysis of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus. However, its impact is limited by the incremental nature of the improvement and the omission of relevant related work. While the paper is technically sound and well-written, its significance is somewhat diminished by these shortcomings. I recommend **weak accept** with a request for the authors to address the missing references and provide a more nuanced discussion of the existing literature.  The authors should also clarify the specific graph classes where their approach offers a demonstrable advantage.
