

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum message-passing with splitting to solve the consensus problem in distributed optimization, claiming accelerated convergence rates. The authors establish that Min-Sum Splitting converges to the problem solution, with proper parameter tuning yielding subdiffusive convergence rates matching shift-register methods. They connect their approach to both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first order methods in convex optimization.

The paper makes several contributions: (1) first connection of Min-Sum schemes with lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods, (2) proof that directionality in Belief Propagation can be tuned for acceleration, (3) first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, and (4) a new protocol achieving better rates than previous Min-Sum approaches.

Strengths include a theoretically sound analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting and the novel connection to established acceleration techniques. The proof technique using an auxiliary process to track the algorithm's evolution on nodes is well-motivated and provides a clearer path to analyzing convergence than previous work on Min-Sum for consensus.

However, several weaknesses significantly impact the paper's contribution. The claimed improvement is overstated: the convergence rate of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori offers little practical advantage over the established O(D log(1/ε)) rate from Olshevsky's "Linear Time Average Consensus" (2015), which the authors fail to cite or compare against. The authors' claim that complete graphs yield dimension-independent complexity is misleading—complete graphs actually converge in one step, which should be clarified. The paper also omits crucial related work, including Olshevsky's linear-time consensus algorithm, Shi et al.'s EXTRA method for distributed optimization with linear convergence rates, and Scaman et al.'s work on optimal distributed optimization algorithms. The omission of França and Bento's work on Markov chain lifting and distributed ADMM is particularly egregious given the paper's connection claims.

Several technical issues undermine the paper's credibility. The "auxiliary message-passing" scheme appears to simply mirror Nesterov acceleration applied to the consensus problem, with the contribution being unclear since the consensus analysis reduces to standard linear system analysis. Proposition 3 is trivial and only a supplementary contribution. The claim that the algorithm achieves "subdiffusive" rates is misleading—the worst-case bound remains O(n) since D=Θ(n) for many graph topologies, which is not an improvement over existing methods.

The paper contains several errors that should be corrected: the "Laplacian" on line 220 should be "Lagrangian," and the use of directed edges on a simple graph (line 243) is confusing. The last paragraph of page 5 states a long-known fact about consensus without proper citation to Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew, who established similar connections to Heavy Ball/Nesterov/Polyak methods.

The significance of the work is limited by these omissions and overstatements. While the theoretical connection between Min-Sum and accelerated methods is interesting, the practical contribution is marginal given the lack of comparison to state-of-the-art algorithms. The paper would benefit from a clearer statement of how Min-Sum Splitting differs from existing accelerated consensus methods in both theory and practice.

Quality is adequate but the claims require stronger justification. The theoretical analysis is sound but the practical implications are overstated. Clarity is generally good, though the misleading claims about convergence rates and omitted references detract from the paper's value. Originality is somewhat limited by the lack of proper comparison to established work, though the connection between Min-Sum and acceleration techniques is novel. Significance is modest as the claimed improvement is minimal and the connection to existing methods is not sufficiently differentiated.

Pro arguments for acceptance: Novel theoretical connection between Min-Sum and acceleration techniques, sound convergence analysis, useful proof technique with auxiliary process.

Con arguments for rejection: Overstated claims about convergence rate improvements, missing key references and comparisons, misleading statements about graph classes, trivial contribution in Proposition 3, technical errors.

The paper makes an interesting theoretical connection but fails to properly contextualize its contribution within the existing literature on distributed consensus and optimization. The claimed improvements are minimal compared to established results, and the paper would benefit from significantly revised claims and a more thorough literature review. Without these revisions, the paper's contribution appears marginal for NIPS.
