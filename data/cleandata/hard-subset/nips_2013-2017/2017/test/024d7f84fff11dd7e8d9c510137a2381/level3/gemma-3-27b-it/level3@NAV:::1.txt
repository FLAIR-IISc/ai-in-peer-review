## Review of “Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting”

This paper investigates the application of a modified Min-Sum message-passing protocol – specifically, Min-Sum Splitting – to the distributed consensus problem. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that, unlike the standard Min-Sum algorithm, this splitting approach *can* converge, and moreover, achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence rates matching those of shift-register methods. Importantly, the paper establishes a novel and insightful connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization, providing a deeper understanding of its theoretical underpinnings.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly situates its contribution within the existing literature. It meticulously details related work in distributed optimization, consensus algorithms (including diffusive methods and accelerated approaches like those based on lifted Markov chains), and message-passing techniques. The authors effectively highlight the limitations of prior work and precisely define how their approach advances the state-of-the-art. The theoretical analysis appears sound and the results are novel; while I am not an expert in this specific area, the arguments are presented convincingly and the connection to established convergence rates (shift-register methods) strengthens the claim. The derivation of the auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) is a particularly clever move, simplifying the analysis and allowing for a clearer understanding of the convergence behavior.

However, the presentation on page 4 – specifically the wording “splitting each term φ_vw into Γ_vw terms, and each φ_v into δ terms” – is ambiguous. While the authors introduce δ and Γ_vw as real numbers earlier, describing the “splitting” as creating a discrete number of terms feels imprecise and could benefit from further clarification, potentially with a simple example. This isn’t a major flaw but introduces a moment of confusion. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of Min-Sum Splitting to consensus and the achievement of accelerated rates are novel contributions.
*   **Significance:** The improved convergence rates are potentially impactful for large-scale distributed optimization problems.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis and connects the results to known bounds.
*   **Clarity:** Generally exceptionally well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.
* **Connection to broader fields:** Establishing links between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains and first-order methods provides valuable insight.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Minor Ambiguity:** The explanation of “splitting” on page 4 could be clearer. 

**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The strengths far outweigh the minor weakness. I recommend **acceptance** with a minor revision to clarify the description of the splitting process on page 4.
