Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the average-consensus problem through Min-Sum with Splitting, and shows that a particular parametrization leads to a linear message-passing scheme whose evolution can be tracked by an auxiliary node-based linear system with memory. With an appropriate choice of parameters (δ = 1 and Γ = γW with γ tuned via the spectral gap of W), the method achieves an accelerated, subdiffusive convergence rate that matches shift-register/Chebyshev-accelerated consensus. Conceptually, the authors position their scheme between lifted Markov chains (directionality on a lifted space of directed edges) and multi-step first-order acceleration (momentum/shift-register on nodes).

This connects Min-Sum Splitting to classical acceleration for linear consensus (Chebyshev/shift-register, Heavy Ball, Nesterov) and to work on lifting. It also complements prior observations that ordinary Min-Sum (Gaussian BP) may not converge on graphs with cycles, while Splitting variants can.

Strengths
- Clear derivation of a linear, quadratic-form Min-Sum Splitting for consensus and an auxiliary node-based system whose spectral analysis is tractable.
- Theorem 1 cleanly recovers the known accelerated rate (√-improvement over diffusive mixing), aligning the Min-Sum scheme with shift-register methods.
- The link drawn between directionality on edges and momentum on nodes is insightful and pedagogical; the writing is generally clear and the proofs appear sound.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- Limited novelty in acceleration: The main rate O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles/tori reproduces known shift-register/Chebyshev acceleration. The contribution is primarily a reinterpretation of this acceleration as a specific Min-Sum Splitting plus an auxiliary linear system. Since consensus reduces to analyzing a linear system, the added value beyond reparameterizing known accelerated consensus is modest.
- Claim about “good” graphs is misleading/incomplete: For complete graphs, one can reach the exact average in a single synchronous step with W = 11^T/n; calling the Θ(log(1/ε)) behavior optimal for “good expansion” conflates classes. Please (i) explicitly define what “good graphs” means, (ii) distinguish complete graphs from expanders, and (iii) clarify which graph classes enjoy dimension-independent complexity and under what algorithmic constraints (e.g., nonnegativity of weights/reversibility).
- Comparison to Olshevsky (Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs, 2014) is missing. That work attains O(D log(1/ε)) on relevant classes and, in the worst case, can be competitive or better than your O(D log(D/ε)) bound; when D ≍ n your bound can be strictly worse. You should cite, compare rates and assumptions, and clarify whether your method achieves O(D log(1/ε)) anywhere.
- Missing or underemphasized consensus-optimization references and state-of-the-art linear rates:
  - Shi et al., On the linear convergence of ADMM in decentralized consensus optimization (2014/2015): report and compare linear rates and graph dependence; explain where your method sits versus ADMM-based consensus.
  - Scaman et al., Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks (ICML 2017): provide optimal complexity in terms of condition number and spectral gap; discuss implications for averaging (quadratics) and how your rate compares to their lower/upper bounds.
  - EXTRA (Shi et al., 2015) and fast distributed gradient methods (Jakovetić et al., 2014): both provide linear convergence and accelerated dependence on spectral gap; include an explicit quantitative comparison table or discussion.
- Lifting and distributed ADMM: França and Bento (2016) connect ADMM to lifting. You cite lifting broadly but do not substantively compare your construction and rate to this line; please add a detailed comparison (setup, overhead, rates).
- Known facts and proper attribution: The last paragraph on p.5 states the well-known equivalence between consensus and linear first-order methods with momentum. Please cite Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew (2005) and acknowledge the classic connections to Heavy Ball/Polyak and Nesterov acceleration (also Chebyshev acceleration via Golub–Varga, Young).
- Proposition 3 (in the supplement) appears to be a straightforward linear-algebraic identity supporting the auxiliary-system equivalence. It should be framed as a technical lemma; as is, it reads trivial and supplementary, not a main contribution.
- Spectral-gap handling vs worst-case scaling: Your acceleration depends on γ chosen from ρ(W). This mirrors Nesterov/Chebyshev and is not novel. In worst-case graphs where D = Θ(n) and 1 − ρ(W) = Θ(1/n^2), the improved time is Θ(n log(n/ε)), which remains Θ(n) in D and can be worse than Olshevsky’s O(D log(1/ε)). Please clarify the true worst-case over n-node graphs and when you match O(D log(1/ε)).
- Auxiliary process novelty: The “auxiliary message-passing” appears to be a restatement of known momentum/shift-register dynamics applied to the linear system induced by Min-Sum Splitting. Please better articulate the conceptual or practical benefit over simply running Chebyshev-accelerated consensus.
- Minor technical clarity: Line 243 (p.6): referring to directed edges on a “simple graph” is confusing; say “the directed edge (arc) set associated with the undirected simple graph” or use the directed line graph terminology. Line 220 (p.6): typo—“Laplacian” should be “Lagrangian.”

Quality
The analysis is technically sound and consistent with classical spectral arguments; the rate statements align with known Chebyshev/shift-register results. However, the evaluation versus state-of-the-art is incomplete and some claims are imprecise.

Clarity
Generally well-written. The auxiliary-system derivation helps readability. Needs clearer statements about graph classes, sharper positioning vis-à-vis prior accelerated consensus, and the above corrections.

Originality
Moderate. The main novelty is framing known accelerated consensus as Min-Sum Splitting plus an auxiliary system. The acceleration mechanism and rates themselves are not new.

Significance
Limited-to-moderate. For researchers in message passing, it offers a bridge to accelerated consensus; for consensus/optimization experts, the results largely repackage known accelerations. Impact would improve with thorough empirical/theoretical comparisons and clearer cases where Splitting provides a genuine advantage.

Pros
- Clean connection between Min-Sum Splitting, lifting, and multi-step acceleration.
- Solid linear-algebraic analysis with transparent parameters.
- Potentially useful perspective for extending Min-Sum to other objectives.

Cons
- Modest novelty in rates; worst-case complexity not improved over best-known results.
- Incomplete related-work coverage and comparisons.
- Some misleading/inexact claims about graph classes.

Questions for authors
- Can your tuning achieve O(D log(1/ε)) on cycles/tori (matching Olshevsky), or is the extra log D inherent to your analysis?
- Are there classes beyond tori/cycles where you provably beat diffusive methods and match the best-known linear-in-D rate?
- Any empirical comparison versus Chebyshev, Nesterov-accelerated consensus, EXTRA, and distributed ADMM on representative graphs?
