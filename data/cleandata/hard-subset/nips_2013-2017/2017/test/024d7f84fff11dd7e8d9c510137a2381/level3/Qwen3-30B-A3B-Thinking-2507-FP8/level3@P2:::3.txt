

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum message-passing to solve the consensus problem in distributed optimization. The authors propose a modified version called "Min-Sum Splitting" that incorporates reparametrization to achieve convergence where ordinary Min-Sum fails. They establish theoretical convergence rates for this algorithm, showing it achieves subdiffusive rates (O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle and torus graphs) that match those of shift-register methods, while connecting the approach to both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step convex optimization methods.

The work builds on prior research in distributed optimization, particularly the Min-Sum algorithm (introduced in [28]) and its convergence properties for tree-structured graphs. It extends the work of [27], which showed that Min-Sum with soft constraints does not converge on graphs with cycles, to a framework that achieves accelerated convergence. The paper also connects to the literature on accelerated consensus methods, including lifted Markov chains [8, 5, 37] and multi-step gradient methods [13, 24], establishing a novel theoretical bridge between these seemingly disparate approaches.

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution with well-supported claims. The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear correct. The authors introduce a novel proof technique using an auxiliary linear process to track the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, which is a significant methodological contribution. However, the paper lacks thorough experimental validation to complement the theoretical analysis, and the discussion of practical implementation details is limited. The paper would benefit from addressing the practical limitations of the method and comparing it with existing distributed optimization approaches.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, but contains several typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "theirs neighbors" instead of "their neighbors," "double-stochastic" instead of "doubly-stochastic"). The algorithm descriptions are incomplete, with variables like R̂vw and r̂vw not properly explained, and initializations for the algorithm parameters missing. The connection between the auxiliary process in Algorithm 3 and the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm would benefit from clearer explanation. The paper would be significantly improved with a more detailed algorithm description and a more precise explanation of the auxiliary process.

**Originality**: This paper makes several important original contributions. It establishes the first connection between Min-Sum schemes and both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step convex optimization methods. It provides the first analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, introducing a novel proof technique based on an auxiliary process. The paper demonstrates how directionality embedded in Belief Propagation protocols can be tuned for acceleration, which is a significant theoretical insight. The work is sufficiently different from previous NIPS papers (e.g., [27] and [34]) to be considered novel.

**Significance**: The results are theoretically significant as they establish a new connection between distributed optimization methods and accelerate consensus algorithms. The paper demonstrates that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve convergence rates approaching the theoretical lower bound for consensus problems. However, the paper lacks discussion of the practical relevance of these theoretical results for machine learning applications. The authors should clarify why this approach is particularly valuable compared to existing distributed optimization methods (e.g., ADMM, gradient-based methods) and under what conditions it would be preferable.

**Arguments for acceptance**:
- First theoretical analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting
- Novel proof technique using an auxiliary linear process
- Important theoretical connection between Min-Sum, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step optimization methods
- Demonstrated subdiffusive convergence rates that match the best-known results for consensus problems

**Arguments against acceptance**:
- Lack of experimental validation to complement the theoretical results
- Incomplete algorithm descriptions with missing variable explanations and initializations
- Insufficient discussion of practical relevance for machine learning applications
- Missing citations to key literature on distributed optimization (Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989, Srinivasan et al. 2009, Wei Shi 2015)
- Several grammatical errors and typos throughout the manuscript

The paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of distributed optimization methods. However, it would benefit significantly from addressing the clarity issues, adding experimental validation, and better articulating the practical relevance to machine learning. The authors should clarify the limitations of their approach, provide more intuition behind the auxiliary process, and explicitly state why this method is of interest to the machine learning community.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, as the theoretical contribution is strong and novel, but the paper needs to be significantly improved in terms of clarity, completeness, and practical relevance. The authors should address all the specific comments regarding algorithm description, missing references, and practical discussion to strengthen the paper for publication at NIPS.
