

# Review: "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum message-passing algorithms to distributed consensus optimization, focusing on quadratic consensus problems. The authors propose a Min-Sum Splitting algorithm that achieves accelerated convergence rates compared to classical diffusive methods, and establish connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods. The work builds on Ruozzi & Tatikonda's (2013) reparameterization approach and analyzes convergence for quadratic consensus problems, which is a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of message-passing algorithms in distributed optimization.

The paper's main technical contribution is the introduction of an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. This novel proof technique enables the authors to establish improved convergence rates, showing that proper tuning of the splitting parameters yields subdiffusive convergence rates matching those of shift-register methods. The authors demonstrate that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve convergence times of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, representing a square-root improvement over diffusive methods' O(D² log(1/ε)) rates.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) first connection of Min-Sum schemes with lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods; (2) first proof of how directionality in Belief Propagation protocols can be tuned to accelerate convergence; and (3) first analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting. The theoretical results are supported by correct proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1.

The paper's strengths include its clear exposition of the theoretical connections between Min-Sum Splitting and other accelerated methods, and its rigorous analysis of convergence rates. The work provides valuable theoretical insights into how message-passing algorithms can be adapted to achieve accelerated convergence in distributed consensus problems.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that limit its significance and clarity. The manuscript contains multiple typos and grammatical errors throughout (e.g., "theirs neighbors" → "their neighbors", "double-stochastic" → "doubly-stochastic", "casted" → "cast"). The algorithm descriptions are incomplete: variables like R{vw}, r{vw} are not properly defined, and initializations like \hat{R}^0, \hat{r}^0 are missing. The authors should explicitly identify which variables correspond to the auxiliary linear process in Algorithm 3.

The paper omits significant relevant literature on decentralized optimization (Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989; Srinivasan et al. 2009; Wei Shi 2015) and classical acceleration work (Polyak, Nesterov). This omission weakens the paper's context and originality claims. The authors should also clarify the practical relevance of their method to the machine learning community, as the paper lacks discussion of when this approach would be preferable to existing distributed consensus methods.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the following points: (1) providing intuition for why the auxiliary process is used and how it relates to the Min-Sum Splitting dynamics; (2) discussing the limitations of Min-Sum Splitting and whether it extends beyond quadratic objectives to more general consensus problems common in machine learning; and (3) offering practical guidance on when to use this method versus other distributed optimization approaches.

In terms of the review criteria:

*Quality*: The theoretical results are sound, but the paper lacks practical discussion and fails to address limitations. The manuscript contains numerous typos and grammatical errors that undermine the quality of presentation.

*Clarity*: The paper is generally well-written but suffers from incomplete algorithm descriptions and inconsistent terminology (e.g., "Laplacian" vs. "Lagrangian"). The authors should clarify variable definitions and provide better algorithmic exposition.

*Originality*: The theoretical connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods are novel, but the paper would benefit from more comprehensive literature review to better establish novelty.

*Significance*: The theoretical contribution is interesting and connects to important areas of optimization, but the lack of practical discussion and unclear relevance to machine learning limits its significance for the NIPS community.

In summary, this is a theoretically interesting paper that makes valuable contributions to the understanding of Min-Sum message-passing algorithms for distributed optimization. The paper establishes convergence rates and connections to known methods that will be of interest to the optimization community. However, the lack of practical discussion, incomplete algorithm descriptions, and failure to clearly articulate the relevance to machine learning limit its impact for the NIPS audience. The authors should substantially revise the manuscript to address these issues before it would be suitable for publication at NIPS.

Recommendation: Weak Accept (with major revisions) - The paper has significant theoretical merit but requires substantial revisions to clarify its practical relevance, improve algorithmic exposition, and address omitted literature.
