- Proposes an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting message-passing algorithm for distributed consensus optimization and analyzes it on quadratic problems.  
- Uses reparameterization from Ruozzi & Tatikonda (2013) and introduces an auxiliary linear process to track Min-Sum Splitting.  
- Main technical contribution: a new proof technique via the auxiliary process that yields improved convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting.  
- Establishes connections between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step convex optimization methods.  
- Shows a theoretically significant square-root improvement in asymptotic convergence time over diffusive methods.  
- Manuscript is generally clear and well-written but contains several typos and incorrect terminology (e.g., theirs neighbors, double-stochastic, casted, Laplacian/Laplace vs Lagrangian/Lagrange multipliers).  
- Algorithm descriptions are incomplete: several variables (e.g., R_{vw}, r_{vw}) and initializations (e.g., \hat{R}^0, \hat{r}^0) are not explained, and auxiliary-process variables in Algorithm 3 are not explicitly identified.  
- Omits relevant literature on (sub)gradient and decentralized optimization (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989; Srinivasan et al. 2009; Wei Shi 2015) and classical acceleration (Polyak, Nesterov).  
- Lacks discussion on practicality, when to use the method versus alternatives, and intuitive explanation of limitations and the auxiliary-process approach.  
- Key open questions: limitations of Min-Sum Splitting, intuition behind the auxiliary process, extendability beyond quadratic objectives to general ML consensus problems, and relevance to the ML community.  
- Paper is well-organized and reproducible for experts but needs clearer exposition and fixes to algorithmic details and typos.  
- Originality: novel combination of familiar techniques, with the auxiliary linear process being a new proof tool and new links to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods.  
- Results motivate further work to generalize acceleration from splittings to other problem instances beyond consensus.  
- Arguments for acceptance: new proof technique, novel connections, and significant theoretical speedup.  
- Arguments against acceptance: typos/grammatical errors, incomplete algorithm exposition, missing literature, and insufficient practical discussion and intuition.
