This paper applies an accelerated message-passing variant, Min-Sum Splitting, to the distributed consensus problem. The authors show that while standard Min-Sum fails to converge on general graphs, their proposed method with properly tuned parameters not only converges but also achieves accelerated, subdiffusive rates. This performance matches that of established techniques like shift-register methods. The paper's core contribution is establishing a novel theoretical connection between Min-Sum Splitting, the directionality of lifted Markov chains, and the momentum terms in multi-step first-order optimization methods, thereby unifying three distinct approaches to acceleration.

The paper’s strengths are numerous and compelling, strongly arguing for its acceptance.

*   **Significance and Originality:** The work addresses a fundamental problem in distributed optimization and makes a significant conceptual contribution. By connecting Min-Sum message-passing to the mechanisms of lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods, it provides a new and insightful perspective on achieving accelerated consensus. This unification appears novel and is likely to inspire further research at the intersection of these fields. The results constitute a qualitative advance in understanding how message-passing can be accelerated.

*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical results appear sound and well-supported. The introduction of an auxiliary node-based process to analyze the convergence of the primary edge-based algorithm is a clever and effective proof technique that simplifies the analysis. Theorem 1 provides a rigorous characterization of the accelerated convergence rate, demonstrating a square-root improvement over standard diffusive methods for certain graph classes.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. The introduction clearly situates the work within the vast literature on distributed consensus, making its contributions easy to appreciate for both experts and newcomers.

The paper has one minor weakness, which could be addressed in a final version.

*   **Clarity:** The justification for the name "Splitting" on page 4 is based on an interpretation where the parameters δ and Γ_vw are positive integers. However, the algorithm itself is defined for general real-valued parameters, creating a slight ambiguity. Clarifying that this naming is an intuitive carryover from the integer case would prevent potential confusion for the reader.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and important result that bridges several key areas in distributed optimization and machine learning. The technical contributions are significant and the presentation is excellent. I strongly recommend acceptance.
