Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies the consensus (network averaging) problem through the lens of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol. The main contributions are:
- Establishing that ordinary Min-Sum may fail on loopy graphs, but a modified “Min-Sum Splitting” protocol converges on consensus.
- Proving that a specific tuning of the splitting parameters yields accelerated (subdiffusive) rates that match those of shift-register (second-order) methods for consensus.
- Showing structural connections between Min-Sum Splitting and (i) lifted, non-reversible Markov chains; and (ii) multi-step first-order/shift-register methods.

Technically, the paper (i) specializes Min-Sum Splitting to quadratic consensus, deriving a linear message-passing algorithm on directed edges (Algorithm 2); (ii) introduces an auxiliary node-based, two-step linear system with memory (Algorithm 3) that tracks the Min-Sum iterates and exposes spectral properties more transparently; and (iii) proves Theorem 1, establishing that with δ=1 and Γ=γW (for W symmetric, W1=1), and an optimally tuned γ depending on ρW, the asymptotic contraction factor is ρK = sqrt((1−√(1−ρW^2))/(1+√(1−ρW^2))) < ρW. This yields a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive schemes (from Θ(D^2) to Θ(D) up to logs on cycles/tori), approaching the O(D log(1/ε)) best-known rates from Nesterov-type accelerations.

The work is positioned relative to: Min-Sum convergence conditions [28,25,34,35]; consensus propagation [27]; classic and optimal linear gossip/Metropolis methods [44,37]; lifted Markov chains [5,8,18,37]; and multi-step (heavy ball, Nesterov, shift-register) accelerations [13,24,45,4,14]. Compared to [27], which ties rates to a 2m-dimensional edge dynamics and analyzes cycles (and conjectures tori), the present paper provides a general rate analysis for arbitrary graphs via a 2n-dimensional auxiliary system and recovers shift-register rates.

Strengths
- Novelty: First rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus; clear new connections to lifted chains and multi-step linear methods.
- Technical contribution: Clever auxiliary node-based process (Algorithm 3) that makes the spectral analysis tractable; precise tuning and rate bound (Theorem 1) matching shift-register methods.
- Significance: Brings BP/Min-Sum into the mainstream of accelerated consensus methods, showing the potential to leverage message-passing structure without explicit graph lifting.
- Clarity and positioning: Generally very well written, with comprehensive context and citations. The message-passing derivations and the mapping between edge- and node-based dynamics are presented cleanly.
- Scope of results: Convergence for any graph topology; explicit asymptotics that improve over diffusive methods and get close to known lower bounds.

Weaknesses / points for clarification
- Ambiguity in the “splitting” interpretation: On p.4, “splitting each term φvw into Γvw terms, and each φv into δ terms” suggests integer counts, yet δ and Γvw are introduced as real numbers. Please clarify that the combinatorial replication interpretation only holds when δ, Γvw are positive integers; otherwise, Min-Sum Splitting should be understood as a reparametrization with real scaling weights. Consider rephrasing accordingly.
- Practicality of tuning: The accelerated rate requires γ = 2/(1+√(1−ρW^2)), which depends on global spectral information. How sensitive is performance to misspecification of γ? Is there a safe, conservative choice that still improves over diffusive rates? Could one estimate ρW in a distributed fashion (e.g., power iterations/Lanczos on W) with modest overhead?
- Assumptions and generality: The analysis is restricted to quadratic (averaging) objectives, synchronous updates, and symmetric W. Please comment on:
  - Extension to general convex φv (e.g., smooth/strongly convex) and to constraints incorporated as hard barriers.
  - Asynchronous or time-varying graphs.
  - Nonsymmetric W (e.g., row-stochastic but not symmetric).
- Empirical evidence: The paper is theory-centric. Even small-scale experiments on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs would illustrate constants, robustness to tuning errors, and compare against Metropolis, heavy-ball/Nesterov, and ADMM-based methods.
- Initialization and positivity conditions: Proposition 1 requires 1 + δ Σ Γ R̂ > 0 for all t. With R̂0=r̂0=0 this is trivial, but a brief discussion of allowed initializations and stability would help readers using nonzero initial messages.

Evaluation against review criteria
- Quality: The technical development is sound and well motivated. The main theorem aligns with known results for shift-registers and appears carefully derived via the auxiliary system. Some robustness/practicality aspects (tuning, asynchronous settings) are left open.
- Clarity: Generally excellent. Please fix the “splitting into terms” ambiguity and consider emphasizing the required conditions on W (symmetry, stochasticity, ρ(W−11^T/n)<1) and how to choose W in practice (e.g., Metropolis vs SDP-optimized).
- Originality: High. This is, to my knowledge, the first to connect Min-Sum Splitting with lifted chains and multi-step accelerations and to provide general convergence rates for consensus.
- Significance: High for communities working on distributed optimization, consensus, and message passing. Establishes a bridge that could enable new accelerated BP-like algorithms beyond averaging.

Specific suggestions
- Clarify the role of δ: Theorem 1 fixes δ=1; earlier sections develop general δ. Is there any advantage to δ≠1 for consensus? Could δ help robustness to γ misspecification?
- Compare more explicitly to ADMM-based accelerations (e.g., [3,38,12]): where does Min-Sum Splitting sit in terms of rate, memory/communication overhead, and parameter tuning requirements?
- Add a brief discussion on how to pick W and estimate ρW in a decentralized system; include a remark on sensitivity to γ.
- Minor notation: define diag(Γ1) at first occurrence; ensure consistency between E and E for directed edges.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and technically solid contribution establishing accelerated rates for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus.
- Bridges previously distinct literatures (BP, lifted Markov chains, multi-step accelerations).
- General analysis for arbitrary graphs; rates close to optimal on geometric graphs.

Arguments against acceptance
- Dependence on global spectral information for optimal tuning with limited discussion of practicality/robustness.
- Restricted to quadratic objectives and synchronous settings; no empirical validation.

Overall recommendation: Accept. This is a well-written, theoretically meaningful paper that advances our understanding of accelerated consensus via message passing and opens avenues for broader applications.

Reviewer confidence: Moderate (not a specialist in Min-Sum/BP theory, but familiar with consensus and accelerated methods).
