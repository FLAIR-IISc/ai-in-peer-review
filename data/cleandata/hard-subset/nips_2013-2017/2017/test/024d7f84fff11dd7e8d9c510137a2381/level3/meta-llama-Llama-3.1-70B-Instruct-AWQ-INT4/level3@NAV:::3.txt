This paper proposes an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting message-passing algorithm for distributed consensus optimization. The authors use reparameterization from Ruozzi & Tatikonda (2013) and analyze convergence on quadratic consensus problems. The main technical tool is an auxiliary linear process that tracks the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow. The authors provide a thorough review of the literature on consensus problems and distributed optimization, which is helpful in understanding the context and significance of their work.

The main contributions of the paper are: (1) a new proof technique via an auxiliary process, (2) improved convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, and (3) connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods.

The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm and its variants. They also provide a detailed analysis of the convergence rates of the algorithm, which is supported by theoretical results and numerical simulations.

However, there are some minor issues with the paper. The authors mention that the manuscript contains several typos and grammatical errors throughout, which can be distracting and affect the overall quality of the paper. Additionally, the algorithm descriptions are incomplete, and some variables are not explained.

The paper also omits relevant literature on (sub)gradient and decentralized optimization, which could provide additional context and insights into the authors' work.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a new approach to distributed consensus optimization, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors' use of an auxiliary linear process to track the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm is a novel technique that has not been explored before.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to improve the efficiency and scalability of distributed optimization algorithms. The authors' results show that the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm can achieve better convergence rates than traditional methods, which could have important implications for large-scale machine learning and optimization problems.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, but with some minor revisions to address the issues mentioned above.
