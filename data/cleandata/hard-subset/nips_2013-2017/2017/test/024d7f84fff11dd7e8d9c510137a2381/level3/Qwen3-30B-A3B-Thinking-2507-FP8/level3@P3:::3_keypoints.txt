- Studies Min-Sum Splitting message-passing for distributed consensus, focusing on quadratic consensus problems.  
- Proposes a Min-Sum Splitting algorithm that attains accelerated convergence over classical diffusive methods.  
- Introduces an auxiliary linear process as a novel proof technique to track Min-Sum Splitting dynamics.  
- Shows proper tuning yields subdiffusive rates matching shift-register methods and O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles/tori versus O(D² log(1/ε)).  
- Claims three main contributions: links to lifted Markov chains & multi-step methods; tuning directionality in Belief Propagation for acceleration; first convergence analysis for Min-Sum Splitting.  
- Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 are correctly proved.  
- Strength: clear exposition of theoretical connections and rigorous convergence analysis.  
- Weakness: numerous typos and grammatical errors throughout the manuscript.  
- Weakness: incomplete algorithm descriptions—undefined variables (e.g., R_{vw}, r_{vw}), missing initializations (ĤR^0, Ĥr^0), and unclear identification of auxiliary-process variables in Algorithm 3.  
- Weakness: omits significant related work in decentralized optimization (Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989; Srinivasan et al. 2009; Wei Shi 2015) and classical acceleration (Polyak, Nesterov).  
- Weakness: lacks discussion of practical relevance to machine learning and guidance on when to prefer this method over existing distributed consensus approaches.  
- Requested revisions: provide intuition for the auxiliary process, discuss limitations and extensions beyond quadratic objectives, and give practical usage guidance versus other methods.  
- Review criteria summary: Quality — sound theory but poor presentation and missing limitations; Clarity — generally good but incomplete/ inconsistent algorithmic exposition; Originality — novel connections but under-reviewed literature; Significance — theoretically interesting but limited ML impact.  
- Recommendation: Weak Accept with major revisions — significant theoretical merit but needs substantial revision to clarify practicality, algorithms, and literature context.
