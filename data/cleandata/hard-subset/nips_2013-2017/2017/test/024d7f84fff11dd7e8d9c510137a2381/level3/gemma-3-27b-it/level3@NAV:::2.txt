## Review of “Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting”

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum splitting to the consensus problem in distributed optimization. The authors aim to demonstrate accelerated convergence rates compared to traditional diffusive methods. They establish a connection between their approach and both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, claiming a convergence rate of O(D log(D/ε)). While the paper is well-written and the problem setting is clearly presented, the claimed improvements feel incremental and the novelty somewhat overstated, given missing citations and a lack of comprehensive comparison to existing state-of-the-art algorithms.

A major weakness lies in the claim regarding complete graphs and the definition of “good” graphs. Complete graphs converge in a single step via standard consensus algorithms, so stating Min-Sum splitting provides an advantage is misleading. The authors need to clearly define which graph classes exhibit dimension-independent complexity with their method, as well as providing a rigorous justification for this claim.

Furthermore, the paper omits a crucial comparison to Olshevsky’s “Linear Time Average Consensus…” (2017), which achieves competitive or even better convergence rates. The claimed D log(D/ε) rate doesn't offer a substantial improvement over Olshevsky's D log(1/ε) and is even worse in the case where D ~ n. Several other important consensus-optimization references are missing, including Shi et al. (ADMM linear convergence, 2016) and Scaman et al. (EXTRA, 2017), along with a reporting of state-of-the-art linear rates. The related work on Markov chain lifting and distributed ADMM by Franca and Bento (2017) is also not discussed.

The final paragraph of page 5 states a well-known fact without proper citation to foundational works by Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew, and fails to acknowledge the connection to Heavy Ball/Nesterov/Polyak acceleration methods. Similarly, references to EXTRA by Shi et al. and fast distributed gradient methods by Jakovetic et al. are absent. Proposition 3 appears trivial and adds little to the core contribution. The auxiliary message-passing likely replicates Nesterov acceleration, but the authors do not explicitly establish this connection, and the contribution remains unclear, especially since consensus analysis reduces to a linear system. The gains likely stem from improved spectral gap handling, but the worst-case bound remains O(n) because O(n) = O(D). 

Finally, minor points: Line 243 (p.6) uses directed edges on a simple graph, which is confusing and should be corrected. Line 220 (p.6) contains a typo ("Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian").

**Arguments for acceptance:** Clear presentation of the problem and algorithm, and a mathematically sound analysis.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Incremental improvement over existing methods, significant omissions in related work, misleading claims about complete graphs, lack of convincing novelty, trivial proposition.

**Overall Score:** 5/10. While the work is technically sound, the limited contribution and significant omissions make it a weak submission for NIPS. Addressing the cited issues would substantially improve the paper.
