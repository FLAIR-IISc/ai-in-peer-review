## Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum splitting to the consensus problem, aiming to achieve accelerated convergence rates. The authors demonstrate that a modified Min-Sum algorithm, through appropriate parameter tuning, can achieve subdiffusive convergence, matching rates obtained by shift-register methods. The core idea is to leverage the splitting technique to introduce directionality into the message passing, drawing parallels to lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods.

The paper is well-written and logically structured. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the broader context of distributed consensus algorithms. The mathematical derivations appear sound, and the connection to existing techniques like shift-registers is a valuable observation. However, while the paper presents a technically correct approach, the claimed improvement over existing methods feels incremental. The convergence rate of D log(D/ε) doesn’t represent a substantial leap beyond the D log(1/ε) rate achieved by Olshevsky’s work, and can even be worse in scenarios where D is comparable to n. 

A significant weakness lies in the incomplete related work. The paper omits crucial citations. Notably, Olshevsky’s “Linear Time Average Consensus…” (2017) is not referenced, despite presenting a competitive or superior convergence rate. Furthermore, important consensus-optimization references like Shi et al. on ADMM linear convergence and Scaman et al. are missing, hindering a comprehensive comparison. The omission of Franca and Bento’s work on Markov chain lifting and distributed ADMM is also a notable oversight. The final paragraph of page 5 states a well-known fact without citing Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew, and fails to acknowledge the established connections to Heavy Ball/Nesterov/Polyak methods. Additional relevant work, such as EXTRA by Shi et al. and fast distributed gradient methods by Jakovetic et al., are also absent.

Proposition 3 feels somewhat trivial and doesn't contribute significantly to the overall advancement of the field. The claim regarding the auxiliary message-passing mirroring Nesterov acceleration is plausible but lacks sufficient justification, as consensus analysis ultimately reduces to a linear system. The authors acknowledge that gains likely stem from spectral-gap handling, but the worst-case bound remains O(n), diminishing the practical impact. Minor issues include a typo on page 6 ("Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian") and a confusing statement about directed edges on a simple graph.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-written.
*   Establishes a connection between Min-Sum splitting and existing accelerated methods.
*   Provides a theoretical analysis of convergence rates.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental improvement over existing methods.
*   Significant omissions in related work.
*   Proposition 3 is trivial.
*   Lack of a strong justification for the claimed novelty.



**Overall Score:** Borderline Reject. While the paper is not fundamentally flawed, the lack of significant novelty and the substantial gaps in the related work prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. Addressing the missing citations and providing a more compelling argument for the practical benefits of the proposed approach would significantly strengthen the paper.
