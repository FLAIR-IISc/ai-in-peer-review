- Paper analyzes Min-Sum Splitting for distributed consensus, introducing an auxiliary node-based linear process to prove accelerated, subdiffusive convergence matching shift-register methods.
- Novel theoretical contribution: auxiliary-process proof technique connects edge-based Min-Sum dynamics to a tractable node-based process for explicit convergence rates.
- Improved convergence rates: shows square-root acceleration over diffusive methods, competitive with Nesterov-like and shift-register accelerations.
- New connections: links message-passing, lifted non-reversible Markov chains, and multi-step optimization/momentum methods.
- Generally clear and well-written with a logical progression from algorithm to analysis.
- Presentation and reproducibility issues: numerous typos, incomplete algorithm descriptions (missing initializations and unspecified message parameters), unclear state definitions, and a terminology misuse (Laplacian vs Lagrangian).
- Literature omissions: missing foundational and relevant works (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989, distributed gradient/subgradient literature, EXTRA 2015) and original momentum papers (Polyak, Nesterov).
- Lack of practical guidance and intuition: insufficient discussion of trade-offs (communication, memory, implementation) and limited explanation of why the auxiliary process is the right analytical tool or its limitations.
- Limited scope to quadratic (averaging) objectives: no discussion of extending the framework or acceleration results to general convex losses common in ML, reducing immediate ML relevance.
- Question: When is Min-Sum Splitting preferable to accelerated ADMM or Nesterov-accelerated distributed methods in practice?
- Question: Why is the auxiliary process the appropriate analytical tool and what are its limitations?
- Question: Can the framework be extended to general convex consensus problems; if so, what are the main challenges?
- Question: Why is this message-passing approach to acceleration particularly important for the ML community versus direct momentum on distributed gradients?
- Summary: Strong and elegant theoretical core with novel insights, but needs substantial revision on clarity, related work, practical implications, and extensibility to be a complete, impactful contribution.
