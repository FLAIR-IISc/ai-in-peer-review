Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies Min-Sum message passing with “splitting” (Ruozzi & Tatikonda, 2013) for distributed consensus/averaging. The authors focus on the quadratic consensus objective and show: (i) ordinary Min-Sum can diverge on graphs with cycles (consistent with prior work), but Min-Sum Splitting converges; (ii) with a specific parameter choice (δ = 1, Γ = γW with W symmetric and stochastic), the method achieves a subdiffusive, accelerated rate that matches shift-register/second-order methods; (iii) the analysis uses a novel auxiliary linear process on nodes to track the edge-based Min-Sum dynamics. This yields a clean spectral characterization in terms of a 2n×2n matrix K that coincides with the state matrix for shift-register acceleration. The authors also position the method relative to lifted Markov chains (nonreversible walks on lifted graphs) and multi-step first-order methods (heavy-ball/Nesterov/shift-register), arguing Min-Sum Splitting implicitly introduces directionality and memory.

Compared to [27] (Consensus Propagation), which relates a modified Min-Sum to a 2m-dimensional edge process and provides bounds for cycles (and a conjecture for tori), this paper gives rates for general graphs and recovers the O(D log(D/ε)) convergence on cycles and tori, improving over diffusive O(D^2 log(1/ε)) and approaching recent optimal O(D log(1/ε)) rates via Nesterov-style methods. The work also complements convergence guarantees for Gaussian BP with splitting [35] by providing explicit rates.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The propositions and Theorem 1 appear correct. The derivation that the Min-Sum Splitting messages remain quadratic, the edge-based linear recursion (Algorithm 2), and the node-based auxiliary recursion (Algorithm 3) are coherent. The spectral analysis ties directly to known results for Chebyshev/shift-register methods.
- Assumptions: The main result requires δ=1 and Γ=γW where W is symmetric with W1=1 and ρ(W−11^T/n)<1. The optimal γ depends on the spectral gap of W, a global quantity. The proofs assume quadratic objectives; general convex objectives are not treated.
- Completeness: The asymptotic rate and its dependence on ρW are made explicit; the square-root improvement over diffusive methods is clearly derived. Some algorithmic details (initializations, variable definitions) need clarification (see Clarity).

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-structured, with a helpful narrative connecting Min-Sum to lifting and multi-step acceleration. However:
  - Algorithms: Several variables (e.g., R̂vw, r̂vw) are not defined in prose before use; initial conditions (R̂0, r̂0) are not specified in the main text near Algorithm 2, though Theorem 1 later takes R̂0=r̂0=0. Please define all variables and include default initializations where your theorems apply.
  - Mapping between Algorithms 2 and 3: Proposition 2 gives the mapping, but it would help to explicitly state which vectors in Algorithm 3 correspond to node-wise aggregations of edge parameters and to explain the role of Qs, qs in the final estimator.
  - Minor terminology: Possible misuse of “Laplacian/Laplace multipliers” where “Lagrangian/Lagrange multipliers” is intended (lines ~220–221).
  - Typos/grammar: Multiple minor issues (see Typos).

Originality
- First convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, with a new auxiliary-process proof technique.
- Conceptual link between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted nonreversible chains, and multi-step acceleration is insightful and, to my knowledge, new.
- While accelerated consensus via shift-register/Chebyshev and lifted chains is known, showing Min-Sum Splitting recovers these rates with a principled parameterization is novel.

Significance
- The results sharpen our theoretical understanding of message-passing for consensus and show Min-Sum Splitting can be competitive with the best-known accelerated methods.
- The auxiliary-process technique may be broadly useful for analyzing Min-Sum variants.
- Practical impact would be strengthened by guidance on parameter selection and comparisons in experiments; currently the paper is primarily theoretical and focused on quadratics.

Pros
- Clear theoretical contribution: rates for Min-Sum Splitting with a sharp spectral characterization.
- Unifies perspectives from BP, lifted chains, and multi-step optimization.
- Analysis technique (auxiliary node process) is elegant and may generalize.

Cons
- Practical guidance is limited: γ requires global spectral information; no adaptive or locally computable tuning is proposed.
- Scope restricted to quadratic consensus; no extensions to general convex objectives or noisy/asynchronous networks.
- Algorithmic exposition (initializations, variable roles) needs tightening.
- Limited discussion of when to prefer Min-Sum Splitting over existing accelerated consensus/ADMM/EXTRA methods.

Questions for the authors
1. Parameter selection: How do you recommend choosing γ in practice without global spectrum knowledge? Could you use distributed power iteration or conservative bounds to retain acceleration?
2. Beyond quadratics: Can your auxiliary-process approach extend to strongly convex smooth objectives (e.g., by linearization or proximal splitting)? Any obstacles to composite objectives common in ML?
3. Robustness: What happens under asynchrony, delays, or time-varying graphs? Is the auxiliary-process analysis adaptable?
4. Practical trade-offs: Compared with shift-register/Chebyshev or ADMM/EXTRA, when is Min-Sum Splitting preferable (communication, memory, computation, convergence constants)?
5. Initialization: Please clarify the admissible ranges ensuring positivity in Proposition 1 and why R̂0=r̂0=0 satisfies the conditions.

Suggestions
- Clarify all algorithmic variables and provide a self-contained pseudo-code with default initializations; explicitly state the assumptions under which xt is well-defined.
- Expand discussion on how to estimate γ in a decentralized manner (e.g., distributed power method, rough bounds, or adaptive momentum).
- Add intuition for the auxiliary process: brief derivation linking edge-directionality to node-memory terms and why this yields the K matrix of shift-registers.
- Include at least a brief empirical comparison on canonical graphs (cycle, grid, random geometric) against Metropolis gossip, Chebyshev/shift-register, and ADMM/EXTRA to illustrate constants and robustness.
- Broaden related work: cite Polyak’s heavy-ball (1964) and Nesterov (1983) explicitly; classic decentralized optimization (Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis, 1989; Tsitsiklis et al., 1986); and distributed subgradient/dual averaging.
