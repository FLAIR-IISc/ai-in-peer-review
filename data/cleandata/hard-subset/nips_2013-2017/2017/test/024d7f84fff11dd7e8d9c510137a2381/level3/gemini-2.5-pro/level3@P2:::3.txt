This paper studies the Min-Sum message-passing algorithm for the distributed consensus problem. The authors focus on a variant known as Min-Sum Splitting, which introduces reparameterization of the objective function via tunable parameters. The paper's main contributions are threefold. First, it provides the first convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, showing that while ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge on graphs with cycles, Min-Sum Splitting can converge to the correct solution. Second, it demonstrates that with a proper choice of the splitting parameters, the algorithm achieves an accelerated, subdiffusive convergence rate that matches the performance of established shift-register methods. Third, it draws a novel and insightful connection between the acceleration mechanism in Min-Sum Splitting and two other, seemingly independent lines of research in accelerated distributed algorithms: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods from convex optimization. The core of the analysis relies on a clever proof technique where the authors introduce an auxiliary linear process on the graph's nodes to track the evolution of the more complex Min-Sum algorithm, which operates on the graph's directed edges.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the first convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting. The connections drawn between a belief propagation-style algorithm and mainstream accelerated optimization methods (lifted Markov chains, multi-step gradient methods) are highly original and conceptually valuable. This bridges a gap between different communities and provides a new perspective on how message-passing schemes can achieve acceleration.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical work appears sound. The derivation of the auxiliary message-passing scheme is an elegant way to simplify the analysis, relating the convergence of a complex process on 2m variables (edges) to a more tractable one on 2n variables (nodes). The main result in Theorem 1, which recovers the accelerated rates of shift-register methods, is a strong and convincing demonstration of the algorithm's power.

3.  **New Insights:** The paper provides a compelling explanation for acceleration in this context: the splitting parameters introduce a "directionality" or "momentum" into the message-passing updates. This insight successfully links the algorithm's mechanics to the core principles behind other accelerated methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript's clarity could be significantly improved.
    *   There are numerous typos and grammatical errors throughout the paper (e.g., "evolved independently of each others", "double-stochastic" -> "doubly-stochastic", "casted" -> "cast"). This detracts from the overall quality and makes the paper harder to read.
    *   The descriptions of Algorithm 1 and 2 are incomplete. Key variables and initialization procedures are not clearly defined. For example, the initial message functions `µ̂0` are mentioned but their specific form for the quadratic case (Proposition 1) is not explicitly stated as an input to Algorithm 2. This lack of detail hinders reproducibility.
    - The relationship between the variables in the main algorithm (Algorithm 2) and the auxiliary algorithm (Algorithm 3) should be made more explicit to help the reader follow the technical argument.

2.  **Related Work:** The literature review is good but misses some key references. While the paper connects to accelerated first-order methods, it omits citations to the foundational work in this area by Polyak (heavy ball method) and Nesterov. Furthermore, the discussion of decentralized optimization could be strengthened by referencing classic works like Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis (1989) and more recent, highly relevant work on exact first-order methods for decentralized consensus, such as the EXTRA algorithm by Shi et al. (2015).

3.  **Scope and Practical Implications:** The paper's focus is almost exclusively on the network averaging problem (quadratic objectives). While this is a standard benchmark, the NIPS audience is broadly interested in solving large-scale machine learning problems. The authors should discuss whether this framework can be extended to more general convex consensus problems (e.g., minimizing a sum of logistic regression or SVM losses). The paper also lacks a discussion of the practical aspects of the proposed method. A section comparing its communication/computation overhead and practical applicability against state-of-the-art methods like decentralized ADMM or gradient tracking algorithms would significantly increase its impact.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**

*   Please perform a thorough proofread of the manuscript to correct typos and grammatical errors.
*   In Algorithm 1, what does `minz∈R` operate on? It seems to be a functional minimization. Please clarify the notation. In Algorithm 2, please explicitly define the initial conditions `R̂0` and `r̂0` that are used to obtain the results in Theorem 1 (i.e., `R̂0 = r̂0 = 0`).
*   In the text on lines 220–221, the terms "Laplacian" and "Laplace multipliers" are used. Is this a typo for "Lagrangian" and "Lagrange multipliers"?
*   Could you provide more intuition on the construction of the auxiliary process? Why does this reparameterization work, and what is the physical or mathematical meaning of the auxiliary variables `Q` and `q`?
*   The acceleration depends on `ρW`, which is a global property of the graph. How does this fit into the "local message-passing" paradigm? Does this require a pre-computation step to estimate `ρW`, and if so, how does that affect the overall distributed nature of the algorithm?
*   How does Min-Sum Splitting compare to other popular distributed optimization methods like ADMM or D-GD for the consensus problem? A brief discussion on the relative pros and cons (e.g., convergence speed, communication cost, ease of implementation) would be very helpful.
*   What are the main obstacles to extending this analysis beyond quadratic objective functions to more general convex functions? Does the linearity of the message updates (Proposition 1) break down? This is a crucial point for assessing the potential impact on the machine learning community.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper with novel ideas and significant results. The connection it establishes between Min-Sum splitting and other accelerated methods is insightful. However, the paper's impact could be substantially increased by addressing the weaknesses in clarity, expanding the discussion on related work, and providing more context on the practical implications and potential for generalization to broader classes of problems relevant to machine learning.
