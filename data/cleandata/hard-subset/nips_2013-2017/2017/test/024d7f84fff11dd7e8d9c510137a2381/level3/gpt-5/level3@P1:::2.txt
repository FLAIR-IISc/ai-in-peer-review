This paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for average consensus. The authors show that ordinary Min-Sum can diverge on loopy graphs, whereas Splitting with tuned parameters converges and achieves an accelerated, subdiffusive rate. The main technical device is an auxiliary node-based linear, two-step iteration that tracks the edge-based Min-Sum Splitting dynamics and reveals a spectral connection to shift-register (Chebyshev-type) methods. With a particular choice Γ = γW (W symmetric, stochastic) and γ depending on ρ(W−11^T/n), the method attains the same asymptotic rate as shift-register accelerations, leading to O(D log(D/ε)) iterations on cycles and tori. The paper also draws conceptual parallels to lifted Markov chains (directionality via splitting) and to multi-step first-order acceleration (momentum/shift-register memory).

Relation to prior work: The paper aligns Min-Sum Splitting with two well-established acceleration paradigms: nonreversible/lifted Markov chains (Diaconis–Holmes–Neal; Chen–Lovász–Pak; Jung–Shah–Shin) and multi-step accelerations for consensus/optimization (heavy-ball/Polyak, Nesterov, Chebyshev/shift-register; Xiao–Boyd; Ghadimi–Shames–Johansson). It is close in spirit and rate to Olshevsky’s “Linear Time Average Consensus,” which achieves O(D log(1/ε)) and, in some cases, stronger guarantees. The use of a node-based 2n×2n linear system to analyze a message-passing algorithm is reminiscent of standard reductions of consensus to linear systems and of known ADMM/gradient-lifting correspondences (França–Bento). While the paper clearly positions Min-Sum Splitting within this landscape, several important references and comparisons are missing (see below).

Strengths
- Clear technical development of Min-Sum Splitting for quadratic consensus; edge-to-node reduction (auxiliary scheme) is neat and useful for analysis.
- Rigorous spectral analysis tying parameters to the optimal shift-register rate; the result that Min-Sum Splitting recovers the Chebyshev-type rate is solid.
- Conceptual unification: connects BP-like directionality to lifting and to multi-step momentum.
- Complete proofs (supplement) and reasonably clear exposition.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in rates: the main guarantee matches known shift-register/Nesterov/Chebyshev rates, and the headline O(D log(D/ε)) is not better—and can be worse—than known O(D log(1/ε)) (e.g., Olshevsky).
- The “dimension-independent complexity” discussion is potentially misleading (complete graphs can reach exact averaging in one step with W = 11^T/n); the paper should precisely define the regime, what “good” graphs mean, and in which classes the claimed rates are optimal/near-optimal.
- Missing and incomplete related work: accelerated consensus (Olshevsky), distributed ADMM with linear rates (Shi et al.), optimal lower/upper bounds in decentralized optimization (Scaman et al.), EXTRA (Shi et al.), fast distributed gradient (Jakovetić et al.), and distributed ADMM/lifting (França–Bento).
- Proposition 3 (in the supplement) appears trivial and incremental; its status as a standalone contribution is weak.
- Practicality: the accelerated parameters depend on global spectral information (ρ(W)), as in other accelerations. The paper does not discuss how to set or estimate γ in a decentralized way, nor compare per-iteration communication/compute costs to existing accelerated consensus.
- The “auxiliary message-passing” mainly recovers a known two-step linear system; the genuine algorithmic novelty over standard accelerated consensus is unclear.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The main theorem and its reduction to a 2-step linear system mirror classical Chebyshev/shift-register analyses and appear sound. However, the practical implications and novelty over existing accelerations are modest.
- Completeness: The theoretical development is complete for the quadratic case. Empirical validation is absent; comparisons to state-of-the-art accelerated consensus would strengthen the paper.
- Carefulness: Several missing citations and confusing statements (see Detailed comments) need revision.

Clarity
- Generally well written; the auxiliary system clarifies the structure.
- Some notation and phrasing are confusing: the use of directed edges on a simple graph without early clarification; minor typos.

Originality
- Conceptual insight that Min-Sum Splitting can be tuned to emulate shift-register acceleration is interesting, but the resulting rates and mechanisms are not novel relative to known accelerations. The new angle is the BP/Splitting lens and the auxiliary proof technique.

Significance
- The paper helps bridge communities (BP/splitting, lifting, multi-step optimization) and may motivate analogous analyses beyond consensus. However, for consensus itself, the contribution is incremental relative to existing accelerated methods with equal or better guarantees.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Clarify claims about “good” graphs and dimension-independent time. On complete graphs, exact averaging in one step is possible with W = 11^T/n; please state precisely the model and constraints when making asymptotic claims.
- Compare directly to Olshevsky (Linear Time Average Consensus): quantify regimes where your O(D log(D/ε)) improves or worsens relative to O(D log(1/ε)).
- Add and discuss: Shi et al. (linear convergence of decentralized ADMM), Scaman et al. (optimal algorithms and lower bounds), França–Bento (ADMM as lifting), Shi et al. (EXTRA), Jakovetić et al. (fast distributed gradient).
- The connection between consensus and heavy-ball/Nesterov/Polyak is long known; please cite early works such as Rabbat–Nowak–Bucklew and Xiao–Boyd, and recent surveys (Ghadimi et al.).
- Practicality: discuss decentralized/online estimation of γ, robustness to spectral-gap misspecification, and per-iteration complexity versus Chebyshev-accelerated gossip and ADMM.
- Line 243 (p.6): using directed edges on a simple graph is confusing; clarify the notation early (e.g., E for undirected, overline E for directed).
- Line 220 (p.6): “Laplacian” should be “Lagrangian.”
- Proposition 3 (supplement): seems straightforward; either strengthen or demote emphasis.

Pros
- Solid analysis; clear auxiliary reduction; useful conceptual bridge across methods.
- Shows BP/Splitting can match best-known accelerated consensus rates.

Cons
- Limited novelty in rate/algorithm; missing key related work and comparisons.
- Practical deployment issues (global spectral info) not addressed.
- Claims about improvement and “good graphs” need clarification; some statements are misleading.

After rebuttal
- The authors’ clarifications improved my understanding, especially around parameter selection and notation. However, the core evaluation remains unchanged: the contribution is primarily an insightful rederivation/unification rather than a step beyond the state of the art in consensus rates.

Overall evaluation: borderline reject.
