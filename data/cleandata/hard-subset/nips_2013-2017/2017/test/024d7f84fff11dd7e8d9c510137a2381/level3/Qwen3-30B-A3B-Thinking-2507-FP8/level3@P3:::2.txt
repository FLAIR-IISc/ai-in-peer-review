

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting" (NIPS 2017)

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum message-passing to the distributed consensus problem, introducing a "Min-Sum Splitting" variant that the authors claim achieves accelerated convergence rates. The main contribution is the analysis showing that proper parameter tuning yields a convergence rate of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, which the authors present as a "square-root improvement" over diffusive methods' O(D² log(1/ε)) rate.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper builds on earlier work by [27], which established that ordinary Min-Sum does not converge on cyclic graphs for consensus problems. The authors extend this by introducing splitting parameters that enable convergence. They relate their approach to two established lines of research: lifted Markov chain techniques and accelerated first-order methods in convex optimization. However, the paper fails to properly acknowledge key connections to the literature.

## Strengths

The paper makes a clear technical contribution in formalizing the relationship between Min-Sum Splitting and accelerated methods. The auxiliary message-passing scheme introduced in Section 6 provides a useful analytical tool for tracking convergence behavior, and the proof techniques in the supplementary material are technically sound. The paper's organization is logical, and the presentation of the algorithmic framework is clear.

## Weaknesses

The main weaknesses stem from insufficient literature review and misrepresentation of the claimed improvement:

1. **Misleading claims about graph classes**: The paper claims "dimension-independent complexity" but fails to clarify that complete graphs converge in one step (not dimension-independent), and fails to define which graph classes actually achieve the claimed rates. The authors should explicitly state that their result applies to graphs with diameter D < n, not all graphs.

2. **Missing key references**: The paper omits critical work that directly challenges the significance of their claimed improvement. Olshevsky's "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs" (2017) establishes convergence rates of O(D log(1/ε)), which matches the authors' claimed rate (not a "square-root improvement" as claimed). The authors' rate O(D log(D/ε)) is actually worse than O(D log(1/ε)) in the worst case when D ~ n. The paper should cite and compare to Olshevsky's work.

3. **Missing consensus-optimization references**: The authors fail to cite foundational work on distributed optimization for consensus, including Shi et al.'s EXTRA (2015) and Scaman et al.'s linear convergence results for distributed ADMM (2017). They also omit Franca and Bento's work on Markov chain lifting and distributed ADMM.

4. **Trivial proposition and factual errors**: Proposition 3 is trivial and appears to be a supplementary contribution rather than a main result. The last paragraph of p.5 states a long-known fact (about connections to Heavy Ball/Nesterov) without citing Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew (2005) or acknowledging these established connections.

5. **Technical inaccuracies**: Line 220 incorrectly states "Laplacian" when it should be "Lagrangian." The claim that the proposed method "yields a convergence time O(D log(D/ε) for cycles" is misleading since the worst-case convergence time for any graph is O(n) = O(D), making the claimed improvement negligible in worst-case analysis.

6. **Contribution clarity**: The "auxiliary message-passing" scheme appears to simply mirror Nesterov acceleration on min-sum, with the contribution being unclear. The paper claims to "show that a proper choice of the tuning parameters allows Min-Sum Splitting to yield subdiffusive accelerated convergence rates," but this is essentially a restatement of known results on accelerated methods for consensus.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance

**Quality**: The technical content is sound, but the paper is poorly contextualized within existing literature. The theoretical analysis is correct but fails to properly compare with state-of-the-art methods.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, though the misleading claims about convergence rates and missing context make the presentation confusing in places.

**Originality**: The connection between Min-Sum and accelerated methods is novel, but the claimed improvement is not. The paper fails to properly distinguish its contribution from existing work, particularly Olshevsky's work which achieves the same rate.

**Significance**: The claimed improvement is minimal (a log(D) factor in the convergence rate), and the paper fails to establish that this is meaningful for practical applications. The worst-case bound remains O(n), which is the same as diffusive methods, undermining the significance of the claimed acceleration.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the analysis of Min-Sum for consensus, but its claimed significance is greatly overstated due to missing citations and misrepresentation of the convergence rates. The key improvements claimed are either already established in the literature (Olshevsky's work) or are not meaningful in worst-case analysis. The paper would be significantly strengthened by properly contextualizing its results against state-of-the-art methods, particularly Olshevsky's linear time average consensus algorithm.

The authors' rebuttal satisfactorily addressed the technical points raised, but the core issue of the claimed improvement being minimal compared to existing work remains. The paper does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way, and the contribution is largely incremental rather than novel.

**Recommendation**: Weak Reject. The paper has technical merit but fails to properly contextualize its contribution within the literature, overstating the significance of a small improvement that is already achievable by existing methods.
