- Paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for quadratic consensus, introduces an auxiliary node-level linear process, and proves subdiffusive (shift-register/Chebyshev-type) convergence rates for specific parameters (δ=1, Γ=γW).

- Connects Min-Sum/splitting to lifted/non-reversible Markov chains (directionality) and to multi-step acceleration/memory (auxiliary process).

- Novel proof technique: reducing edge-message dynamics to a 2n-dimensional node-level linear system is technically neat and self-contained for quadratics.

- Main theoretical results (Propositions 1–2, Theorem 1) appear correct and recover known accelerated rates on graphs with geometry.

- Originality: route of accelerating Min-Sum via splitting and auxiliary-process analysis is new despite matching known rates.

- Significance: strengthens theoretical understanding and builds bridges between BP, Markov chain lifting, and convex optimization communities.

- Practicality concerns: parameter selection requires global spectral information (γ), no empirical validation, and no robustness analysis (mis-specified γ, asynchrony, noise, quantization).

- Algorithmic exposition problems: missing definitions/initializations, unclear mapping between edge-level and node-level variables (Algorithms 2 and 3), and likely typos in formulas (e.g., Algorithm 2 output line).

- Clarity issues: numerous minor typos/wording problems; key variable mappings should be explicit in the main text.

- Scope limitation: analysis restricted to quadratic consensus; extensions to general smooth/strongly-convex or composite objectives are not discussed.

- Requests on practicality: compare per-iteration computational/communication complexity to gossip, Chebyshev, EXTRA, gradient tracking, and distributed ADMM; discuss when Min-Sum Splitting is preferable and how sensitive it is to γ mis-specification or whether γ can be estimated online.

- Requests on intuition/limitations: explain why the auxiliary process captures splitting and how its memory term relates to heavy-ball/shift-register; clarify limits like need for symmetric W, positivity condition 1+δ∑Γwv R̂>0, synchronous/static graphs.

- Requests beyond quadratics: discuss prospects for non-quadratic potentials, constraints, proximal/min-sum extensions, and stochastic gradients.

- Requests for ML relevance: state how accelerated averaging helps decentralized SGD/dual averaging, links to gradient tracking, and potential benefits for communication-limited federated learning.

- Algorithm 2 specifics: define R̂ and r̂, provide explicit initializations (R̂0,r̂0) in main text, and correct malformed output formula (suggested correct form provided).

- Algorithm 3 specifics: explicitly identify auxiliary-process variables (which track numerators/denominators and beliefs) and summarize initialization mapping from Proposition 2 inline.

- Positivity condition: clarify and prove 1+δ∑Γwv R̂t_wv>0 holds for recommended parameters and initializations.

- Related work to add: foundational decentralized optimization (Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis; Tsitsiklis et al.; Ram–Nedić–Veeravalli), modern decentralized methods (Wei & Ozdaglar, Shi et al., gradient tracking literature), and original acceleration references (Polyak, Nesterov).

- Positioning vs prior rates: explicitly compare assumptions and parameter knowledge requirements to Chebyshev-accelerated consensus (Xiao & Boyd) and other accelerated gossip methods.

- Minor technical corrections: fix “Laplacian/Laplace multipliers” → “Lagrangian/Lagrange multipliers,” be consistent on “doubly-stochastic” vs symmetric W, and state δ=1 in main theorem (and comment on δ≠1).

- Typos to fix: numerous wording errors (e.g., “theirs neighbors,” “double-stochastic,” “casted as,” “each others,” agreement errors).

- Pros for acceptance: novel auxiliary-process proof, conceptual unification, correct accelerated rates, advances understanding of Min-Sum Splitting.

- Cons for acceptance: limited to quadratics, incomplete algorithmic specs, weak practical discussion/comparisons, missing citations, and many minor writing issues.

- Overall: theoretically interesting and potentially strong with improved exposition, broader related work, practical guidance, and clarity fixes.
