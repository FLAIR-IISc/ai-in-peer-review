Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies Min-Sum message passing for the equality-constrained consensus problem and shows that the classical Min-Sum diverges on loopy graphs, whereas Min-Sum Splitting (Ruozzi & Tatikonda 2013) converges and, with appropriate tuning, achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) rates. The core technical contribution is a reparameterization leading to an auxiliary linear process on nodes whose dynamics matches a two-step linear iteration (a shift-register method) known to accelerate consensus beyond the diffusive barrier. The resulting rate matches that of optimal second-order linear methods (Golub & Varga; Xiao & Boyd) and yields O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori, versus Θ(D^2 log(1/ε)) for standard reversible Markov-chain-based consensus (Boyd et al. 2006; Diaconis–Saloff-Coste). The paper clarifies connections between Min-Sum Splitting and both (i) lifted, nonreversible Markov chains (Chen–Lovász–Pak; Jung–Shah–Shin) and (ii) multi-step first-order methods (Polyak heavy-ball; Nesterov acceleration; Ghadimi–Shames–Johansson), and positions Min-Sum as providing acceleration without explicitly constructing a lifted graph.

On the technical side, for quadratic consensus φv(z)= 1/2 z^2 − b_v z, the authors show the Min-Sum Splitting messages remain quadratic, the edge-based dynamics is linear, and the node-level auxiliary dynamics takes the form [r^t; q^t] = K(δ, Γ)[r^{t−1}; q^{t−1}], with outputs x^t_v = r^t_v / R^t_v. With δ = 1 and Γ = γW, and γ chosen as a function of the spectral gap ρ(W − 11^T/n), the node-level operator coincides with the shift-register matrix K, yielding a spectral radius ρ_K strictly smaller than ρ_W and a square-root improvement in asymptotic convergence time. This gives theoretical evidence that directionality induced by splitting corresponds to memory at nodes, paralleling acceleration mechanisms in both lifted chains and multi-step optimization.

Strengths:
- Clear, theoretically grounded acceleration result: first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, achieving near-optimal O(D log(D/ε)) on geometric graphs.
- Elegant proof technique via an auxiliary process on nodes that makes the spectral analysis tractable and connects directly to known accelerated iterations.
- Useful conceptual bridges to lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods; helps unify previously separate lines of work.
- Results sharpen and extend earlier Min-Sum consensus analyses (Moallemi & Van Roy; Ruozzi & Tatikonda; Consensus Propagation), and improve over prior edge-state analyses that were limited to regular graphs or special topologies.

Weaknesses:
- Practical guidance is limited. The accelerated tuning requires global spectral information (ρ(W)), raising questions about distributed estimation, robustness to mis-specification, and applicability on large dynamic networks.
- Scope restricted to quadratic consensus; extensions to general convex φ_v or constrained objectives common in ML are only discussed qualitatively.
- Algorithmic exposition needs refinement: some variables and initializations are not clearly defined in Algorithms 1–3; mapping between the edge-based and node-based variables could be made more explicit.
- Limited discussion of per-iteration communication/compute costs and memory relative to accelerated node-based methods (Chebyshev/shift-register, heavy-ball, Nesterov) and distributed ADMM.
- No empirical validation to illustrate constants, robustness, or performance on representative graphs (cycles, grids, random geometric graphs, expanders).

Quality:
The technical development appears sound. Propositions 1–2 and Theorem 1 are consistent with known analyses of second-order linear iterations; the spectral bounds and choice of γ match classical Chebyshev-type acceleration. Assumptions and conditions (positivity of denominators in Algorithm 3) are reasonable but should be stated more transparently, together with initialization choices that guarantee r^t/R^t is well-defined.

Clarity:
Generally well-written but marred by typos and a few terminology slips. The auxiliary-process intuition is compelling; however, the presentation would benefit from a concise, self-contained mapping of variables between Algorithms 1–3 and a table of symbols. Highlight earlier that the node-level K coincides with a standard shift-register scheme.

Originality:
High within the Min-Sum literature: first rates for Splitting on consensus and a novel auxiliary-process proof. The conceptual links to lifted chains and multi-step methods are insightful, even if the final rates match known optimal linear iterations.

Significance:
The results make Min-Sum Splitting a credible competitor to state-of-the-art accelerated consensus, clarifying when and how message-passing can achieve subdiffusive rates. This advances theory and may inform designs for other objectives if the approach extends beyond quadratics.

Requests for clarification and suggestions:
- Practicality: When should one use Min-Sum Splitting versus Chebyshev/shift-register, heavy-ball, Nesterov, or distributed ADMM? Compare per-iteration costs, memory, and sensitivity to γ.
- Parameter selection: How can ρ(W) (or γ) be estimated in a distributed, robust manner? Sensitivity to misspecification?
- Extensions: Can the auxiliary-process approach handle strongly convex smooth φ_v, composite objectives, or constraints beyond equality (e.g., ADMM-like formulations)?
- Asynchrony and time-varying graphs: Does the analysis extend? Any guidance or empirical evidence?
- Experiments: Add simulations on cycles, grids, random geometric graphs, and expanders versus Metropolis, Chebyshev, heavy-ball, Nesterov, EXTRA/Exact Diffusion, and ADMM.
- Clarify conditions ensuring R^t_v > 0 and the division in Algorithm 3 is safe under the recommended initialization R̂^0 = r̂^0 = 0.

Missing or undercited literature:
- Foundational decentralized optimization: Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis (1989); Tsitsiklis et al. (1986).
- Accelerated consensus and multi-step methods: Polyak (heavy-ball), Nesterov, Chebyshev semi-iterative methods; recent exact first-order methods (EXTRA, Exact Diffusion), and Scaman et al. (optimal rates on graphs).
- ADMM and lifting connections: Wei & Ozdaglar; Shi et al.; França & Bento.

Typos and wording issues (examples):
- “theirs neighbors” → “their neighbors”
- “double-stochastic” → “doubly-stochastic”
- “casted” → “cast”
- “Laplace multipliers” → “Lagrange multipliers”
- Subject–verb agreement in several sentences; minor punctuation issues

Pros for acceptance:
- First rigorous rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting with clear acceleration
- Conceptually unifying and technically clean auxiliary-process proof
- Potential to inspire extensions beyond consensus

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited practical guidance and no experiments
- Dependence on global spectral knowledge
- Exposition gaps (algorithms/initializations) and typos

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contribution and unifying insights merit publication, provided the authors improve clarity, add practical discussion (and ideally experiments), and correct the noted issues.

Reviewer confidence: High.
