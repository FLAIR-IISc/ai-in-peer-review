- Paper shows Min-Sum Splitting converges for consensus and achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) rates comparable to shift-register methods.  
- Establishes a novel connection between Min-Sum algorithms, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous with proof sketches in paper and full proofs in supplementary material.  
- Results are novel and constitute a qualitative advance in distributed optimization.  
- The authors carefully situate the work within prior literature and provide thorough references.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written and logically organized from algorithm to convergence analysis.  
- The analysis includes consideration of necessary conditions and limitations (e.g., choices of δ and Γ).  
- Minor ambiguity in the description of the "splitting" process on page 4 (δ and Γ introduced as reals, then described as counts).  
- Subsequent algorithmic description clarifies the ambiguous splitting explanation, but initial wording could be improved.  
- Limited empirical evaluation — strong theoretical focus but few experiments to validate practical performance.  
- Practical implications: faster convergence rates could benefit distributed optimization applications.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant theoretical advance, novelty, clear writing, and potential practical impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical validation and the minor clarity issue on page 4.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept — contributions are significant and easily address minor issues.
