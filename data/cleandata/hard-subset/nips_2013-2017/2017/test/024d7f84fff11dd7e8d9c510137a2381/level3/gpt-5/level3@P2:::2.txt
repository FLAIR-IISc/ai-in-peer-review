Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the Min-Sum message-passing protocol with Splitting to solve average consensus. For quadratic objectives, the authors show: (i) ordinary Min-Sum can diverge on loopy graphs, but Min-Sum Splitting converges; (ii) with a particular choice of splitting parameters, the algorithm achieves an accelerated, subdiffusive rate that matches the asymptotic rates of shift-register (Chebyshev-like) methods. A key technical device is an auxiliary linear, two-state process on nodes that tracks the edge-based message dynamics, enabling a clean spectral analysis. The resulting rate translates to O(D log(D/ε)) iterations on cycles and tori (D is graph diameter), improving over classical diffusive methods. The paper also draws parallels to lifted non-reversible Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods (Heavy Ball/Nesterov), arguing that Splitting induces directionality (on edges) and memory (on nodes).

This sits among a large literature on accelerated consensus and distributed optimization. Closely related are linear-time consensus via polynomial filtering/shift registers (e.g., Olshevsky, “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications…,” arXiv), Chebyshev/second-order methods (Golub–Varga; Xiao–Boyd), and recent distributed optimization methods with linear convergence (e.g., Shi et al. on decentralized ADMM; EXTRA by Shi et al.; Jakovetić–Xavier–Moura). On lifted Markov chains, there is substantial prior work (Chen–Lovász–Pak; Diaconis–Holmes–Neal; Jung–Shah–Shin; Li–Dai), and links between lifting and distributed ADMM (França–Bento). The heavy-ball/Nesterov connections in consensus are also well-known (e.g., Ghadimi–Shames–Johansson; Cao–Spielman–Yeh; Liu et al.; Xiao–Boyd).

Quality
- Theory: The derivation of the edge-based linear update (Algorithm 2) and its node-based auxiliary counterpart (Algorithm 3) is careful, and the convergence/rate proof (Theorem 1) is technically sound. That said, the auxiliary dynamics essentially reduce to a standard two-term linear recurrence whose spectral analysis follows known Chebyshev arguments; Proposition 3 (not shown in main text) appears trivial and mainly supplemental.
- Claims and comparisons: Some claims need tightening. The statement about dimension-independent behavior on “good” graphs is potentially misleading; complete graphs reach consensus in a single step with standard nonnegative-weight updates, so please clarify which graph classes you mean and define “good.” The claimed O(D log(D/ε)) rate is only a logarithmic improvement over O(D log(1/ε)) and in worst-case graphs (D ≍ n) the extra log D can be a disadvantage relative to Olshevsky’s linear-time results. The paper would benefit from an explicit, apples-to-apples comparison with Olshevsky and with modern decentralized optimization methods (ADMM/EXTRA/fast gradient) that enjoy linear rates on strongly convex problems, as well as Scaman et al. on optimal rates and lower bounds.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with a clear line of argument and helpful formulations. However:
- Please clarify the terminology “directed edges on a simple graph.” What you implement is an orientation of each undirected edge, yielding the standard set of directed arcs E; spell this out to avoid confusion.
- Line 220 (p.6): “Laplacian” should be “Lagrangian.”
- Be explicit about which W and spectral quantities are required to tune γ, and how these are to be obtained in practice.
- Define “good graphs” precisely when discussing dimension-independent convergence.

Originality
The use of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus, the explicit node-based auxiliary system, and the observation that tuning can match shift-register rates are nice, but the conceptual connections to lifted chains and multi-step acceleration are largely known. The main novelty is framing these accelerations through Min-Sum Splitting and proving the corresponding rate within that framework. This is incremental relative to the consensus literature but potentially valuable for researchers using BP-like methods.

Significance
For consensus per se, the improvement over state of the art is limited: the rate matches classical Chebyshev/shift-register behavior and lags by a log D factor behind O(D log(1/ε)) bounds. The requirement of global spectral information to tune γ further limits practicality. The significance could be higher if the Splitting-based acceleration extends cleanly to broader BP settings where existing accelerations are not directly applicable; the paper hints at this but does not demonstrate it.

Pros
- Clean linearization and auxiliary process on nodes; analysis is transparent.
- Solid, rigorous rate guarantee matching shift-register methods.
- Helpful discussion connecting BP/Splitting to directionality/memory and to lifting/multi-step methods.

Cons
- Incremental relative to well-known accelerated consensus techniques; no empirical or theoretical superiority.
- Missing or weak comparisons to key prior art (Olshevsky; ADMM/EXTRA; Scaman et al.; França–Bento).
- Practicality limited by need for global spectral information for γ.
- Some misleading/muddled statements (complete graphs; “good” graphs), minor typos.

Requested changes
- Add and discuss the following references and compare rates and assumptions: Olshevsky (linear-time consensus), Shi et al. on decentralized ADMM (and its linear convergence), EXTRA (Shi et al.), Jakovetić–Xavier–Moura, Scaman et al. on optimal algorithms, França–Bento on lifting and ADMM.
- Clarify the complete-graph case and define the class of graphs for which you claim dimension-independent behavior.
- Explain the relationship between your K matrix and standard Chebyshev/shift-register schemes, and clearly acknowledge the long-known Heavy Ball/Nesterov/Polyak connections (also see Rabbat–Nowak–Bucklew for early consensus formulations).
- Discuss how γ can be selected or approximated in a decentralized manner; what happens with misspecification? Any robust variant?
- Fix the “directed edges on a simple graph” phrasing and the “Laplacian”→“Lagrangian” typo.
- If space permits, add numerical comparisons on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs vs. Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, Olshevsky, ADMM, and EXTRA.

After rebuttal
The authors’ responses addressed several clarity points and added some missing comparisons. I appreciate the effort; however, my overall evaluation remains unchanged because the main technical contribution still appears incremental relative to established accelerated consensus methods.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The work is solid and could be of interest to the BP community, but its incremental nature and limited advantage over existing accelerated consensus approaches reduce its impact.

Confidence
