

This paper proposes an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for distributed consensus optimization, establishing convergence rates for quadratic consensus problems. The authors leverage reparameterization from Ruozzi & Tatikonda (2013) and introduce an auxiliary linear process to track the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm's evolution, enabling convergence rate analysis. The main contributions include a novel proof technique via the auxiliary process, improved convergence rates matching shift-register methods, and connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods.

The paper demonstrates theoretical soundness with correct proofs for Propositions 1, 2, and Theorem 1, and the core technical contribution is significant in establishing accelerated convergence for Min-Sum protocols. The paper's novelty lies in connecting Min-Sum message-passing to two established acceleration paradigms (lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization), which is a valuable theoretical insight.

However, the manuscript suffers from several issues that limit its impact. While generally well-written, it contains multiple typos ("theirs neighbors" instead of "their neighbors," "double-stochastic" instead of "doubly-stochastic") and grammatical errors that distract from the technical content. More critically, algorithm descriptions are incomplete: key variables like R{vw} and r{vw} lack explanations, initializations for \hat{R}^0 and \hat{r}^0 are missing, and Algorithm 3 fails to explicitly identify which variables correspond to the auxiliary process. The paper omits crucial related work on decentralized optimization (Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989; Shi 2015) and classical acceleration techniques (Polyak, Nesterov), weakening the contextualization of the contribution.

The significance is limited by the lack of practical discussion. The authors fail to address when practitioners should prefer this method over established distributed consensus approaches, provide intuition for the auxiliary process, or discuss limitations of Min-Sum Splitting. The connection to machine learning applications remains unclear despite consensus being widely used in distributed ML.

While the theoretical contribution is sound and novel, the paper's practical relevance and clarity need substantial improvement. The authors should explicitly address the missing algorithmic details, provide a clearer discussion of practical utility, and better contextualize their work within the broader literature on distributed optimization.
