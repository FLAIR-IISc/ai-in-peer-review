Summary: The paper studies distributed consensus via a Min-Sum Splitting message-passing algorithm. Building on the reparameterization framework of Ruozzi & Tatikonda (2013), the authors analyze convergence on quadratic consensus/averaging and introduce an auxiliary linear process on nodes that tracks the edge-based Min-Sum Splitting dynamics. With a specific tuning (δ = 1, Γ = γW), they prove accelerated, subdiffusive rates comparable to shift-register methods, and highlight connections to lifted nonreversible Markov chains and multi-step first-order acceleration (Polyak/Nesterov).

Quality: The theoretical core is solid. The statements and proofs of Propositions 1–2 and Theorem 1 appear correct; the auxiliary-process technique is technically neat and makes the spectral analysis transparent. However, the practical side is underdeveloped: the method requires global spectral information (ρW) to set γ, and there is little guidance on when it should be preferred over standard decentralized methods (e.g., EXTRA, gradient tracking, distributed ADMM), or how to robustify against asynchrony, noise, or time-varying graphs. Algorithmic descriptions need completion (see below).

Originality: The proof technique via an auxiliary node-level linear system is novel in this context and clarifies the link between BP-style Splitting, lifted chains, and multi-step acceleration. This is a meaningful conceptual contribution.

Significance: The paper advances understanding of accelerated consensus with Min-Sum Splitting and may stimulate further work on extending acceleration beyond quadratics. Impact would be greater with clearer algorithms and a discussion of applicability beyond idealized settings.

Clarity: Generally well-written but marred by typos and missing definitions. Please fix: “theirs neighbors” → “their neighbors”; “double-stochastic” → “doubly-stochastic”; “casted” → “cast”; and likely “Laplace/Laplacian multipliers” on lines 220–221 should be “Lagrange/Lagrangian multipliers.” In Algorithm 2/3, define all variables (e.g., Rvw, rvw), provide initializations (e.g., R̂0, r̂0), ensure equation numbering consistency (K̂ is referenced as (5) but earlier is (4)), and explicitly identify in Algorithm 3 which variables constitute the auxiliary process that corresponds to the edge-level parameters.

Related work: Please cite foundational decentralized optimization and acceleration literature: Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis (1989), Tsitsiklis et al. (1986), Srinivasan et al. (2009), Wei & Ozdaglar/Wei Shi et al. (EXTRA, ADMM convergence), and classical acceleration (Polyak 1964; Nesterov 1983/2004).

Questions for authors:
- Practicality: How to set/estimate γ without global eigen-information? Can you provide heuristics or adaptive tuning?
- Limitations/intuition: When does Min-Sum Splitting fail or underperform? Give intuition for the auxiliary process and its memory term.
- Beyond quadratics: Can the framework extend to general strongly convex, composite, or stochastic consensus common in ML?
- Why ML interest: Clarify scenarios (e.g., federated averaging under bandwidth constraints) where this approach is preferable.

Pros: solid theory; new proof technique; clear links to known accelerations. Cons: incomplete algorithms; limited practical guidance; missing references; typos.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying algorithms and strengthening practical discussion.
