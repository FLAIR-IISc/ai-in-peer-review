This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum Splitting to the consensus problem in distributed optimization. The authors demonstrate that while the standard Min-Sum algorithm may not converge, a modified version—Splitting—does converge and, crucially, achieves accelerated convergence rates comparable to shift-register methods. The work establishes a connection between Min-Sum schemes and both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization, offering a unifying perspective on acceleration in this context.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly situates its contribution within the existing literature. The introduction provides a comprehensive overview of relevant work in distributed optimization, consensus algorithms, and message-passing techniques, drawing connections to work by Boyd et al., Tsitsiklis, and others. The authors diligently cite related work, including recent NIPS proceedings (e.g., [27, 30, 36]), demonstrating awareness of the current research landscape. The motivation is clear, the problem statement is well-defined, and the presentation is logically structured.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting is, to the best of my understanding, a novel contribution. Establishing a theoretical foundation for the acceleration provided by splitting is a significant advance.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper appears technically rigorous. The authors provide a detailed description of the algorithm and supporting propositions, culminating in Theorem 1, which establishes the accelerated convergence rate. The connection to shift-register methods lends further credibility.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is remarkably clear and well-organized. The authors adeptly explain complex concepts and provide sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand the key ideas.
*   **Significance:** The results are potentially significant. Accelerated convergence is crucial for large-scale distributed optimization problems, and the presented method offers a competitive alternative to existing approaches. The connection to multiple areas – lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods – suggests broader implications.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper successfully draws connections between Min-Sum Splitting and existing theoretical frameworks, providing a unified understanding of acceleration techniques in distributed optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Ambiguity in Explanation (Minor):** On page 4, the explanation of splitting – "splitting each term φ\_vw into Γ\_vw terms, and each φ\_v into δ terms" – is somewhat ambiguous given that δ and Γ\_vw were introduced as real numbers, not quantities representing a number of splits. A more precise explanation of this reparameterization would be helpful.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the theoretical results are compelling, the paper lacks empirical validation. Including some simulations to demonstrate the performance of Min-Sum Splitting on different graph topologies would strengthen the findings.
*   **Assumptions in Theorem 1:** The assumptions made in Theorem 1, particularly regarding the choice of γ and the properties of the matrix W, might be restrictive in practice. Discussing the robustness of the results to deviations from these assumptions would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound analysis of Min-Sum Splitting, a promising approach to accelerated distributed optimization.
*   The results are significant and offer a potential improvement over existing methods.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly positioned within the relevant literature.
*   The established connection between Min-Sum schemes, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods provides a valuable unifying perspective.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical validation limits the practical impact of the results.
*   The assumptions underlying Theorem 1 might be restrictive.
*   The minor ambiguity in the explanation on page 4, while not critical, could be clarified.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The theoretical results are compelling, the presentation is excellent, and the connections to existing work are insightful. While the lack of empirical validation is a minor concern, the theoretical significance of the work outweighs this limitation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the ambiguity on page 4 and potentially include some discussion of the practical implications and limitations of the assumptions made in Theorem 1.



**Rating:** 7 (Strong Accept)
