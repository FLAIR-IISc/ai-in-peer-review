This paper investigates the application of the Min-Sum Splitting message-passing algorithm to the average consensus problem. The authors show that while the standard Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge on graphs with cycles, the "Splitting" variant, which introduces tunable parameters, can be made to converge. The main contribution is a proof that with a specific choice of these parameters, the algorithm achieves an "accelerated" or "subdiffusive" convergence rate. The analysis is performed by introducing an auxiliary message-passing scheme on the graph's nodes, which tracks the evolution of the primary algorithm running on the directed edges. The authors connect their approach to two established lines of work on accelerated consensus: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods from convex optimization, arguing that the splitting parameters introduce a form of momentum or directionality analogous to these other methods.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper is the novel connection it establishes between Min-Sum Splitting, a technique from the graphical models community, and the well-studied field of accelerated distributed optimization. Drawing parallels between the "splitting" mechanism and concepts like lifted Markov chains and momentum methods is insightful and could potentially open up new avenues for designing and analyzing message-passing algorithms. The introduction of an auxiliary message-passing scheme to simplify the analysis by moving from the edge space to the node space is a clever technical contribution that makes the convergence analysis more tractable.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the interesting conceptual connections, the paper has several significant weaknesses regarding its positioning within the literature, the significance of its results, and clarity.

1.  **Insufficient Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The paper omits several crucial and highly relevant references, leading to an overstatement of its contribution.
    *   Most notably, the paper does not cite or compare its results to Olshevsky (2017), "Linear Time Average Consensus...". Olshevsky presents an algorithm with a convergence time of O(D log(1/ε)), where D is the graph diameter. The rate claimed in this paper is O(D log(D/ε)). This is not a clear improvement and is strictly worse for graphs with large diameters (e.g., when D is on the order of n). The authors must address this comparison, as Olshevsky's rate appears superior.
    *   The broader literature on accelerated consensus and distributed optimization is not adequately covered. There is no mention of seminal works like EXTRA (Shi et al., 2015), other fast distributed gradient methods (e.g., Jakovetić et al., 2014), or recent work on optimal rates for distributed optimization (Scaman et al., 2017). The linear convergence of ADMM for consensus has also been established (Shi et al., 2014), which is relevant context.
    *   The connection between message-passing ADMM and lifted Markov chains has been explored by França and Bento (2016), which should be cited as related work.

2.  **Significance of the Contribution:**
    *   The claimed convergence rate of O(D log(D/ε)) offers marginal, if any, improvement over existing work. For many graph topologies, the additional log(D) factor makes the rate non-competitive.
    *   The claim that classical methods suffer from a "diffusive" rate while this method is "subdiffusive" is well-established for multi-step/momentum methods. The connection made in the last paragraph of Section 4—that multi-step methods can be written as a linear system on an expanded state—is a long-known fact in the consensus literature (e.g., related to Chebyshev acceleration, Polyak/Nesterov momentum). This link was discussed in early work by Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew (2005). The authors should acknowledge this prior art more clearly.
    *   The novelty of the "auxiliary message-passing" scheme is questionable. For the quadratic consensus problem, the analysis ultimately reduces to the spectral properties of a linear system. It is highly likely that the auxiliary algorithm is a re-derivation of applying a standard acceleration technique (like a shift-register or Nesterov's method) to the Min-Sum formulation. The paper needs to clarify what is fundamentally new about this scheme beyond re-casting a known acceleration principle in the language of Min-Sum.

3.  **Clarity and Minor Issues:**
    *   The paper states that for graphs with good expansion properties, the convergence time scales "independently of the dimension of the problem." This is misleading. For complete graphs, consensus is achieved in one step. The authors should clarify for which graph classes (e.g., expanders) this dimension-independent complexity holds and define what constitutes a "good" graph in this context.
    *   Proposition 2, which connects the edge-based algorithm to the node-based auxiliary algorithm, appears to be a relatively straightforward change of variables. Its presentation as a core proposition seems to overstate its role; it is an instrumental step for the analysis but perhaps better suited for the supplementary material.
    *   The worst-case complexity across all n-node graphs remains O(n), since the diameter D can be O(n). The acceleration primarily benefits specific graph topologies (like grids and cycles) and does not improve the fundamental worst-case dependency on n.

In summary, while the paper presents an interesting bridge between Min-Sum methods and accelerated consensus, its claims of novelty and performance improvement are not well-supported due to a lack of engagement with the current state-of-the-art literature. The achieved convergence rate is not better than existing results and can be worse. The main value lies in the conceptual framing, but the technical contribution needs to be more clearly distinguished from existing acceleration techniques for linear systems.
