The paper "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting" presents a novel approach to solving the consensus problem in distributed optimization using the Min-Sum message-passing protocol. The authors show that a modified version of the Min-Sum algorithm, known as Splitting, yields convergence to the problem solution, and they prove that a proper choice of tuning parameters allows Min-Sum Splitting to achieve subdiffusive accelerated convergence rates.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the Min-Sum protocol and its application to the consensus problem. The main contributions of the paper are the first connection of Min-Sum schemes with lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step methods in convex optimization, the first proof of how the directionality embedded in Belief Propagation protocols can be tuned and exploited to accelerate the convergence rate towards the problem solution, and the first analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting.

However, the reviewer has some concerns regarding the claimed improvement in convergence rates. The paper claims that the proposed Min-Sum Splitting algorithm achieves a convergence rate of O(D log(D/ε)), which is an improvement over the O(D log(1/ε)) rate achieved by previous algorithms. However, the reviewer notes that this improvement is not impressive, especially considering that the worst-case bound remains O(n), which is equivalent to O(D) across all n-node graphs.

The reviewer also points out that the paper is missing some important citations and comparisons to previous work. For example, the paper does not cite Olshevsky's "Linear Time Average Consensus..." paper, which achieves a competitive or better convergence rate. Additionally, the paper omits important references on consensus-optimization, such as Shi et al.'s work on ADMM linear convergence and Scaman et al.'s work on state-of-the-art algorithms and their linear rates.

Furthermore, the reviewer notes that the paper's claim about complete graphs is misleading, as complete graphs converge in one step. The authors should clarify which graph classes yield dimension-independent complexity and define "good" graphs.

The reviewer also suggests that the paper's contribution is unclear, as the proposed "auxiliary message-passing" algorithm likely mirrors Nesterov acceleration on min-sum. The reviewer notes that any gains likely stem from spectral-gap handling, but the worst-case bound remains O(n).

Finally, the reviewer points out some minor errors in the paper, such as a typo on page 6, where "Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian," and a confusing statement on page 6 regarding the use of directed edges on a simple graph.

In conclusion, while the paper presents some interesting ideas and contributions, the reviewer has some concerns regarding the claimed improvement in convergence rates and the omission of important citations and comparisons to previous work. The authors should address these concerns and provide a clearer explanation of their contributions and the significance of their results.
