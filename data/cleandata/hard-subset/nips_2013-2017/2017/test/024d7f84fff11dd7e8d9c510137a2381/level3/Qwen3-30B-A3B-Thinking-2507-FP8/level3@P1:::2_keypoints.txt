- Studies Min-Sum Splitting for consensus, claiming accelerated convergence and linking it to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods.  
- Claimed contributions: first connection of Min-Sum to lifted Markov chains/multi-step methods; tunable directionality in BP for acceleration; first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting; a protocol with improved rates over prior Min-Sum.  
- Strength: solid theoretical convergence analysis and a novel connection to acceleration techniques; auxiliary-process proof technique is well-motivated and clearer than prior Min-Sum analyses.  
- Main overstatement: O(D log(D/ε)) rate for cycles/tori offers little practical benefit over O(D log(1/ε)) from Olshevsky (2015), which is not cited or compared.  
- Misleading claim about complete graphs: complete graphs converge in one step, so saying dimension-independent complexity is confusing and needs clarification.  
- Crucial related work omitted: Olshevsky (linear-time consensus), Shi et al. (EXTRA), Scaman et al. (optimal distributed optimization), and França & Bento (Markov chain lifting and distributed ADMM).  
- Technical concern: the auxiliary message-passing resembles Nesterov acceleration and the analysis reduces to standard linear system analysis, making the novelty unclear.  
- Proposition 3 is trivial and adds little substantive value.  
- "Subdiffusive" claim is misleading: worst-case complexity can still be O(n) when D = Θ(n), so no worst-case improvement.  
- Specific errors to fix: "Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian" (line 220); use of directed edges on a simple graph (line 243) is confusing.  
- Missing citation for the long-known connection between consensus and Heavy Ball/Nesterov/Polyak methods (Rabbat, Nowak, Bucklew).  
- Overall significance limited: interesting theoretical link but marginal practical impact without stronger comparisons and clearer differentiation from existing accelerated consensus methods.  
- Decision pros: novel theoretical connection and useful proof technique; cons: overstated improvements, missing key references, misleading statements, trivial contributions, and technical errors.  
- Suggested revisions: temper claims, add thorough literature review and empirical/theoretical comparisons, and clearly state how Min-Sum Splitting differs from existing accelerated methods.
