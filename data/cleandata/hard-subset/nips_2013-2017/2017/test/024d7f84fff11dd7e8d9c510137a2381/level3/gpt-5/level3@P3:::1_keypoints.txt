- Paper studies distributed averaging via Min-Sum and shows Min-Sum Splitting yields convergent, accelerated consensus through a reparameterization with splitting parameters δ and Γ.  
- Main technical idea: introduce δ and Γ to induce a directional edge-based linear dynamics and construct an exact auxiliary two-step node-level linear system that tracks the algorithm.  
- With δ=1 and Γ=γW (W symmetric, W1=1) and γ tuned by ρ(W−11ᵀ/n), the method matches shift-register/second-order accelerated rates and achieves subdiffusive convergence (O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles/tori vs. Θ(D² log(1/ε))).  
- Situates work relative to Min-Sum convergence literature, prior Min-Sum for consensus, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step first-order accelerations.  
- Novelty: connects Min-Sum Splitting to lifted non-reversible Markov chains and multi-step acceleration, with a clean spectral analysis via the auxiliary process.  
- Strength: precise auxiliary two-state node-level linear system makes spectral analysis tractable and yields a clear rate bound (Theorem 1).  
- Strength: provable acceleration (square-root improvement over diffusive consensus) with asymptotic rates matching shift-register methods.  
- Strength: conceptual bridge between directionality/momentum interpretations of accelerated consensus.  
- Strength: well written and carefully positioned within related work.  
- Strength: perceived novelty in linking Min-Sum Splitting to known acceleration paradigms and producing associated rate analysis.  
- Weakness: ambiguous language about “splitting each term into Γ_vw/δ terms” since δ and Γ are real, not integer counts—needs clarifying that this is a reweighting interpretation.  
- Weakness: core rate result requires synchronous updates and global spectral information (ρ_W) to choose γ; robustness to mis-specified γ and tuning methods are not discussed.  
- Weakness: primarily theoretical with limited empirical validation; experiments comparing to Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, heavy-ball, Nesterov, and ADMM would strengthen claims.  
- Weakness: Proposition 1 uses a positivity condition on R̂^t — need clarity when this holds under recommended initialization or prefer Algorithm 3 to avoid the condition.  
- Weakness: scope limited to quadratic averaging; unclear which elements extend to strongly convex separable objectives or non-quadratic settings.  
- Quality: analysis is technically sound with precise statements and clean spectral characterization; proofs deferred to supplement but assumptions explicit.  
- Clarity: generally excellent, but suggest fixing splitting ambiguity, reducing notation overload, briefly justifying the K(δ,Γ) block form, and summarizing key spectral steps.  
- Originality: novel connection between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted chains, and multi-step acceleration; auxiliary tracking and rate guarantees appear new.  
- Significance: advances a foundational primitive (consensus) by showing Min-Sum Splitting can match accelerated rates and offers a new tool for further work (non-quadratic, ADMM links, asynchronous).  
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to γ mis-specification; distributed estimation/tuning of γ; memory/stability in node-level implementation; prospects for time-varying/asynchronous graphs; extension to strongly convex separable objectives.  
- Minor comments: rephrase splitting interpretation to avoid confusion, define 1 and diag(Γ1) explicitly, and compare per-iteration complexity (messages/state) to standard consensus.  
- Arguments for acceptance: clear, well-placed, technically solid contribution with novel conceptual bridge and provable accelerated rates.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical validation, restricted scope to quadratic synchronous settings, and dependence on global spectral tuning; some minor presentation issues.  
- Reviewer confidence: moderate (familiar with accelerated consensus and related methods but not an expert in Min-Sum/BP convergence).
