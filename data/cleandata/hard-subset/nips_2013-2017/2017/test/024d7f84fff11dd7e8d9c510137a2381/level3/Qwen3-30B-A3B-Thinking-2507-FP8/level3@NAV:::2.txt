

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum message-passing with splitting to solve the average consensus problem, claiming accelerated convergence rates compared to traditional diffusive methods. The authors establish that Min-Sum Splitting yields linear message-passing dynamics on directed edges and propose an auxiliary algorithm on the original graph to analyze convergence. They claim a convergence rate of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, which they position as a "square-root improvement" over diffusive methods' O(D² log(1/ε)).

While the technical content is generally sound and the paper is well-written, several critical issues undermine the claimed contributions. The most significant problem is the misleading claim about complete graphs: the paper states "for complete graphs, the convergence time scales like Θ(D log(1/ε))" when complete graphs actually converge in one step. The paper fails to clarify which graph classes yield dimension-independent complexity or define "good" graphs.

Crucially, the paper omits key related work. It misses Olshevsky's "Linear Time Average Consensus" (which establishes optimal Θ(D log(1/ε)) rates), Shi et al.'s EXTRA algorithm, Scaman et al.'s work on distributed ADMM, and Franca and Bento's research on Markov chain lifting. The paper also fails to cite Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew for the well-known connection to Heavy Ball/Nesterov acceleration.

The claimed rate improvement is minimal: D log(D/ε) offers little advantage over Olshevsky's D log(1/ε) and is worse in worst-case scenarios (D ~ n). The "auxiliary message-passing" scheme appears to merely rederive Nesterov acceleration, and the main contribution is unclear as the analysis reduces to spectral gap considerations. Proposition 3 is trivial, and the paper contains technical errors (e.g., "Laplacian" instead of "Lagrangian" on p.6).

While the paper demonstrates a connection between Min-Sum and accelerated methods, the novelty is overstated, and the technical contribution is limited. The significance is diminished by the failure to properly contextualize the work within the extensive literature on accelerated consensus. The paper would benefit from thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods and clearer definition of the claimed improvements.
