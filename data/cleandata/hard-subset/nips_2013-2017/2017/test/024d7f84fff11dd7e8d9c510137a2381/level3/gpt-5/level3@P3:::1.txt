Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies distributed averaging/consensus through Min-Sum (a.k.a. BP for quadratic objectives) and shows that the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm can be made convergent and accelerated via a “splitting” reparameterization. The main technical idea is to introduce splitting parameters (δ and Γ) that induce a directional, edge-based linear dynamics, and then construct an auxiliary node-based, two-step linear system that exactly tracks the algorithm’s output. With a specific choice of parameters (δ = 1, Γ = γW with W symmetric, W1 = 1, and γ tuned via ρ(W − 11ᵀ/n)), the method matches the accelerated rates of shift-register/second-order methods and achieves subdiffusive convergence: for cycles and tori the asymptotic convergence time becomes O(D log(D/ε)) vs. Θ(D² log(1/ε)) for diffusive consensus.

The paper situates the work relative to: (i) Min-Sum convergence results (e.g., scaled diagonally dominant conditions; failures on loopy graphs), (ii) prior Min-Sum for consensus [Moallemi & Van Roy], which established non-convergence of ordinary Min-Sum and analyzed a softened variant with rates tied to a Markov chain on directed edges, (iii) lifted Markov chains for accelerated mixing, and (iv) multi-step first-order (heavy-ball/Nesterov/shift-register, Chebyshev) accelerations on the original graph. The novelty is the connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods, plus a clean spectral analysis via the auxiliary node-level process that recovers shift-register rates.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: A precise auxiliary two-state node-level linear system that tracks the edge-based Min-Sum Splitting. This makes the spectral analysis tractable and leads to a clear rate bound (Theorem 1).
- Acceleration: Establishes a square-root improvement over diffusive consensus, yielding O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles/tori and rates matching shift-register methods asymptotically.
- Conceptual connections: A compelling bridge between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted non-reversible Markov chains (directionality), and multi-step first-order methods (memory/momentum).
- Writing and positioning: The paper is well written and carefully places the contribution within the state of the art. The presentation of related accelerated consensus approaches is balanced and informative.
- Novelty: To the best of my knowledge (not an expert), the connection between Min-Sum Splitting and these two acceleration paradigms, plus the associated rate analysis, is new.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Clarity on “splitting”: On page 4 the wording “splitting each term φ_vw into Γ_vw terms, and each φ_v into δ terms” is ambiguous because δ and Γ_vw are introduced as real numbers. This interpretation only makes literal sense for positive integers; otherwise it is a reparameterization/reweighting viewpoint. Please clarify that the “multiple term” interpretation is conceptual and only exact when parameters are integers; otherwise it’s a continuous reweighting that yields the same update equations.
- Scope: The core rate result is derived for quadratic averaging with synchronous updates and requires global spectral information (ρ_W) to choose γ. Discussion of robustness to mis-specified γ and ways to estimate/tune γ (e.g., polynomial approximations or safeguarded schedules) would strengthen practical relevance.
- Empirical validation: The paper appears primarily theoretical. Experiments on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs comparing to Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, heavy-ball, Nesterov, and ADMM-based consensus would substantiate the theory and show constant factors.
- Assumptions and initialization: Proposition 1 includes a positivity condition on terms built from R̂^t. It would help to state when this condition is guaranteed under the recommended initialization (R̂0 = r̂0 = 0) and parameters of Theorem 1, or to move to Algorithm 3 as the primary vehicle to avoid such conditions.
- Generality: Results are for averaging; do any elements extend beyond quadratic consensus (e.g., strongly convex separable objectives)? Even partial extensions or a discussion of technical obstacles would be useful.

Quality
- The analysis is technically sound, with a clean spectral characterization via the auxiliary process and clear links to known shift-register results. Proof details are deferred to the supplement, but the statements are precise and assumptions explicit.
- The improvement over [27] is qualitative and quantified: convergence for general graphs with subdiffusive rate versus only partial guarantees for specific topologies.

Clarity
- Generally excellent. The algorithms and matrices are well defined; the roles of δ and Γ are clear in the main theorem.
- Suggested improvements: fix the “splitting into terms” ambiguity; reduce notation overload (Γ as both weights and “number of splits”); briefly recap why the auxiliary K(δ,Γ) block matrix takes its specific form; and summarize the key spectral steps.

Originality
- The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step acceleration is, to my knowledge, novel. The auxiliary node-level tracking and the resulting rate guarantee for Min-Sum Splitting appear new.

Significance
- Consensus is a foundational primitive. Showing that Min-Sum Splitting can match accelerated consensus rates and tying it to established acceleration mechanisms provides both conceptual insight and a new tool that others may build upon (e.g., towards non-quadratic objectives, ADMM connections, or asynchronous settings).

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to γ mis-specification? Is there a conservative γ that still accelerates without precise ρ_W?
- Can you estimate γ in a distributed manner (e.g., via power iterations, Chebyshev bounds, or local degree-based heuristics)?
- Does Algorithm 3 suggest memory requirements of just two states per node in practice? Are there stability issues when implementing directly at node level?
- Any prospects for time-varying/asynchronous graphs?
- Can the auxiliary-process approach extend to strongly convex separable objectives beyond averaging?

Minor comments and typos
- Clarify that the “splitting into Γ_vw and δ terms” is an interpretation that is exact for integer parameters; otherwise it is a reweighting. Consider rephrasing to avoid confusion.
- Define 1 explicitly where used and remind the reader of diag(Γ1).
- A brief comparison of complexity per iteration (messages/state) versus standard consensus would help.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, well-placed, and technically solid contribution.
- Novel conceptual bridge between disparate acceleration paradigms and Min-Sum.
- Provable accelerated rates with a transparent spectral mechanism.
- Potential to influence follow-up work on accelerated message passing and distributed optimization.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical validation.
- Scope restricted to quadratic consensus and synchronous settings; dependence on global spectral information for tuning.
- Some presentation ambiguities (minor and fixable).

Reviewer confidence
- Moderate: I am not an expert on Min-Sum/BP convergence theory, but I am familiar with accelerated consensus, lifted chains, and polynomial/shift-register methods.
