### **Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the distributed consensus problem, a fundamental primitive in distributed optimization and machine learning. The authors apply a generalized version of the Min-Sum message-passing algorithm, known as Min-Sum Splitting, to the network averaging problem. While standard Min-Sum is known to fail on graphs with cycles, this work demonstrates that the "Splitting" variant not only converges but, with a proper choice of its tuning parameters, achieves an accelerated, subdiffusive rate of convergence.

The core technical contribution is a rigorous analysis of the algorithm's convergence rate. The authors cleverly construct an auxiliary multi-step linear process on the graph's nodes to track the evolution of the original, more complex, edge-based message-passing scheme. This novel analytical tool allows them to prove that Min-Sum Splitting can match the accelerated rates of shift-register methods, which represent a square-root improvement over classical diffusive methods for graph topologies like cycles and tori.

A key conceptual contribution of the paper is establishing a previously unrecognized connection between three distinct lines of research: Min-Sum message-passing, lifted Markov chain techniques for accelerating random walks, and accelerated multi-step first-order methods from convex optimization. The paper convincingly argues that the "splitting" parameters introduce a form of directionality or memory into the message-passing protocol, which serves as the mechanism for acceleration, analogous to the mechanisms used in the other two fields.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The results appear to be a significant and novel advancement. Demonstrating that a general message-passing framework like Min-Sum can be tuned to achieve state-of-the-art accelerated convergence rates for consensus is a powerful finding. This elevates Min-Sum Splitting from a heuristic for ensuring convergence to a principled method for designing high-performance distributed algorithms. The paper's most impactful contribution may be the conceptual bridge it builds between message-passing, lifted Markov chains, and momentum-based optimization. This connection is insightful and is likely to inspire further research into using message-passing formalisms to design and analyze other accelerated distributed methods.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The introduction of the auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) to analyze the primary algorithm (Algorithm 2) is an elegant and effective proof strategy. It successfully transforms a difficult-to-analyze system on the graph's edges into a more tractable multi-step system on the nodes. The main result, Theorem 1, is precise and rigorously connects the algorithm's performance to the spectral properties of the underlying graph communication matrix, correctly identifying the parameter choice that recovers the known accelerated rates of shift-register methods.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and a pleasure to read. The authors do an excellent job of situating their work within the vast and complex literature on distributed consensus, clearly distinguishing their contributions from prior work on both Min-Sum algorithms (e.g., [27]) and other acceleration techniques. The motivation is clear, the problem is well-defined, and the theoretical development flows logically.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Ambiguity in the "Splitting" Interpretation:** There is a minor but notable point of confusion in the presentation on page 4, Section 2. The text describes the algorithm's name as originating from an interpretation where "each term φ_vw [is split] into Γ_vw terms, and each term φ_v into δ terms." This language strongly suggests that δ and Γ_vw are positive integers. However, the algorithm itself is defined for arbitrary real-valued parameters, and the subsequent analysis relies on this generality (e.g., γ in Theorem 1 is real). This discrepancy could be confusing to readers. I suggest the authors clarify that the integer-based splitting is the *motivation* for the algorithm's structure and name, but that the formulation presented is a more general one valid for real parameters.

2.  **Limited Exploration of Parameter Space:** The analysis focuses on a specific parameter setting (δ=1, Γ=γW) that is carefully chosen to recover the shift-register dynamics. While this is a powerful and important result, it leaves the role of the splitting parameters somewhat underexplored. For instance, what is the effect of choosing δ ≠ 1? Does the structure of Γ offer other avenues for acceleration beyond a simple scaling of a base matrix W? A brief discussion on the sensitivity to these parameters or the intuition behind their roles would strengthen the paper, though the current focused result is already a complete and valuable contribution.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization. It is well-motivated, technically solid, and clearly written. The discovery that Min-Sum Splitting can be used to achieve accelerated consensus, combined with the novel conceptual links it establishes to other acceleration techniques, makes this work highly original and impactful. The weaknesses identified are minor points of presentation that can be easily addressed. I strongly recommend acceptance.
