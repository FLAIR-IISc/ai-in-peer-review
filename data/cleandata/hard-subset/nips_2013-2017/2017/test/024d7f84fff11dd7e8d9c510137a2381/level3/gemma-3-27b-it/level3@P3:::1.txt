This paper investigates the convergence of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem in distributed optimization. The authors demonstrate that, unlike the standard Min-Sum algorithm, a modified version (splitting) *does* converge, and importantly, achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence rates comparable to shift-register methods. A key contribution is establishing a novel connection between min-sum algorithms and lifted Markov chain techniques, drawing parallels to accelerated first-order methods in convex optimization.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly situates its contribution within the existing literature. The introduction provides a thorough overview of prior work on Min-Sum, distributed consensus, and acceleration techniques. The authors meticulously reference relevant publications, including work from NIPS (e.g., [27, 36]) and other key venues. They convincingly motivate their work by highlighting the limitations of existing approaches (diffusive rates) and explicitly addressing how their contribution advances the state-of-the-art. The organization is logical, building from the general Min-Sum splitting algorithm to its application to the consensus problem, followed by a rigorous analysis of convergence rates.

The results appear novel and constitute a qualitative advance. While I am not an expert in this specific area, the theoretical analysis and proof sketches (with full proofs in the supplementary material) appear sound. The connection drawn between Min-Sum splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods is a significant insight, suggesting a deeper underlying relationship between these seemingly disparate approaches. The established convergence rates, and the demonstrated link to shift-register methods, provide a concrete and quantifiable improvement over existing methods. The paper’s careful consideration of the necessary conditions and limitations (e.g., the choice of δ and Γ) adds to its credibility.

A minor weakness lies in the clarity of the description on page 4. The wording “splitting each term φ_vw into Γ_vw terms, and each φ_v into δ terms” is indeed ambiguous, as δ and Γ_vw were previously introduced as real numbers, not as counts of terms. Providing a more precise mathematical formulation or a clearer explanation of this "splitting" process would improve understanding. While the subsequent algorithmic description clarifies this point, the initial explanation could be more intuitive.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, including convergence proofs.
*   **Novelty:** The application of Min-Sum splitting to consensus, the proof of its convergence, and the analysis of its accelerated rates are novel contributions.
*   **Clear Writing & Contextualization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly explaining complex concepts and situating the work within the broader literature.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The established connections to lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods provide valuable insights.
*   **Practical Implications:** The faster convergence rates offered by Min-Sum splitting have practical implications for distributed optimization problems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Ambiguous Description (Page 4):** The initial description of the "splitting" process is slightly unclear.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper focuses heavily on theoretical analysis. While justifiable for a NIPS submission, some limited empirical validation could further strengthen the results.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   This paper presents a significant theoretical advance in the field of distributed optimization.
*   The established convergence rates and connections to other accelerated methods are valuable contributions.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and provides a clear and insightful analysis.
*   The novelty of the approach and the potential for practical impact make this work highly relevant to the NIPS community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The limited empirical evaluation might be seen as a drawback by some reviewers.
*   The clarity issue on page 4, although minor, could detract from the overall readability.

**Overall:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The theoretical contributions are significant, the writing is excellent, and the results represent a genuine advance in the field. The minor weakness regarding clarity is easily addressed, and the lack of extensive empirical evaluation is understandable given the focus of the work. This paper aligns well with the scope of NIPS and is likely to be of significant interest to researchers in distributed optimization, machine learning, and related areas.
