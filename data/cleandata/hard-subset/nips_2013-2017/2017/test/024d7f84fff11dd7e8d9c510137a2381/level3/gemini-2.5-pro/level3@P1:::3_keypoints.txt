- Paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for distributed consensus (quadratic objectives) and shows Splitting can be tuned to guarantee convergence where ordinary Min-Sum fails on loopy graphs.  
- Main contribution: theoretical convergence-rate analysis using an auxiliary linear node process that yields accelerated, subdiffusive convergence matching shift-register methods.  
- Key insight: splitting parameters introduce momentum/directionality, connecting Min-Sum Splitting to lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods.  

Strengths
- Originality/significance: first formal convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, showing a square-root acceleration over diffusive methods.  
- Novel connections: draws a compelling bridge between message-passing, accelerated optimization, and accelerated random walks.  
- Technical soundness: clever auxiliary node message-passing tool; proofs appear correct and leverage spectral analysis of shift-register methods.  

Weaknesses
- Limited scope/practicality: analysis restricted to quadratic (averaging) problems; extension to general convex objectives is not discussed.  
- Clarity/exposition: algorithm descriptions lack definitions/initializations (e.g., initial messages µ̂0vw), and intuition for the auxiliary process is weak.  
- Incomplete literature review: omits foundational acceleration methods (Polyak, Nesterov) and key distributed optimization works (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis).  

Detailed comments / requests
- Typos/minor errors: manuscript needs proofreading (several grammatical and terminology mistakes noted).  
- Algorithm clarity: specify initialization of R̂0 and r̂0 in Algorithm 2 (Theorem 1 assumes zeros); clarify that rs, qs, Rs, Qs in Algorithm 3 are vectors in R^V.  
- Practicality question: clarify scenarios where Min-Sum Splitting outperforms methods like ADMM or accelerated dual ascent (communication, robustness, implementation).  
- Generalization question: discuss extension to non-quadratic objectives where Min-Sum updates lack closed-form solutions and whether the auxiliary linear analysis can be generalized or approximated.  
- Intuition question: explain why a 2m-edge-variable process can be exactly tracked by a 2n-node-variable auxiliary process and whether this reflects a deeper consensus-structure property.  

Overall takeaway
- Theoretical and conceptual contribution is solid and valuable, but the paper’s impact is reduced by scope limits, exposition issues, and incomplete positioning in related literature; addressing generalization and practical relevance would strengthen the work.
