**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting message-passing algorithm for distributed consensus optimization. The authors use a reparameterization technique from Ruozzi and Tatikonda (2013) and analyze the convergence of the algorithm on quadratic consensus problems. The main technical tool used in the paper is an auxiliary linear process that tracks the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. The authors establish improved convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting and show connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods.

**Strengths of the Paper**

* The paper presents a new proof technique via an auxiliary process, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The authors provide improved convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, which is a widely used algorithm in distributed optimization.
* The paper establishes connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods, which is an important contribution to the understanding of distributed optimization algorithms.
* The manuscript is generally clear and well-written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

* The paper contains several typos and grammatical errors throughout, which can be distracting for the reader.
* The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear correct, but the authors could provide more details and explanations to make the proofs more accessible to a wider audience.
* The algorithm descriptions are incomplete, and some variables are not explained. The authors should provide more details about the initialization of the variables and the auxiliary linear process.
* The paper omits relevant literature on (sub)gradient and decentralized optimization, which is an important area of research in machine learning.
* The authors do not provide a clear discussion of the practicality of the method and when to use it versus other distributed consensus methods.
* The paper lacks intuition behind using the auxiliary process and does not provide a clear explanation of the limitations of Min-Sum Splitting.
