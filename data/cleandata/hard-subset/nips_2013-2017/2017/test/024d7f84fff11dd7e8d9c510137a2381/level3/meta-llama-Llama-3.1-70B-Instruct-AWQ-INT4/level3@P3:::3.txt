This paper proposes an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting message-passing algorithm for distributed consensus optimization. The authors use reparameterization from Ruozzi & Tatikonda (2013) and analyze convergence on quadratic consensus problems. The main technical tool is an auxiliary linear process that tracks the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. The authors establish a new proof technique via an auxiliary process, improved convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, and connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods.

The manuscript is generally clear and well-written, but contains several typos and grammatical errors throughout. For example, "theirs neighbors" should be replaced with "their neighbors", "double-stochastic" should be replaced with "doubly-stochastic", and "casted" should be replaced with "cast". Additionally, the authors misuse "Laplacian"/"Laplace multipliers" instead of "Lagrangian"/"Lagrange multipliers" on lines 220-221.

The algorithm descriptions are incomplete, with some variables (e.g., R{vw}, r{vw}) not explained and initializations (e.g., \hat{R}^0, \hat{r}^0) missing. The authors should explicitly identify which variables correspond to the auxiliary linear process in Algorithm 3.

The paper omits relevant literature on (sub)gradient and decentralized optimization, such as Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis (1989), Srinivasan et al. (2009), and Wei Shi (2015), and classical acceleration work (Polyak, Nesterov). The authors should comment on the practicality of their method and when to use it versus other distributed consensus methods.

Key questions for the authors include: What are the limitations of Min-Sum Splitting, and what is the intuition behind using the auxiliary process? Can the framework be extended beyond quadratic objectives to more general consensus problems common in machine learning? Why is the approach of interest to the machine learning community?

The paper's main contributions are the connection of Min-Sum schemes with lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step methods in convex optimization, the proof of how the directionality embedded in Belief Propagation protocols can be tuned and exploited to accelerate the convergence rate, and the analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting. The new proof technique based on the introduction of an auxiliary process is also a significant contribution.

The paper is well-organized and provides enough information for the expert reader to reproduce its results. However, the authors could improve the clarity of the manuscript by addressing the aforementioned issues.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques. The use of an auxiliary linear process to track the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm is a new proof technique that establishes improved convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting. The connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods are also new.

The results are significant, as they establish a square-root improvement for the asymptotic convergence time over diffusive methods. The paper's findings motivate further studies to generalize the acceleration due to splittings to other problem instances, beyond consensus.

Overall, this is a theoretically interesting work that establishes convergence rates and links to known methods. However, the paper lacks practical discussion, intuition, and clearer algorithmic exposition.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a new proof technique that establishes improved convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting.
* The connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods are novel and significant.
* The results are theoretically interesting and establish a square-root improvement for the asymptotic convergence time over diffusive methods.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The manuscript contains several typos and grammatical errors throughout.
* The algorithm descriptions are incomplete, and some variables are not explained.
* The paper omits relevant literature on (sub)gradient and decentralized optimization.
* The authors do not provide practical discussion, intuition, or clearer algorithmic exposition.
