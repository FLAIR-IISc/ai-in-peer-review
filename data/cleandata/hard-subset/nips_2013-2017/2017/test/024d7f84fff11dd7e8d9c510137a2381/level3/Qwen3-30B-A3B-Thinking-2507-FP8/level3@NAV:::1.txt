

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum message-passing to distributed consensus problems, introducing Min-Sum Splitting as an accelerated variant that achieves subdiffusive convergence rates. The authors establish a novel connection between Min-Sum algorithms and lifted Markov chain techniques, demonstrating that proper tuning of splitting parameters yields convergence rates matching shift-register methods. The paper is well-positioned within the literature, clearly differentiating itself from previous work on Min-Sum for consensus (e.g., [27]) while connecting to two independent lines of research: lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods in convex optimization.

The paper excels in several dimensions. Its theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured, establishing convergence rates that constitute a qualitative advance over previous work. The authors demonstrate how Min-Sum Splitting achieves O(D log(D/ε)) convergence time for cycle graphs and tori, representing a square-root improvement over diffusive methods. This represents a significant contribution to distributed optimization, as it provides the first analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting and establishes a theoretical foundation for its accelerated behavior.

The main weakness concerns clarity on page 4, where the wording "splitting each term φ_vw into Γ_vw terms, and each φ_v into δ terms" is ambiguous since δ and Γ_vw were introduced as real numbers (not integers). This could confuse readers familiar with the standard interpretation of splitting as integer partitioning. While the rest of the paper is exceptionally well-written, this specific wording should be clarified to avoid misinterpretation.

In terms of evaluation criteria:
- Quality: Strong technical contribution with well-supported theoretical claims.
- Clarity: Generally excellent, with one specific ambiguity to address.
- Originality: The connection between Min-Sum and lifted Markov chains is genuinely novel.
- Significance: The accelerated convergence rates have clear practical implications for distributed systems, particularly for networks with geometric structure.

The paper makes a compelling case for Min-Sum Splitting as a competitive approach to consensus problems, with theoretical results that advance the state of the art. The minor clarity issue on page 4 is easily fixable, and the paper's significant theoretical contribution warrants acceptance. I recommend acceptance with the authors addressing the ambiguous wording.
