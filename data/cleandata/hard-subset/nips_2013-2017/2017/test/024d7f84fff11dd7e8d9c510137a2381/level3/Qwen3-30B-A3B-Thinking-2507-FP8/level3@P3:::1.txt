

**Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"**

This paper introduces an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for solving the distributed consensus problem. The authors demonstrate that while ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge for graphs with cycles, a modified version called Min-Sum Splitting converges to the problem solution with subdiffusive rates. The key contribution is establishing that proper tuning of parameters (δ and Γ) enables Min-Sum Splitting to achieve convergence rates matching shift-register methods, which represent state-of-the-art accelerated approaches for consensus.

The paper situates its contribution well within the existing literature on distributed optimization. It builds upon [27], which investigated Min-Sum for consensus but only established convergence for specific graph topologies with diffusive rates. The authors connect their work to two main lines of research: lifted Markov chain techniques [37] and accelerated first-order methods in convex optimization [13]. The paper's primary theoretical contribution is establishing Min-Sum Splitting as a novel bridge between these two approaches.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly situates its contribution in the state of the art. The authors provide a careful explanation of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm and its application to the consensus problem. Their main theoretical advance is a new proof technique based on an auxiliary linear process that tracks the algorithm's evolution on the nodes of the graph rather than the directed edges, allowing them to establish convergence rates by analyzing the spectral properties of a matrix.

Strengths:
- The paper establishes a novel connection between Min-Sum algorithms and lifted Markov chain techniques, which is a significant theoretical contribution.
- The convergence rate analysis is rigorous and provides a clear qualitative advance over previous work.
- The paper is well-structured with clear motivation and careful technical presentation.
- The results achieve convergence times scaling as O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, matching the best known rates for accelerated methods.

Weaknesses:
- The presentation on page 4 is ambiguous regarding "splitting each term φ_vw into Γ_vw terms, and each φ_v into δ terms" since Γ_vw and δ were introduced as real numbers, not integers. This creates confusion about the interpretation of the algorithm.
- The paper doesn't fully explore the sensitivity of convergence rates to parameter choices beyond the optimal setting in Theorem 1.
- The connection to practical implementation in real distributed systems is not thoroughly discussed.

Arguments for acceptance:
- The paper makes the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, addressing a significant gap in the literature.
- The novel connection between Min-Sum algorithms and lifted Markov chain techniques is theoretically important.
- The results provide a clear qualitative advance over previous work on Min-Sum for consensus.
- The paper is well-written and makes a strong case for its significance.

Arguments against acceptance:
- The ambiguity on page 4 could confuse readers about the algorithm's implementation.
- The paper doesn't address how the global information required for optimal parameter tuning (e.g., ρ_W) would be obtained in a distributed setting.
- The practical implications could be strengthened with more discussion of implementation considerations.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that makes a meaningful advance in distributed optimization. The novel connection between Min-Sum algorithms and lifted Markov chain techniques is particularly significant, revealing deeper theoretical relationships between seemingly disparate approaches to accelerating consensus. The results provide a clear path to achieving better convergence rates without requiring the construction of lifted graphs, which is a notable advantage over existing methods.

The paper meets all criteria for acceptance at NIPS. The technical content is sound, the presentation is clear (with one minor ambiguity), the contribution is original, and the significance is substantial for the field. The minor issue with the presentation on page 4 is easily fixable and does not detract from the paper's overall quality. The authors successfully demonstrate that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve the same accelerated rates as shift-register methods while providing a new theoretical perspective on the acceleration mechanism. This work opens important new directions for research on accelerating distributed algorithms and should be accepted for publication at NIPS 2017.
