

# Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting" for NIPS 2017

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum message-passing with splitting to solve the consensus problem in distributed optimization. The authors claim that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve subdiffusive convergence rates, matching those of shift-register methods, and establish connections to lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization. The paper focuses on the network averaging problem (a specific instance of consensus) and analyzes the convergence behavior of Min-Sum Splitting when applied to this problem.

The paper builds on prior work in distributed optimization and consensus algorithms. It references [27] which showed that ordinary Min-Sum does not converge for graphs with cycles, while [34] introduced the Splitting modification. The authors claim to establish the first convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, and to connect this approach to two established lines of research: lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods. However, the paper fails to properly contextualize its contributions within the broader literature on distributed consensus, missing several key references and failing to adequately distinguish their work from existing approaches.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper provides a clear theoretical analysis of Min-Sum Splitting for the consensus problem, establishing a connection to auxiliary linear processes.
2. The technical content is generally sound and the mathematical derivations are correct.
3. The paper identifies an interesting connection between Min-Sum message-passing and accelerated methods.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Originality:** The main contribution (connecting Min-Sum Splitting to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods) appears to be either well-known or trivial. The paper misses crucial citations to Olshevsky's "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs" (which establishes optimal rates for many graph classes), Shi et al.'s EXTRA algorithm, and Franca and Bento's work on distributed ADMM. The connection to Heavy Ball/Nesterov/Polyak methods is also not properly acknowledged as a known relationship.
   
2. **Significance:** The claimed improvement of O(D log(D/ε)) over the O(D log(1/ε)) rate from Olshevsky's work is marginal at best. For graphs where D is proportional to n (like complete graphs, where D=1, or cycle graphs, where D~n), the claimed rate is actually worse than the established rates. The paper's claim about "complete graphs" being a special case is misleading since complete graphs converge in one step (not requiring the rate analysis provided).

3. **Quality:** The paper lacks sufficient comparison to state-of-the-art algorithms. It fails to report the actual convergence rates of competing methods for the same graph classes. The claim that Min-Sum Splitting "gets close to the optimal rate" is unsubstantiated as the paper doesn't compare to the optimal rate established in the literature.

4. **Clarity:** The paper contains several confusing statements. The phrase "directed edges on a simple graph" (line 243) is contradictory, as simple graphs don't have directionality. There's a typo in line 220 where "Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian." The "auxiliary message-passing" scheme appears to be a standard reparameterization that mirrors Nesterov acceleration, making the contribution unclear.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper presents a theoretically sound analysis of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus.
- The connection between Min-Sum and accelerated methods is interesting, though not novel.
- The paper establishes a new proof technique using an auxiliary process.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The claimed improvement is marginal and not properly contextualized within the existing literature.
- The paper misses several key references that would have strengthened the comparison to existing work.
- The significance of the contribution is overstated given the marginal improvement in convergence rates.
- The connection to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods appears to be a well-known relationship rather than a novel insight.

**Overall assessment:** The paper makes a marginal theoretical contribution to the literature on distributed consensus algorithms. While the technical content is sound, the paper fails to properly contextualize its contribution within the broader literature, misses key references, and overstates the significance of its results. The claimed improvement in convergence rates is not compelling, particularly since the paper fails to demonstrate how the proposed method compares to the best existing approaches. The connection to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods appears to be well-known rather than novel, and the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art algorithms.

The paper is borderline in terms of quality and significance, but the lack of proper contextualization and the overstated claims make it difficult to recommend for acceptance. The contribution is not sufficiently novel or impactful to warrant publication at NIPS given the current state of the field. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to properly compare with existing methods, clarify the novelty of their approach, and more carefully frame the significance of their results.
