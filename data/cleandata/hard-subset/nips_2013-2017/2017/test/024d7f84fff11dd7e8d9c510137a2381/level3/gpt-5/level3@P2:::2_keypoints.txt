- Paper studies Min-Sum with Splitting for average consensus; shows ordinary Min-Sum can diverge on loopy graphs while Min-Sum Splitting converges for quadratics.  
- With a particular splitting parameter, algorithm attains accelerated, subdiffusive rates matching shift-register/Chebyshev-like methods.  
- Key technical tool: an auxiliary linear two-state node process tracks edge-message dynamics, enabling clean spectral analysis.  
- Achieved iteration complexity translates to O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori, improving over diffusive methods.  
- Authors draw parallels between Splitting-induced directionality/memory and lifted non-reversible Markov chains and multi-step methods (Heavy Ball/Nesterov).  
- Theoretical derivation of edge- and node-based updates and convergence proof are technically sound; spectral analysis follows standard Chebyshev arguments.  
- Some results (e.g., Proposition 3) are trivial/supplemental and not novel.  
- Claims need tightening: “dimension-independent” behavior and “good graphs” are vague and can be misleading (complete graphs converge in one step).  
- The O(D log(D/ε)) rate is only a logarithmic improvement over O(D log(1/ε)) and can be worse than Olshevsky’s linear-time bounds in worst-case (D ≍ n).  
- Paper lacks explicit, apples-to-apples comparisons with key prior work (Olshevsky; ADMM/EXTRA; fast gradient methods; Scaman et al.; França–Bento).  
- Clarity issues: ambiguous phrasing “directed edges on a simple graph” (should state orientation of undirected edges).  
- Typo: “Laplacian” on line 220 should be “Lagrangian.”  
- Need to specify which W and spectral quantities are required to tune γ and how to obtain them in practice.  
- Define “good graphs” precisely when claiming dimension-independent convergence.  
- Originality is incremental: novelty is framing acceleration via Min-Sum Splitting and proving rates within that framework, but connections to lifting and multi-step acceleration are largely known.  
- Practical significance limited by matching existing Chebyshev/shift-register rates, an extra log D factor vs best bounds, and need for global spectral information to tune γ.  
- Pros: clean linearization/auxiliary process, rigorous rate matching shift-register methods, and useful discussion linking BP/Splitting to lifting and multi-step methods.  
- Cons: incremental vs known accelerated consensus techniques, weak/missing comparisons to prior art, need for global spectral info, and some misleading statements/minor typos.  
- Requested changes: add and compare to listed references (Olshevsky; Shi et al.; EXTRA; Jakovetić–Xavier–Moura; Scaman et al.; França–Bento), clarify complete-graph case and “good graphs,” relate K matrix to Chebyshev/shift-register and acknowledge Heavy Ball/Nesterov history, discuss decentralized/robust selection of γ, fix phrasing/typos, and add numerical comparisons vs Metropolis, Chebyshev, Olshevsky, ADMM, EXTRA.  
- After rebuttal the authors fixed several clarity points but reviewer’s overall evaluation remains unchanged (contribution still incremental).  
- Recommendation: Borderline reject.  
- Reviewer confidence: High.
