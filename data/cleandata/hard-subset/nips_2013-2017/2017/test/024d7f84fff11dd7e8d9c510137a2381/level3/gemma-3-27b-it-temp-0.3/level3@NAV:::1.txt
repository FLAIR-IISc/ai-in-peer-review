## Review of “Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting”

This paper investigates the application of a modified Min-Sum message-passing algorithm – specifically, Min-Sum Splitting – to the distributed consensus problem. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that, unlike the standard Min-Sum algorithm, this splitting variant *does* converge and, crucially, achieves subdiffusive convergence rates comparable to those obtained by more sophisticated shift-register methods. The authors establish a novel connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chain techniques and accelerated first-order methods in convex optimization, providing a broader theoretical understanding of this approach.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly situates its contribution within the existing literature on distributed consensus and message-passing algorithms. The authors thoroughly review relevant work, including classical diffusive methods, accelerated approaches based on lifted Markov chains, and multi-step optimization techniques. This contextualization is invaluable for understanding the novelty of their results. The theoretical analysis appears sound, and the convergence rates established seem to represent a qualitative advance, though I lack the deep expertise to fully verify the technical details. The connection drawn between Min-Sum Splitting and existing accelerated methods is particularly insightful, offering a unifying perspective on these seemingly disparate approaches.

However, the presentation on page 4 regarding the “splitting” process is somewhat unclear. The wording “splitting each term φ_vw into Γ_vw terms, and each φ_v into δ terms” is ambiguous, as δ and Γ_vw were previously introduced as real numbers, not as indicators of the number of terms. Clarifying this point with a more precise explanation would significantly improve readability. While the paper is generally well-organized, the jump between introducing the auxiliary process in Section 6 and presenting the convergence theorem in Section 7 feels a bit abrupt; a more gradual transition would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of Min-Sum Splitting to consensus and the establishment of its accelerated convergence rates are novel contributions.
*   **Significance:** The results offer a potentially more efficient approach to distributed consensus, a fundamental problem in various fields.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis supporting its claims.
*   **Clarity (Overall):** The paper is generally very well-written and clearly explains the core ideas.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity (Page 4):** The explanation of the splitting process requires clarification.
*   **Transition (Sections 6 & 7):** The transition between the auxiliary process and the convergence theorem could be smoother.



**Overall:** I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field, with a clear presentation and strong theoretical foundation. Addressing the minor clarity issues would further enhance its quality.
