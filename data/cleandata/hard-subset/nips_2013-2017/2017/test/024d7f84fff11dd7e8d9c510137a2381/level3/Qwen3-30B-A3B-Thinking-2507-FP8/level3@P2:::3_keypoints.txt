- Proposes "Min-Sum Splitting," a reparametrized Min-Sum message-passing variant that converges where ordinary Min-Sum fails for consensus.
- Establishes theoretical convergence rates (subdiffusive O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles and tori) matching shift-register methods.
- Connects Min-Sum Splitting to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization, bridging previously separate literatures.
- Introduces a novel proof technique using an auxiliary linear process to track Min-Sum Splitting dynamics.
- Proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear technically sound and well-supported.
- Demonstrates that tuning directionality in Belief Propagation can yield accelerated consensus performance.
- Lacks experimental validation to complement and illustrate theoretical claims.
- Algorithm descriptions are incomplete: variables like R̂vw and r̂vw are unexplained and parameter initializations are missing.
- The connection between Algorithm 3’s auxiliary process and Min-Sum Splitting needs clearer explanation and intuition.
- Contains several typos and grammatical errors that hinder clarity (e.g., "theirs neighbors," "double-stochastic").
- Does not sufficiently discuss practical relevance or scenarios where this method outperforms ADMM/gradient methods.
- Missing citations to key distributed optimization literature (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989, Srinivasan et al. 2009, Wei Shi 2015).
- Significant originality: first analysis of Min-Sum Splitting convergence rates and first formal link to lifted chains and multi-step methods.
- Theoretical significance is high, but practical impact for machine learning applications is not clearly established.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical analysis, new proof technique, and matching best-known consensus rates.
- Arguments against acceptance: incomplete algorithmic details, lack of experiments, missing references, and clarity issues.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on improving clarity, completing algorithm descriptions, adding experimental validation, and addressing missing citations.
