This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum splitting to the consensus problem, aiming to achieve accelerated convergence rates. The core idea is to leverage a modified Min-Sum algorithm that, unlike the standard version, provably converges and exhibits subdiffusive behavior. The authors establish a convergence rate of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles and tori, framing their work within the context of both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods. While the paper is technically sound and well-written, the claimed improvements are modest, and significant related work is missing.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical analysis appears rigorous, culminating in Theorem 1 which formally establishes the convergence rate. The connection to shift-register methods is a notable result.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written, making the core ideas accessible. The motivation and positioning within the existing literature are reasonably well-explained.
*   **Novelty (Incremental):** The application of Min-Sum splitting to consensus and the detailed convergence analysis represent an incremental advance. The connection to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods provides a useful unifying perspective.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Improvement:** Despite the technical work, the reported rate of O(D log(D/ε)) does not constitute a substantial improvement over existing methods, particularly Olshevsky’s linear-time average consensus algorithm which achieves O(D log(1/ε)). In the worst case (D ~ n), the proposed rate is even worse.
*   **Misleading Claim about Complete Graphs:** The statement regarding complete graphs is inaccurate. Complete graphs converge in a single step; the authors need to clarify for which graph classes they demonstrate dimension-independent complexity and rigorously define what constitutes a “good” graph.
*   **Missing Citations:** The paper conspicuously omits crucial references. Olshevsky’s "Linear Time Average Consensus…" (2017) is a direct comparison point and must be discussed, as its convergence rate is competitive or better. Furthermore, key works on consensus optimization, such as Shi et al. on ADMM linear convergence and Scaman et al. on EXTRA, are missing. The paper also fails to acknowledge related work on Markov chain lifting and distributed ADMM by Franca and Bento (2017).
*   **Omitted Context:** The last paragraph of page 5 states a well-known fact about diffusion random walks and should cite Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew (1998) and acknowledge the established connection to Heavy Ball/Nesterov/Polyak acceleration techniques.
*   **Proposition 3 (Trivial):** Proposition 3 appears to be a trivial observation and adds little substantive value to the paper.
*   **Unclear Contribution of Auxiliary Message Passing:** While the auxiliary message-passing scheme is presented, its novelty is questionable. The analysis ultimately reduces to a linear system, suggesting the “acceleration” mirrors Nesterov acceleration.
*   **Spectral Gap Handling:** The gains are likely due to effective handling of the spectral gap. However, the worst-case bound remains O(n) because O(n) = O(D) for all n-node graphs.
*   **Technical Errors:** Line 243 (p.6) mentions "directed edges on a simple graph," which is confusing and should be clarified. Line 220 (p.6) has a typo: "Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian."

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound analysis of Min-Sum splitting for consensus.
*   The connection to shift-register methods and the unification with lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods are valuable insights.
*   The paper addresses a relevant problem in distributed optimization.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   The improvement over existing methods is modest.
*   Significant related work is missing, weakening the claim of novelty and providing an incomplete picture of the field.
*   The misleading statement about complete graphs and minor technical errors detract from the overall quality.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a solid, but not exceptional, contribution. While the technical analysis is well-executed and the connections made are insightful, the limited practical improvement, omission of critical related work, and minor inaccuracies prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. I believe the paper would benefit significantly from addressing the aforementioned weaknesses.

**Score:** 5/10 (Reject with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the identified concerns).
