## Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates accelerated convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors build upon the reparameterization technique from Ruozzi & Tatikonda (2013) and introduce an auxiliary linear process to analyze the algorithm’s convergence on quadratic consensus problems. The core contribution lies in establishing a novel proof technique via this auxiliary process, demonstrating improved convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, and drawing connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods.

The paper is generally well-written and the core ideas are clearly presented. The authors successfully establish a link between Min-Sum Splitting and known acceleration techniques in other fields, positioning their work within a broader context of distributed optimization. The theoretical analysis, particularly the proof sketches provided, appears sound, and the correctness of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 is plausible.  The connection to shift-register methods and the resulting convergence rate improvement are particularly noteworthy. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While conceptually strong, the practical implications remain underexplored. The authors should more clearly articulate *when* this method would be preferable to existing distributed consensus approaches, particularly those with established implementations. A discussion on computational complexity and scalability would be beneficial.  The motivation for using the auxiliary process is adequately explained, but a more intuitive explanation of its role in simplifying the analysis would strengthen the presentation. 

The algorithmic descriptions (Algorithm 3 specifically) are incomplete; several variables (Rvw, rvw) are introduced without clear definitions, and initializations (R^0, r^0) are missing, hindering reproducibility. The connection between variables in Algorithm 3 and the auxiliary process needs explicit clarification. Furthermore, the manuscript contains numerous minor typos and grammatical errors (e.g., “theirs neighbors,” “double-stochastic,” “casted”).  A careful proofread is essential. There’s a potential misuse of "Laplacian" versus "Lagrangian" on lines 220-221. 

Finally, the paper omits relevant literature on (sub)gradient and decentralized optimization (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989, Srinivasan et al. 2009, Wei Shi 2015) and classical acceleration work (Polyak, Nesterov). The authors should also address the limitations of Min-Sum Splitting and whether this framework could be extended beyond quadratic objectives to more general consensus problems common in machine learning, explicitly stating its relevance to the machine learning community.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical analysis leading to improved convergence rates.
*   Interesting connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization.
*   Clear presentation of core ideas.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of practical discussion and implementation details.
*   Algorithmic descriptions are incomplete and lack clarity.
*   Omission of relevant related work.
*   Numerous minor errors in writing.



**Overall:** The paper presents theoretically interesting work with a strong potential for advancing our understanding of distributed consensus optimization. However, the lack of practical considerations, clarity in the algorithmic presentation, and comprehensive literature review necessitate revisions before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field. I recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit.
