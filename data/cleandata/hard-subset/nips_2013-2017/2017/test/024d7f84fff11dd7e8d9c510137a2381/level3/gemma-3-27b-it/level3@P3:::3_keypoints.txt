- Core contribution: novel proof technique for accelerated Min-Sum Splitting, improved convergence rates, and connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step acceleration methods.  
- Writing clarity: core ideas are presented clearly, but the manuscript contains numerous typos and grammatical errors that harm readability.  
- Technical soundness: proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear correct and rigorous.  
- Terminology errors: authors sometimes conflate "Laplacian" with "Lagrangian" and "Laplace multipliers" with "Lagrange multipliers", needing correction.  
- Incomplete algorithm descriptions: Algorithm 1/2 introduce variables (R_{vw}, r_{vw}) without sufficient explanation and omit initializations for \hat{R}^0 and \hat{r}^0, hurting reproducibility.  
- Missing linkage: the relationship between Algorithm 3 variables and the auxiliary linear process is not explicitly stated, making the analysis hard to follow.  
- Lack of contextualization: relevant decentralized optimization literature (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989, Shi 2015) and foundational acceleration work (Polyak, Nesterov) are omitted.  
- Comparative discussion missing: paper lacks comparison of Min-Sum Splitting to established methods like ADMM and gradient-based decentralized algorithms.  
- Practical implications absent: no discussion of implementation complexity, parameter sensitivity, scalability, or when to prefer Min-Sum Splitting in practice.  
- Scope limitation: unclear whether the framework extends beyond quadratic objectives to non-smooth or general consensus problems common in ML.  
- ML relevance unclear: authors state ML applications but do not explain how theoretical results translate to concrete ML improvements or which tasks benefit.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty of analysis, theoretical rigor, and potential significance for large-scale distributed ML.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient practical discussion, incomplete algorithmic exposition, missing related work, and need for substantial polishing.  
- Required revisions: fix terminology and typos, complete algorithmic descriptions and initializations, explicitly connect algorithms to the auxiliary process, and add related-work and practical-comparison sections.  
- Questions for authors: practical implementation advice and comparisons (when to choose Min-Sum vs ADMM/gradients), limitations and graph/problem structures causing poor performance, extension to non-quadratic/non-smooth objectives, and explicit ML use-cases.  
- Recommendation: accept with major revisions contingent on addressing reproducibility, contextualization, practical discussion, and manuscript polish.
