This paper investigates accelerated convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem, a fundamental primitive in distributed optimization. The authors build upon the reparameterization introduced by Ruozzi & Tatikonda (2013) and provide a theoretical analysis focusing on quadratic consensus problems. A key technical contribution is the introduction of an auxiliary linear process used to track the algorithm's evolution, enabling the establishment of convergence guarantees. The paper’s strength lies in demonstrating connections between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step methods from convex optimization.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the core ideas. However, numerous minor typos and grammatical errors throughout detract from the overall polish (e.g., "theirs neighbors" should be "their neighbors," "double-stochastic" should be "doubly-stochastic," "casted" should be "cast"). While the proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appear logically sound, the density of notation makes detailed verification difficult for a reader not intimately familiar with the field.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work demonstrating a theoretical result. The authors carefully evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of their approach within the context of existing literature. The analysis appears rigorous and the proofs are presented in a logical manner. However, the lack of practical consideration weakens the overall impact.

**Clarity:** The clarity is hampered by several issues. Algorithm descriptions are incomplete; for example, variables like R{vw}, r{vw} are not fully defined, and initializations for parameters like  \hat{R}^0 and \hat{r}^0 are missing. The connection between variables in Algorithm 2 and their counterparts in the auxiliary process described in Algorithm 3 is not explicitly stated, making it difficult to follow the correspondence. Furthermore, the introduction assumes a high level of prior knowledge, and a more intuitive explanation of the auxiliary process would be beneficial.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel combination of existing techniques, specifically applying a reparameterization approach (Ruozzi & Tatikonda, 2013) to the Min-Sum algorithm and then analyzing its convergence properties in the context of distributed consensus. The connection drawn between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods is insightful. However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly in the areas of (sub)gradient methods and decentralized optimization (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis, 1989; Srinivasan et al., 2009; Wei Shi, 2015) and classical acceleration techniques like those developed by Polyak and Nesterov.

**Significance:** The results are potentially significant, as they establish convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting and highlight its relationship to well-established acceleration techniques. However, the lack of discussion regarding the practical implications of these results limits the broader impact. It is unclear when this method would be preferred over existing distributed consensus algorithms. The paper would benefit from a discussion of computational costs and potential scalability issues. The connection to machine learning could be emphasized more clearly; as it stands, the relevance to the broader NIPS audience is somewhat limited.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of Min-Sum Splitting, establishing convergence rates.
*   Establishes a novel connection between Min-Sum Splitting and other acceleration techniques in different domains.
*   The auxiliary process provides a useful tool for analyzing the algorithm's behavior.
*   The mathematical treatment is generally sound and the proofs appear correct.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lacks a discussion of practical considerations and limitations.
*   The clarity is hampered by incomplete algorithmic descriptions and a high level of assumed prior knowledge.
*   The paper omits relevant literature in decentralized optimization and acceleration methods.
*   The connection to machine learning applications is not clearly articulated.
*   Numerous minor typos and grammatical errors detract from the overall presentation.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you comment on the practical considerations and potential computational overhead of implementing Min-Sum Splitting compared to other distributed consensus methods? Under what conditions would this method be advantageous?
2.  Could you provide more intuition behind the use of the auxiliary process? What motivated this approach, and what specific insights does it provide that would be difficult to obtain otherwise?
3.  Can the framework presented in this paper be extended beyond quadratic objective functions to address more general consensus problems commonly encountered in machine learning?
4.  What are the limitations of Min-Sum Splitting, and are there specific graph topologies or problem settings where it is expected to perform poorly?
5.  Could you elaborate on why these results are particularly relevant to the machine learning community?



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution, establishing convergence rates and connections to known methods in the field of distributed consensus. However, the lack of practical discussion, intuitive explanation, and clearer algorithmic exposition weakens its overall impact. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit, addressing the concerns outlined above. The authors should focus on improving the clarity of the presentation, expanding the related work section, and providing a more compelling argument for the practical relevance of their findings.
