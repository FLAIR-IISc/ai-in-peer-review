**Summary of the Paper**

The paper "Accelerated Consensus via Min-Sum Splitting" presents a modified version of the Min-Sum algorithm, called Min-Sum Splitting, to solve the consensus problem in distributed optimization. The authors show that the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm does not converge, but the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm yields convergence to the problem solution with subdiffusive accelerated convergence rates, matching the rates obtained by shift-register methods. The paper also establishes a connection between Min-Sum Splitting and lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first order methods in convex optimization.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. **Technical soundness**: The paper presents a thorough analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, including its convergence properties and acceleration mechanisms.
2. **Originality**: The paper introduces a new algorithm, Min-Sum Splitting, which is a modification of the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm.
3. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments.
4. **Significance**: The paper addresses a significant problem in distributed optimization, namely, the consensus problem, and presents a new solution with improved convergence rates.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. **Limited improvement**: The claimed improvement in convergence rates is not impressive, and the authors should clarify which graph classes yield dimension-independent complexity.
2. **Missing citations**: The paper lacks citations and comparisons to relevant works, such as Olshevsky's "Linear Time Average Consensus..." and Shi et al.'s work on ADMM linear convergence.
3. **Omitted related work**: The paper omits important references on Markov chain lifting and distributed ADMM by Franca and Bento.
4. **Trivial contribution**: Proposition 3 appears to be a trivial contribution, and its significance is unclear.
5. **Unclear contribution**: The proposed "auxiliary message-passing" algorithm may mirror Nesterov acceleration on min-sum, and its contribution is unclear since consensus analysis reduces to a linear system.
