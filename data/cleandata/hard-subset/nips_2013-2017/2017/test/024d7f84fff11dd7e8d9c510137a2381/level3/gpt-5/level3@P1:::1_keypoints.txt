- Studies distributed averaging via Min-Sum Splitting and shows proper splitting yields convergent, accelerated consensus where ordinary Min-Sum fails on loopy graphs.
- Main technical contributions: (i) linear, edge-based message update specialized to quadratic consensus objectives.
- Main technical contributions: (ii) an auxiliary node-based two-step linear process that exactly tracks the algorithm’s output.
- Main technical contributions: (iii) a splitting parameter choice that recovers shift-register (Chebyshev/second-order) accelerated rates.
- Shows improvement of diffusive O(D^2) dependence to O(D) (up to logs) on geometric graphs (cycles, tori) via the shift-register rate.
- Connects Min-Sum Splitting to lifted, non-reversible Markov chains and to multi-step first-order/momentum acceleration.
- Derives exact equivalence between edge-based Algorithm 2 and node-based Algorithm 3 (Proposition 2), enabling clean spectral analysis.
- Theorem 1: with δ=1 and Γ=γW (γ=2/(1+√(1−ρW^2))), convergence factor ρK < ρW gives shift-register asymptotics and iteration complexity O((1/(1−ρK)) log((1/(1−ρK))/ε)).
- Analysis requires global spectral information (ρW) to tune γ, paralleling other accelerated methods and lifted-chain constructions.
- Technical development is sound, aligns with known shift-register analyses, and includes explicit constants/assumptions (symmetric W, exact γ).
- Clarity issues: ambiguous wording about "splitting" (integer vs. real parameters) needs clarification or explicit continuity argument.
- Clarity issues: Algorithm 2 output formula appears to miss division by effective coefficient—should be x_t,v = r_t,v / R_t,v after mapping.
- Presentation issues: some equation/label misalignments and heavy notation; a short notation table for Γ, A, W, δ, and K matrices is recommended.
- Originality: high — first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus and first explicit bridge between BP-splitting, lifting, and acceleration.
- Significance: conceptually valuable for BP and distributed optimization communities, with near-optimal rates on geometric graphs.
- Practical limitations: requires global spectral info, assumes synchronous updates and symmetric/undirected W, and is limited to quadratic objectives.
- Practical limitations: no empirical results to demonstrate constants, robustness to γ mis-specification, or message complexity compared to Metropolis/Chebyshev/Nesterov/consensus propagation.
- Suggested improvements: discuss distributed estimation or robust/adaptive γ choices (e.g., Chebyshev schedules), and add modest experiments on cycles/grids to illustrate performance.
- Overall recommendation: leaning accept — theoretical advances and conceptual unification outweigh practical gaps; adding clarity fixes and experiments would strengthen the paper.
