- Introduces Min-Sum Splitting, a modified Min-Sum message-passing algorithm that achieves accelerated convergence for consensus problems.  
- Shows ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge on graphs with cycles, while Min-Sum Splitting converges with subdiffusive rates matching shift-register methods.  
- Main theoretical contribution: a novel auxiliary linear-process proof technique to track Min-Sum Splitting and establish convergence rates for any graph topology.  
- Establishes the first proof of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, with correct proofs for Propositions 1, 2, and Theorem 1.  
- Draws meaningful connections between Min-Sum Splitting and two acceleration paradigms: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order optimization methods.  
- Technical analysis is sound and the auxiliary process construction is a clear pathway to the results.  
- Paper’s scope is narrow, focusing only on quadratic consensus problems, limiting perceived significance to the ML community.  
- Algorithm descriptions are incomplete: key variables (e.g., R̂vw, r̂vw) and initializations are not properly explained.  
- The relationship between Algorithm 3’s auxiliary process and the original Min-Sum Splitting algorithm is not clearly articulated.  
- Manuscript contains numerous typos and grammatical errors that undermine professionalism.  
- Omits relevant literature on decentralized optimization and classical acceleration (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989, Srinivasan et al. 2009, Shi 2015, Polyak, Nesterov), making novelty assessment harder.  
- Lacks practical discussion: no guidance on when to prefer Min-Sum Splitting, sensitivity to parameter choices, computational overhead, or empirical comparisons.  
- Recommendations: improve exposition of algorithms, clarify auxiliary/original algorithm relationship, include broader literature context, and add practical experiments/comparisons.  
- Overall assessment: theoretically interesting and novel contribution to message-passing analysis, but limited impact due to clarity, contextual, and practical deficiencies.
