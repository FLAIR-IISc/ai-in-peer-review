This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors demonstrate that, under appropriate parameter tuning, Min-Sum Splitting achieves accelerated convergence, specifically a rate of O(D log(D/ε)), where D is the graph diameter and ε is the desired accuracy. The work establishes a connection between Min-Sum schemes, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The mathematical derivations appear sound, and the organization facilitates understanding of the proposed approach. The authors appropriately situate their work within the broader context of distributed optimization and consensus algorithms. However, while technically sound, the novelty and significance of the results are somewhat limited by existing work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The analysis appears rigorous, and the proof techniques seem appropriate for the problem at hand. The connection established between Min-Sum Splitting and established methods like shift-registers is insightful.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, making the core ideas accessible.
*   **Connection to Related Work:** The authors attempt to connect their work to existing literature on lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, demonstrating awareness of the field.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Improvement:** The claimed improvement in convergence rate (O(D log(D/ε))) offers a relatively modest gain over existing algorithms, particularly Olshevsky’s “Linear Time Average Consensus…” which achieves a rate of O(D log(1/ε)). In the worst case, where D ~ n, the proposed rate is even less favorable. This lack of substantial improvement diminishes the practical significance of the result.
*   **Incomplete Literature Review:** The paper overlooks several crucial references in the consensus optimization literature. Specifically, the authors should cite Olshevsky's work, as well as more recent advances such as Shi et al. on ADMM linear convergence and Scaman et al. on accelerated gradient methods. Additionally, the related work on Markov chain lifting and distributed ADMM by França and Bento is missing.
*   **Misleading Claim:** The statement regarding complete graphs (p.5) is misleading. Complete graphs converge in a single step, rendering the discussion of their complexity irrelevant. The authors should clarify which graph classes benefit from the proposed approach and properly define what constitutes a “good” graph in this context.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** Proposition 3 appears trivial and adds little to the overall contribution of the paper.
*   **Oversight in Connections:** The statement on p.5 regarding a “long-known fact” (related to acceleration methods) lacks proper attribution. Citations to Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew, as well as acknowledging connections to Heavy Ball/Nesterov/Polyak methods, are necessary.
*   **Minor Technical Issues:** There are minor typos (e.g., “Laplacian” should be “Lagrangian” on p.6) and phrasing inconsistencies (e.g., discussing directed edges on a simple graph on p.6).
*   **Auxiliary Process Insight:** While the auxiliary message-passing scheme is presented, its potential mirroring of Nesterov acceleration on min-sum is not fully explored, limiting its insightful value. The convergence analysis ultimately reducing to a linear system further diminishes the novelty of this auxiliary approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.
*   The connection established to existing accelerated methods is interesting.
*   The paper contributes to the understanding of message-passing algorithms in distributed optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The improvement in convergence rate is modest and may not justify publication in a highly competitive venue like NeurIPS.
*   The literature review is incomplete, overlooking several important contributions in the field.
*   The presented work feels incremental, lacking a truly novel and significant contribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid, but ultimately incremental, contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The technical analysis is sound, and the connections to existing work are relevant. However, the limited improvement in convergence rate, coupled with the incomplete literature review, makes it difficult to justify a strong recommendation for acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject. The paper, while not incorrect, does not present a compelling enough advancement to warrant publication at NeurIPS. Addressing the missing literature and clarifying the practical implications of the results might strengthen the work for future consideration.
