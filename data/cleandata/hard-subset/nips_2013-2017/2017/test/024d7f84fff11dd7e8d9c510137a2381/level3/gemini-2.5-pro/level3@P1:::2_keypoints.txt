1. Studies Min-Sum Splitting for average consensus and shows the Splitting variant converges on loopy graphs with appropriate parameter choices.  
2. Main technical claim: a specific choice of splitting parameters yields an "accelerated" (subdiffusive) convergence rate, analyzed via an auxiliary node-based message-passing scheme tracking the edge-based algorithm.  
3. Authors link Splitting to lifted Markov chains and multi-step/momentum first-order methods, interpreting splitting parameters as introducing momentum/directionality.  
4. Strength: novel conceptual bridge between Min-Sum Splitting (graphical models) and accelerated distributed optimization / lifted chains.  
5. Strength: the auxiliary node-space message-passing formulation is a useful technical tool that simplifies convergence analysis.  
6. Major omission: fails to cite/compare to Olshevsky (2017), which gives O(D log(1/ε)) convergence and appears strictly better than the paper’s O(D log(D/ε)) bound.  
7. Major omission: does not adequately cover related accelerated consensus and distributed optimization work (EXTRA, Jakovetić et al., Scaman et al., linear-convergence ADMM results, etc.).  
8. Also misses prior connection work between message-passing ADMM and lifted Markov chains (França & Bento, 2016).  
9. The claimed convergence rate O(D log(D/ε)) is marginal and can be worse than existing results for many graph topologies.  
10. The assertion that the method converts diffusive to subdiffusive behavior is already well-known for multi-step/momentum methods (Chebyshev, Polyak/Nesterov; earlier literature like Rabbat et al. 2005).  
11. The auxiliary message-passing scheme’s novelty is questionable since the quadratic consensus case reduces to spectral analysis and likely re-derives standard acceleration techniques.  
12. Paper’s claim about "dimension-independent" convergence on expanders is misleading and needs precise qualification of the graph classes involved.  
13. Proposition 2 appears to be a straightforward change of variables (edge-to-node mapping) and its prominence is overstated.  
14. Worst-case complexity remains O(n) because D can be O(n); acceleration benefits are topology-dependent rather than improving the worst-case.  
15. Overall: interesting conceptual framing but novelty and performance claims are undermined by insufficient literature engagement and only marginal (or worse) theoretical improvements.
