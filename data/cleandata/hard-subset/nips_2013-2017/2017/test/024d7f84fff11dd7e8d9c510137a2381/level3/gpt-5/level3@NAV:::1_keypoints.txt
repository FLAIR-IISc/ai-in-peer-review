- Applies Min-Sum Splitting to distributed consensus and provides a rigorous convergence-rate analysis.  
- Shows that with δ=1 and Γ=γW (γ chosen from W’s spectrum) the method converges and attains square-root accelerated rates matching shift-register methods.  
- Analysis casts edge-based dynamics as a linear update and introduces an auxiliary node-based two-step linear system for tractable spectral analysis.  
- Identifies a conceptual bridge: splitting induces directionality like nonreversible lifting and a memory term like momentum.  
- Delivers topology-agnostic rates and improves diameter dependence from Θ(D^2 log(1/ε)) to O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles/tori.  
- Theoretical development is careful and technically sound; Theorem 1 is precise and leverages known spectral results.  
- Assumptions (symmetric W, synchronous updates, quadratic objectives) are explicit; need to clarify initialization/positivity necessity.  
- Reliance on global spectral information (ρ(W)) for tuning limits practical deployability.  
- Presentation issue: phrase suggesting integer “splitting” is ambiguous since δ and Γ_vw are real-valued — clarify interpretation.  
- Originality: strong—first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and novel min-sum–lifting/first-order connection.  
- Significance: high—qualitative advance for distributed averaging with potential impact on broader convex programs.  
- Pros: clear, rigorous analysis with accelerated rates; novel conceptual connection; elegant, reusable auxiliary formulation.  
- Cons: requires global tuning info; no empirical validation or robustness studies; restricted to quadratic, synchronous setting.  
- Suggestions: add experiments for constants and robustness; discuss distributed/online estimation and sensitivity of ρ(W)/γ; clarify splitting wording; expand comparisons to ADMM/Nesterov/heavy-ball and per-iteration cost.
