- Paper analyzes Min-Sum message passing for equality-constrained consensus and shows classical Min-Sum diverges on loopy graphs while Min-Sum Splitting converges and can be accelerated.  
- Core technical contribution: reparameterization to an auxiliary node-level linear process matching a two-step shift-register iteration that yields acceleration.  
- For quadratic consensus, Min-Sum Splitting messages remain quadratic and node dynamics reduce to [r^t; q^t] = K(δ, Γ)[r^{t−1}; q^{t−1}] with outputs x^t_v = r^t_v / R^t_v.  
- With δ=1 and Γ=γW and γ tuned by the spectral gap, the node operator equals the shift-register K, giving spectral radius ρ_K < ρ_W and square-root improvement in convergence time.  
- Theoretical rates match optimal second-order linear methods, achieving near-optimal O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori versus Θ(D^2 log(1/ε)) for reversible consensus.  
- Conceptual links are drawn between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted nonreversible Markov chains, and multi-step first-order acceleration methods.  
- Strength: first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus with near-optimal acceleration on geometric graphs.  
- Strength: elegant auxiliary-process proof that makes spectral analysis tractable and connects to known accelerated iterations.  
- Strength: unifies separate literatures (Min-Sum, lifted chains, multi-step methods) and sharpens prior analyses limited to special graphs.  
- Weakness: practical guidance limited — tuning requires global spectral information (ρ(W)), raising distributed estimation and robustness concerns.  
- Weakness: analysis restricted to quadratic objectives; extensions to general convex or constrained problems are only qualitative.  
- Weakness: algorithmic exposition needs clearer variable definitions, initializations, and mapping between edge- and node-based variables.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of per-iteration communication, computation, and memory compared to other accelerated or ADMM-like methods.  
- Weakness: no experiments to validate constants, robustness, or empirical performance on representative graphs.  
- Quality: technical development appears sound and consistent with known second-order analyses; assumptions (e.g., positivity of denominators) should be stated more transparently.  
- Clarity: generally well-written but marred by typos, some terminology slips, and would benefit from a concise variable-mapping/table and earlier highlight of the shift-register connection.  
- Originality: high within Min-Sum literature — first rates for Splitting and a novel auxiliary-process proof, despite matching known optimal linear rates.  
- Significance: makes Min-Sum Splitting a credible accelerated-consensus method and could inform extensions if generalized beyond quadratics.  
- Requests: compare practicality versus Chebyshev/shift-register, heavy-ball, Nesterov, and ADMM; discuss per-iteration costs, memory, and sensitivity to γ.  
- Requests: provide distributed/robust methods to estimate ρ(W) or γ and analyze sensitivity to misspecification.  
- Requests: clarify potential extensions to strongly convex smooth φ_v, composite objectives, constraints, asynchrony, and time-varying graphs.  
- Requests: add experiments on cycles, grids, random geometric graphs, and expanders versus Metropolis, Chebyshev, heavy-ball, Nesterov, EXTRA/Exact Diffusion, and ADMM.  
- Missing citations: classical decentralized optimization (Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis; Tsitsiklis et al.), accelerated methods (Polyak, Nesterov, Chebyshev), EXTRA/Exact Diffusion, Scaman et al., and ADMM/lifting works (Wei & Ozdaglar; Shi et al.; França & Bento).  
- Noted typos and wording fixes needed (examples: “theirs neighbors”→“their neighbors”, “double-stochastic”→“doubly-stochastic”, “casted”→“cast”, “Laplace multipliers”→“Lagrange multipliers”).  
- Pros for acceptance: first rigorous rate analysis, unifying insights, and clean auxiliary-process proof.  
- Cons for acceptance: lack of experiments, dependence on global spectral knowledge, and exposition/initialization gaps.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on improved clarity, practical discussion (ideally experiments), and corrections.
