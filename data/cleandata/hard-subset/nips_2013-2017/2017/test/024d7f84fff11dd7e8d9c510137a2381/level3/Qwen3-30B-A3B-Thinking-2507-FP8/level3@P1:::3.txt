

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum message-passing to solve the consensus problem in distributed optimization, introducing a modified version called Min-Sum Splitting that achieves accelerated convergence rates. The authors establish that while ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge on graphs with cycles, Min-Sum Splitting converges to the correct solution with subdiffusive rates matching shift-register methods. The key theoretical contribution is a novel proof technique using an auxiliary linear process to track the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, which allows the authors to establish convergence rates for any graph topology. The paper makes an important connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two distinct lines of accelerated consensus methods: lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first-order optimization methods. This bridges a theoretical gap between message-passing algorithms and established acceleration techniques.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with correct proofs for Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1. The theoretical analysis is sound, with the auxiliary process construction providing a clear pathway to establish convergence rates. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the theoretical understanding of message-passing algorithms by establishing the first proof of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting. The authors properly contextualize their work within the literature, noting the prior work on Min-Sum for consensus by [27] and the limitations of their approach.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues that hinder understanding. Algorithm descriptions are incomplete, with key variables (e.g., R̂vw, r̂vw) not properly explained and initializations missing. The connection between the auxiliary process in Algorithm 3 and the original Min-Sum Splitting algorithm is not clearly articulated, requiring readers to carefully cross-reference multiple sections. The manuscript contains numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "theirs neighbors" instead of "their neighbors," "double-stochastic" instead of "doubly-stochastic") that undermine professionalism.

The paper's significance is somewhat limited by its narrow focus on quadratic consensus problems. While the theoretical connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods are interesting, the authors fail to adequately address why this work matters to the machine learning community. The paper omits relevant literature on decentralized optimization (Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989, Srinivasan et al. 2009, Shi 2015) and classical acceleration techniques (Polyak, Nesterov), making it difficult to assess the novelty of the connections claimed. The paper also lacks practical discussion: it doesn't address when to prefer Min-Sum Splitting over existing methods, how sensitive the algorithm is to parameter choices, or the computational overhead of implementing the auxiliary process.

The paper makes several important theoretical contributions that advance our understanding of message-passing algorithms. The proof technique using an auxiliary linear process is novel and could be useful for analyzing other message-passing protocols. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods is theoretically interesting, though the authors don't sufficiently explain why these connections matter for practical distributed optimization.

Major weaknesses include the incomplete algorithm descriptions, grammatical errors, and lack of practical context. The paper would benefit significantly from a more detailed discussion of when Min-Sum Splitting would be preferable to existing methods, along with empirical comparisons. The authors should also clarify the relationship between their auxiliary process and the original Min-Sum Splitting algorithm in Algorithm 3.

The paper's main strengths are its theoretical novelty (the auxiliary process proof technique), the correct proofs of convergence rates, and the insightful connections to two different lines of accelerated consensus methods. The main weaknesses are the poor clarity of the algorithm descriptions, the lack of practical discussion, and the omission of relevant literature.

The paper would be stronger with clearer exposition of the algorithms, better contextualization within the broader literature on distributed optimization, and a more explicit discussion of practical applications. While the theoretical contribution is sound and novel, the lack of practical relevance and clarity limits its significance for the NIPS community.

Pro: Novel proof technique via auxiliary linear process; Correct theoretical analysis; Meaningful connections to two different acceleration approaches.

Con: Poor algorithm clarity and missing variable explanations; Numerous grammatical errors; Lack of practical discussion and comparison with existing methods; Omission of relevant literature on decentralized optimization; Insufficient explanation of relevance to machine learning community.

Overall, this is a theoretically interesting paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the understanding of message-passing algorithms. However, the lack of clarity in algorithm description, the omission of practical considerations, and the failure to adequately contextualize the work within the broader distributed optimization literature significantly limit its impact. The paper would benefit from substantial revisions to improve clarity and provide better justification for its relevance to the machine learning community.
