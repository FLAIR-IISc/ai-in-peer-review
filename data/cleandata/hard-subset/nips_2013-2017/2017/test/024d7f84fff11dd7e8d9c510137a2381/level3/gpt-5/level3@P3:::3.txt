Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies consensus optimization via a modified Min-Sum (a.k.a. Gaussian BP in the quadratic case) using the reparameterization/splitting framework of Ruozzi & Tatikonda (2013). The authors focus on the quadratic consensus/averaging problem and introduce an auxiliary linear process on nodes that tracks the Min-Sum Splitting dynamics on directed edges. With a specific choice of parameters (δ = 1 and Γ = γW, where W is a symmetric averaging matrix and γ depends on the spectral gap of W), they establish subdiffusive convergence rates matching those of shift-register (Chebyshev-type) multi-step methods. They also articulate connections to lifted Markov chains (directionality/non-reversibility via edge messages) and to multi-step first-order acceleration (memory/momentum via the auxiliary process).

This work relates to several strands: (i) Min-Sum and its reparameterizations [Ruozzi & Tatikonda 2013, 2012], (ii) consensus and accelerated gossip (e.g., Boyd et al., Xiao & Boyd, Cao-Spielman-Yeh, Liu et al.), (iii) lifted Markov chains (Chen-Lovász-Pak; Diaconis-Holmes-Neal; Jung-Shah-Shin), and (iv) multi-step/accelerated methods (Polyak heavy-ball, Nesterov, Chebyshev/shift-register). The new proof technique via an auxiliary node-level process is novel and technically neat, and the achieved rates align with best-known subdiffusive rates on graphs with geometry.

Evaluation
- Quality: The main results (Propositions 1–2 and Theorem 1) appear correct; the linearization for quadratics, the equivalence to a node-level 2n-dimensional linear system, and the spectral analysis that recovers shift-register rates are sound. The theoretical development is self-contained for the quadratic setting. However, the algorithmic descriptions contain missing definitions/initializations and a few likely typos in formulas (details below), which hinder reproducibility.
- Clarity: Generally well-written with clear positioning and intuition for acceleration via directionality and memory. That said, there are numerous minor typos/wording issues, and key algorithm variables are insufficiently defined. The mapping between edge-level messages and the auxiliary node-level variables should be made explicit in the main text.
- Originality: While the achieved rates match existing accelerated consensus methods, the route—accelerating Min-Sum via splitting and analyzing it through an auxiliary process—is original. The conceptual bridges to lifted chains and multi-step optimization are valuable.
- Significance: The results strengthen the theoretical understanding of Min-Sum Splitting, provide rates, and connect communities (BP, Markov chain lifting, convex optimization). Practical impact would be increased by discussing when/why to deploy this method versus established decentralized optimization algorithms and by broadening beyond quadratics.

Strengths
- Clean, correct analysis yielding accelerated rates; novel auxiliary-process proof technique.
- Insightful unification: directionality on edges ↔ non-reversibility; memory on nodes ↔ multi-step acceleration.
- Square-root improvement over diffusive rates on graphs with geometry; rates commensurate with shift-register methods.

Weaknesses
- Practicality is under-discussed: parameter selection requires global spectral information; no empirical validation or robustness analysis (mis-specified γ, asynchronous updates, quantization/noise).
- Algorithmic exposition has gaps: missing definitions and initializations; unclear mapping of variables between Algorithms 2 and 3; an apparent typo in the output formula of Algorithm 2.
- Literature coverage could better credit classical and decentralized optimization foundations.
- Scope limited to quadratic consensus; extensions to broader ML-relevant objectives are not discussed.

Specific comments and requests
- Practicality and when to use: Please discuss the computational/communication complexity per iteration versus standard gossip, Chebyshev-accelerated consensus, EXTRA, accelerated gradient tracking, and distributed ADMM. When would Min-Sum Splitting be preferable (e.g., architectural constraints favoring edge-supported state, ease of implementation on message-passing systems)? How sensitive is performance to γ mis-specification, and can γ be estimated online (e.g., via power iteration or robust Chebyshev schedules)?
- Intuition and limitations: Provide more intuition for why the auxiliary process captures the effect of splitting and how its “memory” term relates to heavy-ball/shift-register. Clarify limitations of Min-Sum Splitting (need for symmetric W, requirement that 1 + δ∑ Γwv R̂t_wv > 0, synchronous updates, static graphs) and how severe they are in practice.
- Beyond quadratics: Can the framework extend to general smooth/strongly convex objectives (e.g., regularized empirical risk) or composite problems via proximal/min-sum on separable convex terms? Any prospects for non-quadratic potentials, constraints beyond equality, or stochastic gradients?
- Relevance to ML: State more clearly why ML practitioners should care: e.g., accelerated averaging as a building block for decentralized SGD/dual averaging, connections to gradient tracking, or potential gains in communication-limited federated settings.

Algorithmic clarity and correctness
- Algorithm 2: Define R̂s_vw and r̂s_vw explicitly and motivate their roles. Provide explicit initializations (R̂0, r̂0) in the main text (not only in propositions). The output line appears malformed; likely it should read xt_v = (bv + δ∑_{w∈N(v)} Γ_wv r̂t_wv)/(1 + δ∑_{w∈N(v)} Γ_wv R̂t_wv). Please confirm and correct.
- Algorithm 3: Explicitly identify which variables constitute the auxiliary process tracking Min-Sum (e.g., r and q track numerators, R and Q denominators; how they correspond to beliefs µt_v). Summarize the initialization mapping from Proposition 2 inline.
- Clarify the positivity condition 1 + δ∑ Γwv R̂t_wv > 0 and demonstrate it holds for the recommended parameters and initializations.

Related work to add or better position
- Foundational decentralized optimization: Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis (1989), Tsitsiklis-Bertsekas-Athans (1986), Sundhar Ram–Nedić–Veeravalli (2009).
- Modern decentralized first-order: Wei & Ozdaglar (distributed ADMM), Shi et al. (EXTRA, ADMM linear convergence), and gradient tracking/accelerated methods (e.g., Scaman et al. 2017). Cite Polyak (1964) and Nesterov (1983/2004) for original acceleration methods, beyond survey-style citations.
- Position your rates versus Chebyshev-accelerated consensus (Xiao & Boyd) and accelerated gossip (Cao-Spielman-Yeh; Liu et al.), highlighting similarities/differences in assumptions and parameter knowledge.

Minor technical comments
- Lines 220–221: “Laplacian/Laplace multipliers” likely meant “Lagrangian/Lagrange multipliers.”
- Consistency on “doubly-stochastic” versus “symmetric W with W1 = 1, 1T W = 1T.” Be precise about assumptions needed in Theorem 1 (symmetry, stochasticity).
- State explicitly that δ = 1 in the main theorem (despite general δ framework); comment on whether δ ≠ 1 offers advantages.

Pros and cons for acceptance
- Pros: Novel proof technique via auxiliary process; clear conceptual connections across fields; correct and meaningful accelerated rates; advances understanding of Min-Sum Splitting.
- Cons: Limited scope (quadratic consensus only), incomplete algorithmic specification, thin discussion of practicality and comparisons, missing citations, and numerous minor writing issues.

Overall
A theoretically interesting contribution that tightens the link between Min-Sum, lifting, and multi-step optimization and establishes accelerated rates. With improved exposition, broader related work, and clearer guidance on practical deployment, this would be a solid NIPS paper.
