Summary and relation to prior work: This paper applies Min-Sum Splitting—an accelerated variant of min-sum message passing—to the distributed consensus (network averaging) problem. The core contribution is a rigorous convergence-rate analysis: ordinary min-sum can diverge on loopy graphs, but with splitting and tuned parameters (δ = 1, Γ = γW with γ chosen from the spectrum of W), the method converges and attains subdiffusive, square-root accelerated rates matching those of shift-register methods. The analysis proceeds by (i) casting the edge-based dynamics as a linear update and (ii) introducing an auxiliary, node-based two-step linear system whose spectral properties are tractable. The work situates itself relative to classical diffusive gossip, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step first-order methods, clarifying a novel conceptual bridge: splitting induces directionality akin to nonreversible lifting and a memory term akin to momentum. Compared to prior min-sum work on consensus (e.g., consensus propagation and analyses restricted to d-regular graphs), this paper provides topology-agnostic rates and improves the dependence on diameter from Θ(D^2 log(1/ε)) to O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles/tori.

Quality: The theoretical development appears careful and technically sound; Theorem 1 is precise and leverages known spectral results for shift-register methods. Assumptions (symmetric W, synchronous updates, quadratic objectives) are clearly stated, though a brief discussion of the initialization/positivity condition and its necessity would help. The reliance on global spectral information (ρ(W)) for tuning is acknowledged but limits deployability.

Clarity: The paper is well written and well positioned in the state of the art. One presentation issue: on p.4 the phrase “splitting each term φ_vw into Γ_vw terms, and each φ_v into δ terms” is ambiguous because δ and Γ_vw were introduced as real numbers; please clarify that the integer splitting is only an interpretation, while the algorithm operates for real-valued parameters.

Originality: Strong. Establishing the min-sum–lifting/first-order connection and providing the first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting are novel and insightful.

Significance: High. The results constitute a qualitative advance for distributed averaging and may influence designs for broader convex programs.

Pros:
- Clear, rigorous analysis with accelerated rates across topologies.
- Novel conceptual connection to lifted chains and multi-step methods.
- Auxiliary node-based formulation is elegant and potentially reusable.

Cons:
- Tuning requires global spectral information; practical distributed estimation is not addressed.
- No empirical validation or robustness studies (parameter misspecification, asynchrony, noise).
- Restricted to quadratic, synchronous setting.

Suggestions: Add experiments to validate constants and robustness; discuss online/distributed estimation of ρ(W) and sensitivity to γ; clarify the splitting interpretation; and expand comparisons to ADMM-based and Nesterov/heavy-ball methods in practice and per-iteration cost.
