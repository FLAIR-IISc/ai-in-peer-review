This paper investigates the application of the Min-Sum Splitting message-passing algorithm to the distributed consensus problem, specifically for quadratic objective functions (network averaging). The authors demonstrate that while the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge on graphs with cycles, the "Splitting" variant, a reparameterization introduced by Ruozzi & Tatikonda (2013), can be tuned to guarantee convergence. The main contribution is a theoretical analysis of this algorithm's convergence rate. By introducing an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting updates on the graph's nodes, the authors prove that a proper choice of splitting parameters yields an accelerated, subdiffusive convergence rate. This rate matches that of shift-register methods, a known multi-step acceleration technique in convex optimization. A key insight of the paper is the connection it establishes between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chain techniques, and accelerated first-order optimization methods, arguing that the "splitting" parameters introduce a form of momentum or directionality that speeds up convergence.

**Strengths (Arguments Pro Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper provides the first formal convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. Showing that it can achieve accelerated rates (a square-root improvement over standard diffusive methods) is a significant theoretical contribution.
2.  **Novel Connections:** The work draws a compelling and novel connection between three previously distinct areas: message-passing algorithms (Belief Propagation/Min-Sum), accelerated optimization (multi-step/momentum methods), and accelerated random walks (lifted Markov chains). This conceptual bridge is valuable and could inspire further research at the intersection of these fields.
3.  **Technical Soundness:** The core technical tool—the introduction of an auxiliary message-passing scheme on the nodes to analyze the primary algorithm running on the edges—is clever and effective. The proofs for the propositions and the main theorem appear to be correct and build upon established spectral analysis of shift-register methods.

**Weaknesses (Arguments Con Acceptance)**

1.  **Limited Scope and Practicality:** The analysis is restricted to the network averaging problem (quadratic objectives). While this is a standard benchmark, the paper does not discuss how or if this framework could be extended to more general convex functions, which are common in machine learning (e.g., for distributed logistic regression or SVMs). Without this, the practical significance for the NIPS community is somewhat limited. The paper would be much stronger if it discussed the challenges or possibilities of such an extension.
2.  **Clarity and Exposition:** While generally well-written, the manuscript suffers from a lack of clarity in its algorithmic descriptions and could benefit from more intuition. The descriptions of Algorithm 1 and 2 are incomplete, as some variables are used without clear definition or initialization (e.g., how to set the initial messages `µ̂0vw`). Furthermore, the motivation for the auxiliary process in Algorithm 3 is purely analytical; providing more intuition on *why* this reparameterization works and what it represents would greatly improve the paper's accessibility.
3.  **Incomplete Literature Review:** The paper positions itself relative to accelerated consensus literature but omits several foundational works. There is no mention of the classical acceleration methods by Polyak (Heavy-ball) or Nesterov, which are the conceptual parents of the multi-step methods discussed. The review of distributed optimization literature could also be strengthened by referencing seminal works like Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis (1989) on parallel and distributed computation, and more recent relevant work on decentralized optimization.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

The paper is theoretically interesting, but its presentation and positioning could be significantly improved. I encourage the authors to address the following points in a revised version.

*   **Typos and Minor Errors:** The manuscript needs a careful proofreading pass. Examples include:
    *   "each of which depends of a subset" -> "on a subset"
    *   "of each others" -> "of each other"
    *   "doubly-stochastic" is the standard term, not "double-stochastic"
    *   In the supplementary material (not provided, but mentioned), lines 220-221, "Laplacian/Laplace multipliers" seems to be a typo for "Lagrangian/Lagrange multipliers".

*   **Clarity of Algorithms:**
    *   Please provide explicit guidance on the initialization of `R̂0` and `r̂0` in Algorithm 2. Theorem 1 assumes they are zero, but is this a general requirement?
    *   In Algorithm 3, it would be clearer to state explicitly that `rs`, `qs`, `Rs`, `Qs` are vectors in `RV`, representing the state at each node.

*   **Questions:**
    1.  **Practicality:** Given the wealth of existing methods for distributed consensus (e.g., ADMM, accelerated dual ascent), what are the practical scenarios where Min-Sum Splitting would be the preferred method? Does it offer advantages in terms of communication overhead, robustness, or ease of implementation?
    2.  **Generalization:** How does this analysis extend to non-quadratic objective functions? The message updates in Min-Sum become minimization problems that may not have closed-form solutions. Does the linear auxiliary process still hold in some approximate sense, or is a completely new analytical tool needed? Addressing this is critical for assessing the work's impact on machine learning.
    3.  **Intuition:** Could you provide more intuition for the auxiliary process? Why is it possible to exactly track a process on `2m` edge variables with a process on `2n` node variables? Does this reflect a deeper structural property of the consensus problem?

In summary, this paper presents a solid and novel theoretical result connecting Min-Sum Splitting to accelerated optimization. The core technical contribution and the conceptual links it establishes are valuable. However, its weaknesses in terms of practical discussion, limited scope to quadratic objectives, and clarity of presentation currently detract from its overall impact. I would be inclined to raise my score if the authors can convincingly address the potential for generalization and better articulate the practical relevance of their proposed method.
