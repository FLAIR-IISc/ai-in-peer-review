- Proposes an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for distributed consensus optimization with convergence rates for quadratic consensus problems.
- Uses reparameterization (Ruozzi & Tatikonda 2013) and introduces an auxiliary linear process to track Min-Sum evolution for convergence analysis.
- Main contributions: a novel proof technique via the auxiliary process and improved convergence rates matching shift-register methods.
- Establishes connections between Min-Sum message-passing, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step convex optimization methods.
- Theoretical results are sound with correct proofs for Propositions 1, 2, and Theorem 1; core technical contribution is significant.
- Paper’s novelty lies in linking Min-Sum to two established acceleration paradigms, offering valuable theoretical insight.
- Manuscript contains distracting typos and grammatical errors that reduce readability.
- Algorithm descriptions are incomplete: variables R{vw}, r{vw} are unexplained, initializations for \hat{R}^0 and \hat{r}^0 are missing, and Algorithm 3 does not clearly map auxiliary-process variables.
- Omits important related work in decentralized optimization and classical acceleration (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989; Shi 2015; Polyak; Nesterov), weakening contextualization.
- Lacks practical discussion: no guidance on when to prefer this method over established approaches, limited intuition for the auxiliary process, and unclear limitations of Min-Sum Splitting.
- Needs clearer discussion of practical utility and better contextualization within distributed optimization and machine learning applications.
