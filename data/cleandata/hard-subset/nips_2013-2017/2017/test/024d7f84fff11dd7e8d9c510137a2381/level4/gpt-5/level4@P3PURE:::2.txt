This paper studies the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method for the average consensus problem. In general, the paper reads well, but the improvement over existing results does not appear compelling. Detailed comments follow.

1) The paper states: “This rate is optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph. In this case the convergence time, i.e., the number of iterations required to reach a prescribed level of error accuracy in the… of the dimension of the problem, as…”. For complete graphs, the linear convergence factor is zero because all nodes reach the average in one step; moreover, complete graphs are too special to be representative. Please clarify:
- For which general families of graphs does the complexity not depend on the dimension (number of nodes)?
- Which general categories of graphs are considered “good” in your sense?

2) In the same context, the literature review should include Olshevsky’s “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control.” Its convergence rate should be reported accurately (see also comment 8). That reference achieves a competitive, and in some cases better, bound than the submission.

3) At the top of page 2, for consensus optimization, the following important references should be cited:
- Shi, Ling, Kun, Wu, and Yin: “On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization.”
- Scaman, Bach, Bubeck, Lee, and Massoulié: “Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks.”
The paper should also summarize the state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization and their (linear) convergence rates.

4) When discussing lifted graphs and Markov chains, the paper overlooks a closely related work: Franca and Bento, “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM.”

5) The content of the last paragraph of page 5 is a long-known fact. Please cite Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew: “Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links.” The subsequent connection to Heavy-ball/Nesterov/Polyak acceleration is also well known to the field.

6) Additional important references on consensus optimization that are missing:
- Shi, Ling, Wu, and Yin: “EXTRA: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization.”
- Jakovetic, Xavier, and Moura: “Fast distributed gradient methods.”

7) Proposition 3 appears trivial and is a supplementary, rather than substantive, contribution.

8) The rate achieved in this paper, D log(D/ε), does not appear to be a significant improvement over the D log(1/ε) rate achieved in “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control.” In particular, in the worst case over all graphs, where D ≍ n, your bound is even worse than what that paper attains.

9) The “Linear Time Average Consensus…” result improves the bound via Nesterov’s acceleration. I suspect the proposed “auxiliary message-passing scheme” is again Nesterov acceleration applied to the min-sum algorithm. This is perfectly reasonable, but since the analysis for average consensus reduces to a linear system, it is not especially difficult. In this light, the core contribution of the paper is not clear.

10) The small improvement may stem from a more careful treatment of the spectral gap. Nevertheless, the worst-case bound remains O(n), since O(n) = O(D) over the class of all n-node graphs.

11) Line 243, page 6: the graph is simple, but directed edges are used. This is confusing—please clarify the modeling choice and its implications.

12) Typo at line 220, page 6: “Laplacian” should be “Lagrangian.”
