This paper investigates the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method applied to the average consensus problem. While generally well-written, the presented improvement over existing results appears limited. Specific comments are detailed below.

(1) The authors state, “This rate is optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph… the convergence time… depends on the dimension of the problem.” For complete graphs, convergence is achieved in a single step, resulting in a linear rate of 0. Furthermore, complete graphs represent a highly specific case and are not representative of broader network topologies. The paper should clarify for which general classes of graphs the complexity is independent of the problem dimension (number of nodes), and explicitly define what constitutes a “good” graph in this context.

(2) The literature review in this section should include the work “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control” by Olshevsky. The authors should accurately report its convergence rate (further details in comment 8). The reported bounds in that work are competitive with, and potentially superior to, the results presented in this submission.

(3) At the top of page 2, when discussing consensus optimization, key references such as “On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization” by Shi, Ling, Kun, Wu, and Yin, and “Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks” by Scaman, Bach, Bubeck, Lee, and Massoulié, should be cited. The authors should also present the current state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization and their corresponding linear convergence rates.

(4) When discussing lifted graphs and Markov chains, the paper neglects the closely related work “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM” by Franca and Bento.

(5) The content of the final paragraph of page 5 represents a well-established result. Reference should be made to “Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links” by Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew. The connection between these variants and Heavy ball/Nesterov/Polyak methods is also widely known within the field.

(6) The authors have omitted several important references relevant to consensus optimization. Examples include “Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization” by Shi, Ling, Wu, and Yin, and “Fast distributed gradient methods” by Jakovetic, Moura, and Xavier.

(7) Proposition 3 appears to be a trivial contribution and may be more suitable for supplementary material.

(8) The convergence rate achieved by this paper, D log(D/eps), does not demonstrate a substantial improvement over the D log(1/eps) rate attained by “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs…” (see comment 2). In the worst-case scenario, where D ~ n (number of nodes), the proposed bound is even weaker.

(9) “Linear Time Average Consensus…” leverages Nesterov’s acceleration to achieve its results. The reviewer suspects that the authors’ proposed “Auxiliary message-passing scheme” is effectively a re-application of Nesterov’s acceleration to the min-sum algorithm. While this is acceptable, the analysis concerns consensus, which reduces to analyzing a linear system and is not expected to be particularly challenging. Therefore, the paper's contribution is unclear.

(10) Any improvement in performance is likely attributable to a careful analysis of the spectral gap of the graph. However, the worst-case bound remains O(n), as O(n) = O(D) for the set of all graphs with n nodes.

(11) Line 243 of page 6 depicts a simple graph but uses directed edges, which is confusing.

(12) Typo at line 220 of page 6: Laplacian should be Lagrangian.
