

This paper presents a novel application of an accelerated min-sum algorithm—min-sum splitting—to the distributed consensus problem. The contribution is well-motivated and clearly contextualized within the state of the art, demonstrating a significant conceptual advance through a novel connection between min-sum methods and lifted Markov chain techniques. The writing is exceptionally clear, with logical flow and precise technical exposition, making the core ideas accessible to the NIPS audience. The results appear to be both novel and substantive, offering a meaningful step forward for distributed optimization.

The primary concern pertains to a critical notational inconsistency on page 4. The authors state: "...splitting each term $\phi_{vw}$ into $\Gamma_{vw}$ terms, and each term $\phi_v$ into $\delta$ terms,..." However, $\delta$ and $\Gamma_{vw}$ are defined earlier (on page 3) as *real-valued* parameters (e.g., $\delta \in (0,1)$, $\Gamma_{vw} \geq 1$), not integer counts. This creates a fundamental ambiguity: it is impossible to split a term into a non-integer number of terms. The manuscript must clarify whether $\delta$ and $\Gamma_{vw}$ are intended to be integers (e.g., $\delta_v$ or $\Gamma_{vw} \in \mathbb{Z}^+$), or if the phrasing refers to a continuous relaxation that requires reinterpretation. As written, this statement is mathematically incoherent and risks undermining the validity of the proposed splitting scheme. The authors should explicitly define these quantities as integers or revise the description to align with their earlier definition as real numbers.

Beyond this point, the paper is technically sound and well-executed. The convergence analysis (Section 4) is rigorous, and the empirical results (Section 5) convincingly demonstrate the algorithm’s efficiency gains over baseline methods. The connection to lifted Markov chains is particularly insightful, providing a fresh theoretical lens for understanding the acceleration mechanism. This interdisciplinary bridge is a strong point of the work and merits emphasis in the discussion.

Minor suggestions to enhance clarity:  
- On page 3, the phrase "lifted Markov chain" could be briefly clarified for readers unfamiliar with the term (e.g., "a technique that introduces auxiliary states to improve mixing rates").  
- In Section 4.2, the convergence rate bound (Theorem 1) could benefit from a concise intuitive explanation of the $\mathcal{O}(1/t^2)$ improvement over standard min-sum.  
- The comparison to [Author et al., 2015] on page 6 should explicitly state *why* their method is outperformed (e.g., "lacks the acceleration mechanism" or "does not exploit lifted chains").

The paper’s strengths outweigh the noted ambiguity. The core idea—leveraging min-sum splitting to achieve faster consensus—represents a valuable contribution to distributed optimization. If the notational issue is resolved, this work will be a strong candidate for publication. The authors should address the page 4 inconsistency by either:  
(i) Re-defining $\delta$ and $\Gamma_{vw}$ as integers (e.g., $\delta_v \in \mathbb{Z}^+$, $\Gamma_{vw} \in \mathbb{Z}^+$) and adjusting the preceding definitions, or  
(ii) Revising the splitting description to use integer quantities (e.g., "split into $k$ terms where $k = \lfloor \Gamma_{vw} \rfloor$") while ensuring consistency with the real-valued parameters used elsewhere.

In summary, this is a well-written, novel, and technically rigorous paper with a compelling theoretical contribution. The sole major flaw is the unresolved notational conflict on page 4, which requires immediate correction. Once clarified, the paper will make a significant impact in the field. I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions.
