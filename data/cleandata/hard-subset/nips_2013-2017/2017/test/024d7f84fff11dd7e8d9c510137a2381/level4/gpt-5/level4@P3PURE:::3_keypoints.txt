- Introduces an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting protocol for distributed consensus and proves convergence rates for quadratic objectives using an auxiliary linear process.
- Main contributions: novel auxiliary-process-based analysis, a Min-Sum consensus protocol with improved convergence, and conceptual links to lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization.
- Paper is generally clear and well written but contains several typos and grammatical errors that need correction.
- Proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear correct.
- Major presentation limitation: insufficient practical guidance on when and how to use the method in distributed settings.
- Lacks intuition for the auxiliary linear process and why it yields acceleration.
- Limited discussion of relevance and applicability beyond quadratic objectives common in machine learning.
- Algorithmic clarity issues: several algorithm variables are undefined or uninitialized (e.g., R{vw}, r{vw}, \hat{R}^0, \hat{r}^0) and the auxiliary process should be explicitly mapped to Min-Sum variables.
- Missing citations for decentralized/subgradient methods and acceleration (suggested: Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989; Ram, Nedić & Veeravalli 2009; Shi et al. 2015; Polyak; Nesterov; and original literature references).
- Requests for clarification: explain practicality vs. alternatives (EXTRA, gradient tracking, accelerated gossip), communication/computation trade-offs, robustness to noise, memory and sync assumptions.
- Requests to spell out limitations: sensitivity to graph topology, parameter tuning, initialization, scalability, handling asynchrony or time-varying graphs.
- Ask for more intuition on how the auxiliary process relates to momentum, lifting, or spectral-gap improvements and whether the framework can extend beyond quadratics or via approximations.
- Overall summary: solid and technically sound theoretical contribution but needs better exposition, practical guidance, broader context/references, stronger intuition, and explicit ML motivation.
