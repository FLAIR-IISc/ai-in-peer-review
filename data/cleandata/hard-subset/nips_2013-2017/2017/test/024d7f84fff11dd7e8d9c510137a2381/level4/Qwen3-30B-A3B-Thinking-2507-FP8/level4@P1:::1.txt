

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum Splitting to the distributed consensus problem, establishing convergence rates and revealing connections to lifted Markov chain techniques and accelerated first-order methods in convex optimization. The authors build upon prior work showing that the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge in cyclic graphs while Min-Sum Splitting does, but they establish the first rigorous convergence rates for this approach on arbitrary graph topologies. This work extends the analysis in [27], which showed convergence for specific graph topologies but did not establish rates for general graphs.

The paper's primary strengths include a clear theoretical analysis that establishes convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting across all graph topologies, a novel connection between Min-Sum algorithms and two major streams of accelerated consensus algorithms, and an elegant proof technique using an auxiliary process to track the algorithm's evolution. The authors demonstrate a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods, achieving rates of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, matching the best known rates for other accelerated methods. This represents a significant theoretical advance, as the paper provides the first proof of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, which has been previously observed to yield convergence in settings where ordinary Min-Sum fails [35].

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with contributions properly contextualized within the literature. The connection to both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first-order methods is particularly insightful, offering a unified perspective on accelerated consensus algorithms. The authors effectively demonstrate that Min-Sum Splitting's acceleration mechanism resembles both approaches: it operates on a lifted space (the space of directed edges) analogous to lifted Markov chains, while the directionality introduced by splitting translates into a memory term that relates to multi-step first-order methods. This dual connection is novel and significantly advances our understanding of accelerated consensus algorithms.

However, the paper contains one significant conceptual confusion that needs clarification. In Section 2, the authors state that "minimize ∑v∈V ∑δk=1 φkv(xv) + ∑{v,w}∈E ∑Γvw k=1 φkvw(xv, xw), with φkv := φv/δ and φkvw := φvw/Γvw," and claim this corresponds to "splitting each term φvw into Γvw terms, and each term φv into δ terms." This interpretation is problematic because δ and Γvw are introduced as real numbers (not integers) in the algorithm description. The phrase "splitting into Γvw terms" is misleading when Γvw is not necessarily an integer, creating a conceptual gap that needs to be resolved in the revision.

The paper would also benefit from a more detailed comparison with the most recent accelerated consensus methods, particularly those in [13, 24] and [30, 36], which have established the optimal rate O(D log(1/ε)). While the authors note that their method approaches this rate, a clearer exposition of the precise relationship to these state-of-the-art methods would strengthen the significance claim.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- First rigorous convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, filling a critical gap in the literature.
- Novel connection between Min-Sum algorithms and two major streams of accelerated consensus algorithms (lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods).
- Demonstrates a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods, achieving rates matching the best known for other accelerated methods.
- Well-organized and clearly written theoretical contribution.

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Conceptual confusion regarding the interpretation of splitting parameters as "splitting terms" (real numbers into integer terms) needs resolution.
- Limited exploration of practical implementation considerations and communication complexity.
- Insufficient empirical comparison with state-of-the-art accelerated methods.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of accelerated consensus algorithms. The conceptual confusion regarding the splitting parameters is a significant weakness that needs to be addressed, but if resolved, the paper would represent an important advance in the field. The paper's theoretical insights are valuable for both the distributed optimization and message-passing algorithm communities, and the results are likely to inspire further research on accelerating distributed algorithms. With the clarification of the splitting parameter interpretation, this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
