- Paper proposes an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting message-passing protocol for distributed consensus optimization using Ruozzi & Tatikonda reparametrization.
- Establishes convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting on quadratic consensus objectives via an auxiliary linear process that tracks algorithm evolution.
- Main contributions: new proof technique via an auxiliary process; a Min-Sum protocol with improved convergence; connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods.
- Proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear correct.
- Paper is generally well written and easy to follow but contains several typos and grammatical mistakes throughout.
- Typos and wording errors are numerous (examples provided for specific lines) and need correction.
- Some algorithmic quantities are not defined or initialized (e.g., R{vw}, r{vw}, \hat{R}^0, \hat{r}^0), and the auxiliary process variables are not clearly indicated in Algorithm 3.
- Several relevant references on (sub)gradient and accelerated methods are missing and should be included (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989; Ram et al. 2009; Wei Shi 2015; Polyak; Nesterov).
- Review asks for clarification if "Lagrangian" and "Lagrange multipliers" were intended instead of "Laplacian" and "Laplace multipliers" in specific lines.
- Reviewer requests discussion on practicality: when to use this method versus other distributed consensus methods.
- Reviewer asks authors to state limitations of Min-Sum Splitting.
- Reviewer requests intuition for introducing the auxiliary linear process.
- Reviewer asks whether the framework can extend beyond quadratic objectives to broader ML consensus problems.
- Reviewer asks authors to justify relevance and interest of this approach for the Machine Learning community.
- Strengths summarized: novel proof technique, improved convergence protocol, and links to known methods (lifted Markov chains, multi-step methods).
- Weaknesses summarized: typos, missing references/context, limited discussion of practicality/limitations, and insufficient intuition for core ideas.
- Recommendation: accept after revisions addressing typos, missing references, algorithm clarifications, and expanded discussion on practicality, limitations, intuition, and ML relevance.
