**Summary of the Paper**

The authors present an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for solving consensus problems in distributed optimization. They use reparametrization techniques to establish rates of convergence for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. The main tool used for the analysis is the construction of an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

**Main Contributions**

The main contributions of the paper can be summarized as follows: (i) providing analysis for the Min-Sum splitting algorithm using a new proof technique based on the introduction of an auxiliary process, (ii) designing a Min-Sum protocol for consensus problems that achieves better convergence than previously established results, and (iii) showing the connection between the proposed method, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The motivation and contributions of the paper are clear. The paper is well-written and easy to follow. However, it contains several typos and grammatical mistakes. The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appear to be correct.

The paper is missing several references, including (sub)gradient methods for consensus optimization, work by Polyak (Heavy-ball), and work by Nesterov.

**Minor Comments**

* Lines 220 and 221: The authors may mean "Lagrangian" and "Lagrange multipliers" instead of "Laplacian" and "Laplace multipliers".
* The authors present three algorithms, and the quantities involved are not always explained or described. For example, what is R{vw} and r{vw} in Algorithm 2? Also, in Algorithm 2, the quantities \hat{R}^0 and \hat{r}^0 do not appear to be initialized.
* The authors should clearly state which variables correspond to the auxiliary linear process in Algorithm 3.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

* The authors should comment on the practicality of the method and when such a method should be used as opposed to other distributed methods for consensus optimization.
* The authors should discuss the limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method.
* The authors should provide intuition behind using the auxiliary process in the Min-Sum Splitting method.
* The authors should clearly state why such an approach is of interest in the context of Machine Learning and for the Machine Learning community.
* The authors should consider extending the framework to solve more general consensus problems that often arise in Machine Learning.

**Originality and Significance**

The paper presents a new proof technique based on the introduction of an auxiliary process to establish rates of convergence for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. The connection between the proposed method, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step methods in convex optimization is also novel.

**Clarity and Organization**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. However, the authors could improve the clarity and organization of the paper by providing more explanations and descriptions of the quantities involved in the algorithms.

**Quality and Technical Soundness**

The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appear to be correct. However, the authors should address the missing references and provide more explanations and descriptions of the quantities involved in the algorithms.

**Overall Assessment**

The paper is a purely theoretical paper that establishes rates of convergence using a new proof technique and shows the connections between the proposed method and well-established methods in the literature. While the ideas presented in the paper are interesting, the practicality of the method and intuition behind the results are missing, as well as some justification for the importance of this result for the Machine Learning community.
