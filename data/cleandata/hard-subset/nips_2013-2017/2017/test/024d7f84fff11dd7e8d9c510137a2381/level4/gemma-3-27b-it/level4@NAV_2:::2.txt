This paper studies the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method applied to the average consensus problem. The work presents a theoretical analysis establishing convergence and rates, relating the approach to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods. Overall, the paper is technically sound and clearly written, but the practical significance of the improvement over existing methods appears limited. Detailed comments are provided below.

(1) The statement regarding optimal rates for complete graphs is imprecise. Complete graphs achieve consensus in a single step, representing a rate of 0, and are a special case not representative of general graph structures. The paper should clarify for which broader classes of graphs the convergence complexity is independent of the number of nodes (dimension of the problem). What defines a “good” graph in this context?

(2) When discussing convergence rates, the paper should acknowledge and cite “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control” by Olshevsky et al. The achieved convergence rate should be reported and compared directly. This work presents competitive, and potentially superior, bounds to those presented in the submission.

(3) In the discussion of consensus optimization (around the top of page 2), key references such as “On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization” by Shi et al. and “Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks” by Scaman et al. are missing. The authors should provide a more comprehensive overview of state-of-the-art consensus algorithms and their corresponding linear convergence rates.

(4) When discussing lifted graphs and Markov chains, the related work “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM” by França and Bento should be cited, as it is directly relevant to the techniques discussed.

(5) The claim in the last paragraph of page 5 regarding the connection to heavy ball/Nesterov/Polyak methods is a well-known observation and should be placed in context with appropriate citations, such as “Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links” by Rabbat et al.

(6) Several important references related to consensus optimization have been omitted. Examples include “Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization” by Shi et al. and “Fast distributed gradient methods” by Jakovetić et al. A more thorough literature review is needed.

(7) Proposition 3 appears to be a trivial result and adds little to the overall contribution of the paper.

(8) The achieved rate of D log(D/ε) does not represent a substantial improvement over the rate of D log(1/ε) reported in “Linear Time Average Consensus…” (see comment 2). In the worst-case scenario where D ~ n (number of nodes), the proposed bound is even worse.

(9) The reviewer suspects that the “Auxiliary message-passing scheme” is, in fact, an application of Nesterov’s acceleration to the min-sum algorithm. While this is a valid approach, the analysis is performed for consensus, which simplifies to analyzing a linear system – a problem not expected to be overly challenging. The novelty and contribution of the paper become less clear in this context.

(10) Even with careful analysis of the spectral gap, the worst-case bound remains O(n) since O(n) = O(D) for the set of all graphs with n nodes.

(11) Line 243 of page 6: The paper refers to a "simple graph" but then discusses directed edges, which can be confusing for the reader.

(12) Typo at line 220 of page 6: “Laplacian” should be “Lagrangian”.

**Summary of arguments pro and con acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound analysis of the min-sum splitting algorithm.
*   The work clarifies the relationship between min-sum splitting and other established techniques like lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods.
*   The paper provides theoretical convergence guarantees for a specific parameter setting.

**Con:**

*   The practical improvement in convergence rate over existing methods (particularly those based on Nesterov’s acceleration) appears limited.
*   The literature review is incomplete, omitting several relevant and impactful works.
*   Some of the observations presented are well-known within the field and lack significant novelty.
