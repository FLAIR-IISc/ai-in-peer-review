

This paper presents an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for solving consensus problems in distributed optimization. The authors apply the reparametrization techniques from [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013] to analyze the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, establishing convergence rates for consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. The core contribution is the development of an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, enabling the analysis of convergence rates.

The main contributions can be summarized as follows: (i) introducing a novel proof technique based on an auxiliary process to analyze Min-Sum Splitting, (ii) designing a Min-Sum protocol for consensus that achieves superior convergence rates compared to prior work, and (iii) demonstrating connections between the proposed method and both lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization.

The paper's motivation and contributions are clearly articulated, and the writing is generally clear and well-structured. However, the manuscript contains several typos and grammatical errors that need correction (listed below). The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appear to be technically sound.

The paper would benefit from addressing the following points:
- Several important references are missing, particularly regarding distributed consensus optimization methods: Bertsekas and Tsitsiklis (1989), Sundhar Ram et al. (2009), and Shi et al. (2015) on decentralized optimization.
- The paper references Polyak's "heavy ball" method (Line 229) and Nesterov's acceleration (Line 232) without proper citations.
- The authors should provide a discussion of the practical applicability of their method, including when it should be preferred over existing distributed consensus methods.
- The limitations of Min-Sum Splitting should be clearly stated.
- The intuition behind using the auxiliary process should be explained more thoroughly.
- The paper should address whether the framework can be extended to non-quadratic consensus problems common in machine learning.
- The significance of these results for the machine learning community should be explicitly articulated.

In summary, this paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting using a novel proof technique, and by connecting the method to established approaches in the literature. While the theoretical results are strong, the paper would benefit significantly from addressing the practical implications, limitations, and significance for machine learning applications. The technical content is sound, but the presentation could be improved by addressing the noted issues.
