This paper investigates the convergence properties of the Min-Sum Splitting message-passing algorithm when applied to the distributed average consensus problem. The authors show that while the standard Min-Sum algorithm does not converge for graphs containing cycles, the Splitting variant can be tuned to not only ensure convergence but also to achieve an accelerated "subdiffusive" rate. The primary theoretical contribution is Theorem 1, which establishes an asymptotic convergence time of O(D log(D/ε)), representing a square-root improvement over standard O(D^2) diffusive methods. A key insight of the work is the connection it draws between the Min-Sum Splitting framework and two other major approaches to acceleration in distributed consensus: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods from convex optimization. The analysis is built upon a novel auxiliary message-passing scheme that translates the algorithm's dynamics from the graph's edges to its nodes, which simplifies the spectral analysis required to prove the convergence rates.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Conceptual Contribution:** The paper's main strength lies in the novel conceptual bridge it builds between Min-Sum message-passing, non-reversible lifted Markov chains, and momentum-based acceleration schemes. This connection provides a new and valuable perspective on how message-passing protocols can be designed for acceleration, a contribution that could be of broad interest to researchers in optimization and machine learning.

*   **Analytical Technique:** The introduction of the auxiliary node-based process (Algorithm 3) to track the evolution of the edge-based Min-Sum algorithm (Algorithm 2) is an elegant and effective analytical tool. This reparametrization is a solid technical contribution that makes the convergence analysis more tractable.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The authors do a good job of introducing the general Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, specializing it to the consensus problem, and then using the auxiliary scheme to derive the main theoretical results.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

The paper is promising, but its current contribution is limited by an incomplete engagement with the relevant literature and a need for a clearer discussion of the significance of its results.

*   **Positioning Relative to State-of-the-Art:** The manuscript overlooks several highly relevant and recent papers on accelerated consensus, making it difficult to assess the novelty and impact of the proposed method. The authors are strongly encouraged to discuss and compare their results with the following works:
    *   The optimal algorithms of Scaman et al. (ICML 2017) [36] and Olshevsky (2017) [30], which achieve O(D log(1/ε)) convergence rates for consensus using Nesterov-style acceleration.
    *   Foundational work on fast first-order methods for distributed optimization, such as Shi et al. (SIAM J. Optim. 2017) [38] and Jakovetić et al. (IEEE Trans. Autom. Control 2014) [16].
    *   The work of Franca and Bento (IEEE Sig. Proc. Letters 2016) [12], which directly connects lifted Markov chains with distributed ADMM, appears particularly pertinent to the connections claimed in this paper.

*   **Significance of the Convergence Rate:** The achieved O(D log(D/ε)) convergence time is a significant improvement over diffusive methods. However, its advantage over other accelerated algorithms is not clearly established. When compared to the O(D log(1/ε)) rate established by Olshevsky [30], the proposed algorithm's rate contains an extra log(D) factor, which could make it asymptotically slower, especially on graphs with large diameters (e.g., path graphs where D is O(n)). The paper would be substantially improved by a more nuanced discussion of this comparison, perhaps by identifying a class of problems or graph topologies where the proposed method offers a distinct advantage.

*   **Originality of the Acceleration Mechanism:** The analysis reveals that the optimally tuned auxiliary scheme recovers the dynamics of shift-register methods, which are known to be related to Chebyshev acceleration. Since average consensus on a fixed graph is fundamentally a linear problem, the contribution could be viewed as providing a new derivation for a known acceleration technique within the Min-Sum framework, rather than discovering a fundamentally new acceleration mechanism. Clarifying this point would help to better frame the paper's core contribution.

*   **Minor Points on Clarity:**
    *   The claim in the introduction that O(log 1/ε) convergence on graphs with "good expansion properties" is independent of the dimension (number of nodes) should be made more precise. For a complete graph, convergence is achieved in a single step, making it a trivial example. It would be helpful to specify the general class of graphs for which this dimension-independent scaling holds.
    *   The link between multi-step updates and momentum methods is a well-known concept (e.g., Rabbat et al. [32]). Acknowledging this broader context more explicitly would strengthen the paper's framing.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents an interesting analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, providing a valuable contribution by demonstrating its potential for acceleration and connecting it to other important concepts in distributed optimization. However, to be a strong contribution to the field, the work needs to be more thoroughly situated within the context of state-of-the-art accelerated methods. By addressing the missing literature and providing a more detailed comparison of the convergence rates, the authors can significantly enhance the impact and clarity of their submission.
