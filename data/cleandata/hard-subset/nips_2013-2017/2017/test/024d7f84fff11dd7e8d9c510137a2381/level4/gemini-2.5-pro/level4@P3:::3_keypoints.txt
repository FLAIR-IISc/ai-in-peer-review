- Introduces an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting message-passing algorithm for distributed consensus (network averaging) with provable convergence and "subdiffusive" acceleration.  
- Key contribution: first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting using a novel auxiliary linear process to track edge-based message dynamics.  
- Shows a protocol achieving a square-root improvement in convergence time on certain graphs, matching shift-register methods.  
- Establishes a theoretical connection between Min-Sum message passing, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step accelerated convex optimization methods.  
- Strength: original conceptual insight that splitting parameters can induce momentum-like acceleration, potentially inspiring new accelerated distributed algorithms.  
- Strength: technically sound analysis with a clever auxiliary-process proof that simplifies prior analyses and applies to general graphs.  
- Strength: paper is generally well-written and clearly motivated within distributed optimization.  
- Weakness: analysis is restricted to quadratic consensus (network averaging), limiting immediate applicability to broader convex distributed problems.  
- Weakness: lacks discussion of practical implications, comparisons to methods like EXTRA or accelerated ADMM, and communication/computational trade-offs.  
- Weakness: algorithm presentation needs clearer initialization and variable definitions (notably R̂⁰, r̂⁰ and auxiliary-process variables).  
- Weakness: literature review omits key foundational and recent references in distributed optimization and acceleration.  
- Suggested improvements: discuss challenges in extending to general strongly-convex objectives and potential obstacles.  
- Suggested improvements: add practical guidance on choosing W, impact of needing ρ(W), and per-iteration/communication complexity comparisons.  
- Suggested improvements: clarify algorithm initializations and roles of R, r, Q, q in Algorithms 2–3 for reproducibility.  
- Missing references to include: Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis (1989) on distributed methods, Polyak (heavy-ball), Nesterov (acceleration), and Shi et al. (EXTRA, 2015).  
- Minor issues: several typos and wording errors—recommend thorough proofreading.  
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution with elegant analysis; recommend acceptance if authors address generalization, practical relevance, algorithm clarity, and literature gaps.
