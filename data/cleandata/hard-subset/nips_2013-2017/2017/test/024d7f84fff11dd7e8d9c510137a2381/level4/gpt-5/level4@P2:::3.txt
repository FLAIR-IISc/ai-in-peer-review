Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies consensus via a modified Min-Sum (belief propagation) scheme based on reparameterization/splitting. The authors show that while the standard Min-Sum algorithm can fail on loopy graphs, the Splitting variant converges on consensus problems with quadratic objectives and, with appropriate parameter tuning, achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence rates. The analysis relies on recasting the edge-based message updates as an auxiliary linear process on nodes, which enables a spectral analysis of the convergence rate. The accelerated rate matches that of shift-register (second-order) methods for consensus and is comparable to acceleration obtained by Nesterov-type schemes.

The work connects three lines of literature: (i) Min-Sum and Gaussian belief propagation with reparameterizations/splittings as in Ruozzi and Tatikonda; (ii) accelerated consensus via nonreversible, lifted Markov chains; and (iii) multi-step first-order methods (heavy-ball, Nesterov, Chebyshev/shift-register), including earlier results on optimal linear iterations for averaging. The paper’s main result (Theorem 1) shows that an appropriate choice of splitting parameters yields a square-root improvement in the asymptotic convergence factor compared to diffusive consensus, leading to O(D log(D/ε)) iterations on cycles and tori.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: A clear, principled analysis of Min-Sum Splitting for quadratic consensus, including explicit rates and a precise connection to shift-register methods. The auxiliary node-based linear system is an elegant tool that greatly simplifies the analysis.
- Conceptual novelty: The paper bridges message passing with both lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods, clarifying the role of directionality and memory in acceleration.
- Clarity of the main ideas: The overall motivation and the structure of the results are well presented, and the contributions are clearly delineated.

Weaknesses
- Scope and practicality: The results are restricted to quadratic consensus. The paper would benefit from a discussion of when Min-Sum Splitting is preferable to established accelerated consensus methods, including implementation and communication costs.
- Missing intuition: The auxiliary process is central to the proof; providing more intuition for why it captures the edge-based dynamics and how directionality translates to memory would improve accessibility.
- Presentation gaps: Some variables in the algorithms are not explicitly defined or initialized, and a number of typos and minor grammatical issues hinder readability. Several relevant references are missing.

Assessment by review criteria
- Quality: The technical development appears sound; the propositions and theorem are consistent with prior theory on shift-register acceleration. The assumptions are clearly stated, and the rates are supported by spectral arguments.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written, but key quantities (e.g., variables in Algorithm 2; explicit initialization of parameters) need clearer definitions. Fixing typos and tightening notation would further improve clarity.
- Originality: Establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus and making explicit the links to lifted chains and multi-step methods are novel and insightful.
- Significance: The results add theoretical understanding of accelerated message passing for consensus and suggest pathways to extend acceleration to other classes of problems. Practical impact would be strengthened by broader instances or empirical comparisons.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- New analysis technique via an auxiliary node-based process.
- Accelerated rate matching shift-register methods, near known lower bounds on geometric graphs.
- Conceptual unification of message passing, lifting, and multi-step acceleration.

Cons:
- Limited to quadratic consensus; unclear applicability to more general ML objectives.
- Insufficient discussion of practical trade-offs versus existing accelerated consensus/ADMM methods.
- Presentation issues (definitions, typos, missing references).

Specific comments and questions for the authors
- Practicality and scope: In which regimes (graph families, network sizes, communication/computation budgets) is Min-Sum Splitting preferable to Chebyshev/shift-register consensus, EXTRA, or distributed ADMM?
- Limitations: What are the main bottlenecks (initialization, parameter tuning requiring global spectral information, sensitivity to noise/asynchrony)?
- Intuition: Provide a concise explanation of why splitting-induced directionality on edges becomes a memory term on nodes in the auxiliary system.
- Beyond quadratics: Can the approach extend to strongly convex smooth objectives or composite problems commonly seen in ML? What obstacles arise (e.g., non-quadratic pairwise terms, constraints)?
- Relevance to ML: Clarify concrete ML scenarios (e.g., decentralized training/aggregation) where the proposed scheme would be advantageous, and how global tuning information could be estimated in practice.

Overall evaluation
This is a solid theoretical contribution that clarifies how Min-Sum Splitting can be tuned to achieve accelerated consensus rates and reveals deep connections to lifted chains and multi-step methods. I recommend acceptance if the authors (i) improve exposition and notation, (ii) add missing references, and (iii) expand the discussion of practicality, limitations, and potential generalizations beyond quadratic consensus.
