This paper investigates the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method applied to the average consensus problem. While the paper is generally well-written, the presented improvement over existing results appears limited. Specific comments are detailed below.

(1) The authors state a convergence rate that is “optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph,” linking convergence time to the problem dimension. However, for complete graphs, the consensus is achieved in a single iteration, resulting in a linear convergence rate of 0. Furthermore, complete graphs represent a highly specific case. The paper should clarify for which broader classes of graphs the complexity is independent of the number of nodes (dimension). It should also explicitly define what constitutes a “good” graph in this context.

(2) The literature review in this section lacks a critical reference: “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control” by Olshevsky. The authors should include this work and accurately report its convergence rate (further detail provided in comment 8), as the presented bound is competitive with, or even superior to, the results in this submission.

(3) When discussing consensus optimization (top of page 2), important related works should be included, such as “On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization” by Shi, Ling, Kun, Wu, and Yin, and “Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks” by Scaman, Bach, Bubeck, Lee, and Massoulié. The authors should also provide a comparative analysis of the state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization and their respective linear convergence rates.

(4) The discussion of lifted graphs and Markov chains omits a highly relevant paper: “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM” by Franca and Bento.

(5) The content of the last paragraph of page 5 represents a well-established result and should be attributed to “Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links” by Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew. The subsequent connection to Heavy ball, Nesterov, and Polyak methods is also widely known within the field.

(6) The paper overlooks several other important references in the area of consensus optimization, including “Extra: An Exact First-Order Algorithm for Decentralized Consensus Optimization” by Shi, Ling, Wu, and Yin, and “Fast Distributed Gradient Methods” by Jakovetic, Moura, and Xavier.

(7) Proposition 3 appears to be a relatively trivial contribution and could be considered supplementary material.

(8) The achieved convergence rate of D log(D/eps) does not demonstrate a substantial improvement over the D log(1/eps) rate previously obtained by “Linear Time Average Consensus…” (see comment 2). In the worst-case scenario, where D approaches n (the number of nodes), the submitted paper’s bound is even less favorable.

(9) The “Linear Time Average Consensus…” paper utilizes Nesterov's acceleration to improve its bound. The reviewer suspects the proposed “Auxiliary message-passing scheme” is essentially a reapplication of Nesterov's acceleration to the min-sum algorithm. While this is acceptable, the analysis is performed on a consensus problem, which can be reduced to analyzing a linear system – a task not generally considered difficult. Consequently, the paper's contribution is unclear.

(10) Any improvement in performance is likely attributable to a refined analysis of the graph's spectral gap. However, the worst-case bound remains O(n) as O(n) = O(D) for all graphs with n nodes.

(11) Line 243 of page 6 uses directed edges on a graph described as simple, which creates confusion.

(12) Line 220 of page 6 contains a typographical error: “Laplacian” should be “Lagrangian”.
