

This paper introduces min-sum splitting, an accelerated variant of the min-sum algorithm, to solve the distributed consensus problem. The work is exceptionally well-structured, with contributions clearly contextualized within the state of the art. To the reviewer's knowledge, the results represent a significant theoretical advance, particularly through the novel connection established between min-sum algorithms and lifted Markov chain techniques. This insight offers valuable perspective for both optimization and distributed systems communities.

The primary concern arises from a notation inconsistency on page 4. The authors state: "splitting each term $\phi_{vw}$ into $\Gamma_{vw}$ terms, and each term $\phi_v$ into $\delta$ terms." However, $\delta$ and $\Gamma_{vw}$ are defined as real-valued parameters on the preceding page (Section 2.1), not integer counts. This creates a critical ambiguity: splitting a term into a non-integer number of pieces is mathematically undefined. The notation strongly implies that $\delta$ and $\Gamma_{vw}$ should represent integer quantities (e.g., the number of auxiliary variables), yet their prior definition as continuous parameters contradicts this interpretation. The authors must clarify whether these symbols denote integers (requiring a correction to their initial definition) or if a rounding/quantization step is implicitly assumed. Without this resolution, the proposed splitting scheme lacks a rigorous foundation, potentially undermining the algorithm's validity.

The paper's technical core is otherwise sound, with clear derivations and compelling convergence guarantees. The experimental results (Section 4) effectively demonstrate the algorithm's efficiency gains over baseline methods in distributed settings. The discussion of connections to lifted Markov chains (Section 3.2) is particularly insightful and well-motivated, suggesting broader applicability beyond consensus problems.

Minor suggestions for improvement:  
- Explicitly define $\delta$ and $\Gamma_{vw}$ as positive integers at their first appearance (Section 2.1), or clarify the discretization mechanism if they are derived from continuous parameters.  
- Briefly justify why the splitting approach (as defined) preserves the equivalence to the original optimization problem, especially given the non-integer ambiguity.  
- Consider adding a brief comparison to recent distributed ADMM variants (e.g., [Author et al., 2016]) to further contextualize the novelty.

Overall, this is a strong contribution with clear theoretical and practical merit. The identified notation issue is the sole technical flaw requiring resolution. Addressing it would significantly strengthen the paper's foundation. The writing is clear, the problem is well-motivated, and the results are convincing. The novelty of linking min-sum methods to lifted Markov chains is particularly noteworthy and merits publication, provided the notation is clarified. The authors should revise the manuscript to resolve this inconsistency before acceptance.
