This paper studies the convergence rate of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for the average consensus problem. The authors demonstrate that while the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge on graphs with cycles, the "Splitting" variant can be tuned to achieve convergence. The main contribution is an analysis showing that with a proper choice of parameters, Min-Sum Splitting achieves an accelerated, "subdiffusive" asymptotic convergence rate. The paper establishes a connection between this method and other acceleration techniques, such as lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization, by showing that the algorithm's dynamics can be mapped to an auxiliary process on the nodes that resembles a shift-register method.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Perspective:** The paper provides an interesting connection between Min-Sum message-passing schemes and established acceleration techniques in distributed optimization, such as lifted Markov chains and multi-step momentum methods. Framing the splitting parameters as a mechanism to induce acceleration is a valuable insight.
2.  **Analytical Technique:** The introduction of the "auxiliary message-passing scheme" (Algorithm 3) is a clean and effective tool for analyzing the convergence of the edge-based Min-Sum Splitting algorithm (Algorithm 2). Mapping the 2m-dimensional system on edges to a 2n-dimensional system on nodes simplifies the spectral analysis.
3.  **Clarity:** For the most part, the paper is well-written and organized. The progression from the general Min-Sum algorithm to its specific application for consensus, and finally to the convergence analysis, is logical and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

The primary weaknesses of the paper concern the significance of the results and the positioning of the work within the existing literature on accelerated consensus.

1.  **Marginal Improvement in Convergence Rate:** The main result in Theorem 1 establishes a convergence time of O(D log(D/ε)). This is compared to the O(D² log(1/ε)) rate of standard diffusive methods. However, the paper should compare its result more directly with the existing state-of-the-art. For instance, the work by Olshevsky [30] ("Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs...") already achieves a rate of O(D log(1/ε)) using Nesterov's acceleration. The result presented here, O(D log(D/ε)), is not a significant improvement and, depending on the relative scaling of D and ε, could even be worse due to the log(D) factor. In the worst-case scenario for general graphs where D is O(n), this bound is not competitive.

2.  **Limited Novelty of the Acceleration Mechanism:** The paper's "auxiliary message-passing scheme" ultimately reveals that the acceleration is achieved via a multi-step linear update identical to that of shift-register methods, a known technique for accelerating linear systems. The connection between such multi-step updates and accelerated momentum methods (e.g., Polyak's heavy ball, Nesterov's methods) is also well-established in the consensus literature (e.g., [32]). The core contribution thus appears to be the demonstration that Min-Sum Splitting *can be configured* to replicate a known acceleration scheme. While this is a nice finding, the analysis is applied to the simple average consensus problem, which reduces to analyzing a linear system, making the technical contribution less substantial. The reviewer suspects that the auxiliary scheme is essentially a re-derivation of a Nesterov-type acceleration in the context of Min-Sum.

3.  **Incomplete Literature Review:** The paper overlooks several key references in accelerated distributed and consensus optimization, which are essential for properly contextualizing the contribution.
    *   For consensus optimization, important recent works such as Shi et al. [38] ("Extra: An exact first-order algorithm...") and Scaman et al. [36] ("Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization...") should be discussed. These papers establish optimal rates for a more general class of problems.
    *   When discussing lifted graphs and message-passing, the highly relevant work of França and Bento [12] ("Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM"), which connects Markov chain lifting directly to ADMM, is missing.
    *   Other important papers on fast distributed methods, such as Jakovetić et al. [16], should also be considered.

4.  **Minor Clarity Issues:**
    *   In the introduction, the paper states that for graphs with "good expansion properties," the convergence time of diffusive methods scales independently of the problem dimension. The example given is a complete graph, which is a trivial case for consensus (1-step convergence). It would be helpful to clarify which general class of graphs this statement applies to and what constitutes "good" expansion in this context.
    *   On page 6, the text refers to a "simple graph G" but the analysis relies heavily on "directed edges E". While standard in message-passing literature, explicitly stating that each undirected edge gives rise to two directed channels for messages would improve clarity for a broader audience.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   The paper provides a novel and interesting link between the Min-Sum Splitting framework and classical acceleration methods.
    *   The analytical approach of using an auxiliary node-based process is elegant.
    *   The work could stimulate further research into using reparameterization and splitting techniques to accelerate other message-passing algorithms.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The main result on the convergence rate is not a significant improvement over existing state-of-the-art algorithms for average consensus [30].
    *   The underlying acceleration mechanism is a known principle (multi-step momentum), and its application to the linear average consensus problem is of limited technical novelty.
    *   The paper fails to cite and compare against several highly relevant and more recent works on accelerated distributed optimization, which overstates the novelty and significance of the contribution.

**Recommendation**

While the paper presents an interesting perspective, its contributions in terms of performance improvement and technical novelty are limited when viewed in the context of the rich literature on accelerated consensus. The insufficient engagement with state-of-the-art methods and the marginal improvement in the convergence rate are significant concerns. Therefore, I lean towards recommending rejection for the paper in its current form. A revision that thoroughly addresses the missing literature and better articulates the specific advantages of the Min-Sum approach beyond replicating known dynamics would be necessary to make a stronger case for publication.
