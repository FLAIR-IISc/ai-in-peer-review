Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies the consensus (network averaging) problem through the lens of Min-Sum message passing. The main idea is to use the Min-Sum Splitting formulation—which reparametrizes the original objective via node and edge “splits”—to obtain a linear, convergent message-passing scheme that attains accelerated (subdiffusive) rates on general graphs. The authors show that ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge on graphs with cycles, whereas Min-Sum Splitting, with appropriate tuning, converges and achieves a square-root improvement over classical diffusive consensus iterations.

Technically, the paper develops two equivalent representations of the algorithm: (i) a linear process on directed edges (Algorithm 2), and (ii) a coupled, multi-step linear process on nodes with memory (Algorithm 3). The latter enables a clean spectral analysis via a 2n × 2n matrix K whose spectral radius controls the rate. The main theorem shows that with δ = 1 and Γ = γW, where W is a symmetric mixing matrix (W1 = 1) and γ is chosen as a function of the spectral gap of W, the Min-Sum Splitting scheme matches the asymptotic rates of shift-register methods. This provides a conceptual bridge between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains (non-reversibility/directionality on a lifted space of directed edges), and multi-step first-order methods (momentum/shift-register on the original node space). The results refine and extend the analysis in [27], and complement prior convergence guarantees for Gaussian belief propagation with splitting [35].

Strengths:
- Novelty and insight: Establishes the first explicit convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, recovering the accelerated rates of shift-register methods. The auxiliary node-based analysis is technically clean and likely reusable.
- Conceptual unification: Clarifies how “splitting” induces directionality analogous to lifted non-reversible chains, while the node-level auxiliary process exhibits momentum-like behavior as in multi-step methods. This cross-connection is valuable to both communities.
- Generality of rates: The analysis yields subdiffusive convergence across arbitrary graphs and gives concrete scaling O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori, improving over O(D^2 log(1/ε)) diffusive methods and approaching known near-optimal rates O(D log(1/ε)) from Nesterov-based schemes.
- Clarity and organization: The paper is well-written, clearly situating contributions relative to prior work and providing a coherent narrative from algorithms to spectral analysis.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Scope of problem class: Results are established for quadratic consensus; extension to general convex φv is not analyzed. The paper motivates broader applicability but leaves it open.
- Parameter knowledge and decentralization: The accelerated tuning requires γ based on ρW, a global spectral quantity. This is common in accelerated consensus, but it limits full decentralization. It would be helpful to discuss practical schemes to estimate or adapt γ in a distributed fashion, and robustness to mis-specification.
- Role of δ: Although splitting is introduced via (δ, Γ), the main theorem specializes to δ = 1. It would be useful to clarify when δ ≠ 1 is beneficial and whether it can further improve robustness or rate constants.
- Initialization and well-posedness: The theorem assumes R̂0 = r̂0 = 0 and uses symmetric W. Proposition 1 requires positivity conditions (e.g., 1 + δ∑w Γwv R̂twv > 0). Please clarify conditions on Γ (including possible negative entries) and initializations that guarantee convexity/well-defined updates for all t, and whether rates extend beyond the zero-initialization case.
- Empirical validation: The paper is purely theoretical. Even small-scale experiments on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs comparing Min-Sum Splitting to Metropolis-Hastings, optimal symmetric W, and shift-register/Nesterov methods would strengthen the claims and illustrate constants and robustness.

Clarity issues and suggestions:
- Splitting interpretation: On page 4 you write that Min-Sum Splitting corresponds to “splitting each term φvw into Γvw terms, and each term φv into δ terms,” yet δ and Γvw were introduced as real parameters. Later you note that for strictly positive integers the reparametrization corresponds to literal replication, and for general real values Algorithm 1 is still well-defined. Please make this distinction explicit in the main text when the statement first appears (i.e., say “for integer δ, Γvw this corresponds to splitting into that many terms; for general real δ, Γvw this is a reweighting/reparametrization that yields the same updates”). This will avoid confusion.
- Minor typos/notation:
  - In the quadratic objective for consensus, several places appear to omit the square: φv(z) should be 1/2 z^2 − bv z.
  - There is a typesetting artifact “st Conference…” in the introduction.
  - Ensure consistency between Algorithm 2/3 definitions and piecewise definitions of K̂(δ, Γ); a compact matrix form or a brief derivation sketch would aid readability.
- Practical guidance: Since Γ = γW must respect graph sparsity, a brief note on how to choose W in practice (e.g., Metropolis-Hastings vs optimized symmetric W, and the implications for γ) would be helpful.

Evaluation:
- Quality: The analysis appears technically sound, with careful derivations and a clear spectral argument leveraging the auxiliary process. Assumptions are standard for consensus and clearly stated.
- Clarity: Generally high. Addressing the splitting interpretation, minor typos, and adding brief practical guidance would make it even clearer.
- Originality: Strong. The rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus and the explicit link to both lifted chains and multi-step methods are, to my knowledge, new.
- Significance: High for researchers working on distributed optimization, belief propagation, and consensus. The tools and insights should inspire extensions beyond quadratic consensus.

Pros for acceptance:
- First rigorous accelerated-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus.
- Elegant auxiliary-process proof technique; insightful conceptual connections.
- General graph results with meaningful improvements over diffusive methods.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited to quadratic objectives; no experiments.
- Requires global spectral information for tuning and assumes symmetric W.
- Some clarity issues around the meaning of “splitting” for real-valued parameters and conditions ensuring well-posedness.

Overall, I view this as a solid theoretical contribution that advances understanding of Min-Sum methods and their acceleration. Addressing the noted clarity points and, if possible, adding brief empirical illustrations would further strengthen the paper.
