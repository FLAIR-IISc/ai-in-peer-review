Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies consensus via the Min-Sum (a.k.a. max-product for log potentials/Gaussian BP) message-passing protocol. It shows that the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm can diverge on loopy graphs for consensus, but that the reparameterized Min-Sum Splitting scheme (Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013) converges and can be tuned to accelerate convergence. The main technical device is an auxiliary linear process on the nodes that exactly tracks the evolution of the edge-based Min-Sum Splitting iterates. For quadratic consensus (network averaging), the authors prove that a particular choice of splitting parameters yields subdiffusive, “square-root accelerated” rates matching those of shift-register/second-order methods. The work draws a clear connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two established acceleration paradigms: lifted, non-reversible Markov chains (e.g., Chen–Lovász–Pak; Diaconis et al.) and multi-step first-order methods (Polyak’s heavy ball, Nesterov, Chebyshev/shift-register methods). The analysis improves on prior Min-Sum consensus results that relied on edge-based dynamics and were limited to specific graph classes (e.g., Moallemi and Van Roy; subsequent analyses on cycles/regular graphs).

Quality
The technical results are carefully stated and, to the extent I could check, the proofs of Propositions 1–2 and Theorem 1 are sound. The auxiliary process reduces a 2m-dimensional edge system to coupled n-dimensional node systems whose convergence can be analyzed via the spectrum of Γ (and W), which is both insightful and technically useful. The main theorem gives explicit tuning (γ as a function of ρ(W)) and establishes a spectral rate ρK strictly smaller than ρW, yielding the desired O(D log(D/ε)) iteration complexity on cycles and tori. Assumptions and limits are mostly clear, although some conditions (e.g., positivity of certain quadratic coefficients, the distributed feasibility of computing/approximating ρ(W), and initialization details) would benefit from clearer discussion.

Clarity
The paper is generally well structured and readable. The overview of classical diffusive algorithms and accelerated approaches provides useful context. However, there are numerous typos and a few missing definitions/initializations that impede readability (see “Typos and minor corrections”). In particular, Algorithms 2 and 3 should more clearly define all variables on first use, state initial conditions explicitly, and mark which variables constitute the auxiliary process. A brief high-level explanation of why the auxiliary node process captures the edge dynamics (intuition for the reparameterization and “directionality/memory” mapping) would also help.

Originality
As far as I am aware, this is the first rigorous convergence-rate analysis of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus and the first explicit link to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step acceleration. The auxiliary-process proof technique is novel for this setting and appears of independent interest.

Significance
The result provides a principled way to achieve square-root acceleration for consensus with a message-passing algorithm closely related to BP, with complexity comparable to standard linear protocols. This is important for graphs with geometry, where diffusive methods are provably suboptimal. The work is of interest to the NeurIPS community given the broad use of consensus in distributed learning and inference. That said, practicality is limited by (i) the need for global spectral information to set γ; (ii) focus on quadratic objectives; (iii) synchronous/static-graph assumptions; and (iv) lack of empirical evaluation. Broader impact would be clearer with guidance on robust parameter choices, applicability beyond quadratics, and comparisons to ADMM/EXTRA and accelerated consensus variants.

Pros
- Clear, principled analysis yielding accelerated rates; proofs appear correct.
- Conceptually unifies Min-Sum Splitting with lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods.
- Auxiliary node-based process is elegant and may be useful beyond this problem.
- Improves over earlier Min-Sum consensus analyses and approaches near-optimal rates on geometric graphs.

Cons
- Requires global spectral information (ρ(W)) for tuning; sensitivity to misspecification not discussed.
- Scope limited to quadratic consensus; no results for general convex or constrained settings.
- Synchronous setting; no discussion of time-varying/asynchronous networks.
- Presentation issues: typos, missing definitions/initializations; limited intuition for the auxiliary mapping.
- No experiments or empirical illustrations.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Practicality and tuning: How should γ be chosen in practice when ρ(W) is unknown? Is there a distributed estimator, or a robust default (e.g., Chebyshev-like schedules) with provable guarantees under misspecification?
- Cost and scalability: What are the per-iteration communication and computation costs relative to standard gossip, EXTRA, and ADMM? Any gain/loss in wall-clock convergence?
- Extensions: Can the analysis extend to strongly convex non-quadratic objectives (e.g., via quadratic upper/lower models) or constraints (e.g., equality constraints beyond averaging)? Any path toward stochastic/noisy settings?
- Network models: Can the method handle asynchronous updates, directed graphs, or time-varying topologies?
- Intuition: Please expand on the “directionality induces memory” intuition and provide a short derivation sketch showing how edge messages reduce to the node multi-step recursion.
- Relevance to ML: Clarify when Min-Sum Splitting is preferable in ML pipelines (e.g., distributed averaging in federated learning, sensor networks), and discuss interplay with ADMM and gradient tracking methods.

Overall evaluation
A solid theoretical contribution that clarifies the behavior of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus and connects it to influential acceleration frameworks. With improved exposition, discussion of practical tuning/limitations, and (ideally) empirical illustration, the paper would be a valuable addition to the literature.
