This paper applies an accelerated variant of the min-sum algorithm, Min-Sum Splitting, to the distributed consensus problem. The manuscript is clearly written, situates the contribution well within the existing literature, and, to the best of my knowledge, presents novel results. In particular, it draws a new and interesting connection between min-sum methods and lifted Markov chain techniques.

Quality and significance: The work appears technically sound and offers a qualitative advance. The authors show that while standard Min-Sum can fail to converge on graphs with cycles, the Splitting variant converges and, with appropriate tuning, achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence rates comparable to those of shift-register methods. The paper’s analysis via an auxiliary linear process on the nodes is insightful and seems broadly useful. The demonstrated links to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods help unify strands of the literature and could influence future algorithm design.

Clarity: The exposition is generally very good. However, there is one point that is unclear. On page 4, when describing the equivalent objective minimized by the min-sum algorithm that yields the splitting scheme, the paper states that it involves “splitting each term φ_vw into Γ_vw terms, and each term φ_v into δ terms.” As introduced on the previous page, δ and Γ_vw are real numbers, not necessarily integers. It is therefore unclear what “splitting into Γ_vw terms” means when Γ_vw (and δ) are real-valued. Later the text notes that, despite this interpretation, the algorithm is defined for arbitrary real δ and Γ, but the current wording still invites confusion. I recommend clarifying this point explicitly. For example, the authors could state that the “splitting into δ and Γ_vw parts” is an interpretive device that corresponds literally when these parameters are positive integers (reparameterizing the objective into replicated terms), whereas the actual algorithm and analysis allow general real-valued parameters without requiring such a literal decomposition. Alternatively, provide a brief explanation of how to interpret the reparameterization when δ and Γ_vw are not integers.

Originality: The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and acceleration mechanisms known from lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods is, to my knowledge, new and clearly articulated. The convergence-rate results and the auxiliary node-based linear system appear original and add theoretical depth to the understanding of Min-Sum Splitting.

Overall assessment: Strong, well-motivated contribution with clear writing and a minor presentation issue that can be fixed with a short clarification.
