

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum message-passing to the distributed consensus problem, introducing a modified version called Min-Sum Splitting that achieves accelerated convergence rates. The authors establish that while ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge for graphs with cycles, the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm with proper parameter tuning yields convergence to the problem solution with subdiffusive rates matching those of shift-register methods. The paper makes a significant contribution by connecting Min-Sum schemes with both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization, a connection previously unexplored in the literature.

The paper is well-positioned in the context of recent NIPS work on distributed optimization. It builds upon prior work on Min-Sum message-passing (e.g., [28, 25, 34, 35]) and distributed consensus algorithms (e.g., [20, 21, 9, 19, 42, 41]), while addressing a key limitation in the literature: the lack of theoretical convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting. The authors provide a thorough comparison with two main lines of accelerated consensus methods: lifted Markov chains (e.g., [8, 5, 37]) and multi-step first-order methods (e.g., [13, 24]), demonstrating how Min-Sum Splitting shares conceptual similarities with both approaches.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical analysis establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting. The proofs are well-constructed, and the authors provide a clear connection between their auxiliary process analysis and the spectral properties of the underlying graph. The theoretical results are supported by the analysis of specific graph topologies (cycles, tori), showing how the algorithm achieves O(D log(D/ε)) convergence time, which represents a square-root improvement over diffusive methods. The paper appropriately acknowledges limitations, noting that the optimal parameter selection requires global graph information.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear organization and logical flow. The technical content is presented accessibly, with appropriate mathematical notation and well-chosen examples. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and existing methods is made particularly clear through the detailed exposition in Sections 5-7. The supplementary material provides additional proofs, enhancing the paper's reproducibility.

Originality: This paper makes several significant original contributions. First, it establishes the first connection between Min-Sum schemes and lifted Markov chain techniques. Second, it provides the first proof of how directionality in belief propagation can be tuned to accelerate convergence. Third, it develops a novel proof technique using an auxiliary process to track the Min-Sum evolution on the graph nodes. Fourth, it demonstrates that Min-Sum Splitting achieves better convergence rates than the method proposed in [27], which had previously investigated Min-Sum for consensus. The paper thoroughly addresses related work, making it clear how the contributions differ from prior NIPS publications.

Significance: The results have clear significance for distributed optimization, as they provide a new algorithm that achieves near-optimal convergence rates for consensus problems on graphs with geometry. The paper demonstrates that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve convergence times of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, which matches the rates of recent accelerated methods based on Nesterov's acceleration [30, 36]. This represents a meaningful advancement over classical diffusive algorithms, which scale as O(D² log(1/ε)). The connection to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods suggests broader applicability to other distributed optimization problems.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-organized exposition
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with meaningful convergence rates
- Novel connection between Min-Sum message-passing and two established acceleration techniques
- Comprehensive comparison with related work
- Practical implications for distributed systems

Weaknesses:
- The interpretation of the splitting parameters as "splitting into Γvw terms" (page 4) is confusing since Γvw and δ are introduced as real numbers, not positive integers. The authors should clarify whether this is a misstatement or whether they're considering a more general interpretation of the splitting operation.
- The paper focuses primarily on the quadratic consensus problem; extending the results to more general objective functions would strengthen the contribution's broader applicability.
- While the paper mentions that the optimal parameter choice requires global information (e.g., ρW), it would be helpful to discuss how this limitation might be addressed in practical settings where global information is not readily available.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization with clear theoretical advances and practical implications. The results are novel, well-supported, and represent an important step toward understanding the connections between different distributed optimization approaches. I recommend acceptance.
