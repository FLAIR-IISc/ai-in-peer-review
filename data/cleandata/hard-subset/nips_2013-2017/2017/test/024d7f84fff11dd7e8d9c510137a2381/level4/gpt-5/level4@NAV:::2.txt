This paper investigates the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method for the average consensus problem. The manuscript is generally readable, but the claimed improvement appears limited. Detailed comments are below.
(1) The paper states that “This rate is optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph. In this case the convergence time, i.e., the number of iterations required to reach a prescribed error level, is … independent of the dimension of the problem, as …”. For a complete graph, the effective linear rate is zero because consensus is achieved in one iteration; moreover, complete graphs are too special to be illustrative. For which broad families of graphs is the complexity independent of the dimension (number of nodes)? Which classes are meant by “good”?
(2) In the same discussion, the literature review should cite “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control” (Olshevsky). Its convergence rate needs to be stated accurately (see also comment 8). That reference achieves a bound that is competitive with, or even superior to, the result here.
(3) At the top of page 2, for consensus optimization, important references are missing, including:
- “On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization” (Shi, Ling, Wu, Yin),
- “Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks” (Scaman, Bach, Bubeck, Lee, Massoulié).
Please also summarize the state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization and their (linear) convergence rates.
(4) The discussion of lifted graphs and Markov chains omits the closely related “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM” (Franca and Bento).
(5) The content of the last paragraph on page 5 reports a long-known fact; it should reference “Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links” (Rabbat, Nowak, Bucklew). The links between those variants and Heavy-ball/Nesterov/Polyak acceleration are well known in the field.
(6) Several key references on consensus optimization are missing, e.g.:
- “EXTRA: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization” (Shi, Ling, Wu, Yin),
- “Fast distributed gradient methods” (Jakovetić, Xavier, Moura).
(7) Proposition 3 appears trivial and only tangentially contributes to the main results.
(8) The rate obtained here, D log(D/ε), does not clearly improve upon D log(1/ε) in “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control” (see comment 2). In the worst case (over all graphs), where D ≍ n, your bound can be weaker than that prior result.
(9) The “Linear Time Average Consensus …” result leverages Nesterov’s acceleration. The proposed “auxiliary message-passing scheme” seems to be Nesterov-style acceleration applied to min-sum. This is acceptable, but for consensus the analysis reduces to a linear system and should be relatively straightforward, which makes the incremental contribution unclear.
(10) Any minor improvement may stem from a more careful handling of the spectral gap. Ultimately, the worst-case bound remains O(n), since O(n) = O(D) over the class of n-node graphs.
(11) Line 243 of page 6: the graph is described as simple, yet directed edges are used; this is confusing.
(12) Typo at line 220 of page 6: “Laplacian” should be “Lagrangian.”

I appreciate the authors’ response. However, my overall evaluation remains unchanged.
