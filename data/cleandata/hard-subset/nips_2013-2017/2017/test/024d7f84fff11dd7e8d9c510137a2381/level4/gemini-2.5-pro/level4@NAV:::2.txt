This paper analyzes the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method applied to the average consensus problem. While the manuscript is clearly written, the claimed improvement over existing results appears to be marginal. Specific comments are provided below.

(1) The paper states, "This rate is optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph. In this case the convergence time..." This example is not illustrative, as average consensus on a complete graph is achieved in a single step, making the notion of a convergence rate moot. Furthermore, complete graphs represent a very specific case. The authors should clarify for which broader, more representative classes of graphs the claimed dimension-independent complexity holds and provide a precise definition of what constitutes a graph with "good" expansion properties in this context.

(2) In the same paragraph discussing convergence rates, the literature review is incomplete. The work by Olshevsky, "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control," is a critical omission. This paper presents a convergence rate that is competitive with, and in some cases superior to, the bound derived in this submission (as detailed further in comment 8).

(3) The discussion of consensus optimization at the beginning of page 2 is missing several foundational references. Notably, works such as "On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization" by Shi et al. and "Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks" by Scaman et al. should be cited. The authors should also provide a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization, including their established linear convergence rates.

(4) When discussing lifted graphs and Markov chains, the paper overlooks the highly relevant work "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM" by Franca and Bento.

(5) The final paragraph on page 5 presents a result that is well-established in the literature but fails to provide attribution. This finding should be credited to "Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links" by Rabbat et al. Moreover, the connection between these algorithmic variants and momentum-based acceleration methods (e.g., Heavy ball, Nesterov, Polyak) is widely known and should be acknowledged as such.

(6) The literature review on consensus optimization is further weakened by the omission of other significant works, such as "Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization" by Shi et al. and "Fast distributed gradient methods" by Jakovetic, Xavier, and Moura.

(7) Proposition 3 appears to be a straightforward result, and its contribution to the paper's main thesis is supplementary at best.

(8) The main result, a convergence rate of D log(D/eps), offers only a marginal improvement over the existing D log(1/eps) rate from the aforementioned work by Olshevsky. In the worst-case scenario, where the graph diameter D is proportional to the number of nodes n, the proposed bound is actually weaker than the established result.

(9) The novelty of the proposed "Auxiliary message-passing scheme" is questionable. It appears to be a direct application of Nesterov's acceleration to the min-sum algorithm. While this is a valid approach, its application to the average consensus problem—which reduces to the analysis of a linear system—is a relatively straightforward exercise. This raises questions about the significance of the paper's core technical contribution.

(10) It is possible that the minor improvement in the rate stems from a more refined analysis of the graph's spectral gap. However, this does not alter the fundamental worst-case complexity, which remains O(n) since D can be O(n) for a graph with n nodes.

(11) On page 6, line 243, the use of directed edges is confusing, as the graph is defined as being simple.

(12) A typo is present on page 6, line 220: "Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian."

The authors' rebuttal has satisfactorily addressed the points raised. However, my assessment of the paper's overall contribution remains unchanged, and thus the score will not be adjusted.
