1. Presents an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for consensus in distributed optimization using reparametrization and an auxiliary linear process.  
2. Main contributions: (i) novel auxiliary-process-based proof technique, (ii) improved Min-Sum protocol with stronger convergence, (iii) connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization.  
3. Motivation and contributions are clearly articulated and the writing is generally lucid.  
4. Proofs for Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear sound.  
5. Manuscript contains multiple typographical and grammatical errors (line-numbered corrections provided by reviewer).  
6. Specific wording/terminology issues noted (e.g., "double-stochastic"→"doubly-stochastic", "can be casted as"→"can be cast as").  
7. Minor semantic comment: "Laplacian"/"Laplace multipliers" may be incorrect and "Lagrangian"/"Lagrange multipliers" could be more appropriate.  
8. Algorithm clarity issues: several quantities in Algorithm 2 (R_vw, r_vw, \hat{R}^0, \hat{r}^0) are not defined or initialized, and correspondence to the auxiliary process in Algorithm 3 is not made explicit.  
9. Missing references on (sub)gradient consensus methods (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989; Ram et al. 2009; Wei Shi 2015) and vague citation of "original literature".  
10. Missing citations for optimization accelerations (suggest Polyak's Heavy-ball and Nesterov references).  
11. Reviewer requests discussion of practical applicability and comparative advantages versus other distributed consensus methods.  
12. Reviewer requests discussion of limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method.  
13. Reviewer requests clearer intuition behind use of the auxiliary linear process.  
14. Reviewer suggests investigating extensions beyond quadratic consensus objectives to more general ML-relevant problems.  
15. Reviewer asks for a clearer justification of the approach’s significance to the Machine Learning community.  
16. Summary: solid theoretical contributions and novel analysis, but manuscript needs proofreading, clearer algorithmic definitions, more references, and expanded discussion on practicality, intuition, limitations, and ML relevance.
