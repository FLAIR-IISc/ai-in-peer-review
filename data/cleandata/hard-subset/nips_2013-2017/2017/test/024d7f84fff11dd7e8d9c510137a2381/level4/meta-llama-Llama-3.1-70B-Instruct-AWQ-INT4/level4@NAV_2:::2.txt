This paper presents a novel application of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol to solve the consensus problem in distributed optimization. The authors show that a modified version of the Min-Sum algorithm, known as Splitting, yields convergence to the problem solution, and they establish rates of convergence for this algorithm.

The paper begins by introducing the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm and its application to the consensus problem. The authors provide a clear explanation of the algorithm and its underlying principles, making it easy to follow for readers familiar with message-passing protocols. The paper then reviews the classical diffusive algorithms for consensus and discusses the limitations of these methods.

The authors also provide a comprehensive review of accelerated algorithms for consensus, including lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first-order methods. They show that the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm bears similarities with these accelerated methods, particularly in its ability to achieve subdiffusive convergence rates.

The main contribution of the paper is the proof that the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm achieves accelerated convergence rates, specifically a square-root improvement over diffusive methods. The authors establish this result through a novel auxiliary message-passing scheme, which allows them to track the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm via a linear process with memory supported on the nodes of the graph.

The paper also discusses the connection between the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm and other accelerated methods, including lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first-order methods. The authors show that the acceleration mechanism exploited by Min-Sum Splitting is analogous to the acceleration mechanism exploited by these other methods.
