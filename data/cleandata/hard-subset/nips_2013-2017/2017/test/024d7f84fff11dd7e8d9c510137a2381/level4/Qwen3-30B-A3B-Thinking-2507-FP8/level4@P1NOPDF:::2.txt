

This paper examines the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method for the average consensus problem. While the paper is generally well-written, the claimed improvement over existing results appears marginal and fails to significantly advance the state-of-the-art. Below are specific concerns:

(1) The paper states, "This rate is optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph. In this case, the convergence time... scales as O(D log(D/ε)) with respect to the dimension of the problem." However, for complete graphs, convergence occurs in a single step (linear rate 0), rendering them too restrictive to be representative. The paper must clarify which *general* graph classes achieve dimension-independent complexity (i.e., complexity not scaling with the number of nodes *n*). What constitutes a "good" graph class in this context?

(2) The literature review should include Olshevsky (2015) ["Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control"], which establishes a competitive or superior bound for the same problem. The paper's convergence rate must be compared directly to Olshevsky's result (see point 8).

(3) For consensus optimization, key references are missing: Shi et al. ["On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization"] and Scaman et al. ["Optimal Algorithms for Smooth and Strongly Convex Distributed Optimization in Networks"]. The authors should explicitly cite state-of-the-art algorithms and their linear convergence rates to contextualize their contribution.

(4) The discussion of lifted graphs and Markov chains overlooks Franca and Bento's ["Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM"], which is highly relevant to the technical approach.

(5) The final paragraph of page 5 states a well-known fact about consensus variants. This should reference Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew ["Generalized Consensus Computation in Networked Systems with Erasure Links"]. The connection to Heavy Ball/Nesterov/Polyak methods is also standard in the field and requires proper attribution.

(6) Critical references on consensus optimization are omitted: Shi et al. ["Extra: An Exact First-Order Algorithm for Decentralized Consensus Optimization"] and Jakovetic et al. ["Fast Distributed Gradient Methods"]. Their omission weakens the paper's positioning against prior work.

(7) Proposition 3 appears trivial and constitutes a supplementary contribution at best, not a core advance.

(8) The paper claims a rate of O(D log(D/ε)), but Olshevsky (2015) achieves O(D log(1/ε))—a strictly better bound. Crucially, in the worst case (all graphs with *n* nodes), D = Θ(n), making the paper's bound O(n log(n/ε)) while Olshevsky's is O(n log(1/ε)). Thus, the paper's bound is *worse* than Olshevsky's for general graphs.

(9) Olshevsky (2015) improves the bound via Nesterov acceleration. The authors' "auxiliary message-passing scheme" likely reinterprets this acceleration for the min-sum algorithm. While this is acceptable, the analysis for consensus reduces to solving a linear system—a standard task. The paper's contribution becomes unclear given this context.

(10) The marginal improvement may stem from refined handling of graph spectral gaps, but the worst-case bound remains Θ(n) (since D = Θ(n) for all graphs), aligning with known limits. The paper does not clarify why this refinement matters for practical graph classes.

(11) Line 243 (page 6) uses directed edges for a "simple" graph, causing confusion.

(12) Line 220 (page 6) contains a typo: "Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian."

After the authors' rebuttal, the reviewer remains satisfied with their clarifications. However, the paper's core claim of a meaningful improvement over Olshevsky (2015) is unconvincing, and the evaluation score remains unchanged. The work does not sufficiently distinguish itself from established results in the literature.
