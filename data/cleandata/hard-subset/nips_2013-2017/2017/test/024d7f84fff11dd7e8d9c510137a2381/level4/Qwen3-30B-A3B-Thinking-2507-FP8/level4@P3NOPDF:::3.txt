

The paper introduces an accelerated Min-Sum message-passing protocol for distributed consensus optimization, leveraging reparametrization techniques from [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013] to establish convergence rates for quadratic objective functions. The core novelty lies in a new proof technique using an auxiliary linear process to track the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm’s evolution. Key contributions include: (i) a rigorous analysis via this auxiliary process, (ii) a protocol achieving superior convergence rates compared to prior work, and (iii) theoretical connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods. The motivation and contributions are clearly articulated, and the paper is generally well-written and logically structured.

The technical content is sound: Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1, are correctly proven. However, several minor issues require correction:

The paper omits key references that would strengthen its context:  
- For (sub)gradient methods in consensus optimization: Bertsekas and Tsitsiklis (1989), Ram et al. (2009), Shi et al. (2015).  
- For connections to heavy-ball (Polyak, 1964) and Nesterov’s acceleration (1983) in Line 229–232.  
- Line 170 references "the original literature" without citation.  

Critically, the paper’s theoretical focus neglects two vital aspects for the ML community:  
1. **Practicality and scope**: The authors do not discuss when this method should be preferred over existing distributed approaches (e.g., EXTRA, D-ADMM), nor its computational overhead in real-world settings.  
2. **ML relevance**: The connection to machine learning is superficial. The paper should explicitly justify *why* this theoretical advance matters for ML—e.g., for large-scale distributed training with quadratic objectives (e.g., ridge regression), or as a stepping stone for non-quadratic problems.  

The following questions remain unanswered:  
- What are the limitations of Min-Sum Splitting (e.g., sensitivity to graph structure, non-quadratic extensions)?  
- What is the intuition behind the auxiliary linear process? Why does it effectively capture Min-Sum dynamics?  
- Can the framework handle general consensus problems (e.g., non-quadratic ML losses like logistic regression)?  
- How does the convergence rate compare to practical methods in terms of communication steps or wall-clock time?  

While the theoretical contribution is significant—offering a novel proof technique and tighter rates—the paper falls short in contextualizing its value for ML practitioners. The absence of discussion on practical applicability, limitations, and ML relevance weakens its impact. Theorems and proofs are elegant, but without addressing *why* this matters for learning systems, the work risks being perceived as purely mathematical.  

In summary, this is a technically solid paper with a compelling theoretical contribution. The auxiliary process technique is novel and well-executed, and the convergence rates improve upon prior work. However, the lack of practical discussion, incomplete algorithmic descriptions, and insufficient justification for ML relevance prevent it from fully realizing its potential. Addressing these gaps—particularly clarifying the method’s utility in ML applications and its comparative advantages—would substantially strengthen the paper. As currently written, it would benefit from a stronger emphasis on *why* the ML community should care about these theoretical advances.
