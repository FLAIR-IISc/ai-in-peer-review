- Paper analyzes Min-Sum Splitting for average consensus and claims accelerated convergence via a novel parametrization of Min-Sum.
- Strength: Introduces an auxiliary-process framework that tracks Min-Sum Splitting on original graph nodes, simplifying convergence analysis.
- Strength: Establishes theoretical connections between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step first-order methods.
- Strength: Proof technique using the auxiliary process is a valuable methodological contribution for message-passing analysis.
- Weakness: Claimed O(D log(D/ε)) rate is not a clear advance—Olshevsky (2015) achieves O(D log(1/ε)), which is stronger.
- Weakness: Literature review omits key related works (Olshevsky 2015; Scaman et al. 2017; França & Bento 2017; Shi et al. 2015).
- Weakness: Technical inaccuracy in claiming dimension-independent scaling for good expanders—complete graphs trivialize that claim and require clearer qualifiers.
- Weakness: Overstates novelty—[27] already analyzed Min-Sum convergence for d-regular graphs under specific initialization, so contribution is incremental.
- Weakness: Connection to Nesterov/multi-step methods appears to rediscover known techniques and lacks clear distinction from prior work.
- Quality: Technical analysis is rigorous but significance and practical implications are limited.
- Clarity: Paper is well-organized and clearly written, though some technical details (e.g., role of directed edges) need clarification.
- Originality/Significance: Minor contribution by connecting existing streams; core acceleration mechanism not novel and does not advance state-of-the-art.
- Recommendation: Recommend rejection—technically sound but insufficiently significant; suggest stronger contextualization, missing references, and clearer novelty claims.
