This paper applies an accelerated variant of the min-sum algorithm—min-sum splitting—to the distributed consensus problem. The manuscript is very well written and clearly situates its contributions within the state of the art. To the best of my knowledge (though I am not an expert on this topic), the results are novel and represent a qualitative advance. In particular, the paper draws a new and interesting connection between min-sum algorithms and lifted Markov chain techniques.

One point in the presentation is unclear. On page 4, when describing the equivalent objective function that the min-sum algorithm minimizes to produce the min-sum splitting scheme, the authors write: “...splitting each term φ_vw into Γ_vw terms, and each term φ_v into δ terms, ...” It is not clear what this operation means, because δ and Γ_vw were introduced on the previous page as real-valued parameters. Are Γ_vw and δ intended to be integers counting the number of replicated terms? If not, does “splitting” mean scaling (e.g., decomposing φ_vw into Γ_vw identical fractional components), or a reweighting of factors in a factor graph? Please clarify the precise mathematical meaning of “splitting into Γ_vw (or δ) terms,” how this decomposition is defined when these quantities are non-integer, and how it leads to the stated update equations. It would also help to state any conditions on Γ and δ under which this construction is well-defined.
