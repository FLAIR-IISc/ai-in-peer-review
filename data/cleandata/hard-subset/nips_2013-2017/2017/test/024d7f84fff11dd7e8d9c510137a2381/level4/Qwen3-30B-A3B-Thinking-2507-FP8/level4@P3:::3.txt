

This paper introduces Min-Sum Splitting, a variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for solving consensus problems in distributed optimization. The authors demonstrate that while ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge on cyclic graphs, Min-Sum Splitting achieves convergence with subdiffusive rates matching those of shift-register methods. The key contribution lies in establishing a connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two established acceleration techniques: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods. The paper proves that proper tuning of splitting parameters yields accelerated convergence, with convergence time scaling as O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, compared to the O(D² log(1/ε)) of diffusive methods.

The paper builds on prior work by Ruozzi and Tatikonda (2013), who proposed splitting as a reparametrization technique for Min-Sum, and extends the theoretical analysis to consensus problems. The authors relate their approach to classical consensus algorithms (e.g., Metropolis-Hastings) and accelerated methods like Nesterov's acceleration, showing how Min-Sum Splitting bridges these two strands of literature. This connection is novel and significant, as it reveals a unified perspective on distributed optimization acceleration techniques.

The paper excels in technical quality. The theoretical analysis is sound, with Propositions 1 and 2 correctly establishing the equivalence between Min-Sum Splitting and the auxiliary process, and Theorem 1 rigorously proving the convergence rates. The proof technique using an auxiliary linear process to track the evolution of Min-Sum Splitting on the original graph is elegant and novel. The paper carefully addresses both strengths and limitations of the approach, noting that the method is currently limited to quadratic objectives and requires knowledge of global graph properties (via ρW) for optimal parameter tuning.

The writing is generally clear and well-organized, though several grammatical errors and minor typographical issues need correction (e.g., "double-stochastic" should be "doubly-stochastic," "can be casted" should be "can be cast"). The paper would benefit from more explicit explanations of key variables in Algorithms 2 and 3 (e.g., defining R̂vw and r̂vw earlier), and a clearer statement of which variables correspond to the auxiliary process. The authors should also address the minor but important question about whether "Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian" in lines 220-221.

Regarding significance, the paper makes an important theoretical contribution to distributed optimization, but its relevance to machine learning could be more clearly articulated. While the paper mentions applications in sensor networks and distributed learning, it doesn't sufficiently explain why this particular acceleration technique would be preferred over existing methods (e.g., ADMM, Nesterov's acceleration) in machine learning contexts. The practical implications and limitations of the method—such as the need for global graph knowledge, communication overhead, and scalability to large networks—require more detailed discussion.

The paper's main strengths are:
- First theoretical analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting
- Novel connection between Min-Sum message-passing and two established acceleration frameworks
- Elegant proof technique using an auxiliary process
- Rigorous demonstration of accelerated convergence rates

The main weaknesses are:
- Limited to quadratic objective functions, without discussion of extensions
- Insufficient discussion of practical implementation considerations
- Underdeveloped justification for significance in machine learning applications
- Some unclear algorithmic details and grammatical errors

The paper addresses an important theoretical gap in distributed optimization. The convergence rate improvement represents a meaningful advancement over existing Min-Sum methods for consensus problems, and the connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods provide valuable theoretical insights. However, the paper would benefit from a stronger justification of its relevance to machine learning practitioners, who often deal with non-quadratic objectives and practical constraints like communication costs and decentralized knowledge.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as a theoretical contribution to distributed optimization, which is a core topic at the conference. While the results are primarily theoretical, they advance the understanding of distributed algorithms for optimization—a key area in machine learning. The paper's connection to two major streams of research (lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods) demonstrates its significance to the field.

For the authors to strengthen their submission, I recommend:
1) Expanding the discussion of practical implementation challenges
2) Clarifying the relationship between Min-Sum Splitting and existing machine learning algorithms
3) Adding more references to relevant distributed optimization literature (e.g., Bertsekas and Tsitsiklis, 1989; Shi et al., 2015)
4) Correcting grammatical errors and clarifying algorithmic details

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of distributed optimization algorithms. The paper's main limitation is the lack of discussion about practical implementation and relevance to machine learning applications. With these improvements, it would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
