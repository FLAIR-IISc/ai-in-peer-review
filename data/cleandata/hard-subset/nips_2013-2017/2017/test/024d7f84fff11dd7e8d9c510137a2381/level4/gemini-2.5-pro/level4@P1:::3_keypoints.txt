- Introduces an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for distributed consensus optimization and analyzes its convergence for quadratic objectives.  
- Main technical novelty is an auxiliary linear process used to track and prove convergence of the Min-Sum algorithm.  
- Provides the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and shows faster convergence than previous methods.  
- Connects the accelerated Min-Sum scheme to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods.  
- Theoretical results (Propositions 1–2, Theorem 1) appear sound and the core idea is novel and interesting.  
- Exposition is generally clear and well-motivated, but the paper needs careful proofreading to fix multiple typos and grammar errors.  
- Algorithm descriptions lack clarity: Algorithm 2 uses R{vw}, r{vw} without definitions and initial conditions R̂⁰, r̂⁰ are not stated.  
- Auxiliary process (Algorithm 3) is central but variables and their correspondence to the main algorithm are not explicitly explained.  
- Possible misuse of terminology around “Laplacian”/“Laplace multipliers” vs. “Lagrangian”/“Lagrange multipliers” on lines 220–221.  
- Related work section is weak and should cite foundational and recent consensus/subgradient methods (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989; Ram et al. 2010; Shi et al. 2015).  
- When discussing multi-step methods, the paper should cite original acceleration works such as Polyak (Heavy-ball) and Nesterov.  
- Lacks discussion of practical implications and limitations (communication overhead, parameter tuning, scenarios where method is preferable).  
- Needs more intuition for why the auxiliary process simplifies analysis and why the construction works.  
- Scope is limited to quadratic objectives; authors should discuss potential extensions to general convex functions to broaden relevance.  
- Should better articulate the relevance and impact for the machine learning community and practical use cases.  
- Overall: interesting and novel theoretical contribution with correct results, but significance would increase after improved positioning, clearer algorithms, expanded discussion, and proofreading.
