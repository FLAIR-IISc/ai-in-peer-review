This paper analyzes the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method for the average consensus problem. While the paper is generally well-written, the theoretical contributions appear to be marginal, and the work does not sufficiently engage with the existing literature. The main result offers only a minor improvement over prior work and relies on techniques that seem to be adaptations of standard acceleration methods.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Marginal Improvement and Unclear Claims on Optimality:**
    The paper claims a convergence rate of D log(D/eps). This offers only a slight, logarithmic improvement over the known D log(1/eps) rate established in "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs..." by Olshevsky. In the worst-case scenario where the graph diameter D is on the order of the number of nodes n, the bound is no better, and potentially worse, than existing results. The claim that this rate is "optimal for graphs with good expansion properties" is not well-supported. The provided example, the complete graph, is misleading, as consensus is achieved in a single step (i.e., the linear rate is 0). The authors should precisely define the general class of "good" graphs for which their complexity does not depend on the network dimension (n) and provide a more rigorous justification for optimality.

2.  **Insufficient Literature Review and Comparison to State-of-the-Art:**
    The paper overlooks a significant body of highly relevant work, which weakens its positioning and claims of novelty.
    *   **Average Consensus:** The work by Olshevsky ("Linear Time Average Consensus...") is a critical baseline that achieves a nearly identical rate. The submission must include a thorough comparison to this work, especially since Olshevsky also employs Nesterov-style acceleration.
    *   **Consensus Optimization:** The discussion of consensus optimization is incomplete. Foundational papers such as "On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization" (Shi et al.), "EXTRA: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization" (Shi et al.), "Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks" (Scaman et al.), and "Fast distributed gradient methods" (Jakovetic et al.) should be cited and discussed. The authors should report the state-of-the-art convergence rates for this problem to properly contextualize their contribution.
    *   **Related Techniques:** The discussion of lifted graphs and Markov chains omits the highly relevant paper "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM" by Franca and Bento. Furthermore, the content in the last paragraph of page 5, which connects consensus variants to momentum methods (Heavy ball/Nesterov), is a long-established fact in the community (e.g., "Generalized consensus computation..." by Rabbat et al.) and should be cited as such, not presented as a new insight.

3.  **Limited Technical Novelty:**
    The paper's proposed "Auxiliary message-passing scheme" appears to be a direct application of Nesterov's acceleration to the min-sum algorithm. Since the average consensus problem reduces to analyzing a linear system, applying acceleration techniques is a standard and well-understood approach. Given that prior work has already used this strategy to achieve similar bounds, the technical novelty of the analysis is questionable. The paper needs to clearly articulate what makes this analysis distinct from a straightforward application of Nesterov's method.

**Minor Comments**

*   **Proposition 3:** This proposition seems to be a trivial result and does not add significant value to the paper.
*   **Graph Notation:** On page 6, line 243, the graph is described as "simple," but the notation seems to imply directed edges. This should be clarified.
*   **Typo:** On page 6, line 220, "Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian."
