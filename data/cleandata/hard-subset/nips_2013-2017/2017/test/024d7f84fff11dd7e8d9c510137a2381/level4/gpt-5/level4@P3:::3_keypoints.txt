- Paper shows ordinary Min-Sum can diverge on loopy graphs while Min-Sum Splitting converges and can be tuned to accelerate consensus.  
- Main technical tool: an auxiliary linear node process that exactly tracks edge-based Min-Sum Splitting iterates.  
- For quadratic consensus, a specific splitting yields square-root (“√”) acceleration matching shift-register/second-order methods.  
- Establishes explicit tuning (γ as function of ρ(W)) and a spectral rate ρK < ρW, giving O(D log(D/ε)) iterations on cycles/tori.  
- Draws clear connections between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted non-reversible Markov chains, and multi-step first-order acceleration.  
- Improves on prior Min-Sum consensus analyses that were limited to specific graph classes.  
- Technical results and proofs (Propositions 1–2, Theorem 1) appear sound and carefully stated.  
- Auxiliary process reduction (2m edge dims → coupled n node dims) is insightful and useful for spectral analysis.  
- Some assumptions/limits need clearer discussion (positivity of coefficients, distributed feasibility of approximating ρ(W), initialization).  
- Paper is generally well structured and readable with helpful overview of diffusive and accelerated algorithms.  
- Presentation suffers from numerous typos, missing definitions/initializations, and unclear variable exposition in Algorithms 2–3.  
- Needs a brief high-level intuition for why the auxiliary node process captures edge dynamics (directionality/memory mapping).  
- Originality: first rigorous convergence-rate analysis of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus and first explicit link to lifted chains and multi-step methods.  
- Auxiliary-process proof technique is novel and likely of independent interest.  
- Significance: provides a principled message-passing method to achieve √-acceleration, important for graphs with geometry.  
- Practicality limited by reliance on global spectral information (ρ(W)), focus on quadratic objectives, synchronous/static-graph assumptions, and lack of experiments.  
- Pros: clear principled analysis, conceptual unification, elegant auxiliary process, improved rates on geometric graphs.  
- Cons: requires global spectral tuning, limited to quadratics, synchronous-only, presentation issues, and no empirical validation.  
- Key open practicality question: how to choose γ when ρ(W) is unknown — distributed estimators or robust defaults?  
- Complexity question: compare per-iteration communication/computation and wall-clock convergence versus gossip, EXTRA, ADMM.  
- Extension question: can analysis extend to strongly convex non-quadratic objectives, constrained problems, or stochastic/noisy settings?  
- Network model question: can method handle asynchronous updates, directed graphs, or time-varying topologies?  
- Request for more intuition/derivation showing how edge messages reduce to node multi-step recursion.  
- Ask authors to clarify ML relevance and compare with ADMM, gradient tracking, and when Min-Sum Splitting is preferable.  
- Lists many specific typos and algorithm-definition omissions that should be fixed; suggests adding citations (Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis, Boyd et al., EXTRA, Polyak, Nesterov).  
- Overall: solid theoretical contribution that needs improved exposition, practical tuning discussion, and empirical illustration to maximize impact.
