Summary
This paper presents an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for distributed consensus. Building on the reparametrization techniques of Ruozzi and Tatikonda (2013), the authors analyze the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm on quadratic consensus objectives and prove convergence rates. The key analytic device is an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the algorithm. The paper also draws connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step (momentum-type) methods in convex optimization.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and an overall well-structured exposition.
- A novel proof technique via an auxiliary linear process, which appears both elegant and useful.
- Improved convergence behavior for consensus relative to previously established Min-Sum results.
- Insightful connections to lifted chains and classical acceleration methods.
- The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appear correct.

Weaknesses and areas for improvement
- Clarity: Some algorithmic quantities are insufficiently defined, and initializations are incomplete. Since the auxiliary process is central to both the method and analysis, the mapping between the algorithmic variables and the auxiliary process should be made explicit.
- Scope: The results are limited to quadratic consensus; implications for broader classes of distributed optimization problems common in machine learning are not discussed.
- Practicality: The paper offers little guidance on when the proposed method is preferable in practice, and what trade-offs it entails versus standard distributed methods.
- Intuition: The role and intuition of the auxiliary process in accelerating Min-Sum Splitting is not clearly articulated.
- Presentation: Numerous typos and minor grammatical errors distract from the presentation (listed below).
- Related work: Several relevant references are missing, especially on distributed (sub)gradient and exact first-order methods.

Requests for clarification and suggestions
- Practicality and use cases: Please comment on when Min-Sum Splitting is advantageous relative to alternatives (e.g., consensus ADMM, distributed gradient methods, EXTRA). What network regimes, objective structures, or communication/computation budgets favor your approach?
- Limitations: What are the main limitations of Min-Sum Splitting (e.g., sensitivity to graph topology, parameter tuning, memory/communication overhead, robustness to noise/asynchrony)?
- Intuition for the auxiliary process: Provide a high-level explanation of how the auxiliary linear process yields acceleration and how it relates to lifting and multi-step methods.
- Beyond quadratics: Can the framework extend to more general convex or composite objectives relevant in machine learning? What are the main obstacles and what partial results might be feasible?
- Relevance to ML: Explain why this acceleration mechanism and its guarantees matter for machine learning applications (e.g., large-scale decentralized training, sensor networks, federated settings).
- Algorithmic clarity:
  - Algorithm 2: Define R{vw} and r{vw}. Ensure that all quantities are introduced before use. It appears that hat{R}^0 and hat{r}^0 are not initialized—please add their initialization.
  - Algorithm 3: Since the auxiliary linear process is the analytical backbone, explicitly identify which variables constitute this process and how they correspond to the original Min-Sum Splitting variables.
  - Notation: On lines 220–221, did you intend to write “Lagrangian” and “Lagrange multipliers” rather than “Laplacian” and “Laplace multipliers”?

Overall assessment
This is a solid theoretical paper that introduces a useful analytic perspective—via an auxiliary linear process—on Min-Sum Splitting for quadratic consensus, delivers nontrivial convergence guarantees, and relates the method to well-studied acceleration mechanisms. The work is well motivated and largely well written, and the main proofs appear sound. However, the paper would benefit from:
- clearer and fully specified algorithms and notation,
- a more thorough discussion of practical relevance and limitations,
- additional intuition for the auxiliary process and its accelerating effect,
- an expanded related-work section, and
- corrections to presentation issues.

Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the paper’s clarity, impact, and relevance for the machine learning community.
