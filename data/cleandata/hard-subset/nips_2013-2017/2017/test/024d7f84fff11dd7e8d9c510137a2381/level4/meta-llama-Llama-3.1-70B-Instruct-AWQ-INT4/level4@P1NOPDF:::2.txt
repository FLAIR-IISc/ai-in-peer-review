This paper studies the convergence rate of the min-sum splitting method on the average consensus problem. In general, the paper reads fine, but the improvement of the result seems not impressive. Detailed comments are as follows.

(1) The paper states that "This rate is optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph. In this case, the convergence time, i.e., the number of iterations required to reach a prescribed level of error accuracy in the dimension of the problem, is..." However, for complete graphs, the linear rate is 0 because everyone converges to the average in 1 step. Moreover, complete graphs are too special to be representative. Therefore, it would be helpful to specify which general category of graphs has a complexity that does not depend on the dimension (number of nodes). Which general category of graphs is considered to have good expansion properties?

(2) In the same paragraph, the literature review should include "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control" by Olshevsky. The convergence rate of this reference should be properly reported (more explanation will be given in comment 8). The reference mentioned here has achieved a rather competitive, or even better, bound compared to the result of the submission.

(3) At the top of page 2, for consensus optimization, important references such as "On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization" by Shi, Ling, Kun, Wu, and Yin, and "Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks" by Scaman, Bach, Bubeck, Lee, and Massoulié should be cited. The authors should also report the state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization and their corresponding (linear) convergence rates.

(4) When discussing lifted graphs and Markov chains, this paper ignores a very related paper "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM" by Franca and Bento.

(5) The content of the last paragraph of page 5 is a long-known fact. The authors should refer to "Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links" by Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew. Furthermore, the connection between those variants and Heavy ball/Nesterov/Polyak is well-known in the field.

(6) There are many important references regarding consensus optimization that the authors have ignored. For example, "Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization" by Shi, Ling, Wu, and Yin, and "Fast distributed gradient methods" by Jakovetic, J Xavier, and Moura.

(7) Proposition 3 seems to be trivial and is a supplementary contribution.

(8) The rate achieved by this paper, D log(D/eps), does not seem to have a significant improvement on the rate D log(1/eps) that has been reached by "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control" (see comment 2). Especially in the worst-case scenario (which holds for all graphs), D~n, the bound is even worse than that achieved in "Linear Time Average Consensus…".

(9) The paper "Linear Time Average Consensus…" improves the bound through Nesterov's acceleration. The reviewer suspects that the so-called "Auxiliary message-passing scheme" proposed by the authors is again a Nesterov's acceleration applied to the min-sum algorithm. This is fine, but the analysis is done for consensus, which boils down to analyzing a linear system and is supposed to be not hard. The contribution of the paper becomes unclear given this situation.

(10) The tiny improvement may come from a careful handling of the spectral gap of graphs. However, the worst-case bound is still O(n) because O(n) = O(D) for the set of all graphs with n nodes.

(11) Line 243 of page 6: The graph is simple, but the author is using directed edges. This is confusing.

(12) Typo at line 220 of page 6: Laplacian → Lagrangian.

The reviewer is satisfied with the authors' response. However, the evaluation score from this reviewer remains the same.
