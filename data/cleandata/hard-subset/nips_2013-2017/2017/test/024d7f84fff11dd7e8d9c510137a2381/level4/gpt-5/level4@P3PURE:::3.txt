This paper introduces an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for distributed consensus optimization. Building on the reparametrization techniques of Ruozzi and Tatikonda (2013), the authors establish convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm on consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. The key analytical tool is an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting updates.

Contributions
- A new analysis of Min-Sum Splitting via an auxiliary linear process, yielding a clean proof technique.
- A Min-Sum-based protocol for consensus that achieves improved convergence rates compared with prior results.
- Conceptual connections between the proposed approach and lifted Markov chains as well as multi-step methods in convex optimization.

Evaluation
The motivation and contributions are clear. The paper is generally well written and easy to follow, though it contains several typos and grammatical errors (listed below). The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear correct. The main limitation of the presentation is the lack of practical guidance on when and how the method should be used in distributed settings, limited intuition for the auxiliary-process viewpoint, and insufficient discussion of relevance to machine learning beyond the quadratic case. Several references are also missing.

Other minor comments
- Lines 220–221: Did you mean “Lagrangian” and “Lagrange multipliers” rather than “Laplacian” and “Laplace multipliers”?
- Algorithmic clarity: The three algorithms introduce quantities that are not always defined when first used. For instance, what are R{vw} and r{vw} in Algorithm 2? In the same algorithm, it appears that the quantities \hat{R}^0 and \hat{r}^0 are never initialized. Since the auxiliary linear process is central to both the analysis and the main idea, Algorithm 3 should clearly identify which variables constitute this auxiliary process and how they map to the Min-Sum Splitting variables.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Practicality and use cases: Although the paper is theoretical, please discuss when this approach is preferable to other distributed consensus/optimization methods (e.g., EXTRA, gradient tracking, accelerated gossip), considering communication/computation trade-offs, robustness to noise, memory requirements, and synchronization assumptions.
- Limitations: What are the main limitations of Min-Sum Splitting (e.g., sensitivity to graph topology, parameter tuning, initialization, scalability to large networks, handling of asynchrony or time-varying graphs)?
- Intuition for the auxiliary process: Provide more intuition for why introducing the auxiliary linear process leads to acceleration and sharper analysis. For example, how does it relate to momentum, lifting, or spectral gap improvements?
- Beyond quadratics: The results focus on quadratic objectives. Can the framework be extended to more general convex or smooth objectives commonly encountered in machine learning? If not directly, what are the key obstacles (e.g., nonlinearity of messages, loss of linearity in the auxiliary process), and are there approximate variants?
- Relevance to machine learning: Please articulate why this approach is particularly interesting to the ML community (e.g., implications for decentralized training, federated learning with limited communication, accelerated consensus for averaging gradients or model parameters).

Summary
This is a solid theoretical contribution that establishes convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting using a novel auxiliary-process-based analysis and clarifies connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods. The results are interesting and technically sound, but the paper would benefit from: (i) clearer exposition of the algorithms and variables, (ii) a stronger discussion of practicality and limitations, (iii) additional intuition for the auxiliary process and its acceleration effects, (iv) broader context and references, and (v) explicit motivation and impact for machine learning applications.
