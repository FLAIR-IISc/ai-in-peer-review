This paper presents an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for solving distributed consensus optimization problems. Leveraging reparametrization techniques from Ruozzi and Tatikonda (2013), the authors establish convergence rates for their Min-Sum Splitting algorithm on problems with quadratic objective functions. The core of their analysis relies on a novel proof technique: the construction of an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the algorithm. The main contributions are threefold: (i) a new analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm via this auxiliary process, (ii) a Min-Sum protocol for consensus that demonstrates faster convergence than previously established results, and (iii) an insightful connection between the proposed method, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step methods in convex optimization (e.g., Heavy-ball, Nesterov's acceleration).

The motivation and contributions of the paper are clearly articulated. The exposition is generally well-written and easy to follow. The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appear to be sound. The introduction of the auxiliary process is an elegant and powerful tool for the analysis.

While the paper presents interesting theoretical ideas, there are several areas that require improvement to strengthen the submission.

Additionally, there are some minor points of confusion:
- **Terminology:** In lines 220 and 221, the terms "Laplacian" and "Laplace multipliers" are used. Is it possible the authors meant "Lagrangian" and "Lagrange multipliers"? Please clarify.
- **Algorithm Descriptions:** The descriptions of the algorithms could be more self-contained. For example, in Algorithm 2, the quantities `R{vw}` and `r{vw}` are used without definition. Furthermore, `\hat{R}^0` and `\hat{r}^0` are not explicitly initialized. Given that the auxiliary linear process is central to the paper's contribution, the authors should clearly state which variables in Algorithm 3 correspond to this process to make the connection explicit for the reader.

**Missing References and Context:**

The paper would be strengthened by positioning the work more thoroughly within the existing literature on distributed optimization. Several key references appear to be missing:
- **(Sub)gradient Methods (Lines 41, 43):** The discussion on first-order methods for consensus optimization could be enriched by including foundational and more recent works, such as:
    - Bertsekas and Tsitsiklis, "Parallel and distributed computation: numerical methods," 1989
    - Nedic and Ozdaglar, "Distributed subgradient methods for multi-agent optimization," 2009
    - Shi et al., "EXTRA: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization," 2015
- **Acceleration Methods:** The connection to accelerated methods is a key contribution. It is therefore essential to cite the original seminal works when mentioning them, specifically Polyak's Heavy-ball method (Line 229) and Nesterov's acceleration schemes (Line 232).
- The reference on Line 170 ("The original literature…") is incomplete.

**Broader Impact and Discussion:**

The paper is presented as a purely theoretical contribution, but its value would be significantly enhanced by a discussion of its broader implications. I would encourage the authors to address the following points in a revised manuscript:
1.  **Practicality and Use Cases:** While theoretical in nature, the paper should comment on the practical aspects of the proposed algorithm. When would this method be preferable to other distributed optimization techniques like ADMM or distributed (sub)gradient descent? What are the communication and computation trade-offs?
2.  **Limitations:** What are the key limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method? For instance, how does it scale with network size or topology? Does its performance degrade under certain conditions?
3.  **Intuition:** What is the intuition behind using an auxiliary process? A brief, high-level explanation could help readers grasp why this particular proof technique is effective and what it represents.
4.  **Generalizability:** The analysis is restricted to consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. This is a common starting point for analysis, but its direct applicability in machine learning can be limited. Can this framework be extended to more general convex (or non-convex) objectives that are prevalent in machine learning, such as logistic regression or training neural networks? A discussion of the challenges involved would be valuable.
5.  **Relevance to Machine Learning:** Given the NIPS audience, it is crucial for the authors to articulate why this specific approach is of interest to the machine learning community. What types of large-scale ML problems could benefit from this accelerated message-passing framework?

**Conclusion:**

This paper introduces an interesting and technically sound approach for accelerating the Min-Sum protocol for consensus optimization. The use of an auxiliary process for the convergence analysis is novel and elegant. However, the paper's impact is currently limited by a lack of discussion on the method's practical implications, limitations, and relevance to the broader machine learning community. Addressing the points on clarity, missing context, and the broader impact questions would substantially improve the quality and significance of this submission.
