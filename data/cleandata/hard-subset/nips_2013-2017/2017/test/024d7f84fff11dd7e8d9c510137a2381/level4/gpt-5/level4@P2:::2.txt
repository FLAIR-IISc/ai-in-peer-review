Summary
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for average consensus. It shows that while standard Min-Sum may diverge on graphs with cycles, an appropriately parameterized splitting variant converges to the exact consensus value. The authors derive a linear edge-based message-passing representation, construct an auxiliary node-based two-step linear process that tracks the output, and analyze its spectral properties. With a specific parameter choice, they prove a “subdiffusive” accelerated rate that matches shift-register/Chebyshev methods, giving O(D log(D/ε)) iterations on cycles and tori, where D is graph diameter. They also relate the mechanism to lifted (non-reversible) Markov chains and to multi-step first-order methods (heavy-ball/Nesterov).

Relation to prior work includes Min-Sum/GBP and convergence conditions (e.g., Moallemi–Van Roy; Malioutov et al.; Ruozzi–Tatikonda), consensus and accelerated gossip (e.g., Xiao–Boyd; Olshevsky; Jung–Shah–Shin; Chen–Lovász–Pak), and distributed optimization (e.g., ADMM and its linear rates; EXTRA; fast multi-step gradient). The paper extends earlier Min-Sum-based approaches for consensus (e.g., Moallemi–Van Roy; Ruozzi–Tatikonda; Moallemi–Van Roy’s consensus propagation; the min-sum variant in [27]) by providing a general convergence-rate analysis for splitting and by making explicit connections to acceleration mechanisms.

Strengths
- Clear linearization and auxiliary node-based formulation that links an edge-based BP protocol to a two-step state-space recursion, enabling tractable spectral analysis.
- Establishes convergence and accelerated rates for Min-Sum Splitting across general graph topologies, addressing gaps left in earlier Min-Sum consensus work.
- Conceptual connections to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods are insightful and potentially useful beyond this setting.
- Analysis recovers shift-register rates with an explicit parameter choice tied to the spectrum of W.

Weaknesses
- Acceleration over best-known methods appears modest: O(D log(D/ε)) versus O(D log(1/ε)) achieved elsewhere (e.g., Olshevsky; shift-register/Chebyshev/Nesterov-based schemes).
- Several important related works are missing or under-discussed, especially on distributed optimization with linear rates and lifting/ADMM connections.
- Some claims about “optimality” on expanders and discussion of complete graphs could be more precise; the notion of “good” graphs and dimension-free rates should be clarified.
- Parts of the contribution (e.g., one proposition) seem straightforward; the novelty may primarily lie in tailoring known acceleration ideas to Min-Sum Splitting.
- Presentation issues: minor typos and notational choices that may confuse readers (e.g., directed edges on a simple undirected graph).

Detailed comments and suggestions
1) On “good” graphs and dimension-free rates: Complete graphs converge in one step with standard averaging weights; they are special and not representative. Please clarify the general class of graphs (e.g., expanders) for which convergence time is dimension-free (Θ(log 1/ε)) and define “good expansion” more precisely.

2) Literature on linear-time consensus: Please cite and compare carefully with Olshevsky, “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control.” Report the precise rate and assumptions; this result is competitive and in some regimes stronger than your O(D log(D/ε)) bound.

3) Consensus optimization with linear rates: Please add and position
- Shi, Ling, Yuan, Wu, Yin, “On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization.”
- Scaman et al., “Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks.”
Provide a concise summary of state-of-the-art linear rates and how your result compares in scope and assumptions.

4) Lifting and ADMM: The related paper “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM” (França and Bento) should be cited in your discussion of lifting; it is closely aligned with your perspective on directionality and acceleration.

5) Multi-step acceleration lineage: The paragraph connecting your auxiliary scheme to momentum/shift-register methods should also cite Rabbat–Nowak–Bucklew, “Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links,” and standard heavy-ball/Nesterov links in the consensus literature.

6) Additional missing references on accelerated consensus/optimization:
- EXTRA (Shi, Ling, Wu, Yin).
- Fast distributed gradient methods (Jakovetić, Xavier, Moura).
Please situate your rate and assumptions accordingly.

7) Proposition 3 (as numbered in the submission) appears to be a straightforward consequence of the linear formulation; its contribution is minor relative to the main theorem.

8) Rate comparison: Your O(D log(D/ε)) bound is close but not stronger than O(D log(1/ε)) (e.g., Olshevsky) and may be worse in the worst case where D ≍ n. Please clarify the regimes where your bound is tight or advantageous, and whether the extra log D factor is intrinsic to your analysis or can be removed.

9) Nature of the auxiliary scheme: The proposed auxiliary two-step recursion appears closely related to applying Nesterov/shift-register acceleration to a linear consensus operator. If so, please make this equivalence explicit (including parameter mapping), and delineate what is new specifically about the Min-Sum Splitting perspective beyond offering an alternative derivation.

10) Worst-case scaling: Since D = Θ(n) in general, the worst-case bound remains O(n log(n/ε)). Please discuss whether stronger graph-dependent constants or tuning can improve the extra log factor, and whether your approach can match the best-known O(D log(1/ε)) rates.

11) Notation: You refer to a simple undirected graph while operating on directed edges (messages). Please clarify this modeling choice early to avoid confusion.

12) Typos/minor issues: Line 220 on page 6: “Laplacian” should be “Lagrangian.” Please proofread the claims around complete graphs and the definition of “optimal” rates on expanders.

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality: The technical development appears sound; the main theorem is well-motivated and the auxiliary formulation is clean. Stronger empirical or numerical illustration would help but is not essential for this theoretical paper.
- Clarity: Generally well written, but the literature review is incomplete and some statements could be more precise. A tighter positioning versus O(D log(1/ε)) results would help readers.
- Originality: Moderate. The key ideas (lifting/directionality, two-step acceleration) are known; the novelty lies in articulating them through Min-Sum Splitting and proving general rates in this framework.
- Significance: Incremental. The analysis is of interest to researchers working on BP and distributed optimization, but the practical advantage over existing accelerated consensus methods is limited.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear theoretical treatment of Min-Sum Splitting with general graph rates.
- Nice unification of BP splittings, lifted walks, and multi-step methods.
- Addresses convergence-rate questions left open in prior Min-Sum consensus work.

Cons for acceptance
- Modest improvement relative to state-of-the-art accelerated consensus.
- Several important related works are missing or under-explained.
- Some components are relatively straightforward; novelty may feel limited.

After rebuttal
The authors addressed many of the literature and clarity concerns satisfactorily. My overall evaluation, however, remains unchanged.
