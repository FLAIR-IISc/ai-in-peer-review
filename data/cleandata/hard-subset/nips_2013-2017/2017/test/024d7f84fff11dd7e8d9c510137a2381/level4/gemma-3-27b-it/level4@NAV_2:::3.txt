In this paper, the authors present an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for solving consensus problems in distributed optimization. The authors leverage the reparametrization techniques proposed in [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013] and establish convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. The core analytical tool is the construction of an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

The main contributions of the paper can be summarized as follows: (i) a novel analysis of the Min-Sum splitting algorithm based on a new proof technique utilizing an auxiliary process, (ii) the design of a Min-Sum protocol for consensus achieving improved convergence compared to prior results, and (iii) the demonstration of connections between the proposed method and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods in convex optimization. 

The motivation and contributions are clearly articulated. The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow, though it contains several minor typos and grammatical errors (listed below). The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appear to be logically sound.

It would be valuable if the authors addressed the following points:
- While primarily theoretical, a discussion of the practical applicability of the method, and scenarios where it would be preferred over other distributed consensus algorithms, would be beneficial.
- A clear articulation of the limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method would strengthen the paper.
- Expanding on the intuition behind the auxiliary process and its connection to the accelerated convergence would enhance understanding.
- The results are currently presented for consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. A discussion of potential extensions to more general consensus problems common in Machine Learning would be worthwhile.
- More explicitly stating the relevance of this work to the Machine Learning community would broaden its appeal.

In summary, this paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis of an accelerated Min-Sum variant for consensus, introducing a novel proof technique and establishing connections to related work. The ideas presented are interesting and contribute to the understanding of accelerated distributed optimization. However, further discussion of the method's practicality, intuition, and relevance to machine learning would enhance its impact.

Arguments for acceptance:
* The paper presents a novel theoretical result with a clear contribution to the field of distributed optimization.
* The analysis is rigorous and the proofs appear correct.
* The connection to existing methods like lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods is insightful.
* The use of the auxiliary process is a clever technique for analyzing the convergence behavior.

Arguments against acceptance:
* The paper is highly theoretical and lacks a discussion of practical implications.
* Some minor typos and grammatical errors detract from the overall polish.
* The connection to machine learning is not explicitly highlighted, potentially limiting its appeal to that community.
* The lack of thorough exploration of limitations weakens the presentation.

Overall assessment: Accept with minor revisions. Addressing the comments regarding practicality, clarity, and references would significantly strengthen the paper.
