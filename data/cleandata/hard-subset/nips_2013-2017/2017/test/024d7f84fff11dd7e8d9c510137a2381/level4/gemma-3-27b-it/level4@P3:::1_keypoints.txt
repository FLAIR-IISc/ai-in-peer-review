- Paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for distributed consensus, connecting message-passing to acceleration techniques.  
- Establishes a novel link between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis showing subdiffusive (accelerated) convergence rates for consensus.  
- Well-written and clearly situated within distributed optimization and message-passing literature.  
- Mathematical derivations appear sound and the contribution seems novel even to non-specialists.  
- Strength: clear novelty in connecting disparate acceleration frameworks to Min-Sum Splitting.  
- Strength: strong theoretical rigor in convergence-rate analysis.  
- Strength: high clarity of presentation and comprehensive related-work coverage.  
- Strength: potential practical significance for distributed ML and sensor networks due to faster convergence.  
- Weakness: explanation of “splitting” (use of δ and Γvw) is unclear since those are defined as real numbers, not cardinalities.  
- Weakness: missing discussion of computational complexity and implementation costs compared to existing methods.  
- Weakness: no empirical validation or experiments on representative graph topologies.  
- For acceptance: novel, theoretically sound contribution with impactful insights and improved rates.  
- Against acceptance: practical performance is unverified empirically and a minor clarity issue remains.  
- Overall: strong accept recommendation; address minor clarification and consider adding empirical results in a future version.
