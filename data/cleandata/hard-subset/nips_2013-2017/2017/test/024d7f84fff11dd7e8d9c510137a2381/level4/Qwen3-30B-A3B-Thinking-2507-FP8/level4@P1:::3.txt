

This paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution to distributed optimization, specifically addressing the consensus problem through a modified Min-Sum message-passing protocol called Min-Sum Splitting. The authors demonstrate that while ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge for general graphs, a properly parameterized version of Min-Sum Splitting achieves accelerated convergence rates for consensus problems. The work establishes a significant connection between Min-Sum-based algorithms and two established approaches to acceleration: lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization. The paper builds on the reparametrization framework introduced in [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013] and extends the theoretical understanding of Min-Sum algorithms beyond previous work on convergence guarantees.

The paper makes three main contributions: (1) a novel proof technique using an auxiliary linear process to analyze Min-Sum Splitting, (2) a Min-Sum protocol for consensus that achieves better convergence rates than previously established results, and (3) a theoretical connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods. The authors provide a clear theoretical analysis showing that proper tuning of splitting parameters yields a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods, achieving O(D log(D/ε)) convergence for cycle graphs and tori, where D is the graph diameter.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making the technical content accessible. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and existing accelerated methods is particularly valuable, as it bridges two previously separate lines of research. The theoretical analysis is sound, with Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appearing to be correctly established. The proof technique using an auxiliary linear process represents a significant methodological contribution that could be useful for analyzing other message-passing algorithms.

Regarding strengths, the paper makes a clear theoretical contribution to understanding distributed optimization algorithms. The connection to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods provides valuable context for the proposed approach, demonstrating how Min-Sum Splitting relates to established acceleration techniques. The convergence rate analysis for general graph topologies is particularly noteworthy, as previous work [27] only established results for specific graph classes.

The paper has several minor weaknesses that could be addressed. There are a few grammatical errors and typos (e.g., "double-stochastic" should be "doubly-stochastic," "can be casted" should be "can be cast"). The explanation of variables in Algorithms 2 and 3 could be clearer, particularly regarding the initialization of R̂0 and r̂0 in Algorithm 2. Some key references are missing, particularly regarding gradient-based distributed methods for consensus optimization (e.g., Bertsekas and Tsitsiklis, 1989; Sundhar Ram et al., 2009; Shi et al., 2015). The discussion of practical implications is limited, and it would be helpful to have more concrete guidance on when this method should be preferred over existing alternatives.

The paper's significance lies in its theoretical contribution to understanding message-passing algorithms for distributed optimization. While the focus is on quadratic objective functions, the authors note that the framework could be extended to more general problems. However, the connection to practical machine learning applications could be strengthened. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of when Min-Sum Splitting would be advantageous compared to other distributed optimization methods, and what limitations the approach might have in practice.

The paper is technically sound (Quality), with well-supported theoretical claims. The clarity is generally good, though some algorithm descriptions could be improved. The originality is strong, as the connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods represent novel insights not previously established in the literature. The significance is moderate to high for the theoretical community working on distributed optimization, though the practical implications for machine learning applications could be better articulated.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution with novel proof technique; Clear connection to established acceleration methods; Sound theoretical analysis; Well-structured paper.

Con: Limited discussion of practical implications; Some minor grammatical errors; Missing key references; Variable initialization could be clarified.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of distributed optimization algorithms. The connection between Min-Sum message-passing and established acceleration techniques is particularly insightful and should be of interest to the NIPS community. While the practical implications could be better developed, the theoretical results are significant and well-supported. The paper should be accepted for presentation at NIPS, as it advances our theoretical understanding of distributed optimization algorithms in a meaningful way.
