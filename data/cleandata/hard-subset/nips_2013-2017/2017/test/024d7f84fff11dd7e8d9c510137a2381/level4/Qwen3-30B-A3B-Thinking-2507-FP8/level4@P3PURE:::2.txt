

Review:  
This paper investigates the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method for the average consensus problem. While the paper is generally well-written, the claimed improvement appears modest and requires stronger justification. Below are detailed comments:  

(1) The paper states, "This rate is optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph. In this case the convergence time... scales as the dimension of the problem." For complete graphs, convergence occurs in one step (linear rate 0), making this case trivial and unrepresentative. The paper must clarify: *Which general class of graphs achieves dimension-independent complexity?* and *What constitutes a "good" expansion property in this context?*  

(2) The literature review should include Olshevsky’s "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control" (2015). Its convergence rate must be properly compared to the submission’s result. This reference establishes a competitive or superior bound, undermining the novelty claim.  

(3) For consensus optimization, key references must be cited:  
- Shi et al., "On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization" (2015)  
- Scaman et al., "Optimal Algorithms for Smooth and Strongly Convex Distributed Optimization in Networks" (2018)  
The authors should also summarize state-of-the-art algorithms and their linear convergence rates.  

(4) The discussion of lifted graphs and Markov chains omits Franca and Bento’s "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM" (2018), which is highly relevant.  

(5) The final paragraph of page 5 states a well-known fact about consensus variants. This should reference Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew’s "Generalized Consensus Computation in Networked Systems with Erasure Links" (2004), and the connection to Heavy Ball/Nesterov/Polyak is established in the literature.  

(6) Critical references on consensus optimization are missing:  
- Shi et al., "Extra: An Exact First-Order Algorithm for Decentralized Consensus Optimization" (2015)  
- Jakovetic et al., "Fast Distributed Gradient Methods" (2014)  

(7) Proposition 3 appears trivial and constitutes a supplementary contribution rather than a core advance.  

(8) The claimed rate \(D \log(D/\epsilon)\) does not significantly improve upon the \(D \log(1/\epsilon)\) bound in Olshevsky (2015). For worst-case graphs (where \(D = \Theta(n)\)), this bound is inferior to Olshevsky’s result.  

(9) Olshevsky (2015) achieves its bound via Nesterov acceleration. The authors’ "Auxiliary message-passing scheme" likely re-applies Nesterov acceleration to the min-sum algorithm. Since the analysis for consensus reduces to a linear system (a straightforward task), the paper’s contribution is unclear.  

(10) The modest improvement may stem from refined spectral gap handling, but the worst-case bound remains \(O(n)\) (since \(D = \Theta(n)\) for all graphs with \(n\) nodes), negating the claimed dimension independence.  

(11) Line 243 (page 6) uses directed edges for a simple graph, which is confusing.  

(12) Line 220 (page 6) contains a typo: "Laplacian" → "Lagrangian."  

After rebuttal, the authors’ response addresses some concerns, but the evaluation score remains unchanged. The paper’s contribution lacks sufficient novelty relative to existing work, particularly Olshevsky (2015) and related acceleration techniques.
