This paper applies an accelerated variant of the min-sum algorithm, known as min-sum splitting, to the distributed consensus problem. The manuscript is clearly written and situates the contribution well within the existing literature. To the best of my knowledge (though I am not a specialist in this area), the results are novel and represent a meaningful conceptual advance. In particular, the paper elucidates a new connection between min-sum methods and lifted Markov chain techniques.

One point in the presentation is unclear. On page 4, when discussing the equivalent objective function minimized by the min-sum algorithm to obtain the min-sum splitting scheme, the authors write: “...splitting each term phi_vw into Gamma_vw terms, and each term phi_v into delta terms...” As introduced on the previous page, Gamma_vw and delta are real numbers. It is therefore unclear how to interpret “into ... terms.” Are Gamma_vw and delta intended as integer multiplicities (i.e., numbers of replicated factors), or as real-valued weights that define a decomposition (e.g., a convex or linear splitting) of each cost term into multiple components? If the latter, what precisely constitutes a “term,” how are the weights defined and normalized, and how does this decomposition map to the factor-graph representation used by min-sum? Clarifying whether the splitting denotes replication, scaling, or a weighted partition—together with the associated constraints (integrality, sum-to-one conditions, etc.)—would significantly improve readability. A brief formal definition or a small illustrative example would resolve the ambiguity.
