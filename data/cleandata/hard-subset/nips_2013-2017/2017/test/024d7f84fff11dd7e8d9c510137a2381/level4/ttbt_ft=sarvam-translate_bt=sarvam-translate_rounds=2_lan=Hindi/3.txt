In this paper, the authors present a quick version of the Min-Sum Message-Passing Protocol to solve consensus problems in distributed optimization. They use the re-parameterization techniques proposed in [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013] and establish convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting Algorithm to solve consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. The main tool used for analysis is the construction of an auxiliary linear process that tracks the development of the Min-Sum Splitting Algorithm.

The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows: (i) providing an analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting Algorithm using a new proof technique based on the introduction of an auxiliary process, (ii) designing a Min-Sum protocol for consensus problems that achieves better convergence than previously established results, and (iii) showing the relationship between the proposed method and multi-step methods in convex optimization and leveraged Markov chains.

The motivation and contributions of this paper are clear. The paper is well-written and easy to understand, however, it contains several typos and grammatical errors (listed below). The proofs for premises 1 and 2, and theorem 1 seem correct.

It would be interesting and useful if the authors could answer/comment on the following and address it in the paper:
- Although this paper is a theoretical paper, the authors should comment on the practicality of the method, and when such a method should be used, as opposed to other distributed methods for consensus optimization.
- What are the limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method?
- What is the intuition behind using the auxiliary process in the Min-Sum Splitting method?
- The results given in this paper are for consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. Can this framework be extended to solve more general consensus problems that often arise in machine learning?
- The authors should also clearly state why such an approach is interesting in the context of machine learning and for the machine learning community.

In summary, this paper is a purely theoretical paper in which the authors establish convergence rates using a new proof technique and show the relationship between their method and well-established methods in the literature. Overall, the ideas presented in this paper are interesting, however, the practicality of the method and the intuition behind the results are missing, as is some justification for the importance of this result for the machine learning community.
