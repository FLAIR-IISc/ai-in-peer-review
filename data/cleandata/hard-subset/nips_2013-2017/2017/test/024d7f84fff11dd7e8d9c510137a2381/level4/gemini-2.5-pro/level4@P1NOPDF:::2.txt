This paper studies the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method for the average consensus problem. While the paper is generally well-written, the improvement over existing work appears to be marginal.

Detailed comments are as follows:

1.  The central claim is a convergence rate of D log(D/ε), which is compared to the established D log(1/ε) rate from Olshevsky ("Linear Time Average Consensus..."). This improvement is incremental, as it only introduces a log(D) factor inside the logarithm. Moreover, for graphs where the diameter D is on the order of the number of nodes n (the worst-case), this new bound is potentially inferior to the existing one. While the marginal improvement may stem from a more refined analysis of the graph's spectral properties, it does not alter the fact that the worst-case complexity is still O(n).

2.  The paper's novelty is a concern. The work by Olshevsky improves the convergence bound by applying Nesterov's acceleration. The "Auxiliary message-passing scheme" proposed in this paper appears to be a similar application of a Nesterov-style momentum technique to the min-sum algorithm. While this is a valid approach, its application to the average consensus problem—which reduces to analyzing a linear system—is a relatively standard exercise. This makes the conceptual contribution of the work unclear.

3.  The paper asserts that the proposed rate is "optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph." This example is not representative. On a complete graph, average consensus is achieved in a single step, making it an unsuitable benchmark for asymptotic analysis. The authors should provide a more precise definition of the general class of graphs they consider to have "good expansion properties," for which the claimed dimension-independent complexity holds.

4.  The literature review is incomplete and overlooks several key related works:
    *   **Consensus Optimization:** The discussion at the top of page 2 is missing crucial references such as Shi et al. ("On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM...") and Scaman et al. ("Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex..."). A proper comparison to the state-of-the-art algorithms and their convergence rates for consensus optimization is necessary. Other important papers in this area, like Shi et al. ("EXTRA: An exact first-order algorithm...") and Jakovetic et al. ("Fast distributed gradient methods"), have also been omitted.
    *   **Lifted Graphs:** The discussion of lifted graphs and Markov chains should cite the highly relevant paper by Franca and Bento ("Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM").
    *   **Momentum Methods:** The connection between certain algorithm variants and momentum methods (e.g., Heavy ball, Nesterov, Polyak), which is discussed in the last paragraph of page 5, is a well-established fact in the field. This section should acknowledge prior work, for instance, by citing Rabbat et al. ("Generalized consensus computation...").

5.  The contribution of Proposition 3 seems marginal. The result is quite direct, and its significance to the paper's main thesis is not clearly explained.

6.  There is a potential inconsistency on page 6, line 243. The graph is described as simple, yet the notation appears to use directed edges. This should be clarified.

7.  A minor typo exists on page 6, line 220: "Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian".
