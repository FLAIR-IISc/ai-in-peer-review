

In this paper, the authors introduce an accelerated version of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for addressing consensus problems in distributed optimization. They employ reparametrization techniques from [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013] and derive convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. The primary analytical tool is the construction of an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

The key contributions of the paper are: (i) presenting an analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm using a novel proof technique based on an auxiliary process, (ii) proposing a Min-Sum protocol for consensus problems that achieves superior convergence rates compared to prior results, and (iii) establishing connections between the proposed method, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The paper's motivation and contributions are well-defined, and the writing is clear and accessible. However, it contains several typographical and grammatical errors (detailed below). The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 are verified as correct.

Additional minor comments:
- Lines 220 and 221: Are "Laplacian" and "Laplace multipliers" intended to be "Lagrangian" and "Lagrange multipliers"?
- The paper presents three algorithms, but key quantities are inconsistently explained. For instance, the meaning of $R_{vw}$ and $r_{vw}$ in Algorithm 2 is unclear. Additionally, $\hat{R}^0$ and $\hat{r}^0$ in Algorithm 2 lack initialization. Since the auxiliary linear process is central to the analysis, the correspondence between its variables and Algorithm 3 should be explicitly stated.

The authors should address the following points to enhance the paper:
- Discuss the practical applicability of the method and its comparative advantages over existing distributed consensus techniques.
- Clarify the limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting approach.
- Explain the intuition behind the auxiliary process used in the analysis.
- Explore whether the framework extends to non-quadratic consensus problems common in machine learning.
- Explicitly justify the relevance of this work to the machine learning community.

In summary, this is a theoretically sound paper that establishes convergence rates via a novel proof technique and connects to established optimization methods. While the ideas are interesting, the paper lacks discussion of practical utility, intuitive explanations for the results, and a clear justification for its significance within machine learning.
