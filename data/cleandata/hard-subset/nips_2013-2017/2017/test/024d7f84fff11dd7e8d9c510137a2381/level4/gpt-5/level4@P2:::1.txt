This paper studies the application of the Min-Sum message-passing algorithm with splitting to the distributed consensus (network averaging) problem. The authors show that while standard Min-Sum may fail to converge on graphs with cycles, the Splitting variant converges to the correct solution. With an appropriate choice of tuning parameters, Min-Sum Splitting achieves accelerated, subdiffusive convergence rates that match shift-register methods. The paper also highlights conceptual links between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted nonreversible Markov chains, and multi-step first-order optimization methods, and analyzes convergence by introducing an auxiliary node-level linear process that tracks the edge-based updates.

Strengths:
- The paper is clearly written and well-situated relative to prior work.
- To the best of my knowledge (though I am not a specialist), the results are novel and represent a qualitative advance.
- The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and lifted Markov chains is particularly interesting and, as far as I know, new.
- Establishing accelerated convergence rates and relating them to the spectrum of an associated matrix is valuable and timely.

Clarification needed:
- On page 4, when introducing the “equivalent objective” underlying Min-Sum Splitting, the manuscript states that it is obtained by “splitting each term phi_vw into Gamma_vw terms, and each term phi_v into delta terms.” However, delta and Gamma_vw were previously defined as real-valued parameters. It is therefore unclear how to interpret this splitting literally when these parameters are non-integers. Please clarify the intended meaning of this reparametrization for real (non-integer) choices of delta and Gamma (e.g., as a formal scaling of potentials rather than a literal replication of terms), and specify any assumptions under which Algorithm 1 remains well-defined in this case.

Overall, the contribution is clearly presented, appears technically sound, and makes a meaningful conceptual connection to accelerated consensus via both lifted chains and multi-step methods. Aside from the above point about the interpretation of splitting with real-valued parameters, the presentation is strong.
