This paper presents an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol designed for solving consensus problems within distributed optimization. Leveraging reparametrization techniques from Ruozzi and Tatikonda [2013], the authors establish convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. A key analytical tool is the construction of an auxiliary linear process that models the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

The primary contributions of this work are threefold: (i) a novel proof technique, based on the introduction of an auxiliary process, for analyzing the Min-Sum splitting algorithm; (ii) a Min-Sum protocol for consensus that achieves improved convergence compared to existing results; and (iii) the demonstration of connections between the proposed method and lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The motivation and contributions are clearly articulated, and the paper is generally well-written and accessible. However, several typos and grammatical errors were identified (detailed below). The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1, appear correct.

**Minor Comments and Requests for Clarification:**

*   Lines 220 and 221: Please verify if "Lagrangian" and "Lagrange multipliers" are more appropriate terms than "Laplacian" and "Laplace multipliers" in this context.
*   The paper introduces three algorithms, but the meaning of certain quantities is unclear. For example, what are R<sub>vw</sub> and r<sub>vw</sub> in Algorithm 2? Furthermore, the initial values \hat{R}^0 and \hat{r}^0 appear to be undefined in Algorithm 2. Crucially, given the central role of the auxiliary linear process, the authors should explicitly map the variables of this process to those presented in Algorithm 3.

**Missing References:**

*   Lines 41 and 43: The discussion of (sub)gradient methods for consensus optimization would benefit from inclusion of the following references: Bertsekas and Tsitsiklis, *Parallel and distributed computation: numerical methods* (1989); Srinivasan et al., *Incremental stochastic subgradient algorithms for convex optimization* (2009); and Shi, *Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization* (2015) (among others).
*   Line 170: The phrase "The original literature…" is vague and should be clarified with specific citations.
*   Line 229: Relevant work by Polyak on the heavy-ball method should be cited.
*   Line 232: Work by Nesterov on accelerated gradient methods is missing.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   Although this is a theoretical work, the authors should discuss the practical implications of their method. Under what conditions would this approach be preferable to other existing distributed consensus algorithms?
*   What are the inherent limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method?
*   Providing intuition behind the use of the auxiliary process would significantly enhance the paper’s clarity and impact.
*   The current results focus on quadratic objective functions. Could this framework be generalized to handle the more complex consensus problems commonly encountered in Machine Learning?
*   A clear statement articulating the relevance of this work to the Machine Learning community would be beneficial. Why should researchers in this field be interested in this approach?

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically sound analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, establishing convergence rates through a novel proof technique and highlighting connections to existing methods. The ideas are interesting, but the paper lacks a discussion of practical considerations, intuitive explanations, and a clear justification of its significance for the Machine Learning community. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the paper.
