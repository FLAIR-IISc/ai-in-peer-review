This paper introduces an accelerated version of the Min-Sum message-passing algorithm for the distributed consensus problem. The core idea is to apply the Min-Sum Splitting framework, a reparametrization technique, to the network averaging problem (consensus with a quadratic objective). The authors prove that while the standard Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge on graphs with cycles, their proposed method not only converges to the correct solution but can also achieve an accelerated, "subdiffusive" rate of convergence with a proper choice of tuning parameters.

The main contributions are threefold. First, the paper provides the first convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. The analysis is based on a novel and elegant proof technique that introduces an auxiliary linear process on the graph's nodes to track the evolution of the primary message-passing algorithm, which operates on the graph's directed edges. This simplifies the analysis compared to prior work. Second, the authors design a Min-Sum protocol for consensus that achieves a square-root improvement in convergence time over standard "diffusive" methods on certain graph topologies, matching the rates of shift-register methods. Third, and perhaps most significantly, the work establishes a clear theoretical connection between Min-Sum message passing, lifted Markov chain techniques, and multi-step accelerated methods from convex optimization. This insight provides a new perspective on acceleration mechanisms in distributed optimization.

### **Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength lies in its novel conceptual contribution connecting three distinct areas of research: Belief Propagation-style algorithms, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods. Showing that the "splitting" parameters in a message-passing scheme can be tuned to induce momentum-like acceleration is a beautiful and important insight. This could inspire new approaches to designing accelerated distributed algorithms.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears to be sound. The introduction of an auxiliary process to analyze the algorithm's dynamics is a clever technique that makes the analysis more tractable and allows for the derivation of convergence rates on general graphs. This is a clear improvement over previous analyses of Min-Sum for consensus, which were restricted to specific graph types.
*   **Clarity of Motivation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly motivated. It does a good job of positioning the problem within the context of distributed optimization and explaining the limitations of existing methods that this work aims to overcome.

### **Weaknesses**

*   **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is restricted to the consensus problem with quadratic objective functions (i.e., network averaging). While this is a fundamental problem, the paper's impact on the machine learning community would be significantly greater if the authors discussed the potential for extending these results to more general convex consensus problems (e.g., for training distributed logistic regression or SVMs). The paper currently feels like a specialized theoretical result, and its broader applicability is not addressed.
*   **Lack of Practical Discussion:** As a purely theoretical paper, it lacks a discussion of the practical implications. The authors should comment on when their method might be preferable to other well-known accelerated distributed methods (e.g., distributed ADMM, Nesterov-accelerated gradient descent). A discussion of limitations, computational complexity, and communication overhead would strengthen the paper and make the contribution more concrete.
*   **Clarity in Algorithm Presentation:** The presentation of the algorithms could be improved for reproducibility. For instance, in Algorithm 2, the initial conditions `R̂⁰` and `r̂⁰` are used but not explicitly initialized. Furthermore, the variables in the key auxiliary process (Algorithm 3) should be more clearly defined and their roles explained to improve reader understanding.
*   **Incomplete Literature Context:** The paper is missing several key references in the areas of distributed optimization and accelerated methods. Citing foundational work would better situate the contribution within the broader historical context of the field.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

The paper is promising, but addressing the following points would substantially improve its quality and impact.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Generalization:** Could the authors discuss the main challenges in extending this framework to general strongly-convex objective functions? Even a high-level discussion of the obstacles would be valuable for future work and would help the reader gauge the potential impact of this approach.
2.  **Practicality and Comparison:** Please add a discussion on the practical aspects of the proposed method. How should one choose the matrix `W` in practice? Since obtaining `ρ(W)` requires global information, how does this affect the algorithm's distributed nature? How does the method compare in terms of per-iteration complexity or communication cost to algorithms like EXTRA or accelerated ADMM?
3.  **Relevance to Machine Learning:** The introduction mentions large-scale machine learning as a key application. It would be beneficial to explicitly state why this Min-Sum-based approach is of particular interest to this community, beyond providing another way to solve consensus. Are there specific problem structures where it might excel?
4.  **Algorithm Clarity:** Please clarify the initialization and definitions of variables in Algorithms 2 and 3. For example, specify that `R̂⁰` and `r̂⁰` are inputs, and perhaps add a sentence explaining the role of `R`, `r`, `Q`, `q` in the auxiliary scheme of Algorithm 3.

**Missing References:**

The literature review could be strengthened by including the following foundational works:
*   On distributed (sub)gradient methods: The work of Bertsekas and Tsitsiklis ("Parallel and distributed computation: numerical methods", 1989) is a cornerstone.
*   On momentum methods: The seminal works of Polyak on the heavy-ball method and Nesterov on accelerated gradients should be cited when discussing multi-step methods.
*   Recent consensus algorithms: Including more recent, relevant work such as Shi et al. ("EXTRA: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization", 2015) would provide more contemporary context.

**Minor Typos and Corrections:**

The draft contains several typos. For example: "with theirs neighbors" -> "with their neighbors"; "can be casted as" -> "can be cast as"; and others noted in the text. A thorough proofreading is recommended.

### **Summary**

This is a strong theoretical paper with a novel and significant core idea: demonstrating that Min-Sum Splitting can be used to create an accelerated consensus algorithm and formally connecting it to other acceleration techniques. The new proof technique based on an auxiliary process is elegant and powerful. However, the paper's current scope is narrow (quadratic objectives only), and it lacks discussion of practical relevance, which limits its immediate impact for the NIPS audience. I believe the paper is a good candidate for acceptance if the authors can address the weaknesses, particularly by discussing the potential for generalization and providing more context on the practical implications of their method.
