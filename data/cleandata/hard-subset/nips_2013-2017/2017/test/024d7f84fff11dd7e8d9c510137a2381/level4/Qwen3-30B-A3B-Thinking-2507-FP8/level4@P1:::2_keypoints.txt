- Studies Min-Sum Splitting for consensus, claiming accelerated convergence over diffusive methods.
- Shows properly tuned Min-Sum Splitting achieves subdiffusive rates matching shift-register methods.
- Establishes connections between Min-Sum Splitting and lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods.
- Proves Min-Sum Splitting converges where ordinary Min-Sum fails.
- Provides convergence-rate results for arbitrary graph topologies.
- Demonstrates O(D log(D/ε)) convergence time for cycle and toroidal graphs with tuned parameters.
- Strength: fills a gap with solid theoretical convergence analysis for Min-Sum Splitting.
- Novel proof technique: auxiliary message-passing scheme tracking nodes instead of edges.
- Theoretical connections to lifted chains and multi-step methods offer valuable insight.
- Weakness: claimed improvement over state-of-the-art is marginal and not clearly better asymptotically.
- Fails to cite Olshevsky’s work which achieves O(D log(1/ε)) and can be better in worst-case (O(n) vs O(n log n)).
- Omits several critical related works that should be cited (Olshevsky; Shi et al.; Scaman et al.; França & Bento; Shi et al. EXTRA).
- Novelty appears overstated; auxiliary scheme closely resembles Nesterov acceleration applied to Min-Sum.
- Literature review contains inaccuracies about diffusive method scaling and misuse of “good expansion properties.”
- Minor technical issues: claimed novelty of links to Heavy Ball/Nesterov is questionable (see Rabbat et al.).
- Typo: "Laplacian" should read "Lagrangian" (line 220).
- Confusing presentation: use of directed edges for a simple undirected graph (page 6).
- Significance limited: practical benefits only for graphs with small D relative to n, unclear overall advantage.
- Quality: proofs appear correct and presentation is generally clear but novelty and citations are lacking.
- Main contribution is the auxiliary process analysis technique, which alone doesn’t justify the claimed significant improvement.
