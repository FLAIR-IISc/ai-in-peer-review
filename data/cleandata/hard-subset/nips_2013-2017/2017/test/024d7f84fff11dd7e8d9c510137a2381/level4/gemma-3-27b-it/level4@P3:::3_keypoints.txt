- Presents an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting variant for distributed consensus, using reparametrization techniques and an auxiliary linear process to analyze convergence for quadratic objectives.  
- Main contributions: novel auxiliary-process-based analysis, improved convergence for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, and connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods.  
- Paper is well-written and accessible, with motivation and contributions clearly articulated.  
- Proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear logically sound.  
- Contains several minor typos and grammatical errors (review lists specific line-by-line corrections).  
- Algorithm clarity issues: some quantities (e.g., R_vw and r_vw) are unspecified and initializations of \hat{R}^0 and \hat{r}^0 are not stated.  
- Needs clearer correspondence between variables in Algorithm 2 and the auxiliary linear process in Algorithm 3.  
- Reference list is incomplete; reviewer suggests adding classic distributed optimization and acceleration literature (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis, Shi et al. EXTRA, Polyak/Nesterov references).  
- Suggests verifying whether “Lagrangian/Lagrange multipliers” should replace “Laplacian/Laplace multipliers” in lines 220–221.  
- Requests discussion of practical implications and scenarios where the method outperforms other distributed consensus algorithms.  
- Asks authors to clarify inherent limitations and conditions where Min-Sum Splitting may not outperform existing approaches.  
- Requests elaboration on the intuition behind the auxiliary process and how it produces acceleration.  
- Asks whether the framework can be extended beyond quadratic consensus problems to broader machine learning applications.  
- Recommends a more explicit articulation of the work’s relevance and potential impact for the machine learning community.  
- Overall assessment: theoretically sound and interesting contribution that demonstrates novel analysis and rigorous results.  
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretical novelty, proven convergence/acceleration, connections to existing methods, and sound proofs.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited practical discussion, incomplete explanation of some variables/concepts, and unclear ML relevance.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions addressing clarity, references, practical discussion, and stated corrections.
