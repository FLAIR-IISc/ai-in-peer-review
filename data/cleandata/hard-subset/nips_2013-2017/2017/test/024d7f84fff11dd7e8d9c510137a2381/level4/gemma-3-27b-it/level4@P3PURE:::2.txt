This paper studies the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method applied to the average consensus problem. While the paper is generally well-written, the improvement in convergence rate presented does not appear substantial. Detailed comments are provided below.

(1) The paper states that “This rate is optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph. In this case the convergence time, i.e., the number of iterations required to reach a prescribed level of error accuracy, is proportional to the dimension of the problem.” For complete graphs, the convergence rate is actually 0, as all nodes reach the average in a single step. Furthermore, complete graphs are a highly specific case and not representative of general network topologies. The authors should clarify for which broader categories of graphs the complexity does *not* depend on the number of nodes (dimension), and define what characteristics constitute a “good” graph in this context.

(2) The literature review in the relevant paragraph should include “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control” by Olshevsky. The convergence rate achieved in that work should be explicitly stated and properly compared; it reaches a competitive, if not superior, bound to the results presented in this submission (further details in comment 8).

(3) At the top of page 2, when discussing consensus optimization, important references such as “On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization” by Shi, Ling, Kun, Wu, and Yin, and “Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks” by Scaman, Bach, Bubeck, Lee, and Massoulié should be cited. The authors should also report the state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization and their corresponding linear convergence rates.

(4) When discussing lifted graphs and Markov chains, the paper overlooks the closely related work “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM” by Franca and Bento.

(5) The content of the final paragraph on page 5 is a well-known result. The authors should cite “Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links” by Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew. The subsequent connection between these variants and Heavy ball/Nesterov/Polyak-type methods is established in the field.

(6) The authors have omitted several important references relevant to consensus optimization. For example, “Extra: An Exact First-Order Algorithm for Decentralized Consensus Optimization” by Shi, Ling, Wu, and Yin, and “Fast distributed gradient methods” by Jakovetic, Xavier, and Moura should be included.

(7) Proposition 3 appears trivial and represents a minor contribution.

(8) The convergence rate achieved by this paper, D log(D/eps), does not represent a significant improvement over the D log(1/eps) rate obtained by “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control” (see comment 2). In the worst-case scenario (applicable to all graphs), where D~n, the bound presented is even weaker than that reported in the aforementioned work.

(9) The “Linear Time Average Consensus…” paper leverages Nesterov’s acceleration to achieve its results. The reviewer suspects that the “Auxiliary message-passing scheme” proposed by the authors is, in effect, a re-application of Nesterov’s acceleration to the min-sum algorithm. If this is the case, the analysis simplifies to analyzing a linear system, which is not inherently difficult. Given this, the paper’s contribution is unclear.

(10) Any minor improvement may stem from a careful analysis of the spectral gap of the graphs. However, the worst-case bound remains O(n) because O(n) = O(D) for the set of all graphs with n nodes.

(11) Line 243 of page 6 depicts a simple graph but employs directed edges, which is confusing.

(12) Typo at line 220 of page 6: “Laplacian” should be “Lagrangian.”
