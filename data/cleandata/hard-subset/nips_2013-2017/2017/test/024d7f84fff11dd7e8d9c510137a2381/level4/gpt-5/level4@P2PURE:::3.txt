This paper introduces an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for distributed consensus optimization. Using the reparameterization techniques of Ruozzi and Tatikonda (2013), the authors establish convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm on consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. The analysis is driven by constructing an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of Min-Sum Splitting.

The main contributions are:
- An analysis of Min-Sum Splitting based on a new proof technique that introduces an auxiliary process.
- A Min-Sum-based protocol for consensus that attains faster convergence than prior results.
- A demonstration of connections between the proposed method and lifted Markov chains as well as multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The motivation and contributions are clear. The paper is generally well written and easy to follow, though it contains a number of typos and grammatical errors (listed below). The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear correct.

It would be helpful if the authors addressed the following:
- Although the paper is theoretical, please comment on the practicality of the method and when it should be preferred over other distributed consensus optimization methods.
- What are the limitations of Min-Sum Splitting?
- What is the intuition for using the auxiliary process in Min-Sum Splitting?
- The results are for consensus with quadratic objectives. Can the framework extend to more general consensus problems common in machine learning?
- Please clearly articulate why this approach is of interest to the machine learning community.

In summary, this is a purely theoretical paper that establishes convergence rates using a new proof technique and clarifies connections to well-known methods. The ideas are interesting, but the paper would benefit from more discussion of practical relevance, intuition behind the results, and a stronger justification of the method’s importance for the machine learning community.
