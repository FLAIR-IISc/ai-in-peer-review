

In this paper, the authors present an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for solving consensus problems in distributed optimization. Building on reparametrization techniques from [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013], they establish convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. The primary analytical tool is the construction of an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

The main contributions are as follows: (i) a novel proof technique for analyzing the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm via an auxiliary process; (ii) a Min-Sum protocol for consensus problems achieving faster convergence than prior results; and (iii) a demonstration of connections between the proposed method, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The motivation and contributions are clear, and the paper is well-written and accessible. However, it contains several typos and grammatical errors (detailed below). The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appear correct.

**Key questions for the authors to address:**
1. The paper is theoretical; comment on the practicality of the method and when it should be preferred over existing distributed consensus methods.
2. State the limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method.
3. Explain the intuition behind using the auxiliary process.
4. Discuss whether the framework extends to non-quadratic consensus problems common in machine learning.
5. Clarify the significance of this work for the machine learning community.

In summary, this paper establishes convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm using a novel proof technique and connects it to established methods. The ideas are interesting, but the paper lacks discussion of practicality, intuition for the auxiliary process, and justification for its relevance to machine learning. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the contribution.
