### Review

**Summary**

This paper investigates the application of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, a variant of the standard Min-Sum message-passing protocol, to the distributed consensus problem. The authors first establish that while the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge on graphs with cycles, the Min-Sum Splitting version can be configured to converge to the correct solution.

The main contribution of the paper is to demonstrate that with a proper choice of its tuning parameters, Min-Sum Splitting achieves an accelerated, "subdiffusive" convergence rate. This rate provides a square-root improvement over classical "diffusive" methods for consensus and matches the performance of accelerated shift-register methods. To prove this, the authors introduce a novel analytical technique: they construct an auxiliary message-passing scheme on the graph's nodes that tracks the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, which operates on the graph's directed edges. This simplifies the spectral analysis significantly.

Conceptually, the paper draws a compelling and novel connection between three seemingly disparate areas of research in accelerated distributed optimization: (1) Min-Sum/Belief Propagation schemes, (2) lifted Markov chain techniques, and (3) multi-step first-order methods from convex optimization.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength lies in the novel connections it establishes. To my knowledge, this is the first work to formally link Min-Sum message-passing to the acceleration mechanisms found in lifted Markov chains and multi-step gradient methods. By showing that the "splitting" parameters can be tuned to introduce a form of momentum or directionality analogous to these other methods, the paper provides a new perspective on how message-passing algorithms can be accelerated. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could inspire further research into accelerating other belief propagation-style algorithms beyond the consensus problem.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical work appears sound. The introduction of the auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) is an elegant and effective analytical tool. It allows the authors to map the complex dynamics on the 2m-dimensional space of directed edges to a more tractable 2n-dimensional state-space system on the nodes. This enables them to directly leverage existing spectral analysis of shift-register methods (Theorem 1) to characterize the convergence rate of their Min-Sum protocol. This approach is more general and provides stronger results than prior work on Min-Sum for consensus [27], which was limited to regular graphs and offered conjectures for other topologies.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The introduction provides an excellent overview of the consensus problem, clearly situating the work within the context of both diffusive and accelerated methods. The contributions are explicitly listed and well-motivated. The logical flow from the general Min-Sum Splitting algorithm to its specific application for consensus, and finally to the auxiliary analysis, is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity on Splitting Parameters:** A minor point of confusion arises in the presentation of the splitting motivation. In Section 2, the algorithm is introduced with real-valued parameters `δ` and `Γvw`. Immediately after, the name "splitting" is justified by an interpretation where these parameters are integers representing the number of times a function is split. The text states: "...splitting each term `φvw` into `Γvw` terms, and each term `φv` into `δ` terms...". This is potentially confusing since the parameters are defined as real numbers. While a footnote clarifies that the algorithm is well-defined for any real choice of parameters, the exposition in the main text could be improved by stating this upfront to avoid ambiguity for the reader.

2.  **Dependence on Global Information:** The accelerated rate demonstrated in Theorem 1 requires setting the parameter `γ` based on `ρW`, the spectral radius of `W - 11T/n`. This `ρW` is a global property of the graph. The paper correctly notes the parallel with other accelerated methods that also require global information. However, it does not discuss the practical implications of this requirement. In a truly distributed system, obtaining this global spectral information is a non-trivial problem in itself. A brief discussion of this limitation or potential distributed methods for estimating this parameter would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is performed for the network averaging problem, i.e., consensus with a quadratic objective function. While this is a standard and crucial benchmark problem, it would be valuable for the authors to comment on the potential for extending this acceleration analysis to consensus problems with more general convex functions `φv`. It is not immediately clear if the linear dynamics and the resulting acceleration would hold in a more general setting.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**
*   Presents a novel and insightful connection between Min-Sum, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step optimization methods.
*   Provides the first convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.
*   Demonstrates that a message-passing algorithm can achieve competitive, accelerated rates for a fundamental distributed problem.
*   Introduces an elegant and powerful proof technique (the auxiliary process) that is more general than prior approaches.
*   The paper is very clearly written and places the contributions in excellent context.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Cons):**
*   The optimal parameter setting requires global information about the graph's spectral properties, a practical limitation that is not fully addressed.
*   The analysis is confined to the quadratic case (network averaging).
*   There is a minor point of confusion in the motivation for the term "splitting."

**Conclusion:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The strengths, particularly the novel conceptual bridge built between different classes of algorithms and the rigorous technical analysis, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The results are significant and likely to be of high interest to researchers in machine learning, signal processing, and optimization. I strongly recommend acceptance.
