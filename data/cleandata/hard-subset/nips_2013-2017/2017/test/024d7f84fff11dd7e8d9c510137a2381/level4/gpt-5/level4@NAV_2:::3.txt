This manuscript studies an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing scheme for consensus in distributed optimization. Building on the reparameterization approach of Ruozzi and Tatikonda (2013), it establishes convergence-rate guarantees for Min-Sum Splitting on quadratic consensus objectives. The core analytical device is an auxiliary linear process that mirrors the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting updates.

The principal contributions are: (i) an analysis of Min-Sum Splitting via a new proof technique based on an auxiliary node-level process; (ii) a Min-Sum protocol for consensus that achieves faster convergence than previously reported bounds; and (iii) an explicit connection between the proposed scheme and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods.

The motivation and contributions are clearly articulated. The paper is generally well organized and readable, though it contains a number of typos and grammatical errors (listed below). The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear sound.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Although the paper is theoretical, please discuss when this approach is practical and preferable relative to other distributed methods for consensus optimization.
- What are the main limitations of Min-Sum Splitting in this setting?
- Provide intuition for why the auxiliary process captures and simplifies the analysis of Min-Sum Splitting.
- Can the framework extend beyond quadratic objectives to more general consensus formulations common in machine learning?
- Clarify the relevance and potential impact of this approach for machine learning practitioners.

In summary, this is a theoretical contribution that derives convergence rates via a new proof strategy and situates the method within the landscape of lifted Markov chains and multi-step accelerations. The ideas are interesting, but the paper would benefit from clearer exposition of the algorithms’ variables and initializations, a more explicit discussion of practical relevance and limitations, and additional references situating the work within the consensus and optimization literature.
