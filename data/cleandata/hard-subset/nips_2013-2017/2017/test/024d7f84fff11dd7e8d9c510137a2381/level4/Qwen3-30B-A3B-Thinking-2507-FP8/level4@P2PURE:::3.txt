

In this paper, the authors present an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for solving consensus problems in distributed optimization. Building on reparametrization techniques from [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013], they establish convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. The primary analytical tool is the construction of an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

The main contributions are: (i) a novel proof technique for analyzing the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm via an auxiliary process, (ii) a Min-Sum protocol for consensus problems achieving superior convergence rates compared to prior results, and (iii) a demonstration of connections between the proposed method, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The paper's motivation and contributions are clear, and it is well-written and accessible. However, it contains several typographical and grammatical errors (detailed below). The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appear correct.

**Suggested improvements for the paper:**
- Comment on the practical applicability of the method versus other distributed consensus techniques.
- Discuss limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method.
- Explain the intuition behind using the auxiliary process.
- Address whether the framework extends to non-quadratic consensus problems common in machine learning.
- Explicitly justify the relevance of this approach to the machine learning community.

In summary, this paper presents a purely theoretical contribution establishing convergence rates via a novel proof technique and connecting the method to established literature. The ideas are interesting, but the paper lacks discussion of practical utility, intuition for the auxiliary process, and justification for its significance to machine learning.
