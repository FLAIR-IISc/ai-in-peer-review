

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the min-sum splitting method for the average consensus problem. The authors establish that a properly tuned min-sum splitting protocol achieves subdiffusive convergence rates (O(D log(D/ε))) that match the rates of shift-register methods, where D is the graph diameter. The work connects min-sum schemes to both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization, demonstrating that the directionality introduced by splitting parameters resembles the acceleration mechanisms in these approaches. The analysis employs an auxiliary message-passing scheme to track convergence, providing a novel proof technique that relates the convergence time to the spectral gap of a matrix describing the dynamics.

The paper demonstrates several strengths. It provides the first theoretical analysis of convergence rates for min-sum splitting, which is a significant contribution given that prior work only established convergence without rate analysis. The connection to two different lines of research (lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods) is insightful and well-supported. The auxiliary message-passing scheme represents a novel technical contribution that facilitates the convergence analysis.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses. First, the improvement over existing work is not clearly demonstrated. The claimed rate of O(D log(D/ε)) appears to be an incremental improvement over the O(D log(1/ε)) rate established in Olshevsky's "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs" (which has a more general applicability), and the paper fails to properly contextualize this improvement. The paper also omits several key references that are directly relevant to the problem, including Olshevsky's work (which achieves the optimal rate for all graphs), Scaman et al.'s "Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization," Shi et al.'s "On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM," and Franca and Bento's "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM." The discussion of the rate for complete graphs is misleading, as complete graphs converge in a single step (rate 0), making them unrepresentative of general graphs. The paper should clarify which graph classes have convergence times independent of the number of nodes.

The connection between the proposed auxiliary scheme and Nesterov's acceleration (as noted in comment 9) is not properly distinguished from existing methods. The paper's main contribution appears to be a careful analysis of the spectral gap for the specific consensus problem, rather than a novel acceleration technique. The claim that the min-sum splitting method is competitive with recent accelerated methods is not convincingly supported, especially when the worst-case bound remains O(n) (since D ≤ n for all graphs).

Specific technical concerns include:
(1) The paper incorrectly states that the diffusive rate is optimal for complete graphs without acknowledging that complete graphs converge in a single step.
(2) The literature review omits Olshevsky's seminal work on linear time average consensus, which establishes a rate of O(D log(1/ε)) that is superior to the paper's claimed rate.
(3) The paper fails to cite important recent work on consensus optimization, including Scaman et al. and Shi et al., which report state-of-the-art convergence rates.
(4) The connection to lifted Markov chain techniques overlooks Franca and Bento's relevant work.
(5) The discussion of the relationship between heavy ball/Nesterov methods and consensus is a well-known fact that should reference Rabbat et al.'s work.
(6) The paper misses several key references including Shi et al.'s "Extra" algorithm and Jakovetic et al.'s "Fast distributed gradient methods."
(7) Proposition 3 appears to be a trivial extension rather than a substantive contribution.
(8) The claimed rate of O(D log(D/ε)) does not provide a significant improvement over O(D log(1/ε)) established by Olshevsky, especially in the worst case where D ~ n.
(9) The auxiliary message-passing scheme appears to be a standard application of Nesterov's acceleration to min-sum, which is not novel.
(10) The worst-case bound remains O(n) (since D ≤ n for all graphs), which does not represent a meaningful improvement.
(11) The use of directed edges for simple graphs is confusing.
(12) A typo appears on line 220 (Laplacian → Lagrangian).

The paper's contribution is marginal compared to existing work, and it fails to clearly articulate how it advances the state of the art. While the technical analysis is sound, the significance of the results is questionable given the lack of clear novelty and the omission of key references. The authors' rebuttal addressed some concerns but did not substantially alter the assessment of the paper's contribution.
