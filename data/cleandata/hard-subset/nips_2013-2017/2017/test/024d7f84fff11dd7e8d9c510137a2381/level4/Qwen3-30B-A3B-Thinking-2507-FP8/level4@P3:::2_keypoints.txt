- Paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for consensus, claiming subdiffusive acceleration with rates O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori and links to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods.  
- Main contributions: first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and a novel auxiliary message-passing proof technique.  
- Shows Min-Sum Splitting can be seen as operating on a lifted (directed-edge) space, introducing directionality akin to non-reversible Markov chains.  
- Demonstrates splitting parameters create a memory term directly related to multi-step/shift-register first-order methods.  
- Theory is rigorous and well-structured, with clear propositions and proofs (some details in supplement); writing is generally clear.  
- Claimed rate improvement is overstated: Olshevsky (2015) achieves O(D log(1/ε)), which is superior to O(D log(D/ε)), and in worst-case D=Θ(n) the paper’s bound is worse.  
- Literature review is incomplete: missing key citations (Olshevsky 2015; Scaman et al. 2017; Shi et al. 2015; Franca & Bento 2016; Jakovetic et al. 2014) and links to heavy-ball/Nesterov consensus methods.  
- Novelty of the auxiliary message-passing is questionable—essentially implements known Nesterov-style acceleration and reduces to analyzing a linear system.  
- Some claims are confusing or misleading (e.g., “dimension-independent” convergence on complete graphs); need clarification on which graph classes actually exhibit such behavior.  
- Minor errors and unclear notations: typo Laplacian→Lagrangian (line 220), odd use of directed edges for simple graphs (line 243), and vague “general category of graphs” with good expansion.  
- Recommended revisions: more accurate comparison to state-of-the-art, better contextualization of the auxiliary scheme within existing acceleration literature, clearer statement of actual improvements, and inclusion of missing references.  
- Overall: technically sound but incremental; connections to lifted chains and multi-step methods are interesting but the claimed significance and convergence improvement are overstated.
