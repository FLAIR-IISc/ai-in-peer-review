Summary
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for distributed consensus (network averaging). It shows that ordinary Min-Sum may fail on loopy graphs, but a reparameterized “splitting” version converges to the correct solution. The core technical contribution is an auxiliary node-based linear dynamical system that tracks the edge-based Min-Sum Splitting dynamics, enabling spectral analysis. With a specific choice of parameters (δ = 1, Γ = γW with γ tuned from the spectral gap of W), the method attains a subdiffusive, accelerated rate matching shift-register (Chebyshev/second-order) methods. The resulting convergence time is O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori, a square-root improvement over diffusive methods, and close to the O(D log(1/ε)) rates achieved by Nesterov-type accelerations. The paper also makes conceptual connections between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted nonreversible Markov chains, and multi-step first-order methods.

Relation to prior work
- Min-Sum and belief propagation: The paper builds on reparameterization/splitting (Ruozzi & Tatikonda) and prior analysis of Min-Sum for consensus (Moallemi & Van Roy).
- Accelerated consensus: It connects to lifted Markov chains (Chen–Lovász–Pak; Jung–Shah–Shin) and multi-step/shift-register accelerations (Golub–Varga; Xiao & Boyd; Ghadimi et al.), and to recent linear-time consensus results based on Nesterov-type acceleration (Olshevsky).
- Distributed optimization: ADMM-based consensus (Boyd et al.; Wei & Ozdaglar; Shi et al.) and optimal first-order methods on networks (Scaman et al.) provide alternative accelerated frameworks with linear rates under strong convexity/smoothness.

Strengths
- Provides a clear, unified perspective linking Min-Sum Splitting to both lifting and multi-step methods, which is novel and pedagogically valuable.
- Introduces an auxiliary node-based process that simplifies analysis of an otherwise edge-based scheme; this is technically clean and likely reusable.
- Establishes concrete accelerated rates for Min-Sum Splitting on arbitrary graphs, with explicit tuning and a spectral characterization.
- Improves on diffusive consensus, yielding O(D log(D/ε)) on geometric graphs (cycles/tori), with analysis grounded in spectral tools.

Weaknesses and suggestions
1) “Graphs with good expansion” and dimension-independent complexity: Please clarify the class (e.g., expanders, complete graphs) for which the number of iterations does not scale with n (beyond a log(1/ε) factor). Complete graphs are an extreme case; a broader statement about expanders would be more representative.

2) Comparison to linear-time consensus: The work of Olshevsky, “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control,” should be discussed prominently. His O(D log(1/ε)) rate is competitive or better than O(D log(D/ε)) and should be positioned clearly (assumptions, tuning, robustness) relative to your results.

3) Consensus optimization literature: The paper should reference and contrast with:
- Shi, Ling, Yuan, Wu, Yin: linear convergence of decentralized ADMM for consensus.
- Scaman, Bach, Bubeck, Lee, Massoulié: optimal algorithms for smooth/strongly convex distributed optimization.
State explicitly when linear convergence holds (strong convexity) and how your results relate (pure averaging vs. optimization with curvature).

4) Lifting and ADMM: Franca & Bento, “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM,” is directly relevant and should be cited when discussing lifted dynamics.

5) Connection to heavy-ball/Nesterov/Polyak: The observation that certain consensus variants are second-order recursions is known (e.g., Rabbat, Nowak, Bucklew). Please acknowledge this and articulate what is new in your mapping from Min-Sum Splitting to a multi-step node process.

6) Additional consensus references: EXTRA (Shi et al.) and fast distributed gradient methods (Jakovetić et al.) are relevant to accelerated consensus/optimization and should be included.

7) Technical novelty of some propositions: Parts of the mapping (e.g., equivalence between edge-based and node-based linear recursions) are straightforward linear algebra; clearly delineate what is technically new versus convenient reformulation.

8) Rate comparison and worst-case scaling: Your O(D log(D/ε)) vs. O(D log(1/ε)) (Olshevsky) deserves a careful, transparent comparison. In worst-case families where D ≍ n, your bound can be asymptotically worse. Please discuss assumptions, tuning knowledge, and constants.

9) Nature of the acceleration: The auxiliary scheme and the resulting 2n-dimensional linear system appear closely related to known Chebyshev/shift-register/Nesterov accelerations. It would help to explicitly state whether the splitting-induced acceleration is equivalent to applying a known momentum scheme to a linear consensus iteration (and what is genuinely new about the BP/splitting perspective).

10) Global information: Your tuning parameter γ depends on ρ(W). Discuss how such global information can be estimated or approximated in a distributed way, and the robustness of performance to misspecification.

11) Notation: The graph is simple and undirected, but the algorithm runs on directed edges; clarify this early to avoid confusion.

12) Typos/wording: “Laplacian” appears where “Lagrangian” is intended (line 220, p. 6). Please proofread and fix minor typos.

Evaluation
- Quality: Technically sound and careful within the linear/quadratic consensus setting; proofs leverage established spectral tools. Some contributions (e.g., the auxiliary reduction) are more structural than deep.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized, with a few notational ambiguities and missing citations. Adding the comparisons above would enhance readability and context.
- Originality: Moderate. The perspective linking Min-Sum Splitting to lifting and multi-step methods is fresh, but the achieved rates match known shift-register accelerations, and the analysis reduces to linear system arguments.
- Significance: Limited-to-moderate. The method does not improve state-of-the-art rates on key families (e.g., Olshevsky), but it offers a new lens on accelerating BP-type protocols and may spur further work beyond consensus.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, instructive bridge between BP/Min-Sum Splitting, lifted chains, and multi-step acceleration.
- General convergence-rate guarantees for a BP-style protocol on arbitrary graphs.
- Auxiliary node-based analysis technique may be reusable in other message-passing contexts.

Arguments against acceptance
- Does not advance best-known convergence rates; O(D log(D/ε)) is weaker than O(D log(1/ε)) in important regimes.
- Several related works are omitted or under-discussed, and the novelty beyond recasting known accelerations is limited.
- Reliance on global spectral information for tuning, with limited discussion of practical estimation.

Post-rebuttal
The authors addressed several concerns satisfactorily. My overall assessment remains unchanged: the paper offers a useful conceptual contribution and solid analysis but falls short of surpassing state-of-the-art performance and needs a more thorough positioning within the existing literature.
