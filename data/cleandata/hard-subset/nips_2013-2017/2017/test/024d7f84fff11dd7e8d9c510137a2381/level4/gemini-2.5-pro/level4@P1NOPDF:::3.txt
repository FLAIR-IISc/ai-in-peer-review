This paper introduces an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for solving consensus problems in distributed optimization. Drawing on reparametrization techniques from [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013], the authors establish convergence rates for their Min-Sum Splitting algorithm on problems with quadratic objective functions. The core of their analysis is the construction of an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the algorithm.

The main contributions of the paper can be summarized as: (i) providing a novel analysis of the Min-Sum splitting algorithm via an auxiliary process, (ii) designing a Min-Sum protocol for consensus problems that achieves a faster convergence rate than previously established methods, and (iii) establishing a connection between the proposed method, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The paper's motivation and contributions are clearly articulated. The exposition is generally well-written and easy to follow, although it contains several typos and grammatical errors (listed below). The proofs for Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear to be correct.

**Questions for the Authors:**
The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors could address the following questions:
- While the contributions are primarily theoretical, a discussion on the practical implications of the method would be valuable. When should this method be preferred over other distributed approaches for consensus optimization?
- What are the limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method?
- Could the authors provide more intuition behind the construction and use of the auxiliary process in their analysis?
- The current analysis is restricted to quadratic objective functions. Can this framework be extended to more general convex problems commonly found in machine learning? A discussion of the challenges and potential paths for generalization would be welcome.
- The paper should more explicitly articulate the relevance of this work to the machine learning community. What specific ML problems could benefit from this accelerated consensus optimization approach?

**Summary:**
In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper that establishes improved convergence rates for Min-Sum message passing using a novel proof technique. The authors successfully connect their method to established concepts in optimization and graph theory. While the theoretical ideas are interesting and the analysis appears sound, the paper's impact could be increased by addressing its practical implications, providing more intuition behind the core technical ideas, and more clearly motivating its relevance to the machine learning community.
