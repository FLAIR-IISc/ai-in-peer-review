- Paper proposes an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting variant for distributed consensus and analyzes convergence rates for quadratic objectives.  
- Main technical novelty: a new proof technique using an auxiliary linear process to track Min-Sum Splitting.  
- Claims faster convergence than prior Min-Sum results and clarifies links to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods.  
- Presentation is generally clear and proofs of Propositions 1, 2 and Theorem 1 appear correct.  
- Several typos and grammatical errors throughout (author lists many specific line edits to fix).  
- Algorithms are not fully self-contained: missing definitions/initializations (e.g., R_{vw}, r_{vw}, \hat{R}^0, \hat{r}^0) and the auxiliary linear process variables are not clearly identified in Algorithm 3.  
- Missing or incomplete citations to foundational works in distributed optimization and acceleration (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989; Ram, Nedić & Veeravalli 2009; Shi et al. 2015; Polyak; Nesterov).  
- Requests for more practical discussion: when the method is preferable (topology, communication/computation trade-offs, scaling, noise robustness).  
- Requests to clarify limitations: parameter sensitivity, graph-structure dependence, memory needs, and extension beyond quadratics.  
- Requests for more intuition on why the auxiliary linear process produces acceleration and its relation to lifted chains and momentum methods.  
- Asks about generality: potential extensions to composite, nonsmooth, or stochastic objectives common in ML.  
- Wants explicit articulation of implications for ML practice (decentralized training, federated optimization, scalability, privacy).  
- Overall: interesting theoretical contribution that would benefit from clearer algorithms, corrected typos, additional references, and expanded discussion of practicality and ML relevance.
