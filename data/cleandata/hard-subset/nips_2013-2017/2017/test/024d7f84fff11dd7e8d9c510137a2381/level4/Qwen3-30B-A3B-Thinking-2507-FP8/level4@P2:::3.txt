

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of Min-Sum Splitting for solving consensus problems in distributed optimization. The authors establish convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting applied to the consensus problem, demonstrating that proper tuning of splitting parameters yields subdiffusive convergence rates matching those of shift-register methods. The main contributions include: (1) a novel proof technique using an auxiliary linear process to track the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm's evolution, (2) a Min-Sum protocol that achieves better convergence rates than previously established results for Min-Sum methods, and (3) establishing connections between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the technical content accessible to readers familiar with distributed optimization. The authors effectively relate their work to previous literature, particularly connecting Min-Sum Splitting to two established lines of research on accelerated consensus algorithms. The paper builds upon [27], which investigated Min-Sum for consensus but only established convergence for d-regular graphs with specific initial conditions, whereas this work provides a general analysis for any graph topology.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound, with Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appearing to be correct. The authors establish a square-root improvement in convergence rates over diffusive methods, which is significant for graphs with geometry (e.g., cycle graphs and tori). The proof technique using an auxiliary process represents a valuable methodological contribution that could be applied to other distributed optimization problems. However, the paper focuses exclusively on quadratic objective functions for the consensus problem, limiting its immediate applicability to more general machine learning problems.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written with clear organization. The main ideas are presented logically, and the connection to related work is well-articulated. However, some algorithm descriptions could be improved. In Algorithm 2, the initialization of R̂0 and r̂0 is not specified, and the notation R̂vw and r̂vw is not fully explained. The relationship between the auxiliary process (Algorithm 3) and the main Min-Sum Splitting algorithm could be made clearer. The paper contains several minor grammatical errors that should be corrected (e.g., "double-stochastic" should be "doubly-stochastic," "casted" should be "cast," "graph with" should be "graphs with").

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by establishing the first theoretical analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods represents a novel insight that bridges two previously separate lines of research. This connection is not evident from previous work, including [27], which only investigated Min-Sum for consensus without establishing convergence rates for general graphs.

**Significance:** The paper's significance is somewhat limited by its focus on quadratic objectives and the lack of discussion regarding practical implementation challenges. The authors do not address when Min-Sum Splitting should be preferred over other consensus algorithms (e.g., ADMM or Nesterov-accelerated methods), nor do they discuss computational complexity or communication requirements. While the theoretical improvement in convergence rates is valuable, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the practical implications and potential applications in machine learning settings.

The paper would be strengthened by addressing the following points:
1. A clear explanation of the auxiliary process (Algorithm 3) and how it relates to the main Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.
2. A discussion of the practical implementation challenges and computational complexity of Min-Sum Splitting.
3. A more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in terms of communication requirements and computational overhead.
4. A clearer explanation of why this approach is particularly relevant for machine learning applications beyond the consensus problem.
5. A discussion of limitations, including whether the method extends to non-quadratic objectives or non-convex problems.

The paper is missing several key references that would strengthen its context and literature review. For instance, the authors should cite Bertsekas and Tsitsiklis (1989) on distributed optimization, and include Polyak's heavy-ball method (1964) and Nesterov's accelerated gradient methods (1983) in the discussion of multi-step methods.

Overall, this paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution that establishes a novel connection between Min-Sum message-passing and accelerated consensus algorithms. The technical analysis is sound, and the proof technique is valuable for future work. However, the paper would be significantly stronger with clearer explanations of the algorithms, a more thorough discussion of practical implications, and a better articulation of the relevance to machine learning applications. The focus on quadratic objectives limits the immediate applicability of the results, and the paper would benefit from addressing how the method could be extended to more general problems.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a theoretical contribution to distributed optimization with clear connections to established techniques. The main weakness is the limited discussion of practical significance for machine learning applications, which is a key consideration for NIPS. With the suggested improvements, this paper would make a strong contribution to the literature.
