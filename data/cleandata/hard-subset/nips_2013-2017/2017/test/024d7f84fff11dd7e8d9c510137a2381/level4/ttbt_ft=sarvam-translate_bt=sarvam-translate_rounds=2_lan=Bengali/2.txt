This paper discusses the convergence rate of the so-called mean-sum decomposition method in consensus problems. Generally, the article is well-written, but the improvement in results does not seem very impressive. Below are the detailed comments:

(1) It states that "this rate is favorable for graphs with good propagation properties, such as complete graphs. In this case, the convergence time, i.e., the number of iterations required to reach an error accuracy of a certain level of the problem's magnitude, such as..."

For complete graphs, the linear rate is 0, because everyone converges to the mean value in 1 step. Also, complete graphs are very special to represent.

So, for which general class of graphs does the complexity not depend on the number of nodes (number of nodes)? Which general class of graphs is considered good?

(2) This paragraph (like comment 1), the literature review should include Olechansky's "Linear Time Mean Consensus on the Effects of Static Graphs and Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control." Its convergence rate should be mentioned correctly (further explanation will be given in comment 8). The reference mentioned here has reached quite competitive or even better limits compared to the submitted results.

(3) At the beginning of page 2, for consensus optimization, important references such as Shi, Ling, Kun, Wu, and Yin's "Linear Convergence of ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization" and Skaman, Bach, Bubek, Li, Masovite's "Optimal Algorithm for Smooth and Strongly Convex Distributed Optimization in Networks" should be mentioned. Also, the authors' state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization and their corresponding (linear) convergence rates should be mentioned.

(4) While discussing lifted graphs and Markov chains, this paper ignores a related paper by Franka and Benter, "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM."

(5) The content of the last paragraph on page 5 is a well-known fact. References should be given to Rabbat, Novak, and Bakul's "Generalized Consensus Computation in Networked Systems with Eraser Links." In continuation, the connection of these variants with the heavy ball/Nesterov/Polyak is well known.

(6) There are many important references related to consensus optimization that the authors have ignored. For example, Shi, Ling, Wu, and Yin's "Extra: A Correct First-Order Algorithm for Decentralized Consensus Optimization" and Jacovitic, J. Xavier, and Moura's "Rapid Distributed Gradient Method."

(7) Proposal 3 seems trivial and an additional contribution.

(8) The rate mentioned in this paper, the rate obtained by the linear time mean consensus on the effects of D log(D/EPS), static graphs, and decentralized optimization and multi-agent control, does not seem to have improved significantly by D log(1/EPS) (see comment 2). Especially in the worst-case scenario (applicable to all graphs), D~N, this limit is even worse than the limit achieved in "linear time mean consensus..."

(9) The "linear time mean consensus..." paper improves the limit through Nesterov's acceleration. The reviewer suspects that the authors' proposed so-called " సహాయ বার্তা-প্ৰেৰণ প্রকল্প " is again applying Nesterov's acceleration to the mean-sum algorithm. This is fine, but the analysis is done for consensus, which is supposed to analyze a linear system and should not be difficult. In such a situation, the paper's contribution is not clear.

(10) A slight improvement can come by working carefully in the spectral interval of the graph. Ultimately, the worst-case limit is still O(N), because O(N)=O(D) for all graphs with n number of nodes.

(11) Line 243 on page 6. The graph is simple but the author is using directed edges. This is misleading.

(12) Typo in line 220 on page 6. Laplacian—> Lagrangian.
