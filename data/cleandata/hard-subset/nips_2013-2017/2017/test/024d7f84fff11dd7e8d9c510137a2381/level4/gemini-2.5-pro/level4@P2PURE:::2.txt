This paper analyzes the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method for the average consensus problem. While the paper is generally well-written, the claimed improvements over existing results appear to be marginal.

Detailed comments are as follows:
(1) The paper claims that its proposed rate is "optimal for graphs with good expansion properties," citing the complete graph as an example. This example is misleading, as average consensus on a complete graph converges in a single step. Furthermore, the complete graph is a special case and not representative of general networks. The authors should clarify which general categories of graphs are considered to have "good" expansion properties and for which classes of graphs the proposed complexity does not depend on the number of nodes.

(2) The literature review is incomplete. Specifically, the authors should cite and discuss "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control" by Olshevsky. As detailed further in comment (8), the bound presented in that work is highly competitive with, and in some cases superior to, the result in this submission.

(3) In the discussion of consensus optimization (top of page 2), the paper overlooks several key references, including "On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization" by Shi et al. and "Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks" by Scaman et al. To properly contextualize their contribution, the authors should provide a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art algorithms and their established convergence rates.

(4) The discussion on lifted graphs and Markov chains is missing a citation to the highly relevant work, "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM" by Franca and Bento.

(5) The material presented in the last paragraph of page 5 appears to be a well-known result in the field. The authors should cite prior work, such as "Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links" by Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew. Additionally, the connection between these variants and acceleration techniques (e.g., Heavy-ball, Nesterov, Polyak) is already established in the literature and should be acknowledged.

(6) The paper has omitted several other important references on consensus optimization that should be included and discussed. For instance:
*   "EXTRA: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization" by Shi, Ling, Wu, and Yin.
*   "Fast distributed gradient methods" by Jakovetic, Xavier, and Moura.

(7) Proposition 3 appears to be a straightforward result and constitutes a minor contribution.

(8) The main result, a convergence rate of O(D log(D/ε)), does not offer a significant improvement over the existing O(D log(1/ε)) rate from the aforementioned work by Olshevsky. The dependency on D in the logarithmic term is a notable drawback. In the worst-case scenario for general graphs where the diameter D is on the order of the number of nodes n, this new bound is actually worse than the one established in the prior work.

(9) The improvement in Olshevsky's work was achieved by applying Nesterov's acceleration. The proposed "Auxiliary message-passing scheme" appears to be a similar application of Nesterov-style acceleration, but to the min-sum algorithm. While this is a valid approach, its application to the average consensus problem, which reduces to the analysis of a linear system, is not a particularly challenging task. This observation raises questions about the novelty and overall significance of the paper's contribution.

(10) The marginal improvement presented in the paper may stem from a more careful handling of the graph's spectral gap. However, this does not change the worst-case bound, which remains O(n) for general graphs since the diameter D can be on the order of n.

(11) On page 6, line 243, there is a point of confusion: the text refers to a simple graph, yet the notation appears to use directed edges.

(12) Typo: On page 6, line 220, "Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian".

**After Rebuttal:**
The reviewer thanks the authors for their response. While the clarifications are appreciated, the reviewer's overall assessment of the paper's contribution remains unchanged.
