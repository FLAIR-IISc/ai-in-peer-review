This paper applies the Min-Sum message-passing protocol to distributed consensus and establishes convergence rates for a reparametrized variant, Min-Sum Splitting, in the quadratic (averaging) case. The core idea is to introduce splitting parameters (δ, Γ) that make the algorithm converge on general graphs and, when tuned appropriately, achieve subdiffusive (accelerated) rates that match shift-register methods. The technical analysis tracks Min-Sum Splitting via a carefully constructed auxiliary linear process on the nodes, even though the original message passing occurs on directed edges. The authors also highlight connections with lifted, non-reversible Markov chains and with multi-step first-order methods (e.g., heavy-ball, Nesterov, shift-register), emphasizing that the “directionality” induced by splitting mirrors techniques that break diffusion in Markov chains and add memory/momentum in optimization.

Relation to prior work: Ordinary Min-Sum converges on trees but can fail on loopy graphs. Splitting (Ruozzi & Tatikonda, 2013) was known to ensure convergence in certain quadratic settings; this paper provides the first convergence-rate analysis for consensus, complementing earlier Min-Sum-based approaches to consensus (e.g., Moallemi & Van Roy) that analyzed different modifications and obtained diffusive scaling or edge-based dynamics. The accelerated rates here align with known improvements from shift-register/multi-step methods and lifted chains.

Strengths:
- Provides the first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, including rigorous spectral bounds via an auxiliary node-based process.
- Shows that, with appropriate tuning, the method achieves subdiffusive convergence matching shift-register rates (a square-root improvement over classical diffusive methods on graphs with geometry).
- Conceptual contribution: unifies perspectives from belief propagation, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step acceleration.
- Proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear correct.

Weaknesses:
- The paper contains numerous typos and grammatical errors (listed below).
- Some notation and initialization details in the algorithms are underspecified (e.g., variables in Algorithm 2 and their initialization; mapping to the auxiliary process in Algorithm 3).
- Missing references in several places (subgradient/gradient methods; heavy-ball; Nesterov; classical consensus literature).
- The paper is purely theoretical; it lacks discussion of practicality, when to use this method relative to alternatives, limitations, and intuition behind the auxiliary process.
- The motivation for the ML audience and applicability beyond quadratic consensus are not clearly articulated.

Quality: Technically sound; the main results are well motivated and the analysis is careful. Completeness is good for the quadratic case, but limitations and practical considerations are underexplored.

Clarity: Generally well written and easy to follow, but marred by typos and a few notational gaps in the algorithms. The authors should define all variables upon first use and clearly identify which variables constitute the auxiliary process.

Originality: High. This is the first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, and the dual connections to lifted chains and multi-step methods are insightful.

Significance: The theory is interesting and potentially influential for accelerating message-passing algorithms. However, significance for practitioners and the ML community would be strengthened by discussion of practicality and extensions beyond quadratic objectives.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and rigorous convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting.
- Clear conceptual connections bridging distinct literatures.
- Near-optimal asymptotic rates on graphs with geometry.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited to quadratic consensus; practicality and ML relevance not discussed.
- Missing references and notational gaps.
- Numerous typos and minor inaccuracies.

Questions for the authors:
- Practicality: When should Min-Sum Splitting be preferred over other distributed consensus/optimization methods? What are the communication/computation trade-offs?
- Limitations: What are the method’s limitations (e.g., assumptions, parameter tuning, dependence on global information)?
- Intuition: Provide more intuition for the auxiliary process—why it captures the edge-based dynamics and how it enables acceleration.
- Generality: Can the framework extend beyond quadratic consensus to more general ML loss functions and constraints?
- Relevance: Please articulate why this approach is of particular interest to the ML community and which applications would benefit.

In summary, the paper offers a solid theoretical contribution with elegant connections to established acceleration techniques. To maximize impact and clarity, the authors should correct the typos, fill in missing definitions and references, and discuss practicality, limitations, and relevance to ML beyond the quadratic setting.
