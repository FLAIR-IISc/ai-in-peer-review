This paper studies the convergence rate of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for the average consensus problem. The authors show that while the standard Min-Sum algorithm may not converge, the splitting variant can be tuned to achieve an accelerated, subdiffusive convergence rate. The core contribution is an analysis that proves a convergence time of O(D log(D/ε)) and establishes a conceptual link between this Min-Sum approach and other acceleration techniques, namely lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods.

While the paper is generally well-written and the connection between these different families of algorithms is interesting, the significance of the results and the completeness of the related work are areas of concern.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of the paper is the novel conceptual connection it establishes between the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm and other well-known acceleration techniques. By interpreting the splitting parameters as inducing a form of memory or directionality, the work provides a new perspective on how belief propagation-style algorithms can achieve accelerated convergence, unifying ideas from disparate areas of research.

**Weaknesses and Detailed Comments:**

1.  **Incomplete Literature Review:** The paper's positioning relative to the state of the art is weakened by the omission of several highly relevant and recent works on accelerated consensus and distributed optimization. A more thorough comparison is needed to accurately assess the paper's contribution. Specifically:
    *   The work of Olshevsky ("Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs...") achieves a competitive, if not better, rate of O(D log(1/ε)). This result should be discussed and explicitly compared against the rate derived in this paper.
    *   When discussing consensus optimization, the authors should acknowledge and compare their work to key papers establishing state-of-the-art linear convergence rates, such as Shi et al. ("On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization") and Scaman et al. ("Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks").
    *   The discussion on lifted Markov chains would be strengthened by citing the related work of França and Bento ("Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM").
    *   Other important references on fast distributed methods appear to be missing, for example, Shi et al. ("Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization") and Jakovetić et al. ("Fast distributed gradient methods").

2.  **Limited Significance of the Convergence Rate:** The main theoretical result—a convergence rate of O(D log(D/ε))—does not appear to offer a significant improvement over existing methods.
    *   As mentioned, the O(D log(1/ε)) rate was already established. The additional log(D) factor in the proposed bound makes it unclear if there is a practical improvement. In the worst-case scenario for sparse graphs, where the diameter D is on the order of the number of nodes n, this bound could be inferior to existing ones. The potential improvement seems marginal and may only come from a careful handling of the spectral gap. Ultimately, the worst-case bound is still O(n).

3.  **Originality of the Acceleration Technique:** The paper's claim to originality could be strengthened. The proposed "Auxiliary message-passing scheme" is functionally equivalent to applying a multi-step momentum method (specifically, a shift-register method) to the min-sum updates.
    *   Given that the analysis is specialized to the average consensus problem, the proof boils down to a spectral analysis of a linear system. This is a well-understood technique, and it is not clear what novel analytical insights are provided beyond reformulating a known acceleration scheme in the language of Min-Sum Splitting.
    *   The connection between consensus algorithms and momentum methods (e.g., Heavy Ball, Nesterov, Polyak) is already known in the community. For instance, the authors should refer to "Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links" by Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew, which discusses these connections.

4.  **Clarity and Minor Issues:**
    *   In the introduction, the statement that the diffusive rate is "optimal for graphs with good expansion properties" is vague. The provided example of a complete graph is not representative, as consensus is trivially achieved in a single step. The authors should be more precise about the class of graphs for which this optimality holds.
    *   The contribution of Proposition 2 seems supplementary. It serves as a straightforward technical bridge between the two algorithmic formulations rather than a key result in itself.
    *   On page 6 (line 243), the use of "directed edges" in the context of a "simple graph" might be confusing. While this is a standard construction, adding a brief clarification that each undirected edge is treated as two directed edges would improve readability.
    *   There appears to be a typo on page 6 (line 220): "Laplacian" should likely be "Lagrangian."
