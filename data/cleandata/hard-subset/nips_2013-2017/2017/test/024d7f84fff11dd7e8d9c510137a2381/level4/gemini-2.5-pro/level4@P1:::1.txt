This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum Splitting, an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol, to the distributed consensus problem. The authors first show that while the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge on graphs with cycles, the Min-Sum Splitting variant can be tuned to converge to the correct solution. The paper's main contribution is a theoretical analysis of the algorithm's convergence rate. The authors prove that with a proper choice of tuning parameters, Min-Sum Splitting achieves accelerated, subdiffusive convergence rates that match those of established shift-register methods. Furthermore, the paper establishes an insightful connection between the acceleration mechanism in Min-Sum Splitting and two other families of accelerated techniques: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization.

The paper is very well-written, and its contributions are clearly situated within the context of the extensive literature on distributed consensus. The results appear to be novel and represent a significant advance in understanding message-passing algorithms. The connection established between Min-Sum schemes, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step momentum methods is particularly elegant and bridges what seem to be disparate lines of research. Proving that the directionality introduced by the splitting parameters can be exploited to achieve acceleration is a key insight. The derivation of an auxiliary process on the graph nodes to analyze the dynamics of the primary algorithm on the edges is a clever analytical technique that enables the convergence rate analysis.

There is one minor detail in the presentation that could be clarified. On page 4, the authors provide an intuition for the "splitting" name by describing an equivalent objective function. They write that the algorithm corresponds to "splitting each term $\phi_{vw}$ into $\Gamma_{vw}$ terms, and each term $\phi_v$ into $\delta$ terms." This explanation is confusing because the parameters $\delta$ and $\Gamma_{vw}$ are introduced on the previous page as real numbers, not necessarily integers. Clarifying how this conceptual "splitting" into a non-integer number of terms should be interpreted would improve the paper's clarity.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a novel and important result. It provides the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and demonstrates that message-passing schemes can be competitive with state-of-the-art accelerated consensus algorithms. The work is technically sound and is likely to be of high interest to researchers in distributed optimization, message-passing algorithms, and machine learning.
