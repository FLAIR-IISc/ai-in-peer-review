This paper explores the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, a variant of the standard Min-Sum message-passing protocol, for accelerating distributed consensus optimization. Building on the reparametrization techniques from [34], the authors derive convergence rates for this algorithm when applied to the network averaging problem, which involves a quadratic objective function. The core of their analysis introduces a novel proof technique, wherein an auxiliary linear process operating on the graph's nodes is constructed to track the dynamics of the edge-based Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

The main contributions of this work are threefold. First, it presents the first formal convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. Second, leveraging this analysis, it proposes a Min-Sum protocol for consensus that achieves accelerated, subdiffusive convergence, improving on prior Min-Sum-based approaches for this problem [27]. Third, and perhaps most importantly, the paper establishes a conceptual link between Min-Sum schemes and two well-established but previously separate lines of research in accelerated consensus: lifted Markov chains and multi-step momentum methods in convex optimization.

**Quality:** The paper appears to be technically sound. The mathematical derivations, including the formulation of the algorithm as a linear system on the directed edges (Proposition 1), the introduction of the auxiliary node-based process (Proposition 2), and the final convergence rate analysis (Theorem 1), seem correct. The analysis effectively connects the algorithm's performance to the spectral properties of shift-register methods.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making the main arguments easy to follow. However, its clarity could be enhanced. The manuscript would benefit from careful proofreading to address several minor typos and grammatical errors (e.g., L34: "theirs neighbors" -> "their neighbors"; L183: "casted as" -> "cast as"). More substantially, the presentation of the algorithms could be more self-contained. For example, Algorithm 2 uses initial conditions R̂0 and r̂0 without specifying how they are initialized, and the notation R{vw} is not explicitly defined. As the auxiliary process in Algorithm 3 is central to the paper's contribution, explicitly identifying the state variables of this process would improve readability. A minor terminological point: the use of "Laplacian" and "Laplace multipliers" (L220-221) seems non-standard for this optimization context, where "Lagrangian" and "Lagrange multipliers" are expected.

**Originality:** The work's primary originality lies in bridging the gap between belief propagation-style methods and classical acceleration techniques in optimization. Using an auxiliary process as an analytical tool to analyze a message-passing algorithm is also a novel approach. The result that proper parameter tuning in a Belief Propagation framework can yield acceleration analogous to momentum is a significant and original insight.

**Significance:** The theoretical contribution is clear, but the paper's broader significance and impact could be argued more compellingly. The context within the existing literature is somewhat incomplete; for example, the discussions on distributed gradient methods (L41-43) and multi-step methods (L229, L232) would be stronger with citations to foundational works (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989; Polyak; Nesterov).

To strengthen the paper and clarify its contribution, the authors are encouraged to address the following points:
*   **Practicality and Comparison:** The analysis is purely theoretical. A discussion on the practical aspects of the proposed method is needed. In what scenarios would this Min-Sum approach be preferable to other accelerated distributed algorithms like ADMM or Nesterov-accelerated gradient descent?
*   **Limitations:** What are the limitations of this method? For instance, the optimal parameter choice depends on a global property of the graph (the spectral gap ρW), which seems to contradict the purely local nature of message-passing. How might this information be obtained in a distributed setting?
*   **Intuition:** The auxiliary process is a key analytical tool. What is the intuition behind its construction and why it successfully captures the dynamics of the edge-based algorithm? Providing this insight would deepen the reader's understanding.
*   **Generalizability and Relevance to Machine Learning:** The results are derived for quadratic objectives. This is a critical limitation, as many machine learning problems involve more general convex loss functions. Can this framework be extended? The authors should explicitly articulate why these findings are important for the machine learning community, for instance, by connecting them to potential applications in large-scale distributed training.

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically interesting result, establishing that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve accelerated convergence for the consensus problem. Its key strength is the novel connection it draws between message-passing and established acceleration paradigms. The primary weaknesses are the current focus on only quadratic objectives and the lack of discussion on the practical implications, limitations, and direct relevance to the machine learning community. Addressing these issues would significantly enhance the impact of the work.
