In this paper, the authors present a faster version of the min-sum message-passing protocol for solving consensus problems in distributed optimization. The authors use the re-parameterization technique proposed in [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013] and establish the convergence rate of the min-sum splitting algorithm for solving consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. The main tool used for analysis is the construction of an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the min-sum splitting algorithm.

The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows: (i) providing an analysis of the min-sum splitting algorithm using a new proof technique based on the introduction of an auxiliary process, (ii) designing a min-sum protocol for consensus problems that achieves better convergence than previously established results, and (iii) showing the connection between the proposed method and the raised Markov chain and the multi-step method in convex optimization.

The purpose and contributions of this paper are clear. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, but it contains several typos and grammatical errors (listed below). The proof of proposition 1 and 2, and theorem 1, seem to be correct.

It would have been interesting and useful if the authors had answered/commented on the following topics and discussed them in the paper:
- Although the paper is a theoretical paper, the authors should comment on the practicality of the method and when such a method should be used, in contrast to other distributed methods for consensus optimization.
- What are the limitations of the min-sum splitting method?
- What is the insight behind the use of auxiliary processes in the min-sum splitting method?
- The results presented in this paper are for quadratic objective functions with consensus problems. Can this framework be extended to solve more general consensus problems that often arise in machine learning?
- The authors should clearly state why such a method is of interest in the context of machine learning and the machine learning community.

In summary, this paper is a completely theoretical paper where the authors establish the convergence rate using a new proof technique and show the connection between their method and well-established methods in the literature. Overall, the concepts presented in this paper are interesting, but the practicality of the method and the insight behind the results are missing, as well as some rationale for the importance of these results for the machine learning community.
