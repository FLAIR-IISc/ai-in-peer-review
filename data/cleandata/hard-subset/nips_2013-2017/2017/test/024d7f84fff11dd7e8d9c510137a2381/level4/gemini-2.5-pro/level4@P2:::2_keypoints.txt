- Paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for average consensus, showing parameter tuning yields convergence and a "subdiffusive" accelerated asymptotic rate, and links to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods via an auxiliary node process.  
- Strength: Provides a novel perspective connecting Min-Sum message-passing to established acceleration techniques, framing splitting parameters as an acceleration mechanism.  
- Strength: Introduces an auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) that maps a 2m-dimensional edge system to a 2n-dimensional node system, simplifying spectral analysis.  
- Strength: Generally well-written and logically organized, with a clear progression from Min-Sum to consensus and convergence analysis.  
- Weakness: Main theoretical rate O(D log(D/ε)) offers marginal improvement over diffusive methods and is not clearly better than existing state-of-the-art O(D log(1/ε)) results (e.g., Olshevsky), and can be worse when D scales with n.  
- Weakness: Limited novelty because the acceleration reduces to known multi-step/shift-register updates; the paper mainly shows Min-Sum Splitting can replicate known acceleration for a linear consensus problem.  
- Weakness: Incomplete literature review—missing and not adequately comparing to key recent works on accelerated distributed optimization and lifting (e.g., Shi et al., Scaman et al., França & Bento, Jakovetić et al.).  
- Weakness: Minor clarity issues—vague claims about "good expansion properties" and ambiguous notation regarding undirected graphs vs directed message channels that should be clarified.  
- Pro-accept arguments: Novel link between Min-Sum Splitting and classical acceleration, elegant auxiliary analysis, and potential to inspire further reparameterization-based acceleration work.  
- Con-accept arguments: Marginal performance gains over prior art, limited technical novelty, and insufficient citation/comparison to relevant literature lead reviewer to lean toward rejection.  
- Recommendation: Reject in current form; revise to address missing literature and more clearly justify advantages of the Min-Sum approach beyond reproducing known dynamics.
