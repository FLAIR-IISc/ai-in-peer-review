This paper studies the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method applied to the average consensus problem. Overall, the paper is well-written, but the improvement over existing results appears incremental. Detailed comments are provided below.

(1) The paper states, “This rate is optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph. In this case the convergence time… is independent of the dimension of the problem.” For complete graphs, the convergence rate is 0 as all nodes converge to the average in a single step. Also, complete graphs are a special case and not representative of general graph structures. What general category of graphs achieves a convergence complexity independent of the number of nodes? What characteristics define a “good” graph in this context?

(2) In the paragraph discussing diffusive rates (near the top of page 2), the literature review should include “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control” by Olshevsky et al. The convergence rate achieved by this work should be reported and discussed appropriately (further explanation in comment 8). The reference offers competitive, and potentially superior, bounds compared to the submitted work.

(3) At the top of page 2, when discussing consensus optimization, important references such as “On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization” by Shi et al., and “Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks” by Scaman et al. should be cited. The authors should also report the state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization and their corresponding linear convergence rates.

(4) When discussing lifted graphs and Markov chains, the paper overlooks the closely related work “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM” by França and Bento.

(5) The content of the last paragraph of page 5 is well-established. The paper should cite “Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links” by Rabbat et al. Moreover, the connection between these variants and heavy-ball/Nesterov/Polyak methods is known within the field.

(6) The authors have omitted several important references regarding consensus optimization. For example, “Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization” by Shi et al., and “Fast distributed gradient methods” by Jakovetić et al. should be considered.

(7) Proposition 3 appears trivial and represents a minor contribution.

(8) The convergence rate achieved by this paper, O(D log(D/ε)), does not seem to offer a substantial improvement over the rate of O(D log(1/ε)) achieved by Linear Time Average Consensus (see comment 2). In the worst-case scenario (applicable to all graphs), where D ~ n, the bound is even worse than that of “Linear Time Average Consensus…”

(9) The reviewer suspects that the proposed “Auxiliary message-passing scheme” is simply Nesterov’s acceleration applied to the min-sum algorithm. While this is not necessarily a weakness, the analysis is performed for consensus, which reduces to analyzing a linear system and is not expected to be overly challenging. Given this, the paper's contribution becomes less clear.

(10) Any incremental improvement likely stems from a careful handling of the graph’s spectral gap. However, the worst-case bound remains O(n) because O(n) = O(D) for the set of all graphs with n nodes.

(11) Line 243 of page 6: The graph is described as simple, yet the discussion involves directed edges. This is potentially confusing.

(12) Typo at line 220 of page 6: "Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian."
