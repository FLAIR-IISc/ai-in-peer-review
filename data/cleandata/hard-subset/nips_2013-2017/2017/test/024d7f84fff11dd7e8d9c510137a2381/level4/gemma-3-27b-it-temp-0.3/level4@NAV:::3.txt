This paper introduces an accelerated Min-Sum message-passing protocol for distributed optimization, specifically addressing consensus problems. Leveraging reparametrization techniques from [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013], the authors derive convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm when applied to consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. A key analytical tool is the construction of an auxiliary linear process used to track the algorithm’s evolution.

The primary contributions of this work are threefold: (i) a novel proof technique, based on the introduction of an auxiliary process, for analyzing the Min-Sum splitting algorithm; (ii) a Min-Sum protocol for consensus achieving improved convergence compared to existing results; and (iii) a demonstration of the connection between the proposed method and lifted Markov chains, as well as multi-step methods in convex optimization. 

The motivation and contributions are clearly articulated, and the paper is generally well-written and accessible. However, several typos and grammatical errors are present (detailed below). The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1, appear to be correct.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
- While this is a theoretical contribution, discussing the practical implications of the method, and identifying scenarios where it would be preferable to other distributed consensus algorithms, would be valuable.
- Addressing the limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method would strengthen the paper.
- Providing intuition behind the use of the auxiliary process would enhance understanding.
- Exploring the potential for extending this framework to more general consensus problems, common in Machine Learning, would broaden the work’s impact.
- Explicitly articulating the relevance of this approach to the Machine Learning community would increase its appeal.

In conclusion, this paper presents a theoretical advancement in the analysis of Min-Sum splitting algorithms for consensus, establishing convergence rates using a novel proof technique and connecting the method to established optimization techniques. The ideas are interesting, but the paper would be strengthened by addressing the practicality and intuition behind the results, and by providing further justification for its relevance to the Machine Learning field.
