- Introduces Min-Sum Splitting, a variant of Min-Sum message-passing that guarantees convergence for graphs with cycles.  
- Shows ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge on cyclic graphs, while splitting modification converges to the optimal solution.  
- Main technical contribution: proper tuning of splitting parameters yields accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence matching shift-register methods.  
- Establishes formal connections between Min-Sum Splitting and two accelerated approaches: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order convex methods.  
- Proves convergence time O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles and tori, a square-root improvement over diffusive Θ(D² log(1/ε)) rates.  
- Introduces a novel proof technique using an auxiliary linear process tracking evolution on original graph nodes rather than directed edges.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous with clear theorems and detailed proofs (supplementary material provided).  
- Well-positioned in literature, relating to prior work on distributed consensus, Min-Sum variants, lifted chains, and multi-step methods.  
- Presentation is clear, precise, and reproducible for expert readers; organization and mathematical exposition are strong.  
- Originality is high: first formal link between Min-Sum message-passing and both lifted Markov chain and multi-step optimization approaches.  
- Significance is high given consensus problems' central role in distributed optimization and potential broader applicability.  
- Minor concern: ambiguity in describing splitting parameters Γvw and δ as real numbers vs. integer partitioning needs clarification.  
- Minor suggestion: add brief discussion of practical/ computational implementation considerations and how parameter choices affect complexity.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical novelty, rigorous accelerated-rate proofs, clear writing, and high significance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: minor presentation confusion about splitting parameters and limited practical implementation discussion.  
- Overall recommendation: accept.
