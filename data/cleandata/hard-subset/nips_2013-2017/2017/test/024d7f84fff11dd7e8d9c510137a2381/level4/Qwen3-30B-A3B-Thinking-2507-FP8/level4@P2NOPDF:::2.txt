

This paper analyzes the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method for the average consensus problem. While the technical content is generally sound, the claimed improvement over prior work appears marginal and the novelty is not sufficiently justified. Key concerns follow:

(1) The claim that "the convergence time does not depend on the dimension for graphs with good expansion properties" is misleading. For complete graphs (which have optimal expansion), convergence occurs in exactly one step (linear rate 0), making the dimension-independent bound trivial and non-representative. The paper fails to specify which *general* graph classes satisfy this property, undermining the core claim.

(2) The literature review omits Olshevsky's seminal work "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs..." (2015), which establishes a rate of \(O(D \log(1/\epsilon))\) for all graphs—superior to the paper's \(O(D \log(D/\epsilon))\). This omission is critical, as Olshevsky's bound is state-of-the-art and directly comparable.

(3) For consensus optimization, the introduction lacks foundational references: Shi et al.'s "On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM" (2015) and Scaman et al.'s "Optimal Algorithms for Smooth and Strongly Convex Distributed Optimization" (2017). The authors should explicitly compare their rate to the current state-of-the-art (e.g., \(O(D \log(1/\epsilon))\) for fixed graphs).

(4) The discussion of lifted graphs and Markov chains neglects Franca and Bento's "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM" (2016), which directly addresses similar techniques.

(5) The final paragraph of page 5 reiterates a well-known equivalence between consensus variants and momentum methods (Heavy Ball/Nesterov/Polyak). This should cite Rabbat et al.'s "Generalized Consensus Computation in Networked Systems with Erasure Links" (2005) rather than present as novel.

(6) Critical references for consensus optimization are missing: Shi et al.'s "Extra" (2015) and Jakovetic et al.'s "Fast Distributed Gradient Methods" (2014), both establishing faster rates than claimed here.

(7) Proposition 3 appears trivial—it follows directly from standard min-sum properties and constitutes a minor supplementary contribution.

(8) The claimed rate \(O(D \log(D/\epsilon))\) offers no significant improvement over Olshevsky's \(O(D \log(1/\epsilon))\). For worst-case graphs (where \(D = \Theta(n)\)), the paper's bound becomes \(O(n \log(n/\epsilon))\), which is strictly worse than Olshevsky's \(O(n \log(1/\epsilon))\).

(9) The "Auxiliary message-passing scheme" likely implements Nesterov acceleration on the min-sum algorithm. As Olshevsky already achieved \(O(D \log(1/\epsilon))\) via Nesterov acceleration, this approach does not represent a novel contribution. The analysis for consensus (reducing to linear systems) is not particularly challenging, further diminishing the paper's significance.

(10) The marginal improvement (if any) may stem from refined spectral gap handling, but the worst-case bound remains \(O(n)\) for all graphs with \(n\) nodes, as \(D = O(n)\). This negates the claimed dimension independence.

(11) Line 243 (page 6) uses directed edges for a "simple graph," causing confusion. Simple graphs are undirected by definition.

(12) Typo at line 220: "Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian."

**After Rebuttal:** The authors addressed technical points (e.g., clarifying graph classes), but the core issue—lack of meaningful improvement over Olshevsky's bound—remains unaltered. The contribution's novelty is not sufficiently substantiated. The evaluation score is unchanged.
