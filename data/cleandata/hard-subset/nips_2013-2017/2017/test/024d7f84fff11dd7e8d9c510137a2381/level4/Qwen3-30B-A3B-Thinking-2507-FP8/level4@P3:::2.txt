

This paper investigates the convergence rates of Min-Sum Splitting for the consensus problem, claiming subdiffusive acceleration over traditional diffusive methods. The authors demonstrate that proper tuning of splitting parameters yields convergence rates of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, where D is the graph diameter. They establish connections between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first-order optimization methods.

The paper makes several notable contributions. First, it provides the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, establishing a novel proof technique through an auxiliary message-passing scheme. Second, it identifies a meaningful connection between Min-Sum schemes and established accelerated methods, showing that Min-Sum Splitting can be interpreted as a process on a lifted space (directed edges) that introduces directionality similar to non-reversible Markov chains. Third, it demonstrates how the splitting parameters enable a memory term that relates directly to multi-step first-order methods like shift-register techniques.

The paper is well-structured and technically sound. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with clear propositions and proofs (though some details are relegated to supplementary material). The writing is generally clear, though some sections could benefit from more precise language. The connection to prior work on lifted Markov chains and accelerated optimization methods is particularly valuable.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that affect its contribution. The claimed improvement in convergence rate (O(D log(D/ε)) versus existing O(D log(1/ε)) bounds) is less impressive than presented. As noted by Olshevsky (2015), linear time average consensus methods achieve O(D log(1/ε)) rates, which is superior to the paper's O(D log(D/ε)) bound. In the worst case (where D=Θ(n)), this means the paper's bound is actually worse than established results. The authors should clarify this limitation and provide a more nuanced comparison of their results with the state-of-the-art.

The literature review is notably incomplete. The authors fail to cite key references that establish comparable or better convergence rates, including Olshevsky (2015) on linear time average consensus, Scaman et al. (2017) on optimal distributed optimization algorithms, and Shi et al. (2015) on Extra, an exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus. The connection to lifted Markov chains is also incomplete; Franca and Bento (2016) have already explored the relationship between distributed ADMM and lifted Markov chains, which should be acknowledged. The paper also misses important references on consensus optimization methods (Jakovetic et al., 2014; Shi et al., 2015) and the connection between consensus and heavy ball/Nesterov methods.

The claim that the "auxiliary message-passing scheme" is a novel contribution is questionable. The scheme essentially implements Nesterov's acceleration for the Min-Sum algorithm, which is a well-known technique. The analysis for consensus problems boils down to analyzing a linear system, which is not particularly difficult given the problem's structure. The authors should better contextualize this contribution within the existing literature on accelerated methods.

The paper contains some confusing statements. For instance, the claim that "the convergence time scales independently of the dimension of the problem" for complete graphs is misleading - complete graphs achieve convergence in one step, making them unrepresentative of general graphs. The authors should clarify what class of graphs actually achieves dimension-independent convergence.

The paper also contains minor issues: a typo at line 220 (Laplacian→Lagrangian), and a confusing use of directed edges for simple graphs (line 243). The claim about "general category of graphs" with good expansion properties needs clarification.

In summary, the paper makes a technically sound contribution to understanding Min-Sum Splitting for consensus problems, with a novel proof technique and meaningful connections to existing accelerated methods. However, the claimed improvement in convergence rate is less significant than presented, and the literature review is insufficiently comprehensive. The paper would be strengthened by:

1. A more accurate comparison with state-of-the-art methods (particularly Olshevsky 2015 and Scaman et al. 2017)
2. Proper contextualization of the auxiliary scheme within existing acceleration techniques
3. Clarification of the actual improvement over existing methods
4. Inclusion of key references in the literature review

The technical contribution is sound but appears to be incremental rather than transformative. While the connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods are interesting, they don't constitute a major leap forward given the existing literature. The paper's significance is therefore limited.

Overall, the paper is well-written and technically correct, but the claimed improvement in convergence rate is overstated compared to existing work. The authors should revise the paper to provide a more accurate assessment of their contribution relative to the state-of-the-art in distributed optimization.
