- Paper proposes an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for distributed consensus, analyzes it for quadratic objectives, and introduces an auxiliary linear process as the main analytic tool.  
- Draws connections between the method and lifted Markov chains and multi-step (momentum-type) optimization methods.  

Strengths:
- Clear motivation and well-structured exposition.  
- Novel and elegant proof technique via an auxiliary linear process.  
- Demonstrates improved convergence for consensus versus prior Min-Sum results.  
- Insightful connections to lifted chains and classical acceleration methods.  
- Propositions 1–2 and Theorem 1 appear correctly proved.

Weaknesses / areas for improvement:
- Clarity: several algorithmic quantities are insufficiently defined and some initializations are missing; mapping to the auxiliary process is not explicit.  
- Scope: results are restricted to quadratic consensus and do not address broader distributed optimization settings.  
- Practicality: lacks guidance on when the method is preferable and its trade-offs versus standard distributed methods.  
- Intuition: the accelerating role of the auxiliary process is not clearly articulated.  
- Presentation: numerous typos and minor grammatical errors distract from the paper.  
- Related work: several relevant references on distributed (sub)gradient and exact first-order methods are omitted.

Requests for clarification / suggested additions:
- Explain practical regimes and use cases where Min-Sum Splitting outperforms alternatives (e.g., ADMM, distributed gradient, EXTRA).  
- Describe main limitations: sensitivity to topology, parameter tuning, memory/communication overhead, robustness to noise/asynchrony.  
- Provide high-level intuition for how the auxiliary linear process yields acceleration and its relation to lifting and multi-step methods.  
- Discuss extensions beyond quadratics to general convex or composite objectives and outline obstacles/partial results.  
- Argue relevance for ML applications (decentralized training, sensor networks, federated settings).  
- Algorithmic clarity: define R_{vw} and r_{vw} in Algorithm 2 and initialize hat{R}^0, hat{r}^0; explicitly identify auxiliary-process variables in Algorithm 3.  
- Notation check: likely meant “Lagrangian” and “Lagrange multipliers” instead of “Laplacian” and “Laplace multipliers” on lines 220–221.

Missing references to add:
- Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis, Parallel and Distributed Computation: Numerical Methods (1989).  
- Srinivasan et al., Incremental stochastic subgradient algorithms for convex optimization (2009).  
- Shi et al., EXTRA: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization (2015).  
- Polyak’s heavy-ball method and Nesterov’s accelerated methods, plus citation for “original literature” referenced on line 170.

Typos and grammatical fixes (examples):
- Line 34: “theirs neighbors” → “their neighbors”; Line 174: “double-stochastic” → “doubly-stochastic”; Line 183: “casted” → “cast as”; plus many other line-specific corrections listed by reviewer.

Overall assessment:
- Solid theoretical contribution introducing a useful analytic perspective and nontrivial convergence guarantees, but needs clearer algorithms/notation, stronger discussion of practical relevance and limitations, more intuition for the auxiliary process, expanded related work, and presentation fixes.
