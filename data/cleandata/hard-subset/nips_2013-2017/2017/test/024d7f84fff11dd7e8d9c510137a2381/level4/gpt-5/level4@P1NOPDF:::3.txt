This paper presents an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for consensus problems in distributed optimization. Building on the reparametrization techniques of Ruozzi and Tatikonda (2013), the authors establish convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm on consensus problems with quadratic objectives. The central analytical tool is an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of Min-Sum Splitting.

The main contributions are:
- An analysis of Min-Sum Splitting using a new proof technique based on an auxiliary process.
- A Min-Sum protocol for consensus that achieves faster convergence than previously reported results.
- Clarified connections between the proposed method, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The motivation and contributions are clear. The paper is generally well written and easy to follow, though it contains several typos and grammatical errors (listed below). The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear correct.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Practicality: Although the paper is theoretical, please comment on when this method is preferable to other distributed consensus methods (e.g., in terms of network topology, communication/computation trade-offs, problem scaling, or robustness to noise).
- Limitations: What are the main limitations of Min-Sum Splitting (e.g., sensitivity to parameter tuning, dependence on graph structure, memory requirements, or extension beyond quadratic objectives)?
- Intuition: Provide more intuition for why the auxiliary linear process yields acceleration and how it relates to lifted chains and multi-step momentum methods.
- Generality: Can this framework be extended beyond quadratic consensus to more general objectives commonly encountered in machine learning (e.g., composite, nonsmooth, or stochastic settings)?
- Relevance to ML: Please articulate the implications for machine learning practice (e.g., decentralized training, federated optimization, scalability, or privacy-preserving settings).

In summary, this is a purely theoretical paper that establishes convergence rates via a new proof technique and clarifies the relationship to established accelerated methods. The results are interesting, but the paper would benefit from improved clarity in the algorithmic descriptions, correction of the noted typos, inclusion of missing references, and a more thorough discussion of practicality, intuition, and relevance to the machine learning community.
