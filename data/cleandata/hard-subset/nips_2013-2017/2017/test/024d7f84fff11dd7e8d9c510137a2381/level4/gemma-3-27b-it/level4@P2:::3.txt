This paper presents an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for solving consensus problems in distributed optimization. The authors leverage reparametrization techniques from [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013] to establish convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. A key analytical tool is the construction of an auxiliary linear process used to track the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

The main contributions are: (i) analysis of the Min-Sum splitting algorithm using a novel proof technique based on an auxiliary process, (ii) design of a Min-Sum protocol for consensus achieving improved convergence compared to existing results, and (iii) demonstration of connections between the proposed method and lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The motivation and contributions are clear, and the paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. However, several minor typos and grammatical errors exist (detailed below). The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appear sound.

**Minor Comments & Requests for Clarification:**

*   Lines 220 and 221: Are “Lagrangian” and “Lagrange multipliers” intended instead of “Laplacian” and “Laplace multipliers”?
*   The three algorithms presented utilize quantities that are not consistently explained. For example, what are R{vw} and r{vw} in Algorithm 2? Additionally, the initial conditions  \hat{R}^0 and \hat{r}^0 in Algorithm 2 are not explicitly defined. Given the central role of the auxiliary linear process, the correspondence between variables in Algorithm 3 and this process should be clearly stated.
*   Several references appear to be missing. Specifically:
    *   Lines 41 and 43: References on (sub)gradient methods for consensus optimization are lacking. Consider including:
        *   Bertsekas and Tsitsiklis, *Parallel and distributed computation: numerical methods*, 1989
        *   Srinivasan et al., *Incremental stochastic subgradient algorithms for convex optimization*, 2009
        *   Shi et al., *Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization*, 2015
        *   (and others)
    *   Line 170: A broader context of related literature would be helpful.
    *   Line 229: Mention of Polyak’s work on the heavy-ball method is missing.
    *   Line 232: Reference to Nesterov’s accelerated gradient methods is needed.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   While this is a theoretical paper, a discussion on the practical applicability of the method and scenarios where it would be preferable to other distributed consensus algorithms would be valuable.
*   What are the limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method?
*   What is the intuition behind employing the auxiliary process in the Min-Sum Splitting method?
*   The results are currently presented for consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. Could this framework be extended to handle more general consensus problems common in Machine Learning?
*   The authors should elaborate on the relevance of this work to the Machine Learning community and why it is of interest in that context.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretical advance in understanding the convergence properties of Min-Sum message-passing for consensus. The proposed approach and analysis are interesting, and the connections to related techniques (lifted Markov chains, accelerated methods) are valuable. However, the lack of discussion regarding practical implications and the limited engagement with the Machine Learning community weaken the impact of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly clarifying the notation and adding relevant references.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical contribution to the analysis of Min-Sum splitting.
*   Establishment of convergence rates and connection to existing accelerated methods.
*   Clear presentation of the problem and proposed solution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of practical implications and limitations.
*   Insufficient justification of relevance to the Machine Learning community.
*   Minor typographical errors and areas requiring clarification.
