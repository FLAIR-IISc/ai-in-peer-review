### Summary

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum Splitting, a generalized version of the Min-Sum message-passing algorithm, to the distributed consensus problem, specifically focusing on the network averaging case. The authors first note that the standard Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge on graphs containing cycles, a known limitation. They then demonstrate that the Min-Sum Splitting variant not only ensures convergence but, with a proper tuning of its parameters, achieves an accelerated, subdiffusive rate of convergence.

The core contributions are both theoretical and conceptual. Theoretically, the paper provides a rigorous analysis of the convergence rate of Min-Sum Splitting for this problem. The authors introduce a novel proof technique based on an "auxiliary message-passing scheme" that tracks the algorithm's evolution on the graph's nodes rather than its directed edges. This simplifies the analysis and allows them to show that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve an asymptotic convergence time of O(D log(D/ε)) on graphs like cycles and tori (where D is the graph diameter). This represents a square-root improvement over classical "diffusive" methods, which scale as O(D² log(1/ε)), and matches the performance of accelerated shift-register methods.

Conceptually, the paper draws a compelling and novel connection between Min-Sum schemes and two previously distinct lines of research on accelerated consensus: lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first-order optimization methods (e.g., Nesterov's acceleration, Polyak's heavy ball). The authors argue that Min-Sum Splitting implicitly operates on a lifted space (the directed edges) and introduces a tunable directionality, akin to non-reversible lifted Markov chains, without the overhead of explicit graph construction. Simultaneously, the dynamics of the auxiliary process on the nodes resemble a multi-step method with memory, directly linking it to accelerated gradient techniques. This unification of perspectives is a significant conceptual contribution.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by a rigorous theoretical analysis. The derivation of the linear message-passing updates for the quadratic case (Algorithm 2) and the subsequent introduction of the auxiliary process on the nodes (Algorithm 3) are clear and methodologically sound. This auxiliary process is a key innovation, as it makes the spectral analysis tractable by relating the convergence of the 2m-dimensional system on the edges to a 2n-dimensional system on the nodes, whose properties are more directly linked to the graph structure via the matrix Γ. The main result, Theorem 1, precisely characterizes the accelerated convergence rate and its dependence on the spectral properties of the underlying graph operator W. The authors are careful to connect their results to established work on shift-register methods, borrowing from their spectral analysis, which grounds the work in existing literature. The work is presented as a complete theoretical contribution for the network averaging problem.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and clear. The introduction provides an excellent overview of the consensus problem, the limitations of standard diffusive algorithms, and the existing landscape of accelerated methods. This effectively situates the paper's contributions and makes the work accessible even to readers not deeply specialized in message-passing algorithms. The logical flow from the general Min-Sum Splitting algorithm to its specific application for consensus, followed by the introduction of the auxiliary scheme and the final convergence analysis, is easy to follow. The mathematical notation is consistent and clearly defined. The main contributions are explicitly listed, which further enhances clarity.

### Originality

The work is highly original. While Min-Sum Splitting itself was proposed in prior work [34, 35], this paper is the first to provide a convergence rate analysis for it. More importantly, the central contribution is the novel connection it establishes between message-passing schemes (specifically Min-Sum) and the two primary paradigms for accelerating consensus. This conceptual bridge is new and insightful, suggesting that the "splitting" reparameterization in belief propagation can serve as a general mechanism for acceleration. The use of an auxiliary process to analyze the algorithm's dynamics is also a novel analytical technique in this context. The results significantly advance upon prior work on Min-Sum for consensus [27], which provided less general results (for regular graphs), achieved slower rates, and did not establish this broader connection to other accelerated methods.

### Significance

The results are significant for several reasons.
1.  **Performance:** The paper demonstrates that a simple, distributed message-passing algorithm can achieve accelerated convergence rates that are competitive with state-of-the-art specialized methods. This elevates Min-Sum from a general-purpose heuristic to a provably fast algorithm for this fundamental distributed computing primitive.
2.  **Conceptual Unification:** The connection to lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods provides a new perspective on acceleration in distributed systems. It suggests that message-passing on directed edges with memory or directionality is a powerful and perhaps unifying mechanism. This is likely to inspire follow-up work exploring this connection in other problems beyond consensus.
3.  **Future Research:** The work opens up clear avenues for future investigation, such as generalizing the acceleration mechanism to other optimization problems solvable with Min-Sum or designing data-dependent methods for choosing the splitting parameters in a distributed fashion.

### Arguments for Acceptance (Strengths)

*   **Novel Conceptual Contribution:** Establishes a previously unknown and insightful connection between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods.
*   **Strong Theoretical Results:** Provides the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, proving it achieves an accelerated, subdiffusive rate that matches that of shift-register methods.
*   **Innovative Proof Technique:** The introduction of the auxiliary message-passing scheme on the nodes is an elegant and effective analytical tool that simplifies a complex problem.
*   **High Clarity and Quality:** The paper is very well-written, placing the contributions clearly within the context of related work.

### Arguments against Acceptance (Weaknesses)

*   **Minor Lack of Clarity on Parameter Interpretation:** There is a small point of confusion in the motivation for the "splitting" name, which could be easily clarified.
*   **Practicality of Parameter Selection:** The optimal choice for the acceleration parameter γ depends on a global property of the graph (the spectral radius ρ_W). The paper does not discuss how this parameter might be computed or estimated in a fully distributed setting, which is a key practical challenge for this class of accelerated algorithms.

### Detailed Comments

The paper is excellent and presents a valuable contribution. My only minor suggestion for improvement relates to a point of clarity in the presentation.

On page 4, when describing the equivalent objective function that motivates the Min-Sum Splitting name, the authors state: "...splitting each term φ_vw into Γ_vw terms, and each term φ_v into δ terms...". This interpretation naturally implies that δ and Γ_vw are positive integers. However, throughout the rest of the paper, including in Algorithm 1 and the main theoretical results, these are treated as general real numbers. For instance, the optimal γ in Theorem 1 is real-valued. It would improve clarity to briefly acknowledge this distinction, perhaps by stating that while the algorithm was originally motivated by an integer-based splitting of cost functions, it is well-defined for real-valued parameters, and the analysis in this paper proceeds with this more general real-valued formulation. This is a very minor point in an otherwise outstanding paper.
