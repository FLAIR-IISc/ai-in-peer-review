This paper studies the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method applied to the average consensus problem. The core idea is to demonstrate that a modified min-sum algorithm, through a splitting technique, can achieve accelerated convergence compared to traditional diffusive methods. While the paper is generally well-written, the claimed improvement in convergence rate appears incremental, and the paper lacks sufficient contextualization within the broader landscape of consensus algorithms.

The paper begins by introducing the min-sum splitting algorithm and its application to consensus, framing it as a solution to limitations of standard min-sum methods on graphs with cycles. The authors then establish convergence rates for this algorithm, drawing connections to lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods in convex optimization. A key technical contribution is the introduction of an auxiliary message-passing scheme to facilitate analysis of the algorithm’s convergence.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations and proofs appear rigorous, and the connection to existing techniques like lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods is thoughtfully presented. The introduction of the auxiliary message-passing scheme is a clever analytical tool.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, making the main ideas accessible. The notation is consistent, and the algorithm descriptions are easy to follow.
*   **Novelty:**  The specific application of min-sum splitting to consensus and the analysis of its convergence rate represent a contribution, though its significance is debatable (see weaknesses). The connection made to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods is interesting.
*   **Completeness:** The paper presents a complete analysis of the proposed algorithm within the scope of its stated problem.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Significance:** The reported convergence rate of *O(D log(D/ε))* does not appear to represent a substantial improvement over existing results. The claim that this is an improvement over *O(D log(1/ε))* is misleading in the worst case (where *D* is proportional to the number of nodes *n*), as the rate becomes *O(n log(n/ε))*, which is not necessarily better than existing bounds.
*   **Insufficient Literature Review:** The paper lacks a comprehensive review of the relevant literature. Several important works are missing, diminishing the perceived novelty of the contribution. Specifically:
    *   The paper should discuss and properly cite "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control" by Olshevsky et al. This work achieves competitive or superior bounds under certain conditions.
    *   Important references on consensus optimization, such as “On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization” (Shi et al.) and “Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks” (Scaman et al.), are missing.
    *   The discussion of lifted graphs and Markov chains neglects “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM” (Franca and Bento).
    *   Related works on accelerated methods (e.g., Extra, fast distributed gradient methods) should be mentioned.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** The “auxiliary message-passing scheme” appears to be a restatement of Nesterov’s acceleration applied to the min-sum algorithm. While demonstrating this connection is valuable, the analysis is performed for a relatively simple problem (consensus, which reduces to analyzing a linear system), diminishing the perceived novelty.
*   **Confusing Terminology:** The use of "directed edges" in a "simple graph" (page 6, line 243) is slightly confusing and requires clarification.
*   **Minor Errors:** A minor typo exists on page 6, line 220 ("Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian").

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound analysis of a modified min-sum algorithm for consensus.
*   The connection to established techniques (lifted Markov chains, accelerated methods) provides valuable context.
*   The auxiliary message-passing scheme is a useful analytical tool.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The claimed improvement in convergence rate is incremental and potentially misleading.
*   The paper lacks a thorough review of the relevant literature, failing to adequately position the contribution within the existing body of work.
*   The core idea (applying Nesterov’s acceleration) is not entirely novel in the context of consensus optimization.
*   The overall contribution feels more like an application of existing techniques than a significant breakthrough.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid, technically sound piece of work. However, the incremental nature of the contribution, combined with the incomplete literature review, makes it a weak submission for NIPS. While the paper is not fundamentally flawed, it does not demonstrate sufficient novelty or impact to warrant acceptance in a highly competitive conference like NIPS.  I recommend **Reject** with the encouragement to significantly expand the literature review and more clearly articulate the unique contributions of this work in relation to existing methods.
