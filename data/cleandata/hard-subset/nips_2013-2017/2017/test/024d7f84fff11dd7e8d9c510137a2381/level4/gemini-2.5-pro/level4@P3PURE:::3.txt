This paper presents an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for solving distributed consensus optimization problems. Using reparametrization techniques from [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013], the authors establish convergence rates for their Min-Sum Splitting algorithm on consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. The primary analytical tool is a novel proof technique based on the construction of an auxiliary linear process that tracks the algorithm's evolution.

The main contributions of the paper can be summarized as follows: (i) an analysis of the Min-Sum splitting algorithm using a new proof technique based on an auxiliary process; (ii) the design of a Min-Sum protocol for consensus problems that achieves better convergence rates than previously established results; and (iii) an exploration of the connection between the proposed method and lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The paper's motivation and contributions are clear. It is well-written and generally easy to follow, and the proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appear to be correct. However, the work could be significantly strengthened by addressing the points below.

**Major Comments and Questions for the Authors**

The paper would benefit from a broader discussion regarding the practical implications and scope of the proposed method.
*   **Practicality:** Although this is a theoretical paper, the authors should comment on the practicality of the method. When should this algorithm be used as opposed to other distributed methods for consensus optimization (e.g., subgradient-based methods)?
*   **Limitations:** What are the key limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method?
*   **Intuition:** The auxiliary process is central to the analysis. What is the intuition behind its use in the Min-Sum Splitting method? A more conceptual explanation would improve the paper's accessibility.
*   **Generalization:** The results provided are for consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. Can this framework be extended to solve the more general consensus problems that often arise in Machine Learning?
*   **Relevance to Machine Learning:** The authors should clearly state why this approach is of interest in the context of Machine Learning and for the NIPS community.

**Minor Comments and Technical Clarity**

*   **Algorithm Descriptions:** The algorithms are presented without a full description of the quantities involved. For example, R{vw} and r{vw} in Algorithm 2 are not defined. In the same algorithm, the quantities \hat{R}^0 and \hat{r}^0 do not appear to be initialized. Moreover, since the auxiliary linear process is central to the paper's analysis, the authors should explicitly state which variables in Algorithm 3 correspond to it.
*   **Terminology:** On lines 220 and 221, do the authors mean "Lagrangian" and "Lagrange multipliers" instead of "Laplacian" and "Laplace multipliers"?

**Missing References**

The paper appears to be missing several key citations:
*   **Lines 41 and 43 (Consensus Optimization):** There are several foundational references on (sub)gradient methods that could be included, for example:
    *   Bertsekas and Tsitsiklis, "Parallel and distributed computation: numerical methods," 1989
    *   Sundhar Ram et al., "Incremental stochastic subgradient algorithms for convex optimization," 2009
    *   Wei Shi et al., "Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization," 2015
*   **Line 170:** The phrase "The original literature..." should be accompanied by a citation.
*   **Line 229:** The connection to the Heavy-ball method should cite the work of Polyak.
*   **Line 232:** The discussion of accelerated methods should reference the foundational work of Nesterov.

**Summary**

In conclusion, this is a strong theoretical paper that establishes convergence rates for an accelerated Min-Sum algorithm using a novel proof technique and shows interesting connections to well-established methods. However, the paper's impact could be significantly enhanced by providing more intuition, discussing the method's practical implications and limitations, and more clearly justifying its relevance to the Machine Learning community.
