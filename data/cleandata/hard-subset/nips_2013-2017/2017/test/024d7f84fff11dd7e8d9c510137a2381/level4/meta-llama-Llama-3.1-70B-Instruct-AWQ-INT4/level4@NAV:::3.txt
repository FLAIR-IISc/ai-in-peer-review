This paper presents an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for distributed optimization consensus problems. The authors employ reparametrization techniques from [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013] and derive convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm when applied to quadratic objective functions. A key analytical tool is the construction of an auxiliary linear process that mirrors the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

The paper's primary contributions are threefold: (i) it introduces a novel proof technique based on an auxiliary process for analyzing the Min-Sum splitting algorithm, (ii) it designs a Min-Sum protocol for consensus problems that achieves improved convergence rates over existing results, and (iii) it establishes connections between the proposed method and lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The paper's motivation and contributions are clearly articulated. The writing is clear and easy to follow, although it contains several typos and grammatical errors (detailed below). The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appear to be correct.

It would be beneficial for the authors to address the following points:
- Comment on the practicality of the method and its suitability for specific distributed optimization scenarios.
- Discuss the limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method.
- Provide intuition behind the use of the auxiliary process in the Min-Sum Splitting method.
- Explore the possibility of extending the framework to solve more general consensus problems commonly encountered in Machine Learning.
- Clearly articulate the relevance and importance of this approach for the Machine Learning community.

In summary, this paper is a theoretical contribution that establishes convergence rates using a novel proof technique and highlights connections to established methods. While the ideas presented are intriguing, the paper would benefit from discussions on practicality, intuition, and the significance of the results for the Machine Learning community.
