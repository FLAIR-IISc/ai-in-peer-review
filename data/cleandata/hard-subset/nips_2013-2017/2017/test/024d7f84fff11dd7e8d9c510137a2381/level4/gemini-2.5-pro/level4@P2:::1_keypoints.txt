- Paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for distributed consensus (network averaging) and shows it converges where standard Min-Sum fails on loopy graphs.
- With proper tuning, Min-Sum Splitting achieves accelerated, subdiffusive convergence.
- Introduces an auxiliary node-based message-passing scheme to simplify analysis of edge-based dynamics.
- Proves asymptotic convergence time O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori, a square-root improvement over diffusive O(D² log(1/ε)).
- Matches performance of accelerated shift-register methods via spectral analysis.
- Conceptually links Min-Sum Splitting to lifted (non-reversible) Markov chains and multi-step first-order (accelerated) optimization methods.
- Argues splitting implicitly lifts to directed-edge space and introduces tunable directionality and memory.
- High technical quality: rigorous analysis, clear derivation of linear updates (Algorithm 2) and auxiliary process (Algorithm 3).
- Theorem 1 precisely characterizes accelerated rate in terms of graph spectral properties.
- Exceptionally clear and well-written; good organization and consistent notation.
- Highly original: first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and novel conceptual unification.
- Significant impact: demonstrates a simple distributed message-passing algorithm can be provably accelerated and suggests broad research directions.
- Strengths: novel conceptual contribution, strong theoretical results, innovative proof technique, high clarity.
- Weaknesses: minor confusion over the "splitting" parameter interpretation (integer vs real) and lack of discussion on distributed/ practical estimation of the global tuning parameter γ.
