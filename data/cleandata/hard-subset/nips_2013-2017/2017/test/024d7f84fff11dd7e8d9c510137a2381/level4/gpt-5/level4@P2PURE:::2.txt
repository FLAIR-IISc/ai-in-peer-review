This paper analyzes the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method for the average consensus problem. Overall the paper reads well, but the improvement in the main result does not appear very significant. Detailed comments follow.

(1) The paper states: “This rate is optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph. In this case the convergence time, i.e., the number of iterations required to reach a prescribed level of error accuracy in the … of the dimension of the problem, as …”. For complete graphs, the linear contraction factor is 0 (everyone converges to the average in one step), so this example is too special to be representative. Please clarify:
- For which general families of graphs does the complexity not depend on the dimension (number of nodes)?
- What general class of graphs do you consider “good”?

(2) In the same paragraph as in comment (1), the literature review should include “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control” by Olshevsky. Its convergence rate should be reported properly (see comment (8) for more detail). That reference achieves a bound that is competitive with, or even better than, the result in this submission.

(3) At the top of page 2, for consensus optimization, the following important references should be cited:
- “On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization” by Shi, Ling, Kun, Wu, and Yin.
- “Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks” by Scaman, Bach, Bubeck, Lee, and Massoulié.
In addition, please summarize the state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization and report their (linear) convergence rates.

(4) In discussing lifted graphs and Markov chains, the paper omits a closely related reference: “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM” by Franca and Bento.

(5) The content of the last paragraph on page 5 is a long-known fact; it should cite “Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links” by Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew. Moreover, the connection between those variants and Heavy-ball/Nesterov/Polyak acceleration is well known in the field.

(6) Several important references on consensus optimization are missing, e.g.:
- “EXTRA: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization” by Shi, Ling, Wu, and Yin.
- “Fast distributed gradient methods” by Jakovetic, J. Xavier, and Moura.

(7) Proposition 3 appears trivial and only a supplementary contribution.

(8) The rate achieved in this paper, D log(D/eps), does not appear to significantly improve upon the D log(1/eps) rate achieved in “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control” (see comment (2)). In the worst case (over all graphs), where D ~ n, your bound is even worse than that achieved in that reference.

(9) The “Linear Time Average Consensus …” paper improves the bound via Nesterov acceleration. I suspect the proposed “auxiliary message-passing scheme” is essentially Nesterov’s acceleration applied to min-sum. This is acceptable, but since the analysis is for consensus (reducing to a linear system), it is not especially hard. In light of this, the contribution of the paper is unclear.

(10) The small improvement may stem from a careful handling of the spectral gap. However, the worst-case bound remains O(n), since O(n) = O(D) over the set of all graphs with n nodes.

(11) Line 243 on page 6: the graph is simple, yet the figure uses directed edges. This is confusing.

(12) Typo at line 220 on page 6: “Laplacian” should be “Lagrangian”.

After rebuttal:
I am satisfied with the authors’ response, but my evaluation score remains unchanged.
