This paper introduces an accelerated version of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol designed for solving consensus problems within distributed optimization. Leveraging reparametrization techniques from [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013], the authors derive convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. A core component of their analysis is the construction of an auxiliary linear process used to track the algorithm’s evolution.

The key contributions of this work are threefold: (i) a novel proof technique, employing an auxiliary process, for analyzing the Min-Sum splitting algorithm; (ii) a Min-Sum protocol for consensus achieving improved convergence compared to existing results; and (iii) a demonstration of the connection between the proposed method and related concepts in convex optimization, specifically lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods. 

The motivation and contributions are clearly articulated, and the paper is generally well-written and accessible. However, several minor typographical and grammatical errors are present (detailed below). The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, as well as Theorem 1, appear to be correct.

**Other Comments:**
- Lines 220 and 221: Consider whether "Lagrangian" and "Lagrange multipliers" would be more appropriate than "Laplacian" and "Laplace multipliers."
- The paper presents three algorithms, but some quantities lack sufficient explanation. For instance, the meanings of R{vw} and r{vw} in Algorithm 2 are unclear. Furthermore, \hat{R}^0 and \hat{r}^0 in Algorithm 2 do not appear to be initialized. Given the centrality of the auxiliary linear process to the analysis, the correspondence between its variables and those in Algorithm 3 should be explicitly stated.

-- Line 170: Elaborate on "The original literature…" with specific citations.

**Questions for the Authors:**
- While this is a theoretical contribution, a discussion of the method’s practical implications and appropriate use cases, compared to other distributed consensus methods, would be valuable.
- What are the inherent limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method?
- What is the intuition motivating the use of the auxiliary process within the Min-Sum Splitting method?
- The current results focus on quadratic objective functions. Could this framework be generalized to address more complex consensus problems common in Machine Learning?
- A clearer articulation of the relevance and potential impact of this work for the Machine Learning community is needed.

In summary, this paper provides a theoretical analysis of an accelerated Min-Sum protocol, establishing convergence rates using a novel proof technique and connecting it to established methods. The ideas are intriguing, but the paper would benefit from addressing the practicality of the method, providing further intuition, and clearly justifying its significance within the field of Machine Learning.
