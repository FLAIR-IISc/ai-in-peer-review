In this paper, the authors present an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for solving consensus problems in distributed optimization. They leverage the reparametrization techniques proposed in [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013] and establish convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. The main analytical tool employed is the construction of an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

The main contributions of the paper can be summarized as follows: (i) analysis of the Min-Sum splitting algorithm using a novel proof technique based on the introduction of an auxiliary process; (ii) design of a Min-Sum protocol for consensus problems achieving improved convergence compared to previously established results; and (iii) demonstration of the connection between the proposed method and lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The motivation and contributions of the paper are clear. The paper is well written and easy to follow, though it contains several typos and grammatical errors (listed below). The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appear to be correct.

Other minor comments:

- The authors present three algorithms, but the quantities involved are not always explained or defined. For example, what are R{vw} and r{vw} in Algorithm 2? Additionally, in Algorithm 2, the quantities \hat{R}^0 and \hat{r}^0 do not appear to be initialized. Furthermore, as the auxiliary linear process is central to the analysis and the core idea of the paper, the authors should clearly indicate which variables in Algorithm 3 correspond to it.

It would be interesting and useful if the authors could address the following points, either by answering these questions or commenting on them within the paper:
- Although this is a theoretical work, the authors should discuss the practicality of the method and under what conditions it might be preferable to other distributed methods for consensus optimization.
- What are the limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method?
- What is the intuition behind utilizing the auxiliary process within the Min-Sum Splitting method?
- The presented results are for consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. Can this framework be extended to solve more general consensus problems, which frequently arise in Machine Learning?
- The authors should clearly articulate why this approach is of interest to the Machine Learning community.

In summary, this paper is a purely theoretical work establishing convergence rates using a new proof technique and demonstrating connections between the proposed method and well-established techniques in the literature. Overall, the ideas presented are interesting. However, the practicality of the method and the intuition behind the results are not fully explored, nor is there substantial justification for the relevance of this result to the Machine Learning community.
