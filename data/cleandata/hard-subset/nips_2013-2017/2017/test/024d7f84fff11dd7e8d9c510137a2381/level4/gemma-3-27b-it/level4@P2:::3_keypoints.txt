- Paper proposes an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting message-passing algorithm for consensus and analyzes its convergence for quadratic objectives using a novel auxiliary linear process.  
- Main contributions: novel auxiliary-process-based analysis, improved Min-Sum consensus protocol with faster convergence, and connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods.  
- Motivation and contributions are clear; paper is generally well-written and proofs of Propositions 1–2 and Theorem 1 appear sound.  
- Several minor typos and grammatical errors are present and enumerated by line (examples include "theirs neighbors" → "their neighbors" and "double-stochastic" → "doubly-stochastic").  
- Notation and algorithms need clarification: quantities like R_{vw}, r_{vw} and initial conditions \hat{R}^0, \hat{r}^0 in Algorithm 2 are not defined.  
- The correspondence between variables in Algorithm 3 and the auxiliary linear process should be explicitly stated.  
- Several relevant references are missing; reviewer suggests adding foundational works on distributed (sub)gradient methods and specific references (Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989, Srinivasan et al. 2009, Shi et al. 2015) and citations to Polyak and Nesterov.  
- Broader literature context (around line 170) should be expanded.  
- Clarify whether “Lagrangian”/“Lagrange multipliers” were intended instead of “Laplacian”/“Laplace multipliers” (lines 220–221).  
- Questions for authors: discuss practical applicability and scenarios where method outperforms other distributed consensus algorithms.  
- Questions for authors: state limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method and provide intuition for using the auxiliary process.  
- Questions for authors: can the framework extend beyond quadratic consensus objectives to more general ML-relevant problems?  
- Authors should elaborate on the relevance and interest of this work to the Machine Learning community.  
- Overall assessment: solid theoretical advance with valuable connections; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to fix typos, clarify notation, add references, and discuss practical implications.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical contribution, established convergence rates, clear problem presentation; arguments against: limited practical discussion, unclear ML relevance, and minor editorial/clarity issues.
