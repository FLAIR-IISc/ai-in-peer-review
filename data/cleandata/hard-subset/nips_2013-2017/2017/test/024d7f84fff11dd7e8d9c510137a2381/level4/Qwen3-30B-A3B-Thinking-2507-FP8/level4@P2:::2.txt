

This paper investigates the convergence properties of Min-Sum Splitting for the average consensus problem, claiming to achieve accelerated convergence rates through a novel parametrization of the Min-Sum algorithm. The authors establish that proper tuning of splitting parameters yields subdiffusive convergence rates matching shift-register methods, with theoretical connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods.

The paper builds on established work in distributed optimization, particularly referencing [27] which previously analyzed Min-Sum for consensus but failed to establish convergence rates for general graphs. The authors correctly note that Min-Sum typically diverges on cyclic graphs while Min-Sum Splitting converges, but their analysis requires careful contextualization.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper provides a novel analytical framework for tracking Min-Sum Splitting via an auxiliary process on the original graph nodes rather than directed edges, which simplifies convergence analysis.
2. The theoretical connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods is insightful and well-motivated.
3. The proof technique using the auxiliary process represents a valuable methodological contribution to the analysis of message-passing algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Insufficient contextualization of contribution:** The claimed improvement to O(D log(D/ε)) convergence rates does not significantly advance the state-of-the-art. Olshevsky's "Linear Time Average Consensus" (ICASSP 2015, arXiv 2015) already achieves O(D log(1/ε)) for all graphs, which is superior to the paper's O(D log(D/ε)) bound. For cycle graphs where D=Θ(n), the paper's bound is actually worse than O(D log(1/ε)).

2. **Missing critical references:** The literature review omits key works that directly address the problem, including:
   - Olshevsky's "Linear Time Average Consensus" (2015)
   - Scaman et al.'s "Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization" (ICML 2017)
   - França and Bento's "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM" (IEEE SP Letters 2017)
   - Shi et al.'s "Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization" (SIAM J. Optim. 2015)

3. **Technical inaccuracies:** The claim that "for graphs with good expansion properties, convergence time scales independently of dimension" is misleading. For complete graphs (the canonical example of good expansion), convergence occurs in a single step, making the rate trivial. The paper should clarify which graph classes satisfy non-trivial dimension-independent convergence.

4. **Overstated significance:** The paper claims to "establish the first analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting," but [27] already established convergence for d-regular graphs under specific initialization. The paper's contribution is incremental rather than transformative.

5. **Ambiguous claims about novelty:** The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and Nesterov acceleration (Section 7) appears to be a rediscovery of known techniques, as the auxiliary process essentially implements a multi-step method similar to those in [13, 24]. The paper fails to clearly distinguish its novel contribution from existing approaches.

**Quality:** The technical content is sound but the significance is overstated. The theoretical analysis is rigorous but the practical implications are limited by the marginal improvement over existing methods.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, though some technical points (e.g., the role of directed edges in simple graphs) could be clarified. The connection between the auxiliary process and multi-step methods is well-explained.

**Originality:** The paper makes a minor contribution by connecting Min-Sum Splitting to two existing streams of work, but the core acceleration mechanism is not novel. The "auxiliary message-passing scheme" is essentially a standard multi-step method.

**Significance:** The results do not substantially advance the state-of-the-art in distributed consensus optimization. The O(D log(D/ε)) bound is surpassed by existing methods, and the paper fails to demonstrate practical advantages over established approaches.

**Pro and Con Acceptance:**
*Pro:* The paper offers a clear analytical framework for studying Min-Sum Splitting, provides valuable theoretical connections between different acceleration techniques, and presents a novel proof technique using the auxiliary process.

*Con:* The claimed convergence rate improvement is marginal and not superior to existing work, the literature review is incomplete, and the significance of the contribution is overstated compared to the field's current state.

**Recommendation:** The paper makes a minor contribution to the literature on distributed optimization but fails to demonstrate sufficient significance to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The results are technically sound but do not advance the field meaningfully beyond existing work. I recommend rejection, though the paper could be strengthened with a more accurate assessment of the state-of-the-art and a clearer articulation of the novel contribution.
