This research paper examines the convergence rate of the so-called minimum sum decomposition method on the average consensus problem. Generally, the paper seems fine, but the improvement in results is not impressive. The detailed comments are as follows:

(1) It states that "this rate is optimal for graphs with good span properties, such as complete graphs. In this case, the convergence time, i.e., the number of iterations required to reach a specified level of error accuracy in the problem's dimension..."

For complete graphs, the linear rate is 0 because all converge to the average in 1 step. Also, complete graphs are too specific to be representative.

So, for what general range of graphs does the complexity not depend on the dimension (number of nodes)? What general range of graphs is considered good?

(2) This paragraph (similar to comment 1) should include a literature review by Olshchevsky on "Linear time average consensus on static graphs and its implications for decentralized optimization and multi-agent control." Its convergence rate should be properly stated (more clarification will be given in comment 8). The reference mentioned here has reached a significantly competitive or better range compared to the presented results.

(3) At the top of page 2, for consensus optimization, important references such as Xie, Ling, Kun, Wu, and Yin's "Linear convergence of ADMM in decentralized consensus optimization" and Scammann, Bach, Bubek, Li, and Masovei's "Optimal algorithm for smooth and robustly convex distributed optimization in networks" should be mentioned. Also, the authors should report on state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization and their respective (linear) convergence rates.

(4) While discussing lifted graphs and Markov chains, this paper has overlooked a very relevant paper by Franka and Bento titled "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM."

(5) The content of the last paragraph on page 5 is a long-known fact. "Generalized consensus computation in network systems with eraser links" by Rabbat, Novak, and Buckley should be mentioned. Subsequently, the relationship between those variants and the heavy ball/Nesterov/Polyak is known in the field.

(6) There are several important references regarding consensus optimization that the authors have overlooked. For example, "Additional: A precise first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization" by Xie, Ling, Wu, and Yin. "Fast Distributed Gradient Methods" by Jacovetic, J. Xavier, and Moura.

(7) Proposal 3 seems trivial and is a supplementary contribution.

(8) The rate obtained by this paper, D log(D/eps), does not show a significant improvement over the rate D log(1/eps) obtained on linear time average consensus and its implications for static graphs and decentralized optimization and multi-agent control (see comment 2). Especially in the worst-case scenario (valid for all graphs), the D~n limit is even worse than the limit obtained in "Linear time average consensus..."

(9) The paper "Linear time average consensus..." improves the limit through Nesterov's acceleration. The reviewer suspects that the so-called " सहायक message-passing scheme" proposed by the authors is again Nesterov's acceleration applied to the minimum sum algorithm. This is fine, but the analysis is done for consensus, which boils down to analyzing a linear system and should not be considered difficult. In such a situation, the paper's contribution is not clear.

(10) A slight improvement can come from carefully considering the spectral gap of the graph. Ultimately, the worst-case limit is still O(n) because O(n)=O(D) for all sets of graphs with n nodes.

(11) Line 243 on page 6. The graph is simple, but the author is using directed edges. This is misleading.

(12) Typo on line 220 on page 6. Laplacian—> Lagrangian.
