This paper investigates the application of an accelerated min-sum algorithm, specifically min-sum splitting, to the problem of distributed consensus. The presentation is exceptionally clear and well-written, effectively situating the contribution within the existing body of work on this topic. While I am not a specialist in this area, the results appear novel and represent a significant advance, particularly the insightful connection established between min-sum algorithms and lifted Markov chain techniques.

The core contribution lies in leveraging min-sum splitting to improve the convergence rate of distributed consensus algorithms. The authors clearly articulate the benefits of this approach and provide a compelling rationale for its adoption. The mathematical derivations are generally well-structured and easy to follow, facilitating understanding of the proposed method.

However, a specific detail in the presentation requires clarification. On page 4, when describing the equivalent objective function minimized by the min-sum algorithm and its relation to the min-sum splitting scheme, the authors state: “…splitting each term $\phi_{vw}$ into $\Gamma_{vw}$ terms, and each term $\phi_v$ into $\delta$ terms…” This statement is ambiguous. As $\delta$ and $\Gamma_{vw}$ are defined on the preceding page as real numbers, it’s unclear what “splitting…into” these quantities signifies. Does it refer to a decomposition involving these parameters, or some other operation? A more explicit explanation of this splitting process would significantly improve the clarity of the argument. For instance, providing an example or elaborating on the mathematical consequence of this splitting would be beneficial.

Beyond this specific point, the paper is technically sound. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature and clearly identify the limitations of existing approaches. The proposed method addresses these limitations in a principled manner. 

The empirical evaluation, while not extensive, supports the theoretical claims and demonstrates the potential of the proposed algorithm. Additional experiments, perhaps exploring the performance on larger-scale networks or under more challenging conditions (e.g., asynchronous communication), would further strengthen the paper. 

**Strengths:**

*   Clear and concise writing.
*   Novel contribution with a promising connection between min-sum algorithms and lifted Markov chains.
*   Well-motivated and technically sound approach.
*   Solid theoretical foundation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Ambiguity in the explanation of the splitting process on page 4.
*   Limited empirical evaluation.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field of distributed consensus. Addressing the clarification request regarding the splitting process would significantly enhance the paper’s quality. While more extensive empirical validation would be desirable, the current results are sufficient to support the claims made in the paper. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the point raised regarding clarity in the explanation of the min-sum splitting process.
