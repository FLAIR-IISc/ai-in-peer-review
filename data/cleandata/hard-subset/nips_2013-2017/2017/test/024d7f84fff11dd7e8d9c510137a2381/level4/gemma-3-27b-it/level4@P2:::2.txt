This paper investigates the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method applied to the average consensus problem. The core idea is to modify the standard min-sum algorithm to achieve faster convergence, and the paper provides theoretical analysis of this modified approach. While technically sound and well-written, the improvement demonstrated over existing methods appears incremental and may not represent a substantial advance.

The paper begins by framing the problem and relating it to existing work in distributed optimization and consensus algorithms. The authors correctly identify the limitations of standard min-sum in certain graph topologies. However, the discussion of optimal convergence rates for complete graphs is misleading, as these graphs achieve consensus in a single step. The authors should clarify for which general graph categories the complexity doesn’t scale with the number of nodes, and define what constitutes a “good” graph in this context.

The literature review, while extensive, is incomplete. Crucially, the paper omits discussion of “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control” [Olshevsky], which presents a competitive, and potentially superior, convergence rate. This omission significantly weakens the claim of novelty. Furthermore, key references in consensus optimization such as “On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization” [Shi et al.] and “Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks” [Scaman et al.] should be included and their respective convergence rates compared.  The authors should provide a comprehensive overview of the state-of-the-art in consensus optimization alongside their results.

Regarding connections to related techniques, the work neglects “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM” [Franca & Bento], a highly relevant paper in the field. The observation presented at the end of page 5 regarding the connection to heavy ball/Nesterov/Polyak methods is well-known and should be placed in the context of existing literature, referencing “Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links” [Rabbat et al.].

Additional relevant references for consensus optimization that the authors should consider include “Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization” [Shi et al.] and “Fast distributed gradient methods” [Jakovetic et al.]. Proposition 3 feels somewhat trivial and adds limited value to the overall contribution.

The achieved convergence rate of *D*log(*D*/*ε*) does not represent a substantial improvement over the *D*log(1/*ε*) rate achieved by Linear Time Average Consensus [Olshevsky]. In the worst-case scenario, where *D* ~ *n* (number of nodes), the proposed method’s bound is actually worse.

The reviewer suspects the "Auxiliary message-passing scheme" is, in essence, Nesterov’s acceleration applied to the min-sum algorithm. While this is a valid approach, the analysis is performed on the consensus problem, which simplifies to analyzing a linear system—a task that shouldn’t be inherently difficult. The paper’s contribution becomes less distinct given this context. The improvement, if any, appears to stem from a careful handling of the graph's spectral gap, but the worst-case bound remains *O(n)*.

The use of directed edges on a simple graph (line 243, page 6) is potentially confusing and should be clarified. A minor typo exists on line 220 of page 6 ("Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian").

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound analysis of the min-sum splitting algorithm.
*   It correctly identifies a modification to the standard min-sum that guarantees convergence in certain cases where the original algorithm fails.
*   The paper establishes a formal convergence rate for the proposed method.
*   The connection drawn to lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods is insightful.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The improvement in convergence rate is incremental and may not warrant publication in a top-tier conference like NIPS.
*   The literature review is incomplete and fails to adequately acknowledge related work, particularly [Olshevsky].
*   The analysis focuses on a relatively simple case (consensus) and it is unclear whether the results generalize to more complex optimization problems.
*   The core idea appears to be a re-implementation of Nesterov’s acceleration within the min-sum framework, diminishing the novelty.

**Overall:** The paper is a solid piece of work but lacks the significant novelty or impact expected of a NIPS publication. While the technical details are correct, the improvements over existing methods are limited and the literature review is incomplete. The paper feels more like an incremental extension of existing work rather than a breakthrough contribution.
