- Introduces Min-Sum Splitting, an accelerated Min-Sum variant for distributed consensus optimization using a reparametrization approach.  
- Main contributions: (1) novel auxiliary linear-process proof technique to analyze Min-Sum splitting.  
- Main contributions: (2) new Min-Sum protocol for consensus with improved convergence rates for quadratic objectives.  
- Main contributions: (3) exposition of connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods.  
- Technical strengths: paper is well-written and motivations are clear; proofs for Propositions 1–2 and Theorem 1 appear sound.  
- Analysis limitation: convergence results are restricted to quadratic objective functions only.  
- Practicality concern: lacks discussion on when practitioners should prefer this algorithm over other distributed consensus methods.  
- Limitations & intuition: authors should explain potential limitations and provide clearer intuition for using the auxiliary process.  
- Generalization request: discuss extending the framework to general convex (non-quadratic) problems common in ML.  
- Relevance to ML: needs a clearer justification of significance and interest for the machine learning community/NIPS audience.  
- Algorithm clarity issues: several algorithmic quantities (e.g., R_{vw}, r_{vw}, \hat{R}^0, \hat{r}^0) are not defined or initialized; mapping between Algorithm 3 and the auxiliary process should be explicit.  
- Terminology/notation queries: check whether "Laplacian" and "Laplace multipliers" should be "Lagrangian" and "Lagrange multipliers."  
- Numerous typos and grammatical errors throughout (specific line corrections provided by reviewer).  
- Missing citations: foundational distributed/subgradient and acceleration works (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989; Sundhar Ram et al. 2009; Wei Shi et al. 2015; Polyak; Nesterov) and other original literature references are absent and should be added.  
- Overall verdict: strong theoretical contribution with a novel proof technique and useful connections, but impact reduced by missing practical discussion, limited scope, clarity issues, and missing references.
