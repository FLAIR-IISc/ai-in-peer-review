This paper applies an accelerated variant of the min-sum algorithm, called min-sum splitting, to the distributed consensus problem. The paper is very well written, with the contribution clearly placed in the context of the state of the art in the topic. To the best of my knowledge (although I am not an expert on the topic), the results are novel and constitute a qualitative advance. In particular, the paper presents a novel connection between min-sum algorithms and lifted Markov chain techniques. 

There is a detail which is not entirely clear in the presentation. On page 4, when describing the equivalent objective function minimized by the min-sum splitting algorithm, the authors write: "...splitting each term $\phi_{vw}$ into $\Gamma_{vw}$ terms, and each term $\phi_v$ into $\delta$ terms,...". However, it is unclear what this means, as $\delta$ and $\Gamma_{vw}$, as introduced previously, are real numbers.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm and establishes convergence rates. The proofs appear solid, and the connection to shift-register methods is compelling.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly presented, and the technical details are explained in a manner accessible to researchers familiar with distributed optimization.
*   **Originality:** The combination of Min-Sum splitting with the consensus problem, and the subsequent analysis revealing acceleration and connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods, appears to be a novel contribution. The theoretical results regarding convergence rates are also original.
*   **Significance:** The results are important because they provide a method for accelerating consensus algorithms, a fundamental primitive in distributed systems and machine learning. The convergence rate improvement, particularly for graphs with geometry, is a significant advance. The connections drawn to other areas of optimization (lifted Markov chains, multi-step methods) are also valuable.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Ambiguous wording:** The description of the splitting process in terms of splitting terms into real numbers is confusing. A more precise explanation would be beneficial.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical results demonstrating accelerated convergence.
*   Novel connection between Min-Sum algorithms and related areas of optimization.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Significant potential impact on the field of distributed optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The minor ambiguity regarding the splitting process. This, however, does not detract from the overall quality of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The technical analysis is rigorous, the writing is excellent, and the results are both novel and important. I strongly recommend acceptance.
