This paper analyzes the convergence rate of the min-sum splitting method for the average consensus problem. While the paper is generally well-written, the significance of the presented improvement seems marginal.

Detailed comments are as follows:

(1) The paper states: "This rate is optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph. In this case the convergence time, i.e., the number of iterations required to reach a prescribed level of error accuracy in the… of the dimension of the problem, as…". This claim is problematic. For a complete graph, average consensus is achieved in a single step, meaning the linear convergence rate is effectively zero. Furthermore, complete graphs are a highly specialized case and not representative of general network topologies. The authors should clarify for which general classes of graphs their complexity does not depend on the number of nodes and provide a more precise definition of what constitutes a graph with "good expansion properties."

(2) In the same paragraph as the point above, the literature review omits the crucial work "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control" by Olshevsky. The convergence rate from this paper should be properly reported and compared against. The bounds presented in the Olshevsky paper appear to be competitive with, or even superior to, the results in this submission.

(3) At the top of page 2, the discussion on consensus optimization is missing several key references. For instance, the authors should cite "On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization" by Shi et al. and "Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks" by Scaman et al. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization and their corresponding linear convergence rates.

(4) When discussing lifted graphs and Markov chains, the paper overlooks the highly relevant work "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM" by Franca and Bento.

(5) The content in the last paragraph of page 5 appears to present a long-known fact. The authors should cite prior work, such as "Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links" by Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew. Furthermore, the connection described between these variants and momentum-based methods (e.g., Heavy Ball, Nesterov, Polyak) is already well-established in the field.

(6) The literature review on consensus optimization is incomplete. Several other important works have been omitted, for example, "Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization" by Shi et al. and "Fast distributed gradient methods" by Jakovetic, Xavier, and Moura.

(7) Proposition 3 appears to be a trivial result and offers only a supplementary contribution to the paper's main claims.

(8) The main result, a convergence rate of D log(D/eps), does not represent a significant improvement over the existing rate of D log(1/eps) established in "Linear Time Average Consensus..." (mentioned in comment 2). In the worst-case scenario where the graph diameter D is on the order of the number of nodes n, this new bound is actually worse than the one achieved by Olshevsky.

(9) The paper "Linear Time Average Consensus..." improves its bound via Nesterov's acceleration. I suspect that the "Auxiliary message-passing scheme" proposed here is essentially another application of Nesterov's acceleration to the min-sum algorithm. While this is a valid approach, the analysis is performed on the average consensus problem, which reduces to analyzing a linear system. Such an analysis is not expected to be difficult, which makes the novelty and significance of the contribution unclear.

(10) The marginal improvement in the rate may stem from a more careful analysis of the graph's spectral gap. However, the worst-case bound is ultimately still O(n), since the diameter D can be O(n) for a graph with n nodes. This does not represent a fundamental improvement over existing results.

(11) On page 6, line 243, the authors state the graph is simple, yet they use notation for directed edges. This is confusing and should be clarified.

(12) Typo on page 6, line 220: "Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian."

I am satisfied with the authors' response. However, my evaluation of the paper remains unchanged.
