This paper investigates the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method applied to the average consensus problem. While the paper is generally well-written, the improvement offered over existing results appears incremental. Specific comments are detailed below.

(1) The authors claim optimality for graphs with “good expansion properties,” citing the complete graph as an example. However, the complete graph achieves linear convergence (rate 0) as all nodes converge to the average in a single step, making it a trivial and unrepresentative case. The paper should clearly define what constitutes a “good” graph and specify for which broader classes of graphs the complexity does *not* scale with the problem dimension (number of nodes).

(2) The literature review lacks a crucial citation: Olshevsky’s “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control.” This work achieves a competitive, and potentially superior, convergence bound which should be properly reported and discussed. The authors should contextualize their results in relation to this existing work (further details in comment 8).

(3) When discussing consensus optimization, the authors should cite key references such as Shi et al.’s “On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization” and Scaman et al.’s “Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks.”  A thorough comparison of the state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization, along with their corresponding linear convergence rates, is necessary to position this work effectively.

(4) The discussion of lifted graphs and Markov chains omits a highly relevant paper: Franca and Bento’s “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM.” This omission is significant given the close relationship between the two works.

(5) The statement in the last paragraph of page 5 is a well-established result, originally presented in Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew’s “Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links.” The connection between these variants and algorithms like Heavy Ball, Nesterov, and Polyak is also well-known in the field.

(6) The paper overlooks several important contributions in consensus optimization, including Shi et al.’s “Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization” and Jakovetic et al.’s “Fast distributed gradient methods.”  

(7) Proposition 3 appears to be a relatively trivial extension and contributes little novelty.

(8) The achieved convergence rate of D log(D/eps) does not represent a substantial improvement over the D log(1/eps) rate reported in Olshevsky’s work.  In the worst-case scenario (where D ~ n, the number of nodes), the proposed bound is even *worse* than that achieved by “Linear Time Average Consensus…”

(9) Olshevsky’s work leverages Nesterov’s acceleration to improve the convergence rate. The reviewer suspects that the authors’ proposed “Auxiliary message-passing scheme” is, in fact, an application of Nesterov's acceleration to the min-sum algorithm. While this is acceptable, the analysis is performed for consensus, which simplifies to analyzing a linear system—a generally tractable problem. Given this, the paper’s contribution is unclear.

(10) Any marginal improvement likely stems from a careful handling of the graph's spectral gap. However, the worst-case bound remains O(n) since O(n) = O(D) for the set of all graphs with n nodes.

(11) Line 243, page 6: The graph is described as simple, yet the figure uses directed edges. This is potentially confusing.

(12) Line 220, page 6: Typo – “Laplacian” should be “Lagrangian.”
