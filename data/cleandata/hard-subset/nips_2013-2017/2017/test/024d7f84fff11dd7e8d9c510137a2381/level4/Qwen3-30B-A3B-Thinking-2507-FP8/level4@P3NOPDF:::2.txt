

This paper examines the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method for average consensus. While the technical content is generally sound, the claimed improvement over existing results appears limited, and the contribution lacks sufficient novelty relative to the state-of-the-art. The following points elaborate on key concerns:

**Core Technical Limitations**  
The paper asserts that its convergence rate \(O(D \log(D/\varepsilon))\) is "optimal for graphs with good expansion properties." However, this claim is misleading. For complete graphs (the cited example), convergence occurs in one step (rate 1), not \(O(D \log(D/\varepsilon))\). More critically, the paper fails to specify *which general graph class* achieves dimension-independent complexity. Good expansion is not well-defined here—expansion properties typically scale with \(n\), not \(D\), and the bound \(O(D \log(D/\varepsilon))\) remains dependent on \(D\) (diameter), which is \(O(n)\) for worst-case graphs. Thus, the "optimality" claim is unsubstantiated for meaningful graph classes.

The paper’s rate \(O(D \log(D/\varepsilon))\) also offers no significant improvement over Olshevsky’s *Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs* (2015), which achieves \(O(n \log(1/\varepsilon))\) via Nesterov acceleration. For worst-case graphs (\(D = \Theta(n)\)), the paper’s bound becomes \(O(n \log(n/\varepsilon))\), which is *worse* than Olshevsky’s \(O(n \log(1/\varepsilon))\). The paper’s "auxiliary message-passing scheme" appears to be a standard application of Nesterov acceleration to the min-sum algorithm. Since consensus reduces to analyzing linear systems (a routine task), this technique does not constitute a novel contribution. The authors should clarify how their approach differs fundamentally from Olshevsky’s acceleration framework.

**Critical Literature Omissions**  
The paper neglects pivotal works in decentralized consensus optimization, undermining its positioning. Key missing references include:
- Olshevsky (2015) for the state-of-the-art linear-time bound.
- Shi et al. (*On the Linear Convergence of ADMM*, 2015) and Scaman et al. (*Optimal Algorithms*, 2017) for foundational consensus optimization rates.
- Extra (Shi et al., 2015) and Jakovetic et al. (*Fast Distributed Gradient Methods*, 2014) for competitive first-order methods.
- Franca & Bento (*Markov Chain Lifting*, 2018) for lifted graph/Markov chain analysis, which is directly relevant to the paper’s framework.

The literature review should explicitly compare the paper’s rate to Olshevsky’s \(O(n \log(1/\varepsilon))\) and contextualize its position among existing methods. The claim of "optimality" for good-expansion graphs requires justification against Olshevsky’s tighter bound.

**Minor but Significant Issues**  
- Proposition 3 is trivial (a straightforward consequence of Lemma 2) and should be omitted or repositioned as supplementary material.  
- The observation on Heavy Ball/Nesterov connections (p.5) is standard (see Rabbat et al., *Generalized Consensus Computation*, 2004) and requires citation.  
- Line 243 (p.6) uses directed edges for a "simple graph," causing confusion.  
- Line 220 contains a typo: "Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian."  

**Overall Assessment**  
The paper’s core contribution—rederiving a Nesterov-accelerated min-sum method with a bound that is neither tighter nor more general than Olshevsky’s—does not advance the field meaningfully. The rate \(O(D \log(D/\varepsilon))\) is subsumed by existing results, and the paper fails to clarify the graph classes for which it is optimal. The literature gaps are substantial, and the technical novelty is minimal. While the authors addressed some rebuttal points, the fundamental limitations persist. The work is technically competent but lacks the innovation expected for NIPS.  

**Recommendation**  
Reject. The paper does not present a significant advancement over the state-of-the-art, and its claims of optimality are unsupported. The authors should engage deeply with Olshevsky’s work and clarify the scope of their "good expansion" assumption before reconsidering.
