

This paper investigates the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method for the average consensus problem. While the paper is generally well-written, the claimed improvement appears limited. Detailed comments follow:

(1) The paper states: "This rate is optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph. In this case the convergence time... scales with the dimension of the problem." For complete graphs, the convergence occurs in one step (linear rate 0), making this example trivial and unrepresentative. The paper fails to specify which general graph class achieves dimension-independent complexity. Clarify the definition of "good expansion properties" and identify the relevant graph class.

(2) The literature review should include Olshevsky's "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control." The convergence rate must be correctly reported, as this work achieves a competitive or superior bound compared to the submission.

(3) At the top of page 2, key references for consensus optimization are missing: Shi et al.'s "On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization" and Scaman et al.'s "Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks." The authors must cite state-of-the-art algorithms and their linear convergence rates.

(4) The discussion of lifted graphs and Markov chains omits Franca and Bento's "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM," which is highly relevant.

(5) The final paragraph of page 5 states a well-known fact. This should reference Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew's "Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links." The connection between the discussed variants and Heavy Ball/Nesterov/Polyak methods is standard in the field.

(6) Critical references on consensus optimization are omitted, including Shi et al.'s "Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization" and Jakovetic et al.'s "Fast distributed gradient methods."

(7) Proposition 3 appears trivial and constitutes a supplementary contribution rather than a core advance.

(8) The claimed rate \(D \log(D/\varepsilon)\) offers no significant improvement over the \(D \log(1/\varepsilon)\) rate achieved by Olshevsky's work (see Comment 2). In the worst case (all graphs with \(n\) nodes, where \(D \sim n\)), the bound is inferior to Olshevsky's result.

(9) Olshevsky's work achieves its bound via Nesterov's acceleration. The authors' "Auxiliary message-passing scheme" likely represents a Nesterov acceleration applied to the min-sum algorithm. While this is acceptable, the analysis for consensus reduces to studying a linear system—a standard task. The paper's contribution becomes unclear given this context.

(10) The minor improvement may stem from refined handling of graph spectral gaps. However, the worst-case bound remains \(O(n) = O(D)\) across all graphs with \(n\) nodes, as \(D = O(n)\).

(11) Line 243 (page 6) uses directed edges for a simple graph, causing confusion.

(12) Line 220 (page 6) contains a typo: "Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian."

After rebuttal: The reviewer is satisfied with the authors' response. The evaluation score remains unchanged.
