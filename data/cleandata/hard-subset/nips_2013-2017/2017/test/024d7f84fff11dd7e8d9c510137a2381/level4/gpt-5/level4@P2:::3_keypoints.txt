- Paper studies Min‑Sum Splitting for consensus, proving convergence for quadratic objectives and showing accelerated (subdiffusive) rates via a node-based auxiliary linear system and spectral analysis.  
- Accelerated rate matches shift‑register (second‑order) methods and is comparable to Nesterov‑type acceleration; yields O(D log(D/ε)) iterations on cycles/tori.  
- Connects three literatures: reparameterized Min‑Sum/Gaussian BP, lifted/nonreversible Markov chain acceleration, and multi‑step first‑order methods (heavy‑ball, Nesterov, Chebyshev/shift‑register).  
- Technical strength: principled, clear analysis of Min‑Sum Splitting for quadratic consensus with explicit rates and an elegant auxiliary node‑based system.  
- Conceptual novelty: clarifies how message‑passing acceleration relates to directionality/memory and unifies message passing, lifting, and multi‑step acceleration.  
- Presentation strength: motivation and result structure are clear and contributions well delineated.  
- Main limitation: results are restricted to quadratic consensus; applicability to nonquadratic or broader ML objectives is unclear.  
- Practicality concern: lacks discussion comparing Min‑Sum Splitting to established accelerated consensus/ADMM methods in terms of implementation and communication costs.  
- Missing intuition: more explanation needed for why the auxiliary node process captures edge‑based dynamics and how directionality becomes node memory.  
- Presentation gaps: some algorithm variables are undefined/uninitialized, there are typos and grammatical issues, and several relevant references are missing.  
- Quality assessment: technical development appears sound and assumptions/rates are justified by spectral arguments.  
- Clarity assessment: generally well written but needs clearer definitions (e.g., variables in Algorithm 2, initialization of R̂0 and r̂0) and tightened notation.  
- Originality assessment: novel in establishing convergence rates for Min‑Sum Splitting and linking it to lifted chains and multi‑step methods.  
- Significance assessment: advances theoretical understanding of accelerated message passing; practical impact would be stronger with broader problem classes or empirical comparisons.  
- Pros for acceptance: new auxiliary analysis technique, accelerated rates matching shift‑register near known bounds, and conceptual unification across fields.  
- Cons for acceptance: restricted to quadratics, insufficient practical/trade‑off discussion versus existing methods, and presentation/notation issues.  
- Requests for authors: clarify regimes (graph families, sizes, budgets) where Min‑Sum Splitting outperforms Chebyshev/shift‑register, EXTRA, or ADMM.  
- Requests for authors: discuss main bottlenecks (initialization, need for global spectral tuning, sensitivity to noise/asynchrony) and how to mitigate them.  
- Requests for authors: provide concise intuition for splitting→directionality→memory mapping and whether approach extends to strongly convex smooth or composite objectives.  
- Requests for authors: clarify concrete ML use cases (e.g., decentralized training) and how global tuning info could be estimated in practice.  
- Typos and grammar: reviewer lists many specific fixes (examples: Line 34 “with theirs neighbors”→“with their neighbors”; Line 174 “double‑stochastic”→“doubly‑stochastic”; plus other line‑specific corrections).  
- Algorithm clarifications requested: define Rvw and rvw when first used, explicitly initialize R̂0 and r̂0, and map variables in Algorithm 3 to beliefs in Algorithm 2; clarify whether “Lagrangian/Lagrange multipliers” was intended instead of “Laplacian/Laplace multipliers.”  
- Missing citations recommended: classic consensus/subgradient and distributed optimization works (Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989; Ram et al. 2009; Shi et al. EXTRA 2015) and acceleration literature (Polyak heavy‑ball, Nesterov).  
- Overall recommendation: solid theoretical contribution; recommend acceptance conditional on improved exposition/notation, addition of missing references, and expanded discussion of practicality, limitations, and generalizations.
