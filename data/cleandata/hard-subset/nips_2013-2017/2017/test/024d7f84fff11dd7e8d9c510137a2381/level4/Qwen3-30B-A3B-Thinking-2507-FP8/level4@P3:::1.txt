

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum message-passing to the distributed consensus problem, introducing a novel variant called Min-Sum Splitting that achieves accelerated convergence rates. The authors demonstrate that while the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge for graphs with cycles, the splitting modification guarantees convergence to the optimal solution. The key contribution lies in establishing that proper tuning of the splitting parameters yields subdiffusive convergence rates matching those of shift-register methods, and in revealing the theoretical connections between Min-Sum Splitting and two distinct accelerated approaches: lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization.

The paper is well-positioned within the literature. It properly situates its work in relation to prior research on distributed consensus (e.g., [42, 41] for classical diffusive methods, [27] for Min-Sum variants), and acknowledges the two main lines of accelerated methods that have evolved independently: lifted Markov chains [8, 5, 37] and multi-step first-order methods [13, 24]. The authors' novel contribution is precisely connecting Min-Sum Splitting to both these approaches, revealing how the directionality introduced through splitting parallels the directionality in non-reversible lifted random walks, while simultaneously relating to the memory terms in multi-step methods.

The paper excels in quality, providing rigorous theoretical analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting. The authors prove that with proper parameter selection, the algorithm achieves convergence time O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, representing a square-root improvement over diffusive methods (which scale as Θ(D² log(1/ε))). The proof technique, which introduces an auxiliary linear process to track the Min-Sum evolution on the original graph nodes rather than on directed edges, is novel and well-executed. The paper demonstrates technical soundness through clear theorems and proofs (with details in supplementary material), and the authors carefully address both strengths and limitations of their approach.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The writing is precise, the organization follows a logical flow from problem definition to theoretical analysis, and the mathematical presentation is well-structured. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the results, with clear definitions of all key concepts and algorithms. The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and prior work is well-explained, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with distributed optimization.

The originality is strong. The paper establishes the first formal connection between Min-Sum message-passing and both lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step convex optimization methods. While [27] previously investigated Min-Sum for consensus, they only considered the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm (which doesn't converge for cyclic graphs) and didn't establish convergence rates. The current paper's theoretical contributions—particularly the proof of accelerated convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting—represent a significant advance over prior work.

The significance is high. Consensus problems are fundamental to distributed optimization across numerous applications, including sensor networks, multi-agent systems, and large-scale machine learning. The paper addresses a key limitation of existing methods (diffusive convergence rates for graphs with geometry) and offers a theoretically sound approach to overcome it. The connection to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods suggests broader applicability beyond consensus, potentially influencing future work in distributed optimization.

One minor concern arises in the presentation of the splitting mechanism. On page 4, the authors state that "splitting each term φvw into Γvw terms, and each term φv into δ terms," but Γvw and δ are defined as real numbers (not integers) in the preceding section. This creates confusion about the interpretation of the splitting operation, as the term "splitting" typically implies integer partitioning. Clarifying this point—whether the splitting can be meaningfully interpreted for real-valued parameters or whether the integer interpretation is strictly maintained in the theoretical analysis—would strengthen the paper's presentation.

Another minor suggestion concerns the practical implementation of the algorithm. While the paper focuses on theoretical convergence rates, briefly discussing computational considerations (e.g., how the choice of parameters affects computational complexity beyond the asymptotic convergence rate) would provide additional context for potential applications.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong theoretical contribution with novel connections to two established approaches in distributed optimization
- Rigorous proof of accelerated convergence rates for consensus problems
- Well-written, clear, and well-organized presentation
- Significant impact on a fundamental problem in distributed optimization
- Properly situated within the literature with comprehensive related work discussion

Arguments against acceptance:
- Minor confusion regarding the interpretation of the splitting parameters (Γvw and δ as real numbers versus integer splits)
- Limited discussion of practical implementation considerations beyond theoretical convergence

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization by establishing a novel theoretical connection between Min-Sum message-passing and accelerated consensus methods, while providing rigorous proof of accelerated convergence rates. The minor concerns raised do not undermine the paper's substantial theoretical advances. The work is of high quality, clear in presentation, original in its connections, and significant for the field. I recommend acceptance.
