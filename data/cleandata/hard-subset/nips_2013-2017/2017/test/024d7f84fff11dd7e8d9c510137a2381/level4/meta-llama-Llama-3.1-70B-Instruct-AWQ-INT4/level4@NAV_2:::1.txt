This paper presents a novel application of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm to solve the consensus problem in distributed optimization. The authors demonstrate that a modified version of the Min-Sum algorithm, known as Splitting, yields convergence to the problem solution, whereas the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm does not converge. The paper establishes rates of convergence for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm and shows that it achieves subdiffusive accelerated convergence rates, matching the rates obtained by shift-register methods.

The paper is well-written and clearly places its contribution in the context of the state of the art in the topic. The authors provide a thorough review of previous work on consensus algorithms, including classical diffusive algorithms and accelerated methods such as lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first order methods. They also highlight the novelty of their approach, which connects Min-Sum algorithms with lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The technical content of the paper is sound, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, including its convergence rates and the choice of tuning parameters. The paper also includes a useful comparison with other accelerated methods, such as shift-register methods, and demonstrates that the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm achieves a square-root improvement over the convergence time of diffusive methods.

One minor point of clarification is needed in the presentation. On page 4, the authors describe the equivalent objective function that is minimized by the Min-Sum algorithm to yield the Min-Sum Splitting scheme. However, it is not clear what is meant by "splitting each term $\phi_{vw}$ into $\Gamma_{vw}$ terms, and each term $\phi_v$ into $\delta$ terms," since $\delta$ and $\Gamma_{vw}$ are introduced as real numbers on the previous page. A brief explanation or rephrasing of this sentence would help to clarify the meaning.

Overall, the paper is well-written and presents a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The results are novel and constitute a qualitative advance in the topic.
