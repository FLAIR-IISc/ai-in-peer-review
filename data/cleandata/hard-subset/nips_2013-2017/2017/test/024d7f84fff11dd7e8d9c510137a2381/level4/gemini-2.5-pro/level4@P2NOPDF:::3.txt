### Review

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol, termed the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, for distributed consensus optimization. Building on the reparametrization techniques of Ruozzi and Tatikonda (2013), the authors establish convergence rates for problems with quadratic objective functions. The core of their analysis is a novel proof technique centered on an auxiliary linear process designed to track the algorithm's evolution.

The main contributions are:
1.  A new analysis for the Min-Sum splitting algorithm using the proposed auxiliary process proof technique.
2.  The design of a Min-Sum protocol for consensus problems that achieves an improved convergence rate over existing methods.
3.  An elucidation of the connection between the proposed method and other established techniques, such as lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

**Overall Assessment**

The paper is well-motivated and clearly written, making the core ideas and contributions easy to follow. The theoretical analysis, particularly the proofs for Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1, appears to be sound. The core ideas are interesting, and the connections drawn to other areas of optimization are insightful. However, the paper's impact could be significantly strengthened by addressing its current limitations, namely the lack of discussion on practical relevance, the limited scope of the analysis, and a need for a clearer justification of its importance to the machine learning community. The manuscript would also benefit from a thorough proofreading to correct several typos and minor grammatical errors.

**Major Comments and Questions for the Authors**

To improve the paper, I would encourage the authors to address the following points in a revised version:

1.  **Practicality and Applicability:** While the paper focuses on theory, a discussion on the practical implications would strengthen it significantly. When would this method be preferable to other distributed optimization techniques like (sub)gradient methods? What are the primary limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting approach in terms of communication overhead, computational complexity, or underlying assumptions?

2.  **Intuition behind the Auxiliary Process:** The auxiliary process is central to the analysis and represents the paper's main technical innovation. Could the authors provide more intuition behind its construction and explain why it is a powerful tool for analyzing the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm?

3.  **Generalization and Relevance to Machine Learning:** The analysis is currently restricted to quadratic objective functions. What are the prospects and potential challenges in extending this framework to more general convex functions commonly encountered in machine learning (e.g., logistic regression, SVMs)? Furthermore, the paper should more explicitly articulate its contribution to the machine learning community. Why is this particular theoretical advance important for the NIPS/NeurIPS readership, and what new possibilities does it open for machine learning applications?

**Minor Comments, Typos, and Missing References**

**Clarity and Presentation:**
*   **Algorithms:** The algorithms, as presented, lack some necessary detail. For instance, in Algorithm 2, the variables `R{vw}` and `r{vw}` are used without definition. Furthermore, the quantities `\hat{R}^0` and `\hat{r}^0` are not initialized. Since the auxiliary linear process is a key idea, please clarify which variables in Algorithm 3 correspond to this process.
*   **Lines 220-221:** The terms "Laplacian" and "Laplace multipliers" are used. Is it possible that "Lagrangian" and "Lagrange multipliers" were intended?

**Missing Citations:**
The paper would be improved by including references to foundational and related work in consensus optimization.
*   **Lines 41 and 43 (Sub)gradient methods:** Consider adding seminal works such as:
    *   Bertsekas and Tsitsiklis, "Parallel and distributed computation: numerical methods," 1989.
    *   Nedich and Ozdaglar, "Distributed subgradient methods for multi-agent optimization," 2009.
    *   Wei Shi et al., "EXTRA: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization," 2015.
*   **Line 170:** The phrase "The original literature..." should be supported by a citation.
*   **Line 229:** The connection to the heavy-ball method should cite the work of Polyak.
*   **Line 232:** The connection to accelerated methods should cite the work of Nesterov.

**Conclusion**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes an interesting contribution to the literature on distributed optimization. The novel proof technique is elegant, and the connections to other methods are insightful. The primary weaknesses are the lack of discussion on the practical applicability, the limited scope of the analysis, and an insufficient connection to the broader machine learning context. Addressing the questions and suggestions outlined above would substantially improve the paper and clarify its impact on the NIPS community.
