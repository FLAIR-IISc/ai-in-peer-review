### Summary

This paper presents a min-sum splitting method for the average consensus problem and analyzes its convergence rate. The authors derive a complexity of D log(D/eps), where D is the graph diameter, for reaching an epsilon-accurate solution. While the paper is generally well-written, the significance of the contribution is questionable due to the marginal improvement over existing results and a number of omissions in the literature review.

### Major Concerns

1.  **Limited Improvement in Convergence Rate and Misleading Claims:**
    The central claim of the paper is a convergence rate of D log(D/eps). This does not represent a significant improvement over the D log(1/eps) rate established by Olshevsky in "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control." In the worst-case scenario for general graphs, where the diameter D can be on the order of the number of nodes n, the proposed bound may even be inferior. The slight improvement appears to come from a more careful handling of the spectral gap, but the overall worst-case complexity remains O(n), which has been achieved by prior work.

    Furthermore, the paper's claim that this rate is "optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph," is misleading. For a complete graph, average consensus is achieved in a single step, making an asymptotic rate analysis irrelevant. The authors should provide a more precise definition of the class of "good" graphs for which their rate offers a clear, dimension-independent advantage.

2.  **Incomplete Literature Review and Positioning:**
    The paper fails to cite and compare against several key and state-of-the-art works in decentralized consensus and optimization. This makes it difficult to properly contextualize and evaluate the paper's contribution. Notable omissions include:
    *   **Consensus Optimization:** The discussion on consensus optimization is missing seminal references such as Shi et al. (2015), "On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization," and Scaman et al. (2017), "Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks." The paper should report the state-of-the-art linear convergence rates from these and other related works.
    *   **First-Order Methods:** Foundational algorithms like EXTRA (Shi et al., 2015, "Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization") and fast distributed gradient methods (Jakovetic et al., 2014, "Fast distributed gradient methods") are not discussed.
    *   **Lifted Markov Chains:** When discussing lifted graphs, the highly relevant paper by Franca and Bento (2016), "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM," is ignored.
    *   **Established Concepts:** The connection between consensus variants and momentum methods (e.g., Heavy ball, Nesterov) discussed on page 5 is a well-known concept. This should be attributed to earlier works, such as Rabbat et al. (2005), "Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links."

3.  **Questionable Novelty of the Proposed Method:**
    The paper's "Auxiliary message-passing scheme" appears to be a direct application of Nesterov's acceleration to the min-sum algorithm. While this is a valid technique, its application to the average consensus problem—which reduces to analyzing a linear system—is not a particularly challenging or novel contribution. The analysis is expected to be more straightforward in this setting, which diminishes the perceived significance of the work. The paper would be stronger if it clarified the unique analytical challenges or insights that this specific application provides.

### Minor Issues

*   **Trivial Contribution:** Proposition 3 seems to be a straightforward and supplementary result that adds little to the paper's core thesis.
*   **Confusing Notation:** On page 6, line 243, the text refers to a simple graph but uses notation for directed edges. This should be clarified.
*   **Typo:** On page 6, line 220, "Laplacian" should be corrected to "Lagrangian."

### Conclusion

In its current form, the paper's contribution is not substantial enough for publication. The improvement in the convergence rate is marginal and not universally applicable. The paper's primary weakness is its failure to engage with a large body of highly relevant literature, which prevents a fair assessment of its novelty and significance. The core technical idea seems to be a straightforward application of a known acceleration technique to a well-studied problem.

### Post-Rebuttal Comments

The authors' response has satisfactorily addressed some of the questions raised. However, the core concerns regarding the limited novelty and marginal performance improvement remain. Therefore, my overall evaluation of the paper is unchanged.
