This paper introduces an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for solving distributed consensus optimization problems. Building on reparametrization techniques, the authors establish convergence rates for their "Min-Sum Splitting" algorithm on consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. The core of their analysis relies on a novel proof technique: the construction of an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the main algorithm.

The primary contributions are: (1) providing the first convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, enabled by the new auxiliary process proof technique; (2) designing a Min-Sum protocol for consensus that achieves a faster convergence rate than previously established methods; and (3) illuminating the connections between this accelerated Min-Sum scheme and two other major families of acceleration techniques: lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The paper's strengths lie in its clear motivation and well-defined contributions. The exposition is generally easy to follow, and the core theoretical results in Propositions 1-2 and Theorem 1 appear to be sound. The central idea of using an auxiliary process for the analysis is novel and interesting.

However, the paper could be improved in several areas concerning clarity, positioning within the literature, and discussion of its broader impact.

*   Line 174: "double-stochastic" should be "doubly-stochastic"

The clarity of the algorithms could also be improved. In Algorithm 2, the variables R{vw} and r{vw} are used without a clear definition, and the initial conditions R̂⁰ and r̂⁰ are not explicitly stated. Furthermore, given that the auxiliary process in Algorithm 3 is central to the paper's analysis, it would be very helpful to explicitly state what each variable in this algorithm corresponds to.

A minor point on lines 220-221: the authors may mean "Lagrangian" and "Lagrange multipliers" instead of "Laplacian" and "Laplace multipliers."

**Related Work**

The discussion of related work could be strengthened by including several key references. The section on (sub)gradient methods for consensus optimization would benefit from citing foundational and more recent works such as:
*   Bertsekas, D. P., & Tsitsiklis, J. N. (1989). *Parallel and distributed computation: numerical methods.*
*   Ram, S. S., Nedić, A., & Veeravalli, V. V. (2010). Incremental subgradient methods for convex optimization. *SIAM Journal on Optimization*.
*   Shi, W., Ling, Q., Wu, G., & Yin, W. (2015). Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization. *SIAM Journal on Optimization*.

Additionally, when discussing multi-step methods, citing the original works of Polyak (Heavy-ball method) and Nesterov would be appropriate.

**Discussion and Scope**

To improve the paper's impact and significance, I encourage the authors to expand the discussion to address the following points:
*   **Practicality and Limitations:** While the work is theoretical, a discussion on practical implications would be valuable. In what scenarios would this method be preferable to other distributed consensus algorithms? What are its primary limitations (e.g., communication overhead, parameter tuning)?
*   **Intuition:** Could the authors provide more intuition behind the choice of an auxiliary process as an analysis tool? Why does this particular construction work and simplify the analysis of the main algorithm?
*   **Generalization:** The current results are for quadratic objective functions. How might this framework extend to more general convex functions commonly found in machine learning problems? Addressing this would significantly broaden the paper's relevance.
*   **Relevance to Machine Learning:** The paper should more explicitly articulate its contribution to the machine learning community. Why is an accelerated Min-Sum protocol for consensus an important tool for machine learning practitioners or researchers?

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution, providing the first convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm and revealing its connection to other accelerated optimization methods. The proof technique is novel and the results appear correct. However, the paper's significance could be substantially enhanced by addressing the current lack of discussion on the method's practical implications, its limitations, the intuition behind the analysis, and its direct relevance to the broader machine learning community. Addressing these points would make a strong theoretical paper even more impactful.
