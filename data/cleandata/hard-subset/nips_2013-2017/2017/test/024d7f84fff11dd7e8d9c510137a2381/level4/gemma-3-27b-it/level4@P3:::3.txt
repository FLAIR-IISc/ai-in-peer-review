This paper presents an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for solving consensus problems in distributed optimization. The authors leverage reparametrization techniques [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013] to establish convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. A key analytical tool is the construction of an auxiliary linear process used to track the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

The paper’s main contributions are threefold: (i) it provides an analysis of the Min-Sum splitting algorithm using a novel proof technique based on the introduction of an auxiliary process; (ii) it designs a Min-Sum protocol for consensus problems that achieves improved convergence compared to previously established results; and (iii) it elucidates the connection between the proposed method and existing techniques like lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The motivation and contributions are clearly articulated. The paper is generally well-written and accessible, although it contained several minor typos and grammatical errors, which have been addressed below. The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appear logically sound.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   While this is primarily a theoretical work, a discussion of the practical implications and scenarios where this method might be preferred over other distributed consensus algorithms would be valuable.
*   What are the inherent limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method? Under what conditions might it *not* outperform existing approaches?
*   Could the authors elaborate on the intuition behind employing the auxiliary process? How does this process facilitate the analysis and lead to the acceleration observed?
*   The current results focus on consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. Is there potential to extend this framework to tackle more general consensus problems commonly encountered in machine learning applications?
*   Finally, a more explicit articulation of the relevance and potential impact of this work within the machine learning community would strengthen the paper. Why should machine learning researchers care about this theoretical advance?

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically sound and interesting contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the relevant literature and develop a novel analytical approach. While the paper is currently strong in its theoretical development, addressing the points raised above – particularly regarding practical considerations, limitations, and broader relevance to the machine learning community – would significantly enhance its impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical Novelty:** The paper introduces a new analysis technique using an auxiliary process to understand the convergence behavior of Min-Sum Splitting.
*   **Convergence Guarantee:** The paper establishes convergence rates and demonstrates acceleration compared to traditional diffusive methods.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors successfully link their method to related areas like lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods.
*   **Soundness:** The proofs appear to be correct and the analysis is rigorous.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Practical Discussion:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the practical implications and potential challenges of implementing the proposed method.
*   **Missing Context:** Some variables and concepts are not fully explained, potentially hindering understanding.
*   **ML Relevance:** The connection to and potential impact on the machine learning community are not clearly articulated.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should address the comments and suggestions regarding clarity, completeness of references, and discussion of practical implications. A more compelling justification for the relevance of this work to the machine learning community would also be beneficial.
