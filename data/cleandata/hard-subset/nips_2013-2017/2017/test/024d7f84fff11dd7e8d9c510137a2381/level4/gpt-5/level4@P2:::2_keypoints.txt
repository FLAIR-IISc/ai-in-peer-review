- Paper shows Min-Sum Splitting can be parameterized to converge to exact average consensus despite standard Min-Sum diverging on loopy graphs.  
- Authors derive an edge-based linear message-passing form and an auxiliary node-based two-step linear process for spectral analysis.  
- With a specific parameter choice they prove a “subdiffusive” accelerated rate O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori, matching shift-register/Chebyshev methods.  
- They relate the mechanism to lifted (non-reversible) Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods (heavy-ball/Nesterov).  

- Strength: Clear linearization and auxiliary node-based formulation enabling tractable spectral analysis.  
- Strength: Establishes convergence and accelerated rates for Min-Sum Splitting on general graphs, filling prior gaps.  
- Strength: Insightful conceptual connections to lifted chains and multi-step methods.  
- Strength: Analysis recovers shift-register rates with explicit spectrum-tied parameter choice.  

- Weakness: Acceleration is modest—O(D log(D/ε)) vs. O(D log(1/ε)) obtainable by other methods.  
- Weakness: Several important related works (distributed optimization, lifting/ADMM) are missing or under-discussed.  
- Weakness: Claims about “optimality” on expanders and complete graphs need precision; “good” graphs/dimension-free rates unclear.  
- Weakness: Some contributions (e.g., one proposition) appear straightforward; novelty mainly in adapting known acceleration ideas to Min-Sum Splitting.  
- Weakness: Presentation issues—minor typos and confusing notation (directed edges on an undirected graph).  

- Request: Clarify which graphs yield dimension-free Θ(log 1/ε) convergence and precisely define “good expansion.”  
- Request: Cite and compare with Olshevsky’s linear-time consensus result, specifying rates and assumptions.  
- Request: Add and position ADMM/linear-rate distributed optimization literature (Shi et al.; Scaman et al.) and compare scope/assumptions.  
- Request: Cite lifting/ADMM related work (França and Bento) in the discussion of lifting.  
- Request: Add citations connecting to prior multi-step/momentum consensus work (e.g., Rabbat–Nowak–Bucklew) and heavy-ball/Nesterov literature.  
- Request: Add missing references on accelerated consensus/optimization (EXTRA; fast distributed gradient methods) and situate results accordingly.  
- Comment: Proposition 3 seems a straightforward consequence of the linear formulation and adds limited value.  
- Request: Clarify whether the extra log D factor is intrinsic or removable and specify regimes where the bound is tight or advantageous.  
- Request: Make explicit the equivalence between the auxiliary two-step recursion and Nesterov/shift-register acceleration, including parameter mapping, and state what is novel in the Min-Sum Splitting perspective.  
- Request: Discuss worst-case scaling when D = Θ(n) and whether tuning can remove the extra log factor to match O(D log(1/ε)).  
- Request: Clarify modeling: explain use of directed edge messages on a simple undirected graph early.  
- Minor fix: Correct typos (e.g., Line 220 “Laplacian” → “Lagrangian”) and proofread claims about complete graphs and “optimal” rates on expanders.  

- Quality: Technical development appears sound; main theorem well-motivated and auxiliary formulation is clean.  
- Clarity: Generally well written but literature review incomplete and some statements need precision, especially versus O(D log(1/ε)) results.  
- Originality: Moderate—ideas (lifting/directionality, two-step acceleration) are known; novelty is framing/proof within Min-Sum Splitting.  
- Significance: Incremental; of interest to BP and distributed optimization researchers but limited practical advantage over existing accelerated consensus.  

- Pro accept: Clear theoretical treatment of Min-Sum Splitting with general graph rates.  
- Pro accept: Good unification of BP splittings, lifted walks, and multi-step methods.  
- Pro accept: Addresses convergence-rate questions left open in prior Min-Sum consensus work.  

- Con accept: Modest improvement over state-of-the-art accelerated consensus.  
- Con accept: Important related works are missing or under-explained.  
- Con accept: Some components are straightforward, limiting perceived novelty.  

- After rebuttal: Authors addressed many literature and clarity concerns, but reviewer’s overall evaluation remains unchanged.
