- Studies an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting protocol for consensus, using a reparametrization and an auxiliary linear process to analyze convergence on quadratic objectives.  
- Main contributions: new analysis via an auxiliary process, a faster-converging Min-Sum consensus protocol, and connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods.  
- Paper is generally well written and easy to follow, and proofs of Propositions 1–2 and Theorem 1 appear correct.  
- Contains numerous typos and grammatical errors throughout that need correction (many specific line-by-line fixes provided).  
- Algorithmic details are incomplete: some quantities in the algorithms (e.g., R{vw}, r{vw}, \hat{R}^0, \hat{r}^0) are undefined and the auxiliary process variables are not clearly identified in Algorithm 3.  
- Suggests additional/missing references for (sub)gradient and consensus methods, and for original sources of related literature, Polyak, and Nesterov.  
- Requests clarification on practicality: when Min-Sum Splitting is preferable vs. methods like gradient tracking, ADMM, or EXTRA, including communication/computation trade-offs and parameter sensitivity.  
- Asks for explicit discussion of limitations: graph assumptions, restriction to quadratic objectives, initialization needs, robustness to noise/asynchrony, and parameter tuning.  
- Requests intuition for why the auxiliary linear process captures and accelerates dynamics and how this relates conceptually to lifting and multi-step momentum.  
- Questions about generality: whether the framework can extend beyond quadratic consensus to broader convex, composite, or stochastic settings and what obstacles exist.  
- Wants clearer relevance to ML: justify interest to ML community in terms of scalability, iteration complexity improvements, and links to decentralized training.  
- Overall assessment: solid, technically sound theoretical paper introducing a useful analytical perspective and convergence results, but needs clearer algorithmic exposition, corrected typos, added references, and deeper discussion of practicality and relevance.
