This paper studies an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for consensus in distributed optimization. Building on the reparametrization techniques of Ruozzi and Tatikonda (2013), the authors establish convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting on quadratic consensus objectives. The core analytical tool is an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the algorithm.

Main contributions:
- A new analysis of Min-Sum Splitting via an auxiliary process and proof technique.
- A Min-Sum protocol for consensus that achieves faster convergence than prior results.
- A clear connection between the proposed method, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The motivation and contributions are clearly articulated. The paper is generally well written and easy to follow, though it contains numerous typos and grammatical errors (listed below). The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear correct.

Questions and points for clarification:
- Practicality: Under what conditions is Min-Sum Splitting preferable to other distributed consensus/optimization methods (e.g., gradient tracking, ADMM, EXTRA)? Please discuss communication/computation trade-offs and parameter sensitivity.
- Limitations: What are the main limitations (e.g., graph assumptions, requirement of quadratic objectives, initialization, robustness to noise/asynchrony, parameter tuning)?
- Intuition: Provide intuition for why the auxiliary linear process captures and accelerates the dynamics; how does it relate conceptually to lifting and multi-step momentum?
- Generality: Can the framework extend beyond quadratic consensus to broader convex objectives common in machine learning (e.g., composite or stochastic settings)? What obstacles arise?
- Relevance to ML: Clarify why this approach is of interest to the ML community (e.g., scalability on networks, reduced iteration complexity, links to accelerated decentralized training).

In summary, this is a solid theoretical paper that introduces a useful analytical perspective (via an auxiliary process), derives convergence rates, and connects Min-Sum Splitting to established acceleration paradigms. The ideas are interesting and technically sound. However, the paper would benefit from clearer exposition of algorithmic details, correction of typos, additional references, and a deeper discussion of practicality, intuition, limitations, and relevance to machine learning.
