- Studies consensus (network averaging) using Min-Sum Splitting to produce a linear, convergent message-passing scheme with accelerated (subdiffusive) rates on general graphs.  
- Shows ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge on graphs with cycles, while Min-Sum Splitting with proper tuning converges and gives a square-root improvement over classical diffusive consensus.  
- Presents two equivalent algorithm representations: a linear edge-based process (Alg.2) and a coupled multi-step node process with memory (Alg.3).  
- Node-based representation enables spectral analysis via a 2n×2n matrix K whose spectral radius governs the convergence rate.  
- Main theorem: with δ = 1 and Γ = γW (W symmetric mixing, W1=1) and γ tuned by the spectral gap of W, Min-Sum Splitting matches asymptotic rates of shift-register methods.  
- Establishes a conceptual bridge among Min-Sum Splitting, lifted non-reversible Markov chains, and multi-step/momentum first-order methods.  
- Refines and extends prior analysis in [27] and complements Gaussian belief propagation with splitting results in [35].  
- Novelty: first explicit convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, recovering accelerated rates.  
- Technical contribution: auxiliary node-based analysis is clean, technically reusable, and enables the spectral-rate result.  
- Conceptual unification: clarifies how splitting induces directionality (lifted chains) and momentum-like behavior (multi-step methods).  
- Generality: proves subdiffusive convergence on arbitrary graphs and gives concrete O(D log(D/ε)) scaling on cycles/tori, improving over O(D^2 log(1/ε)).  
- Paper is well-written and clearly situates contributions relative to prior work.  
- Limitation: analysis restricted to quadratic consensus; extension to general convex φv is not addressed.  
- Practical limitation: accelerated tuning requires global spectral quantity ρW, which limits decentralization and demands discussion of distributed estimation or robustness to mis-specification.  
- Unclear role of δ: main results specialize to δ = 1, leaving unclear when δ ≠ 1 helps or improves robustness/rate constants.  
- Initialization and well-posedness concerns: theorem assumes zero initializations and symmetric W; need clarification on conditions for Γ (including possible negative entries) and initialization choices that guarantee valid updates and extension of rates.  
- Empirical gap: no experiments; small-scale comparisons on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs against Metropolis-Hastings, optimized symmetric W, and shift-register/Nesterov would strengthen claims.  
- Clarity fix: explicitly state the integer vs real interpretation of splitting (replication vs reweighting) when δ, Γ are first introduced.  
- Minor typos/notational issues: missing square in φv(z), stray typesetting artifact in intro, and consistency needed between algorithm definitions and K̂(δ,Γ).  
- Practical guidance missing: brief recommendations on choosing W in practice (e.g., Metropolis-Hastings vs optimized symmetric W) and implications for γ would be helpful.  
- Evaluation summary: technically sound, original, and significant for distributed optimization and belief propagation, but limited by quadratic scope, need for global spectral info, and lack of experiments.  
- Acceptance pros: first rigorous accelerated-rate result for Min-Sum Splitting, elegant auxiliary-process proof, and meaningful improvements over diffusive methods.  
- Acceptance cons: restricted to quadratic objectives, no empirical validation, requires global spectral tuning and assumes symmetric W, plus some clarity/well-posedness issues.
