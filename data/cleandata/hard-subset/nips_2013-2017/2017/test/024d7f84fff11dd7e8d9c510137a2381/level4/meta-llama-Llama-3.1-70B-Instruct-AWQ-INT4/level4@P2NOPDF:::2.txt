This paper studies the convergence rate of the min-sum splitting method on the average consensus problem. While the paper is generally well-written, the improvement in the result appears to be marginal. The following comments provide a detailed evaluation of the paper.

(1) The paper claims that the achieved rate is optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph. However, for complete graphs, the linear rate is 0 because everyone converges to the average in one step. Moreover, complete graphs are too special to be representative. Therefore, it would be beneficial to identify a more general category of graphs for which the complexity does not depend on the dimension (number of nodes). The authors should clarify which general category of graphs is considered to have good expansion properties.

(2) In the same paragraph, the literature review should include "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control" by Olshevsky. The convergence rate of this reference should be properly reported (see comment 8). This reference has achieved a competitive or even better bound compared to the result of the submission.

(3) At the top of page 2, important references such as "On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization" by Shi, Ling, Kun, Wu, and Yin, and "Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks" by Scaman, Bach, Bubeck, Lee, and Massoulié should be cited. The authors should also report the state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization and their corresponding (linear) convergence rates.

(4) When discussing lifted graphs and Markov chains, the paper overlooks a related paper "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM" by Franca and Bento.

(5) The content of the last paragraph of page 5 is a well-known fact. The authors should refer to "Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links" by Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew. Furthermore, the connection between those variants and Heavy ball/Nesterov/Polyak is known to the field.

(6) There are several important references regarding consensus optimization that the authors have ignored. For example, "Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization" by Shi, Ling, Wu, and Yin, and "Fast distributed gradient methods" by Jakovetic, J Xavier, and Moura.

(7) Proposition 3 appears to be trivial and is a supplementary contribution.

(8) The rate achieved by this paper, D log(D/eps), does not seem to have a significant improvement on the rate D log(1/eps) achieved by "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control" (see comment 2). Especially in the worst-case scenario (holds for all graphs), D~n, the bound is even worse than that achieved in "Linear Time Average Consensus…".

(9) The paper "Linear Time Average Consensus…" improves the bound through Nesterov's acceleration. This reviewer suspects that the "Auxiliary message-passing scheme" proposed by the authors is again a Nesterov's acceleration applied to the min-sum algorithm. While this is acceptable, the analysis is done for consensus, which boils down to analyzing a linear system and is supposed to be not hard. Consequently, the contribution of the paper becomes unclear in this context.

(10) The tiny improvement may come from a careful handling of the spectral gap of graphs. However, the worst-case bound is still O(n) because O(n)=O(D) for the set of all graphs with n nodes.

(11) Line 243 of page 6: The graph is simple, but the authors use directed edges, which is confusing.

(12) Typo at line 220 of page 6: Laplacian should be Lagrangian.
