- Paper introduces an accelerated Min-Sum message-passing protocol for distributed consensus optimization with reparametrization and establishes convergence rates for quadratic objectives.  
- Core novelty is a new proof technique using an auxiliary linear process to track Min-Sum Splitting’s evolution.  
- Key contributions: (i) rigorous analysis via the auxiliary process, (ii) protocol with superior convergence rates to prior work, (iii) theoretical links to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex methods.  
- Writing and structure are clear and logical; motivation and contributions are well articulated.  
- Technical content is sound: Propositions 1–2 and Theorem 1 are correctly proven.  
- Numerous minor language/typo fixes are needed (examples: “with theirs neighbors” → “with their neighbors”, “double-stochastic” → “doubly-stochastic”, “can be casted as” → “can be cast as”, etc.).  
- Algorithm 2 is unclear: R_vw and r_vw are undefined, initializations of hat{R}^0 and hat{r}^0 are missing, and the auxiliary linear process is not explicitly mapped to Algorithm 3’s variables.  
- Lines 220–221 likely misuse “Laplacian/Laplace multipliers” instead of “Lagrangian/Lagrange multipliers.”  
- Important references are missing for consensus/subgradient methods (Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989; Ram et al. 2009; Shi et al. 2015) and for heavy-ball/Nesterov acceleration (Polyak 1964; Nesterov 1983); “original literature” is cited without specifics.  
- The paper lacks discussion of practicality and scope: when to prefer this method over existing distributed approaches (e.g., EXTRA, D-ADMM) and its computational/communication overhead in real settings.  
- ML relevance is superficial: the authors need to justify why the theoretical advance matters for ML (e.g., large-scale quadratic problems, extensions to non-quadratic losses).  
- Unanswered questions include limitations of Min-Sum Splitting (sensitivity to graph structure, non-quadratic extensions), intuition for why the auxiliary linear process captures Min-Sum dynamics, applicability to general consensus problems (e.g., logistic regression), and comparison of convergence in terms of communication steps or wall-clock time.  
- Overall assessment: technically solid with a novel, well-executed auxiliary-process proof and improved rates, but weakened by incomplete algorithmic descriptions, missing references, and insufficient practical/ML contextualization.
