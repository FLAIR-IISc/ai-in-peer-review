This paper discusses the convergence rate of the so-called mean-sum splitting method in the average consensus problem. In general, the article is well-written, but the improvement in results does not seem very impressive. Detailed comments are given below:

(1) It states that "this rate is favorable for graphs with good propagation characteristics, such as complete graphs. In this case, the convergence time, i.e., the number of iterations required to reach an error accuracy of a certain level of the problem's magnitude, such as..."

For complete graphs, the linear rate is 0, because everyone converges to the average value in 1 step. Also, complete graphs are very special to represent.

So, for which general class of graphs does the complexity not depend on the number of nodes (number of nodes)? Which general class of graph is considered good?

(2) In this paragraph (like comment 1), the literature review should include Oleschansky's "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control." Its convergence rate should be mentioned correctly (further explanation will be given in comment 8). The reference mentioned here has reached a quite competitive or even better limit compared to the submitted results.

(3) At the beginning of page 2, for consensus optimization, important references such as Shi, Ling, Kun, Wu, and Yin's "On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization," and Skaman, Bach, Bubek, Li, Masovite's "Optimal Algorithms for Smooth and Strongly Convex Distributed Optimization in Networks" should be mentioned. Also, the authors should mention the state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization and their corresponding (linear) convergence rates.

(4) While discussing lifted graphs and Markov chains, this paper has ignored a related paper by Franka and Benter, "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM."

(5) The content of the last paragraph on page 5 is a well-known fact. Reference should be given to Rabbat, Novak, and Bakul's "Generalized Consensus Computation in Networked Systems with Eraser Links." In continuation, the connection of these variants with the heavy ball/Nesterov/Polyak is well known.

(6) There are many important references related to consensus optimization that the authors have ignored. For example, Shi, Ling, Wu, and Yin's "Extra: An Exact First-Order Algorithm for Decentralized Consensus Optimization," and Jacovetic, J. Xavier, and Moura's "Fast Distributed Gradient Methods."

(7) Proposal 3 seems trivial and is an additional contribution.

(8) The rate mentioned in this paper, D log(D/eps), does not seem to have significantly improved the rate D log(1/eps) obtained by the linear time average consensus on fixed graphs and implications for decentralized optimization and multi-agent control (see comment 2). Especially in the worst-case scenario (applicable to all graphs), D~n, this limit is even worse than the limit achieved in the "linear time average consensus..."

(9) The "linear time average consensus..." paper improves the limit through Nesterov's acceleration. The reviewer suspects that the authors' proposed so-called " सहायक message-sending scheme" is again applying Nesterov's acceleration to the mean-sum algorithm. This is fine, but the analysis is done for consensus, which is supposed to analyze a linear system and should not be difficult. In such a situation, the contribution of the paper is not clear.

(10) A slight improvement can come by working carefully on the spectral gap of the graph. Ultimately, the worst-case limit is still O(n), because O(n)=O(D) for all graphs with n number of nodes.

(11) Line 243 on page 6. The graph is simple but the author is using the indicated edges. This is misleading.

(12) Typo on line 220 on page 6. Laplacian—> Lagrangian.
