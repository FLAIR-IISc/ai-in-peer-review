

The paper presents an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for distributed consensus optimization, leveraging reparametrization techniques from Ruozzi and Tatikonda (2013) to establish convergence rates for quadratic objective functions. The core analytical contribution is a novel proof framework based on an auxiliary linear process that tracks the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm’s evolution.  

The main contributions are: (i) a new proof technique using an auxiliary process for analyzing Min-Sum Splitting; (ii) a Min-Sum protocol achieving faster convergence than prior results; and (iii) connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods. The paper’s motivation and theoretical contributions are clear, and the writing is well-organized. The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1, appear correct. Additionally, minor technical gaps require attention:  
- Lines 220–221: "Laplacian" and "Laplace multipliers" likely refer to "Lagrangian" and "Lagrange multipliers."  
- Algorithm 2 defines quantities R_{vw} and r_{vw} without explanation, and initializes \hat{R}^0, \hat{r}^0 inconsistently. Since the auxiliary linear process is central to the analysis, Algorithm 3 should explicitly map variables to this process.  

The paper omits key references:  
- For (sub)gradient methods (Lines 41, 43), include Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis (1989), Sundhar Ram et al. (2009), and Shi et al. (2015).  
- Line 170: "Original literature" should cite specific works.  
- Line 229: Reference Polyak’s Heavy-ball method.  
- Line 232: Reference Nesterov’s accelerated methods.  

The authors should address these critical questions to strengthen the paper’s impact:  
1. **Practicality**: When should this method be preferred over existing distributed consensus algorithms (e.g., EXTRA, D-ADMM)?  
2. **Limitations**: What are the constraints on problem structure or communication topology?  
3. **Intuition**: Why does the auxiliary process enable improved convergence?  
4. **Generality**: Can the framework extend beyond quadratic objectives to typical ML problems (e.g., logistic regression, non-smooth losses)?  
5. **ML Relevance**: Explicitly state why this theoretical advance matters to the machine learning community.  

While the theoretical contributions are significant—establishing new convergence rates via a novel proof technique and linking to established methods—the paper lacks discussion of practical applicability and ML relevance. The absence of concrete examples, comparisons with state-of-the-art distributed methods, or justification for the method’s utility in real ML settings weakens its contribution. Theoretical elegance alone is insufficient for a NeurIPS submission; the authors must clarify *why* this work advances the field beyond the existing literature.  

In summary, this is a technically sound theoretical paper with a novel proof framework. However, the omission of practical context, unresolved technical ambiguities, and insufficient ML relevance detract from its significance. Addressing these points would substantially improve the paper’s impact and alignment with NeurIPS’ focus on both theory and practical machine learning.
