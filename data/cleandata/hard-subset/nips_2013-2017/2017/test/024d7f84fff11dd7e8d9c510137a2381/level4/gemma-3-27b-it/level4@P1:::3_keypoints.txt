- Paper presents an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting variant for consensus in distributed optimization using reparametrization from Ruozzi & Tatikonda (2013).
- Establishes convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting on quadratic consensus problems via an auxiliary linear process that tracks the algorithm.
- Main contributions: (i) new proof technique with an auxiliary process, (ii) a Min-Sum protocol with improved convergence, (iii) connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods.
- Motivation and contributions are clear; paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
- Proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear correct.
- Paper contains several typos and grammatical errors (enumerated by line), requiring proofreading.
- Some algorithmic quantities are not explained or initialized (e.g., R{vw}, r{vw}, R̂⁰, r̂⁰) and the auxiliary process variables should be clearly identified in Algorithm 3.
- Reviewer questions the use of "Laplacian" and "Laplace multipliers" vs. "Lagrangian" and "Lagrange multipliers" in Lines 220–221.
- Several important references in distributed optimization and consensus are missing (e.g., Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989; Srinivasan et al. 2009; Wei Shi 2015), as well as classical acceleration works (Polyak, Nesterov).
- Reviewer asks for discussion of practicality: when to use this method over existing distributed consensus methods.
- Reviewer requests clarification on limitations of Min-Sum Splitting and intuition behind the auxiliary process.
- Asks whether the framework can be extended beyond quadratic objectives to more general consensus problems common in ML.
- Reviewer requests explicit articulation of the paper’s relevance and importance to the Machine Learning community.
- Summary assessment: theoretically interesting and novel with sound analysis, but lacks practical discussion, missing references, and clearer exposition of algorithmic variables and ML relevance.
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, theoretical soundness, significance for accelerating consensus, and originality.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient practical discussion, missing citations, minor clarity issues, and unclear ML relevance.
