This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum Splitting, an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum algorithm, to the distributed consensus problem. The work is exceptionally well-written, clearly articulating the contribution within the existing literature. To the best of my knowledge, and despite not being a specialist in this area, the results appear novel and represent a significant advancement. A key contribution lies in establishing a novel connection between Min-Sum algorithms and lifted Markov chain techniques, alongside parallels to accelerated first-order methods.

The paper builds upon prior work showing that the standard Min-Sum algorithm may not converge for arbitrary graphs, and that a modified version – Splitting – can achieve convergence. This paper goes further, rigorously establishing convergence *rates* for Min-Sum Splitting when applied to the consensus problem, demonstrating subdiffusive, accelerated convergence matching rates achieved by shift-register methods. This is a substantial result, offering a performance improvement over classical diffusive methods. The authors expertly connect their work to related research in lifted Markov chains and accelerated optimization, providing valuable context and highlighting the broader implications of their findings.

A minor point of clarification is needed in the description of the equivalent objective function resulting from the splitting scheme (page 4). The authors state that the algorithm splits each term φ<sub>vw</sub> into Γ<sub>vw</sub> terms and each term φ<sub>v</sub> into δ terms. However, given that δ and Γ<sub>vw</sub> are defined as real numbers on the preceding page, it’s unclear what “splitting into” these terms signifies. Providing further elaboration on this process would improve clarity.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Technical Contribution:** The paper presents a rigorous analysis of Min-Sum Splitting, providing theoretical convergence rate guarantees.
*   **Clear Exposition:** The writing is exceptionally clear and well-organized, making the complex ideas accessible. The paper effectively situates the work within the broader context of distributed optimization.
*   **Novelty:** The connection between Min-Sum algorithms, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated optimization techniques appears to be a novel contribution.
*   **Significance:** The acceleration achieved by Min-Sum Splitting is demonstrably significant, offering improvements over existing methods for distributed consensus.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Minor Clarity Issue:** The explanation of the objective function splitting requires further clarification (as noted above).
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, the paper would benefit from some experimental validation to demonstrate the practical performance gains of Min-Sum Splitting, even if limited in scope.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental problem in distributed optimization with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The rigorous analysis and clear presentation make a significant contribution to the field.
*   The connection to related work highlights the broader implications of the findings.
*   The potential for practical impact through improved convergence rates is substantial.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical validation, while not a fatal flaw, weakens the practical impact of the results.
*   The minor clarity issue, though easily addressed, detracts slightly from the overall presentation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper with significant theoretical contributions. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, with a request that the authors clarify the description of the objective function splitting. While empirical validation would be a valuable addition, it is not essential for acceptance given the strength of the theoretical results and the clarity of the presentation.
