- Paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for distributed average consensus, proving convergence with proper parameters and analyzing its convergence rate.  
- Main technical contribution: an auxiliary node-based message-passing scheme that reduces edge dynamics to a 2n-dimensional linear system for spectral analysis.  
- Shows Min-Sum Splitting can produce accelerated, “subdiffusive” convergence and connects this to lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods.  
- Claims to recover shift-register dynamics and obtains a convergence time of O(D log(D/ε)) for certain graph families.  

Strengths:
- Novel explicit connection between Min-Sum message-passing and acceleration techniques (lifted Markov chains, momentum-like methods).  
- Elegant analytical technique (Algorithm 3) mapping 2m edge dynamics to a tractable 2n node-based matrix.  
- Paper is generally well-written, clearly structured, and methodical in its presentation.  

Weaknesses:
- Significance of rate: O(D log(D/ε)) includes a log(D) factor vs. Olshevsky’s O(D log(1/ε)), making improvement unclear or potentially worse in common regimes.  
- Insufficient literature review: omits key recent works (Scaman et al. 2017, Shi et al. on ADMM/EXTRA, Jakovetić et al., França & Bento) necessary to position contribution.  
- Limited novelty: specific parameter choices recover known shift-register/accelerated methods, suggesting interpretation rather than a fundamentally new faster algorithm.  

Detailed questions/suggestions:
- Clarify comparison with Olshevsky [30] and identify any regimes where the presented bound is superior.  
- Make precise the claim about dimension-independent scaling for graphs with good expansion and specify broader graph classes beyond the complete graph.  
- Acknowledge prior literature linking multi-step updates to acceleration (e.g., Rabbat et al.) and clarify how this work builds on or differs from that understanding.  
- Incorporate the missing references and explicitly compare rate, assumptions, and implementation complexity with modern distributed optimization algorithms.  
- State early and clearly that the algorithm is defined over directed edges derived from an undirected graph to avoid confusion.  

Summary (accept vs. reject arguments):
- For acceptance: provides a novel perspective linking Min-Sum Splitting to known acceleration techniques and an elegant auxiliary-process analysis; well-presented.  
- Against acceptance: convergence rate offers no clear improvement over state-of-the-art and literature omissions weaken claims of originality and significance.
