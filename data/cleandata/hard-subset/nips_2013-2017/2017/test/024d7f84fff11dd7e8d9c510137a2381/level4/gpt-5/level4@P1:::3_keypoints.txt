- Applies Min-Sum Splitting to distributed consensus and proves convergence rates for the quadratic (averaging) case.  
- Introduces splitting parameters (δ, Γ) that ensure convergence on general graphs and, when tuned, yield subdiffusive (accelerated) rates matching shift-register methods.  
- Technical analysis follows Min-Sum Splitting via a constructed auxiliary node-based linear process even though messages run on directed edges.  
- Draws connections between splitting-induced directionality and lifted non-reversible Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods (heavy-ball, Nesterov, shift-register).  
- First convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, complementing earlier Min-Sum work that obtained diffusive scaling or different dynamics.  
- With appropriate tuning, achieves near-optimal asymptotic rates (square-root improvement) on graphs with geometry.  
- Strength: provides rigorous spectral bounds and correct proofs for Propositions 1, 2 and Theorem 1.  
- Strength: conceptual unification of belief propagation, lifted chains, and multi-step acceleration; high originality.  
- Weakness: numerous typos and grammatical errors throughout the manuscript.  
- Weakness: some notation and initialization details in algorithms are underspecified (notably Algorithm 2 and mapping to Algorithm 3).  
- Weakness: missing key references (subgradient/gradient consensus methods, heavy-ball, Nesterov, classical consensus/gossip literature).  
- Weakness: paper is purely theoretical and lacks discussion of practicality, trade-offs, limitations, and implementation overhead.  
- Weakness: limited to quadratic consensus; unclear extensibility and relevance beyond quadratic objectives for ML.  
- Quality: technically sound and carefully analyzed for the quadratic case, but practical considerations are underexplored.  
- Clarity: generally readable but hurt by typos and a few notational gaps; needs all variables defined on first use and clearer identification of the auxiliary process.  
- Originality: high — first rate analysis in this setting with insightful dual connections to other acceleration techniques.  
- Significance: theoretically interesting and potentially influential, but practical impact for practitioners/ML community currently under-argued.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel rigorous analysis, clear conceptual connections, and near-optimal asymptotic rates.  
- Cons for acceptance: restricted to quadratic consensus, missing references, notational gaps, and many typographical issues.  
- Reviewer requests: fix typos, define all algorithmic quantities (e.g., R{vw}, r{vw}, ĤR0, Ĥr0), and clearly map Algorithm 2 to the auxiliary process in Algorithm 3.  
- Reviewer-specified missing references include Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis (1989), Sundhar Ram et al. (2009), Shi et al. (EXTRA, 2015), Polyak (heavy-ball), Nesterov, and foundational consensus/gossip works.  
- Questions for authors: when prefer Min-Sum Splitting vs alternatives, communication/computation trade-offs, limitations and parameter tuning dependence, intuition for the auxiliary process, extensibility beyond quadratic losses, and ML-relevance/applications.  
- Recommendation summary: solid theoretical contribution with elegant connections; authors should correct typos, fill missing definitions/references, and add discussion of practicality, limitations, and ML relevance to maximize impact.
