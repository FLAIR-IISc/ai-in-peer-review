- Applies Min-Sum Splitting to distributed consensus and shows Min-Sum Splitting can converge where ordinary Min-Sum fails on loopy graphs.
- Main contribution: with proper tuning, Min-Sum Splitting achieves accelerated "subdiffusive" convergence (square-root improvement over diffusive methods) matching shift-register methods.
- Introduces an auxiliary node-based message-passing scheme that tracks edge-based Min-Sum Splitting, simplifying spectral analysis.
- Technical novelty: maps 2m-dimensional edge dynamics to a 2n-dimensional node state-space and leverages shift-register spectral analysis (Theorem 1).
- Conceptual novelty: formally connects Min-Sum/Belief Propagation, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step first-order optimization as acceleration mechanisms.
- Provides the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and generalizes beyond prior work limited to regular graphs.
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and situates contributions relative to diffusive and accelerated methods.
- Minor clarity issue: exposition mixes real-valued splitting parameters with an integer "splitting" interpretation, which could confuse readers.
- Practical limitation: the accelerated tuning requires global spectral information (ρW), and the paper does not discuss distributed estimation or practical implications of this requirement.
- Scope limitation: analysis is confined to quadratic network averaging; extension to general convex φv is not addressed.
- Pros summarized: novel connections, rigorous analysis, competitive accelerated rates, and an elegant auxiliary-process proof technique.
- Cons summarized: need for global spectral info, quadratic-only analysis, and the minor splitting-interpretation confusion.
- Overall recommendation: high-quality, significant contribution with strengths outweighing minor weaknesses; strong acceptance recommendation.
