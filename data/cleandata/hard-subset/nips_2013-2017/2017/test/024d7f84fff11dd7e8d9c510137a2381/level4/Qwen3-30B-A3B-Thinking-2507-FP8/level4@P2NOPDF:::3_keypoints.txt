- Main contribution: New proof framework using an auxiliary linear process to analyze Min-Sum Splitting and establish convergence rates for quadratic objectives.  
- Algorithmic result: Presents a Min-Sum protocol that achieves faster convergence for consensus problems than prior results.  
- Connections: Demonstrates links between the proposed method, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step convex optimization techniques.  
- Presentation quality: Paper is generally readable and well-structured but contains several grammatical errors and minor presentation issues.  
- Proofs: Proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear sound.  
- Minor copyedits: Numerous line-level corrections required (e.g., “with theirs neighbors” → “with their neighbors”, “double-stochastic” → “doubly-stochastic”, etc.).  
- Algorithm clarity: Algorithm 2 lacks definitions/initialization for key quantities (R_{vw}, r_{vw}, hat{R}^0, hat{r}^0); Algorithm 3 should explicitly map variables to the auxiliary system.  
- Terminology confusion: Lines 220–221 likely misuse “Laplacian” and “Laplace multipliers” instead of “Lagrangian” and “Lagrange multipliers.”  
- Literature gaps: Missing key references on distributed optimization (Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989; Ram et al. 2009; Shi et al. 2015 EXTRA) and classic acceleration methods (Polyak, Nesterov).  
- Practical relevance: Paper lacks justification of when Min-Sum Splitting should be preferred over established methods (e.g., gradient-based, EXTRA) in practice.  
- Trade-offs and scalability: Authors must clarify practical trade-offs (communication, computation, scalability) compared to alternative distributed consensus methods.  
- Limitations: Need explicit discussion of limitations (sensitivity to graph structure, restriction to quadratic objectives, communication overhead).  
- Intuition: Paper should provide deeper intuition for why the auxiliary linear process effectively captures Min-Sum dynamics beyond serving as a proof tool.  
- Generalization: Unclear whether the framework extends to non-quadratic ML problems (e.g., logistic regression, SVMs); authors should state barriers or possible extensions.  
- ML impact and recommendation: While theoretically solid, the paper currently lacks ML motivation and broader relevance, making it borderline for acceptance; addressing major concerns would substantially strengthen the submission.
