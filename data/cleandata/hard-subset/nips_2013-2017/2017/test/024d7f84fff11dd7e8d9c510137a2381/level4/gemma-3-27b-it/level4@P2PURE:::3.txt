This paper presents an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for solving consensus problems in distributed optimization. The authors leverage the reparametrization techniques introduced in [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013] and derive rates of convergence for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. The core analytical tool is the construction of an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

The main contributions of the paper are threefold: (i) providing a novel analysis of the Min-Sum splitting algorithm based on the introduction of an auxiliary process, (ii) designing a Min-Sum protocol for consensus problems achieving improved convergence compared to existing results, and (iii) demonstrating connections between the proposed method and lifted Markov chains, as well as multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The motivation and contributions are clearly articulated. The paper is generally well-written and accessible, though it contains several typos and grammatical errors (detailed below). The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appear correct.

It would be beneficial if the authors addressed the following questions/points in the paper:
- While this is a theoretical work, commenting on the practicality of the method and identifying scenarios where it would be preferable to other distributed consensus optimization techniques would be valuable.
- What are the limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method?
- What is the underlying intuition for using the auxiliary process within the Min-Sum Splitting method?
- The current results apply to consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. Can this framework be generalized to handle more complex consensus problems common in Machine Learning?
- The authors should clarify why this approach is relevant to, and of interest for, the Machine Learning community.

In summary, this paper is a theoretical contribution establishing convergence rates using a novel proof technique and highlighting connections to established methods. The ideas presented are interesting, but the paper would benefit from discussing the practicality of the method, providing intuition for the results, and articulating its relevance to the Machine Learning community.
