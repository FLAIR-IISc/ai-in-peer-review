This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum Splitting, a generalized version of the Min-Sum message-passing algorithm, to the distributed consensus problem. The authors first establish that while the ordinary Min-Sum protocol fails to converge on general graphs, the Splitting variant can be made to converge to the correct solution. The main contribution is to show that a proper tuning of the algorithm's splitting parameters leads to accelerated, subdiffusive convergence rates. The work situates this acceleration mechanism in the broader context of distributed optimization by drawing novel connections to two established lines of research: lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first-order methods, such as shift-register methods.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper's main contribution appears to be highly original and significant. It presents the first analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting and demonstrates that it can achieve accelerated performance matching that of shift-register methods. The connection established between the reparametrization in Min-Sum Splitting and the acceleration mechanisms in lifted Markov chains and multi-step momentum methods is a particularly insightful and valuable contribution that bridges previously distinct areas of research. This work constitutes a substantial advance for message-passing algorithms in distributed optimization.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears sound, culminating in Theorem 1, which formally establishes the accelerated convergence rate. The introduction of the auxiliary message-passing scheme on the nodes is a clever analytical tool that simplifies the analysis of the original algorithm running on the edges.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a thorough overview of the consensus problem and related literature, clearly positioning the paper's contributions. The logical flow from the general algorithm to its specific application and analysis is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses/Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   A minor point regarding clarity could be improved. In Section 2, the authors provide an intuition for the algorithm's name, stating it can be interpreted as "...splitting each term $\phi_{vw}$ into $\Gamma_{vw}$ terms, and each term $\phi_v$ into $\delta$ terms." This suggests that $\Gamma_{vw}$ and $\delta$ are positive integers representing counts. However, throughout the paper, these are defined and used as real-valued parameters. While the authors correctly state that Algorithm 1 is defined for any real choice of parameters, the initial explanation could be confusing. Clarifying that this interpretation is meant to provide intuition for the integer-valued case would be helpful to the reader.
