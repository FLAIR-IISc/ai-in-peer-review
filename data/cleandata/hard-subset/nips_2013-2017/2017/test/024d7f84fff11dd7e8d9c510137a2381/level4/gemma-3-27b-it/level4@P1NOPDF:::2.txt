This paper studies the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method applied to the average consensus problem. While the paper is generally well-written, the improvement offered by the result appears incremental. Detailed comments are as follows:

(1) The paper states: “This rate is optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph. In this case the convergence time, i.e., the number of iterations required to reach a prescribed level of error accuracy, scales with the dimension of the problem.” For complete graphs, the convergence rate is actually 0, as all nodes converge to the average in a single step. Furthermore, complete graphs are a rather specific case and not representative of general network topologies. 
Therefore, for what general categories of graphs does the complexity *not* depend on the dimension (number of nodes)? What criteria define a graph as having “good” expansion properties in this context?

(2) In the paragraph discussing convergence rates (same as comment 1), the literature review should include the paper “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control” by Olshevsky. Its convergence rate should be reported accurately, as it represents a strong baseline (further details in comment 8). The bounds achieved in this paper are competitive with, or even better than, the results presented in this submission.

(3) At the top of page 2, when discussing consensus optimization, relevant references such as “On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization” by Shi, Ling, Kun, Wu, and Yin, and “Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks” by Scaman, Bach, Bubeck, Lee, and Massoulié, should be cited. The authors should also provide a summary of the state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization and their corresponding (linear) convergence rates.

(4) When discussing lifted graphs and Markov chains, the paper overlooks a highly relevant work: “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM” by Franca and Bento.

(5) The content of the last paragraph of page 5 represents a well-established result. The authors should cite “Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links” by Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew. The connection between these variants and techniques like Heavy-ball, Nesterov’s, and Polyak’s methods is also widely known within the field.

(6) The authors have omitted several important references pertaining to consensus optimization. For example, “Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization” by Shi, Ling, Wu, and Yin, and “Fast distributed gradient methods” by Jakovetic, Xavier, and Moura should be included.

(7) Proposition 3 appears to be a trivial contribution and could be considered supplementary material.

(8) The convergence rate achieved by this paper, D log(D/eps), does not represent a significant improvement over the rate D log(1/eps) obtained in “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control” (see comment 2). In the worst-case scenario (which holds for all graphs), where D ~ n, the bound is even *worse* than that reported in “Linear Time Average Consensus…”.

(9) The paper “Linear Time Average Consensus…” leverages Nesterov’s acceleration to achieve its results. The reviewer suspects that the authors’ proposed “Auxiliary message-passing scheme” is effectively Nesterov’s acceleration applied to the min-sum algorithm. If so, the analysis should be straightforward, as it amounts to analyzing a linear system. Given this, the contribution of the paper becomes less clear.

(10) Any marginal improvement may stem from a careful analysis of the spectral gap of the graphs. However, the worst-case bound remains O(n) because O(n) = O(D) for the set of all graphs with n nodes.

(11) Line 243 of page 6 depicts a simple graph but utilizes directed edges. This is potentially confusing.

(12) Typo at line 220 of page 6: “Laplacian” should be “Lagrangian.”
