This work introduces an accelerated version of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol tailored for consensus problems within distributed optimization. Leveraging the reparametrization techniques from [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013], the authors derive convergence rates for their Min-Sum Splitting algorithm when applied to consensus problems with quadratic objectives. The analysis hinges on the construction of an auxiliary linear process designed to track the algorithm's evolution.

The paper's primary contributions are threefold: (i) developing an analysis for the Min-Sum splitting algorithm via a novel proof technique that relies on an auxiliary process, (ii) proposing a Min-Sum protocol for consensus problems that demonstrates improved convergence over existing results, and (iii) elucidating the connection between the proposed methodology and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The motivation and contributions of the paper are well-articulated. The manuscript is generally clear and readable, although it would benefit from a thorough proofreading to correct several typographical and grammatical errors (detailed below). My inspection of the proofs for Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1, suggests they are sound.

**Other Minor Comments:**
*   Lines 220 and 221: Is the intended terminology "Lagrangian" and "Lagrange multipliers" rather than "Laplacian" and "Laplace multipliers"?
*   The paper outlines three algorithms, but the notation is not always fully described. For instance, the roles of R{vw} and r{vw} in Algorithm 2 are not defined. Furthermore, the variables \hat{R}^0 and \hat{r}^0 in Algorithm 2 appear to lack initialization. Given that the auxiliary linear process is a central concept, it would be beneficial for the authors to explicitly identify which variables in Algorithm 3 correspond to this process.

**Missing References:**

    *   Bertsekas and Tsitsiklis, Parallel and distributed computation: numerical methods, 1989
    *   Sundhar Ram Srinivasan et. al., Incremental stochastic subgradient algorithms for convex optimization, 2009

To improve the paper, I encourage the authors to address the following points:
*   While this is a theoretical work, a discussion on the method's practicality would be valuable. When should this approach be preferred over other distributed methods for consensus optimization?
*   What are the inherent limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method?
*   Could the authors provide more intuition regarding the use of an auxiliary process in their analysis?
*   The current results are for consensus problems with quadratic objectives. Can this framework be generalized to the broader class of consensus problems frequently encountered in Machine Learning?
*   The authors should explicitly articulate why this approach is of interest to the Machine Learning community and what its potential impact could be.

In conclusion, this is a theoretical contribution that establishes convergence rates using a novel proof technique and highlights connections to established methods. While the core ideas are novel and interesting, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion on the method's practical implications, the intuition behind its design, and its specific relevance to the Machine Learning audience.
