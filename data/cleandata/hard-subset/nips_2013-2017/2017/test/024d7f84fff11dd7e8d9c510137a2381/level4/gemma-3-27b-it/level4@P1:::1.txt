This paper applies an accelerated variant of the min-sum algorithm, called min-sum splitting, to the distributed consensus problem. The paper is very well written, with the contribution clearly positioned within the context of the state of the art in the field. To the best of my knowledge (although I am not an expert in this specific area), the results are novel and represent a significant advance. In particular, the paper establishes a novel connection between min-sum algorithms and lifted Markov chain techniques. 

There is one detail that could be clarified in the presentation. On page 4, when describing the equivalent objective function minimized by the min-sum algorithm to yield the min-sum splitting scheme, the authors state: "...splitting each term $\phi_{vw}$ into $\Gamma_{vw}$ terms, and each term $\phi_v$ into $\delta$ terms,...". However, it is unclear what this means, as $\delta$ and $\Gamma_{vw}$, as introduced on the previous page, are real numbers. It would be helpful to provide more detail regarding this splitting process – perhaps clarifying that these parameters are used as weights in a re-formulation of the objective function.

**Summary of strengths:**

*   **Strong technical contribution:** The paper presents a novel analysis of Min-Sum Splitting and demonstrates its ability to achieve accelerated convergence rates for the consensus problem.
*   **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the background, methodology, and results. The connection to related work is thoroughly discussed.
*   **Novelty:** The connection between Min-Sum schemes, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step methods is a valuable contribution to the field.
*   **Rigorous analysis:** The paper includes a thorough theoretical analysis and establishes clear convergence rates.

**Summary of weaknesses:**

*   **Minor clarity issue:** The explanation of the splitting process involving δ and Γvw could be more detailed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in distributed optimization.
*   The proposed approach achieves accelerated convergence rates, representing a significant improvement over existing methods.
*   The analysis is rigorous and well-supported by theoretical results.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and contributions.
*   The connection to related work is comprehensive and insightful.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The minor clarity issue regarding the splitting process could potentially hinder understanding for some readers. However, this is a relatively minor point and can be easily addressed with a more detailed explanation.

**Overall assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The technical analysis is rigorous, the results are significant, and the paper is well-written. The minor clarity issue is easily addressable and does not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance.
