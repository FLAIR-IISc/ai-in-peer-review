This paper analyzes the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method applied to the average consensus problem. While the paper is generally well-written, the reported improvements over existing work appear incremental. Several points require clarification and additional context within related literature.

Specifically, the claim regarding optimality for graphs with good expansion properties (e.g., complete graphs) is misleading. The linear convergence rate for complete graphs is 0, as consensus is achieved in a single iteration. Framing the result in terms of complete graphs, which are a highly specific case, weakens the perceived generality of the work. The authors should clearly delineate for which broader classes of graphs the proposed method exhibits complexity independent of the problem dimension (number of nodes) and provide a more precise characterization of what constitutes a "good" graph in this context.

The literature review lacks crucial references. The paper "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control" by Olshevsky (2010) is notably absent. This work achieves a convergence rate that, upon proper reporting, is competitive with – and in some cases superior to – the results presented in this submission. A thorough comparison, including the specific convergence rates achieved by Olshevsky’s algorithm, is necessary.

Furthermore, when discussing consensus optimization (near the top of page 2), the authors should acknowledge and cite relevant work such as “On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization” (Shi et al., 2014) and “Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks” (Scaman et al., 2011). A comprehensive overview of state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization and their associated linear convergence rates is essential for properly positioning this contribution.

The discussion of lifted graphs and Markov chains overlooks the related paper “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM” by Franca and Bento (2016). This omission is significant given the close connection between the techniques. 

The assertion in the last paragraph of page 5 regarding generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links is a well-established result (Rabbat et al., 2005). The subsequent connection to Heavy ball, Nesterov, and Polyak methods is also known within the field and should be properly acknowledged.

The paper would benefit from citing additional relevant literature in consensus optimization, including “Extra: An Exact First-Order Algorithm for Decentralized Consensus Optimization” (Shi et al., 2015) and “Fast distributed gradient methods” (Jakovetic & Moura, 2014). 

Proposition 3 appears to be a relatively trivial extension and constitutes a minor contribution.

The achieved convergence rate of *D*log(*D*/ε) does not represent a substantial improvement over the *D*log(1/*ε*) rate established by Olshevsky (2010). In the worst-case scenario, where *D* is proportional to the number of nodes *n*, the proposed bound is even less favorable than that of Olshevsky's work.

The reviewer suspects that the “Auxiliary message-passing scheme” proposed by the authors is, in effect, an application of Nesterov's acceleration to the min-sum algorithm. While this is a valid approach, the analysis for consensus ultimately reduces to analyzing a linear system, which is generally considered a tractable problem.  Therefore, the novelty of the paper is unclear.

Any potential improvement likely stems from a careful analysis of the graph’s spectral gap. However, the worst-case bound remains O(*n*), as O(*n*) = O(*D*) for graphs with *n* nodes.

Minor issues include a potentially confusing use of directed edges in a simple graph (line 243, page 6) and a typographical error ("Laplacian" instead of "Lagrangian" at line 220, page 6).
