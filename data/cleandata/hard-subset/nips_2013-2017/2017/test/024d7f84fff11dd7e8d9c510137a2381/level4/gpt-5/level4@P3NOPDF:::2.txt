Summary
The paper analyzes the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method for the average consensus problem and proposes an auxiliary message-passing scheme. While the paper is clearly written and the analysis appears careful, the improvement over existing results seems modest, and several important related works are missing or underrepresented.

Major comments
- Claims of optimality and graph classes: The paper states that the obtained rate is optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph, and that the convergence time (iterations to reach a prescribed error) is independent of the problem dimension in such cases. This needs clarification.
  - For complete graphs, standard consensus with appropriate weights reaches the average in one iteration; thus a “linear rate” discussion there is uninformative. Complete graphs are too special to be representative.
  - Please precisely define what is meant by “good expansion properties,” and specify the broad graph families (e.g., expanders, bounded-diameter families, Ramanujan graphs) for which the complexity is dimension-independent. Beyond complete graphs, what general conditions on spectral gap, conductance, or diameter guarantee the claimed behavior?

- Comparison to Olshevsky (Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs…): The literature review in the paragraph discussing convergence time should include Olshevsky’s work and report its convergence rate correctly. That work achieves a D log(1/ε) rate (D = diameter) and is highly competitive with, or better than, the result claimed here (D log(D/ε)), especially in worst-case regimes. A head-to-head comparison across canonical graph families (paths, cycles, grids, expanders) would be helpful.

- Scope versus novelty: The proposed auxiliary message-passing scheme appears closely related to Nesterov-style acceleration applied to a linear system (the consensus dynamics). If so, this is a reasonable approach, but the novelty is then limited, as the analysis reduces to linear system acceleration. Please clarify whether the scheme is essentially an accelerated variant of min-sum (e.g., heavy-ball/Nesterov/Polyak) and delineate what is genuinely new in the algorithm or analysis.

- Worst-case bounds: The claimed improvement D log(D/ε) over D log(1/ε) is not compelling, and in the worst case D ≍ n, yielding an O(n log(n/ε)) bound, which is weaker than the linear-time O(n log(1/ε)) result from Olshevsky in that regime. Please discuss whether your bound can match O(n log(1/ε)) uniformly over all graphs, or whether there are structural advantages your method exploits that others cannot.

Related work coverage
Several key references on consensus and decentralized optimization are missing or under-discussed. Including and properly situating your contributions relative to these will strengthen the paper:
- Average consensus and acceleration:
  - Olshevsky, “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control.”
  - Rabbat, Nowak, Bucklew, “Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links” (connections to heavy-ball/Nesterov-type acceleration are well known and should be acknowledged).
  - Franca, Bento, “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM” (relevant to lifted chains and acceleration).
- Decentralized optimization with linear convergence:
  - Shi, Ling, Wu, Yin, “On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization.”
  - Scaman, Bach, Bubeck, Lee, Massoulié, “Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks.”
  - Shi, Ling, Wu, Yin, “EXTRA: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization.”
  - Jakovetić, Xavier, Moura, “Fast distributed gradient methods.”
Please report the best-known linear convergence rates (and optimality claims) of these methods and clearly position your results relative to them, especially in terms of dependence on spectral gap, condition number, and diameter.

Technical comments
- Clarify the precise conditions under which the complexity becomes dimension-free: which spectral properties (second-largest eigenvalue, conductance), graph families, and parameter choices (including splitting parameters) are required?
- If the improvement hinges on refined spectral arguments or lifted Markov chains, please explain the relationship to known lifting/acceleration techniques and whether your construction yields provable optimality within a given model class.
- Proposition 3 appears straightforward and reads as a supplementary observation rather than a core contribution; consider moving it to an appendix or reframing its role.
- Provide explicit constants (when possible) in the convergence bounds to enable practical comparison with existing accelerated consensus schemes.
- Elaborate on whether your auxiliary messages require additional memory, synchronization, or knowledge of global parameters (e.g., spectral gap), and how this compares to the requirements in prior accelerated methods.

Clarity and presentation
- The paragraph claiming optimality on graphs with good expansion should be rewritten for precision. Avoid relying on complete graphs as a primary example; instead, use expander families with explicit spectral properties.
- The discussion tying variants of consensus to heavy-ball/Nesterov/Polyak acceleration is a known connection; cite prior art and clearly articulate what is new here.
- Page 6, line 243: The graph is described as simple, yet directed edges are used; this is confusing. Please reconcile the definitions and notation.
- Page 6, line 220: Typo: “Laplacian” should be “Lagrangian.”

Overall evaluation
- Strengths: Clear writing; careful spectral perspective; potentially useful practical scheme if the acceleration is simple to implement.
- Weaknesses: Incremental theoretical improvement; insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art accelerated consensus/optimization methods; lack of clarity on the generality of dimension-free convergence; several missing citations.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. With a more comprehensive related-work discussion, sharper positioning versus Olshevsky and modern decentralized optimization results, clearer claims about the graph classes for which the method is optimal or dimension-free, and a more transparent connection to acceleration methods (or a distinctly novel mechanism), the paper could become much stronger.

After rebuttal
I am satisfied with the authors’ responses and clarifications. However, my evaluation score remains unchanged.
