This paper investigates the convergence rate of a min-sum splitting method applied to the average consensus problem. While the paper is generally well-written, the presented improvement over existing results appears incremental. Specific comments are detailed below.

(1) The claim that the achieved rate is optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, specifically citing the complete graph as an example, is questionable. The convergence rate for complete graphs is 0, as consensus is reached in a single iteration. Furthermore, complete graphs represent a highly specific case and may not be representative of broader network topologies. Clarification is needed regarding the general classes of graphs for which the complexity is independent of the problem dimension (number of nodes), and a more precise definition of what constitutes a “good” graph is required.

(2) The literature review in the relevant section should include the work “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control” by Olshevsky. A proper reporting of its convergence rate is necessary, as the bounds achieved in that work are competitive with, or even surpass, those presented in this submission (further details in comment 8).

(3) When discussing consensus optimization, key references such as “On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization” by Shi, Ling, Kun, Wu, and Yin, and “Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks” by Scaman, Bach, Bubeck, Lee, and Massoulié should be included. The authors should also provide a comprehensive overview of state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization and their corresponding linear convergence rates.

(4) The discussion of lifted graphs and Markov chains omits a closely related paper: “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM” by Franca and Bento.

(5) The statement in the last paragraph of page 5 represents a well-established result, and should be attributed to “Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links” by Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew. The subsequent connection to Heavy ball, Nesterov, and Polyak methods is also known within the field.

(6) The authors have overlooked several important references in the area of consensus optimization, including “Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization” by Shi, Ling, Wu, and Yin, and “Fast distributed gradient methods” by Jakovetic, Moura, and Xavier.

(7) Proposition 3 appears to be a trivial result and represents a minor contribution.

(8) The achieved convergence rate of D log(D/eps) does not represent a substantial improvement over the D log(1/eps) rate obtained in “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control” (see comment 2). In the worst-case scenario, where D ~ n, the proposed bound is even less favorable.

(9) The work “Linear Time Average Consensus…” leverages Nesterov’s acceleration to improve its bound. The reviewer suspects that the proposed “Auxiliary message-passing scheme” is, in essence, an application of Nesterov’s acceleration to the min-sum algorithm. While this is not inherently problematic, the analysis is performed for consensus, which reduces to analyzing a linear system and should not be overly challenging. Consequently, the paper’s contribution is unclear.

(10) Any potential improvement may stem from a careful analysis of the graph’s spectral gap. However, the worst-case bound remains O(n) since O(n) = O(D) for the set of all graphs with n nodes.

(11) Line 243 of page 6 depicts a simple graph but utilizes directed edges, which is potentially confusing.

(12) A typographical error exists at line 220 of page 6: “Laplacian” should be “Lagrangian”.

After rebuttal:
The reviewer is satisfied with the authors’ response. However, the evaluation score remains unchanged.
