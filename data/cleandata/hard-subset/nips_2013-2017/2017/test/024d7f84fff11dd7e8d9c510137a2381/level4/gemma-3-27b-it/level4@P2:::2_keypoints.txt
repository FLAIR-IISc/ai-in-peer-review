- Paper studies a modified min-sum splitting method for average consensus and provides theoretical convergence analysis.
- Technically sound and well-written, but improvement over existing methods is incremental.
- Claim about optimal convergence on complete graphs is misleading; complete graphs reach consensus in one step.
- Authors should clarify which graph classes have complexity independent of node count and define “good” graphs.
- Literature review is incomplete—omits Olshevsky’s Linear Time Average Consensus, weakening novelty claims.
- Missing important references: Shi et al. (ADMM linear convergence), Scaman et al. (optimal distributed algorithms), Franca & Bento (Markov chain lifting & ADMM), Rabbatt et al., Jakovetic et al., and Shi et al. (Extra).
- Proposition 3 appears trivial and adds limited value.
- Achieved rate D·log(D/ε) is not a substantive improvement over Olshevsky’s D·log(1/ε) and can be worse when D ~ n.
- The “auxiliary message-passing” likely corresponds to Nesterov acceleration applied to min-sum, reducing perceived novelty.
- Analysis focuses on consensus (a linear system), which simplifies the problem and limits the contribution’s distinctiveness.
- Improvement seems driven mainly by handling the graph spectral gap; worst-case bound remains O(n).
- Notational/clarity issues: directed edges on a simple graph are confusing; typo “Laplacian” should be “Lagrangian.”
- Arguments for acceptance: sound analysis, modification ensures convergence in cases where original fails, formal rate established, insightful connections to lifting and accelerated methods.
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental benefit, incomplete literature review (notably Olshevsky), limited generalization beyond consensus, core idea resembles re-implementation of Nesterov acceleration.
- Overall: solid, correct work but lacks the significant novelty or impact expected for a top-tier conference.
