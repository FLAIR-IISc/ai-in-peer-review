### Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

**Summary**

This paper investigates the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for solving the consensus problem in distributed optimization. The authors show that while the standard Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge on graphs with cycles, a modified version known as Min-Sum Splitting, first proposed in [34], can be tuned to provably converge to the correct solution. The core of the paper is a theoretical analysis establishing the convergence rates for this algorithm in the context of network averaging (a consensus problem with a quadratic objective). The main analytical tool is a novel proof technique that involves constructing an auxiliary linear process on the graph's nodes to track the evolution of the message-passing dynamics, which occur on the directed edges. By appropriately setting the splitting parameters, the authors demonstrate that the algorithm achieves an accelerated, subdiffusive convergence rate, matching the performance of established shift-register methods and offering a square-root improvement over standard diffusive methods. A key conceptual contribution is the connection drawn between the acceleration mechanism in Min-Sum Splitting and those in two other classes of algorithms: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Theoretical Contribution:** This paper provides the first convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. The development of an auxiliary message-passing scheme to facilitate this analysis is a clever and novel proof technique that could potentially be applied to analyze other message-passing algorithms.

2.  **Significant Results:** The paper demonstrates that with proper tuning, Min-Sum Splitting can achieve accelerated convergence rates for the consensus problem. The ability to match the rates of shift-register methods establishes this message-passing approach as a competitive alternative to other well-known accelerated distributed optimization algorithms.

3.  **Conceptual Insight:** The paper does an excellent job of connecting three seemingly disparate lines of research: Min-Sum message passing, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated multi-step optimization methods (e.g., heavy ball, Nesterov). Highlighting that the directionality introduced by the splitting parameters serves a similar function to momentum or non-reversible state transitions is a valuable insight for the community.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection)**

1.  **Limited Scope and Practicality:** The analysis is confined to the network averaging problem, which involves a simple quadratic objective. The paper lacks a discussion on whether these theoretical results can be extended to more general convex functions, which are common in machine learning applications (e.g., distributed logistic regression or SVMs). Without this, the practical impact for the NIPS audience remains unclear. The authors should comment on the limitations of the method and the challenges in extending it.

2.  **Lack of Intuition and Justification for ML Community:** While the theoretical results are strong, the paper would benefit from a deeper discussion of the intuition behind the method. For example, what is the intuition behind using the auxiliary process? Furthermore, the paper does not explicitly state why this particular approach to consensus is important for the machine learning community, beyond the general use of consensus in distributed learning. A more direct motivation would strengthen the paper's significance.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation Issues:** The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity is hampered by several issues.
    *   The algorithms are not self-contained. Key variables are introduced without clear definitions or initialization procedures. For example, in Algorithm 2, the initial terms `R̂0` and `r̂0` are used but not initialized, and the meaning of `R{vw}` and `r{vw}` is not explicitly defined in the text.
    *   In Algorithm 3, which is central to the analysis, it is not immediately clear which variables correspond to the auxiliary process. Explicitly labeling these would improve readability.
    *   There are several typographical and grammatical errors throughout the manuscript (a detailed list is provided below).

4.  **Incomplete Literature Review:** The paper is missing several key references in the area of distributed optimization and accelerated methods. This gives the impression that the work is not fully situated within its broader context. Relevant missing works include foundational texts on distributed optimization, as well as seminal papers on momentum and acceleration.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a novel contribution to the analysis of message-passing algorithms. The connection established between Min-Sum Splitting and other accelerated methods is insightful and significant. The proofs appear sound, and the results demonstrate a competitive convergence rate for the consensus problem.

However, the paper's weaknesses, particularly its limited scope to quadratic objectives and its lack of discussion on practical relevance for the broader machine learning community, temper its overall impact. The clarity of the algorithm presentations also needs improvement.

The paper presents interesting and novel ideas. If the authors can address the clarity issues and add a more thorough discussion on the limitations, potential for generalization, and relevance to machine learning problems, it would be a solid contribution to the conference.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

It would be beneficial if the authors could address the following points in a revised version:

*   **Practicality:** Under what conditions would this Min-Sum Splitting method be preferable to other distributed methods like ADMM or accelerated (dual) gradient descent for solving consensus optimization problems?
*   **Generalization:** What are the primary obstacles to extending this analysis beyond quadratic objective functions to more general classes of convex functions? Does the linearity of the message updates (Proposition 1) break down?
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The optimal choice of the parameter `γ` depends on `ρW`, the spectral gap of the matrix `W`. This is global information. How does this requirement affect the "distributed" nature of the algorithm? Do you have suggestions for how this parameter might be estimated in a distributed fashion?
