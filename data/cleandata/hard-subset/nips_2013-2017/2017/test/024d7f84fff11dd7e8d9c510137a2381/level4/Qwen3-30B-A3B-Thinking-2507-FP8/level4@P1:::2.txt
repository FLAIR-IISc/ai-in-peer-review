

This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving the consensus problem in distributed optimization, claiming to achieve accelerated convergence rates compared to standard diffusive methods. The authors demonstrate that a properly tuned Min-Sum Splitting protocol yields subdiffusive convergence rates, matching those of shift-register methods. The paper establishes connections between Min-Sum Splitting and two established acceleration techniques: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization.

The paper makes several technical contributions: (1) it proves Min-Sum Splitting converges for the consensus problem where ordinary Min-Sum fails, (2) it establishes convergence rates for arbitrary graph topologies, and (3) it reveals connections to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods. The authors show that proper tuning of splitting parameters yields convergence time O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle and toroidal graphs, where D is the graph diameter.

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, which fills a gap in the literature. The proof technique using an auxiliary message-passing scheme to track the algorithm's evolution on nodes rather than edges is novel and well-executed. The connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods provide valuable theoretical insights.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that limit its contribution. First, the claimed improvement over state-of-the-art methods appears marginal. The authors claim convergence time O(D log(D/ε)), but this is not a substantial improvement over O(D log(1/ε)) achieved by Olshevsky's "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs" (which the paper fails to cite). When D ~ n (the worst case for all graphs), the authors' bound becomes O(n log n), while Olshevsky achieves O(n) convergence time. The authors should clarify why their result is considered an improvement when it's asymptotically weaker in the worst case.

Second, the paper omits several critical references that directly address the same problem. Key works that should be cited include:
- Olshevsky's "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs" (which achieves optimal O(D log(1/ε)) rate)
- Shi et al.'s "On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization"
- Scaman et al.'s "Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization"
- França and Bento's "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM"
- Shi et al.'s "Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization"

Third, the paper's claim of novelty appears overstated. The "Auxiliary message-passing scheme" bears striking similarity to Nesterov's acceleration, and the authors fail to properly acknowledge this connection. In fact, the paper's approach seems to be a specific application of Nesterov's acceleration to the Min-Sum algorithm, which is not novel in the broader context of distributed optimization.

Fourth, the literature review contains inaccuracies. For instance, the paper claims that diffusive methods achieve "convergence time that scales independently of the dimension of the problem, as Θ(log 1/ε)" for graphs with good expansion properties. This is misleading—complete graphs achieve convergence in one step (not Θ(log 1/ε)), and "good expansion properties" typically refers to graphs with constant spectral gap, not graphs where convergence time is independent of problem size.

The paper also contains minor technical issues:
- On page 5, the connection between Min-Sum variants and Heavy Ball/Nesterov/Polyak methods is presented as novel, but this is well-known in the literature (see Rabbat et al.'s "Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links").
- Line 220 contains a typo: "Laplacian" should be "Lagrangian."
- The use of directed edges for a simple graph (page 6) creates confusion.

The paper's significance is limited by the marginal nature of its contribution compared to existing work. While the theoretical analysis is sound, the practical implications are unclear since the claimed improvement is only meaningful for specific graph topologies where D is small relative to n (not the worst case). The authors should clarify why their approach is preferable to existing methods that achieve better worst-case bounds.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound and the proofs appear correct, but the novelty claim is overstated. The clarity is generally good, with clear mathematical presentation, though some sections could benefit from more precise wording. The originality is questionable given the connections to established acceleration techniques and the omission of key references.

In summary, the paper makes a modest technical contribution to the analysis of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus problems, but the claimed improvement over state-of-the-art methods is not compelling. The connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods are interesting but not sufficiently novel to warrant publication at NIPS. The paper would benefit significantly from a more accurate assessment of related work and a clearer statement of how the results improve upon existing methods, particularly Olshevsky's work.

The main contribution appears to be the auxiliary process technique for analyzing Min-Sum Splitting, but this alone does not justify the paper's claims of significant improvement. The authors should reconsider their novelty claim in light of the existing literature.
