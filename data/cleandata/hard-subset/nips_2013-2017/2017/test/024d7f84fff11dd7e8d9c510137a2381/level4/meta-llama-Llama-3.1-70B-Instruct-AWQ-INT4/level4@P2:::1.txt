This paper applies an accelerated variant of the min-sum algorithm, called min-sum splitting, to the distributed consensus problem. The authors provide a thorough review of the existing literature on the topic, clearly placing their contribution in the context of the state of the art. The paper is well-written, and to the best of my knowledge, the results are novel and constitute a qualitative advance.

The paper's main contribution is the application of the min-sum splitting algorithm to the consensus problem, which can be formulated as an equality-constrained problem in optimization. The authors establish rates of convergence for the min-sum splitting algorithm and show that it achieves subdiffusive accelerated convergence rates, matching the rates obtained by shift-register methods.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to connect min-sum algorithms with lifted Markov chain techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization. The authors provide a clear explanation of how the min-sum splitting algorithm can be seen as a process on a lifted space, and how the introduction of splitting parameters induces a directionality in the message exchange that is analogous to the directionality introduced in non-reversible random walks on lifted graphs.

The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the convergence behavior of the min-sum splitting algorithm, including a proof of the asymptotic rate of convergence. The authors show that the choice of splitting parameters that yields subdiffusive rates depends on global information about the graph, which is a common theme in accelerated methods for consensus.

One minor point of clarification is needed in the presentation. On page 4, the authors describe the equivalent objective function that is minimized by the min-sum algorithm to yield the min-sum splitting scheme. However, the explanation of how the terms are split is not entirely clear, particularly with regards to the real numbers $\delta$ and $\Gamma_{vw}$.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The paper is clearly written, and the authors provide enough information for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel connection between min-sum algorithms and lifted Markov chain techniques, which is a significant contribution to the field.

In terms of significance, the paper's results have the potential to impact the field of distributed optimization and consensus, particularly in applications where fast convergence rates are critical.

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper for publication.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel connection between min-sum algorithms and lifted Markov chain techniques.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the convergence behavior of the min-sum splitting algorithm.
* The paper's results have the potential to impact the field of distributed optimization and consensus.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* Minor point of clarification needed in the presentation.
