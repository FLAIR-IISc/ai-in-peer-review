Summary
The paper analyzes the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for the average consensus problem. The authors show: (i) ordinary Min-Sum may not converge on graphs with cycles, (ii) a Splitting reparameterization ensures convergence, and (iii) with an appropriate choice of parameters (δ, Γ), Min-Sum Splitting achieves accelerated, subdiffusive convergence rates that match those of shift-register (multi-step) methods. A key technical device is an auxiliary, node-based linear process with memory that tracks the edge-based message passing, allowing the rate to be tied to the spectral gap of a simpler matrix. The paper also highlights conceptual links between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted non-reversible Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods (heavy-ball/Nesterov).

Strengths
- Clear formulation of Min-Sum Splitting and a careful specialization to quadratic consensus.
- Useful auxiliary node-based linear system that makes the analysis tractable and connects the edge process to multi-step methods.
- Provable square-root speedup over diffusive consensus on graphs with geometry, yielding O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori.
- Conceptual connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization are interesting and timely.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
1) Claims around “good graphs” and dimension-independence. The paper states that for “graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph,” the convergence time scales as Θ(log 1/ε) and is independent of problem dimension. On a complete graph the iteration can converge in a single step if W = 11T/n is used, so this example is not representative of the general phenomenon. Please clarify:
- What general class of graphs do you mean by “good” (e.g., expanders)? Provide a precise definition and typical bounds on 1 − ρ(W).
- For which classes does complexity provably not depend on n? Please be explicit.

2) Literature on linear-time average consensus. The discussion of dimension-independence should cite and compare with “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control” (Olshevsky). Its guarantees should be reported accurately (see also comment 8). That work achieves rates that are competitive with, and in some regimes better than, those reported here.

3) Consensus optimization literature. The paper should cite and position itself relative to:
- Shi, Ling, Yuan, Wu, Yin, “On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization.”
- Scaman, Bach, Bubeck, Lee, Massoulié, “Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks.”
Please summarize state-of-the-art rates for consensus optimization (not just averaging), including known linear rates and the dependence on condition numbers and network spectra.

4) Lifting and ADMM. The discussion of lifted graphs and non-reversible Markov chains should include “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM” (França and Bento), which is closely related to your perspective.

5) Multi-step/acceleration connections are known. The observation in the last paragraph of page 5 is well known. Please cite “Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links” (Rabbat, Nowak, Bucklew) and related work that connect consensus variants to heavy-ball/Nesterov/Polyak-style acceleration.

6) Additional missing references. Please include:
- “EXTRA: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization” (Shi, Ling, Wu, Yin).
- “Fast distributed gradient methods” (Jakovetić, Xavier, Moura).
These are central to the consensus optimization literature.

7) Proposition 3. This proposition appears trivial and seems like a supplementary observation rather than a core contribution.

8) Rate comparison and significance. Your rate O(D log(D/ε)) is only a logarithmic improvement over diffusive rates and is weaker than O(D log(1/ε)) achieved by Olshevsky (“Linear Time Average Consensus…”). In the worst case over all graphs with n nodes, D = Θ(n), so your bound becomes O(n log(n/ε)), which is worse by a log n factor than O(n log(1/ε)). The paper should clearly position its contribution against this prior result and explain where, if anywhere, it provides a tangible advantage.

9) Novelty of the auxiliary scheme. The auxiliary node-based two-step recursion appears to be a standard multi-step acceleration (akin to Nesterov/shift-register) applied to the linear consensus dynamics induced by Min-Sum Splitting. Since the problem is quadratic and the induced dynamics are linear, the analysis is not particularly hard. Please clarify the conceptual novelty beyond rederiving known accelerated rates via a Min-Sum formulation, and delineate what new insight this brings for problems beyond consensus.

10) Worst-case dependence. Any improvement here seems to rely on a sharper use of spectral information. However, the worst-case dependence remains O(D), which for general graphs is O(n). Please discuss this limitation explicitly.

11) Notational clarity. You assume a simple (undirected) graph but index messages on directed edges. This is standard in message passing but initially confusing; please state this explicitly when introducing E and explain the rationale.

12) Minor: Typo on page 6, line 220: “Laplacian” should be “Lagrangian.”

Pros for acceptance
- Clean analysis that ties Min-Sum Splitting to a well-understood accelerated linear system.
- Clear, constructive connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization.
- Generality across graph topologies and a square-root improvement over diffusive methods on geometric graphs.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental improvement relative to known O(D log(1/ε)) results (and worse by a log D factor).
- Several important related works are omitted or under-discussed, weakening the positioning.
- The main technique reduces to analyzing a linear, two-step recursion; the conceptual advance beyond known accelerations is limited.
- Some clarity and exposition issues.

Recommendation
Borderline. The analysis is correct and the perspective through Min-Sum Splitting is interesting, but the practical/significant improvement over the best-known rates is limited, and the literature review needs to be strengthened.

After rebuttal
