This research paper examines the convergence rate of the so-called least-sum decomposition method on the average consensus problem. Generally, this paper seems fine, but the improvement in results is not impressive. The detailed comments are as follows:

(1) It states that "this rate is optimal for graphs with good span properties, such as complete graphs. In this case, the convergence time, i.e., the number of iterations required to reach a specified level of error accuracy in the problem's dimension..."

For complete graphs, the linear rate is 0 because everyone converges to the average in 1 step. Also, complete graphs are too specific to be representative.

So, for what general category of graphs does the complexity not depend on the dimension (number of nodes)? What general category of graphs is considered good?

(2) In this paragraph (similar to comment 1), the literature review should include "Implications for linear time average consensus and decentralized optimization and multi-agent control on static graphs" by Olshchevsky. Its convergence rate should be properly stated (more clarification will be given in comment 8). The reference mentioned here has reached a significantly competitive or better range compared to the presented results.

(3) At the top of page 2, for consensus optimization, important references such as "Linear convergence of ADMM in decentralized consensus optimization" by Xie, Ling, Kun, Wu, and Yin, and "Optimal algorithm for smooth and robustly convex distributed optimization in networks" by Scammann, Bach, Bubek, Li, and Masovei should be cited. Also, the authors should report on state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization and their respective (linear) convergence rates.

(4) While discussing lifted graphs and Markov chains, this paper has overlooked a very relevant paper titled "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM" by Franka and Bento.

(5) The content of the last paragraph on page 5 is a long-known fact. "Generalized consensus computation in network systems with eraser links" by Rabbat, Novak, and Buckley should be mentioned. Subsequently, the relationship between those variants and the heavy ball/Nesterov/Polyak is known in the field.

(6) There are several important references regarding consensus optimization that the authors have overlooked. For example, "Additional: A precise first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization" by Xie, Ling, Wu, and Yin. "Fast distributed gradient methods" by Jacovetic, J. Xavier, and Moura.

(7) Proposition 3 seems trivial and is a complementary contribution.

(8) The rate obtained by this paper, D log(D/eps), does not show a significant improvement over the rate D log(1/eps) obtained on linear time average consensus and implications for static graphs and decentralized optimization and multi-agent control (see comment 2). Especially in the worst-case scenario (valid for all graphs), D~n is even worse than the range obtained in the limit "linear time average consensus..."

(9) The paper "Linear time average consensus..." improves the limit through Nesterov's acceleration. The reviewer suspects that the so-called " सहायक message-passing scheme" proposed by the authors is again Nesterov's acceleration applied to the least-sum algorithm. This is fine, but the analysis is done for consensus, which boils down to analyzing a linear system and should not be considered difficult. In such a situation, the paper's contribution is not clear.

(10) A slight improvement may come from carefully paying attention to the spectral gap of the graph. Ultimately, the worst-case limit is still O(n) because O(n)=O(D) for all sets of graphs with n nodes.

(11) Line 243 on page 6. The graph is simple, but the author is using directed edges. This is misleading.

(12) Typo on line 220 on page 6. Laplacian—> Lagrangian.
