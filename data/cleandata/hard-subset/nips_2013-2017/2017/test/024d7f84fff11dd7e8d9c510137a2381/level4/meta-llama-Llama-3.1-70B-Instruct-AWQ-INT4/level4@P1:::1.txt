This paper applies an accelerated variant of the min-sum algorithm, called min-sum splitting, to the distributed consensus problem. The paper is well-written, with the contribution clearly placed in the context of the state of the art in the topic. To the best of my knowledge, the results are novel and constitute a qualitative advance. In particular, the paper presents a novel connection between min-sum algorithms and lifted Markov chain techniques.

The authors provide a thorough review of the existing literature on distributed consensus algorithms, including classical diffusive methods and accelerated approaches such as lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods. They clearly explain how their proposed min-sum splitting algorithm relates to these existing approaches and highlight its advantages.

The technical presentation is clear and well-organized, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and understand the underlying mathematics. The paper includes a detailed analysis of the convergence rates of the proposed algorithm, which is a significant contribution to the field.

One minor point that is not entirely clear in the presentation is the explanation of the equivalent objective function that is minimized by the min-sum algorithm to yield the min-sum splitting scheme. Specifically, on page 4, the authors write: "...splitting each term φvw into Γvw terms, and each term φv into δ terms,..." However, it is not immediately clear what this means, as δ and Γvw are introduced as real numbers on the previous page. A brief clarification or rephrasing of this sentence might help to avoid confusion.

Overall, I believe that this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed consensus algorithms and is a strong candidate for publication.
