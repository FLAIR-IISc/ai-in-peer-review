The authors present an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for solving consensus problems in distributed optimization. They utilize the reparametrization techniques proposed in [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013] and establish rates of convergence for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for solving consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. The main tool employed for the analysis is the construction of an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

The primary contributions of the paper can be summarized as follows: (i) providing analysis for the Min-Sum splitting algorithm using a novel proof technique based on the introduction of an auxiliary process, (ii) designing a Min-Sum protocol for consensus problems that achieves better convergence than previously established results, and (iii) demonstrating the connection between the proposed method and lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The motivation and contributions of the paper are clear. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, although it contains several typos and grammatical mistakes. The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appear to be correct.

There are several minor errors and suggestions for improvement. Specifically, the following corrections should be made: Line 34: "…with theirs neighbors…" -> "…with their neighbors…"; Line 174: "double-stochastic" -> "doubly-stochastic"; Line 183: "… can be casted as…" -> "… can be cast as…"; Line 192: "…class of graph with…" -> "…class of graphs with…"; Line 197: "…which seems to…" -> "…which seem to…"; Line 206: "…additional overheads…" -> "…additional overhead…"; Line 225: "…pugging…" -> "…plugging…"; Line 238: "…are seen to…" -> "…are able to…"; Line 240: "…both type of…" -> "…both types of…"; Line 248: "…also seen to…" -> "…also shown to…"; Line 279-280: "…to convergence to…" -> "…to converge to…"; Line 300: "…,which scales like…" -> "…,which scale like…"; Line 302: "…for the cycle,…" -> "…for cycle graphs,…".

Additionally, the authors should consider the following minor comments. In Lines 220 and 221, it appears that "Lagrangian" and "Lagrange multipliers" are meant instead of "Laplacian" and "Laplace multipliers." Furthermore, the authors present three algorithms, but the quantities involved are not always explained or described. For example, the variables R{vw} and r{vw} in Algorithm 2 are not defined. Similarly, the quantities \hat{R}^0 and \hat{r}^0 in Algorithm 2 do not appear to be initialized. Since the auxiliary linear process is a key component of the analysis and central idea of the paper, the authors should clearly state which variables correspond to this in Algorithm 3.

The paper is also missing several relevant references. Specifically, the authors should consider including the following references: (Sub)gradient methods for consensus optimization (Bertsekas and Tsitsiklis, Parallel and distributed computation: numerical methods, 1989; Sundhar Ram Srinivasan et. al., Incremental stochastic subgradient algorithms for convex optimization, 2009; Wei Shi, Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization, 2015). Additionally, the authors should provide more specific references for Lines 170 ("The original literature…"), 229 (work by Polyak, Heavy-ball), and 232 (work by Nesterov).

It would be beneficial for the authors to address the following questions and comments in the paper. Although the paper is theoretical in nature, the authors should discuss the practicality of the method and when it should be used in comparison to other distributed methods for consensus optimization. The authors should also elaborate on the limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method. Furthermore, it would be helpful to provide intuition behind using the auxiliary process in the Min-Sum Splitting method. The results provided in this paper are limited to consensus problems with quadratic objective functions, and it would be interesting to explore whether this framework can be extended to solve more general consensus problems that often arise in Machine Learning. Finally, the authors should clearly state why this approach is of interest in the context of Machine Learning and for the Machine Learning community.

In summary, this paper presents a purely theoretical contribution, establishing rates of convergence using a novel proof technique and demonstrating connections between the proposed method and well-established methods in the literature. While the ideas presented in this paper are interesting, the practicality of the method, intuition behind the results, and justification for the importance of this result for the Machine Learning community are not fully addressed.
