The authors present an accelerated variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol for solving consensus problems in distributed optimization. This work leverages reparametrization techniques proposed in [Ruozzi and Tatikonda, 2013] and establishes rates of convergence for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm in the context of quadratic objective functions. A key analytical tool employed in this paper is the construction of an auxiliary linear process that tracks the evolution of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

The primary contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows: (i) providing analysis for the Min-Sum splitting algorithm using a novel proof technique based on the introduction of an auxiliary process, (ii) designing a Min-Sum protocol for consensus problems that achieves improved convergence rates compared to previously established results, and (iii) highlighting the connection between the proposed method, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step methods in convex optimization.

The paper's motivation and contributions are clearly articulated. The writing is generally clear and easy to follow, although it contains several typos and grammatical errors, which are listed below. The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, and Theorem 1 appear to be correct.

- The paper presents three algorithms, but the quantities involved are not always explained or described. For instance, the variables R{vw} and r{vw} in Algorithm 2 are not defined. To further improve the paper, it would be beneficial for the authors to address the following points:

- Although the paper is theoretical in nature, the authors should comment on the practicality of the method and its potential applications compared to other distributed methods for consensus optimization.
- The authors should discuss the limitations of the Min-Sum Splitting method.
- The authors should provide intuition behind the use of the auxiliary process in the Min-Sum Splitting method.
- The results presented in this paper are limited to consensus problems with quadratic objective functions. The authors should explore the possibility of extending this framework to solve more general consensus problems that often arise in Machine Learning.
- The authors should clearly state why this approach is of interest in the context of Machine Learning and for the Machine Learning community.

In summary, this paper presents a theoretical contribution to the field of distributed optimization, establishing rates of convergence using a novel proof technique and highlighting connections to well-established methods in the literature. However, the paper could benefit from additional discussions on the practicality of the method, intuition behind the results, and justification for its importance to the Machine Learning community.
