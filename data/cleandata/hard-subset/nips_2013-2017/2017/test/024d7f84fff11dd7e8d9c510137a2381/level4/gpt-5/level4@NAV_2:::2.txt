This paper analyzes the convergence rate of a Min-Sum Splitting scheme for the average consensus problem. The manuscript is generally clear, but the performance gains appear modest. Detailed comments follow.

(1) The paper states that the “diffusive” rate is optimal for graphs with good expansion (e.g., complete graphs), and that the convergence time can be independent of the problem dimension. On complete graphs, however, consensus is achieved in a single step (effectively zero contraction factor), and such graphs are too special to be representative. Please clarify which broad graph families exhibit dimension-independent complexity and provide a precise definition of what is meant by “good” expansion in this context.

(2) In the discussion around these optimal rates (same passage as in comment 1), the literature review should include “Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control” (Olshevsky). Its convergence guarantees should be accurately reported (see also comment 8). That work attains bounds that are competitive with, and in some regimes better than, those reported here.

(3) Near the top of page 2, for consensus optimization, key references are missing, including:
- “On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization” (Shi, Ling, Yuan, Wu, Yin).
- “Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks” (Scaman, Bach, Bubeck, Lee, Massoulié).
The paper should also summarize state-of-the-art methods for decentralized consensus optimization and their (linear) convergence rates.

(4) In the lifted-graph/Markov chain discussion, the related work “Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM” (França and Bento) should be cited.

(5) The content in the last paragraph of page 5 is well known; please cite “Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links” (Rabbat, Nowak, Bucklew). The connections to Heavy Ball/Nesterov/Polyak-type accelerations are also established in the literature and should be acknowledged.

(6) Several important references on consensus optimization are omitted, e.g.:
- “EXTRA: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization” (Shi, Ling, Wu, Yin).
- “Fast distributed gradient methods” (Jakovetić, Xavier, Moura).

(7) Proposition 3 appears straightforward and more in the nature of a supplementary observation than a core contribution.

(8) The obtained rate O(D log(D/ε)) does not represent a substantial improvement over O(D log(1/ε)) from “Linear Time Average Consensus…”. Moreover, in worst-case families where D ≍ n, the bound here is actually weaker than what that work achieves.

(9) “Linear Time Average Consensus…” improves rates via Nesterov-style acceleration. The proposed “auxiliary message-passing scheme” seems to amount to applying Nesterov-type acceleration to Min-Sum. This is acceptable, but since the analysis for consensus reduces to studying a linear system, the technical difficulty is limited. In that light, the conceptual contribution relative to existing accelerated methods is unclear.

(10) The slight improvement appears to hinge on a careful use of spectral gap properties. However, the worst-case bound remains O(n), since O(n) = O(D) across all n-node graphs.

(11) Line 243 on page 6: the graph is declared simple, yet directed edges are used in the formulation. This is potentially confusing and should be clarified.

(12) Typo on line 220, page 6: “Laplacian” should read “Lagrangian.”

After rebuttal:
The reviewer appreciates the authors’ clarifications. However, the overall evaluation score remains unchanged.
