This paper studies the convergence rate of a so-called min-sum splitting method on the average consensus problem. In general, the paper reads fine, but the improvement of the result seems not impressive. Detailed comments are as follows.

(1) It writes that "This rate is optimal for graphs with good expansion properties, such as the complete graph. In this case, the convergence time, i.e., the number of iterations required to reach a prescribed level of error accuracy in the… of the dimension of the problem, as…". For complete graphs, the linear rate is 0 because everyone converges to the average in 1 step. Also, complete graphs are too special to be representative. So, for which general category of graphs does the complexity not depend on the dimension (number of nodes)? Which general category of graphs is considered as good?

The authors should clarify this point and provide more specific information about the types of graphs for which the convergence time does not depend on the dimension. This will help to better understand the significance of their results.

(2) In this paragraph (same as comment 1), the literature review should include "Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control" by Olshevsky. Its convergence rate should be reported properly (more explanation will be given in comment 8). The reference mentioned here has reached a rather competitive or even better bound compared to the result of the submission.

The authors should include this reference in their literature review and provide a detailed comparison of their results with those obtained by Olshevsky.

(3) At the top of page 2, for consensus optimization, important references like "On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization" by Shi, Ling, Kun, Wu, and Yin, and "Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks" by Scaman, Bach, Bubeck, Lee, Massoulié should be cited. Also, the authors should report the state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization and their corresponding (linear) convergence rates.

The authors should include these references in their literature review and provide a detailed overview of the state-of-the-art algorithms for consensus optimization.

(4) When discussing lifted graph and Markov chain, this paper ignored a very related paper "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM" by Franca and Bento.

The authors should include this reference in their literature review and discuss its relevance to their work.

(5) The content of the last paragraph of page 5 is a long-known fact. Should refer to "Generalized consensus computation in networked systems with erasure links" by Rabbat, Nowak, and Bucklew. In the sequel, the connection between those variants and Heavy ball/Nesterov/Polyak is known to the field.

The authors should include this reference in their literature review and acknowledge the known connections between the variants and Heavy ball/Nesterov/Polyak methods.

(6) There are many important references regarding consensus optimization that the authors have ignored. For example, "Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization" by Shi, Ling, Wu, and Yin, and "Fast distributed gradient methods" by Jakovetic, Xavier, and Moura.

The authors should include these references in their literature review and discuss their relevance to their work.

(7) Proposition 3 seems to be trivial and is a supplementary contribution.

The authors should consider removing or revising Proposition 3 to make it more substantial and relevant to the main contributions of the paper.

(8) The rate reached by this paper, D log(D/eps), does not seem to have a significant improvement on the rate D log(1/eps) that has been reached by Linear Time Average Consensus on Fixed Graphs and Implications for Decentralized Optimization and Multi-Agent Control (see comment 2). Especially in the worst-case scenario (holds for all graphs), D~n, the bound is even worse than that achieved in "Linear Time Average Consensus…".

The authors should provide a more detailed comparison of their results with those obtained by Olshevsky and discuss the significance of their improvements.

(9) The paper "Linear Time Average Consensus…" improves the bound through Nesterov's acceleration. The reviewer suspects that the so-called "Auxiliary message-passing scheme" proposed by the authors is again a Nesterov's acceleration applied to the min-sum algorithm. This is fine, but the analysis is done for consensus, which boils down to analyzing a linear system and is supposed to be not hard. The contribution of the paper becomes not clear given such situation.

The authors should clarify the relationship between their auxiliary message-passing scheme and Nesterov's acceleration and discuss the significance of their contributions in the context of consensus optimization.

(10) The tiny improvement may come from a careful handle on the spectral gap of graphs. Eventually, the worst-case bound is still O(n) because O(n)=O(D) for the set of all graphs with n nodes.

The authors should discuss the limitations of their results and the potential for further improvements.

(11) Line 243 of page 6. The graph is simple, but the author is using directed edges. This is confusing.

The authors should clarify the notation and terminology used in the paper to avoid confusion.

(12) Typo at line 220 of page 6. Laplacian—> Lagrangian.

The authors should correct the typo and ensure that the paper is free of errors.
