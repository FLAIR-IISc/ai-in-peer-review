This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting message-passing algorithm for the distributed average consensus problem. The authors demonstrate that while the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge on graphs with cycles, the "Splitting" variant, with appropriately chosen parameters, can be proven to converge. The main contribution is the analysis of this algorithm's convergence rate. By introducing an auxiliary message-passing scheme that tracks the algorithm's evolution on the graph's nodes (rather than its directed edges), the authors show that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve an accelerated, "subdiffusive" convergence rate. They establish a connection between this acceleration mechanism and those found in lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order optimization methods, showing that their method recovers the dynamics of shift-register methods and achieves a convergence time of O(D log(D/ε)) for certain graph families, where D is the graph diameter.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Connection:** The paper draws an interesting and explicit connection between Min-Sum message-passing schemes and two other established families of acceleration techniques: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods. Highlighting that parameter tuning in Min-Sum Splitting can emulate the momentum-like behavior of accelerated gradient methods is a valuable conceptual contribution.

2.  **Analytical Technique:** The introduction of the auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) is an elegant technique. It successfully maps the complex dynamics on the 2m directed edges to a more tractable 2n-dimensional linear system on the nodes, which greatly simplifies the convergence analysis by connecting it to the spectral properties of a smaller, node-based matrix.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The authors clearly state their contributions and methodically build their argument from the general algorithm to the specific application, analysis, and final rate derivation.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Significance of the Convergence Rate:** The primary weakness is the significance of the final result. The paper establishes an asymptotic convergence time of O(D log(D/ε)), which is compared to the O(D² log(1/ε)) of standard diffusive methods. However, it fails to compare this result adequately with existing accelerated consensus literature. Notably, Olshevsky (Ref [30]) presented a Nesterov-accelerated method with a convergence time of O(D log(1/ε)). The authors' rate of O(D log(D/ε)) introduces a log(D) factor that makes the improvement over [30] unclear and potentially worse, especially in the common worst-case scenario where D is O(n). A more thorough discussion and comparison is needed to position the significance of this result accurately.

2.  **Insufficient Literature Review:** The paper overlooks several highly relevant and recent works in distributed and consensus optimization. This omission makes it difficult to assess the originality and state-of-the-art performance of the proposed method. Important missing references include:
    *   Optimal first-order methods for consensus, such as in "Optimal algorithms for smooth and strongly convex distributed optimization in networks" by Scaman et al. (2017).
    *   State-of-the-art ADMM-based methods and their linear convergence analysis, such as in "On the Linear Convergence of the ADMM in Decentralized Consensus Optimization" by Shi et al. (2016).
    *   Other important first-order methods like EXTRA ("Extra: An exact first-order algorithm for decentralized consensus optimization" by Shi et al., 2015) and fast distributed gradient methods ("Fast distributed gradient methods" by Jakovetić et al., 2014).
    *   The connection between message-passing, ADMM, and lifting is explored in "Markov Chain Lifting and Distributed ADMM" by França and Bento (2016), which seems highly relevant to the paper's claimed contributions.

3.  **Novelty of the Proposed Scheme:** The authors show that their auxiliary scheme, with a specific parameter choice, recovers the update matrix of shift-register methods. While the link itself is novel, this implies that the resulting algorithm is not entirely new but rather a re-derivation of a known accelerated method from a message-passing perspective. The contribution seems to be more about providing a new interpretation for Min-Sum Splitting rather than proposing a fundamentally new, faster algorithm for consensus. The analysis, being for a linear system, follows from well-established techniques for accelerated methods.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

*   Could you please elaborate on the comparison between your O(D log(D/ε)) rate and the O(D log(1/ε)) rate in Olshevsky [30]? Is there a regime where your bound is superior? The additional log(D) factor appears to be a significant drawback.

*   In Section 1, you state that for graphs with good expansion properties, the convergence time of diffusive methods "scales independently of the dimension of the problem." This statement could be more precise. For a complete graph, convergence is achieved in one step. Could you clarify for which broader, non-trivial class of graphs this dimension independence holds?

*   The connection between multi-step updates (like your auxiliary scheme) and accelerated methods (Polyak's heavy ball, Nesterov's momentum) has been known in the distributed optimization community for some time (e.g., see Rabbat et al. [32]). It would strengthen the paper to acknowledge this and clarify how your work builds upon or differs from this established understanding.

*   To better contextualize your work, I strongly recommend incorporating the missing references listed above and discussing how your Min-Sum-based approach compares in terms of rate, assumptions, and implementation complexity with these modern distributed optimization algorithms.

*   A minor clarity point: in Section 5, you define the algorithm over the set of directed edges E, derived from an undirected graph G. It would be helpful to state this convention more explicitly early on to avoid any potential confusion for the reader.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:** The paper presents a novel and elegant connection between the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm and established acceleration techniques for optimization. The use of an auxiliary process to simplify the analysis is a strong technical contribution. The paper is well-written and provides a new perspective on message-passing algorithms.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:** The main result—the convergence rate—does not appear to be a significant improvement over existing state-of-the-art methods and may in fact be worse. The paper suffers from an incomplete literature review, omitting several key papers that are essential for contextualizing the contribution. Consequently, the claims of originality and significance are not fully substantiated.

Overall, while the paper contains interesting ideas and a nice analytical technique, its contribution is limited by the lack of a clear performance improvement and insufficient engagement with the relevant literature.
