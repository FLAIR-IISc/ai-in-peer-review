Summary
The paper studies the Min-Sum (a.k.a. belief propagation) message-passing protocol for distributed optimization in the consensus (network averaging) setting. It shows that vanilla Min-Sum generally fails on loopy graphs but a reparameterized variant, Min-Sum Splitting [34], converges for consensus and, with appropriate tuning, achieves accelerated “subdiffusive” rates. The key technical idea is to choose splitting parameters so that the induced edge-based linear iteration can be tracked by a node-based auxiliary two-step linear system. This reveals that Min-Sum Splitting implements a multi-step (shift-register) iteration whose rate matches the best-known accelerated rates for consensus on graphs with geometry. In particular, the paper proves a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods: O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori, close to the O(D log(1/ε)) lower-bound-optimal rates achieved by Nesterov-type schemes [30, 36]. The work further clarifies connections between belief-propagation with splitting, lifted Markov chains [37], and multi-step first-order methods [13, 24, 45, 4].

Relation to prior work
Prior work established correctness conditions for Min-Sum/GBP under diagonal dominance and for unconstrained quadratics, and introduced Splitting to improve convergence behavior [34, 35]. For consensus via Min-Sum-like methods, [27] proposed a soft-barrier formulation and analyzed a linear edge-based process on regular graphs with partial results (e.g., O(D) mixing on cycles, conjectures for tori). Separately, accelerated consensus has been developed via non-reversible lifted chains [8, 5, 18, 37] and multi-step methods (heavy-ball, Nesterov, shift-register) [13, 24, 45, 4]. This paper appears to be the first to (i) analyze rates for Min-Sum Splitting, (ii) give a general graph-topology analysis via a node-based auxiliary system, and (iii) align the obtained rates with shift-register acceleration.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution: a general convergence-rate theorem (Theorem 1) for Min-Sum Splitting in consensus, with explicit parameter choice γ that yields ρK < ρW and a square-root speedup over diffusive rates.
- Conceptual unification: precise and insightful connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods; the auxiliary node-based system K(δ, Γ) explains both convergence and acceleration mechanisms.
- Improved understanding over [27]: general graphs, provable subdiffusive rates matching shift-register asymptotics; avoids reliance on edge-based spectral gaps that are harder to analyze.
- Methodological novelty: the auxiliary process to track edge-based BP by a 2n-dimensional node-based linear system is elegant and likely of independent interest.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practical parameter selection: the accelerated choice requires γ depending on ρ(W − 11T/n). Estimating ρW in a distributed manner is nontrivial; guidance on robust surrogates, upper bounds, or adaptive estimation would increase practical relevance.
- Scope limited to quadratic objectives: the results (linearity and rate analysis) target network averaging. It remains unclear how much of the analysis extends to more general convex φv or composite settings.
- Implementation clarity for Γ: Algorithm 1 divides by Γvw in the pairwise term. When Γ has negative entries (which can arise for optimal W), convexity of the subproblems and existence of minimizers require clarification. The quadratic specialization resolves this analytically, but explicit admissibility conditions for Γ in the general template would help.
- No empirical validation: numerical experiments comparing Min-Sum Splitting with Metropolis, best symmetric W, heavy-ball/Nesterov/shift-register, lifted chains, ADMM [3, 43, 38], and consensus propagation [27] on cycles, grids, random geometric graphs would substantiate the theory and expose constants and robustness.

Quality
The technical development is careful and internally consistent. The reduction from edge-based to node-based dynamics (Algorithms 2→3, Propositions 1–2) is convincing and enables a clean spectral analysis. Theorem 1 provides explicit rates and ties to known accelerated matrices. Some details (e.g., admissible Γ ensuring well-defined updates in Algorithm 1) should be made explicit, even in the quadratic specialization.

Clarity
The paper is well organized, with a clear progression from Min-Sum Splitting to consensus specialization, auxiliary system construction, and the main theorem. The algorithms are stated precisely. Readability could be further improved by adding a small running example (e.g., a 4-node path) and a brief intuitive derivation of the node-based two-step system.

Originality
High. Prior work did not analyze rates for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus nor make the concrete link to shift-register acceleration and lifted processes in this setting.

Significance
High for distributed optimization and message-passing. The results suggest that carefully tuned BP-style protocols can match the best accelerated consensus rates, closing the gap between probabilistic inference algorithms and optimization-oriented accelerations. The auxiliary-process technique may extend to other constrained Min-Sum settings.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Discuss how to choose or approximate γ in practice. Provide bounds based on graph properties (e.g., degree, conductance), or simple distributed estimators (power/Lanczos iterations), and quantify the loss from using conservative choices.
- Clarify conditions on Γ to ensure well-posedness of Algorithm 1 in the quadratic case (existence/uniqueness of minimizers at each step) and reconcile with potentially negative entries.
- Add at least one numerical experiment demonstrating the predicted square-root speedup and comparing to shift-register baselines on canonical graphs.

Optional:
- Discuss extensions beyond quadratics (e.g., smooth + strongly convex φv) and whether the auxiliary-process perspective still yields rates.
- Comment on asynchronous or stochastic updates and robustness to communication noise.
- Provide implementation guidance on initializing messages (beyond R̂0 = r̂0 = 0) and memory/communication costs per iteration relative to multi-step consensus.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, novel theory with clear acceleration guarantees and insightful connections across literatures.
- General graph results that improve upon prior Min-Sum consensus analyses.
- Potential impact on both BP-style algorithms and distributed optimization.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of empirical validation and limited guidance for parameter selection may hinder practical adoption.
- Results are restricted to quadratic consensus; broader applicability remains to be shown.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contribution and conceptual unification are solid and timely; addressing the essential suggestions would strengthen the submission further.
