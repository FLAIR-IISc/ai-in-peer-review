

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm for distributed consensus problems, establishing convergence rates and connections to existing accelerated methods. The main claims are that Min-Sum Splitting (a modified version of the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm) yields convergence to the consensus solution where ordinary Min-Sum fails, and that proper parameter tuning achieves subdiffusive convergence rates matching shift-register methods. The authors further establish theoretical connections between Min-Sum Splitting and two distinct lines of accelerated consensus research: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods.

The paper's strengths are significant theoretical contributions. The authors provide a novel proof technique using an auxiliary process to track convergence behavior, establishing Theorem 1 which rigorously shows the accelerated convergence rates. The connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods are insightful and well-supported by theoretical analysis. The paper demonstrates strong field knowledge, with comprehensive and relevant citations to prior work on consensus algorithms, message-passing methods, and accelerated optimization.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. The theoretical analysis is not complemented by empirical validation, which is particularly concerning given the paper's claims about convergence rates and practical performance. The lack of experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical relevance of the theoretical findings. While theoretical contributions are valuable, the absence of any empirical results to support the claims about "accelerated convergence" is a significant limitation for a distributed systems paper.

The paper acknowledges that parameter tuning requires global graph knowledge (e.g., spectral properties), which may limit practical applicability in some distributed settings. The authors could strengthen their case by addressing how this global information could be obtained in practice or by providing empirical evidence of the claimed rate improvements.

Overall, this is a technically sound theoretical contribution that establishes an important connection between message-passing algorithms and accelerated consensus methods. However, the lack of empirical validation significantly weakens the paper's impact. I recommend rejection in its current form but encourage the authors to submit a revised version with empirical results demonstrating the claimed convergence rate improvements. The theoretical foundation is strong, but the paper would benefit greatly from practical validation.
