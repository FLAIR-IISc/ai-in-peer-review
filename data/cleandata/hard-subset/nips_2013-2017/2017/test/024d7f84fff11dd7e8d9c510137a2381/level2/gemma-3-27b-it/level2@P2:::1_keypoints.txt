- Shows Min-Sum Splitting converges for consensus and achieves a square-root (subdiffusive) improvement over diffusive methods, matching shift-register rates.  
- Connects Min-Sum Splitting to lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods, highlighting versatility for distributed optimization.  
- Fills a gap in prior work by providing rigorous convergence guarantees for splitting, building on [27], [34,35], and related literature.  
- Novelty: First to establish accelerated (subdiffusive) rates for Min-Sum Splitting and relate it to known acceleration techniques.  
- Rigorous analysis: Uses an auxiliary process and spectral arguments to derive convergence rates with an innovative proof technique.  
- Clear statement of main results (Theorem 1) and well-articulated link to shift-register methods.  
- Well-motivated problem and comprehensive, appropriate literature review.  
- Notation and readability are problematic—dense Sections 5–6, many algorithms/matrices (K, K̂) cause cognitive load and disjoint flow.  
- Lacks discussion of practical implications such as computational complexity, implementation effort, and which graph topologies benefit most.  
- Analysis relies on specific initial conditions and parameter choices; robustness/sensitivity to deviations is not explored.  
- Limited empirical validation—no simulations to support theoretical claims or illustrate practical behavior.  
- Repetitive motivation and related-work text reduces clarity and flow.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically strong, significant improvement in convergence, and valuable connections to existing methods.  
- Arguments against acceptance: dense presentation/complex notation, missing empirical evaluation, and unexplored sensitivity to parameters.  
- Overall assessment: Strong theoretical contribution worth acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—improve clarity/notation, add practical discussion and preliminary simulations, and remove repetitive sections.
