- Shows ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge on graphs with cycles, but Min-Sum Splitting converges to the consensus solution.
- Proves that proper parameter tuning gives Min-Sum Splitting subdiffusive (accelerated) convergence matching shift-register methods.
- Derives convergence time scaling O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles and tori, where D is graph diameter.
- Establishes links between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step first-order optimization methods.
- Interprets splitting as a lifted process on directed edges and as a memory term in an auxiliary node-based algorithm.
- Introduces a novel auxiliary message-passing scheme that maps edge dynamics to a linear process with memory on nodes.
- Relates convergence rates to spectral properties of graph-associated matrices rather than direct edge-based analysis.
- Theorem 1 characterizes convergence and shows a square-root improvement over diffusive methods.
- Paper is well-organized, clearly written, with consistent notation and technically sound proofs.
- Provides sufficient related-work background and situates results within accelerated consensus literature.
- Strength: first theoretical convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting.
- Strength: novel proof technique using an auxiliary node-based process.
- Strength: precise convergence-rate analysis across graph topologies and practical connection to shift-registers.
- Weakness: no analysis of computational complexity versus other accelerated methods.
- Weakness: focus limited to quadratic objectives, restricting applicability to non-quadratic cases.
- Weakness: parameter tuning depends on global spectral information (ρW), complicating fully distributed deployment.
- Weakness: lacks empirical validation of theoretical results through experiments.
- Suggestion: include discussion of implementation challenges and global-parameter tuning in distributed settings.
- Overall: significant theoretical contribution to distributed optimization and accelerated consensus; well-suited for NIPS.
