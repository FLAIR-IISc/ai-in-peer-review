- Presents theoretical analysis and application of Min-Sum Splitting to consensus, showing accelerated convergence and links to lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods.
- Main contributions: proves convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, reveals its acceleration mechanism, and introduces an auxiliary-process proof technique.
- Novelty: first theoretical convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and insightful connections to other acceleration frameworks.
- Technical rigor: detailed, spectral-analysis-based proofs and a creative auxiliary process to track algorithm evolution.
- Significance: practical implications for distributed optimization with a demonstrated square-root improvement over classical diffusive methods.
- Clarity of contributions: clearly outlines the protocol design achieving improved convergence and connections to prior acceleration methods.
- Weakness: lacks empirical validation—no simulations or real-world experiments to demonstrate practical performance.
- Weakness: acceleration depends on global graph properties (e.g., spectral gap), limiting applicability in fully decentralized settings.
- Weakness: insufficient experimental comparison with state-of-the-art methods like Nesterov acceleration or distributed ADMM.
- Weakness: some mathematical sections (Algorithms 2 and 3) need clearer explanations or visual aids for broader accessibility.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a significant problem, provides novel theoretically grounded solutions, and advances the state of the art.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited immediate applicability due to missing empirical results and reliance on global information.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions—add empirical results and discuss strategies to mitigate dependence on global graph information.
