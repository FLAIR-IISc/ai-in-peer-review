This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The work builds upon previous research on Min-Sum algorithms and their limitations, particularly in graphs with cycles. The core contribution lies in establishing subdiffusive convergence rates – specifically, a square-root improvement over diffusive methods – under a proper choice of tuning parameters. The analysis leverages an auxiliary linear process to track the algorithm’s evolution and draws parallels between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the challenges of standard Min-Sum and the potential benefits of the splitting approach. The connections drawn to lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods are insightful and help contextualize the work within a broader landscape of distributed optimization techniques. The analytical results – particularly Theorem 1 – are significant, providing a quantifiable improvement in convergence rates. The careful consideration of parameter tuning and its dependence on graph properties (spectral gap) adds to the rigor and practical relevance of the study. The paper is thoroughly grounded in existing literature, citing relevant works and accurately positioning its contributions. 

However, the paper suffers from a degree of density and can be difficult to follow without significant prior familiarity with the field. The notation is heavy and, while generally consistent, could benefit from more frequent explanations. The numerous references to related work, while comprehensive, sometimes interrupt the flow of the main argument. The discussion of existing literature in Sections 3 and 4, while valuable, feels somewhat disjointed from the core contribution of the paper. While the authors acknowledge related work on ADMM (Section 4), the connection to Min-Sum is not fully explored and could be strengthened.  The assumption that 0 < bv < 1 is stated without full justification and its impact on the generalizability of results should be discussed.

The strength of the paper lies in its rigorous analysis and concrete improvement in convergence rates. The theoretical foundation is solid, and the connection to shift-register methods is a compelling result. The weakness is the paper’s accessibility and the density of technical details, which may limit its impact on researchers not deeply immersed in this specific area.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical results demonstrating accelerated convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting.
*   Clear and mathematically sound analysis.
*   Well-motivated problem and relevant connection to existing work.
*   Demonstrated connection to shift-register methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   High technical density and challenging readability.
*   Potential lack of broader appeal due to specialized nature.
*   Limited exploration of connections to related approaches (e.g., ADMM).
*   Some unexplained assumptions.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The theoretical results are significant, and the analysis is rigorous. While the paper could benefit from improved clarity and accessibility, its strengths outweigh its weaknesses. A revision addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding clarity and connections to other approaches, would significantly strengthen the paper.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The authors should focus on improving the clarity of the presentation, providing more intuitive explanations of the technical details, and expanding on the connections to related work. Specifically, elaborating on the relationship with ADMM and justifying the assumption regarding the bounds on bv would be beneficial.
