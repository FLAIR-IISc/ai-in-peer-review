Summary
The paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for distributed averaging (consensus) on graphs. It shows: (i) ordinary Min-Sum (BP) fails on loopy graphs for consensus, but a reparameterized “Splitting” variant converges; (ii) with an appropriate choice of parameters (δ = 1 and Γ = γW for a symmetric averaging matrix W and a scalar γ tuned from the spectral gap of W), the algorithm enjoys accelerated, subdiffusive rates. The core technique is to track the edge-based linear message recursion via an auxiliary node-based 2-step linear iteration, revealing that Min-Sum Splitting implements a shift-register (second-order) consensus method. The resulting asymptotic rate ρK matches that of Chebyshev/shift-register accelerations and yields O(D log(D/ε)) iterations on cycles and tori, a square-root improvement over diffusive O(D2 log(1/ε)) mixing. The paper connects Min-Sum Splitting both to lifted, nonreversible Markov chains (directionality on edges) and to accelerated first-order methods (momentum on nodes).

Relation to prior work is carefully discussed: [27] analyzed Min-Sum with soft constraints and derived rates via edge-space spectral gaps for special cases; [34,35] introduced Splitting and proved convergence (not rates) in some quadratic settings; accelerated consensus via polynomial/second-order methods and lifting is well-documented [4,7,13,18,24,37,45]. This paper appears to be the first to prove accelerated rates for Min-Sum Splitting and to explicitly bridge it to these two acceleration paradigms.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution: a principled derivation showing that Min-Sum Splitting equals a second-order, node-space accelerated scheme (shift-register) for consensus, explaining acceleration via both “lifting” and “momentum” viewpoints.
- Solid theoretical result: Theorem 1 provides an explicit convergence factor ρK and a parameter choice γ yielding provable subdiffusive scaling on geometrically structured graphs; the analysis leverages a clean auxiliary 2n-by-2n linear system.
- Broad relevance: consensus is a canonical primitive in distributed learning and optimization; understanding message-passing acceleration is valuable for ML systems and graphical models.
- Comparison to [27] clarifies improvements (general graphs, explicit rates, simpler spectral object on nodes rather than edges).

Weaknesses and concerns
Major:
- Scope limited to quadratic objectives (averaging) with synchronous, noise-free updates; general convex consensus or constraints are not treated. The introduction hints at broader convex settings, but proofs and results focus on the quadratic case.
- Parameter selection depends on global spectral information (ρW). This is typical for accelerated consensus, but raises practicality questions for decentralized implementation. No discussion of how to estimate/tune γ in a distributed/robust way or sensitivity to mis-specification.
- Lack of empirical validation. Given the algorithm’s claim of acceleration and practical relevance, experiments on standard topologies (cycles, grids, random geometric, expanders) would help assess constants, transients, robustness to model mismatch, and comparison to state-of-the-art accelerated methods (Chebyshev, Nesterov, “fast linear iterations” [44], EXTRA, ADMM).
- The requirement in Proposition 1 that 1 + δ Σ Γwv R̂t_wv > 0 to ensure well-defined estimates is nontrivial; with zero initialization it seems to hold, but the paper does not discuss whether it is guaranteed for all t or under perturbations.

Minor:
- Clarity could be improved by writing explicitly the node-space two-step recurrence for xt (e.g., the standard shift-register form xt = (1+α)Wxt−1 − αxt−2) and mapping α to γ to make the link to [4,24,45] immediate.
- Discuss computational/communication costs per iteration versus classical consensus and second-order methods (edge vs node state).
- The discussion of lifted chains is insightful; adding a small illustrative figure or example mapping the edge-directionality to nonreversibility would aid intuition.

Quality
The results are technically sound for the stated setting. The derivation of the auxiliary node-space process and the spectral analysis are convincing and align with known shift-register bounds. Some assumptions (global parameter tuning, synchronous updates) constrain applicability; robustness (asynchrony, delays, time-varying graphs) is not treated.

Clarity
Generally well-organized with a logical progression: algorithm → specialization → auxiliary system → rate theorem. Notation density is high; a succinct summary of the final node update and a small running example would improve readability. A brief proof sketch of Theorem 1 in the main text would help.

Originality
Novel and interesting: first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus; a clean equivalence to shift-register acceleration; explicit bridge to lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization. Prior works did not establish these rates for Splitting.

Significance
Solid theoretical advance for a widely used primitive. While quadratic consensus is a restricted setting, the conceptual bridge may inspire accelerated message-passing for broader problems (e.g., Gaussian BP, structured convex programs). Practical impact will depend on addressing tuning and robustness.

Reasons to accept
- First rigorous accelerated rates for Min-Sum Splitting, matching shift-register performance.
- Clear, well-motivated connections to lifted chains and multi-step methods.
- General graph rates with simpler spectral characterization than prior Min-Sum analyses.

Reasons to reject
- No experiments; practicality (tuning, robustness) not addressed.
- Restricted to quadratic consensus and synchronous settings.

Questions for the authors
- Can you write the explicit node-space recurrence for xt and map its parameters to γ (and W) to highlight equivalence to known second-order schemes?
- How sensitive is performance to γ mis-specification? Can you propose distributed estimators of ρW or adaptive tuning?
- Does the positivity condition in Proposition 1 hold automatically under your initialization for all t? Can you provide a short argument?
- Are there extensions to mildly asynchronous updates or stochastic communication?
- How does the per-iteration cost compare to Chebyshev-accelerated consensus and ADMM-based consensus?

Suggestions for improvement
- Include a small experimental section on canonical graphs comparing to Metropolis, optimal symmetric W, Chebyshev/shift-register, and ADMM/EXTRA, with sensitivity to γ.
- Provide a proof sketch of Theorem 1 and an explicit xt recurrence.
- Discuss practical parameter selection and robustness.

Minor comments/typos
- There are some formatting glitches (e.g., “st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems”). Clean up footnotes and references.
- Ensure consistent notation for norms and spectral radius and clarify assumptions on Γ’s diagonal entries.
