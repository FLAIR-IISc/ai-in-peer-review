## Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem in distributed optimization. The core claim is that, with a proper choice of tuning parameters, Min-Sum Splitting achieves subdiffusive, accelerated convergence rates matching those obtained by shift-register methods. The authors establish a connection between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step first-order methods, highlighting a unifying perspective on acceleration techniques.

The paper builds upon prior work on Min-Sum algorithms [28, 34, 35] and consensus problems [42, 41, 27]. It correctly identifies the limitations of standard Min-Sum and diffusive algorithms, motivating the investigation of accelerated methods. The authors effectively position their work within the broader landscape of accelerated consensus algorithms, referencing both lifted Markov chain approaches [37] and accelerated first-order methods [13]. The introduction of an auxiliary process to analyze the algorithm’s convergence is a clever technique, allowing for a more tractable analysis than previous approaches [27]. 

The strength of the paper lies in its theoretical analysis and the establishment of convergence rates. Theorem 1 provides a concrete result demonstrating the accelerated convergence and linking it to the spectral properties of the underlying graph. The connection to shift-register methods is particularly insightful. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a comprehensive review of related work. 

However, the paper lacks practical evaluation. While the theoretical results are compelling, there is no experimental validation to demonstrate the performance of Min-Sum Splitting in real-world scenarios or even simulations. It would be beneficial to see how the algorithm performs on different graph topologies and compare its performance against existing accelerated methods. Furthermore, the discussion of parameter tuning (specifically, the choice of γ) could be more detailed, providing guidance on how to select appropriate parameters for different graphs. The paper also assumes a quadratic objective function; exploring the applicability of the method to more general convex functions would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical analysis of Min-Sum Splitting convergence rates.
*   Establishment of a connection to shift-register methods and other acceleration techniques.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation of the material.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of experimental validation.
*   Limited discussion of parameter tuning.
*   Restricted to quadratic objective functions.

**Overall:**

Despite the lack of experimental results, this paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The analysis is rigorous, the results are compelling, and the connections to existing work are well-articulated. I recommend acceptance with the encouragement to the authors to include experimental validation and explore the generalization of their results to broader problem settings in a future iteration.
