### Review

**Summary of the paper**

This paper investigates the application of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, a generalization of the standard Min-Sum message-passing protocol, to the distributed consensus problem, specifically network averaging. The authors first note that the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge on graphs with cycles. They then demonstrate that the Min-Sum Splitting variant, with appropriate choices for its "splitting" parameters (δ and Γ), not only guarantees convergence but also achieves accelerated, subdiffusive rates.

The main contributions of the work are threefold. First, it establishes a novel and insightful connection between Min-Sum schemes, lifted Markov chain techniques, and multi-step first-order methods from convex optimization, three areas that have largely developed in parallel to address the acceleration of consensus. Second, it provides the first convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, proving that it can achieve a square-root improvement in convergence time over standard "diffusive" methods for certain graph topologies (e.g., cycles and tori). The achieved rate matches that of shift-register methods. Third, the authors introduce an elegant proof technique based on an auxiliary message-passing scheme defined on the graph's nodes, which tracks the output of the original algorithm running on the directed edges. This significantly simplifies the spectral analysis required to establish the convergence rate.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength lies in its novel conceptual contribution of unifying three distinct approaches to accelerated consensus. By showing that the Min-Sum Splitting framework can be understood both as a process on a lifted space (the directed edges) and as a multi-step method with memory on the original nodes, the authors provide a new and valuable perspective. This connection is significant and likely to inspire further research at the intersection of graphical models, optimization, and distributed systems.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears sound and is presented clearly. The introduction of the auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) to analyze the convergence of the original algorithm (Algorithm 2) is a clever and effective technique. It transforms the problem of analyzing a 2m x 2m matrix associated with the edge dynamics into analyzing a more structured 2n x 2n matrix tied to the nodal dynamics and the spectral properties of Γ, which is a significant simplification. The main result, Theorem 1, rigorously establishes the accelerated convergence rate.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. It begins with a comprehensive review of the consensus problem, standard diffusive algorithms, and the main families of accelerated methods. This background provides excellent context for the reader and clearly positions the paper's contributions. The logical flow from the general Min-Sum Splitting algorithm to its specific linear form for consensus, and then to the auxiliary scheme for analysis, is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Practicality of Parameter Selection:** The accelerated rate presented in Theorem 1 is contingent on a specific choice of the parameter γ, which in turn depends on ρ_W, the second largest eigenvalue modulus of the matrix W. This is a global property of the graph that is typically not known locally at each node and can be computationally expensive to determine. While this is a common requirement for many accelerated distributed methods, the paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this limitation. For example, could γ be estimated in a distributed manner, or could an adaptive scheme be developed?

2.  **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is focused entirely on the quadratic network averaging problem. While this is a cornerstone of the consensus literature, the Min-Sum framework is applicable to more general optimization problems. It would be beneficial for the authors to comment on the potential for extending their acceleration results to consensus problems with more general convex functions. Does the linear structure of the message updates, which is key to the analysis, hold only in the quadratic case? A brief discussion of the challenges involved in a more general setting would be welcome.

3.  **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper demonstrates that Min-Sum Splitting can match the rate of shift-register methods, which is a strong result. However, for certain graph classes, recent work on Nesterov-style acceleration (e.g., Scaman et al., 2017) has established an optimal rate of O(D log(1/ε)). The rate derived here for tori, O(D log(D/ε)), is slightly suboptimal due to the logarithmic factor. While this is a minor point, acknowledging this and placing their result in the context of these optimal algorithms would provide a more complete picture.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on distributed optimization. The conceptual bridge built between message-passing, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step methods is insightful and impactful. The technical results are strong, and the analytical approach is novel and elegant. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate more to practical implementation and the scope of the current analysis, rather than any fundamental flaws in the work. They represent interesting directions for future research. The paper is of high quality and is a clear fit for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
