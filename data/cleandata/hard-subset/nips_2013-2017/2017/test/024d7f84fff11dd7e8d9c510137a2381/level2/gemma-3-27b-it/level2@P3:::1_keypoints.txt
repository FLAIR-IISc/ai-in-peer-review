- Studies convergence of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus, proving a subdiffusive (square-root) improvement over diffusive methods with proper tuning.
- Uses an auxiliary linear process to track algorithm evolution and draws parallels to lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods.
- Theorem 1 gives a significant, quantifiable improvement in convergence rates.
- Highlights a connection to shift-register methods.
- Parameter tuning analysis is careful and tied to graph spectral gap, increasing practical relevance.
- Well-motivated and thoroughly grounded in existing literature, accurately positioning its contributions.
- Dense presentation and heavy notation make the paper difficult to follow without strong prior knowledge.
- Frequent, comprehensive references to related work sometimes interrupt the narrative and disrupt flow.
- Discussion in Sections 3–4 feels somewhat disjoint from the core contribution.
- Connection to ADMM is acknowledged but not sufficiently explored or integrated with Min-Sum results.
- The assumption 0 < bv < 1 is stated without full justification; its impact on generalizability needs discussion.
- Strengths: novel theoretical results, rigorous mathematical analysis, and relevant problem framing.
- Weaknesses: limited accessibility, specialized appeal, unexplained assumptions, and incomplete comparison to related approaches.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions focused on improving clarity, adding intuitive explanations, elaborating the ADMM connection, and justifying the bv bound.
