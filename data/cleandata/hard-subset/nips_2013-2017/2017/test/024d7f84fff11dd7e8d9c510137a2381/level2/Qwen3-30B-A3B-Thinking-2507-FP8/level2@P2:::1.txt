

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum message-passing to distributed consensus problems, introducing a "Splitting" modification that enables convergence and accelerated rates. The authors prove that proper tuning of parameters yields subdiffusive convergence rates matching shift-register methods, and establish theoretical connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting applied to consensus problems. Second, it establishes a novel theoretical connection between Min-Sum message-passing protocols and two established accelerated methods (lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods), showing that the directionality introduced by splitting is analogous to the directionality in non-reversible random walks and the memory term in multi-step methods. Third, the authors introduce an auxiliary process that tracks the evolution of Min-Sum Splitting on the original graph nodes rather than on directed edges, enabling a cleaner spectral analysis.

The quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with clear proofs of convergence rates and connections to existing methods. The paper properly situates its contributions within the literature, particularly by contrasting with the work of [27] which investigated Min-Sum for consensus but did not establish convergence rates for general graphs. The paper also correctly identifies the limitations of diffusive methods and shows how Min-Sum Splitting overcomes them.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the connections to prior work are well-explained. The authors effectively use the auxiliary process construction as a novel technical contribution to simplify the convergence analysis.

The originality is significant. While Min-Sum has been applied to consensus problems before, the paper establishes a new theoretical connection between Min-Sum and accelerated methods, and provides the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting. The paper distinguishes itself from prior work by showing how the splitting parameters can be tuned to achieve accelerated rates, which was previously unestablished.

The significance is high. Consensus is a fundamental problem in distributed optimization, and the paper demonstrates a method that achieves subdiffusive convergence rates (O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori), which is a square-root improvement over diffusive methods. This represents a meaningful advancement in the state of the art for distributed consensus algorithms.

However, there are some limitations that should be addressed. The paper focuses almost exclusively on the quadratic case (network averaging), which limits its applicability to more general consensus problems. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't discuss how their approach might be extended to non-quadratic problems. Additionally, the parameter tuning requires global information about the graph (specifically the spectral gap of the matrix W), which may not be practical in truly distributed settings where global information is unavailable. The paper doesn't discuss how this limitation could be mitigated in practice.

The paper would benefit from more empirical validation of the theoretical results. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the authors don't provide simulation results comparing Min-Sum Splitting with existing methods (e.g., shift-register methods or lifted Markov chain approaches) on concrete graph topologies. This would help demonstrate the practical impact of their theoretical findings.

Another minor weakness is that the paper doesn't clearly explain why the choice of δ = 1 and Γ = γW (as in Theorem 1) is optimal for achieving the best convergence rates. A more detailed discussion of the parameter tuning process would strengthen the paper.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes significant contributions to the understanding of Min-Sum message-passing for distributed consensus. The theoretical results are sound, the connections to existing work are insightful, and the paper advances the state of the art in distributed optimization. The limitations regarding the quadratic case and global parameter tuning are notable but don't undermine the core contributions.

Strengths: Novel theoretical connections, sound mathematical analysis, clear presentation of the auxiliary process technique, significant improvement over diffusive methods.

Weaknesses: Limited to quadratic case, requires global information for parameter tuning, lacks empirical validation.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of distributed optimization and would be of interest to researchers working on consensus algorithms, message-passing methods, and accelerated optimization. I recommend acceptance.
