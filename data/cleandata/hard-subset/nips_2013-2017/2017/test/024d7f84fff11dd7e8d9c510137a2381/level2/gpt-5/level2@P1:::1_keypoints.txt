- Shows ordinary Min-Sum can diverge on loopy graphs for consensus, while Min-Sum Splitting converges.  
- Proves that with specific splitting parameters Min-Sum Splitting attains accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence matching shift-register/Chebyshev methods.  
- Introduces an auxiliary linear node-based process that tracks edge-message dynamics, enabling spectral analysis tied to a chosen symmetric matrix W.  
- Draws conceptual connections between BP splitting, lifted nonreversible Markov chains (edge directionality), and multi-step first-order acceleration (memory on nodes).  
- For cycles and tori, achieves O(D log(D/ε)) iterations — a square-root improvement over diffusive consensus.  
- Positions work relative to prior consensus/acceleration literature, recovering known shift-register rates via splitting.  
- Extends prior Min-Sum analyses (which were limited to cycles/regular graphs) to arbitrary graphs and provides the first rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus.  
- Fills a gap in Splitting/BP literature by providing concrete rate results for Gaussian/quadratic consensus cases.  
- Main results are technically sound: Propositions 1–2 link edge-based splitting to node auxiliary system; Theorem 1 gives explicit parameter choices and nonasymptotic bounds.  
- Assumes W symmetric with W1=1 and spectral gap ρ(W−11^T/n)<1; initialization R̂0=r̂0=0 and positivity conditions are required for well-defined beliefs.  
- Practicality concern: the optimal γ depends on global spectral information (ρW), impacting distributed implementation.  
- Scope limited to quadratic averaging; extensions to general convex consensus are qualitative and left for future work.  
- Paper is generally clear and well organized; Algorithms 1–3 and the role of δ and Γ are well explained.  
- Minor clarity suggestions: explicitly note δ=1 vs Γ choices, clarify positivity condition under the chosen initialization, and add a simple illustrative example.  
- Original contribution: novel auxiliary-node analysis and first rate proof for Min-Sum Splitting; conceptual bridge to liftings and acceleration is original.  
- Significance: strengthens understanding of BP/Splitting and shows Min-Sum can match best-known first-order accelerations, though practical impact is limited by global-parameter needs and quadratic focus.  
- Pro: Rigorous derivation of accelerated rates for arbitrary graphs.  
- Pro: Elegant conversion of edge-space BP into a 2n-dimensional node linear system tied to Γ and W.  
- Pro: Concrete parameter choice achieving shift-register rates and square-root improvement on geometric graphs.  
- Pro: Thoughtful connections to liftings and accelerated optimization that may inspire future work.  
- Con: Sensitivity to γ — needs study of misspecification tolerance and possible adaptive/distributed estimation of ρW.  
- Con: Lacks empirical validation; experiments on cycles, grids, and random graphs comparing to Metropolis, Chebyshev, Nesterov, ADMM are needed.  
- Con: Role of δ is unclear since main theorem uses δ=1 — potential benefits of δ≠1 for robustness or constants are unexplored.  
- Con: Edge-based Algorithm 2 has O(m) state overhead versus node-based O(n); unclear if Algorithm 3 can be implemented without edge message storage.  
- Con: Analysis assumes symmetric/undirected graphs and quadratic objectives; extension to directed/time-varying graphs or non-quadratic φv is open.  
- Con: The formal link to lifted nonreversible chains is qualitative; a rigorous correspondence with provable mixing gains is not provided.  
- Suggestions: add experiments quantifying rates, constants, and γ-sensitivity; compare to competing accelerated methods.  
- Suggestions: discuss distributed ways to choose W and γ (e.g., spectral bounds, Chebyshev approximations, degree-based conservative γ).  
- Suggestions: clarify/lemma the positivity condition for beliefs with proposed initialization and parameters.  
- Suggestions: examine whether auxiliary node-based updates can serve as a direct, lower-memory implementation.  
- Recommendation: strong theoretical contribution, lean to accept; encourage adding empirical validation and practical parameter-selection guidance to broaden impact.
