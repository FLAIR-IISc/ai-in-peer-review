This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors demonstrate that, unlike the standard Min-Sum algorithm, a modified version (splitting) converges and achieves accelerated convergence rates – specifically, a square-root improvement over diffusive methods – matching those of shift-register methods. They establish a connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods, highlighting its potential as a versatile approach to distributed optimization.

**Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The consensus problem is a fundamental building block in distributed optimization and has been extensively studied. Prior work includes classical linear dynamical systems (e.g., [42, 41]) exhibiting diffusive convergence, and more recent efforts to achieve accelerated rates via lifted Markov chains ([37], and references therein) and accelerated first-order methods ([13]).  The paper builds on the work of [27], which investigated Min-Sum for consensus but didn't provide complete convergence rate analysis.  [34, 35] introduced the splitting technique but lacked theoretical convergence guarantees. This paper fills that gap, providing a rigorous analysis of Min-Sum Splitting’s convergence and relating it to existing acceleration techniques.  The authors correctly position their work within the context of existing literature, citing relevant papers and acknowledging previous contributions.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, especially achieving subdiffusive rates matching shift-register methods, is a significant contribution.  The connection drawn between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods provides a deeper understanding of the algorithm.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper presents a strong theoretical analysis, using an auxiliary process to track the evolution of the algorithm and deriving convergence rates based on spectral properties. The proof technique seems innovative and effective.
* **Clear Presentation of Results:** Theorem 1 clearly states the convergence rate and conditions under which it holds.  The link to shift-register methods is well-articulated.
* **Well-Motivated Problem:** The consensus problem is a relevant and widely studied problem with many practical applications. The motivation for seeking accelerated algorithms is clear.
* **Comprehensive Literature Review:** The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the related literature, providing appropriate citations and context.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Notation and Readability:** The paper is quite dense, particularly in Sections 5 & 6. The introduction of numerous algorithms (1, 2, 3) and matrices (K, K̂) can be overwhelming.  Improved clarity in notation and organization, perhaps with more intuitive variable names, would enhance readability. The constant jumping between algorithms and propositions feels disjointed at times.
* **Practical Implications:** While the theoretical results are strong, the paper lacks discussion of the practical implications.  How does this algorithm compare to existing accelerated consensus algorithms in terms of computational complexity and implementation effort?  Are there specific graph topologies where Min-Sum Splitting shines?
* **Initialization Sensitivity:** The analysis relies on specific initial conditions and parameter choices. The sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to these choices is not thoroughly explored.  A discussion of robustness to deviations from optimal parameters would be valuable.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:**  The paper focuses solely on theoretical analysis.  Empirical validation, even with small-scale simulations, would strengthen the claims and provide insights into the algorithm's behavior in practice.
* **Repetitive Motivation:** The motivation and overview of related work are repeated several times throughout the introduction and subsequent sections.  A more concise presentation would improve flow.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm with a significant improvement in convergence rate for the consensus problem.
* The connection to existing acceleration techniques provides valuable insights.
* The analysis is rigorous and well-motivated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper's density and complex notation may hinder understanding.
* The lack of practical evaluation limits the assessment of its real-world applicability.
* The sensitivity to initialization and parameter choices requires further investigation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical contribution to the field of distributed optimization. While the paper could benefit from improved clarity, readability, and empirical validation, the novelty of the results and the rigor of the analysis justify acceptance. The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of the problem and its relationship to existing literature.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.  I recommend the authors focus on improving the clarity of the presentation, providing a brief discussion of practical implications, and considering including some preliminary simulation results. Simplifying the notation and streamlining the repetitive sections would also enhance the paper’s impact.
