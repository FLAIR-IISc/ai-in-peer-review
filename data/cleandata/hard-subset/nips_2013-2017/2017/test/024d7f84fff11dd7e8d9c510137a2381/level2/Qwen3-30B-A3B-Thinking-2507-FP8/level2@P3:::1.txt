

The paper presents a theoretical analysis of Min-Sum message-passing protocols for solving the consensus problem in distributed optimization. It establishes that while ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge for graphs with cycles, a modified version called Min-Sum Splitting yields convergence to the problem solution. The key contribution is the proof that proper tuning of parameters enables Min-Sum Splitting to achieve subdiffusive convergence rates matching those of shift-register methods, with convergence time scaling as O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, where D is the graph diameter.

The work makes several important connections to existing literature. It demonstrates that Min-Sum Splitting bears similarities with both lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization. The authors establish that Min-Sum Splitting can be seen as a process on a lifted space (the space of directed edges) and that the directionality introduced by splitting translates to a memory term in an auxiliary algorithm on the original nodes, directly connecting it to accelerated multi-step methods.

The paper's main theoretical contribution is the introduction of an auxiliary message-passing scheme that tracks the evolution of Min-Sum Splitting via a linear process with memory on the nodes of the graph. This novel proof technique allows the authors to relate convergence rates to the spectral properties of matrices associated with the graph, rather than directly analyzing the more complex edge-based dynamics. Theorem 1 provides a precise characterization of the convergence rate for Min-Sum Splitting when parameters are properly chosen, showing that it achieves a square-root improvement over diffusive methods.

The paper's significance lies in its theoretical contribution to understanding how message-passing algorithms can be accelerated. It addresses a fundamental limitation of Min-Sum algorithms for consensus (non-convergence on cyclic graphs) and provides a principled way to tune the algorithm for accelerated convergence. The connection to two major lines of research in accelerated consensus (lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods) is particularly valuable, as it suggests potential for cross-pollination between these fields.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with each section building logically on the previous one. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The proofs are technically sound, and the theoretical results are well-supported by the analysis. The authors provide sufficient background on related work, including a thorough discussion of diffusive algorithms and accelerated methods.

Strengths:
- First theoretical analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting
- Novel proof technique using an auxiliary process on the original graph
- Establishes meaningful connections between Min-Sum protocols and two major lines of accelerated consensus research
- Provides precise convergence rate analysis for various graph topologies
- Demonstrates practical relevance through the connection to shift-register methods

Weaknesses:
- The paper does not explore the computational complexity of implementing Min-Sum Splitting compared to alternative accelerated methods
- The analysis focuses primarily on quadratic objective functions, limiting immediate applicability to non-quadratic problems
- The dependence on global graph properties (via the spectral radius ρW) for parameter tuning may present challenges in fully distributed settings
- The paper does not provide empirical validation of the theoretical results with experiments

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of distributed optimization algorithms. It addresses an important gap in the literature by providing the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting in consensus problems. The connection to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods represents a valuable theoretical insight that could inspire future research on accelerating distributed algorithms.

The work is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The theoretical results are novel and significant, advancing our understanding of how message-passing protocols can be designed to achieve accelerated convergence rates. The paper's connection to two major lines of research in accelerated consensus methods is particularly strong, suggesting broader applicability beyond the specific consensus problem considered.

The main limitation is the lack of empirical validation, which would have strengthened the paper by demonstrating practical performance. However, given the focus on theoretical analysis, this is understandable within the context of NIPS. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of potential implementation challenges related to the global parameter tuning required.

Overall, the paper makes a substantial contribution to the theoretical foundations of distributed optimization. The results are significant for both the theoretical understanding of Min-Sum message-passing and for the broader field of accelerated consensus algorithms. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and represents a solid contribution to the conference's focus on machine learning and optimization.
