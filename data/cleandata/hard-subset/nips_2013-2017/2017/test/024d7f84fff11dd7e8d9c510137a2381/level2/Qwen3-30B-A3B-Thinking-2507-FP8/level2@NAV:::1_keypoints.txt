- Paper analyzes Min-Sum Splitting for distributed consensus, proving convergence where ordinary Min-Sum can fail.  
- Claims proper parameter tuning yields subdiffusive (accelerated) convergence matching shift-register methods.  
- Establishes theoretical connections between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step first-order methods.  
- Introduces a novel proof technique using an auxiliary process to track convergence and proves Theorem 1.  
- Demonstrates strong literature knowledge with comprehensive citations on consensus, message-passing, and accelerated optimization.  
- Major weakness: no empirical validation or experiments to support theoretical convergence and practical performance claims.  
- Parameter tuning requires global graph knowledge (spectral properties), limiting practical applicability in distributed settings.  
- Authors should address how global information can be obtained or provide empirical evidence of rate improvements.  
- Overall: technically sound and insightful theoretical contribution, but lack of experiments weakens impact.  
- Recommendation: reject in current form but encourage resubmission with empirical validation demonstrating claimed improvements.
