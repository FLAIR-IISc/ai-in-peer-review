- Applies Min-Sum Splitting to distributed consensus and shows ordinary Min-Sum fails on loopy graphs while Splitting converges.
- Main theoretical contribution: tuning splitting parameters yields accelerated, subdiffusive convergence rates.
- First paper to establish convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting.
- Connects message-passing framework to lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods (e.g., shift-registers).
- Proves square-root improvement over standard diffusive methods.
- Uses an elegant auxiliary node-based process to analyze the edge-based algorithm and link performance to graph spectral properties.
- Technical claims are rigorous and well-supported by proofs.
- Practical concern: optimal splitting parameter γ depends on global spectral gap of W, with no discussion of distributed estimation or selection.
- Empirical weakness: entirely theoretical with no numerical simulations to validate claimed rates or illustrate improvement magnitude.
- Overall assessment: high-quality, novel, and important theoretical work; strong recommendation for acceptance.
