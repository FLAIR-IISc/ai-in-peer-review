This paper applies the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, a generalized message-passing protocol, to the fundamental problem of distributed consensus. The authors demonstrate that while the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge for this problem on graphs with cycles, the Splitting variant successfully converges. The paper's main contribution is a theoretical analysis proving that by carefully tuning the algorithm's splitting parameters, one can achieve accelerated, subdiffusive convergence rates. This work is the first to establish convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting and, significantly, connects this message-passing framework to other established acceleration techniques, namely lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods like shift-registers.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength lies in its novel conceptual contribution. By formally linking a belief propagation-style algorithm to mainstream accelerated optimization methods, it provides a new and valuable perspective on acceleration in distributed systems. The problem of accelerating consensus is of high importance to the NIPS community, and the proven square-root improvement over standard diffusive methods is a significant result.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs. The analytical technique of introducing an auxiliary node-based process to track the evolution of the edge-based Min-Sum algorithm is particularly elegant and effective. This allows the authors to cleanly connect the algorithm's performance to the spectral properties of the underlying graph and recover the same dynamics as shift-register methods.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Practical Considerations:** The choice of the optimal splitting parameter γ, which is key to achieving acceleration, depends on a global property of the network (the spectral gap of the matrix W). The paper does not discuss how this parameter might be chosen or estimated in a practical, fully distributed setting. Adding a brief discussion on this point would improve the paper's practical relevance.
*   **Lack of Experiments:** The work is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper would be more compelling with numerical simulations. Even a simple experiment on a cycle or grid graph comparing the proposed method against a standard diffusive algorithm would provide concrete validation of the accelerated rates claimed in Theorem 1 and better illustrate the magnitude of the improvement.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and important theoretical result. It successfully bridges different areas of optimization and distributed computing. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the core contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
