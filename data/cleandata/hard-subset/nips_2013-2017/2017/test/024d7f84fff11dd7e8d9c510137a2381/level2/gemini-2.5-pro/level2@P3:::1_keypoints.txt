- Studies Min-Sum Splitting for distributed consensus and shows the Splitting variant converges on graphs with cycles.  
- Main contribution is a rigorous convergence-rate analysis proving accelerated, subdiffusive convergence.  
- Shows acceleration matches shift-register methods, giving a square-root improvement over classical diffusive consensus.  
- Establishes a conceptual connection between Min-Sum, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step first-order optimization methods.  
- Introduces an auxiliary linear node-space process (Algorithm 3) that simplifies spectral analysis from 2m (edges) to 2n (nodes).  
- Derivation of Min-Sum Splitting dynamics for quadratic consensus (Algorithm 2) is clear and technically sound.  
- Theorem 1 convincingly links the algorithm dynamics to the shift-register update matrix and supports the acceleration claims.  
- Practical weakness: optimal parameter γ depends on the global spectral quantity ρ_W and distributed estimation is not addressed.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and situates the work clearly within existing literature.  
- Highly original: first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting and novel interdisciplinary conceptual bridge.  
- Significant: demonstrates belief-propagation-style algorithms can achieve state-of-the-art acceleration and will likely spur further research.  
- Strengths for acceptance: novel connections, first rigorous rates, demonstrated acceleration, and excellent exposition.  
- Argument against acceptance: omission of discussion on how to set the globally-dependent acceleration parameter γ.  
- Essential suggested revision: add a paragraph on challenges and possible distributed eigenvalue-estimation approaches for γ.  
- Optional suggested revision: discuss potential extensions of the acceleration principle to Min-Sum Splitting for more general optimization problems.
