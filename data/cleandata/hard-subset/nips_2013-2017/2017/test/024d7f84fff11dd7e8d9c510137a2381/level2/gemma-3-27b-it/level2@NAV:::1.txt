## Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem in distributed optimization. The authors demonstrate that, unlike the standard Min-Sum algorithm, a modified version (splitting) converges and, with a proper parameter tuning, achieves subdiffusive convergence rates comparable to shift-register methods. The core contribution lies in establishing these rates analytically and linking the algorithm to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods – highlighting a previously unobserved connection between these techniques.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a key challenge in distributed optimization: overcoming the diffusive convergence rates of classical algorithms. The authors clearly articulate the problem and position their work within the existing literature on consensus algorithms, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated optimization methods. The mathematical development is rigorous, and the auxiliary message-passing scheme introduced to analyze the algorithm is a clever approach. Proving the connection to shift-register methods and recovering known convergence rates is a significant achievement. The clarity of the presentation is commendable, especially considering the complexity of the topic. 

However, the paper could benefit from more illustrative examples. While the theoretical results are strong, a concrete illustration of how the splitting parameters are chosen in practice for a specific graph topology (e.g., cycle graph) would be valuable. The discussion of the limitations is brief. The authors don't delve into scenarios where this algorithm might *not* be advantageous (e.g., very sparse graphs or specific constraints on communication costs).  Additionally, the connection to ADMM mentioned in the conclusion could be expanded upon, potentially as future work, to further solidify the broader impact of this research. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel analytical results on convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting.
*   Establishment of a crucial link between Min-Sum, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated optimization methods.
*   Rigorous mathematical treatment and a clear presentation of complex concepts.
*   Addresses a significant problem in distributed optimization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of practical examples demonstrating parameter tuning.
*   Limited discussion of the algorithm's limitations.
*   Potential for further exploration of the relationship to related algorithms like ADMM.

**Overall:** Despite some minor shortcomings, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. The results are novel, the analysis is sound, and the connections made to other areas of research are insightful. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above. Specifically, adding a worked example and expanding the discussion of limitations would further strengthen the paper.
