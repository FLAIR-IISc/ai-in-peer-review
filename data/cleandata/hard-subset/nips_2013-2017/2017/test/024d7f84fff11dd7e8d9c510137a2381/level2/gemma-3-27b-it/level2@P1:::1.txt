This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors demonstrate that, unlike the standard Min-Sum algorithm, Min-Sum Splitting converges and achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence rates, matching those of shift-register methods. They establish a connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization, highlighting a unifying perspective on acceleration techniques for consensus.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant problem in distributed optimization – achieving faster convergence for consensus algorithms. The connection drawn between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods is a valuable contribution, potentially providing insights into the underlying principles driving acceleration. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, and the convergence rate established in Theorem 1 is a strong result. The paper is clearly written and logically organized, progressing from a general introduction to Min-Sum Splitting to the specific application of consensus and the derivation of convergence rates. The extensive related work section demonstrates a good understanding of the literature.

However, several areas could benefit from clarification and expansion. Firstly, while the theoretical result is strong, the practical implications are not fully explored.  How sensitive are the results to parameter tuning (particularly γ)? A discussion of the computational complexity of implementing Min-Sum Splitting compared to other accelerated methods (e.g., those based on lifted Markov chains) would be helpful. Secondly, the presentation of Algorithm 3, while useful for analysis, feels somewhat detached from the original Min-Sum Splitting algorithm (Algorithm 1/2). A more intuitive explanation of how Algorithm 3 arises as a simplification or reinterpretation of the original algorithm would improve clarity. Specifically, the transition from messages on *edges* in Algorithm 1/2 to variables on *nodes* in Algorithm 3 could be elaborated. Thirdly, the paper relies heavily on existing results regarding shift-register methods. While appropriately cited, briefly outlining the key ideas behind these methods within the context of the paper would make it more self-contained. Fourthly, the connection to ADMM (mentioned in the conclusion) seems promising but underdeveloped. Expanding on this connection, even briefly, would strengthen the paper’s broader impact. Finally, the phrase “global type of information” repeatedly used to describe the dependence on graph properties feels vague.  Being more specific about *what* constitutes this “global information” (e.g., spectral gap, diameter) would enhance the clarity.

**Strengths:**

*   Strong theoretical result establishing accelerated convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting.
*   Novel connection between Min-Sum Splitting and related acceleration techniques.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Comprehensive related work section.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited discussion of practical implications and computational complexity.
*   Algorithm 3’s derivation could be more intuitive.
*   Reliance on existing shift-register results without sufficient context.
*   Underexplored connection to ADMM.
*   Vague description of “global type of information.”

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of distributed optimization.
*   The convergence rate result is significant and advances the state of the art.
*   The connection to other acceleration techniques is insightful.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of discussion on practical considerations and computational complexity limits the paper's immediate impact.
*   Some aspects of the presentation could be improved for clarity and accessibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution and recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, adding a discussion of practical considerations, clarifying the derivation of Algorithm 3, and elaborating on the connection to ADMM would significantly enhance the paper’s value. The theoretical results are compelling and warrant publication at NIPS.
