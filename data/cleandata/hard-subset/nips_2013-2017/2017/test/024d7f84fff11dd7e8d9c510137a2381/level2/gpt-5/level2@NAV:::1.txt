This paper studies the consensus/averaging problem through Min-Sum message passing with “splitting” (Ruozzi & Tatikonda), and claims: (1) ordinary Min-Sum fails on loopy graphs whereas Min-Sum Splitting converges to the exact consensus solution; (2) a particular parameterization yields accelerated, subdiffusive rates matching shift-register (Chebyshev-type) methods, with convergence time O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori; (3) the acceleration mechanism connects to both lifted non-reversible Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods; (4) a new auxiliary node-based linear system enables rate analysis. The paper positions the work relative to Moallemi & Van Roy (convergence of Min-Sum), consensus via Markov chains and accelerated gossip (e.g., Boyd et al., Cao-Spielman-Yeh, Xiao-Boyd), lifted chains (Diaconis et al., Jung-Shah-Shin), and multi-step acceleration (Nesterov/Polyak; shift-register methods).

Quality: The technical core is a clean linearization for the quadratic case (Algorithm 2) and a node-level auxiliary process (Algorithm 3), reducing analysis to the spectral gap of a 2n×2n matrix K. Theorem 1 gives explicit rates and constants and correctly recovers classical Chebyshev acceleration. Assumptions and dependencies (e.g., γ as a function of ρ(W), initialization, positivity of quadratic coefficients) are explicit. However, support is purely theoretical; there are no empirical validations or robustness studies.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and precise, though notation is dense and the dependence on Γ, δ could be summarized more intuitively. The connection to prior accelerated methods is clearly articulated.

Originality: The auxiliary-process analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, and the explicit equivalence to shift-register dynamics for consensus, appear novel. The cross-connection to lifted chains and multi-step methods is insightful and timely.

Significance: For distributed optimization and probabilistic inference, showing that a BP-style protocol can achieve accelerated consensus rates is valuable. The scope is currently restricted to quadratic consensus and synchronous updates; extending beyond averaging would broaden impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- First rigorous rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting with clear acceleration.
- Conceptual bridges to lifted chains and multi-step optimization.
- Analysis holds for arbitrary graphs; rates match best-known linear-time methods up to logs.

Cons for acceptance:
- No experiments to validate constants, robustness, or practical performance.
- Requires global spectral information (ρ(W)) to tune γ; distributed estimation not addressed.
- Focused on quadratic objectives and synchronous setting; limited discussion of asynchrony or noise.

Suggestions:
- Add empirical results on cycles, grids, expanders, and random geometric graphs; compare to Metropolis, Chebyshev, heavy-ball, and ADMM.
- Study sensitivity to γ mis-specification and distributed estimation schemes for ρ(W).
- Discuss communication/memory overhead versus edge- vs node-based implementations and extensions to non-quadratic/constraint settings.
