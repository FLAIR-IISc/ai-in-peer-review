- Studies Min-Sum message-passing with a "Splitting" modification for distributed consensus, enabling convergence and accelerated rates.  
- Proves that proper parameter tuning yields subdiffusive convergence matching shift-register methods and connects to lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods.  
- Provides the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting applied to consensus problems.  
- Introduces an auxiliary process that tracks Min-Sum Splitting on graph nodes (rather than directed edges) to enable cleaner spectral analysis.  
- Theoretical analysis is technically sound with clear proofs and correct positioning relative to prior work (e.g., [27]).  
- Shows that splitting-induced directionality is analogous to non-reversible random walks and the memory term in multi-step methods.  
- Significant originality: new theoretical connection between Min-Sum and accelerated methods and demonstration that splitting parameters can be tuned for acceleration.  
- High significance: achieves subdiffusive convergence rates (O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori), a square-root improvement over diffusive methods.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and uses consistent mathematical notation.  
- Limitation: analysis focuses almost exclusively on the quadratic (network averaging) case, limiting applicability to non-quadratic consensus problems.  
- Practical limitation: optimal parameter tuning requires global graph information (spectral gap of W), which may be impractical in distributed settings.  
- Lacks empirical validation—no simulations comparing Min-Sum Splitting to shift-register or lifted Markov chain methods on concrete topologies.  
- Minor weakness: insufficient explanation why δ=1 and Γ=γW is optimal; parameter tuning discussion could be expanded.  
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical contribution with notable insights and recommendation for acceptance.
