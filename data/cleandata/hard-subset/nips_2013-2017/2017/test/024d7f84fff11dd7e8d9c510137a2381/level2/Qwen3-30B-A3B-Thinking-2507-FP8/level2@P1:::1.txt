

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum message-passing to solve the consensus problem in distributed optimization, introducing a modified version called Min-Sum Splitting that achieves accelerated convergence rates. The authors establish that while ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge on graphs with cycles, Min-Sum Splitting converges to the correct solution with a proper choice of tuning parameters, achieving subdiffusive convergence rates that match those of shift-register methods. The paper makes three main contributions: (1) it establishes the first connection between Min-Sum schemes and both lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization; (2) it provides the first proof of how directionality in Belief Propagation protocols can be tuned for acceleration; and (3) it introduces a novel proof technique using an auxiliary process to track convergence.

The paper is well-motivated and addresses a significant problem in distributed optimization. The consensus problem is fundamental to distributed computing, and the paper provides a theoretically sound approach to accelerating convergence beyond the standard diffusive rates. The authors carefully situate their work within the existing literature, noting that while Min-Sum has been previously applied to consensus (e.g., [27]), they are the first to establish convergence rates and connections to accelerated methods. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with Theorem 1 providing a clear characterization of the convergence behavior under proper parameter tuning.

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical contribution: the connection between Min-Sum Splitting and accelerated methods from two different fields. This is a novel insight that could inspire further research on combining message-passing algorithms with acceleration techniques. The proof technique using the auxiliary process (Algorithm 3) is elegant and provides a clear pathway to analyze convergence rates. The paper also makes a valuable contribution by showing that the acceleration mechanism in Min-Sum Splitting parallels those in lifted Markov chains (which depend on global graph properties like diameter) and multi-step methods (which depend on eigenvalues of the graph matrix).

Some aspects of the paper could be improved. The notation is dense in places, particularly in defining the matrices K̂(δ,Γ) and K(δ,Γ). A more intuitive explanation of why the specific parameter choice (δ = 1 and Γ = γW) achieves the optimal rate would strengthen the paper. The paper states that "the choice of γ that makes the Min-Sum scheme achieve a subdiffusive rate depends on global properties of the graph G" but does not elaborate on how this might be implemented in practice without requiring global knowledge of the graph (which is often unavailable in distributed settings). Additionally, while the paper is theoretically sound, a brief discussion of how these results might translate to practical implementations would be valuable.

The paper is well-written and organized, though some sections could benefit from more detailed explanations of the intuition behind the mathematical constructs. The connection to prior work is well-established, with appropriate citations to relevant literature on Min-Sum (e.g., [28, 34, 35]), consensus algorithms (e.g., [42, 41, 7]), and accelerated methods (e.g., [37, 13]). The authors clearly distinguish their contributions from existing work, particularly from [27] which investigated Min-Sum for consensus but did not establish convergence rates.

The significance of this work is high. Consensus algorithms are fundamental to distributed computing, and the paper provides a theoretically sound approach to accelerating convergence that matches the best-known rates for certain graph topologies. The results show that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve convergence times of O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori, which is a square-root improvement over diffusive methods. This represents a meaningful advance in the theoretical understanding of distributed optimization algorithms.

The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs provided in the supplementary material. The theoretical claims are well-supported, and the authors carefully address the limitations of their approach. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of distributed optimization and establishes a novel theoretical connection that could inspire future work.

Overall, this paper presents a significant theoretical advance in understanding how message-passing algorithms can be accelerated for distributed consensus. While the practical implementation challenges (such as the need for global graph knowledge to set parameters) are acknowledged, the theoretical contribution is substantial and well-executed. The paper would benefit from slightly clearer explanations of the parameter selection process and a brief discussion of practical implementation considerations, but these are minor points given the strength of the theoretical contribution.

The paper is appropriate for NIPS as it makes a clear scientific contribution to a fundamental problem in distributed optimization with well-motivated theoretical analysis. The results advance our understanding of how to accelerate distributed algorithms, which is of significant interest to the machine learning and optimization communities.
