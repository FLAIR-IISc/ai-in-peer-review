- Paper analyzes Min-Sum (belief propagation) for consensus, showing vanilla Min-Sum fails on loopy graphs but Min-Sum Splitting converges and can be accelerated.  
- Key technical idea: choose splitting parameters so edge-based iteration is tracked by a node-based auxiliary two-step linear system.  
- Min-Sum Splitting implements a multi-step (shift-register) iteration achieving accelerated “subdiffusive” rates matching best-known accelerated consensus on geometric graphs.  
- Proven square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods: O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori, near optimal Nesterov-type bounds.  
- Clarifies connections between belief-propagation with splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step first-order methods.  
- Novelty: first analysis of rates for Min-Sum Splitting, general graph-topology analysis via auxiliary system, and alignment with shift-register acceleration.  
- Strength: clear theoretical contribution with a general convergence-rate theorem and explicit parameter choice yielding ρK < ρW.  
- Strength: conceptual unification via K(δ, Γ) auxiliary system explaining convergence and acceleration mechanisms.  
- Strength: improved understanding over prior work [27], providing provable subdiffusive rates without relying on edge-based spectral gaps.  
- Strength: methodological novelty—reducing edge-based BP to a 2n-dimensional node-based linear system is elegant and broadly interesting.  
- Weakness: practical parameter selection requires γ depending on ρ(W − 11T/n), which is hard to estimate distributedly; lacks robust surrogates or adaptive methods.  
- Weakness: scope limited to quadratic objectives—unclear extension to general convex or composite settings.  
- Weakness: Algorithm 1 uses Γ inverses; when Γ has negative entries convexity and existence of minimizers need clarification outside quadratic case.  
- Weakness: no empirical validation; lacks numerical comparisons to Metropolis, heavy-ball/Nesterov/shift-register, lifted chains, ADMM, and consensus propagation on canonical graphs.  
- Quality: technical development is careful and internally consistent; reduction to node-based dynamics enables clean spectral analysis.  
- Clarity: paper is well organized and precise; could benefit from a small running example and intuitive derivation of the two-step system.  
- Originality and significance: high—novel rates for Min-Sum Splitting and important implications for distributed optimization and BP-style accelerations.  
- Essential suggestions: provide practical approaches to choose/approximate γ, clarify admissibility conditions for Γ, and add numerical experiments demonstrating the speedup.  
- Optional suggestions: discuss extensions beyond quadratics, asynchronous/stochastic updates, and provide implementation guidance for initialization and resource costs.  
- Recommendation: weak accept—strong theory and conceptual unification, but needs empirical validation and better parameter-selection guidance.
