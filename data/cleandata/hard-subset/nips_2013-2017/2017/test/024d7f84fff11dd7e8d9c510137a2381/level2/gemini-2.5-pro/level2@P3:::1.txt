This paper investigates the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, a message-passing protocol, for solving the distributed consensus problem. While the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm is known to fail on graphs with cycles, this work demonstrates that the Splitting variant converges to the correct solution. The central contribution is a rigorous analysis of the algorithm's convergence rate. It is shown that by appropriately tuning the splitting parameters, the algorithm achieves an accelerated, subdiffusive rate of convergence. This rate matches the performance of shift-register methods, offering a square-root improvement over classical diffusive consensus algorithms. A key conceptual insight of the paper is the connection it establishes between Min-Sum schemes and two other major families of accelerated distributed algorithms: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods from convex optimization. The analysis is facilitated by a novel proof technique that introduces an auxiliary linear process on the graph's nodes to track the evolution of the primary algorithm, which operates on the graph's directed edges.

**Quality**
The work is technically sound and presents a complete piece of research. The derivation of the Min-Sum Splitting dynamics for the quadratic consensus problem (Algorithm 2) is clear. The introduction of the auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) is a particularly effective analytical tool, simplifying the spectral analysis by moving from the 2m-dimensional edge space to a 2n-dimensional node space. This represents a significant simplification over the analytical approach in related work [27]. The main result, Theorem 1, convincingly establishes the accelerated convergence rate by connecting the algorithm's dynamics to the well-studied shift-register update matrix. The claims are well-supported by this theoretical analysis.

A potential weakness relates to the practical implementation of the accelerated scheme. The optimal choice of the parameter γ, crucial for acceleration, depends on ρ_W, a global spectral property of the graph. The paper correctly notes that this dependence on global information is shared by other accelerated methods, but it does not discuss how this parameter could be obtained in a distributed fashion. While solving this problem is likely beyond the scope of this paper, adding a brief discussion on the challenges and potential approaches for estimating this parameter would enhance the paper's practical relevance and completeness.

**Clarity**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides an excellent and comprehensive review of the consensus problem, clearly situating the work within the existing literature on both standard and accelerated methods. The logical flow from the general Min-Sum Splitting algorithm to its specific application for consensus, and finally to the auxiliary process for analysis, is easy to follow. The statement of propositions and the main theorem is precise. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the setup and the results, though the full proofs are appropriately deferred to supplementary material.

**Originality**
The paper demonstrates high originality on several fronts. It provides the first convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, addressing an open question from prior work [34, 35]. The most novel contribution is the conceptual bridge built between message-passing in graphical models, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order optimization. This connection provides a new lens through which to understand acceleration in distributed systems and is a significant intellectual contribution. The work clearly distinguishes itself from previous Min-Sum-based approaches to consensus [27] by providing a more general analysis, applicable to any graph topology, and demonstrating superior convergence rates. Related work is thoroughly cited and discussed.

**Significance**
The results are highly significant. By demonstrating that a belief-propagation-style algorithm can achieve acceleration competitive with state-of-the-art methods, the paper is likely to stimulate new research at the intersection of graphical models and distributed optimization. The finding that the "splitting" reparameterization can be tuned to induce acceleration is an important insight that could potentially be generalized to other problems beyond consensus. The paper advances the state-of-the-art for Min-Sum-based consensus algorithms and provides a competitive alternative to other accelerated schemes. The insights and the analytical framework are likely to be built upon by other researchers in the field.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses**

*Arguments for acceptance:*
*   Establishes a novel and insightful connection between Min-Sum/BP, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated optimization methods.
*   Provides the first rigorous convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.
*   Demonstrates that the algorithm achieves an accelerated rate, which is a significant improvement over standard methods and prior related work.
*   The paper is very well-written, clear, and places the contributions in excellent context.

*Arguments against acceptance:*
*   The practical issue of setting a globally-dependent parameter for acceleration is not addressed.

**Suggested Revisions**
The strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. The primary weakness could be addressed with a minor revision.
*   *Essential:* Include a paragraph discussing the challenge of setting the parameter γ in a distributed manner. This could involve citing literature on distributed eigenvalue estimation or acknowledging it as a necessary prerequisite, similar to other accelerated methods.
*   *Optional:* A brief discussion on the potential for extending this acceleration principle to Min-Sum Splitting for more general optimization problems beyond quadratic consensus would be a welcome addition to the conclusions.
