Summary
This paper studies Min-Sum (Belief Propagation) with Splitting for the distributed consensus/averaging problem. It shows: (i) ordinary Min-Sum can diverge on loopy graphs for consensus, but Min-Sum Splitting converges; (ii) with a specific tuning of the splitting parameters, the method achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence rates, matching shift-register (Chebyshev/second-order) methods; (iii) the analysis proceeds by introducing an auxiliary linear process on nodes that tracks the message-passing process on directed edges, enabling a spectral analysis tied to a chosen symmetric matrix W. The work draws conceptual connections to both lifted nonreversible Markov chains (directionality on edges) and multi-step first-order methods (memory on nodes). For cycles and tori, the method achieves O(D log(D/ε)) iterations, a square-root improvement over classical diffusive consensus.

Relation to prior work
- Prior consensus work via random walks and symmetric linear iterations (Boyd et al., Xiao & Boyd, etc.) achieves diffusive rates; multi-step methods (heavy-ball, Nesterov, shift-register) accelerate using spectral information. The paper recovers the shift-register rate by appropriate splitting choices.
- Prior Min-Sum for consensus (Moallemi & Van Roy; Moallemi & Van Roy 2006—consensus propagation; and [27]) showed ordinary Min-Sum can fail and analyzed a soft-constraint variant on regular graphs with performance tied to an edge-space chain; rates were only fully characterized on cycles. This paper generalizes to arbitrary graphs and improves rates.
- Splitting/reparametrization of BP (Ruozzi & Tatikonda) had convergence results for Gaussian/quadratic cases but, to my knowledge, no established rate results; this paper fills that gap for consensus.
- Connections to lifted chains (Diaconis et al.; Jung-Shah-Shin; Li-Dai-Zhang) and to accelerated optimization (Ghadimi-Shames-Johansson) are explained and used for intuition.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The main results are precise and grounded in linear/spectral analysis. Propositions 1–2 rigorously connect the edge-based Min-Sum Splitting to the node-based auxiliary system. Theorem 1 provides a concrete parameter choice (δ=1, Γ=γW with γ depending on ρ(W−11^T/n)) and a nonasymptotic bound in terms of ‖(K−K∞)^t‖, with asymptotic rate ρK strictly better than ρW. The rates match known shift-register results.
- Assumptions: W must be symmetric with W1=1 and ρ(W−11^T/n)<1. The initialization R̂0=r̂0=0 is used; positivity conditions ensuring the beliefs are well-defined are discussed. The requirement that γ depends on global spectral information is standard for accelerated methods but does impact practicality.
- Scope: Results are for quadratic consensus (averaging). The links to generic convex consensus are qualitative; formal extensions are left open.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The presentation of Algorithms 1–3, along with the role of δ and Γ, is helpful. The auxiliary process is a key conceptual contribution and is explained clearly.
- Minor suggestions: Make explicit earlier that δ=1 with Γ≠A still corresponds to a splitting (ordinary Min-Sum is only δ=1, Γ=A). Clarify the positivity condition ensuring xt is well-defined under R̂0=r̂0=0. A small illustrative example (e.g., a path/cycle) would help intuition.

Originality
- First rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, and a novel auxiliary-node process to analyze an edge-space message passing scheme.
- Conceptual bridge between BP Splitting, liftings, and multi-step acceleration is original and insightful.

Significance
- The results strengthen understanding of BP/Splitting by providing both convergence and accelerated rates on an important primitive (consensus). They show Min-Sum schemes can be competitive with best-known first-order accelerations, potentially informing future BP design beyond consensus.
- Practical impact is tempered by the need for global spectral information and the restriction to quadratic objectives in the current theory, but the methodological insight is valuable.

Pros
- Clear, rigorous derivation of accelerated rates for Min-Sum Splitting on arbitrary graphs.
- Elegant auxiliary process converting edge-space BP analysis into a node-space 2n-dimensional linear system tied to Γ and W.
- Establishes concrete parameter choice achieving shift-register rates; square-root improvement over diffusive consensus on graphs with geometry.
- Thoughtful connections to liftings and accelerated optimization that may catalyze further research.

Cons / Questions for authors
- Practicality: The optimal γ requires knowledge of ρW. Can the method tolerate misspecification? Empirically, how sensitive is performance to γ? Are there adaptive/distributed schemes to estimate or upper bound ρW?
- Experiments: The paper lacks empirical validation. Simple studies on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs comparing to Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, Nesterov, and ADMM would strengthen the case and illuminate constants/pre-asymptotics.
- Role of δ: The main theorem uses δ=1. Conceptually, splitting is motivated by δ and Γ. Is δ≠1 beneficial for consensus? Can it further improve constants or robustness?
- Complexity/overhead: The edge-based Algorithm 2 uses O(m) state, whereas node-based shift-register methods use O(n). Can Algorithm 3 be implemented directly (node-based) without maintaining edge messages, thereby removing the extra overhead?
- Assumptions and generality: The analysis assumes symmetric W and undirected graphs. Is there a path to directed/asymmetric or time-varying graphs? How about non-quadratic φv (e.g., strongly convex, smooth) where BP is still meaningful?
- Formal link to liftings: Beyond the qualitative analogy, can you formalize when K̂ corresponds to a nonreversible lifted chain with provable mixing improvements?

Suggestions
- Add experiments quantifying convergence rates and constant factors; include sensitivity to γ and comparisons to competing accelerated methods.
- Discuss distributed ways to choose W and γ (e.g., Chebyshev polynomial approximations using bounds on the spectrum, or conservative γ based on degree bounds).
- Clarify the positivity condition for beliefs with the proposed initialization, and provide a short lemma guaranteeing it under the theorem’s parameters.
- Expand on whether the auxiliary node-based updates can be used as the actual implementation to reduce memory/communication.

Recommendation
A strong, well-executed theoretical contribution with clear novelty and solid relevance to distributed optimization and message passing. I lean to accept, and I encourage the authors to add empirical validation and clarify practical parameter selection to broaden impact.
