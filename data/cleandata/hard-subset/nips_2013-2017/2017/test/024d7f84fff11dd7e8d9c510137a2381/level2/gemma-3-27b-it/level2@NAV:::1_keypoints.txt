- Studies convergence rates of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus in distributed optimization.
- Shows Min-Sum Splitting (with proper parameter tuning) converges and achieves subdiffusive rates comparable to shift-register methods.
- Provides analytical derivation of these rates and connects the algorithm to lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods.
- Introduces an auxiliary message-passing scheme as a clever analytical tool.
- Mathematical development is rigorous and the presentation is clear despite topic complexity.
- Recovers known convergence rates and proves a previously unobserved connection between different acceleration techniques.
- Lacks concrete illustrative examples showing how to choose splitting parameters for specific graph topologies (e.g., cycle).
- Discussion of limitations is brief and does not cover cases where the algorithm may be disadvantageous (very sparse graphs, communication-cost constraints).
- Mentions a connection to ADMM in the conclusion but does not develop or substantiate this link.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel analytical results, important theoretical linkages, rigorous treatment, and relevance to distributed optimization.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient practical guidance on parameter tuning, limited limitations discussion, and underexplored relation to related algorithms like ADMM.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to add a worked example and expand the discussion of limitations and related algorithmic links.
