- Paper studies distributed consensus (network averaging) using Min-Sum Splitting, showing standard Min-Sum fails on general graphs but Splitting can converge with proper parameters and achieve accelerated "subdiffusive" rates.

- Main contribution 1: first convergence rate analysis for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

- Main contribution 2: with specific parameter tuning, Min-Sum Splitting attains accelerated convergence O(D log(D/ε)), a square-root improvement over diffusive methods.

- Main contribution 3: establishes a novel conceptual link between belief propagation (Min-Sum), accelerated first-order methods (multi-step/momentum), and lifted Markov chain techniques.

- Technical insight: the splitting parameters introduce memory/directionality akin to mechanisms used for acceleration in other fields.

- Key proof technique: introduction of an auxiliary node-based message-passing scheme that reduces analysis from a 2m-dimensional edge system to a 2n-dimensional node system.

- Theoretical strength: clear, rigorous main result (Theorem 1) relating accelerated rate to spectral properties of the communication matrix.

- Presentation strength: paper is well-written, logically organized, and situates results within prior work.

- Weakness 1: accelerated parameter γ depends on global spectral quantity ρ_W, requiring global graph information.

- Weakness 2: paper lacks empirical validation; no simulations or experiments to illustrate the theoretical speedup.

- Weakness 3: analysis is limited to network averaging with quadratic objectives, leaving generalization to broader convex problems unaddressed.

- Arguments for acceptance: novel interdisciplinary connection, first formal convergence rate for Min-Sum Splitting, strong accelerated theoretical result, and a reusable analysis technique.

- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on global graph information for parameter tuning and absence of experimental results.

- Conclusion: reviewer recommends acceptance, noting strengths outweigh weaknesses though experiments and discussion of global-parameter practicality would improve the paper.
