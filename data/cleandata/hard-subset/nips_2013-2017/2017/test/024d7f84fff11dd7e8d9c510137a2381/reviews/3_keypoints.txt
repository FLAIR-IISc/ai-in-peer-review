- Paper proposes an accelerated Min-Sum Splitting message-passing algorithm for distributed consensus optimization.  
- Authors use reparameterization from Ruozzi & Tatikonda (2013) and analyze convergence on quadratic consensus problems.  
- Main technical tool is an auxiliary linear process that tracks the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.  
- Main contributions: new proof technique via an auxiliary process, improved convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, and connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step convex optimization methods.  
- Manuscript is generally clear and well-written, but contains several typos and grammatical errors throughout.  
- Proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 and Theorem 1 appear correct.  
- Several specific typos/wording issues are listed (e.g., "theirs neighbors" → "their neighbors", "double-stochastic" → "doubly-stochastic", "casted" → "cast", etc.).  
- Minor technical comments: possible misuse of "Laplacian"/"Laplace multipliers" vs. "Lagrangian"/"Lagrange multipliers" on lines 220–221.  
- Algorithm descriptions are incomplete: some variables (e.g., R{vw}, r{vw}) are not explained and initializations (e.g., \hat{R}^0, \hat{r}^0) are missing.  
- The authors should explicitly identify which variables correspond to the auxiliary linear process in Algorithm 3.  
- Paper omits relevant literature on (sub)gradient and decentralized optimization (suggested refs: Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis 1989; Srinivasan et al. 2009; Wei Shi 2015) and classical acceleration work (Polyak, Nesterov).  
- Key questions for authors: comment on practicality and when to use this method versus other distributed consensus methods.  
- Request clarification on limitations of Min-Sum Splitting and intuition behind using the auxiliary process.  
- Ask whether the framework can be extended beyond quadratic objectives to more general consensus problems common in machine learning.  
- Authors should state more clearly why the approach is of interest to the machine learning community.  
- Overall summary: theoretically interesting work establishing convergence rates and links to known methods, but lacking practical discussion, intuition, and clearer algorithmic exposition.
