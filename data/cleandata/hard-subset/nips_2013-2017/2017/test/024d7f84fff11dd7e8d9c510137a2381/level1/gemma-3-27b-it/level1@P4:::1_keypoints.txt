- Paper analyzes convergence rates of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus, showing it converges and attains accelerated (shift-register-like) rates.  
- Establishes connections between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step first-order methods.  
- Builds on prior Min-Sum and distributed consensus literature and positions itself within accelerated methods research.  
- Novelty: first rigorous convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting with a square-root improvement over diffusive methods.  
- Technical soundness: rigorous spectral analysis using an auxiliary message-passing scheme; proofs provided in supplement.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written, organized, and explains links to existing techniques clearly.  
- Significance: results are potentially impactful for large-scale distributed optimization and inspire new algorithm designs.  
- Complexity: paper is dense and assumes strong background in distributed optimization and message-passing.  
- Limited experiments: purely theoretical work with no empirical validation on datasets or network topologies.  
- Initialization dependence: convergence analysis relies on specific initial conditions; sensitivity to initialization is not discussed.  
- ADMM connection: mentions a link to ADMM but does not explore it sufficiently.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically rigorous, significant rate improvements, and valuable connections to established methods.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of empirical validation and potential inaccessibility due to complexity.  
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical contribution warranting acceptance.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revision to discuss initialization sensitivity and expand on the ADMM connection.
