**Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"**

**Summary**

This paper investigates the use of the Min-Sum message-passing algorithm for the distributed consensus problem, specifically network averaging. The authors first acknowledge that the standard Min-Sum algorithm, a form of Belief Propagation, fails to converge on graphs with cycles, as shown in prior work [27]. They then apply a modified version, Min-Sum Splitting [34], which introduces reparametrization through tuning parameters `δ` and `Γ`.

The core contributions are both analytical and conceptual. First, the authors show that for the quadratic objective of network averaging, the Min-Sum Splitting updates become a linear dynamical system on the directed edges of the graph. To analyze this system, they introduce a novel and elegant "auxiliary message-passing scheme" which tracks the evolution of the node beliefs directly. This transforms the problem from analyzing a system of size 2m (edges) into a more tractable 2n (nodes) system with memory. Using this auxiliary system, they prove their main result (Theorem 1): a specific choice of parameters (`δ=1`, `Γ=γW`) makes the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm's dynamics identical to those of accelerated shift-register methods [4, 45]. Consequently, the algorithm achieves a "subdiffusive" convergence rate, providing a square-root improvement over standard diffusive consensus methods. For graphs like cycles and tori, this improves the convergence time from O(D²) to O(D). Conceptually, the paper forges a powerful connection between Min-Sum/Belief Propagation, lifted Markov chain techniques (viewing the edge-based process as a lifted system), and accelerated first-order methods in convex optimization.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength is its novel unification of three distinct research areas: message-passing on graphical models, lifted Markov chains for accelerated mixing, and momentum-based accelerated optimization. Demonstrating that a reparametrization trick within a standard message-passing framework can implicitly implement momentum and achieve acceleration is a highly significant and insightful contribution. This opens up a new perspective on designing fast distributed algorithms and may inspire similar acceleration techniques for other graphical model inference tasks beyond consensus.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical work is sound and rigorous. The introduction of the auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) is a clever analytical device that greatly simplifies the convergence analysis. Instead of analyzing the complex dynamics on the edge graph, as was attempted in [27], the authors map the problem to a well-understood multi-step linear system on the nodes. This allows them to directly leverage existing spectral analysis of shift-register matrices to establish their strong convergence rate results. The proofs appear correct and the derivation is clear.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides excellent background on diffusive consensus, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step methods, making the work accessible to a broad audience. The logical flow—from the general Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, to its specialization for consensus, to the auxiliary scheme, and finally to the main theorem—is easy to follow. The contributions are clearly articulated.

4.  **Advancement over Prior Work:** This work represents a clear advance over the most relevant prior work [27]. While [27] first explored Min-Sum for consensus, its analysis was limited to regular graphs, with rigorous results only for cycles and conjectures for other topologies. This paper provides a complete analysis for any graph topology (for which a suitable `W` matrix exists) and establishes a faster, provably accelerated convergence rate.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Practicality of Parameter Choice:** The main result in Theorem 1 hinges on setting `Γ = γW` with `γ = 2/(1 + sqrt(1-ρ_W²))`. The parameter `ρ_W`, which is the spectral gap of the base consensus matrix `W`, is a global property of the graph. Computing it exactly typically requires centralized computation, which contradicts the distributed nature of the algorithm. While the authors correctly note that this reliance on global information is a common feature of accelerated methods, the paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the practical implications. For example, how sensitive is the algorithm to a suboptimal `γ`? Could `γ` be estimated in a distributed fashion?

2.  **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is performed for the specific case of network averaging, where the local objective functions `φ_v` are quadratic. This allows the message updates to remain quadratic and the dynamics to be linear. The paper's title and introduction suggest broader applicability. A discussion on the challenges of extending this analysis to more general convex functions `φ_v` would be valuable. The linear analysis is the core of the proof, and it is unclear if any of the acceleration guarantees would hold in a non-linear setting.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contribution is strong enough to stand on its own, the inclusion of even simple numerical experiments would have significantly enhanced the paper's impact. A plot comparing the convergence of Min-Sum Splitting against a standard diffusive method (e.g., using `WMH`) and the method from [27] on a cycle or torus graph would have provided a clear and compelling illustration of the proven speedup.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel and highly significant conceptual link between Belief Propagation and accelerated optimization methods.
*   Presents the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, a promising but previously unanalyzed algorithm.
*   The technical approach, using an auxiliary process on the nodes, is elegant and powerful.
*   The result is strong: it proves that a message-passing scheme can achieve state-of-the-art acceleration for consensus, matching known multi-step methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The practical implementation is challenging due to the need for global spectral information to set the parameters.
*   The analysis is confined to the quadratic case, and generalizability is not discussed.
*   The theoretical results are not supported by any empirical evidence.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

The paper makes a fundamental and elegant contribution by connecting the worlds of message-passing and accelerated optimization. The technical quality is high, and the insights are likely to have a lasting impact on how researchers think about designing and analyzing distributed algorithms. While the practical aspects of parameter selection and generalization to other problems are important open questions, the core theoretical and conceptual advance presented here is a substantial contribution that is highly suitable for NIPS.
