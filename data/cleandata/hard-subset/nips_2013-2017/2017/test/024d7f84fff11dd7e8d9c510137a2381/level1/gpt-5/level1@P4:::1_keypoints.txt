- Studies consensus via Min-Sum Splitting: ordinary Min-Sum can fail on loopy graphs, but Min-Sum Splitting converges for consensus.  
- For quadratic objectives, a parametrization yields linear directed-edge message passing (Alg.2) and an equivalent 2n node-memory linear system (Alg.3).  
- The auxiliary node-space system reduces spectral analysis to a graph-supported matrix Γ, simplifying over the 2m edge-space dynamics of prior work.  
- With δ=1 and Γ=γW (γ=2/(1+√(1−ρW^2))), the induced matrix K matches shift-register methods, yielding a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusion.  
- For cycles and tori this gives O(D log(D/ε)) versus diffusive O(D^2 log(1/ε)).  
- Connects Min-Sum Splitting to lifted (non-reversible) Markov chains and to multi-step/momentum acceleration methods.  
- Novel contributions: first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, and the auxiliary-process bridge between edge-space BP and node-space multi-step iterations.  
- Technical soundness: derivations and spectral analysis (Propositions, Theorem 1) appear correct and appropriately referenced.  
- Clearly stated assumptions/limitations: quadratic objectives, synchronous updates, symmetric W, known ρW, and specific initialization.  
- Main technical limitation: requires global spectral knowledge (ρW) to set γ; robustness to misspecification is unaddressed.  
- Clarity: paper is well organized but notation is dense; would benefit from small illustrative examples and clearer δ vs. Γ contrasts.  
- Significance: shows BP-style protocols can achieve accelerated/subdiffusive consensus rates and offers a potentially generalizable analysis technique.  
- Weaknesses/suggestions: add distributed tuning strategies for γ, extend beyond quadratic/synchronous settings, include empirical comparisons, clarify initialization and positivity conditions, and report per-iteration costs.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel rigorous analysis, insightful conceptual connections, and strong spectral results with near-optimal rates on geometric graphs.  
- Arguments against acceptance: dependence on global tuning, limited scope and lack of experiments, and no demonstrated practical advantage over existing accelerated methods.  
- Overall: technically sound and novel; would be strengthened by experiments and discussion of distributed parameter selection.
