Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies the classical consensus (network averaging) problem through the lens of Min-Sum (a.k.a. Belief Propagation) with “splitting” [34, 35]. The authors show:

- Ordinary Min-Sum can fail on loopy graphs for consensus (consistent with [27, 28]).
- A reparametrized Min-Sum variant, Min-Sum Splitting (Algorithm 1), converges for consensus.
- For quadratic objectives (averaging), a particular parametrization yields a linear message-passing scheme on directed edges (Algorithm 2), and an equivalent auxiliary linear process on nodes with memory (Algorithm 3). The auxiliary 2n-dimensional linear system depends spectrally on a graph-supported matrix Γ, simplifying analysis compared to the 2m edge-space dynamics used in [27].
- With δ=1 and Γ=γW for a symmetric W with spectral gap 1−ρW and γ=2/(1+√(1−ρW^2)), the induced 2n×2n matrix K matches that of shift-register (second-order) consensus methods [45, 4, 24]. The resulting rate ρK = √((1−√(1−ρW^2))/(1+√(1−ρW^2))) leads to a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods based on W, i.e., O(1/(1−ρK) log(1/ε)) ≈ O(√(1/(1−ρW)) log(1/ε)). For cycles and tori, this yields O(D log(D/ε)) vs. diffusive O(D^2 log(1/ε)) [33].
- The paper connects Min-Sum Splitting to (i) lifted Markov chains, where directionality in a lifted non-reversible chain improves mixing [8, 5, 37], and (ii) multi-step first-order methods (heavy-ball, Nesterov, shift-register) that leverage memory [13, 24]. Here the splitting induces directionality on edges that becomes a momentum-like node memory in the auxiliary system.

Relative to prior Min-Sum-based consensus [27], which used soft constraints and analyzed a 2m-dimensional edge process (with explicit results only for cycles and conjectures for tori), this work gives general spectral-rate guarantees for all graphs via a node-space auxiliary system. Relative to the broader consensus literature (e.g., Metropolis and optimal W [44], lifted chains [18, 37], and accelerated first-order methods [13, 24, 45]), the novelty is not faster asymptotics per se but establishing that Min-Sum Splitting can realize the same accelerated rates and clarifying the structural connection.

Quality (technical soundness):
- The derivation from Min-Sum Splitting to linear quadratic messages (Proposition 1) is standard for Gaussian/quadratic potentials and appears correct.
- The construction of the auxiliary node-space system (Algorithm 3, Proposition 2) is the main technical device enabling spectral analysis in terms of Γ, with a clean reduction to the known shift-register K when δ=1 and Γ=γW.
- Theorem 1 states convergence to the average with explicit rate and norm bounds, relying on spectral properties of K and established analysis of second-degree methods [15, 24]. The asymptotic complexity claims for cycles and tori follow from known eigenvalue scalings [33, 1].
- Assumptions and limitations are clearly stated: quadratic objectives, synchronous updates, symmetric W with known ρW, and specific initialization (R̂0=r̂0=0 in Theorem 1). The positivity condition in Proposition 1 is noted.
- One technical limitation is reliance on global knowledge (ρW) to set γ, as in lifted chains and accelerated methods; robustness to misspecification is not analyzed.

Clarity:
- The paper is well organized, with clear separation between edge-space (Algorithm 2) and node-space (Algorithm 3) views. The high-level connections to lifting and multi-step methods are well motivated.
- Notation is dense; readability could improve by adding small illustrative examples (e.g., a path or cycle) and summarizing the role of δ vs. Γ. It may help to explicitly contrast δ=1, Γ=A (ordinary Min-Sum) with δ=1, Γ=γW (accelerated splitting) to avoid confusion that δ=1 still yields a distinct “split” via Γ.

Originality:
- First convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting in consensus.
- New auxiliary-process analysis linking edge-space BP with node-space multi-step linear iterations.
- Conceptual bridge between BP splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated multi-step methods.

Significance:
- Establishes that a canonical message-passing algorithm (Min-Sum with splitting) can achieve subdiffusive consensus rates comparable to shift-register methods, potentially increasing the algorithmic toolbox for distributed optimization/inference based on BP-style protocols.
- The analysis methodology (auxiliary node-space reduction) may generalize to other objectives or constraints.
- Practical impact is tempered by the need for global spectral information and the focus on quadratic objectives; nevertheless, the conceptual connections are valuable to both the BP and consensus communities.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Global tuning: The required γ depends on ρW. Discuss how to choose γ in a distributed manner (e.g., via conservative bounds, spectral gap estimation, or adaptive schedules), and quantify rate degradation under misspecification.
- Scope: Results are for quadratic objectives and synchronous updates. Comment on extensions to general convex φv, asynchronous/asymmetric settings, time-varying graphs, and robustness to communication noise or delays.
- Empirical validation: Include simulations comparing Min-Sum Splitting to Metropolis, optimal W, lifted chains, and state-of-the-art multi-step methods across representative graphs (cycle, grid, expander, random geometric), to confirm constants and pre-asymptotic behavior.
- Initialization: Theorem 1 uses R̂0=r̂0=0; clarify behavior and recommended initialization more generally, and ensure positivity conditions in Proposition 1 are easy to check/maintain locally.
- Complexity: Provide per-iteration communication/storage costs relative to standard consensus and accelerated schemes; emphasize that in the quadratic case messages reduce to scalars per directed edge.

Arguments for acceptance:
- First rigorous convergence-rate analysis of Min-Sum Splitting for consensus with general graphs.
- Clear and insightful connection between BP splitting, lifted chains, and multi-step acceleration.
- Solid spectral analysis yielding near-optimal O(D log(D/ε)) rates on geometric graphs.
- Potentially reusable analytical technique (auxiliary node-space reduction).

Arguments against acceptance:
- Dependence on global spectral information for parameter tuning without a distributed selection strategy.
- Results limited to quadratic objectives and synchronous settings; lack of empirical validation.
- Acceleration matches known shift-register rates rather than surpassing state of the art; practical advantage over existing accelerated consensus methods is not demonstrated.

Overall, the paper makes a technically sound and novel contribution by bringing accelerated rates to Min-Sum Splitting and elucidating its connection to established acceleration paradigms. Adding experimental evidence and a discussion of distributed parameter selection would strengthen the work.
