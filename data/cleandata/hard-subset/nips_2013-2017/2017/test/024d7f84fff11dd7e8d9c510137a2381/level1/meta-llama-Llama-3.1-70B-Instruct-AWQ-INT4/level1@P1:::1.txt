This paper introduces the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, a variant of the Min-Sum message-passing protocol, to solve the consensus problem in distributed optimization. The authors show that while the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm does not converge, the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm yields convergence to the problem solution with subdiffusive accelerated convergence rates.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm and its application to the consensus problem. The authors also provide a thorough review of the related work, including classical algorithms for consensus and accelerated methods.

The main contribution of the paper is the proof of convergence and the analysis of the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm. The authors show that the algorithm achieves a square-root improvement over diffusive methods, with a convergence time that scales like O(D log(D/ε)) for cycles and tori, where D is the graph diameter.

The authors also establish a connection between the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm and lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization. This connection is interesting and provides new insights into the acceleration mechanism of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise presentation of the results. The proofs are relegated to the supplementary material, which is a good decision given the complexity of the proofs.

Overall, the paper is a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization and provides new insights into the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm and its application to the consensus problem.
