- Paper studies Min-Sum Splitting (a generalized message-passing) applied to distributed consensus, focusing on quadratic network averaging.
- Standard linear consensus algorithms converge diffusively (O(D²)), while accelerated methods achieve subdiffusive rates (O(D)); ordinary Min-Sum fails on loopy graphs.
- Core claim: Min-Sum Splitting can be tuned to converge and achieve accelerated rates for quadratic consensus.
- Contribution 1: Shows Min-Sum Splitting updates reduce to a linear iterative process on directed edges for the quadratic case.
- Contribution 2: Introduces an auxiliary node-based message-passing scheme equivalent to the edge-based algorithm, simplifying analysis.
- Contribution 3: Proves (Theorem 1) that a specific splitting parameter choice recovers shift-register dynamics, yielding O(D log(D/ε)) convergence on cycles/tori.
- Conceptual contribution: Connects Min-Sum framework to lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order accelerated methods.
- Strength — originality/significance: Novel and significant bridge between belief-propagation-style algorithms and accelerated optimization mechanisms (momentum).
- Strength — technical quality: Sound technical development; effective use of auxiliary process and spectral analysis to prove acceleration.
- Strength — clarity: Well-written, well-organized, with clear literature context and presentation.
- Weakness — parameter practicality: Required splitting parameter depends on global spectral info (ρ_W); no distributed estimation or tuning strategy discussed.
- Weakness — limited scope: Analysis restricted to quadratic objectives; extension to general convex problems is non-trivial and not addressed.
- Weakness — suboptimal log factor: Achieved O(D log(D/ε)) has an extra log(D) versus optimal O(D log(1/ε)); unclear if artifact or fundamental.
- Summary evaluation: Technically sound, original, clear, and significant; provides first convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting.
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel theoretical link, first convergence analysis for Min-Sum Splitting, elegant auxiliary-process technique, and strong square-root improvement.
- Arguments against acceptance: Practical tuning requires global information; analysis limited to quadratic consensus only.
