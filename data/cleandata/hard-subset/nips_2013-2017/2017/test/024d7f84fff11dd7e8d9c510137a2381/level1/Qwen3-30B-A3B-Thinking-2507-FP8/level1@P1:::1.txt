

# Peer Review: "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum message-passing to the consensus problem in distributed optimization. The authors demonstrate that while ordinary Min-Sum may fail to converge for graphs with cycles, a modified version called Min-Sum Splitting (introduced in [34]) converges to the problem solution. They establish that with proper parameter tuning, Min-Sum Splitting achieves subdiffusive convergence rates (O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle and torus graphs) that match shift-register methods, representing a square-root improvement over the diffusive rates (O(D² log(1/ε))) of classical consensus algorithms.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes the first rigorous convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting applied to the consensus problem, which is a notable gap in the literature. Second, it reveals novel connections between Min-Sum message-passing, lifted Markov chain techniques (used in accelerated Markov chain methods), and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization. The authors show that Min-Sum Splitting can be viewed as a process on a lifted space (directed edges) and as a multi-step method on the original graph, bridging these three seemingly disparate research areas. Third, they introduce a novel proof technique using an auxiliary process on the graph nodes to analyze the convergence behavior, which is more tractable than analyzing the dynamics directly on the directed edges as done in [27].

The paper is well-written, logically organized, and thoroughly contextualizes its contributions within the literature. It carefully distinguishes its work from prior results, particularly [27], which established convergence for specific graph topologies but left others as conjectures. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their current analysis (e.g., focus on quadratic consensus problem) and provide clear conditions under which their results hold.

The quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical analysis is technically sound with rigorous proofs, and the results are well-supported. The paper is careful to address both strengths and limitations of the approach. The clarity is excellent, with precise mathematical notation and well-structured exposition that would allow an expert reader to reproduce the key results.

The originality is high. While Min-Sum message-passing has been used in distributed optimization before, the specific application to consensus with convergence rate analysis is novel. The connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step optimization methods represent a significant conceptual advance that has not been previously established in the literature.

The significance of this work is substantial. The improved convergence rates could have practical implications for distributed systems where communication is expensive, such as sensor networks, distributed machine learning, and multi-agent coordination. The theoretical insights about acceleration mechanisms provide a foundation for future work on generalizing the approach to other problems. The connections established between different research areas make the work more impactful for the broader NIPS community.

However, the paper has some limitations. First, it focuses exclusively on the quadratic consensus problem (network averaging) and does not address more general consensus problems or non-quadratic objective functions. It's unclear how the results would extend to more complex distributed optimization problems commonly encountered in machine learning. Second, while the theoretical convergence rates are impressive, the paper lacks empirical validation to demonstrate the practical benefits of Min-Sum Splitting on real graph topologies. Third, the parameter tuning required for optimal performance (which depends on global information like ρW) might be challenging to implement in large-scale distributed systems where global information is difficult to obtain. The paper also doesn't provide a detailed comparison of computational complexity or communication overhead compared to other accelerated methods.

Despite these limitations, the theoretical contribution is strong and the connections established are valuable. The paper makes a clear advance in understanding distributed optimization algorithms and their convergence properties. The results would be particularly valuable for researchers working on distributed optimization, consensus algorithms, and message-passing methods.

In summary, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of distributed optimization. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes significant connections between different research areas. While the scope is limited to the quadratic consensus problem, the theoretical insights are substantial and have potential for broader application. The lack of empirical validation is a minor drawback, but the theoretical results are compelling enough to warrant acceptance.

Arguments for acceptance:
- First rigorous convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting in consensus
- Novel connections between Min-Sum, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step optimization
- Significant theoretical improvement over classical methods (square-root speedup)
- Well-written, clear exposition with careful attention to technical details

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited scope to quadratic consensus problem
- No empirical validation of theoretical results
- Parameter tuning requires global information, potentially challenging in practice

I recommend acceptance to NIPS 2017. The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution that would be of interest to the distributed optimization and machine learning communities. The connections established between different areas of research are particularly valuable for advancing the field.
