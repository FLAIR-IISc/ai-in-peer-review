Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the Min-Sum (a.k.a. max-product / Gaussian BP for quadratics) message-passing protocol for distributed optimization in the specific setting of consensus/averaging. It shows: (i) ordinary Min-Sum may diverge on loopy graphs (matching prior observations), (ii) the “Splitting” variant (Ruozzi & Tatikonda) guarantees convergence for consensus, and (iii) with a careful choice of tunable parameters (δ, Γ), it achieves accelerated, subdiffusive rates comparable to shift-register/Chebyshev-type multi-step methods. The authors develop two analytical devices: a linear dynamics on directed edges and an auxiliary linear two-step process on nodes that captures the algorithm’s output; the latter ties convergence to spectral properties of a chosen matrix Γ, enabling a clean rate analysis. The main theorem sets δ=1 and Γ=γW for a symmetric W with W1=1 and selects γ via the spectrum of W; the resulting lifted two-step matrix K matches the shift-register update and yields a square-root improvement in asymptotic convergence time over diffusion (O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori).

The work sits at the intersection of belief propagation (BP), accelerated averaging/gossip, and nonreversible/lifted Markov chains. Prior work has: (a) characterized when Gaussian BP converges (scaled diagonal dominance) and observed divergence in loopy graphs; (b) applied Min-Sum-like ideas to consensus with soft constraints and linked rates to edge-space chains (Moallemi & Van Roy, “Consensus Propagation” and follow-ups); (c) developed accelerated consensus via Chebyshev/shift-register/Nesterov heavy-ball (Xiao & Boyd; Cao-Spielman-Yeh; Ghadimi et al.) and via lifted nonreversible walks (Diaconis-Holmes-Neal; Chen-Lovász-Pak; Jung-Shah-Shin). This paper’s novelty is to show Min-Sum Splitting can be tuned to recover the same accelerated rates as shift-register methods and to highlight conceptual connections between BP directionality, lifted nonreversible walks, and multi-step acceleration.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The reduction from edge-based Min-Sum Splitting (Algorithm 2) to the node-based auxiliary two-step process (Algorithm 3) is well motivated and appears correct. The choice δ=1, Γ=γW, and the spectral analysis of K−K∞ directly leverage known Chebyshev/second-degree polynomial acceleration results; the rate expression matches the classical √-improvement. Proofs are deferred to the supplement, but the statements are precise, with appropriate conditions (W symmetric, ρ(W−11^T/n)<1, specific initialization).
- Completeness and honesty: The paper clearly states that acceleration requires global spectral information (γ depends on ρW), and that the main theorem is for quadratic objectives (averaging). It also acknowledges that ordinary Min-Sum fails on cycles and contrasts with prior Min-Sum-based consensus [27].
- Limitations: (i) Results are restricted to the quadratic averaging case; no guarantees for general convex φv or constrained variants beyond equality constraints. (ii) The accelerated tuning depends on global spectral quantities, which can be nontrivial to estimate in a decentralized way; the paper does not discuss distributed parameter selection or robustness to mis-specification. (iii) Empirical validation is absent; no numerical experiments compare constants or transient behavior versus Chebyshev/Nesterov/ADMM/lifted-walk methods. (iv) Synchronous updates and specific initializations are assumed; robustness to asynchrony, noise, or link failures is not addressed.

Clarity:
The paper is well organized: Min-Sum Splitting is introduced, consensus and diffusive baselines reviewed, accelerated methods summarized, followed by specialization to consensus, the auxiliary process, and the main theorem. The exposition of K̂ and K is dense but precise; making the intuition behind the auxiliary reparameterization more explicit (e.g., a brief derivation in the main text) would improve readability. A small worked example (cycle) or a figure contrasting edge- vs node-space dynamics would help. References are extensive and relevant.

Originality:
- First convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting in consensus with explicit accelerated rates.
- New conceptual bridge: the same splitting that enforces directionality in BP corresponds to nonreversibility in lifted chains and to momentum/shift-register terms in multi-step methods; the constructed auxiliary process makes this link concrete.
- While the achieved rate matches known shift-register performance rather than surpassing it, porting this acceleration into the BP/Min-Sum framework and proving it rigorously appears novel.

Significance:
- Scientific impact: Clarifies when and how Min-Sum Splitting can be a principled and competitive consensus method, enriching our understanding of message-passing algorithms’ acceleration mechanisms. The auxiliary analysis technique may be reusable beyond consensus.
- Practical impact: For averaging, practitioners already have simple node-based accelerated schemes with the same asymptotic rate. The added value here is conceptual unification and potential portability to other BP-suitable problems (e.g., certain structured quadratics), but the paper does not yet demonstrate such extensions.

Pros:
- Clear, rigorous main theorem with explicit parameterization and rates.
- Elegant auxiliary-node process that simplifies spectral analysis.
- Insightful connections to lifted Markov chains and multi-step acceleration.
- Improves over prior Min-Sum-based consensus analyses that were topology-limited.

Cons:
- Scope limited to quadratic averaging; no experiments.
- Requires global spectral information; no discussion of distributed tuning or robustness.
- Synchronous, exact setting; no treatment of asynchrony/stochasticity.
- Gains over known node-based accelerated algorithms are conceptual rather than algorithmic.

Suggestions/questions for authors:
- Provide numerical experiments on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs comparing transient and asymptotic constants to Chebyshev, heavy-ball/Nesterov, and lifted-walk methods.
- Discuss distributed estimation or safe over-approximation of γ (e.g., via bounds on ρW from local degrees), and sensitivity to γ mis-tuning.
- Clarify initialization requirements (R̂0, r̂0) and the positivity condition; are there simple default initializations that always work?
- Comment on extensions: can the auxiliary-process approach handle general strongly convex φv or ADMM-style penalties? Any insights for nonquadratic objectives?
- Consider asynchronous variants or robustness to communication noise.

Overall recommendation: Weak Accept. The paper is technically solid, original in its analysis of Min-Sum Splitting and the unifying perspective it offers, and relevant to NIPS interests in distributed optimization and message passing. The absence of experiments and the narrow scope (averaging) limit immediate practical impact, but the conceptual and analytical contributions warrant acceptance. Confidence: high.
