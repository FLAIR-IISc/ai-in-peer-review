- Studies Min-Sum Splitting (generalized Min-Sum/BP) for distributed consensus, focusing on network averaging with quadratic objectives.  
- Notes standard Min-Sum fails to converge on graphs with cycles (cites Moallemi & Van Roy 2010).  
- Main contribution: Min-Sum Splitting with parameters δ and Γ guarantees convergence and can be tuned for accelerated rates.  
- Shows for δ=1 and Γ ∝ W the algorithm is equivalent to shift-register methods, yielding subdiffusive rate O(D log(D/ε)).  
- Demonstrates a square-root improvement over diffusive methods (O(D² log(1/ε))) on graphs like cycles and tori.  
- Conceptual contribution: connects Min-Sum Splitting to lifted Markov chains and multi-step (momentum) optimization methods.  
- Argues splitting parameters embed directionality and memory akin to mechanisms used for acceleration in other methods.  
- Technical innovation: introduces an auxiliary node-based linear process that simplifies spectral analysis of edge-based message updates.  
- Strength — significance: elevates Min-Sum relevance by matching state-of-the-art accelerated consensus rates.  
- Strength — originality: novel cross-field insight unifying graphical models, Markov chain lifting, and accelerated optimization.  
- Strength — technical quality: clear derivations (Proposition 1), auxiliary scheme (Proposition 2/Alg 3), and rigorous main result (Theorem 1).  
- Strength — clarity: well-written, organized, and logically presented.  
- Weakness — practicality: required γ depends on global spectral property ρ_W; no distributed estimation/tuning strategy provided.  
- Weakness — scope: analysis restricted to quadratic (network averaging) case; generalization to nonquadratic convex problems is not addressed.  
- Weakness — comparison: lacks quantitative comparisons (constants) with other accelerated methods like Nesterov or ADMM.  
- Recommendation: accept, but authors should add discussion on practical/distributed strategies for setting γ and challenges in generalizing beyond quadratics.
