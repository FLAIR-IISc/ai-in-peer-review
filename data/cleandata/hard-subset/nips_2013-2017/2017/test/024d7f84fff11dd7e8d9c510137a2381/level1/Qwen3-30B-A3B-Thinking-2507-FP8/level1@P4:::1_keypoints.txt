- Paper studies Min-Sum message-passing for consensus and shows Min-Sum Splitting converges on graphs with cycles.  
- With proper parameter tuning, Min-Sum Splitting achieves subdiffusive (square-root) convergence rates matching shift-register methods.  
- Establishes novel connections between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step first-order methods.  
- Builds on prior Min-Sum and consensus literature, extending results that ordinary Min-Sum fails on cyclic graphs.  
- Main contributions: connections to other acceleration techniques, tunable directionality for acceleration, convergence-rate analysis, and a faster Min-Sum consensus protocol.  
- Theoretical analysis is technically sound and uses an auxiliary process to track algorithm evolution.  
- Proves O(D log(D/ε)) convergence on cycles and tori, improving over diffusive O(D² log(1/ε)) rates.  
- Acknowledges need for global information to tune parameters, consistent with other accelerated methods.  
- Paper is well-organized, clearly written, and uses consistent, well-defined notation.  
- Novelty: first to link Min-Sum to lifted chains and multi-step methods and first convergence-rate proof for Min-Sum Splitting.  
- Significance: provides a practically meaningful square-root speedup for consensus, relevant to large-scale distributed systems.  
- Strengths: rigorous theory, clear connections to prior work, well-supported main theorems, and comparative roadmap versus previous methods.  
- Weaknesses: full proofs relegated to supplementary material, limited discussion of practical large-scale implementation details.  
- Weaknesses (cont.): does not explore generalization to non-quadratic objectives or more complex constraints, limiting broader applicability.  
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution, well-motivated and of interest to the community, with clear advances in distributed optimization.
