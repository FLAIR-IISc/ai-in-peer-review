This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The core contribution is establishing subdiffusive convergence rates, specifically showing a square-root improvement over diffusive methods and achieving rates comparable to shift-register methods. The paper cleverly connects Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, providing a broader perspective on the algorithm’s behavior.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a compelling theoretical analysis. The proof strategy, utilizing an auxiliary message-passing scheme, is insightful and allows for a clear link to existing results on shift-register methods. The authors are careful to state assumptions and limitations, and acknowledge connections to prior work. However, the paper is dense and the theoretical derivations could benefit from further simplification or more intuitive explanations. The claim of establishing *the first* convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting is somewhat overstated; while a complete, rigorous analysis to this extent is novel, related work (e.g., [27]) explores convergence under specific conditions. The connection to Nesterov's acceleration, while mentioned, isn't deeply explored. While the authors rigorously prove convergence, a discussion of the practical implications of the parameters (δ and Γ) and potential sensitivities would be valuable.  Is finding an appropriate γW (as defined in Theorem 1) computationally expensive?

**Clarity:** The writing is generally clear, but the mathematical notation is heavy and can be difficult to follow without careful attention. The organization is logical, building from the general Min-Sum algorithm to the specific application to consensus and then the detailed analysis.  However, the numerous cross-references to equations and propositions within the dense mathematical arguments disrupt the flow.  The connection between the "splitting" and its effect on the message-passing dynamics isn’t fully intuitive, despite the explanation in Section 2. A visual aid illustrating the splitting process might be helpful.  The numerous references, while comprehensive, sometimes feel overwhelming and interrupt the narrative. A more concise explanation of relevant prior work *within* each section would enhance readability.

**Originality:** The application of Min-Sum Splitting to consensus, and especially the rigorous analysis of its convergence rates, is a novel contribution. Connecting Min-Sum Splitting to lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods provides a fresh perspective and adds to the originality. The auxiliary process approach is a clever technique for analyzing the algorithm's behavior. However, the idea of accelerating consensus algorithms is well-explored (see Section 4), and the paper’s originality lies more in the specific technique and analysis than in the fundamental problem itself. The relationship to shift register methods is known, so the novelty is in framing Min-Sum Splitting *as* a shift-register-like method.

**Significance:** The results are significant because they demonstrate that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve faster convergence rates than traditional diffusive methods for consensus. This is particularly relevant for large-scale distributed optimization problems, where communication costs are a major bottleneck. The connection to existing acceleration techniques (lifted Markov chains, Nesterov’s acceleration) suggests that Min-Sum Splitting may have broader applicability and could inspire new algorithms for other distributed optimization problems. The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for future research in this area. However, the practicality of the algorithm and the computational cost of parameter tuning remain open questions that limit the immediate impact.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Rigorous theoretical analysis of Min-Sum Splitting’s convergence rates.
*   Novel connection to lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods.
*   Demonstrated subdiffusive convergence rates, a significant improvement over traditional methods.
*   Well-defined auxiliary process for analysis.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Dense mathematical presentation can be challenging for readers.
*   Limited discussion of practical implications and parameter tuning.
*   The claim of “first” convergence rate analysis is slightly overstated.
*   The connection to Nesterov’s acceleration is superficial.




**Overall Assessment:**  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a strong theoretical contribution to the field of distributed optimization. Addressing the clarity concerns, particularly simplifying the mathematical presentation and providing more intuition behind the splitting process, would significantly enhance the paper’s impact. A brief discussion of practical considerations and potential challenges would also be valuable. The technical soundness and novelty of the work outweigh the minor shortcomings in clarity and presentation.
