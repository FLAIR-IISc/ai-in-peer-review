This paper investigates accelerated convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The core idea is to demonstrate that, unlike the standard Min-Sum algorithm which can fail to converge, a modified version (Splitting) *does* converge and, crucially, achieves subdiffusive convergence rates comparable to shift-register methods. The authors relate this acceleration to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization, providing a unifying perspective on these different acceleration techniques.

The paper is well-motivated. The consensus problem is a fundamental primitive in distributed optimization and machine learning, and achieving faster convergence rates is a significant goal. The connection to existing work on lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods is clearly articulated, placing the contribution within a broader context. The authors thoroughly review relevant literature, including [27], which presents a related Min-Sum approach, and highlight the novelty of their theoretical convergence rate analysis.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The theoretical analysis establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting is a significant contribution. Prior work (e.g., [27]) either didn't analyze rates or left them as conjectures.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical analysis, proving a square-root improvement in convergence time over diffusive methods (Theorem 1). The use of an auxiliary message-passing scheme (Algorithm 3) is a clever technique to simplify the analysis and relate it to known results on shift-register methods.
* **Connections to Existing Work:**  The authors successfully draw parallels between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated first-order methods, offering a unifying perspective on acceleration techniques.  This demonstrates a deep understanding of the field.
* **Clarity (mostly):** While dense, the paper is generally well-organized.  The presentation of Algorithms 2 and 3 is clear. The discussion connecting the results to previous work is also well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity (occasionally):**  The paper is *very* dense and relies heavily on mathematical notation. While technically sound, some sections (especially around the introduction of K and K∞ in Section 7) could benefit from more intuitive explanations or examples.  Readers unfamiliar with the specific literature on Markov chain lifting or shift-register methods might struggle.
* **Practical Implications:** The paper focuses heavily on theoretical convergence rates. It would be beneficial to briefly discuss the practical implications of these results. For instance, how sensitive is the performance to the choice of the parameter γ? What are the computational costs associated with implementing the algorithm?
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks any empirical results. While theoretical guarantees are valuable, it would strengthen the contribution to include some simulations demonstrating the improved convergence rate in practice, even on small-scale examples.
* **Repetitive Introduction:** The introduction is quite lengthy and repetitive, reiterating the contributions multiple times. It could be more concise.



**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound analysis of Min-Sum Splitting, establishing accelerated convergence rates for the consensus problem.
* The connections to related work on lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods are insightful and contribute to a broader understanding of the field.
* The analysis is rigorous and the proofs appear to be correct (although a full verification is beyond the scope of this review).

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

* The paper is dense and potentially inaccessible to readers without a strong background in the relevant areas.
* The lack of empirical validation limits the practical impact of the results.
* The lengthy and repetitive introduction could be streamlined.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses related to clarity and the lack of empirical validation, I believe this paper represents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The novel analysis of Min-Sum Splitting and the connections to other acceleration techniques are valuable insights. The theoretical guarantees are strong, and the paper addresses a challenging problem in a rigorous manner.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity and potentially include some preliminary empirical results. Specifically, I suggest adding a section briefly discussing the computational cost of the algorithm and offering some intuition behind the parameter γ's sensitivity.  A slightly more concise introduction would also be beneficial.
