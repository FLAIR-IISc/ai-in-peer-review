Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the Min-Sum (a.k.a. belief propagation for MAP) algorithm for distributed optimization when applied to the consensus/averaging problem. It shows that ordinary Min-Sum may diverge on loopy graphs (consistent with known results), but a reparameterized “Min-Sum Splitting” variant guarantees convergence. In the quadratic consensus case, the authors prove that a particular choice of splitting parameters yields an accelerated, subdiffusive convergence rate that matches the spectral rates of shift-register (second-order, Chebyshev-like) consensus methods. Methodologically, the paper shows: (i) Min-Sum Splitting reduces to a linear edge-based iteration (Algorithm 2) for quadratic consensus; (ii) via a reparametrization, one can track the node estimates with a two-step linear node-based system (Algorithm 3), whose matrix K can be analyzed spectrally; (iii) with δ=1 and Γ=γW, where W is a symmetric averaging matrix and γ depends on ρ(W−11^T/n), the convergence factor becomes ρK=√((1−√(1−ρW^2))/(1+√(1−ρW^2))), giving an O(√) improvement in 1/(1−ρ) and thus O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori. The paper positions these results relative to: consensus propagation [Moallemi–Van Roy], lifted Markov chains (nonreversible acceleration), and multi-step first-order/shift-register methods (Xiao–Boyd; Golub–Varga; Ghadimi et al.), and discusses connections to ADMM-based acceleration.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The reductions from Min-Sum Splitting to a linear edge process (Prop. 1) and then to a node-space two-step process (Prop. 2) are clear and plausible for quadratic objectives; the main theorem’s rate matches known shift-register/Chebyshev rates and is consistent with polynomial filtering literature. The requirement W symmetric, W1=1, and Γ=γW respects sparsity and allows spectral analysis.
- Assumptions and limitations: Acceleration relies on global spectral information (γ depends on ρW), as in Chebyshev/shift-register methods; this is a known tradeoff but should be discussed more in terms of distributed implementability and robustness. The analysis is limited to quadratic consensus; extensions to general (strongly) convex separable φv are not covered. Synchronous updates and static graphs are assumed; no results for asynchrony, delays, or time-varying topologies.
- Completeness: The theoretical contribution is self-contained conceptually, though key proofs are in the supplement. It would be helpful to include finite-time (non-asymptotic) bounds with explicit constants and to state conditions ensuring denominators R_t remain positive (Prop. 1/Alg. 3) under the chosen initialization.
- Empirics: There are no experiments. While not strictly necessary for a theory paper, small simulations on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs could validate constants and compare to Metropolis, Chebyshev-accelerated consensus, and consensus propagation.

Clarity
The paper is dense but generally well-organized. The two algorithmic forms (edge-space and node-space) are clearly stated. Suggestions:
- Explain intuition for splitting parameters: how δ and Γ inject directionality and memory, and why δ=1 suffices in the main theorem.
- Clarify implementability: since Algorithm 3 is node-based, emphasize that one can implement the accelerated scheme without passing edge messages in the quadratic case; specify communication/memory costs per iteration.
- Define all symbols where first used (e.g., make explicit the condition guaranteeing R_t>0 for Algorithm 3 outputs).
- Fix minor formatting glitches in the text and references.

Originality
The core novelty is to show that Min-Sum Splitting, properly tuned, reproduces the same accelerated rates as shift-register methods for consensus, and to formalize two bridges: (i) to lifted/nonreversible Markov chain acceleration via edge-space directionality; (ii) to multi-step optimization methods via a node-space two-step linear recurrence. While the achieved rate is known in other frameworks, obtaining it via a principled Min-Sum reparameterization and providing a spectral analysis through an auxiliary process appears original. The comparison to consensus propagation [Moallemi–Van Roy] and to Ruozzi–Tatikonda’s splitting is appropriate.

Significance
For researchers using message passing, the result is interesting: it shows that BP-style algorithms can be tuned to achieve near-optimal consensus rates without explicit graph lifting, and elucidates the mechanism (directionality/memory) behind acceleration. Practically, the reliance on global spectral information limits plug-and-play use; nonetheless, the conceptual link may inform new adaptive schemes or extensions to other distributed optimization problems. The rate O(D log(D/ε)) is close to the linear-in-diameter optimal O(D log(1/ε)) achieved by recent accelerated consensus methods (e.g., Olshevsky), so the contribution is competitive but not state-of-the-art in complexity.

Pros
- Clear theoretical contribution: a spectral-rate guarantee for Min-Sum Splitting matching shift-register acceleration.
- Novel conceptual links between Min-Sum, nonreversible lifting, and multi-step methods.
- Clean linear-algebraic analysis via a node-based auxiliary system.
- Applicable on arbitrary graphs (not limited to regular graphs), with explicit tuning.

Cons
- Acceleration requires global spectral knowledge; no robustness analysis to mis-specified γ or distributed estimation procedures.
- Results limited to quadratic consensus; no guarantees for general convex φv or constrained variants.
- Synchronous, static-graph assumption; no treatment of asynchrony or time-variation.
- No empirical validation; constants in bounds may be loose (dependence on n in the pre-factor).

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- How sensitive is the rate to mis-tuning γ? Can you provide bounds when γ is chosen using upper/lower spectral bounds (as in Chebyshev methods), or via simple distributed power iterations?
- Can Algorithm 3 be implemented directly in a fully distributed manner with two scalars per node, and what are the exact per-iteration communication/storage costs versus standard accelerated consensus?
- Do your techniques extend to strongly convex separable φv beyond quadratics (e.g., via local quadratic approximations or message linearization), and can one obtain comparable rates?
- Can you formalize the lifting connection further by characterizing nonreversibility and steady-state of the edge process, and relate its spectral gap to that of K?
- Provide small experiments to compare against Metropolis, Chebyshev-accelerated consensus, and consensus propagation on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs.

Overall recommendation
A solid theoretical contribution that will interest the distributed optimization and message-passing communities. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying implementability, discussing tuning/robustness, and adding brief empirical illustrations.
