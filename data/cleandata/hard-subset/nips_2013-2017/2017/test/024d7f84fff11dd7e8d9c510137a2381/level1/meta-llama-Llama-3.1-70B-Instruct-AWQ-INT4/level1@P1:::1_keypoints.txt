- Introduces Min-Sum Splitting, a variant of Min-Sum message-passing, for distributed consensus and proves its convergence.  
- Shows subdiffusive accelerated convergence with square-root improvement over diffusive methods; e.g., O(D log(D/ε)) scaling on cycles and tori.  
- Establishes a novel connection between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step first-order convex optimization methods.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a concise introduction and related-work review.  
- Main contribution is rigorous convergence proof and convergence-rate analysis for the algorithm.  
- Proofs are placed in supplementary material (not included), making them hard to verify from the main paper.  
- Analysis assumes a quadratic objective, limiting applicability to more general problems.  
- No empirical or numerical comparison with other algorithms is provided, hindering practical performance assessment.  
- Strength: proposes a new algorithm specifically for distributed consensus.  
- Strength: provides a thorough and novel theoretical analysis of convergence rates.  
- Strength: draws insightful connections to other acceleration techniques and Markov chain lifting.  
- Weakness: restrictive quadratic objective assumption that may not hold in many applications.  
- Weakness: absence of algorithmic comparisons or experiments to validate practical benefits.  
- Weakness: supplementary proofs not included in the submission text.  
- Originality: presents a new algorithm and novel theoretical perspective linking different acceleration frameworks.  
- Significance: contributes important theoretical insights to distributed optimization and consensus acceleration.  
- Clarity: exposition, related work, and presentation of results are clear and concise.  
- Quality: analysis appears solid and the theoretical contribution is substantial.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel algorithm, rigorous rate analysis, and new cross-field connections.  
- Arguments for rejection: limited to quadratic objectives, lacks empirical comparisons, and missing supplementary proofs.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions; request inclusion of comparisons with other algorithms and full supplementary material.
