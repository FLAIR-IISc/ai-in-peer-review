- Core contribution: proves subdiffusive convergence for Min-Sum Splitting, showing a square-root improvement over diffusive methods and rates comparable to shift-register methods.
- Conceptual advance: connects Min-Sum Splitting to lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, offering a broader perspective on algorithm behavior.
- Technical soundness: rigorous theoretical analysis with a clever auxiliary message-passing proof strategy that links to existing shift-register results.
- Proof clarity: derivations are dense and could benefit from simplification or more intuitive explanations.
- Overstated novelty: the claim of being the “first” convergence rate analysis is somewhat exaggerated given related prior work (e.g., [27]) under specific conditions.
- Incomplete connection: the link to Nesterov-style acceleration is mentioned but not deeply explored.
- Practicality concerns: lacks discussion on practical implications and sensitivity of parameters (δ and Γ), and whether computing γW from Theorem 1 is computationally expensive.
- Notational and organizational issues: heavy mathematical notation, frequent cross-references, and dense arguments disrupt readability.
- Explanatory aids needed: the splitting mechanism’s effect on message-passing isn’t made intuitive; a visual aid would help.
- Originality nuance: novel framing and analysis of Min-Sum Splitting, but acceleration of consensus is a well-explored area; novelty mainly in technique and perspective.
- Significance: results are important for faster consensus in large-scale distributed optimization but immediate practical impact is limited by tuning and computational concerns.
- Acceptance arguments: rigorous convergence analysis, novel theoretical connections, demonstrated subdiffusive rates, and a well-defined auxiliary process.
- Rejection arguments: dense presentation, limited practical guidance on parameter tuning, slightly overstated novelty claim, and superficial treatment of Nesterov connections.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to improve clarity, add intuition/visuals, and discuss practical parameter selection and computational costs.
