This paper investigates the application of the Min-Sum Splitting message-passing algorithm to the distributed consensus problem. The authors first summarize that standard Min-Sum fails to converge on general graphs, while previous modified Min-Sum approaches [27] offered limited analysis and suboptimal rates. The central contribution of this work is to demonstrate that Min-Sum Splitting not only ensures convergence but, with a proper choice of its tuning parameters, achieves an accelerated, subdiffusive convergence rate. This rate matches those of established shift-register methods. Crucially, the paper provides a novel conceptual bridge, connecting Min-Sum schemes with two previously separate lines of research on accelerated consensus: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods from convex optimization.

The paper has several significant strengths and some weaknesses that could be addressed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength is the novel connection established between the "splitting" parameters in a message-passing algorithm and the acceleration mechanisms in lifted Markov chains and momentum-based optimization methods. This is a significant conceptual contribution that provides a new lens through which to view and design accelerated distributed algorithms.
*   **Quality:** The technical work appears sound. The introduction of an auxiliary message-passing scheme on the nodes to analyze the dynamics of the primary algorithm on the edges is an elegant and effective proof technique. This allows for a clear spectral analysis (Theorem 1) that establishes a square-root improvement in convergence time over standard diffusive methods for important graph classes like tori, advancing the state-of-the-art for Min-Sum-based consensus.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It clearly situates the work within the existing literature, explicitly states its contributions, and builds its argument logically from the algorithm's definition to the final convergence theorem.

**Arguments against acceptance (or for improvement):**
*   **Practicality of Assumptions:** The primary weakness is that the proposed acceleration relies on setting the tuning parameter γ based on ρW, the spectral radius of the underlying consensus matrix W. This is a global property of the graph. The paper does not discuss how this global information could be obtained or estimated in a distributed fashion, which somewhat undermines the practical applicability of the proposed distributed protocol.
*   **Limited Scope:** The theoretical analysis is confined to the least-squares network averaging problem. While this is a foundational problem, the paper would be stronger if it included a discussion on the potential for extending this acceleration framework to more general convex consensus problems, which is a natural setting for Min-Sum algorithms.
