

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum message-passing to solve the consensus problem in distributed optimization, introducing a modified version called Min-Sum Splitting that achieves accelerated convergence rates. The authors demonstrate that while ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge on graphs with cycles, Min-Sum Splitting converges to the correct solution with subdiffusive rates matching shift-register methods. The paper establishes novel connections between Min-Sum Splitting and two established acceleration techniques: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization. This builds on prior work by [27], which showed ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge for consensus on cyclic graphs, and on [34] which introduced the splitting technique for Gaussian Belief Propagation.

The paper's strengths are significant. It provides the first rigorous analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, introducing a novel proof technique using an auxiliary process to track the algorithm's evolution. The theoretical connections to existing accelerated methods are well-established and insightful, demonstrating how the directionality introduced by splitting parallels both lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods. The paper is technically sound with well-structured proofs, and the problem is clearly motivated by practical applications in distributed systems.

Weaknesses include limited empirical validation of the theoretical results. While the paper provides theoretical convergence rates, it lacks numerical experiments demonstrating the practical performance gains over standard diffusive methods. The paper also doesn't thoroughly explore the sensitivity of the convergence rates to parameter choices beyond the theoretically optimal setting.

Quality: Strong theoretical foundation with complete proofs (4/5)
Clarity: Well-organized and clearly written (4/5)
Originality: Significant new connections between three distinct areas (5/5)
Significance: Addresses an important practical problem with meaningful acceleration (4/5)

Pro: First rigorous analysis of Min-Sum Splitting convergence rates; novel theoretical connections to established acceleration techniques; well-structured proofs.
Con: Limited empirical validation; parameter sensitivity analysis could be expanded.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by establishing theoretical foundations for accelerating distributed consensus algorithms through Min-Sum message-passing, with clear connections to existing methods. While empirical validation would strengthen the work, the theoretical advances are substantial and likely to inspire further research on generalizing this acceleration technique to other distributed optimization problems.
