

# Peer Review: Accelerated Consensus via Min-Sum Splitting

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum message-passing to solve the consensus problem in distributed optimization. The authors establish that while ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge for graphs with cycles, a modified version called Min-Sum Splitting achieves convergence with accelerated rates. They prove that proper parameter tuning yields subdiffusive convergence rates (O(D log(D/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori), matching the rates of shift-register methods and approaching the optimal rate O(D log(1/ε)).

The paper makes four key contributions: (1) establishing the first connection between Min-Sum schemes and both lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step methods in convex optimization; (2) proving how directionality in Belief Propagation can be tuned to accelerate convergence; (3) providing the first convergence rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting; and (4) designing a Min-Sum protocol that outperforms previous Min-Sum methods for consensus.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a thorough review of related work. The authors effectively contrast their approach with classical diffusive algorithms (which scale as O(D² log(1/ε))) and two main accelerated approaches: lifted Markov chains (which require constructing lifted graphs) and multi-step first-order methods (which use momentum terms). The proof technique using an auxiliary process to track convergence behavior is novel and elegantly connects the edge-based Min-Sum Splitting dynamics to node-based multi-step dynamics.

The theoretical analysis is strong, with careful mathematical formulations and a clear connection between the spectral properties of the graph and the convergence rate. Theorem 1 is particularly significant as it demonstrates how Min-Sum Splitting achieves the same acceleration as shift-register methods through a parameter choice that depends on global graph properties (ρ_W). The paper appropriately acknowledges limitations, noting that the parameter choice requires global knowledge of the graph.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses. First, the analysis is limited to quadratic objectives (network averaging), and it's unclear how the results extend to more general consensus problems with non-quadratic objectives. Second, the parameter tuning (γ = 2/(1 + √(1 - ρ²_W))) requires knowledge of the graph's spectral properties, which is impractical in many distributed settings where such global information is unavailable. Third, the paper lacks empirical validation comparing Min-Sum Splitting against other accelerated methods across various graph topologies. While the theoretical rates are promising, the practical implementation challenges and computational overhead of the method are not addressed.

The paper's claim of being the "first connection" between Min-Sum and other accelerated methods appears valid based on the comprehensive literature review. However, the relationship to distributed ADMM (mentioned in Remark 1) is underdeveloped and could be a significant avenue for future work.

In terms of significance, the results are important as they demonstrate that Min-Sum message-passing can achieve near-optimal convergence rates without requiring lifted graphs (unlike Markov chain lifting techniques) or specialized momentum terms (unlike multi-step methods). This provides a unified perspective on accelerated consensus methods and suggests potential for broader application.

The main weaknesses are the limited scope to quadratic problems and the lack of empirical validation. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the practical relevance would be enhanced by demonstrating how the method performs in real distributed systems, particularly addressing the global information requirement for parameter tuning.

For NIPS 2017, this paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to distributed optimization. The connection between Min-Sum message-passing and accelerated consensus methods is novel, and the convergence rate analysis is rigorous. However, the limited scope of the analysis and lack of empirical validation prevent it from being groundbreaking. The paper would benefit from expanding to non-quadratic problems and including experimental results.

Strengths: Novel theoretical connection between Min-Sum and accelerated methods, rigorous convergence rate analysis, elegant auxiliary process proof technique.

Weaknesses: Limited to quadratic objectives, requires global graph information for parameter tuning, lacks empirical validation, does not address implementation challenges.

The paper advances the state of the art in distributed optimization by establishing a new theoretical framework for accelerated consensus, though practical implementation considerations and broader applicability remain to be demonstrated. It represents a valuable contribution to the field that should be accepted with minor revisions to address the scope limitations.
