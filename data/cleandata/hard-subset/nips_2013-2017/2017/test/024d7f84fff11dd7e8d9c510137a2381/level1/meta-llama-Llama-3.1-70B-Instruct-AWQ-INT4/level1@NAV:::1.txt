The paper "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting" presents a novel approach to solving the consensus problem in distributed optimization using the Min-Sum message-passing protocol. The authors show that a modified version of the Min-Sum algorithm, known as Splitting, yields convergence to the problem solution and achieves accelerated convergence rates.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and understand the contributions of the work. The authors provide a thorough review of previous work on consensus algorithms and Min-Sum schemes, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches and the need for accelerated methods.

The main contribution of the paper is the introduction of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, which is shown to achieve subdiffusive convergence rates, matching the rates obtained by shift-register methods. The authors provide a rigorous analysis of the algorithm's convergence behavior, including a proof of the asymptotic rate of convergence and a bound on the convergence time.

The paper also explores the connection between Min-Sum Splitting and other accelerated methods, such as lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first-order methods. The authors show that the acceleration mechanism exploited by Min-Sum Splitting is analogous to the acceleration mechanism of these other methods, and that the choice of splitting parameters that yields subdiffusive rates depends on global information about the graph.

The paper's strengths include:

* A clear and concise presentation of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm and its analysis
* A thorough review of previous work on consensus algorithms and Min-Sum schemes
* A rigorous analysis of the algorithm's convergence behavior, including a proof of the asymptotic rate of convergence and a bound on the convergence time
* An exploration of the connection between Min-Sum Splitting and other accelerated methods

The paper's weaknesses include:

* The analysis is limited to the case of quadratic objective functions and may not generalize to other types of objective functions
* The paper does not provide experimental results to validate the theoretical analysis
* The connection between Min-Sum Splitting and other accelerated methods is not fully explored and may require further investigation

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization and consensus algorithms. The Min-Sum Splitting algorithm has the potential to be a valuable tool for solving large-scale optimization problems in a distributed setting.
