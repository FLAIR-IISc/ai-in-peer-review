This paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for distributed consensus/averaging. The authors show that ordinary Min-Sum (Gaussian BP) fails on loopy graphs for consensus [27], but a reparameterized “Splitting” variant [34,35] converges. For quadratic consensus, they derive a linear message-passing scheme on directed edges (Algorithm 2) and, crucially, an auxiliary node-based, two-step linear system (Algorithm 3) that tracks the output. With a specific parameter choice δ=1 and Γ=γW, where W is a symmetric averaging matrix and γ=2/(1+√(1−ρW²)), they prove accelerated convergence with rate governed by the second eigenvalue of a shift-register matrix K, matching classical Chebyshev/shift-register acceleration [15,24,45]. This yields a square-root improvement over diffusive consensus (e.g., Metropolis-Hastings) and O(D log(D/ε)) on cycles and tori, aligning the mechanism with both lifted nonreversible chains [5,8,37] and multi-step first-order methods [13,24].

Quality: The technical development is solid and careful. The reduction from edge-based dynamics to a node-based 2n-dimensional linear system is elegant and enables a clean spectral analysis (Theorem 1). Assumptions (quadratic objectives, symmetric W, specific initialization R̂0=r̂0=0) are clearly stated. However, the results are asymptotic; finite-time, non-asymptotic constants and robustness to parameter misspecification (γ) are not quantified. There are no experiments to validate constants or compare wall-clock/communication costs versus EXTRA/ADMM [3,36,38,41].

Clarity: The paper is well-organized, but the notation burden is high. Brief intuition before Equations (4)–(6), a schematic linking Algs. 1–3, and a consolidated comparison of rates/parameters versus Chebyshev, heavy-ball, and Nesterov methods would improve readability.

Originality: High. This appears to be the first convergence-rate analysis of Min-Sum Splitting, the first explicit link between Splitting, lifted chains, and multi-step acceleration, and a new auxiliary-process proof technique.

Significance: Theoretical significance is strong: it advances our understanding of BP-like protocols for optimization and shows they can match best-known accelerated consensus rates. Practical impact is tempered by the need for global spectral information (ρW) and the restriction to quadratic consensus; extensions to general convex objectives, time-varying/asynchronous settings, and robustness would broaden applicability.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and conceptually unifying analysis (Splitting ↔ lifting ↔ multi-step).
- Clear acceleration with precise spectral rates; near-optimal on geometric graphs.
- Technique (auxiliary process) may transfer to other problems.

Cons against acceptance:
- No empirical validation or comparison to ADMM/EXTRA/Nesterov in finite regimes.
- Requires global spectral tuning; robustness not analyzed.
- Scope limited to quadratic consensus and synchronous settings.
