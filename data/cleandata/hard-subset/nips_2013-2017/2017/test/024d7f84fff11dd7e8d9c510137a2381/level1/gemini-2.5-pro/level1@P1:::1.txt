This paper investigates the application of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, a generalization of the standard Min-Sum (or Belief Propagation) message-passing protocol, to the distributed consensus problem. The primary focus is on the network averaging problem, a canonical instance of consensus with quadratic objectives. The authors first relate the consensus problem to the general form of optimization that Min-Sum addresses. They correctly note that the standard Min-Sum algorithm fails to converge for this problem on graphs with cycles, a result previously shown by Moallemi & Van Roy (2010).

The main contribution of the paper is to show that the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, which introduces a reparameterization of the objective function via a scalar `δ` and a matrix `Γ`, not only guarantees convergence but can be tuned to achieve *accelerated* convergence rates. The authors prove that for a specific choice of parameters (`δ=1` and `Γ` proportional to a base consensus matrix `W`), the algorithm's dynamics are equivalent to those of shift-register methods, a known technique for accelerating linear solvers. This leads to a subdiffusive convergence rate, scaling with the graph diameter `D` as O(D log(D/ε)), which represents a square-root improvement over standard "diffusive" methods that scale as O(D² log(1/ε)) on graphs like cycles and tori.

A significant conceptual contribution is the connection the paper establishes between Min-Sum Splitting and two other major families of accelerated consensus algorithms: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods from convex optimization (e.g., Nesterov's acceleration). The paper argues that the "splitting" parameters introduce a directionality and memory into the message-passing scheme that is analogous to the mechanisms used in these other methods to overcome the slow mixing of standard random walks. To facilitate the analysis, the authors introduce a novel and elegant proof technique: they derive an equivalent "auxiliary" linear process with memory that evolves on the graph's nodes, which is much simpler to analyze spectrally than the original message-passing process that lives on the graph's directed edges.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper provides a strong and important result. It demonstrates that a canonical algorithm from the graphical models community (Min-Sum/BP) can be adapted to achieve state-of-the-art accelerated rates on a fundamental problem in distributed computing and machine learning (consensus). This significantly elevates the relevance of Min-Sum schemes for distributed optimization, showing they are competitive with specialized acceleration methods.

2.  **Originality and Insight:** The connection drawn between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step optimization methods is highly novel and insightful. This cross-pollination of ideas from different fields (graphical models, optimization, Markov chain theory) is a major strength. It provides a unifying perspective, suggesting that the "splitting" reparameterization is a general mechanism for embedding momentum-like acceleration into message-passing protocols.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the linear message updates for the quadratic case (Proposition 1) is clear. The introduction of the auxiliary message-passing scheme (Proposition 2 and Algorithm 3) is a clever analytical tool that simplifies the convergence proof significantly. The main result, Theorem 1, is presented rigorously and leverages existing spectral analysis of shift-register matrices to establish the accelerated convergence rate. The proof technique appears to be a valuable contribution in its own right for analyzing edge-based message-passing algorithms.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction clearly situates the work within the extensive literature on consensus, explaining the limitations of diffusive methods and the goals of accelerated methods. The logical flow from the general algorithm to its specific application and finally to the simplified auxiliary analysis is easy to follow. The contributions are clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Practicality of Parameter Selection:** The main result in Theorem 1 relies on setting the parameter `γ = 2 / (1 + sqrt(1 - ρ_W^2))`, where `ρ_W` is the second-largest eigenvalue in magnitude of the base consensus matrix `W`. This value depends on a global spectral property of the graph. The paper correctly notes that this reliance on global information is shared by other accelerated methods, but it does not discuss how this parameter might be computed or estimated in a distributed setting. Without a practical, distributed method for tuning `γ`, the algorithm remains a centralized design for a distributed problem, limiting its immediate applicability. A brief discussion on this limitation or potential avenues for distributed parameter estimation would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Scope of Analysis:** The entire convergence analysis is performed for the specific case of network averaging, where the local objective functions `φ_v` are quadratic. This allows the message updates to be characterized by a simple linear system. While this is a crucial and standard first step, the paper's conclusion that these results "motivate further studies to generalize the acceleration due to splittings to other problems" would be more compelling with a short discussion on the potential challenges. For instance, would the acceleration still hold for general convex functions, where the messages are no longer simple quadratics and the analysis would be substantially more complex?

3.  **Comparison with Other Methods:** While the paper successfully shows that Min-Sum Splitting can match the rate of shift-register methods, the comparison to other accelerated methods, such as those based on Nesterov's scheme or ADMM, is primarily conceptual. A more quantitative comparison of the resulting convergence rates (e.g., the constants in the O-notation) would be beneficial for positioning the work more precisely within the state of the art.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel and significant result by proving that a general message-passing framework (Min-Sum) can achieve accelerated rates for consensus.
*   Establishes an original and insightful conceptual bridge between graphical models, lifted Markov chains, and accelerated optimization, which is of high value to the NIPS community.
*   The technical contribution, including the auxiliary process for analysis, is elegant and rigorous.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions and their context.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The practical implementation is hindered by the need for a globally-defined parameter (`γ`), a point that is not sufficiently addressed.
*   The analysis is limited to the quadratic case, and the path to generalization is not discussed.

**Recommendation:**
Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. The conceptual unification of different acceleration techniques under the umbrella of Min-Sum Splitting is a beautiful and important contribution. The technical result is strong and the analytical method is novel. I recommend **acceptance**. The authors should, however, consider adding a brief discussion in the final version regarding the practical challenge of setting the `γ` parameter in a distributed manner.
