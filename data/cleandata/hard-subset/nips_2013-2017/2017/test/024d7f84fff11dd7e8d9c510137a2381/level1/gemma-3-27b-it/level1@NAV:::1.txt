## Review of “Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting”

This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The authors demonstrate that while the standard Min-Sum algorithm may fail to converge, a modified version – splitting – yields convergence and, crucially, achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) rates matching those of shift-register methods. The connection to lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods is also highlighted, providing a broader perspective on the algorithm’s mechanics.

The paper builds upon previous work on Min-Sum algorithms [28, 34, 35] and distributed consensus [42, 41, 27]. It correctly identifies the limitations of standard Min-Sum for cyclic graphs and positions its contribution as addressing this gap. The authors appropriately cite relevant literature on accelerated consensus methods, including lifted Markov chains [37] and accelerated first-order methods [13]. The comparison to existing work, particularly [27], is well-articulated, and the improvement achieved by the proposed method is clearly stated.

**Strengths:** The paper presents a technically sound analysis with a clear mathematical derivation of the convergence rates. The connection between Min-Sum splitting, lifted Markov chains and accelerated gradient methods is a significant contribution, offering valuable insights into the algorithm's underlying principles. The proof of the convergence rates and the demonstration of subdiffusive scaling are strong points. The organization is logical, building from background to the main results in a coherent manner. 

**Weaknesses:** The paper's clarity suffers slightly from its density and heavy reliance on mathematical notation. While the derivations appear correct, the explanation of the intuition behind the splitting parameters and their relation to the spectral properties of the graph could be more accessible. The discussion of the connection to ADMM [3, 43, 38] feels somewhat brief and could be expanded. The practical implications of the algorithm are not thoroughly discussed. While the theoretical results are strong, the paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational cost of implementing the algorithm, especially compared to existing methods. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical analysis of Min-Sum Splitting convergence rates.
*   Demonstrates subdiffusive convergence, improving on existing algorithms.
*   Establishes a connection to well-established acceleration techniques.
*   The technical contributions are substantial and likely to be of interest to researchers in distributed optimization and message-passing algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is mathematically dense and may be difficult for some readers to follow.
*   Limited discussion of practical considerations and computational costs.
*   The connection to related areas (e.g. ADMM) could be more thoroughly explored.



**Overall:** I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a solid theoretical contribution with significant potential impact. Addressing the clarity concerns and expanding the discussion of practical considerations would further strengthen the work.
