- Paper shows ordinary Min‑Sum may diverge on loopy graphs, while a reparameterized "Min‑Sum Splitting" variant guarantees convergence.  
- For quadratic consensus, a particular splitting choice yields accelerated, subdiffusive convergence matching shift‑register/Chebyshev rates.  
- Method: Min‑Sum Splitting reduces to a linear edge-based iteration (Alg.2) and via reparametrization to a two‑step node‑based linear system (Alg.3) analyzable by matrix K.  
- With δ=1 and Γ=γW (W symmetric averaging), γ chosen from ρ(W−11^T/n) gives convergence factor ρK = √((1−√(1−ρW^2))/(1+√(1−ρW^2))).  
- Result implies O(√) improvement in 1/(1−ρ) and O(D log(D/ε)) rates on cycles and tori.  
- Positions work relative to consensus propagation, lifted/nonreversible Markov chains, shift‑register methods, and ADMM‑based acceleration.  
- Technical soundness: reductions and spectral analysis are clear and plausible for quadratic objectives; assumptions W symmetric and W1=1 are reasonable.  
- Acceleration depends on global spectral information (γ needs ρ(W)), a tradeoff that affects distributed implementability and robustness.  
- Analysis is limited to quadratic consensus; no theoretical extension to general strongly convex separable φv.  
- Model assumes synchronous updates and static graphs; no results for asynchrony, delays, or time‑varying topologies.  
- Completeness: main contributions are self‑contained but key proofs are in supplement; finite‑time bounds and conditions ensuring denominators R_t>0 should be stated.  
- No empirical validation; small simulations on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs are recommended to validate constants and compare baselines.  
- Clarity: paper is dense but organized; edge/node algorithm forms are clearly stated.  
- Suggested clarifications: intuition for splitting parameters (directionality/memory) and why δ=1 suffices; implementability and per‑iteration communication/memory costs.  
- Define all symbols at first use and explicitly state conditions guaranteeing R_t>0 for Algorithm 3.  
- Fix minor formatting glitches in text and references.  
- Originality: novelty in showing Min‑Sum Splitting reproduces shift‑register acceleration and formalizing links to lifting and multi‑step methods; spectral analysis via auxiliary process appears original.  
- Significance: interesting conceptual contribution for message‑passing community; practical use limited by need for global spectral info but may inspire adaptive schemes and extensions.  
- Pros: clear spectral‑rate guarantee, conceptual links between frameworks, clean linear‑algebraic node‑based analysis, applicable to arbitrary graphs with explicit tuning.  
- Cons: requires global spectral knowledge and lacks robustness analysis to mis‑specification, limited to quadratics, assumes synchronous/static graphs, no experiments, possible loose constants (n dependence).  
- Key questions for authors: sensitivity to γ mis‑tuning and bounds using spectral bounds or distributed estimates; can Algorithm 3 be implemented fully distributed with two scalars per node and what are exact costs?  
- Further asks: do techniques extend to strongly convex separable φv (via local quadratic approximations/message linearization)?  
- Suggest formalizing the lifting/nonreversibility connection by characterizing steady state and relating edge‑process spectral gap to K.  
- Recommendation: lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying implementability, discussing tuning/robustness, and adding brief empirical illustrations.
