

# Review: Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting

This paper investigates the application of Min-Sum message-passing to the consensus problem in distributed optimization, specifically demonstrating how a modified version known as Min-Sum Splitting can achieve accelerated convergence rates. The authors establish that while ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge on graphs with cycles, Min-Sum Splitting with proper parameter tuning converges to the optimal solution with subdiffusive rates, matching shift-register methods. The paper makes a significant contribution by connecting Min-Sum Splitting to two distinct accelerated methods: lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization.

The paper builds upon prior work on Min-Sum (e.g., [28, 34, 35]) and consensus algorithms (e.g., [42, 41, 7, 27]), but makes a novel theoretical contribution by establishing convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting. Previous work on Min-Sum for consensus [27] showed that ordinary Min-Sum doesn't converge on cyclic graphs, and proposed a modified version with a soft barrier function, but did not establish convergence rates or provide an analytical comparison to accelerated methods. The authors' key insight is that the "splitting" parameters in Min-Sum Splitting can be tuned to achieve acceleration, and they establish a precise connection to both lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods.

**Quality:** The paper presents technically sound theoretical results with rigorous proofs for the convergence rates. The authors clearly establish the relationship between Min-Sum Splitting and existing accelerated methods through a novel auxiliary process that allows them to track the algorithm's evolution on the original graph rather than on the directed edges. The theoretical analysis in Theorem 1 is well-motivated and carefully constructed, showing that the asymptotic convergence rate is ρK = √[(1-√(1-ρW²))/(1+√(1-ρW²))], which is a square-root improvement over diffusive methods. The paper acknowledges the limitation that the parameter choice depends on global graph properties (ρW), which is consistent with other accelerated methods. The supplementary material provides the complete proofs, making the results reproducible.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly define the consensus problem, the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, and the connection to lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods. The paper's structure flows logically from problem definition to algorithm design to theoretical analysis. The key insight that the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm can be tracked by a linear process on the nodes of the graph (via the auxiliary algorithm) is clearly explained. The notation is consistent throughout, and the authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The paper's clarity is particularly impressive given the technical complexity of the topic.

**Originality:** This paper makes several significant original contributions. First, it establishes the first connection between Min-Sum message-passing and both lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step methods in convex optimization. Second, it provides the first proof of how directionality in Belief Propagation protocols can be tuned to accelerate convergence. Third, it introduces a novel proof technique using an auxiliary process to track the evolution of Min-Sum Splitting. The authors also demonstrate that Min-Sum Splitting achieves better convergence rates than previous Min-Sum methods for consensus [27]. This work significantly advances the understanding of distributed optimization algorithms and their connections to broader theoretical frameworks.

**Significance:** The consensus problem is fundamental to distributed systems with wide-ranging applications in sensor networks, multi-agent coordination, and distributed machine learning. The paper's achievement of subdiffusive convergence rates (O(D log(D/ε)) instead of O(D² log(1/ε)) for cycle graphs and tori) represents a substantial improvement over classical diffusive methods. The paper's connection to two distinct accelerated methods suggests broader implications for distributed optimization. The authors show that Min-Sum Splitting can achieve convergence rates close to the optimal O(D log(1/ε)) recently established for some accelerated methods [30, 36]. This work opens new research directions for generalizing acceleration via splitting to other distributed optimization problems.

**Weaknesses:** While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper could better address the practical implications of the global parameter dependence (ρW). The authors mention that ρW depends on global graph properties, but don't explore how this might be estimated in a distributed setting without knowledge of the full graph structure. Additionally, while the paper focuses on the quadratic consensus problem, it would be valuable to briefly discuss how these insights might extend to non-quadratic problems.

**Pro and Con Acceptance:**

*Pros:*
- First theoretical analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting
- Establishes significant connections to two major lines of accelerated methods
- Provides a novel proof technique using an auxiliary process
- Demonstrates a square-root improvement in convergence rates over diffusive methods
- Well-written with clear organization and mathematical exposition

*Cons:*
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges (global parameter dependence)
- Focuses primarily on quadratic consensus problem (though this is standard for convergence rate analysis)
- Some readers might find the connection to lifted Markov chains less intuitive without visual aids

Overall, this paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that significantly advances our understanding of distributed optimization algorithms. It meets all four NIPS criteria at a high level: the work is technically sound, well-written, original, and significant. The paper establishes a meaningful connection between message-passing algorithms and accelerated optimization methods, opening new research directions for distributed systems. The work is well-positioned to be influential in both the distributed optimization and graphical models communities.

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2017. It represents a substantial advance in our understanding of distributed optimization algorithms and their convergence properties.
