- Paper studies Min-Sum Splitting for distributed consensus/averaging, showing ordinary Min-Sum (Gaussian BP) fails on loopy graphs but a reparameterized Splitting variant converges.
- For quadratic consensus they derive a linear edge-based message-passing scheme (Alg. 2) and an auxiliary node-based two-step linear system (Alg. 3) that tracks outputs.
- With δ=1 and Γ=γW (γ = 2/(1+√(1−ρW²))) they prove accelerated convergence governed by the second eigenvalue of a shift-register matrix K, matching Chebyshev/shift-register acceleration.
- This yields a square-root improvement over diffusive consensus and O(D log(D/ε)) rates on cycles and tori, linking the mechanism to lifted nonreversible chains and multi-step first-order methods.
- Technical development is solid and careful; reduction from edge dynamics to a 2n-dimensional node system is elegant and enables a clean spectral analysis (Theorem 1).
- Assumptions are clearly stated: quadratic objectives, symmetric W, and specific initialization R̂0 = r̂0 = 0.
- Results are asymptotic; finite-time/non-asymptotic constants and robustness to parameter misspecification (γ) are not quantified.
- No experiments are provided to validate constants or compare wall-clock/communication costs against EXTRA/ADMM.
- Paper is well-organized but notation-heavy; it needs more intuition before key equations, a schematic linking Algs. 1–3, and a consolidated comparison versus Chebyshev, heavy-ball, and Nesterov methods.
- Originality is high: first convergence-rate analysis of Min-Sum Splitting and first explicit link between Splitting, lifted chains, and multi-step acceleration, plus a new auxiliary-process proof technique.
- Theoretical significance is strong, advancing understanding of BP-like protocols and showing they can match best-known accelerated consensus rates.
- Practical impact is limited by the need for global spectral information (ρW) and restriction to quadratic, synchronous settings; extensions to general convex objectives, time-varying/asynchronous settings, and robustness would broaden applicability.
- Pros for acceptance: novel unifying analysis, clear spectral acceleration with precise rates, and a transferable technique (auxiliary process).
- Cons against acceptance: no empirical validation, requires global spectral tuning and lacks robustness analysis, and scope is limited to quadratic consensus and synchronous settings.
