- Shows Min-Sum Splitting (with tuned splitting parameters) converges on graphs with cycles and achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) rates; ordinary Min-Sum fails on cyclic graphs.  
- Establishes a precise connection between Min-Sum Splitting and two accelerated methods: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) with asymptotic rate ρK = sqrt[(1-√(1-ρW²))/(1+√(1-ρW²))], a square-root improvement over diffusive methods.  
- Introduces a novel auxiliary-process proof technique that tracks algorithm evolution on nodes rather than directed edges.  
- Builds on prior Min-Sum and consensus work but gives the first convergence-rate analysis and an analytical comparison to accelerated methods.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and reproducible (full proofs in supplementary material).  
- Demonstrates significant practical impact: subdiffusive convergence (O(D log(D/ε)) vs classical O(D² log(1/ε)) for cycles/tori), nearing optimal rates.  
- Key limitation: splitting-parameter choice depends on global graph property ρW, with no distributed estimation strategy provided.  
- Scope limitation: analysis focuses on quadratic consensus; extensions to non-quadratic problems are not explored.  
- Usability note: the connection to lifted Markov chains may be unintuitive for some readers without visual aids.  
- Overall recommendation: technically strong, original, and significant — recommends acceptance.
