## Review of "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting"

This paper investigates the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem in distributed optimization. The core idea is to demonstrate that, unlike the standard Min-Sum algorithm, a modified version (Splitting) *does* converge and, crucially, achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence rates comparable to shift-register methods. The authors establish a connection between Min-Sum Splitting and both lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods, highlighting a unifying perspective on acceleration techniques in this domain.

The paper builds upon prior work on Min-Sum algorithms [28, 35] and consensus problems [42, 41]. It correctly identifies the limitations of standard Min-Sum for graphs with cycles and references relevant work on diffusive convergence rates [44, 2]. The connection to lifted Markov chains [37] and accelerated optimization methods [13] is well-motivated and provides valuable context. The authors appropriately position their work relative to recent advances like those in [27], addressing open questions regarding convergence times for specific graph topologies.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting is a significant contribution, filling a gap in the existing literature.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing convergence rates and relating them to known results in related fields.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors effectively connect their work to a broad range of related research, demonstrating a strong understanding of the field.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear definitions and explanations of the key concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** The mathematical derivations and notation can be dense, potentially making the paper challenging to follow for readers not deeply familiar with the area.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses primarily on theoretical results. While the analysis is strong, the lack of empirical validation limits the practical impact of the findings.
*   **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on specific assumptions (e.g., quadratic objective functions, certain graph properties). The robustness of the results to violations of these assumptions is not discussed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound analysis of a relevant problem in distributed optimization.
*   The results contribute to a deeper understanding of the convergence properties of Min-Sum algorithms and their potential for acceleration.
*   The connections to related fields provide valuable insights and open up new avenues for research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The complexity of the paper may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
*   The lack of empirical validation weakens the practical impact of the findings.



**Overall:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field and should be accepted. The theoretical results are significant, and the connections to related work are well-established. While the lack of empirical validation is a minor concern, the strength of the theoretical analysis outweighs this limitation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and potentially include some preliminary empirical results.
