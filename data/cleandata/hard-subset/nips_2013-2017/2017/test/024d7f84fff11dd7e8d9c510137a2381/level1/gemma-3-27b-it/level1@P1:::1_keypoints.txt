- Core contribution: Shows Min-Sum Splitting converges where standard Min-Sum fails and achieves accelerated convergence rates matching shift-register methods.  
- Unifying perspective: Establishes connections between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step first-order methods to explain acceleration.  
- Theoretical novelty: Provides the first analytical convergence-rate guarantees for Min-Sum Splitting, addressing prior gaps.  
- Theoretical rigor: Uses an auxiliary process and spectral-gap analysis to derive convergence rates, giving a strong mathematical foundation.  
- Clear main result: Theorem 1 explicitly states conditions and the convergence rate for acceleration.  
- Literature positioning: Comprehensive review that situates the work relative to message-passing, splitting methods, lifted chains, and accelerated first-order methods.  
- Practical-limitation: Paper lacks discussion on sensitivity to parameter choices and implementation overhead of splitting.  
- Empirical gap: No simulations or experiments are provided to validate accelerated convergence across graph topologies.  
- Accessibility concern: Mathematical density and reliance on advanced concepts may limit accessibility to non-experts; more intuition would help.  
- Notation issues: Complex and sometimes cumbersome notation for matrices (K, K̂, K∞) harms readability; a notation table or consistency improvements are suggested.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, theoretically sound, resolves an open problem, and links to broader acceleration techniques.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Missing practical validation, accessibility concerns due to complexity, and notation clarity issues.  
- Overall verdict: Significant and rigorous theoretical contribution with recommendation to accept, with suggestions to add practical discussion, limited simulations, and clearer notation in future versions.
