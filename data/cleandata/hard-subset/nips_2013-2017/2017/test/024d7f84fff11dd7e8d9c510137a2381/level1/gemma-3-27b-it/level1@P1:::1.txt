This paper investigates accelerated convergence rates for the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm applied to the consensus problem. The core idea is to show that a modified Min-Sum scheme, utilizing splitting, not only converges where the standard Min-Sum fails but also achieves accelerated convergence, matching rates obtained by shift-register methods. The authors establish a connection between Min-Sum Splitting, lifted Markov chains, and multi-step first-order methods, highlighting a unified perspective on acceleration techniques.

The paper builds on prior work in distributed optimization, specifically message-passing algorithms like Min-Sum and their limitations. References [28, 25] outline convergence conditions for standard Min-Sum, while [34, 35] introduce the splitting approach. The consensus problem itself is well-established, with early work cited in [42, 41] and recent advancements in [29, 40, 17, 10, 6, 16, 39]. The work directly addresses limitations highlighted in [27], which analyzed a related Min-Sum approach but left convergence time analysis open for many graph topologies. The paper also correctly positions its contributions against broader acceleration techniques like lifted Markov chains [37] and accelerated first-order methods [13].

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The analytical establishment of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting is a significant contribution. Prior work lacked theoretical guarantees on convergence speed.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper demonstrates a strong theoretical foundation, using an auxiliary process to track algorithm evolution and deriving convergence rates based on spectral gap analysis.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:**  Successfully links Min-Sum Splitting to both lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods, providing a unifying perspective. This connection highlights the underlying principles driving acceleration in these diverse approaches.
*   **Clear Presentation of Results:** The theorem (Theorem 1) clearly states the convergence rate and the conditions required for acceleration, providing a concrete result for practitioners.
*   **Comprehensive Literature Review:** The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of the related literature, accurately citing relevant papers and positioning their work within the field.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** The paper is mathematically dense and might be challenging for readers not deeply familiar with message-passing algorithms, spectral graph theory, and optimization.  More intuitive explanations of the auxiliary process and its connection to the original algorithm would be beneficial.
*   **Practical Implications:** While the theoretical results are strong, the paper lacks discussion of the practical implications. For instance, how sensitive is the performance to the choice of the parameters? Is there a significant overhead in implementing the splitting compared to standard Min-Sum? A discussion of these points would enhance the paper's impact.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses solely on theoretical analysis. Providing even limited simulations demonstrating the accelerated convergence on various graph topologies (e.g., cycles, grids, random graphs) would strengthen the results and build confidence in their practical relevance.
*   **Notation:** The notation, particularly involving the various matrices (K, K̂, K∞) can be overwhelming. A dedicated notation table or a more consistent use of subscripts/superscripts could improve readability.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of distributed optimization.
*   It resolves an open problem regarding the convergence rates of Min-Sum Splitting.
*   The connections to related acceleration techniques provide valuable insights and a unified perspective.
*   The mathematical analysis is rigorous and well-presented (though dense).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of practical validation and discussion limits the immediate impact of the theoretical results.
*   The complexity of the paper may make it inaccessible to a wider audience.
*   The notation is occasionally cumbersome and could be improved.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its complexity and lack of empirical validation, this paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field. The results are novel, rigorously proven, and offer valuable insights into the acceleration of distributed consensus algorithms. The connection to existing work is well-established, and the potential impact on related areas is considerable.  I believe the strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and I **recommend acceptance**, with the suggestion that the authors consider adding a discussion of practical implications and exploring limited simulations in a future version. The authors should also consider streamlining the notation to improve readability.
