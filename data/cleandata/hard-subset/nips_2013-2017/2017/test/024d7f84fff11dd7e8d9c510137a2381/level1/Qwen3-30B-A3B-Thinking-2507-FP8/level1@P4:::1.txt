

The paper "Accelerated consensus via Min-Sum Splitting" investigates the application of Min-Sum message-passing to solve the consensus problem in distributed optimization. The authors demonstrate that while ordinary Min-Sum fails to converge for graphs with cycles, a modified version called Min-Sum Splitting converges to the solution. They prove that with proper parameter tuning, Min-Sum Splitting achieves subdiffusive convergence rates matching those of shift-register methods. The paper establishes connections between Min-Sum Splitting and two existing acceleration techniques: lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization.

The paper builds on prior work in distributed optimization, particularly Min-Sum algorithms [28, 34, 35] and consensus algorithms [7, 42, 44]. Previous work on Min-Sum for consensus [27] showed that ordinary Min-Sum doesn't converge for graphs with cycles, and they proposed a modified version with a soft barrier function. The current paper extends this work by showing that Min-Sum Splitting (with proper parameters) converges and achieves accelerated rates.

The main contributions are: (1) first connection of Min-Sum schemes with lifted Markov chains and multi-step methods; (2) first proof of how directionality in belief propagation can be tuned for acceleration; (3) first analysis of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting; and (4) design of a Min-Sum protocol for consensus achieving better rates than previous Min-Sum methods [27].

Quality: The paper provides a technically sound theoretical analysis. The authors establish convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting, proving that with proper parameter choices, the algorithm achieves O(D log(D/ε)) convergence time for cycle graphs and tori, which is a square-root improvement over diffusive methods (O(D² log(1/ε))). The proof techniques are sound, using an auxiliary process to track the algorithm's evolution. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the need for global information to tune parameters, which is consistent with other accelerated methods.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement, related work, and proposed method are presented with sufficient technical detail. The main contributions are clearly articulated, and the paper provides a good overview of connections between different acceleration techniques. The notation is consistent and well-defined throughout.

Originality: The paper makes several novel contributions. It's the first to establish connections between Min-Sum schemes and both lifted Markov chains techniques and multi-step methods. The proof of convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting is novel, as previous work had not established these rates. The paper also shows how Min-Sum Splitting can be tuned to recover the same matrix as shift-register methods, which is a significant connection. The paper's claim of being the first to analyze convergence rates for Min-Sum Splitting is well-supported by the literature review.

Significance: The results are significant for distributed optimization. The paper provides a method that achieves better convergence rates than classical diffusive methods for consensus, which is a fundamental primitive in distributed systems. The square-root improvement in convergence time is important for large-scale networks, where communication efficiency is critical. The connection to two established lines of research could inspire further work in both message-passing algorithms and accelerated optimization methods.

Strengths: The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the connections to existing work are well-articulated, and the paper makes clear contributions to the field. The paper's main theorems are well-supported, and it provides a clear roadmap for how Min-Sum Splitting compares to previous methods. The paper effectively demonstrates the advantages of Min-Sum Splitting over previous approaches.

Weaknesses: The paper relies on supplementary material for the full proofs, which may make verification more difficult. The practical implementation details for large-scale systems are not discussed. The paper doesn't explore how the method generalizes to non-quadratic objective functions or more complex constraints, which limits its applicability to broader optimization problems.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of distributed optimization algorithms. The paper makes clear contributions to the field, establishes important connections between different approaches, and provides a new method with improved convergence rates. It is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization. The paper addresses a well-motivated problem with clear theoretical advances that should be of interest to the NIPS community.
