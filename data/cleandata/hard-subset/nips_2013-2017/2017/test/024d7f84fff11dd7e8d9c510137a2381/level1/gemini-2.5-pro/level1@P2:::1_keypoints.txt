- Paper studies Min-Sum for distributed consensus/network averaging, showing ordinary Min-Sum fails on cyclic graphs but Min-Sum Splitting converges with parameter tuning.  
- Main contribution: rigorous proof that appropriate splitting parameters yield accelerated "subdiffusive" convergence with a square-root improvement over diffusive methods, matching shift-register performance.  
- Conceptual contribution: establishes a novel connection between message-passing (Min-Sum), lifted Markov chains, and multi-step (momentum) optimization methods; splitting imparts directionality/memory akin to acceleration.  
- Technical innovation: a novel proof technique mapping edge-based message dynamics to an auxiliary multi-step node process that simplifies analysis.  
- Quality: technically strong and rigorous; linear message-passing updates (Alg. 2) are clearly derived from Min-Sum Splitting (Alg. 1).  
- Proposition 2: correctly establishes equivalence between edge-based and node-based schemes, enabling spectral analysis.  
- Theorem 1: precisely quantifies accelerated convergence and links it to spectral properties of the communication matrix W.  
- Clarity: exceptionally well-written, organized, with consistent notation and clear exposition of limitations and goals.  
- Originality: first work to link splitting to acceleration and to unify three previously distinct frameworks; auxiliary-process analysis is novel.  
- Significance: elevates Min-Sum to a provably fast method, offers a unifying perspective on acceleration, and advances consensus algorithm design.  
- Weakness: optimal splitting parameter γ depends on global graph spectral info (ρW), which is impractical in fully decentralized settings.  
- Suggestion: discuss potential decentralized estimation or adaptive schemes for selecting γ.  
- Weakness: analysis is specific to the quadratic (network averaging) case and does not detail generalization to non-quadratic or constrained problems.  
- Suggestion: provide more intuition on challenges and approaches for extending results to general convex/nonlinear settings and the role of linearity.  
- Arguments for acceptance: first proof of accelerated Min-Sum, novel conceptual unification, powerful proof technique, and excellent clarity and technical soundness.  
- Arguments against acceptance: scope limited to quadratic averaging and reliance on global graph information for optimal parameter tuning.
