- Paper studies accelerated convergence for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus, showing it converges and achieves subdiffusive rates comparable to shift-register methods.  
- Relates acceleration to lifted Markov chains and multi-step first-order methods, unifying different acceleration techniques.  
- Well-motivated study: consensus is fundamental and faster convergence is important for distributed optimization/ML.  
- Thorough literature review and clear placement of contribution relative to prior work (e.g., [27]).  
- Novelty: provides the first theoretical convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting where prior work lacked rates.  
- Theoretical rigor: proves a square-root improvement in convergence time (Theorem 1) using an auxiliary message-passing scheme.  
- Good connections to existing work, demonstrating deep field understanding.  
- Presentation is generally clear and Algorithms 2 and 3 are well explained.  
- Dense notation and some sections (e.g., K and K∞ in Sec.7) lack intuitive explanations, hindering accessibility.  
- Overemphasis on theory; practical implications (parameter sensitivity, computational cost) are insufficiently discussed.  
- No empirical validation; simulations would strengthen evidence of practical speedups.  
- Introduction is lengthy and repetitive and should be more concise.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, rigorous analysis with insightful connections and seemingly correct proofs.  
- Arguments against acceptance: accessibility issues, lack of experiments, and repetitive exposition.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions—improve clarity, add discussion on computational cost and γ sensitivity, and consider preliminary empirical results.
