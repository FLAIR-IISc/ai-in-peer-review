Summary
The paper studies Min-Sum message passing (a.k.a. belief propagation for optimization) for the consensus/averaging problem. It shows that the ordinary Min-Sum algorithm does not converge on loopy graphs (consistent with [27]) but a reparameterized variant, Min-Sum Splitting [34, 35], converges and, with a suitable tuning of the splitting parameters, achieves accelerated (subdiffusive) convergence rates. The authors (i) derive a linear edge-based iteration (Algorithm 2) equivalent to Min-Sum Splitting for quadratic consensus, (ii) construct a node-based auxiliary two-state linear system with memory (Algorithm 3) whose outputs coincide with the Min-Sum estimates under a simple initialization (Proposition 2), and (iii) prove that choosing δ=1 and Γ=γW with γ=2/(1+√(1−ρW^2)) (W symmetric, W1=1) yields asymptotic rate ρK equal to the second-largest eigenvalue magnitude of a shift-register matrix K (Theorem 1). This gives a square-root improvement over diffusive methods, leading to O(D log(D/ε)) time on cycles and tori, approaching the recent O(D log(1/ε)) best-known bounds from accelerated methods [30, 36].

The paper relates Min-Sum Splitting to two strands: lifted non-reversible Markov chains [5, 8, 18, 37], where acceleration comes from directionality on a lifted space, and multi-step first-order methods (heavy-ball, Nesterov, shift registers) for networked optimization [13, 24, 45], where acceleration comes from a memory term. The auxiliary node-based system makes the connection to shift-register methods explicit through the matrix K, recovering their tuned rate.

Relation to prior work
– Min-Sum/BP convergence on convex/quadratic problems has been studied by Moallemi & Van Roy [28], Ruozzi & Tatikonda [34, 35]. This paper is, to my knowledge, the first to provide rates for Min-Sum Splitting and to show it can match shift-register asymptotics on consensus.  
– Consensus via BP (Consensus Propagation) [27] required special initializations on d-regular graphs and analyzed edge-based dynamics via Cesàro mixing; here, consensus is analyzed for arbitrary graphs via a simpler node-auxiliary system and yields explicit accelerated rates.  
– Accelerated consensus via lifted chains [5, 8, 18, 22, 23, 37] and via multi-step methods [13, 14, 24, 45] are well covered; the paper contributes a conceptual bridge: splitting induces directionality on edges and a momentum term on nodes.  
– Recent optimal rates for distributed smooth strongly convex optimization (e.g., [36]) and fast consensus [44] are properly positioned; the paper does not beat these but approaches them with a different paradigm (message passing).

Quality
– The technical core is sound and well-motivated. The equivalence between Algorithms 2 and 3 (Proposition 2) is elegant and is key to the spectral analysis. Theorem 1 reproduces the known shift-register rate ρK with a concrete parameterization of the splitting.  
– Assumptions: synchronous updates, static graph, symmetric W (doubly stochastic-like), quadratic objectives, and zero initialization R̂0=r̂0=0. The requirement of global spectral information (ρW) to set γ is explicitly acknowledged; it matches similar needs in lifted chains and multi-step methods.  
– The positivity condition in Proposition 1 (ensuring well-defined local argmins) is mild given the chosen initialization but could be stated more explicitly for the tuned parameters.  
– Proofs are deferred; given space constraints, this is acceptable, but a brief proof sketch for Theorem 1 would improve readability. No empirical validation is provided.

Clarity
– The paper is generally clear and logically structured. The shift from edge-based to node-based dynamics is a highlight. However:
  - Notation is heavy (K̂ vs K, R̂/R, r̂/r), and a figure summarizing dependencies would help.  
  - Some references are incomplete and there are minor typographical issues.  
  - Practical guidance on choosing W (e.g., Metropolis vs SDP-optimal [44]) and the cost of exchanging quadratic parameters could be summarized.

Originality
– Novel analysis of Min-Sum Splitting with the first convergence-rate guarantees on consensus; the auxiliary system technique is new and insightful. The explicit bridge to lifted chains and multi-step methods is conceptually fresh. Although the achieved rate matches known shift-register rates, demonstrating that a BP-style protocol attains them is original.

Significance
– For researchers in message passing and distributed optimization, this is a meaningful step: it shows Min-Sum Splitting is not only convergent but competitive with accelerated consensus, bringing BP-style methods closer to state-of-the-art convergence.  
– Practical significance is tempered by the need for global spectral tuning and by restriction to quadratics/synchronous settings. Still, the techniques could inspire extensions (e.g., beyond quadratic consensus, to ADMM/BP connections [12]).

Pros
– First convergence-rate analysis for Min-Sum Splitting on consensus; clean spectral result matching shift-register acceleration.  
– Elegant auxiliary node-based system that simplifies analysis and reveals the momentum interpretation.  
– Clear connections drawn to lifted Markov chains and accelerated first-order methods.  
– Improvement over prior BP-based consensus [27], with general graphs and explicit asymptotic rates.

Cons
– Limited scope: quadratic averaging, synchronous static graphs, symmetric W.  
– Requires global spectral information to tune γ; no adaptive/distributed tuning presented.  
– No experiments to validate constants, pre-asymptotic behavior, or robustness (noise, quantization, asynchrony).  
– Communication cost per iteration is higher than scalar gossip (messages carry quadratic parameters), not discussed quantitatively.

Suggestions for improvement
– Add simulations on cycles, grids, and random geometric graphs comparing to Metropolis, Chebyshev/shift-register, Nesterov-type consensus, and lifted gossip, highlighting constants and robustness.  
– Discuss communication/computation overhead (two scalars per directed edge vs one scalar per node) and memory.  
– Provide guidance when ρW is unknown: distributed spectral estimation or adaptive schemes.  
– Clarify the positivity condition in Proposition 1 for the chosen parameters and initializations.  
– Comment on extensions beyond quadratic consensus (e.g., strongly convex separable objectives, constraints) and on asynchronous or time-varying graphs.

Recommendation
Overall, a solid theoretical contribution with clear novelty within the BP literature and a useful conceptual bridge to accelerated consensus. Despite the limited scope and lack of experiments, I lean toward acceptance for its clean analysis and potential to spur further work on accelerated message-passing schemes.
