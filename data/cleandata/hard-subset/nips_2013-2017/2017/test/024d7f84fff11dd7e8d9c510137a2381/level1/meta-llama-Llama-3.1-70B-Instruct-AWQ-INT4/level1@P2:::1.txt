**Summary**

The paper proposes an accelerated consensus algorithm based on the Min-Sum message-passing protocol, which is a local algorithm designed for distributed optimization. The authors show that a modified version of the Min-Sum algorithm, known as Splitting, can achieve subdiffusive convergence rates, matching the rates obtained by shift-register methods. The acceleration scheme embodied by Min-Sum Splitting for the consensus problem bears similarities with lifted Markov chains techniques and with multi-step first-order methods in convex optimization.

**Strengths**

1. **Originality**: The paper introduces a new acceleration scheme for the Min-Sum algorithm, which is a significant contribution to the field of distributed optimization.
2. **Technical soundness**: The authors provide a rigorous analysis of the convergence rates of the Min-Sum Splitting algorithm, including a proof of the asymptotic rate of convergence.
3. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow for readers familiar with the topic.
4. **Significance**: The results have important implications for distributed optimization and consensus problems, which are crucial in many applications, including machine learning, sensor networks, and multi-agent systems.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited scope**: The paper focuses on a specific problem (consensus) and a specific algorithm (Min-Sum Splitting), which may limit its broader impact.
2. **Assumptions**: The analysis relies on certain assumptions about the graph structure and the objective function, which may not always hold in practice.
3. **Comparison to other methods**: While the authors mention other accelerated methods, a more detailed comparison with these methods would be beneficial to understand the strengths and weaknesses of Min-Sum Splitting.
