This paper introduces a novel and powerful information-theoretic framework for analyzing semisupervised clustering based on non-adaptive queries. The core contribution is the formal equivalence established between this clustering problem and the lesser-studied problem of locally encodable source coding. In this model, the goal is to recover `n` labels belonging to `k` classes by asking a minimal number of parallel queries, where each query is a function of a small, constant number (`Δ`) of labels.

Leveraging this connection, the work derives new information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate label recovery. A key finding is the demonstrated sub-optimality of the commonly used 'same-cluster' (or XOR) queries for approximate recovery. To address this, the paper proposes a new type of query, the AND query, and proves its superiority in this setting. For the practical case of noisy query answers from an oracle (e.g., a crowd worker), a concrete algorithm is presented that uses these AND-style queries to achieve approximate recovery of `(1-δ)` of the labels with `O(n log(k/δ))` queries. The theoretical results are supported by a real-world crowdsourcing experiment that validates the proposed algorithm and provides empirical evidence that AND queries lead to lower human error rates than 'same-cluster' queries.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this work is the novel conceptual bridge it builds between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding. This is a significant contribution that provides a new theoretical lens for a problem of growing interest in machine learning and crowdsourcing. This framework is likely to inspire follow-up work and allow for the transfer of techniques from information theory to this domain.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a series of rigorous results. The derived lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery (Theorems 2 and 3) are new and non-trivial. The analysis of the proposed AND query scheme (Theorems 4 and 5) provides strong theoretical guarantees for its performance, demonstrably outperforming existing approaches in the approximate recovery setting.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The proposal of AND queries is not merely a theoretical exercise. The paper provides clear intuition for why these queries might be easier for human workers to answer correctly, and the experimental results (Section 4) compellingly support this claim. Finding that AND queries solicited fewer errors (105) than 'same-cluster' queries (234) on the same set of pairs is a strong and practically important result. The proposed Algorithm 1 is simple and efficient, further enhancing the work's practical value.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. It effectively builds its argument by first establishing the theoretical framework and then applying it to progressively more complex and realistic scenarios: from noiseless exact recovery to noisy approximate recovery. The problem setup and contributions are clearly articulated.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or expanded upon to further improve the work.

1.  **Labeling vs. Clustering with AND Queries:** The introduction makes a distinction between labeling and clustering. The proposed AND query, `Q(X, X') = i if X = X' = i`, seems to be inherently a labeling query, as it requires knowledge of the absolute label `i` to pose the question (e.g., "Are both these movies 'action' movies?"). This contrasts with 'same-cluster' queries, which are invariant to label permutations and thus directly solve the clustering problem. It would be beneficial to discuss the implications of this distinction more explicitly. For instance, how would the proposed scheme work if the goal is purely partitioning, and the semantic meaning of the cluster labels is unknown a priori?
2.  **Sensitivity to Prior Estimation:** Algorithm 1 and its analysis rely on knowledge of the prior probabilities `p` (or relative cluster sizes `n_i/n`). Theorem 6 shows the algorithm works if these sizes are known. In practice, these priors would need to be estimated, likely from a small sample, introducing potential estimation error. A brief discussion on the robustness of Algorithm 1 to inaccuracies in the estimated priors would strengthen the practical claims. For example, how does the performance degrade if the estimated cluster sizes are slightly incorrect?
3.  **Further Intuition for Theoretical Bounds:** The proof sketch for Theorem 4, using a Poisson approximation for vertex degrees, provides excellent intuition. A similar high-level intuitive explanation for the lower bound in Theorem 3, which is quite complex, would be a welcome addition to the main text to make the result more accessible before referring the reader to the appendix.
4.  **Experimental Details:** The experiment provides a valuable proof of concept. To enhance reproducibility and context, it would be helpful to include a few more details. For instance, were the 10 volunteers the same for all 1000 queries, or were they partitioned? Also, providing the synthetic data results for the 'same-cluster' comparison (mentioned as being in Figure 7 in supplementary material) directly in the main paper, perhaps as a smaller companion plot to Figure 5, would make the performance comparison more immediate. These are minor suggestions that would enhance an already effective experimental section.

**Summary of Evaluation:**

This is a high-quality paper with significant contributions in originality, technical depth, and practical relevance. The novel connection to source coding opens up new avenues for research, and the proposed AND query is a simple but powerful idea with demonstrated practical benefits. The suggestions above are intended to further refine and strengthen an already excellent submission.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel and significant theoretical framework connecting clustering to information theory.
*   Provides new, non-trivial lower bounds for the problem.
*   Identifies a weakness in a standard query type ('same-cluster') and proposes a provably and empirically superior alternative (AND query).
*   The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   This reviewer finds no major flaws that would warrant rejection. The suggestions for improvement are minor and could be addressed in a final version.
