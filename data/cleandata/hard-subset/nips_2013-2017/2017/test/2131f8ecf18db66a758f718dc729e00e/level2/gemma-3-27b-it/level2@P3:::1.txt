This paper establishes an equivalence between a recent model of semisupervised clustering with binary queries and locally encodable source coding. This connection allows the authors to derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required for clustering, and to propose a novel querying strategy using AND queries that outperforms traditional ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries in several scenarios. The work is well-motivated, addressing the growing interest in crowdsourcing-based clustering and the need to minimize query costs.

The paper is technically sound and presents a clear mathematical framework. The proofs, while not fully detailed in the main body (references to supplementary material are provided), appear rigorous based on the presented arguments. The connection to locally encodable source coding is a key contribution, bringing tools from information theory to bear on a practical clustering problem. The demonstration of the limitations of XOR queries and the introduction of AND queries as a viable alternative are significant findings. The experimental validation on real datasets, albeit brief, lends further credence to the proposed approach.

A strength of the work is its theoretical focus, providing fundamental limits on query complexity.  The analysis considers noisy query environments, which is crucial for real-world applications involving human annotators. The comparison with existing work, particularly in crowdsourcing and related information-theoretic concepts, is thorough.  The authors acknowledge limitations and address the issue of query repetition effectively, justifying their focus on non-adaptive querying schemes.  The extensions to k > 2 clusters and the discussion of prior knowledge further enhance the generality and practical relevance of the results.

However, the paper could benefit from several improvements. The experimental section is relatively limited. While the authors mention experiments on real datasets, the details provided are sparse.  Including more comprehensive results, perhaps with comparisons to baseline clustering algorithms beyond just spectral clustering, would strengthen the empirical validation. The presentation of the theoretical results could also be improved. While the main ideas are clear, the numerous theorems and equations can be dense and challenging to follow. Providing more intuitive explanations or illustrative examples alongside the formal derivations would enhance clarity. The discussion of related work, although comprehensive, could be more focused on directly comparing the proposed approach with existing crowdsourcing algorithms for entity resolution and clustering.  Specifically, contrasting the query complexity of the proposed AND query scheme with that of existing adaptive querying algorithms (mentioned in the introduction) would be valuable.  The notation could be standardized; for instance, the use of both 'm' and 'd' to represent different parameters throughout the paper can be confusing.  The assumptions made regarding the independence of query errors should be discussed in greater detail.  While the authors justify this assumption, acknowledging potential deviations in real-world scenarios and discussing the implications would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis providing lower bounds on query complexity.
*   Introduction of AND queries as a more efficient alternative to XOR queries.
*   Consideration of noisy query environments.
*   Experimental validation on real datasets (though limited).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation.
*   Dense theoretical presentation requiring significant effort to follow.
*   Lack of detailed comparison with adaptive querying algorithms.
*   Potential for clearer notation and more in-depth discussion of assumptions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions to the area of semisupervised clustering and crowdsourcing. While improvements could be made in terms of experimental validation and presentation clarity, the originality and technical soundness of the work justify acceptance. The potential impact of the findings on the design of efficient and scalable clustering algorithms is substantial.  A minor revision, focusing on expanding the experimental results and enhancing the clarity of the theoretical presentation, is recommended.
