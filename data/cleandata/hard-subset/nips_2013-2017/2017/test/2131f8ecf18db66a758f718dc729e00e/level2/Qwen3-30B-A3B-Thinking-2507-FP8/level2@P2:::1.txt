

This paper establishes an interesting connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding in information theory. The authors propose a model where an oracle can answer pairwise queries about element relationships (specifically "same cluster" queries or AND queries) to cluster unlabeled elements with minimal queries. The key contribution is showing that AND queries significantly outperform traditional "same cluster" queries for approximate recovery, with theoretical guarantees and experimental validation.

The paper's main strengths lie in its theoretical contributions. The authors successfully establish the equivalence between the clustering problem and locally encodable source coding, providing novel information-theoretic lower bounds for both exact and approximate recovery. Their proof of the suboptimality of "same cluster" queries (equivalent to XOR operations) for approximate recovery is particularly compelling, leveraging a classical result by Massey to show fundamental limitations. The proposed AND query scheme with its theoretical guarantees for approximate recovery (Theorem 4) and the main algorithmic contribution for noisy queries (Theorem 5) represent significant advances. The experimental validation on a real crowdsourcing dataset with 100 action and 100 romantic movies is well-executed, showing that AND queries yield significantly fewer errors (105 vs. 234 errors) than "same cluster" queries, which effectively validates the theoretical claims.

The paper is well-written, with clear organization and sufficient mathematical detail. The connection between clustering and source coding is clearly explained, and the authors appropriately place their work in the context of prior research on clustering with queries (citing relevant works like Ashtiani et al., 2016; Mazumdar and Saha, 2017). The technical content is sound, with proofs that appear to be correct and thorough.

However, the paper has some limitations that should be addressed. The main theoretical results focus primarily on the binary case (k=2), with only brief mentions of extensions to larger k. While the authors state their results extend to larger k, the core contributions and most of the proofs are for k=2, which limits the scope of the theoretical contribution. For a conference like NIPS where practical applicability is important, this narrow focus might be seen as a limitation. The real-world experiment, while validating the main claim, is limited in scale (only 10 volunteers, 200 movies total) and might not generalize to more complex or diverse datasets.

The paper also makes the assumption that the prior distribution of labels (or cluster sizes) is known, which is a common assumption in information theory but might be challenging to estimate in practice for many real-world clustering applications. While the authors mention that cluster sizes can be estimated via subsampling, the paper does not explore how estimation errors might affect the performance of their algorithms.

Additionally, the paper claims that "the only place where it is mentioned to the best of our knowledge is in [23]" regarding locally encodable source coding. However, [23] appears to discuss "smooth compression" rather than locally encodable source coding, and the authors might have missed some relevant work on locally encodable codes in the information theory literature. A more thorough literature search in this area would strengthen the paper's claim of novelty.

The paper's significance is strong in the theoretical sense, providing fundamental limits on query complexity for clustering. For practical crowdsourcing applications, the AND query approach could lead to significant cost savings by reducing the number of queries needed. However, the practical impact might be limited by the binary assumption and the need for known cluster sizes.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with novel theoretical contributions that make an important connection between clustering and information theory. The main limitation is the focus on the binary case, which somewhat restricts the applicability of the results. The authors should consider expanding their theoretical analysis to the general k-case or explicitly discussing the challenges of extending their results to multiple clusters. The experimental validation is good but could be strengthened with additional datasets or larger-scale experiments.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the theoretical understanding of query-based clustering and should be accepted with minor revisions addressing the binary case limitation and potential related work. The connection between clustering and source coding is novel and valuable, and the AND query approach represents a significant practical improvement over existing methods.
