## Review of “Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding”

This paper establishes a surprising connection between semisupervised clustering with pairwise queries and locally encodable source coding. The core idea – framing the querying problem as a compression problem where each compressed bit depends on only a few input bits – is novel and allows the authors to leverage information-theoretic tools to derive lower bounds on the number of queries needed for clustering. The paper also proposes using AND queries as an alternative to traditional ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries, demonstrating their potential for improved performance, particularly in approximate recovery scenarios.

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical rigor and the insightful connection it makes between seemingly disparate fields. The lower bounds are well-motivated and derived, and the introduction of AND queries represents a tangible improvement over existing approaches. The authors acknowledge limitations, like the assumption of non-adaptive queries, and justify it in the context of crowdsourcing applications. The experimental validation, albeit limited, provides encouraging evidence supporting the theoretical claims.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The presentation is dense and requires significant background knowledge in information theory to fully grasp. While the theoretical results are strong, the practical implications beyond the specific setting of pairwise queries could be better articulated. The experimental section is somewhat brief. While the authors report validation on real datasets, more extensive experiments, perhaps comparing the performance to existing crowdsourcing algorithms in a wider range of settings, would significantly strengthen the work. Further, while the motivation for non-adaptive queries is reasonable, discussing the potential impact of adaptivity, even briefly, would address a natural question. The connection to other relevant work in active learning and query selection, beyond the cited papers, could also be explored.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and insightful connection between clustering and source coding.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis and derived lower bounds.
*   Practical proposal of AND queries with demonstrated benefits.
*   Acknowledges limitations and provides justifications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Dense presentation requiring specialized knowledge.
*   Limited experimental validation.
*   Missing discussion of adaptive query strategies.
*   Limited connection to broader active learning literature.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution with potential practical implications. While the presentation could be more accessible and the experimental validation more extensive, the novelty and rigor of the work warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically suggesting the authors consider expanding the experimental section and clarifying the broader implications of their findings. A more detailed discussion on how the concepts presented here relate to active learning frameworks would also be valuable.
