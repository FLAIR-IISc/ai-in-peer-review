Summary
The paper studies non-adaptive, budgeted crowdsourced clustering/labeling through the lens of locally encodable source coding. Each query is a simple function of at most Δ labels, and the goal is to minimize the number of queries needed to reconstruct labels exactly or approximately, under noiseless and noisy oracles. The authors (i) formalize an information-theoretic equivalence between semisupervised clustering with local queries and locally encodable source coding, (ii) derive lower bounds on the query complexity under local-encoding constraints, (iii) show limitations of “same-cluster” (XOR) queries for approximate recovery and propose AND queries that can provably use fewer queries, and (iv) design a simple non-adaptive algorithm under noise that achieves (1−δ)-accurate recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) pairwise queries on a d-regular graph via per-node thresholding. A small crowdsourcing experiment (action vs. romance movies) suggests AND queries are easier for humans than same-cluster queries.

Relation to prior work
The crowdsourced clustering literature has examined pairwise same-cluster queries (e.g., Ashtiani et al.) and noisy crowdsourcing (Karger–Oh–Shah). On the information-theoretic side, locally decodable source coding and smooth compression have been studied (Makhdoumi et al., Montanari–Mossel), while locally encodable source coding has seen less formal development. The paper leverages rate-distortion and a classical result by Massey on the suboptimality of linear codes for lossy compression to motivate AND vs. XOR. Overall, the work situates itself reasonably, though some comparisons could be strengthened (see below).

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: A clear and useful reduction of non-adaptive query-efficient clustering to locally encodable source coding, yielding lower bounds and principled design guidance.
- Theory: 
  - Exact recovery (noiseless, k=2): an achievability with sparse-parity queries and ML decoding requiring m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) (< n for a range of p), and a new converse improving on n h(p) for some p.
  - Approximate recovery (noiseless): a locality-aware lower bound beyond standard rate-distortion; evidence (and at p=1/2 a theorem) that XOR is fundamentally limited for lossy recovery; an AND-based achievability with an explicit performance expression that, for p=1/2, beats the XOR bound.
  - Noisy queries (k≥2): a simple, non-adaptive, computationally efficient algorithm on a d-regular graph achieving (1−δ) accuracy with m = O(n log(k/δ)) pairwise AND-like queries; high-probability guarantees via dependency-graph concentration.
- Practical angle: The AND-vs-XOR human study (albeit small) aligns with the modeling intuition; the algorithm uses only local counts and prior sizes.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Computational tractability for exact recovery: Theorem 1 relies on ML decoding over a typical set, which is intractable; no efficient decoder is provided. This limits the algorithmic impact of the exact-recovery achievability.
- Dependence on a conjecture: The comparison that AND outperforms XOR for approximate recovery leans on a conjectured XOR lower bound except at p=1/2. It would be stronger to provide an unconditional lower bound for XOR (even if looser) and compare against that.
- Noisy-case query count inconsistency: The paper repeatedly states the scheme uses “nd^2 queries,” but a d-regular graph on n vertices has |E| = nd/2 edges/queries, which is also what the experiment uses (e.g., n=200, d=10, queries=1000). This is a significant clarity error that should be corrected throughout; the stated O(n log(k/δ)) rate should be expressed in terms of m=nd/2.
- Practicality for k>2: The proposed k-ary pairwise query “return i if both labels equal i else 0” may be much harder for humans than binary answers; the empirical study only covers k=2. The practical claim that AND-like queries are easier than same-cluster is not supported for multi-class settings.
- Theorem 3 (noiseless, approximate recovery) is opaque in the main text; the statement is hard to interpret, and there is no numerical comparison to achievability to show tightness.
- Missing corollaries for AND performance: Theorem 4 provides a complicated exact expression. Extracting an interpretable asymptotic (e.g., δ ≈ p e^{-2m/n}) would make the narrative and comparisons clearer.
- Sensitivity to prior misspecification: The noisy-case algorithm and thresholds depend on p and q; robustness is not analyzed. The “use ni/n as priors” theorem is helpful but lacks finite-sample guidance.
- Empirical evaluation is limited: small-scale, two-class only, no comparison against strong baselines for noisy same-cluster (e.g., spectral or SDP methods tuned for noisy labels), and no human study for k>2.

Quality
The technical ideas are sound overall, and many arguments are plausible. Some proofs depend on typical-set and expurgation arguments (fine for IT converses), but achievability for exact recovery is not algorithmic. The noisy-case analysis is clean but hides constants and has the query-count inconsistency. The reliance on a conjecture for XOR is a weakness in the comparative story.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, but the nd^2 vs. nd/2 confusion is serious and must be fixed. The k-ary threshold Ci should be derived more transparently, with parentheses clarified. Theorem 3 needs more intuition and a plot versus achievability. Extracting simple corollaries from Theorem 4 would improve readability.

Originality
The locally encodable source coding perspective for query-based clustering and the resulting locality-aware bounds appear novel. Using AND queries and showing their advantage for lossy recovery in this setting is also original.

Significance
The results advance our theoretical understanding of non-adaptive query-efficient clustering and suggest a practically relevant design (AND queries) at least for k=2. The O(n log(k/δ)) guarantee under noise with a simple decoder is appealing. The lack of efficient exact-recovery algorithms and limited empirical validation temper the impact.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify the total number of queries in the noisy scheme: is it m=nd/2? If so, adjust theorems and experiments accordingly.
- Can you provide an unconditional lower bound for XOR (even if weaker) to avoid relying on the conjecture in comparisons?
- For Theorem 4, can you state a clean asymptotic corollary (e.g., δ ≈ p e^{-2m/n})?
- How sensitive is Algorithm 1 to misspecifying p and q? Any adaptive thresholding possible?
- For k>2, are there user studies or simulations indicating your k-ary pairwise query is practical and robust?

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the query-count inconsistency and clarify all constants in the noisy-case bounds.
- Provide a simple, interpretable corollary to Theorem 4 and plot it against lower bounds.
- Add robustness analysis to prior/noise misspecification and correlated noise.
- Strengthen empirical comparisons, including state-of-the-art methods using same-cluster queries under noise and, if possible, a multi-class user study.
- Discuss computational issues for exact recovery and whether sparse-graph code decoding (BP) could be adapted.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and insightful theoretical framework; new lower bounds with locality.
- Clear message that AND queries can outperform XOR for lossy recovery; simple noisy algorithm with strong scaling.
- Some empirical validation and human factors insight (for k=2).

Cons for acceptance
- Significant clarity error (query counts) and reliance on a conjectured XOR bound.
- Exact-recovery achievability is not computational.
- Limited empirical scope and unclear practicality for k>2.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on fixing the clarity issues and improving the presentation of approximate-recovery results.
