

This paper establishes a novel equivalence between semisupervised clustering with pairwise queries and locally encodable source coding in information theory, offering significant theoretical and practical contributions to the field. The main claims are: (1) semisupervised clustering with pairwise queries is equivalent to locally encodable source coding; (2) AND queries significantly outperform standard "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery; and (3) the authors derive tight information-theoretic lower bounds for query complexity.

The paper's theoretical contributions are well-supported with rigorous proofs for both lower and upper bounds on query complexity. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of noiseless and noisy query scenarios, with Theorems 1-5 establishing provable guarantees for exact and approximate recovery. The experimental validation using crowdsourced movie genre classification (100 action, 100 romantic movies) effectively demonstrates the practical superiority of AND queries over "same cluster" queries (105 vs. 234 errors in 1,000 queries), aligning with their theoretical predictions.

Strengths include the elegant theoretical framework connecting two seemingly disparate fields (clustering and information theory), the clear demonstration of AND queries' superiority through both theory and experiment, and the practical relevance to crowdsourcing applications where reducing query costs is critical. The paper also properly situates its work within the NIPS literature, citing recent relevant papers on clustering with crowdsourcing [4, 8, 21, 22].

A few limitations should be noted: the paper assumes independent query errors, which may not perfectly reflect real-world crowd behavior, and the experimental section could better discuss implementation challenges of AND queries in large-scale systems. Additionally, while the paper mentions extensions to k>2 clusters, the theoretical results for k>2 are less developed than for k=2.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field, advancing both theoretical understanding and practical approaches to semisupervised clustering. The theoretical insights are sound, the experimental validation is appropriate, and the work clearly demonstrates how information-theoretic principles can improve practical clustering algorithms. The novelty is substantial, as the authors identify fundamental limitations of "same cluster" queries and propose a theoretically grounded, practically superior alternative. This work should be accepted as it represents a meaningful advance in the state of the art for clustering with limited supervision.
