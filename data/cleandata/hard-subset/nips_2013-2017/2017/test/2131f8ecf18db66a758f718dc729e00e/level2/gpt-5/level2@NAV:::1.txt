Summary and relation to prior work: The paper studies nonadaptive, semisupervised clustering via local queries and establishes an information-theoretic equivalence to locally encodable source coding. Main claims: (1) Exact and approximate recovery lower bounds under locality constraints (∆-ary queries), including bounds that can beat the classical nH(p) in certain regimes; (2) fundamental limitations of pairwise “same-cluster” (XOR) queries for approximate recovery, with a constructive alternative using pairwise AND queries; (3) in the presence of independent query noise, a simple thresholding algorithm over a d-regular graph achieves (1−δ) recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive queries; (4) small-scale crowdsourcing experiments support that AND queries are easier and more reliable than XOR. The work connects to crowdsourced ER [Ashtiani et al., Karger–Oh–Shah, Vesdapunt et al.] and to local decoding/smooth compression [Makhdoumi et al., Montanari–Mossel], contributing new results specifically for local encoding.

Quality: Theorems are well-motivated; the exact-recovery achievability (via ML over sparse random parity queries) and converse are sound at a high level, though ML is computationally impractical. Approximate-recovery lower bounds under locality and the use of Massey-type arguments to show XOR suboptimality are appropriate. The noisy-case algorithm is analyzed via Chernoff bounds and dependency arguments; guarantees scale as O(n log(k/δ)). Experimental validation is limited (n≈200, 10 volunteers) but consistent with the theory.

Clarity: Generally clear, though the “equivalence” to locally encodable codes would benefit from a formal theorem statement and a precise mapping. Some proofs are deferred; constants and regimes of tightness could be more explicit. Minor typos and notational overload exist.

Originality: The local-encoding viewpoint for query-based clustering and the AND-vs-XOR performance separation for approximate/noisy recovery appear novel. Lower bounds that improve on nH(p) under locality are also new to my knowledge.

Significance and usefulness: The results suggest a practical change in query design (favor AND) for crowdsourcing systems and provide rate-type guidance on query budgets. The simple algorithm is scalable and nonadaptive.

Limitations and suggestions:
- Formalize the equivalence claim and discuss computational aspects (beyond existence via ML) for exact recovery.
- Compare empirically against strong baselines (e.g., spectral/SDP with same budget) under noisy feedback and varying priors.
- Explore robustness to correlated or worker-dependent noise; discuss heterogeneity.
- Clarify tightness of bounds for general ∆ and multiary outputs; extend lower bounds for k>2 more explicitly.
- Report confidence intervals and statistical tests in the crowdsourcing study.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Conceptually fresh connection; clear theoretical advances; practical, simple algorithm; empirical indication that AND queries reduce human error.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited empirical validation; some achievability relies on computationally infeasible ML; parts of the “equivalence” are informal; assumptions (independent noise, known priors) may be strong.
