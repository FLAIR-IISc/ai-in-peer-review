Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies nonadaptive, semisupervised clustering (multiclass labeling) with a budget of simple queries that each involve at most Δ elements and return a (possibly noisy) discrete answer. The central conceptual contribution is an equivalence between this model and locally encodable source coding: each query answer is a compressed bit that depends on only a constant number of labels. This perspective yields new information-theoretic lower bounds and motivates alternative query primitives beyond the widely used pairwise “same-cluster?” (XOR) query.

Three regimes are treated:
- Noiseless exact recovery (k = 2, Δ ≥ 2): A sparse-parity (XOR) query design and ML decoding achieve recovery with m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries for α = 0.5[1 + (1 − 4p(1 − p))^Δ], strictly below n for a range of p, together with a new converse using degree-limited arguments that can be stronger than the classic n h(p) bound.
- Noiseless approximate recovery (k = 2): A general lower bound accounting for local encodability is established via rate-distortion considerations. The limitations of XOR (linear) queries are connected to classical results of Ancheta–Massey on linear codes for lossy compression. A concrete, non-linear pairwise AND-query scheme is proposed with an explicit error expression; the AND scheme outperforms known or conjectured limits for XOR in various regimes.
- Noisy approximate recovery (general k): A pairwise k-ary “AND-like” query that returns i if both endpoints have label i and 0 otherwise is combined with a simple threshold decoder on a d-regular graph. With independent per-query noise (multiclass symmetric channel with error q), the scheme achieves (1 − δ)-fraction correct labels with m = O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive queries. Theoretical guarantees are complemented by a small crowdsourcing experiment on movies and a synthetic comparison to spectral clustering with same-cluster queries.

The work connects to and extends prior lines on crowdsourcing for clustering and entity resolution with pairwise queries [4, 8, 20–22, 30], on coding-theoretic approaches to crowdsourcing [28], and on (locally) decodable/compressible codes [16, 18, 19, 23, 25, 26]. The explicit focus on local encodability constraints for source coding appears novel within this application, and the use of non-linear AND queries for improved approximate recovery is a notable departure from XOR-based designs.

Strengths:
- Clear conceptual bridge between nonadaptive query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding; this framing yields insightful and in some cases tighter lower bounds.
- Provable improvements for approximate recovery via AND queries, with explicit performance expressions and intuitions (nonlinearity breaks the linear rate-distortion limitations).
- Noisy setting (general k) with a simple, scalable decoder achieving m = O(n log(k/δ)) queries; the dependence on δ is nearly optimal for many crowdsourcing scenarios.
- Attention to practical aspects: independence/no-repeat assumptions common in crowdsourcing are respected, and a small real-data study suggests AND queries are easier for humans than same-cluster queries.
- Results for both exact and approximate recovery, and both noiseless and noisy regimes, provide a reasonably complete picture of the nonadaptive case.

Weaknesses and points for clarification:
- Decoder complexity in the exact noiseless regime relies on ML over the typical set with sparse-parity queries, which is exponential. While acceptable for achievability, it would be valuable to discuss efficient decoding (e.g., message passing on sparse factor graphs) and whether performance similar to Theorem 1 is attainable efficiently.
- The converse in Theorem 2 is promising but somewhat terse. The optimization over ρ, assumptions on typicality, and the precise entropy units/log bases would benefit from a more detailed, self-contained argument and a discussion of tightness versus achievability.
- The approximate-recovery lower bound (Theorem 3) is technically heavy with intuition deferred; providing a simplified corollary or an interpretable scaling (e.g., a tail bound form) would enhance accessibility.
- The comparison to XOR relies partly on a conjectured bound (Conjecture 1), known only at p = 0.5. Claims that AND “beats” XOR should be phrased carefully or supplemented with proven XOR converses in regimes beyond p = 0.5 and/or with empirical comparisons under matched conditions.
- In the noisy k-class setting, the query output is k-ary. For practical crowdsourcing, k-ary answers may be more demanding than binary. A discussion of how to implement the same logic via a few binary questions per pair (e.g., one-vs-rest AND checks) and the impact on query complexity would strengthen practical relevance.
- The experimental validation is limited: a relatively small human study with 1000 pairwise answers and a synthetic comparison to spectral clustering. More extensive synthetic evaluations (varying p, q, k, graph degree, and δ) and comparisons against strong baselines using same-cluster queries (including algorithms optimized for noisy answers and nonadaptive designs) would provide a fuller empirical picture.
- Some proofs (e.g., Theorem 6) and constants in Theorem 5 are deferred; clearer guidance on choosing thresholds Ci and the exact dependence of constants on p, q, k would aid reproducibility.

Quality:
The theoretical contributions appear technically sound and are grounded in standard information-theoretic tools (typicality, expurgation, rate-distortion, Chernoff bounds) and sparse-graph constructions. The use of non-linear queries and corresponding analyses is well-motivated. Some derivations are compressed; expanding key steps in the appendix would improve rigor.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear, though notation and assumptions (entropy base, typical sets, noise models) should be made unambiguous in the main text. Providing high-level intuition next to technical statements (e.g., Theorem 3) would help readers. A brief discussion of computational complexity for each regime would be useful.

Originality:
The locally encodable source coding framing for this clustering-with-queries model, and the advocacy and analysis of AND queries for approximate recovery, appear novel. The noisy k-class scheme with O(n log(k/δ)) queries is simple and effective.

Significance:
The results contribute new lower bounds and practical query/decoder designs with provable guarantees. Given the activity around crowdsourced clustering/entity resolution, these insights are likely to influence both theory and practice, especially the shift away from XOR/same-cluster queries for approximate recovery.

Suggestions for improvement:
Essential:
- Clarify the status of Conjecture 1 and temper claims that rely on it; add proven XOR converses where possible and strengthen empirical comparisons under identical budgets.
- Provide more detail on Theorem 2 and Theorem 3 (intuition, tightness, units), and explicit constants/threshold selection for Theorem 5 to facilitate implementation.
- Discuss computational considerations and, if possible, propose efficient decoders for the noiseless XOR scheme.

Optional:
- Explore binary decompositions of the k-ary query for practical deployment and quantify the overhead.
- Augment experiments with broader synthetic sweeps and baselines tailored for noisy same-cluster queries; include runtime and robustness to mis-specified priors (beyond Theorem 6).
- Add simplified bounds for the AND scheme (e.g., using Poisson approximations) to give intuitive scaling of δ versus m and p.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel conceptual bridge and nontrivial theoretical advances (lower bounds, achievability).
- Clear, provable advantages of AND queries for approximate recovery, including in noisy settings.
- Practical relevance to crowdsourcing with an initial empirical validation.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited empirical evaluation and reliance on a conjectured XOR lower bound for some comparisons.
- Lack of efficient decoding analysis for the exact recovery scheme.
- Practicality of k-ary queries not fully addressed.

Overall recommendation: lean accept, contingent on strengthening clarity around converses, practical considerations for k-ary queries, and expanded empirical comparisons.
