This paper establishes a surprising connection between semisupervised clustering with binary queries and locally encodable source coding. The authors leverage this connection to derive lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering, and propose a novel querying strategy using AND queries which demonstrably outperforms traditional 'same cluster' (XOR) queries in several scenarios. The work is theoretically grounded, well-motivated, and includes a real-world experiment validating the proposed approach.

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of learning cluster assignments with limited, potentially noisy, pairwise queries. This problem has gained prominence with the rise of crowdsourcing for tasks like entity resolution (e.g., [4, 8, 20, 33]). The central idea is to frame this as a source coding problem where queries are 'encoding' operations and the recovered labels are the 'decoded' data.  Crucially, the authors focus on *locally* encodable codes, where each compressed bit (query answer) depends on only a few input bits (element labels).  This is a less explored area within information theory compared to locally *decodable* codes ([19, 16, 26, 6, 25, 5, 32]) and smooth compression ([23]). By establishing this connection, they are able to apply tools from information theory to obtain query complexity bounds.  The proposal of AND queries as an alternative to XOR queries builds upon the Massey result [17] highlighting limitations of linear codes, and represents a novel contribution.  The consideration of noisy query answers, and the validation via Amazon Mechanical Turk experiments ([9, 29]), further strengthens the practical relevance of the work.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Connection:** The linking of semisupervised clustering to locally encodable source coding is a significant and insightful contribution. This provides a fresh perspective and opens up new avenues for theoretical analysis.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is rigorous in its derivations of lower bounds and algorithmic guarantees. The proofs appear sound, and the theoretical results are clearly presented.  The use of information-theoretic tools is appropriate and effective.
* **Practical Relevance:** The consideration of noisy queries and the experimental validation on a real crowdsourcing platform (Amazon Mechanical Turk) enhance the practical impact of the work. The empirical results support the theoretical claims.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized, making the complex theoretical concepts accessible. The notation is generally clear, and the key ideas are highlighted effectively.
* **AND Query Proposal:** The introduction of AND queries as a more efficient alternative to XOR queries is a key contribution, supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Scope:** While the AMT experiment provides validation, it is relatively limited in scope. Expanding the experiments to larger datasets and different types of clusters would further strengthen the empirical evidence.  More detailed analysis of the error types observed in the AMT experiments would also be valuable.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of independent query errors, while common (e.g., [10]), might not always hold in real-world scenarios. Exploring more sophisticated error models could be beneficial.
* **Complexity of Proofs:** Some of the proofs, particularly those relating to the noisy query setting, are quite involved and could benefit from more detailed explanations or supplementary material (referenced but not fully included in the provided text).
* **Relationship to Existing Clustering Algorithms:** The paper primarily focuses on query complexity.  A more in-depth comparison with the performance of existing clustering algorithms (e.g., spectral clustering) given the same query budget, beyond the brief mention in the noisy query section, would be helpful.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and significant connection between two seemingly disparate fields (clustering and source coding).
* The theoretical results are rigorous and well-supported.
* The proposed AND query strategy offers a practical improvement over existing approaches.
* The experimental validation provides evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The work addresses a relevant and important problem in the context of crowdsourcing and entity resolution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
* The assumptions regarding query errors might be limiting.
* The complexity of some proofs might hinder understanding.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is insightful, and the resulting theoretical results and practical improvements are significant. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly expanding the experimental evaluation and providing more detailed explanations of the more complex proofs in supplementary material.
