- Introduces a novel information-theoretic framework linking non-adaptive query-based semisupervised clustering to locally encodable source coding.

- Formally establishes equivalence between recovering n labels in k classes via Δ-local queries and a locally encodable source coding problem.

- Derives new information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries needed for exact and approximate label recovery (Theorems 2 and 3).

- Shows that commonly used 'same-cluster' (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery.

- Proposes AND queries as a new query type and proves their superiority for approximate recovery (Theorems 4 and 5).

- Presents an algorithm using AND-style queries that achieves approximate recovery of (1−δ) labels with O(n log(k/δ)) queries in the noisy oracle setting.

- Empirical crowdsourcing experiment validates theory: AND queries produced fewer human errors (105) than same-cluster queries (234) on the same pairs.

- Paper is original and significant, opening a new theoretical lens likely to inspire follow-up work and technique transfer from information theory.

- Technical results are rigorous and non-trivial, with strong theoretical guarantees for the proposed scheme.

- Practical relevance is high: AND queries have intuitive human-answerability benefits and Algorithm 1 is simple and efficient.

- Paper is well-written and structured, moving cleanly from theory to noisy and practical scenarios.

- Suggestion: clarify distinction between labeling vs. clustering since AND queries require semantic labels while same-cluster queries are permutation-invariant.

- Suggestion: discuss robustness of Algorithm 1 to inaccuracies in prior/cluster-size estimates and how performance degrades with estimation error.

- Suggestion: add high-level intuition for the complex lower bound in Theorem 3 to improve accessibility before the appendix.

- Suggestion: expand experimental details for reproducibility (e.g., whether the 10 volunteers answered all queries) and move key synthetic comparison plots into the main paper.

- Overall evaluation: high-quality contribution with minor, addressable suggestions; strong case for acceptance with no major flaws identified.
