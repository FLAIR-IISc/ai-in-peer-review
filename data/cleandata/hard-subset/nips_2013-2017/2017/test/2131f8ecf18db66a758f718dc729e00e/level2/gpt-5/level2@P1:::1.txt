Summary
The paper studies non-adaptive, low-arity query models for semi-supervised clustering and establishes a rigorous equivalence to locally encodable source coding (LESC), where each compressed bit depends on only a constant number Δ of source bits. Leveraging this viewpoint, the authors derive information-theoretic lower bounds and propose simple, non-adaptive query designs and decoders. A central message is that commonly used pairwise “same-cluster” (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery, and that pairwise AND queries can provably achieve much better query–error trade-offs. The paper covers three settings: (i) noiseless exact recovery (k=2), (ii) noiseless approximate recovery (k=2), and (iii) noisy approximate recovery (general k). Key results include: (a) an upper bound for exact recovery using parity (XOR) queries with constant Δ achieving m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries for suitable priors p, strictly below n in a regime of p; (b) lower bounds that account for locality constraints and show limits below the classical n h(p) bound; (c) a new lower bound for approximate recovery with locality constraints and an achievability for AND queries with a simple local decoder; and (d) for noisy queries and general k, a pairwise k-ary “AND” query and a thresholding decoder achieving (1−δ)-accurate recovery with m = O(n log(k/δ)) queries, with supportive crowdsourcing experiments (k=2) showing fewer worker errors on AND vs. same-cluster queries. The work relates to rate–distortion bounds (Cover–Thomas), linear code limitations for lossy compression (Massey/Ancheta), smooth/local coding (Montanari–Mossel; Makhdoumi et al.; Mazumdar et al.), and to the crowdsourcing/ER query literature (e.g., Ashtiani et al., Karger–Oh–Shah).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The modeling and main probabilistic arguments (typical set, expurgation, Chernoff, dependency bounds) are standard and appear correct at a high level. The noisy approximate recovery algorithm and its analysis are clean and compelling. That said, several statements would benefit from careful consolidation and additional detail:
  - Theorem 1: the displayed formula for m alternates between multiplication and division by log(1/α). From the proof, m should scale as n(h(p)+o(1))/log(1/α). Please correct the statement and give the exact base of the logarithm consistently.
  - Theorem 2: the bound involves parameters (ρ, Δ) inside h(·); it would help to state the valid range of ρ ensuring the ball-volume bound applies and to discuss the regime where this improves upon n h(p).
  - Theorem 3: the lower bound for approximate recovery under locality is intriguing but difficult to parse. A proof sketch in the main text (key steps, intuition, regimes where it is tight) would enhance credibility.
  - Conjecture 1 (limits of XOR/same-cluster): relying on a conjectured lower bound for comparison can be misleading. It would be valuable to state precisely what is proved vs. conjectured, and to give unconditional lower bounds (even if weaker) that still demonstrate a gap with AND queries.
- Algorithmic aspects: For exact recovery (Theorem 1), the decoder is ML over a typical set, which is computationally infeasible; clarify this limitation and whether efficient alternatives exist (even at some loss). For the noisy algorithm (Theorem 5), decoding is linear-time and practical, which is a strong plus.
- Empirics: The small-scale crowdsourcing experiment (k=2) supports the behavioral hypothesis that AND queries elicit fewer worker errors than same-cluster queries. It would strengthen the case to add a synthetic study across a range of q, p, d matching the theory, and a comparison against strong baselines that use same-cluster queries with standard aggregation/graph algorithms (e.g., spectral or SDP on noisy edges).

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and motivates the LESC viewpoint convincingly. Some notation/consistency issues should be fixed (Δ vs. d; m=nd^2; log bases; typos).
- Provide more intuition for why AND creates an expectation gap exploitable by thresholds, whereas XOR is symmetric and provides no such gap in the noisy case.
- For k>2, the k-ary AND query model is natural mathematically; for practice, discuss how such a query would be phrased to workers and whether it requires more cognitive load than binary questions.

Originality
- The LESC framing for query-limited clustering and the derived locality-aware bounds are novel and timely.
- The insight that AND queries outperform XOR for approximate recovery (with proofs and algorithms) is original and non-obvious, especially under noise.
- The connection to classical linear-code limits in lossy compression is a nice bridging of areas.

Significance
- The results refine our understanding of fundamental limits for crowdsourced/interactive clustering under locality constraints and suggest a new primitive (AND) with both theoretical and empirical advantages.
- The noisy k-class algorithm with O(n log(k/δ)) queries and a simple decoder is practically relevant.
- The exact recovery result below n queries (for certain p, Δ) is conceptually interesting, though the lack of an efficient decoder may limit immediate impact.

Pros
- Clear conceptual bridge between query-limited clustering and locally encodable compression.
- New lower bounds that incorporate locality.
- Simple, scalable algorithm for noisy approximate recovery with strong guarantees.
- Compelling case and evidence for AND queries in practice.
- Non-adaptive design and linear-time decoding.

Cons
- Some proofs/results (Theorem 3, Conjecture 1 comparison) need tighter, clearer exposition and unconditional statements.
- Exact recovery scheme relies on infeasible ML.
- Evaluation limited in scope (single small real dataset; limited baselines).
- Minor inconsistencies/typos in statements and notation.

Suggestions and questions
1) Please correct Theorem 1’s expression for m and standardize log bases. Provide a short discussion of the computational complexity of decoding and whether sparse-graph decoding ideas (e.g., peeling/LDPC-style) could yield efficient exact or near-exact recovery.
2) For Theorem 3, add a proof sketch and a plot comparing the lower bound to the rate–distortion curve for several Δ to show the penalty from locality.
3) Provide unconditional lower bounds for same-cluster queries (even if weaker than the Massey-based conjecture) to support the AND-vs-XOR message without relying on conjecture.
4) For k>2, discuss practical phrasing of k-ary AND queries and whether multiple binary questions could simulate them with similar guarantees.
5) Expand experiments: include synthetic results sweeping p, q, d, δ, compare to strong same-cluster baselines (e.g., spectral clustering on a noisy similarity graph with calibrated thresholds), and report wall-clock/labeling costs.
6) Minor: clean up duplicate references and ensure consistent citation formatting.

Overall
This work makes a compelling theoretical and practical case for rethinking the primitive queries used in crowdsourced clustering, grounded in an elegant LESC framework. With clarifications and stronger empirical comparisons, it would be a valuable addition to the literature.
