

This paper establishes an interesting connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding in information theory. The authors consider a model where the goal is to recover cluster labels for n elements using a limited number of queries to an oracle, where each query involves at most Δ elements and returns a binary answer. The key insight is that this problem maps naturally to locally encodable source coding, where each compressed bit depends on only a constant number of input bits.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides theoretical lower bounds on the number of queries required for exact recovery of labels, which are strictly better than the naive n bound for certain label distributions when using pairwise 'same cluster' (XOR) queries. Second, the authors demonstrate that 'same cluster' queries are fundamentally limited for approximate recovery, and propose AND queries as a superior alternative that achieves significantly better query complexity for recovering (1-δ) fraction of labels. Third, they develop a practical algorithm for noisy query settings that achieves (1-δ)-good recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries, validated through a real crowdsourcing experiment on movie labels.

The paper's strengths are its theoretical novelty in connecting two seemingly disparate fields, the clear presentation of the theoretical results, and the practical validation through crowdsourcing experiments. The authors provide tight lower bounds for both exact and approximate recovery scenarios, and their AND query approach is well-motivated and theoretically justified. The real-world experiment with movie labels demonstrates the practical advantages of AND queries over 'same cluster' queries, with 105 errors for AND queries versus 234 for 'same cluster' queries in the same dataset.

The paper's technical content is strong, with careful theoretical analysis supported by clear proofs. The connection to information theory is well-justified, and the authors appropriately cite related work on locally decodable codes and source coding. The results for the k=2 case are particularly compelling, with the AND query approach outperforming 'same cluster' queries across the entire query-performance trade-off curve.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The connection to semisupervised clustering is somewhat tenuous in the introduction - the authors state that their work is "equivalent" to semisupervised clustering, but the problem setup is more specifically a type of active learning with constraints on query complexity rather than standard semisupervised clustering. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how this model fits within the broader context of semisupervised learning.

The experimental validation, while helpful, is limited to a single dataset with 200 movies. While the qualitative results support the theoretical claims, the authors could strengthen this section by including more detailed analysis of the error patterns or comparisons with alternative query strategies beyond the 'same cluster' comparison.

The theoretical results for k>2 clusters are presented in Section 3.3, but the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the AND query approach extends beyond k=2 and whether similar advantages hold for larger k. The authors mention that the AND query is equivalent to the binary AND for k=2, but don't elaborate on how the query function generalizes for k>2.

The paper is well-written and generally clear, though some technical details in the proofs could be made more accessible. The authors effectively use the information theory perspective to derive meaningful bounds, and the connection to source coding is well-motivated.

In terms of significance, this work provides a theoretically grounded approach to query-efficient clustering that has practical implications for crowdsourcing applications. The authors demonstrate that a simple change in query type (from 'same cluster' to AND queries) can lead to substantial improvements in query efficiency, which is valuable for real-world applications where each query has a cost.

The paper is original in its perspective, connecting clustering to locally encodable source coding. While there is related work on query complexity for clustering (e.g., Ashtiani et al. 2016), the authors' information-theoretic approach and the specific AND query construction represent a novel contribution to the field.

Overall, this paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical implications. The connection between clustering and source coding is insightful, and the AND query approach provides a meaningful improvement over existing methods. The theoretical results are sound, and the experimental validation provides good support for the claims. The paper would benefit from slightly more precise connection to semisupervised clustering literature and more detailed experiments for k>2, but these are minor points for a strong theoretical contribution.

The paper meets all the quality criteria: technically sound, well-supported by theory, and complete as a theoretical contribution. It is clearly written and well-organized. The originality is strong, as the authors establish a new connection between two fields and provide novel query strategies. The significance is high for both theoretical information theory and practical crowdsourcing applications.

I recommend acceptance for NIPS 2017. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the theoretical understanding of query-efficient clustering with clear practical implications.
