This paper establishes a novel and insightful connection between the problem of semi-supervised clustering with queries and the information-theoretic concept of locally encodable source coding. The authors leverage this framework to derive new lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery of cluster labels. The paper’s main practical contribution is to demonstrate the limitations of standard pairwise “same-cluster” (XOR) queries and propose pairwise AND queries as a superior alternative, particularly in the noisy, approximate recovery setting common in crowdsourcing.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**
1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of framing the query-based clustering problem as one of locally encodable source coding is highly original and provides a powerful new lens for analysis. The problem itself is of significant interest to the NIPS community, bridging theory and practical applications like crowdsourcing and entity resolution. The paper’s proposal to use AND queries over the standard XOR queries is a simple yet impactful contribution that could change how practitioners design query-based systems.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. It provides new theoretical bounds and presents a clear, simple algorithm (Algorithm 1) for recovery with noisy AND queries. The claims are well-supported by a combination of theoretical analysis (with clear proof sketches) and empirical validation.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** A key strength is the inclusion of a real-world crowdsourcing experiment. While small-scale, it effectively demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed AND queries and shows that the empirical results align well with the theoretical predictions. The finding that workers made fewer errors on AND queries than on "same-cluster" queries provides compelling evidence for the authors' claims.

**Weaknesses (and Suggestions for Improvement):**
1.  **Clarity of Assumptions:** The proof for the performance of AND queries (Theorem 4) relies on a Poisson degree distribution assumption. While this is a common and reasonable approximation for random graphs, it would strengthen the paper to explicitly state this is an approximation and briefly justify its use.

2.  **Experimental Scale:** The crowdsourcing experiment, while a major plus, is based on 200 items and 10 volunteers. The authors should briefly acknowledge the limitations of this scale and perhaps discuss the expected scalability of their approach.

Overall, the paper presents a complete and compelling piece of work. The novel theoretical connection, the practical significance of the proposed query type, and the strong support from both theory and a real-world experiment make it a valuable contribution. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the paper's core merits. I strongly recommend acceptance.
