This paper introduces a novel framework for semisupervised clustering by leveraging the concept of locally encodable source coding. The authors show that a recently popular model of semisupervised clustering can be reduced to locally encodable source coding, leading to new information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required to recover the correct labeling.

The paper's main contributions include (1) noiseless queries and exact recovery, where the authors provide a querying scheme that asks αn, α < 1 number of nonadaptive pairwise 'same cluster' queries and recovers all labels with high probability for a range of prior probabilities; (2) noiseless queries and approximate recovery, where the authors provide a new lower bound on the number of queries required to recover (1− δ) fraction of the labels δ > 0 and propose a new querying strategy based on AND operation that performs substantially better; and (3) noisy queries and approximate recovery, where the authors show that even in the presence of noise in the query answers, it is possible to recover (1− δ) proportion of all labels correctly with only O(n log kδ ) nonadaptive queries.

The paper's strengths include its novel application of locally encodable source coding to semisupervised clustering, its provision of new information-theoretic lower bounds, and its proposal of new querying strategies that outperform existing methods. The paper's experiments on real crowdsourced datasets also demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.

However, the paper's weaknesses include its assumption of a specific noise model for the oracle's answers, which may not be realistic in all scenarios. Additionally, the paper's analysis of the AND query scheme relies on a Poisson approximation, which may not be accurate for all values of the parameters. Furthermore, the paper's experimental evaluation is limited to a single dataset, and more extensive experiments would be needed to fully validate the proposed algorithms.

Overall, this paper makes significant contributions to the field of semisupervised clustering and provides new insights into the fundamental limits of querying strategies. Its proposed algorithms and analysis techniques are likely to be of interest to researchers in machine learning, information theory, and data compression.
