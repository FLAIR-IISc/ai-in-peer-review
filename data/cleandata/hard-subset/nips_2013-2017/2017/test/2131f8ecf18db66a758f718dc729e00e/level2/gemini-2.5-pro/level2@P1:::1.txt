This paper introduces a novel and insightful connection between the problem of non-adaptive, semi-supervised clustering and the information-theoretic concept of locally encodable source coding. The problem setup involves determining the true labels of 'n' elements by asking a limited number of non-adaptive queries, where each query involves a small, constant number of elements (Δ). This framework is highly relevant for applications like crowdsourcing where query complexity must be minimized. The authors leverage this connection to derive new information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery of labels. A key contribution is the proposal of "AND" queries as an alternative to the more common "same-cluster" (XOR) queries. The paper provides strong theoretical arguments and experimental evidence to show that AND queries can be significantly more efficient, particularly for approximate recovery in noisy settings. The main algorithmic result is an efficient querying scheme and recovery algorithm for the noisy, approximate recovery case that requires only O(n log(k/δ)) queries to recover a (1-δ) fraction of labels.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The formulation of the querying problem as one of locally encodable source coding is highly original and provides a powerful new theoretical lens for this area. This fresh perspective allows the authors to derive fundamental limits and offers a principled way to reason about the efficiency of different query types. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could inspire future work at the intersection of clustering, crowdsourcing, and information theory.

2.  **Novelty of AND Queries:** The paper makes a compelling case for the use of AND queries. The demonstration of the theoretical sub-optimality of 'same-cluster' (XOR) queries for approximate recovery (building on Massey's work) and the subsequent analysis showing the superiority of AND queries is a major result. This challenges a common assumption in the literature and provides a practical, well-motivated alternative. The intuitive reason provided—that AND queries can be easier for human workers to answer correctly—is substantiated by a small real-world experiment, adding practical weight to the theoretical findings.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically strong. It presents new, non-trivial lower bounds for locally encodable source coding (Theorems 2 and 3) and provides constructive achievability results (Theorems 1, 4, and 5). The analysis spans several important practical settings: noiseless and noisy oracles, and exact and approximate recovery goals. The main algorithmic contribution for noisy, approximate recovery (Algorithm 1) is simple, efficient, and well-supported by a rigorous analysis.

4.  **Completeness:** The work is comprehensive, covering theoretical lower bounds, the design of new querying and decoding algorithms, and validation through both real and synthetic experiments. This combination of theory and practice makes the paper's contributions more impactful.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity of some Theoretical Results:** While generally clear, the presentation of some results could be improved. For instance, in Section 3.1, the paper states that the proposed scheme requires fewer than 'n' queries for certain priors. While true, this might be slightly confusing as the primary comparison for a source coding problem is usually the entropy bound, nH(p). The provided upper bound m = n(h(p)+o(1))/log(1/α) is indeed less than n for some p, but it is actually greater than nH(p) (since log(1/α) < 1 in that regime). Clarifying that the comparison is against the baseline of n-1 deterministic queries would strengthen the message.

2.  **Scope of Experimental Validation:** The real-world crowdsourcing experiment is a welcome addition and provides excellent validation for the claims about AND queries. However, its scale is relatively small (200 items, 10 volunteers). While this is a primarily theoretical paper and the experiment is illustrative, the empirical claims would be further strengthened by a larger-scale study.

3.  **Applicability of AND Queries:** The paper motivates AND queries in a setting where one label (e.g., 'action movie' vs. 'not action movie') is asymmetrically of interest. It would be beneficial to include a short discussion on how the AND query concept translates to more symmetric multi-class problems where there isn't a natural '0' or default state (e.g., clustering news articles into 'sports', 'politics', or 'finance').

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel and powerful connection between semi-supervised clustering and locally encodable source coding.
*   Presents a significant and practical finding: that AND queries can be provably better than standard 'same-cluster' queries.
*   The paper is technically solid with new lower bounds and efficient algorithms.
*   The work is well-rounded, with theoretical contributions supported by experimental validation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental validation, while positive, is on a small scale.
*   Minor points of clarity in the presentation of theoretical results could be improved.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant contributions in originality, technical depth, and potential impact. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the core strengths of the work. The novel information-theoretic perspective and the introduction and analysis of AND queries are important results that are likely to be influential for researchers in active learning, clustering, and crowdsourcing. I strongly recommend acceptance.
