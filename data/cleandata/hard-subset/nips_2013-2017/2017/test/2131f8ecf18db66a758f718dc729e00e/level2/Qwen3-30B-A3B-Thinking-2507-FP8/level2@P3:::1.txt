

This paper establishes a novel connection between semisupervised clustering with pairwise queries and locally encodable source coding in information theory. The work demonstrates that the problem of recovering cluster labels through queries can be equivalently framed as a source coding problem where each compressed bit depends on only a constant number of input bits. The paper makes several significant contributions: it provides new information-theoretic lower bounds on query complexity for exact and approximate recovery, shows that "same cluster" (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery, and proposes AND queries that achieve substantially better performance. The authors also develop a practical algorithm using AND queries that works well even with noisy oracle responses, validated through real crowdsourcing experiments.

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical novelty and rigorous analysis. The connection to locally encodable source coding is insightful and provides a fresh perspective on a well-studied problem in crowdsourced clustering. The information-theoretic lower bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) are non-trivial and significantly improve upon prior work, particularly for the exact recovery case with two clusters. The theoretical analysis of AND queries (Theorems 4 and 5) is thorough, demonstrating their superiority over XOR queries both theoretically and empirically. The paper also makes a valuable contribution by addressing the noisy query setting, which is more realistic for crowdsourcing applications than the noiseless model.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient technical detail for experts to reproduce the results. The theoretical results are well-supported by both rigorous analysis and empirical validation. The real crowdsourcing experiment on movie classification (Section 4) effectively demonstrates the practical advantages of AND queries over "same cluster" queries, with 105 erroneous responses for AND queries compared to 234 for "same cluster" queries. The experimental setup is reasonable for a proof-of-concept validation.

The paper's main weakness is the limited scope of the theoretical results. While the authors extend their analysis to k>2 clusters in Section 3.3, the main contributions and most detailed analysis focus on k=2 (binary labels). The connection to previous work on locally encodable source coding could be strengthened; while the paper mentions [23] as the only prior reference to this concept, a more comprehensive literature review of related notions like locally decodable codes [19, 16, 26, 6] would better contextualize the novelty. The proof of Theorem 3, while referenced in the supplementary material, is not fully elaborated in the main text, which might make it difficult for readers to assess its validity without consulting the appendix.

The significance of the work is high for the information theory and machine learning communities. The problem of reducing query complexity in crowdsourced clustering is important for practical applications, and the authors provide a theoretically grounded solution that outperforms existing methods. The AND query approach has clear practical advantages, as demonstrated by the lower error rate in the crowdsourcing experiment. The paper advances the state of the art by providing tighter theoretical bounds and a more efficient querying strategy.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by bridging information theory and clustering, offering a new perspective that could inspire further research in both areas. The theoretical results are sound and well-supported, and the practical validation adds credibility to the approach. The authors successfully demonstrate that simple modifications to the query type can lead to substantial improvements in efficiency and accuracy.

For improvement, the authors could provide more discussion of how their results might generalize to other query types or more complex clustering scenarios. The paper might benefit from a more detailed comparison with recent work on clustering with side information [21, 22, 27], particularly regarding query complexity. The connection to prior work on locally encodable source coding could also be expanded to better establish the novelty of their results.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical implications. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant advance in understanding the query complexity of clustering with pairwise queries. The proposed AND query approach represents a meaningful improvement over existing methods, both theoretically and empirically. The work is highly relevant to NIPS, addressing a fundamental problem in machine learning with a novel theoretical perspective that has practical applications in crowdsourcing systems. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission.
