This paper explores a clustering problem where the goal is to reconstruct true labels of elements from potentially noisy, non-adaptive query answers, each involving only a few elements. It derives lower bounds on the number of queries required for exact or partial reconstruction of ground-truth labels in various scenarios and proposes an “AND-queries” algorithm applicable to arbitrary k-cluster cases, evaluating its performance with a real dataset.

While the paper attempts theoretical analysis and algorithm development with real-data validation, it exhibits limitations in presentation, clarity of results, and comparison to related work. Significant revisions and potential new developments are needed for publication.

1. (Constraint: No Repeating Queries): This constraint appears restrictive. Relaxing it—allowing the same queries to mitigate noise—may yield better performance. Justification for the restriction requires extensive real-data simulations comparing the proposed algorithm to one tailored for unrestricted queries, demonstrating superior performance. The authors’ concern about independence vs. dependence of multiple answers to the same query can be addressed by assuming independent answers from distinct annotators.

2. (Theorem 1): The noiseless, exact recovery setting is also examined in [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which also considers XOR-operation ('same-cluster' queries) in a non-adaptive measurement setting. However, the result in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] doesn't match Theorem 1. Specifically, for p=1/2 and a constant Δ, Theorem 1’s query complexity is m=n/log2, while [Ahn-Lee-Suh] predicts m=n log n / Δ. This discrepancy requires clarification. The proof of Theorem 1 is also difficult to follow.

3. (Theorem 3): The lower bound expression for δ is hard to interpret. Determining the relationship between m, n, and δ—allowing for direct assessment of performance improvement—is difficult.

4. (Theorem 4): The same comment applies as for Theorem 3. Additionally, what is the gap between the upper and lower bounds? Can it be unbounded? The proof is also difficult to follow.

5. (Figure 2): No comparison is made to the standard ‘same cluster’ query method; the comparison is only to its lower bound. Is there a crossover point when compared to the full method? The paper claims the proposed approach outperforms the ‘same cluster’ scheme, a claim not supported by Figure 2.

6. (Algorithm 1): The algorithm requires prior knowledge of *p* or the relative sizes of clusters (see Theorem 6). How can this information be obtained practically? Proper justification is needed to validate the algorithm's practicality. The pseudo-code is difficult to understand without reference to the complex proof of Theorem 5.

7. (Figures 3, 4 & 5): No comparison is made to state-of-the-art methods like the ‘same cluster’ query approach, crucial for supporting the claim that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms existing methods.

[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," *Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing*, 2016.
