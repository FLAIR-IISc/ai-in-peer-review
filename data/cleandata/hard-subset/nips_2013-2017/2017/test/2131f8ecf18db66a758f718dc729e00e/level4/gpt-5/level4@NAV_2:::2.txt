This paper studies a clustering/labeling problem where the aim is to recover true labels from possibly noisy, non-adaptive query answers, each involving only a small number of items. It connects a popular semisupervised clustering model to locally encodable source coding, deriving information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries for exact and approximate recovery under various scenarios. It further introduces “AND-queries,” proposes an algorithm that extends to general k-class settings, and evaluates its performance on a real crowdsourced dataset.

While the paper ambitiously spans theory, algorithms, and an empirical study, it has notable issues in exposition, clarity of the formal claims, and positioning against related work. Substantial revisions and possibly additional developments appear necessary before publication. Detailed comments follow.

1. Prohibition on repeating the same query: The restriction against repeated queries seems strong and may harm performance relative to settings that allow repetition, a natural mechanism for mitigating noise. One way to justify this modeling choice would be to provide extensive empirical comparisons against methods that do allow repetition, ideally showing the proposed approach remains competitive or superior under realistic conditions. Otherwise, analyzing the more permissive “repetition allowed” case is arguably more compelling. The dependence issue the paper raises can be sidestepped by assigning identical queries to distinct annotators and assuming independence across annotators, which is common in crowdsourcing models.

2. Theorem 1: Exact recovery in the noiseless, non-adaptive, XOR (“same-cluster”) setting was studied in [Ahn–Lee–Suh], but the scaling stated in Theorem 1 does not align with that prior result. For example, when p = 1/2 and Δ is constant, Theorem 1 suggests m ≈ n/log 2 = n, whereas [Ahn–Lee–Suh] reports m ≈ (n log n)/Δ. This discrepancy needs a precise explanation (e.g., differences in the query ensemble, decoding assumptions, or success criteria). In addition, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow and would benefit from a clearer, step-by-step argument and explicit specification of logarithm bases and typical-set sizes.

3. Theorem 3: The lower bound on δ is hard to interpret operationally. It is unclear how to invert the expression to obtain a clean requirement on m as a function of n and δ that transparently reveals the gain from allowing δ > 0. Presenting an explicit m ≥ m(n, δ, Δ, p) form (even if approximate or asymptotic) would make the implications much clearer.

4. Theorem 4: Similar interpretability issues arise here. How close is this achievability to the lower bound in Theorem 3 across parameter ranges? Can the gap be arbitrarily large? The proof sketch is also difficult to parse; reorganizing the argument and isolating key probabilistic steps (e.g., the Poisson approximation and the two-hop-neighbor error event) would help.

5. Figure 2: The comparison is against a lower bound for “same-cluster” queries, not against a concrete same-cluster method. The paper claims its approach outperforms same-cluster querying, but Figure 2 alone cannot establish that. It would be important to add a direct comparison to a standard same-cluster scheme (with a specified decoder), and discuss whether and why the curves cross.

6. Algorithm 1: The method requires prior knowledge of p or of relative cluster sizes (per Theorem 6). How should practitioners obtain these reliably? A brief, practical procedure (e.g., a small pilot with O(log n) fully queried pairs) and a robustness analysis to prior misspecification would strengthen the case for practicality. The pseudo-code is also difficult to follow without delving into Theorem 5’s proof; clarifying the choice of thresholds, the role of d, and the dependence on q and k in the main text would improve readability.

7. Figures 3, 4, and 5: There is no comparison to state-of-the-art baselines such as a same-cluster querying method with a standard decoder (e.g., spectral clustering or ML under the given model). Such comparisons are essential to substantiate repeated claims that AND-queries outperform same-cluster schemes.

I have read the rebuttal and found that several points were addressed; two items would still benefit from further clarification in a revision:
1. The role of repeated queries: It remains unclear whether repeating the same query is indeed unhelpful. Did the Nature paper cited actually demonstrate that repetition is not beneficial? Please clarify what is theoretically or empirically known here and how it applies to your setting.
2. Comparison to same-cluster methods: Please incorporate into the paper the detailed discussion provided in the response, including explicit experimental and/or theoretical comparisons that substantiate the claimed advantages of AND-queries.
