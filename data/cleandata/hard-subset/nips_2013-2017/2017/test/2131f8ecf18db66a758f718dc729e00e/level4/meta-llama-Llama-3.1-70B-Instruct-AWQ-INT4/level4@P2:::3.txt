This is an interesting paper that explores the task of clustering data points via crowdsourcing. The authors consider the problem of recovering original labels of data points based on the answers to similar cluster queries and propose bounds on the number of queries required to recover the labels considering noisy as well as noiseless answers for the queries. The main contribution of the paper is theoretical, along with some empirical simulations.

The paper is well-written, making it easy to understand the theoretical contributions for a general machine learning audience. The connection with Information theory for deriving the bounds is explained nicely. The authors provide a comprehensive overview of the problem and its relation to previous work, making it easy to follow their arguments.

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical contributions, particularly in the derivation of bounds for the number of queries required to recover the labels. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem, considering both noiseless and noisy answers for the queries. The use of Information theory to derive these bounds is a significant contribution, as it provides a solid foundation for understanding the fundamental limits of the problem.

However, I do have some concerns regarding the paper's contributions. Firstly, the idea of AND queries seems to conflict with the original motivation for crowdsourcing. In the case of an AND query, effectively, the label of the cluster is also given as part of the answer rather than just answering if the data points of interest belong to the same cluster or not. This raises the question of why one would even need to ask those similarity queries, rather than asking the label for a data point itself.

Secondly, the expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1, 2, 3, and 4 are quite complex and may be difficult to interpret. It would be helpful to simplify these expressions while keeping the essence of the results.

Lastly, the experimental study only considers AND queries, which may not be sufficient to validate the paper's contributions. It would be beneficial to include experiments with other types of queries, such as 'same cluster' queries, to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods.

Overall, I am satisfied with the authors' responses to my concerns, and I believe that the paper makes a significant contribution to the field. I vote for the acceptance of this paper.
