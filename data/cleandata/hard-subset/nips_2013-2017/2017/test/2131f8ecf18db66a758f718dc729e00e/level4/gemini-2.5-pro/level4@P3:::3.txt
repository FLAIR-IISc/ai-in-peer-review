### Summary

This paper investigates the problem of semi-supervised clustering where labels for a set of `n` items must be recovered using a limited number of non-adaptive, parallelizable queries. The central contribution is the establishment of a novel equivalence between this clustering problem and the information-theoretic concept of locally encodable source coding, where each compressed bit depends on a small, constant number of input bits.

Leveraging this connection, the authors derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery of labels under noiseless and noisy oracle models. A key finding is the limitation of traditional pairwise "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery. To overcome this, the paper introduces "AND" queries (e.g., "do both items belong to cluster A?") and provides theoretical analysis and algorithms demonstrating their superiority in terms of query complexity, particularly for approximate recovery in noisy settings. The theoretical work is supported by a small-scale crowdsourcing experiment that validates the proposed model and query type.

### Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)

*   **Originality and Significance:** The connection drawn between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding is a primary strength. This is a novel and insightful framing that provides a powerful theoretical lens for a problem of practical importance in crowdsourcing and active learning. It opens up a new avenue for applying information-theoretic tools to this domain.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper presents a solid theoretical contribution. It derives new lower bounds on query complexity (Theorems 2 and 3) that are non-trivial and improve upon standard information-theoretic limits by accounting for the locality constraint of the queries. The analysis of the proposed AND-query-based algorithm is thorough, culminating in a strong performance guarantee (Theorem 5).
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of introducing the information-theoretic perspective and building their arguments step-by-step, making the technical content accessible to a broader machine learning audience. The problem setup and motivations are clearly articulated.
*   **Practical Implications:** The proposal of AND queries is a thought-provoking contribution. The paper provides both theoretical (Theorems 4 and 5) and empirical evidence (Section 4) that this query type can be more efficient and robust to noise than the standard "same cluster" queries. The argument that AND queries may be cognitively easier for crowd workers and thus result in lower error rates is compelling.

### Weaknesses (Points for Improvement)

1.  **Motivation for AND Queries vs. Direct Label Queries:** The paper's core proposal of AND queries raises a fundamental question that could be addressed more explicitly. An AND query, such as "Are both movies 'action' movies?", seems to presuppose that the oracle knows the label ('action'). A reader might naturally ask: why not simply ask a direct label query, e.g., "Is this movie an 'action' movie?"? While the paper briefly justifies combined queries by stating they can require fewer than `n` queries for recovery and offer better error resilience, this crucial argument needs to be more prominent and detailed. Expanding on why this specific form of "function of labels" is preferable to querying labels directly would significantly strengthen the paper's motivation.

2.  **Complexity of Theoretical Bounds:** While technically sound, the expressions for the bounds in several theorems (e.g., Theorem 1, 3, 4) are quite complex. To enhance the paper's impact and the reader's intuition, it would be beneficial to provide simplified corollaries, asymptotic analyses, or more extensive discussion on the practical implications of these formulas. For example, plotting the bounds or providing concrete numerical examples for representative settings could help illustrate the key trade-offs more clearly.

3.  **Experimental Comparison:** The experimental section provides a nice proof-of-concept for the AND query algorithm. However, the comparison could be strengthened. The real-world experiment demonstrates that the algorithm works and that AND queries elicited fewer errors than "same cluster" queries, which is a valuable finding. To make a more complete case, it would be beneficial to also compare against a baseline that uses direct label queries, addressing the concern raised in point (1). Even a synthetic comparison could help empirically ground the theoretical advantages claimed for the proposed query model.

### Overall Recommendation

This is a high-quality theoretical paper that introduces a novel and valuable connection between semi-supervised clustering and information theory. The analysis is rigorous, and the proposed AND query framework is an interesting and potentially impactful idea. The weaknesses identified above are primarily related to clarifying the motivation and improving the presentation of the results, rather than fundamental flaws in the methodology. The paper makes a significant contribution to our understanding of the fundamental limits of query-based learning. Assuming the authors can address the motivational questions regarding the choice of query type in their final version, this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance.
