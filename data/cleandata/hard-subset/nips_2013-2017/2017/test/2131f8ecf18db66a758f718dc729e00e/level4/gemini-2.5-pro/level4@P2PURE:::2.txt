This paper addresses a clustering problem where the objective is to recover the ground-truth labels of elements using responses to non-adaptive queries. These queries can be noisy and each involves only a small subset of elements. The authors derive lower bounds on the query complexity for both exact and partial recovery under several scenarios. They also propose an algorithm based on "AND-queries" for the general k-cluster case and evaluate its performance on a real-world dataset.

While the paper's scope is commendable—combining theoretical analysis, algorithm development, and empirical evaluation—it suffers from significant limitations in its presentation, the clarity of its theoretical claims, and its comparison to related work. Substantial revisions may be necessary for the paper to be ready for publication. Detailed comments follow.

1.  **(Model Constraint: No Repeated Queries)** The constraint of not allowing repeated queries seems restrictive. This restriction may degrade performance, as repeating queries is a natural strategy to mitigate noise. To justify this model, the authors could provide an extensive empirical comparison showing their algorithm outperforms one designed for the unrestricted case. Otherwise, the unrestricted setting, which seems more practical, would be a more compelling model to study. The authors appear concerned that repeated queries introduce dependencies between answers, complicating the analysis. However, this could be circumvented by considering a setting where each repeated query is posed to a different annotator, making the responses independent.

2.  **(Theorem 1)** The problem of exact recovery in the noiseless setting was also studied by [Ahn-Lee-Suh], who considered 'same-cluster' (XOR) queries in a non-adaptive setting. However, the result in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] appears to conflict with the claim in Theorem 1. For instance, for p=1/2 and constant Δ, the query complexity in Theorem 1 is m=n/log2, whereas in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] it is m=nlogn/Δ. The authors should clarify this discrepancy in detail. Additionally, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow.

3.  **(Theorem 3)** The lower bound on δ in Theorem 3 is presented in a form that is difficult to interpret. Specifically, it is not straightforward to derive the implied bound on the number of queries (`m`) as a function of `n` and `δ`. Such a formulation would be more useful, as it would clearly show how the query complexity improves as the recovery requirement (`δ`) is relaxed.

4.  **(Theorem 4)** This same criticism applies to the upper bound in Theorem 4. Furthermore, the authors should discuss the gap between this upper bound and the lower bound from Theorem 3. Is this gap bounded, or can it be arbitrarily large? As with other results, the proof is difficult to follow.

5.  **(Figure 2)** Figure 2 compares the proposed algorithm only to the *lower bound* for the 'same-cluster' query method, not to an actual algorithm based on it. It would be informative to see if a performance crossover point exists when comparing against a concrete 'same-cluster' query *algorithm*. If such a crossover exists, what is the intuition behind it? The current figure is insufficient to support the paper's claim that the proposed approach outperforms the 'same-cluster' query scheme.

6.  **(Algorithm 1)** Algorithm 1 appears to require prior knowledge of the noise rate (`p`) or the relative cluster sizes (as suggested by Theorem 6). The authors should explain how this information would be obtained in a practical setting, as this assumption impacts the algorithm's applicability. The pseudocode is also unclear and difficult to understand without referring to the proof of Theorem 5, which itself is challenging to follow.

7.  **(Figures 3, 4, & 5)** The experiments in Figures 3, 4, and 5 lack comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, including the 'same-cluster' query algorithm. This comparison is crucial to substantiate the paper's repeated claims that the proposed AND-queries algorithm is superior.

[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.

**(Post-rebuttal comments)**
Some comments were addressed in the rebuttal. For further clarification in a revised manuscript:
1.  Regarding the constraint on repeating queries: It remains unclear why this is a necessary or beneficial restriction. If this modeling choice is motivated by another work (e.g., the authors mentioned a Nature paper), this should be explicitly stated and justified in the revision.
2.  Regarding the comparison to the 'same-cluster' query method: The authors provided some clarification in their response. This detailed discussion should be integrated into the main paper to strengthen the experimental sections and clarify the contributions.
