This paper addresses a clustering problem where the goal is to reconstruct the true labels of a set of elements based on answers to non-adaptive queries. Each query involves a small subset of elements, and the answers may be noisy. The authors derive lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and partial recovery of the ground-truth labels under various scenarios. They also propose an "AND-queries" algorithm for the general k-cluster case and evaluate its performance on a real-world dataset.

While the paper's scope is broad, covering theoretical analysis, algorithm development, and empirical evaluation, it suffers from significant limitations in its presentation, the clarity of its results, and its comparison to related work. Substantial revisions and potentially new results may be necessary before this work is ready for publication. Detailed comments are provided below.

1.  **(On the constraint of not repeating queries):** The constraint that queries cannot be repeated seems overly restrictive. This may degrade performance, as repeating queries is a natural strategy to mitigate the effects of noise. To justify this model, the authors should provide an extensive empirical comparison against an algorithm that does allow repeated queries, demonstrating a performance advantage for their proposed approach. Without such a justification, the unrestricted case appears to be a more practical and relevant setting to investigate. The authors appear to be concerned about the analytical challenges posed by dependent answers to the same query. However, this issue can be circumvented by considering a setting where repeated queries are posed to different annotators, making it reasonable to assume the independence of their answers.

2.  **(Theorem 1):** The problem of exact recovery in the noiseless, non-adaptive setting was also studied in the recent work of [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which considers 'same-cluster' (XOR) queries. However, the result presented in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] does not align with the claim in Theorem 1. For example, for p=1/2 and constant Δ, the query complexity in Theorem 1 is given as m = n/log(2), whereas [Ahn-Lee-Suh] reports a complexity of m = n log(n)/Δ. This discrepancy requires detailed clarification. Furthermore, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow.

3.  **(Theorem 3):** The expression for the lower bound on δ is difficult to interpret. Specifically, it is not straightforward to derive the bound on the number of queries, m, as a function of n and δ. Such a formulation would be valuable for directly assessing the performance improvement gained from the relaxed recovery constraint, as captured by δ.

4.  **(Theorem 4):** The same criticism regarding interpretability applies to Theorem 4. Additionally, the authors should clarify the gap between the upper bound in this theorem and the lower bound from Theorem 3. Is it possible for this gap to be unbounded? As with previous theorems, the proof is difficult to follow.

5.  **(Figure 2):** Figure 2 does not include a comparison with an actual 'same-cluster query' algorithm; it only shows a comparison to the theoretical lower bound for such queries. It would be interesting to see if the performance curves cross when comparing against a concrete same-cluster query method. If such a crossing point exists, what is the intuition behind it? The paper's claim that the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster query scheme is not substantiated by the results presented in Figure 2.

6.  **(Algorithm 1):** The proposed algorithm, in light of Theorem 6, appears to require prior knowledge of p or the relative cluster sizes. The authors should discuss how this information could be obtained in a practical setting, as a justification for this requirement is needed to validate the algorithm's practicality. Furthermore, the pseudo-code for Algorithm 1 is difficult to understand without referring to the proof of Theorem 5, which is itself challenging to follow.

7.  **(Figures 3, 4, & 5):** The experiments in these figures lack comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, such as algorithms based on same-cluster queries. This comparison is crucial to substantiate the paper's recurring claim that the proposed AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme.

**Reference**
[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
