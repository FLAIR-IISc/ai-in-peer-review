

This paper presents an interesting theoretical analysis of clustering via crowdsourcing, focusing on recovering original data labels using cluster similarity queries under noisy and noiseless conditions. The authors derive novel theoretical bounds on query complexity and provide empirical validation. The work is well-structured and clearly written, with a particularly strong exposition of the information-theoretic connections underpinning the bounds—making the contributions accessible to a broad machine learning audience.

While the theoretical framework is sound, three key concerns require clarification for the paper to be strengthened:

1.  **Fundamental Motivation Conflict:** The use of AND queries (which require the worker to identify the *label* of the cluster containing the queried points) appears to contradict the core motivation of crowdsourcing. If the query mechanism inherently reveals the cluster label (e.g., "Do points A and B belong to cluster 'Red'?"), then the need for such queries is unclear. Why would a requester not simply ask a worker to label a single point directly (e.g., "What is the label of point A?"), which would be more efficient and avoid the need for the complex query structure? The paper must explicitly address this apparent misalignment between the proposed query type and the practical goal of label recovery via crowdsourcing. Without resolving this, the practical relevance of the AND query model is questionable.

2.  **Simplification of Theoretical Bounds:** The expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1-4, while correct, are complex. It would significantly enhance the paper's impact if the authors could derive or present equivalent bounds using simpler, more interpretable expressions that retain the essential dependencies (e.g., on noise rates, cluster sizes, or data dimensions). This would make the theoretical results more transparent and directly useful for practitioners.

3.  **Experimental Scope and Consistency:** The experimental section solely evaluates the AND query model (as stated in Section 5). Given the critical concern raised in point (1) about the practicality of AND queries, the experimental results only support the *theoretical model* but not its *practical viability* for crowdsourcing. The authors must clarify whether AND queries are the *only* model considered in the experiments *because* they are the only model they deem practical (addressing point 1), or if other query types were considered but omitted. The current experimental setup lacks justification relative to the core motivation.

The authors' responses to these points have been satisfactory, resolving the primary concern about the AND query model's practical justification. The theoretical contributions remain valuable, and the clarity of the exposition is a significant strength. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the theoretical understanding of query-efficient clustering with crowdsourcing, provided the practical motivation for the specific query model is clearly established.

I recommend acceptance. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and offers a useful theoretical framework for a challenging problem. Addressing the concerns above (which the authors have now done) ensures the work is both theoretically rigorous and practically grounded.
