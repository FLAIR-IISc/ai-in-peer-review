Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies query-based semisupervised clustering/labeling with a non-adaptive budget of simple queries to an oracle, and draws a novel equivalence to locally encodable source coding. Each query is a function of at most Δ labels and returns a binary (or, in one section, k-ary) answer. The authors develop information-theoretic lower bounds and matching or near-matching upper bounds for (i) noiseless exact recovery with two classes, (ii) noiseless approximate recovery, and (iii) noisy approximate recovery with k classes. A central message is that standard pairwise “same-cluster” (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery; AND-style queries can dramatically reduce query complexity.

Technically, the paper frames queries as a locally encodable compressor: each compressed bit depends on at most Δ source symbols. This leads to new lower bounds that refine the classical m ≥ nH(p) constraint by incorporating locality, and to algorithms that achieve O(n log(1/δ)) queries for recovering a (1 − δ) fraction of labels under noise. The results connect to information theory (source coding, rate–distortion, smooth/locally decodable compression) and to recent work on crowdsourced clustering and entity resolution (e.g., Ashtiani et al., NIPS’16; Karger–Oh–Shah; Vinayak–Hassibi; Mazumdar–Saha).

Main contributions
- Noiseless exact recovery, k = 2: With Δ-ary XOR queries, all labels can be recovered with high probability using m ≈ n(h(p)+o(1))/log(1/α) queries, where α = 1/2·(1 + (1−4p(1−p))^Δ). For Δ = 2 this gives m < n for a nontrivial range of p, improving over naïve n queries and far below O(n log n) for random queries. A locality-aware lower bound shows necessity exceeding nH(p) for some p, sharpening classical limits.
- Noiseless approximate recovery, k = 2: A new converse shows locality imposes additional distortion beyond the standard rate–distortion curve. The paper argues, using classical results on linear rate–distortion codes (Ancheta–Massey), that XOR (“same-cluster”) queries are fundamentally inefficient for approximate recovery. In contrast, a simple AND-query scheme with a Poisson left-degree model and threshold decoding achieves substantially better δ for a given m, with a closed-form error bound.
- Noisy approximate recovery, general k: Using pairwise “k-ary AND” queries that return i if both items have label i (and 0 otherwise), and a d-regular query graph, a simple counting/thresholding algorithm recovers a (1 − δ) fraction of labels with m = O(n log(k/δ)) queries under i.i.d. noise q. The analysis exploits a gap in expected counts between true and spurious labels and a dependency-graph concentration bound. The method can be implemented with known priors or only the cluster size vector, via randomization of the query graph.
- Experiments: A small crowdsourcing study on movie genres (n=200) shows AND queries produce fewer worker errors than same-cluster queries on matched pairs (105 vs. 234 mislabels in 1000 pair queries), and the algorithm’s empirical error tracks the theory as d increases. A synthetic comparison with spectral clustering on same-cluster answers is also provided.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The information-theoretic framing and bounds are generally solid and well-motivated. The converse integrating locality is interesting. The achievability for exact recovery uses ML decoding (info-theoretic, not computational). The approximate/noisy algorithm is simple and its analysis is clear, relying on standard Chernoff and dependency-graph bounds.
- Assumptions: The i.i.d. label prior and independence of query errors are strong but standard for information-theoretic analyses. The requirement of known priors/noise parameters is relaxed via an empirical-size variant; still, sensitivity to mis-specification deserves discussion.
- Completeness: Some proofs are deferred; the XOR lower bound relies on a conjecture except at p=0.5. It would be helpful to state precisely which parts are unconditional vs. conjecture-based.

Clarity
The paper is readable and the main ideas are communicated, but the exposition can be improved:
- Clarify early the distinction between “labeling” and “clustering” and where each result applies.
- Unify notation (k vs. 2, Δ, XOR vs. “same-cluster”) and fix minor typos.
- Make the role of ML decoding explicit as information-theoretic (not computationally efficient).
- In Sec. 3.3, discuss practicality of k-ary outputs in crowdsourcing and how to implement them with binary micro-questions if needed.

Originality
The equivalence to locally encodable source coding and the resulting locality-aware lower bounds appear novel. The insight that AND queries can outperform XOR for approximate recovery (with rigorous analysis) is new and compelling for ML audiences working on interactive/crowdsourced clustering.

Significance
The work advances our understanding of the fundamental limits of query-based clustering and suggests more effective query primitives in practice. It could influence the design of crowdsourcing tasks and spur further theoretical work on locality in interactive learning. The practical impact is tempered by modeling assumptions and limited experimental scope, but the conceptual contribution is strong.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide an unconditional lower bound for XOR queries beyond p=0.5, or clearly delineate reliance on Massey’s conjecture.
- Analyze robustness to mis-specified p and q; propose adaptive threshold selection or calibration from held-out queries.
- Discuss worker heterogeneity and correlated errors, and whether majority-vote aggregation could be integrated without violating the one-shot model.
- Expand experimental evaluation: larger datasets, comparisons to stronger baselines under noisy answers (e.g., EM/Dawid–Skene-style methods, KOS), and k > 2 with practical implementations of k-ary queries via multiple binary questions.
- Comment on adaptivity: how much could a few rounds reduce queries relative to the non-adaptive design?

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and principled connection to locally encodable source coding; new locality-aware lower bounds.
- Clear, simple algorithms with provable O(n log(k/δ)) query complexity under noise.
- Insightful and practically relevant finding that AND queries can dominate same-cluster XOR for approximate recovery.
- Bridges information theory and ML/crowdsourcing communities.

Arguments against acceptance
- Modeling assumptions (i.i.d. labels, known priors/noise, independent errors) limit applicability to typical clustering settings with structure.
- XOR lower bound partly conjectural; some proofs deferred; ML decoding unimplementable for exact recovery.
- Experimental validation is small-scale and comparisons to state-of-the-art noisy-crowdsourcing inference are limited.
- Practicality of k-ary queries for large k is under-discussed.

Overall, this is a thoughtful and original paper that advances the theory of query-based clustering and offers actionable insights on query design. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying the scope and limitations would further enhance its impact.
