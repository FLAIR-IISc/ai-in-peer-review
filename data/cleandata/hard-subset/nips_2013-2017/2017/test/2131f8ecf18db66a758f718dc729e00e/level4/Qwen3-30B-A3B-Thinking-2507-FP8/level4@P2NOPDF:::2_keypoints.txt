- Paper proposes clustering with non-adaptive, noisy few-element queries, proves lower bounds for exact/partial recovery, and introduces an AND-queries algorithm validated on real data.  
- Major concern: banning repeated queries is unjustified; repeating queries (to independent annotators) is a natural noise-handling strategy and likely helpful.  
- The authors’ claim that repeated queries are “not helpful” lacks strong theoretical or empirical evidence and needs explicit justification (e.g., a lemma) or real-data comparison.  
- Theorem 1 contradicts Ahn–Lee–Suh (Allerton 2016): reported m = Θ(n / log n) vs their Θ(n log n / Δ); this conflict and proof opacity must be resolved or model differences clarified.  
- Theorems 3–4 give unclear bounds: dependence on δ is not explicitly translated to m(n, δ), and the gap between the lower and upper bounds is not shown to be bounded; proofs are hard to follow.  
- Figures 2–5 omit direct comparisons to the same-cluster-query algorithm and other state-of-the-art baselines, undermining claims of superiority.  
- Figure 2 compares only to the same-cluster-query lower bound (not the algorithm), so the claimed outperformance and the missing crossing point are unsupported and need explanation.  
- Algorithm 1 requires prior knowledge of noise rate p or cluster-size ratios, but the paper does not explain how these are obtained in practice; pseudo-code is hard to parse without clearer proofs.  
- The authors’ rebuttal did not fully reconcile the Ahn–Lee–Suh discrepancy and did not provide an explicit justification that repeated queries are uninformative under their model.  
- Recommendation: theoretical contributions are promising but unresolved contradictions, unclear bounds, and insufficient empirical baselines require major revision before publication.  
- Required changes: resolve Theorem 1 conflict, rewrite Theorems 3–4 to express bounds as m(n, δ), add algorithm-level comparisons (same-cluster queries) in figures and explain crossing points, justify the no-repeated-queries assumption theoretically or empirically, clarify Algorithm 1’s practical requirements, and simplify proofs/exposition.
