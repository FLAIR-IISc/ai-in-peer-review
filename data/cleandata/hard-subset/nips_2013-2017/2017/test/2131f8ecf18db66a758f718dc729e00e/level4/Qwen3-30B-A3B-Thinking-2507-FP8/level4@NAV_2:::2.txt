

This paper investigates a clustering problem where the goal is to reconstruct true element labels from potentially noisy, non-adaptive query answers, with each query involving only a few elements. It establishes lower bounds on the number of queries needed for exact or partial label recovery across various scenarios and proposes an "AND-queries" algorithm applicable to arbitrary k-cluster cases, with empirical validation on a real dataset.

While the paper attempts theoretical analyses for multiple scenarios and develops an algorithm with real-data validation, it has limitations in presentation clarity, the precision of claimed results, and comparisons with relevant prior work. Significant revisions may be necessary for publication. Detailed comments follow:

1. (Query repetition constraint): The restriction against repeating the same query appears unnecessarily limiting. Allowing query repetition is a natural approach to mitigate noise, and the justification for this constraint seems weak. A proper justification would require extensive real-data simulations comparing the proposed method with a tailored algorithm for the unrestricted case, demonstrating superior performance of the proposed approach. Alternatively, the unrestricted case would be more plausible to investigate. The authors' concern about independence between multiple answers to the same query could be addressed by considering distinct annotators for repeated queries, ensuring independent answers.

2. (Theorem 1): The noiseless exact recovery setting with XOR-queries ('same-cluster' queries) was also studied in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] under non-adaptive measurement. However, their results differ from Theorem 1; for example, when p=1/2 and Δ is constant, Theorem 1 claims m=n/log2, while [Ahn-Lee-Suh] reports m=nlogn/Δ. This discrepancy requires clarification. Additionally, Theorem 1's proof is difficult to follow.

3. (Theorem 3): The lower bound expression for δ is challenging to interpret. Specifically, it's unclear how to derive a bound on m in terms of n and δ to directly observe the performance improvement from the relaxed constraint reflected in δ.

4. (Theorem 4): The same issues noted for Theorem 3 apply to Theorem 4. Additionally, it's unclear how the upper bound compares to the lower bound in Theorem 3—can the gap be unbounded? The proof is also hard to follow.

5. (Figure 2): The figure only compares to the lower bound of the same-cluster query method, not to the method itself. It's unclear if there's a crossing point when comparing to the actual same-cluster query method, and if so, what explains it. The paper claims the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster query scheme, but Figure 2 doesn't support this claim.

6. (Algorithm 1): The proposed algorithm requires prior knowledge of p or cluster size ratios (as noted in Theorem 6). Practical methods to obtain this information aren't discussed, which undermines the algorithm's practical applicability. Additionally, the pseudo-code is difficult to understand without the detailed proof of Theorem 5, which is also challenging to follow.

7. (Figures 3-5): These figures fail to compare against state-of-the-art methods like the same-cluster-query approach, which is crucial for supporting the paper's claims that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme.

[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
