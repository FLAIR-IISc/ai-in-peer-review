This paper presents an interesting approach to clustering data points using crowdsourcing, framing the problem as recovering original labels based on responses to similarity queries. The authors develop theoretical bounds on the number of queries required to achieve this, considering both noisy and noiseless answer scenarios. The primary contribution lies in the theoretical analysis, supported by empirical simulations. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the theoretical concepts to a broad machine learning audience; the connection to information theory, in particular, is nicely articulated.

However, I had some initial concerns regarding the proposed methodology. Specifically, the use of AND queries feels somewhat at odds with the typical motivations for employing crowdsourcing for clustering. An AND query, as presented, appears to reveal information about the cluster’s label *in addition to* whether data points belong to the same cluster. If my interpretation is correct, this raises the question of why similarity queries are necessary at all – wouldn't directly soliciting labels for individual data points be more efficient? This was my central point of confusion regarding the paper’s contributions, and I am glad to see it was addressed satisfactorily by the authors.

My remaining concerns relate to the presentation of the theoretical results. While the theorems are clearly stated, the expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1, 2, 3, and 4 are quite complex. I encourage the authors to consider whether these expressions can be simplified without sacrificing their core meaning, potentially making the results more accessible and easier to interpret. 

Finally, the experimental evaluation appears to focus solely on AND queries. This limitation weakens the empirical validation unless the rationale for exclusively using AND queries – and the concern raised in point (1) – is convincingly addressed.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a solid theoretical foundation and clear exposition. The authors have thoughtfully addressed my initial questions, and I am confident in recommending this paper for acceptance.
