

This paper establishes a novel connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, where the goal is to reconstruct element labels from non-adaptive queries involving only a constant number of elements. The authors derive information-theoretic lower bounds on query complexity for exact and approximate recovery, and propose AND-queries as a superior alternative to "same cluster" queries (XOR operations) for clustering problems. The paper includes theoretical analyses for noiseless and noisy settings, along with a real crowdsourcing experiment to validate the algorithm.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it identifies a meaningful connection between a popular clustering model and locally encodable source coding, a relatively unexplored area in information theory. Second, it provides new information-theoretic lower bounds that improve upon naive bounds for certain parameter ranges. Third, it demonstrates theoretically and empirically that AND-queries outperform "same cluster" queries for approximate recovery, with a substantial reduction in required queries. The real-world validation through crowdsourcing experiments adds practical relevance to the theoretical results.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that need to be addressed before consideration for publication:

1. The restriction against repeating queries is not properly justified. The authors claim that repeating queries is undesirable due to independence issues, but the paper doesn't address that one could ask the same question to different workers (resulting in independent answers). This is a standard approach in crowdsourcing to combat noise, and the paper should either justify why this isn't feasible or provide a comparison between the restricted model and the standard approach.

2. Theorem 1 appears to contradict a recent work by Ahn et al. (Allerton 2016), which also studies non-adaptive XOR-based queries. When p=1/2 and Δ is constant, Theorem 1 claims m = n/log₂(1/α) queries, while Ahn et al. show m = Θ(n log n/Δ). The authors need to clarify this discrepancy and explain why their bound is different.

3. The lower bounds in Theorems 3 and 4 are difficult to interpret. The expressions for δ in terms of m, n, and other parameters are complex, making it hard to see the performance gain of AND-queries over "same cluster" queries. The paper should provide a more intuitive explanation of how these bounds translate to practical query complexity.

4. The paper claims AND-queries outperform "same cluster" queries but fails to provide a direct comparison in figures. Figure 2 only compares to the lower bound of "same cluster" queries, not to the actual method. To support their claim, they should include performance curves for both methods.

5. Algorithm 1 requires prior knowledge of cluster sizes (p), which may not be available in practice. The paper should address how this information would be obtained in real applications, or discuss the robustness of the algorithm to inaccurate prior knowledge.

6. The real crowdsourcing experiment shows that AND-queries have fewer errors than "same cluster" queries (105 vs 234 errors), but the paper doesn't compare the performance of AND-queries to the theoretical prediction. The experimental results should be directly compared to the theoretical bounds to validate the model.

7. The paper does not sufficiently compare to related works. The connection to locally encodable source coding is novel, but the paper should better position its work against other clustering approaches with side information.

The paper has strong theoretical foundations and promising empirical results, but the current presentation lacks clarity on key points and doesn't adequately support its main claims. The authors should address the above issues before publication. Specifically, they need to:
- Clarify the query repetition issue and provide a comparison with the standard approach
- Resolve the apparent contradiction with Ahn et al.
- Simplify the presentation of lower bounds
- Provide direct comparisons between AND-queries and "same cluster" queries in figures
- Address the practicality of prior knowledge requirements
- Better contextualize the work within existing literature

The theoretical contributions are interesting and potentially significant, but the current version doesn't clearly demonstrate their superiority over existing approaches. With the suggested revisions, this paper could make a valuable contribution to the field.
