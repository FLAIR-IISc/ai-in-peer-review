This paper studies the problem of clustering with access to an oracle. In this model, the learner aims to recover a target clustering by asking a series of queries, such as whether two instances belong to the same cluster. More generally, the queries are modeled as a fixed, binary-valued function of the instance labels. The primary focus of the work is to determine the query complexity of this task, establishing the minimum number of queries required for recovery.

The problem of query-based clustering is well-motivated and highly relevant to modern machine learning applications, including record deduplication, entity resolution, and crowdsourcing, where obtaining labels via pairwise queries is often more practical than obtaining absolute labels for each data point. The paper's main contribution is its rigorous, information-theoretic analysis of this problem. The authors derive both upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed to perfectly or approximately recover the target clustering. The analysis is comprehensive, extending to the practical and challenging setting where the oracle's responses are noisy. A significant strength of this work lies in its methodology; the information-theoretic tools employed are powerful and may be new to a large portion of the machine learning community, potentially inspiring new analytical approaches for other interactive learning problems.

The primary limitation of the analysis is a direct consequence of its generality. By refraining from making structural assumptions about the data (e.g., defining a hypothesis class or assuming geometric properties like linear separability), the resulting bounds are necessarily coarse. The query complexity bounds depend almost entirely on the marginal distribution of the labels and the noise level of the oracle, rather than on properties of the data instances themselves. This makes the setting distinct from many traditional analyses in statistical learning theory, where assumptions about the data-generating distribution or the complexity of the hypothesis class are leveraged to obtain more refined, problem-specific guarantees.

However, this limitation can also be seen as a strength. By focusing on the fundamental information-theoretic aspects, the paper provides a valuable and foundational understanding of the query-based clustering problem. This work should be viewed as an important first step in characterizing the absolute limits of what can be learned with a given query budget. The derived lower bounds, in particular, serve as a crucial benchmark for any future algorithm, regardless of the specific structural assumptions it may employ. This analysis lays the groundwork for future research that could build upon this information-theoretic foundation by incorporating richer structural assumptions to develop tighter, more practical bounds.
