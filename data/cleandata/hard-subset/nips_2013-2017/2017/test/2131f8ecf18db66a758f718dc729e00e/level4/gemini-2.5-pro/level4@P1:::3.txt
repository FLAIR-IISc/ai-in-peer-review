This paper addresses the problem of semi-supervised clustering, where the goal is to recover the ground-truth labels of `n` items by making a series of queries to an oracle. The authors establish a novel and insightful connection between this problem and the information-theoretic concept of locally encodable source coding. This framework allows them to derive theoretical bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery of labels, under both noiseless and noisy oracle models. The paper's primary contributions are theoretical, demonstrating the limitations of standard "same-cluster" queries for approximate recovery and proposing "AND" queries as a provably more efficient alternative in certain regimes. These theoretical results are supported by a small-scale crowdsourcing experiment.

The paper is well-written and presents its theoretical contributions clearly. The connection drawn to information theory and locally encodable source coding is particularly elegant and provides a strong foundation for the analysis. The authors do a commendable job of making these concepts accessible to a general machine learning audience.

While I find the theoretical direction of this work interesting and valuable, I have a few questions and suggestions for improvement.

1.  **Motivation for AND Queries:** The introduction of AND queries is a key contribution, but its practical motivation seems to conflict with the typical crowdsourcing setup. An AND query, such as "Are both items A and B of the 'action movie' genre?", implies that the worker knows the absolute label ('action movie') to provide an answer. This raises a fundamental question: if the worker has this capability, why not simply ask for the label of a single item directly (e.g., "Is item A an 'action movie'?")? The introduction briefly notes that combined queries can be more efficient (requiring fewer than `n` total queries) and offer better robustness to noise. This is a crucial point and the core justification for the proposed model. I would encourage the authors to elaborate on this and perhaps provide more concrete examples to strengthen the argument for why a worker would be asked to evaluate a pair rather than a single item, even when they know the absolute labels.

2.  **Complexity of Theoretical Bounds:** The expressions for the bounds derived in Theorems 1, 2, 3, and 4 are quite involved. While their precise forms are necessary for rigor, their complexity can make it difficult to grasp the key takeaways. To improve readability and impact, the authors might consider presenting simplified or asymptotic versions of these bounds in the main text. This would help readers more quickly understand the essential dependencies on key parameters like the number of items `n`, prior probabilities `p`, and desired error rate `δ`.

3.  **Experimental Scope:** The experiments provide a helpful validation of the proposed AND query scheme on a real-world task. However, the evaluation could be strengthened. Given the central claim that AND queries are superior to "same-cluster" queries, a direct experimental comparison between the two on the same dataset (even a synthetic one, as was done briefly) would be very compelling, especially in the noisy setting. This would more concretely demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed method over the standard approach in the literature.

In summary, this paper makes a strong and novel theoretical contribution to the literature on clustering with queries. The information-theoretic framework is insightful, and the analysis of different query types is significant. My main concerns relate to clarifying the practical motivation for the AND query model and suggesting ways to make the theoretical and experimental results more accessible and comprehensive. I am supportive of the paper and believe it would be a solid contribution to the conference proceedings, especially if the authors can address these points.
