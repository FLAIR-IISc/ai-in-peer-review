The authors investigate a clustering model in which the learner is permitted to query an oracle, with the objective of identifying the target clustering. These queries can take the form of same-cluster queries or, more generally, a fixed binary-valued function of the input instances' labels. The primary focus of the study is on the query complexity of such models.

The query-based clustering model represents a well-motivated and relatively recent area of research, boasting applications in record-deduplication, crowdsourcing, and other domains. The authors approach the problem from an information-theoretic perspective, providing upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required to perfectly or approximately recover the target clustering. They also consider the scenario in which the oracle is noisy. The information-theoretic tools employed in the study are novel and of interest to the machine learning community.

Notably, the analysis is limited by the absence of structural assumptions regarding the problem, such as the presence of a hypothesis class. Consequently, the bounds derived by the authors are primarily based on the marginal distribution of the labels, in addition to the noise level. As a result, the setting examined in the study diverges somewhat from the typical settings encountered in learning theory. Nevertheless, this work represents a crucial first step, offering valuable insights into the information-theoretic aspects of query-based clustering, particularly with respect to the associated lower bounds.
