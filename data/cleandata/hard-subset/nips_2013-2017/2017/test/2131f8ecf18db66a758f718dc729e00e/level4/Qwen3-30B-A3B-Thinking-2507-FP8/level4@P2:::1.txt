

This paper establishes an intriguing equivalence between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, offering new theoretical insights into query-based clustering models. The authors consider a setting where an oracle provides (possibly erroneous) binary answers to queries about element relationships, with each query involving at most a fixed number of elements. The goal is to recover the complete labeling (or clustering) with minimal queries. The authors demonstrate that pairwise "same cluster" queries (equivalent to XOR operations) have fundamental limitations, and propose "AND queries" that achieve better performance in various scenarios.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides information-theoretic lower bounds on query complexity for exact and approximate recovery in both noiseless and noisy settings. For the noiseless case with two clusters, the authors establish a new lower bound that strictly improves upon the naive nH(p) bound for certain label distributions. They also develop a novel querying scheme using AND operations that achieves substantially better query complexity than "same cluster" queries for approximate recovery. In the noisy setting with k clusters, they present a practical algorithm (Algorithm 1) that achieves (1-δ)-good recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries, validated through real crowdsourcing experiments on movie classification.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality with rigorous proofs of both lower and upper bounds. The information-theoretic approach is sound, and the results on locally encodable source coding represent novel contributions to information theory that extend beyond the clustering context. The experimental validation on a real crowdsourcing dataset with 100 action and 100 romantic movies provides convincing empirical support for the theoretical claims, showing that AND queries yield significantly fewer errors (105 vs. 234) compared to "same cluster" queries.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with the technical contributions logically presented. The connection between clustering and source coding is articulated effectively, and the paper adequately contextualizes its contributions within related work (referencing relevant NIPS papers on clustering with side information). The authors provide specific details about their experimental setup, including the use of SurveyMonkey with 10 volunteers, which enhances reproducibility.

The originality of this work is substantial. While query-based clustering has been studied previously (e.g., by Ashtiani et al. in NIPS 2016), this paper's information-theoretic framing and specific focus on locally encodable source coding represent a novel perspective. The authors' discovery that AND queries outperform "same cluster" queries is particularly insightful and addresses a fundamental limitation in prior work. The theoretical results on locally encodable source coding are new, as the authors note that this notion has not been well-studied in the information theory community.

The significance of this work is high, particularly for applications in crowdsourcing where query efficiency and error reduction are critical. The results provide both theoretical foundations and practical algorithms for more efficient clustering with human-in-the-loop systems. The ability to achieve good recovery with significantly fewer queries (O(n log(1/δ)) instead of O(n log n)) has clear implications for reducing costs in crowdsourced data labeling.

A minor limitation is that the theoretical analysis focuses primarily on the marginal distribution of labels as the key parameter, which may limit direct applicability to more structured clustering problems. However, the authors acknowledge this limitation and position their work as a foundational step toward understanding the information-theoretic aspects of query-based clustering.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the literature on clustering with side information. It successfully bridges information theory and machine learning, providing both theoretical insights and practical algorithms. The results are technically sound, well-motivated, and validated through real-world experiments.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
1. Novel theoretical framework connecting clustering to locally encodable source coding
2. Fundamental limitations of "same cluster" queries and superior alternative (AND queries)
3. Tight information-theoretic bounds for both noiseless and noisy settings
4. Rigorous theoretical analysis with complete proofs
5. Practical validation through real crowdsourcing experiments
6. Clear writing and strong organization
7. Significant implications for efficient crowdsourcing systems

Con arguments against acceptance:
1. Theoretical results primarily focus on marginal label distribution rather than structured clustering
2. Some technical details in the proofs could be clarified (e.g., the expurgation argument in Theorem 1)
3. The connection to broader ML applications could be expanded

This paper represents a high-quality contribution that advances our understanding of query-based clustering from an information-theoretic perspective. The theoretical results are sound, the experimental validation is convincing, and the practical implications are significant for crowdsourcing applications. The work is well-positioned for NIPS publication as it addresses a fundamental problem with both theoretical depth and practical relevance. I recommend acceptance.
