

The authors investigate a clustering framework where a learner queries an oracle to identify a target clustering, using same-cluster queries (or more generally, fixed binary functions of instance labels). They analyze the query complexity required to exactly or approximately recover the target clustering. This query-based clustering model is well-motivated, with applications in record deduplication and crowdsourcing, and the authors adopt an information-theoretic approach to derive upper and lower bounds on query requirements. Their analysis also extends to scenarios with a noisy oracle, employing information-theoretic techniques that are novel within the machine learning community.

However, the absence of structural assumptions—such as a hypothesis class or specific data distribution constraints—significantly limits the scope of the analysis. The derived bounds depend primarily on the marginal label distribution (beyond the noise level), making the setting somewhat removed from standard learning theory frameworks. While this work represents a valuable first step toward understanding the information-theoretic foundations of query-based clustering—particularly regarding lower bounds—it does not directly address the practical challenges of real-world clustering tasks where structural assumptions are typically essential. The model’s generality, while theoretically interesting, results in bounds that may not translate to meaningful guarantees under more constrained, realistic problem formulations common in ML. The authors’ contribution is thus best positioned as a foundational exploration of the information-theoretic limits rather than a directly applicable method.
