This is an interesting paper that I enjoyed reading. The authors study clustering via crowdsourcing, focusing on recovering the true labels of data points from answers to same-cluster–type queries. They derive bounds on the number of queries needed to recover the labels under both noiseless and noisy settings. The contribution is primarily theoretical, supported by some empirical simulations. The paper is clearly written, making the theoretical results accessible to a broad machine learning audience. In particular, the information-theoretic connections used to derive the bounds are well explained.

I have the following concerns regarding how the paper could be improved:
(1) While the notion of AND queries is theoretically intriguing, it seems at odds with the original motivation for crowdsourcing. In an AND query, the response effectively reveals the cluster label itself, rather than merely indicating whether two points belong to the same cluster. If my interpretation is correct, why ask similarity queries at all? Why not directly request the label of each data point? This is my primary confusion about the paper’s contributions.
(2) Can the expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1–4 be simplified while preserving their essential content?
(3) In the experiments, it appears that only AND queries are considered. If so, the empirical evaluation is difficult to interpret unless concern (1) is addressed.

I am satisfied with the authors’ responses to the above questions and recommend acceptance.
