- Novel framing: connects semi-supervised clustering to locally encodable source coding, offering a new theoretical lens.  
- Main contribution: derives information-theoretic lower bounds on queries for exact and approximate recovery.  
- AND queries: proposes AND queries and argues they can outperform same-cluster (XOR) queries, especially under noise and symmetry breaking.  
- Analysis breadth: covers noiseless/noisy settings, exact/approximate recovery, and general k-cluster cases.  
- Empirical validation: presents algorithms based on AND queries and experiments on a real crowdsourced dataset.  
- Theorem 1 concern: claimed XOR query complexity (m = O(n) for p=1/2) appears to contradict prior work (Ahn et al. 2016) that reports m = O(n log n).  
- Proof clarity: proof sketches (especially for Theorem 1) are too brief and hard to verify; need fuller derivations and reconciliation with related work.  
- Theorems 3 & 4 clarity: bounds on error δ are hard to interpret; should be re-expressed as m versus n and δ and discuss tightness/gap between bounds.  
- Insufficient empirical baselines: experimental comparisons use a lower bound for same-cluster queries rather than competitive algorithms (e.g., spectral clustering on query graph).  
- Noisy experiments lack comparisons to state-of-the-art noisy query-based clustering methods to validate practical gains.  
- No query repetition model: forbidding repeated queries is restrictive and not well justified; practical crowdsourcing often uses repeated independent workers for noise reduction.  
- Prior knowledge requirement: Algorithm 1 needs cluster priors p; empirical estimation and sensitivity to estimation error are not sufficiently explored.  
- Practical relevance: modeling choices and assumptions (no repetition, known priors) may limit applicability without stronger justification or robustness analysis.  
- Recommendation: not ready for publication in current form; major revisions needed to clarify theory, resolve conflicts with prior work, and strengthen empirical comparisons.  
- Pros after revision: conceptual novelty (locally encodable coding connection) and potential impact of AND queries could justify acceptance if issues are addressed.  
- Cons in current state: apparent theoretical contradictions, weak empirical support for central claims, unclear theoretical statements, and questionable modeling assumptions.
