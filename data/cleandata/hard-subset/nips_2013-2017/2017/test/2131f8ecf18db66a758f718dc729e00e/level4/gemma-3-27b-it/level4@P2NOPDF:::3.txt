This paper presents an interesting approach to data clustering via crowdsourcing, framing the problem as recovering original data point labels based on responses to cluster similarity queries. The authors develop theoretical bounds on the number of queries needed to accurately recover these labels, considering both noisy and noiseless response scenarios. The primary contribution lies in the theoretical analysis, supported by empirical simulations. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the theoretical concepts, making it accessible to a broad machine learning audience. The connection to information theory, used in deriving the bounds, is particularly well articulated.

However, I have several concerns that, while addressed satisfactorily by the authors’ response, warrant further consideration. 

My primary concern relates to the use of AND queries. While theoretically intriguing, this query type appears to deviate from the typical motivation behind crowdsourcing for clustering. An AND query, as presented, effectively reveals the cluster label *along* with the similarity information, rather than simply indicating whether data points belong to the same cluster. If this interpretation is correct, it raises a fundamental question: why employ these similarity queries at all when one could directly query for the label of a data point? This significantly impacts the perceived novelty and practical relevance of the proposed approach.

Secondly, the expressions for the bounds presented in Theorems 1, 2, 3, and 4 are complex. While acknowledging the inherent difficulty of this type of analysis, exploring potential simplifications – without sacrificing the core theoretical results – would enhance the paper’s accessibility and impact.

Finally, the experimental evaluation appears to focus exclusively on AND queries. Without a clear justification for this choice, particularly given the concerns raised above regarding the nature of AND queries, the validity and generalizability of the experimental findings are questionable. Clarifying the rationale behind focusing solely on AND queries is crucial.

Despite these concerns, the authors’ response to my questions has been thorough and convincing. I am therefore voting to accept this paper.
