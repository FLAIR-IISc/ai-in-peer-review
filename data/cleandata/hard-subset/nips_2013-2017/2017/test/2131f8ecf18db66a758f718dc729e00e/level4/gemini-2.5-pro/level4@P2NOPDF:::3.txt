This paper addresses the problem of crowdsourced clustering, where the goal is to recover the true labels of data points by posing similarity queries to an oracle or a crowd. The authors derive theoretical bounds on the number of queries required to recover the ground-truth clustering, considering both noiseless and noisy oracle responses. The primary contribution is theoretical, supported by empirical simulations.

The paper is well-written, and its theoretical contributions are presented with clarity, making them accessible to a general machine learning audience. A key strength is the exposition of the connection to information theory, which provides an elegant framework for deriving the query complexity bounds.

Despite these strengths, I have a few concerns that I would like the authors to address:

1.  **Motivation for "AND" Queries:** My primary concern revolves around the practical justification for "AND" queries. While theoretically interesting, this query type seems to conflict with the core motivation of using crowdsourcing for clustering. As I interpret it, an "AND" query not only asks if a set of items belong to the same cluster but also receives an affirmative answer that implicitly reveals the cluster's identity. If a crowd worker can provide such an answer, it suggests they already know the cluster labels. This raises a fundamental question: if the worker can identify the specific cluster, why not simply ask for the label of a single data point directly? This approach would seem to bypass the need for similarity-based queries altogether. A clearer explanation of a real-world scenario where "AND" queries are natural and necessary would significantly strengthen the paper's premise.

2.  **Clarity of Theoretical Bounds:** The expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1, 2, 3, and 4 are quite complex. While their derivations are sound, their current form makes it challenging to extract immediate intuition about the key factors influencing query complexity. Would it be possible for the authors to provide simplified versions or corollaries of these bounds? For instance, asymptotic expressions or versions that highlight the dominant terms could help readers better appreciate the essence of the theoretical results and their dependencies on problem parameters like the number of clusters or data points.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments appear to rely exclusively on simulations with "AND" queries. This links back to my first point; the practical relevance of the empirical results is contingent on the justification for this query type. If the "AND" query model is not well-motivated for typical crowdsourcing scenarios, the experimental study may not adequately validate the proposed methods for the problems they aim to solve. Clarification on this point is crucial for interpreting the empirical findings.

In summary, this is a technically sound paper with valuable theoretical contributions. My main reservations are centered on the problem formulation, particularly the choice and motivation of the "AND" query model. I am currently leaning toward acceptance, but my final recommendation will depend on the authors' ability to convincingly address the concerns raised above, especially regarding the practical motivation for their query framework.
