- Summary: Studies non-adaptive, Δ-local queries for multiclass labeling, links to locally encodable source coding, gives info-theoretic lower bounds, proposes AND queries with a noisy decoder achieving ~O(n log(k/δ)) queries, and reports a small human experiment favoring AND.

- Relation to prior work: Complements crowdsourced clustering and locality-in-coding literature; relates to parity-measurement community recovery and linear code rate–distortion limits.

- Strength: Provides a unifying locally-encodable information-theoretic perspective that clarifies XOR limitations.

- Strength: Proposes a simple AND query primitive and analyzes a near-linear noisy multiclass decoder.

- Strength: Empirical indication that AND queries may be easier/more reliable for human workers.

- Strength: Addresses multiple regimes (exact/approximate, noiseless/noisy).

- Weakness: Several main theorems are hard to parse, with proofs and assumptions insufficiently transparent and sometimes seemingly inconsistent with prior work.

- Weakness: Strong restriction forbidding query repetition is poorly justified and lacks comparisons to repetition-based baselines.

- Weakness: Approximate recovery bounds are hard to interpret and not explicitly inverted for m(n,δ); the achievability–converse gap is unclear.

- Weakness: Experiments lack comparisons to strong “same cluster” (XOR) baselines and compare to a lower bound rather than implemented methods.

- Detailed comment 1: Must justify/discuss the no-repetition model, compare to repetition baselines, and clarify whether repetition can help for AND queries.

- Detailed comment 2: Reconcile Theorem 1 with Ahn–Lee–Suh and other XOR results by making assumptions explicit (priors, target notion, expurgation vs worst-case, linearity, decoder knowledge of p).

- Detailed comment 3: For Theorem 3, provide explicit inversion m(n,δ,p,Δ), visualizations of m/n vs δ, and discuss how locality degrades rate–distortion.

- Detailed comment 4: For Theorem 4, give a simplified interpretable form δ(m/n,p), quantify gap to converse, and state if gap is bounded or closable by design/decoding improvements.

- Detailed comment 5: Update Figure 2 to include implemented XOR baselines (spectral, ML on random XOR, best known non-adaptive) and explain any crossings and dependency on p,Δ.

- Detailed comment 6: Clarify Algorithm 1’s dependence on p, how p is estimated in practice, robustness to misspecification, threshold definitions, graph construction, and Chernoff-constant choices.

- Detailed comment 7: Expand experiments to include head-to-head XOR comparisons at matched budgets, sensitivity to p,q,d, robustness to prior misestimation, and larger-scale/synthetic tests validating O(n log(k/δ)) scaling.

- Quality: Promising technical ideas but several proofs are opaque, assumptions are not explicit, and key comparisons are missing.

- Clarity: Organization is reasonable but theorems and proofs need significant rewriting, explicit assumptions, and interpretable corollaries/plots.

- Originality: Novel locally-encodable coding perspective and advocacy for AND queries; potentially impactful bounds and algorithm.

- Significance: Could guide practical crowdsourcing if clarified and validated against strong baselines; current impact limited by gaps and experimental weaknesses.

- Pros for acceptance: Novel conceptual bridge to locally encodable coding, identification of XOR limitations with an analyzable AND alternative, and near-optimal non-adaptive query complexity claim.

- Cons for acceptance: Inconsistencies with prior XOR results and hard-to-verify proofs, opaque approximate-recovery bounds and unquantified gap, and weak experimental comparisons.

- After rebuttal: Some issues improved but still need explicit clarification on repetition’s usefulness and a detailed quantitative comparison to same-cluster methods.

- Overall: Promising paper requiring substantial revisions—clarifications, stronger baselines, clearer proofs, and expanded experiments—before acceptance.
