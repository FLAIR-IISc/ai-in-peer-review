The authors study a clustering model where the learner is allowed to ask queries from an oracle and the goal is to find the target clustering. The queries can be same-cluster queries [or more generally, a fixed binary-valued function of the label of the input instances]. The authors study the query complexity of such models. 

The query-based clustering model is a well-motivated and relatively recent research area, with applications in record-deduplication, crowdsourcing, etc. The authors study the problem from the information theoretic perspective. In particular, they provide upper and lower bounds on the number of required queries to perfectly (or approximately) recover the target clustering. They also consider the case that the oracle is noisy. The information-theoretic tools that they use are interesting and new to ML community.

The authors' main contribution is in showing that a recently popular model of semisupervised clustering is equivalent to locally encodable source coding, a data compression problem where each compressed bit depends only on a constant number of input bits. This equivalence leads them to find lower bounds on the number of queries required in various scenarios. They are also able to show fundamental limitations of pairwise ‘same cluster’ queries and propose pairwise AND queries, which provably perform better in many situations.

The paper is well-written and the results are interesting. The authors provide a good discussion on the related work and the significance of their results. The technical content is sound and the proofs are well-presented.

However, because no structural assumption is made about the problem (e.g., having a hypothesis class, etc.) the analysis is limited. In fact, the marginal distribution of the labels is the main parameter that the bounds are based on (aside from the noise level). Therefore, the studied setting is a bit far from the usual settings studied in learning theory. However, this is a first step, and can help in understanding the information-theoretic aspects of query-based clustering, especially the related lower-bounds.

Quality: The paper is technically sound and the results are interesting. However, the analysis is limited due to the lack of structural assumptions.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and the results are clearly presented.

Originality: The authors' main contribution is in showing the equivalence between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, which is a new and interesting result.

Significance: The paper provides a good understanding of the information-theoretic aspects of query-based clustering and the related lower-bounds.
