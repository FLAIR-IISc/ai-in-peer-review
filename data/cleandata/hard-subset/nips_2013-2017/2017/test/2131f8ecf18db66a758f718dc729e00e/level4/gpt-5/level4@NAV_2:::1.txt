The paper examines a clustering framework in which a learner may query an oracle to recover the ground-truth partition. Queries include same-cluster questions and, more generally, fixed binary functions of the instance labels. The work focuses on the query complexity of such models.

This query-driven clustering setup is timely and well-justified, with applications to record deduplication, crowdsourcing, and related domains. The authors take an information-theoretic approach, deriving upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed for exact and approximate recovery, and they also analyze settings with noisy oracle responses. The information-theoretic techniques employed are appealing and likely unfamiliar to much of the ML community.

Since no structural assumptions are placed on the problem (e.g., no explicit hypothesis class), the analysis scope is inherently constrained. In particular, aside from the noise level, the bounds primarily hinge on the marginal label distribution, making the setting somewhat removed from standard learning-theoretic formulations. Nonetheless, this is a valuable first step that can clarify the information-theoretic underpinnings of query-based clustering, especially by illuminating fundamental lower bounds.
