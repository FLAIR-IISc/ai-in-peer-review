This paper establishes a connection between semi-supervised clustering with oracle queries and the information-theoretic problem of locally encodable source coding. The central task is to determine the ground-truth labels of `n` elements into `k` clusters by making a minimum number of non-adaptive queries. Each query is constrained to involve only a small, constant number of elements (e.g., pairs), and returns a binary answer. The authors leverage this information-theoretic framework to derive upper and lower bounds on the query complexity for both exact and approximate recovery of the labels, considering both noiseless and noisy oracle models. This work fits into the growing literature on active clustering and crowdsourcing (e.g., [4, 21]), but distinguishes itself by adopting a rigorous source coding perspective. A key contribution is the proposal and analysis of 'AND-queries' as a more efficient alternative to standard 'same-cluster' (or XOR) queries, particularly for approximate recovery in the noisy setting.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of the paper is its novel framing of the query-based clustering problem in the language of locally encodable source coding. This is a genuinely original perspective that brings new tools and insights to the machine learning community. This framework allows the authors to derive new information-theoretic lower bounds and near-optimal querying schemes.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical results appear sound. The introduction and analysis of AND-queries as a provably superior alternative to 'same-cluster' queries for approximate recovery is a significant and practical contribution. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis, considering exact and approximate recovery in both noiseless and noisy settings, which adds to the completeness of the work. The inclusion of a real-data crowdsourcing experiment to validate the theoretical claims about AND-queries strengthens the paper.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, and the theoretical arguments and problem setup are clearly laid out.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The main limitation of the analysis is its reliance almost exclusively on the prior distribution of the labels (the relative cluster sizes), without incorporating other structural assumptions common in machine learning (e.g., geometric separability or feature-based structures). This makes the setting somewhat disconnected from typical learning theory contexts where one might have access to instance features or a well-defined hypothesis class. Consequently, the derived bounds depend on parameters like `p` (the prior probability) rather than properties of the data distribution itself, which may limit the direct applicability of the results in scenarios where cluster structure is more complex than simple random assignment based on a prior. However, this is not a flaw in the analysis itself, but rather a characteristic of the chosen information-theoretic abstraction.

**Summary of Arguments:**

**For Acceptance:**
*   Establishes a novel and interesting connection between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding.
*   Provides new information-theoretic upper and lower bounds on query complexity for this problem.
*   Introduces AND-queries and demonstrates their superiority over standard 'same-cluster' queries, a result of both theoretical and practical significance.
*   The analysis is comprehensive, covering multiple important settings (exact/approximate recovery, noisy/noiseless oracles).

**Against Acceptance:**
*   The problem formulation, by relying solely on the marginal label distribution, is somewhat detached from mainstream machine learning settings that typically involve structural assumptions about the data. This might limit the immediate impact on related areas of learning theory.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper that introduces a novel perspective and valuable results to an important problem. The strengths, particularly the originality of the approach and the significance of the findings on AND-queries, outweigh the limitations of the theoretical setting.
