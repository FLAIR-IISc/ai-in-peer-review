The authors investigate a clustering model in which the learner can query an oracle to determine if instances belong to the same cluster. The goal is to efficiently recover a target clustering, and the authors focus on the query complexity of this process. Their model generalizes to scenarios where the oracle provides a fixed binary-valued function of the input instance labels.

Query-based clustering is a well-motivated and relatively new research area with practical applications in areas such as record deduplication and crowdsourcing. The authors approach this problem from an information-theoretic perspective, providing upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required to perfectly – or approximately – recover the target clustering. They also extend their analysis to consider scenarios with noisy oracle responses. The information-theoretic tools employed are novel and of potential interest to the machine learning community. 

The analysis is limited by the lack of structural assumptions about the problem – for example, the absence of a predefined hypothesis class. Consequently, the bounds primarily depend on the marginal distribution of the labels (and the noise level). This makes the setting somewhat distant from more traditional learning theory scenarios. However, this work represents an important first step in understanding the information-theoretic limits of query-based clustering, particularly the associated lower bounds.
