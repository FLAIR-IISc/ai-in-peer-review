

This paper investigates clustering via non-adaptive, noisy queries that associate only a few elements, aiming to reconstruct true labels with minimal queries. It establishes theoretical lower bounds for exact/partial label recovery and proposes an "AND-queries" algorithm for general k-clusters, validated on real data. While the work combines theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation, significant issues in presentation, result clarity, and comparative context require substantial revision before publication.  

**Major concerns:**  
1. **Restrictive query model:** The prohibition on repeating queries lacks justification. Allowing repeated queries (e.g., to distinct annotators) is a natural noise-handling strategy and likely improves performance. The authors cite an "independence-vs-dependence" concern, but this is resolvable by assuming independent answers from distinct annotators per query. Without empirical comparison to a no-restriction baseline (e.g., via real-data simulations), the restricted model appears unnecessarily limiting. The claim that repeated queries are "not helpful" requires stronger evidence.  

2. **Theorem 1 conflict:** The noiseless exact-recovery bound in Theorem 1 contradicts [Ahn-Lee-Suh] (Allerton 2016), which studies XOR-based "same-cluster" queries. For p=1/2 and constant Δ, Theorem 1 states m = Θ(n / log n), while [Ahn-Lee-Suh] reports m = Θ(n log n / Δ). This discrepancy must be resolved—either by correcting Theorem 1 or clarifying the model differences. The proof is also opaque.  

3. **Theorems 3–4: Unclear bounds:** The lower bound in Theorem 3 (for relaxed constraints) is expressed in terms of δ without clear derivation of m(n, δ). This obscures how the bound improves with δ. Similarly, Theorem 4’s upper bound lacks comparison to Theorem 3’s lower bound, leaving open whether the gap is bounded. The proofs remain difficult to follow.  

4. **Figures 2–5: Missing baselines:** Figure 2 compares only to the *lower bound* of the same-cluster-query method, not the *method itself*. Thus, the claim that "AND-queries outperforms same-cluster queries" is unsupported. Figure 2 should include a direct comparison to the same-cluster-query *algorithm* (not just its bound), and the absence of a crossing point requires explanation. Figures 3–5 similarly omit comparisons to state-of-the-art methods (e.g., same-cluster queries), undermining the paper’s core claim.  

5. **Algorithm 1: Practicality gap:** The algorithm requires prior knowledge of p (noise rate) or cluster size ratios (per Theorem 6). The paper does not address how to obtain these in practice, weakening its applicability. The pseudo-code is also hard to parse without the opaque Theorem 5 proof.  

**Additional clarifications needed post-rebuttal:**  
- The rebuttal addressed the Ahn-Lee-Suh conflict but did not fully resolve the m = Θ(n / log n) vs. m = Θ(n log n / Δ) discrepancy. A precise reconciliation is essential.  
- The rebuttal clarified that repeated queries *are* not helpful under the proposed model, but this requires explicit justification (e.g., via a lemma showing repeated queries yield no information gain).  
- The comparison to same-cluster-query *method* (not just its bound) must be added to Figure 2, with analysis of the crossing point (if any).  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper’s theoretical contributions are promising but currently undermined by unresolved contradictions, unclear bounds, and insufficient empirical validation against relevant baselines. The restriction on repeated queries needs rigorous justification, and all claims about performance advantages must be supported by direct comparisons. Without substantial revisions—particularly to clarify the Ahn-Lee-Suh conflict, tighten the bounds in Theorems 3–4, and add method-level comparisons to same-cluster queries—the paper does not meet NeurIPS standards for clarity or impact.  

**Summary of required changes:**  
- Resolve Theorem 1 vs. [Ahn-Lee-Suh] conflict with precise analysis.  
- Rewrite Theorems 3–4 to express bounds explicitly in m(n, δ).  
- Add same-cluster-query *algorithm* to Figures 2–5; explain crossing points.  
- Justify the no-repeated-queries model with real-data evidence or theoretical argument.  
- Clarify Algorithm 1’s practical requirements (p, cluster sizes) and provide implementation details.  
- Simplify proofs and improve exposition throughout.  

The work has potential but requires major revisions to establish its novelty, correctness, and practical relevance. As currently presented, it fails to convincingly demonstrate superiority over existing methods or justify its core assumptions.
