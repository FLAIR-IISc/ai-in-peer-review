

This paper investigates a clustering problem where the goal is to reconstruct the true labels of elements from possibly noisy, non-adaptive query answers, each involving only a small number of elements. The authors derive lower bounds on the number of queries required for exact or partial reconstruction of ground-truth labels across various scenarios. They also propose an "AND-queries" algorithm applicable to arbitrary k-cluster cases and validate it through real-world experiments.

While the paper presents theoretical analyses for multiple scenarios and develops an algorithm with empirical validation, it suffers from issues in presentation, clarity of results, and comparison to related work. Significant revisions would be needed for publication. Below are detailed comments:

1. The constraint that the same query cannot be repeated appears restrictive without sufficient justification. Allowing repeated queries is a natural way to combat noise, and the paper should compare against a no-restriction model. The authors justify this constraint by arguing that repeated answers to the same query may not be independent, but this issue could be resolved by considering answers from distinct annotators, which would provide independent responses. The paper should include experiments comparing their approach with one that allows repeated queries to validate this constraint.

2. Theorem 1 claims that for noiseless exact recovery with XOR queries (same-cluster queries), the query complexity is m = n(h(p)+o(1))log₂(1/α) for some α < 1. However, a recent work by Ahn, Lee, and Suh [Ahn-Lee-Suh] studies the same setting and reports a complexity of m = Θ(n log n/Δ) when p = 1/2 and Δ is constant. This discrepancy needs clarification. The proof of Theorem 1 is also difficult to follow.

3. The lower bound in Theorem 3 is expressed in a complex form that makes it challenging to interpret the relationship between m, n, and δ. It would be more useful to express this bound in a form that clearly shows how the query complexity scales with the desired accuracy δ.

4. Similarly, Theorem 4's upper bound is hard to interpret, and it's unclear how it compares to the lower bound in Theorem 3. Is the gap between these bounds bounded or unbounded? The proof of Theorem 4 is also difficult to follow.

5. Figure 2 compares the proposed method to the lower bound for same-cluster queries but does not include a comparison to the actual performance of same-cluster query methods. Without this comparison, it's impossible to verify the paper's claim that AND queries outperform same-cluster queries. The authors should include both the lower bound and the actual performance of same-cluster queries for a fair comparison.

6. Algorithm 1 requires prior knowledge of p or relative cluster sizes (as shown in Theorem 6). The paper should provide a practical method for obtaining this information. The pseudo-code is difficult to follow without the detailed proof of Theorem 5, which itself is challenging to understand.

7. The figures (3, 4, and 5) show results for the proposed algorithm but lack comparison to state-of-the-art methods like same-cluster queries. This is crucial to support the paper's claims about performance improvements.

[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
