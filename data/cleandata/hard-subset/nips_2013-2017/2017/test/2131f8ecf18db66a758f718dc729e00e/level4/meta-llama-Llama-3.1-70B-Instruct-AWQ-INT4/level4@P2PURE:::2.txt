This paper explores a clustering problem where the goal is to reconstruct the true labels of elements from possibly noisy and non-adaptive query answers, with each query associating only a few elements. It derives lower bounds on the number of queries required to reconstruct the ground-truth labels exactly or partially in various scenarios. The paper also proposes an "AND-queries" algorithm applicable to the arbitrary k-cluster case and evaluates its performance on a real dataset.

While the paper attempts to provide theoretical analyses for various scenarios and develops an algorithm with real-data simulations, it has limitations in presentation, clarity of the claimed results, and comparison to related works. Significant changes and possibly new developments are needed for publication. Detailed comments are provided below.

1. The constraint of not repeating the same question appears restrictive. This restriction may degrade performance compared to the no-restriction case, as allowing the same questions is a natural way to combat the noise effect. A possible justification for this restricted model is to provide an extensive set of real-data simulations comparing the proposed algorithm to an algorithm tailored for the no-restriction case, demonstrating the proposed algorithm's better performance. Alternatively, investigating the no-restriction case might be more plausible. The authors' concern about the independence-vs-dependence issue for multiple answers to the same query can be resolved by focusing on a setting where the same query is given to distinct annotators, assuming the answers from the annotators are independent.

2. The noiseless setting with exact recovery was also studied in a recent work [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which considers the XOR-operation ('same-cluster' queries) under a non-adaptive measurement setting. However, the result in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] does not coincide with the one claimed in Theorem 1. For instance, when p=1/2 and \Delta is a constant, the query complexity in Theorem 1 is m=n/log2, while the one in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] is m=nlogn/\Delta. This discrepancy needs to be clarified in detail. Moreover, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow.

3. The expression of the lower bound on \delta in Theorem 3 is hard to interpret. Specifically, it is challenging to infer the bound on m in terms of n and \delta, which would directly show the performance improvement due to the relaxed constraint reflected in \delta.

4. The same comment regarding Theorem 3 applies to Theorem 4. Additionally, how different is the upper bound from the lower bound in Theorem 3? Can the gap be unbounded? Again, the proof is difficult to follow.

5. Figure 2 lacks a comparison to the same-cluster query method; instead, it only compares to the same-cluster-query 'lower' bound. It would be interesting to know if there is a crossing point when compared to the same-cluster-query 'method.' If so, what is the intuition behind this? The paper claims that the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster query scheme, but this cannot be supported by Figure 2.

6. The proposed Algorithm 1 requires prior knowledge of p or the relative sizes of clusters (as seen in Theorem 6). It is unclear how to obtain such information in practice. A proper justification would be necessary to validate the practicality of the algorithm. Furthermore, the pseudo-code is hard to follow without relying on the detailed proof of Theorem 5, which is also difficult to grasp.

7. Figures 3, 4, and 5 lack a comparison to state-of-the-art methods, such as the same-cluster-query method, which is crucial to support the claim that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme.

Reference:
[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
