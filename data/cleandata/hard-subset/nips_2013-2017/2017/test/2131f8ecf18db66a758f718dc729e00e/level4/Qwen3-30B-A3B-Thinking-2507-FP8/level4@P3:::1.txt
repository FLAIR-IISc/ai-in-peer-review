

This paper investigates the connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, with the goal of determining the minimal number of queries required to recover cluster labels. The authors study a model where an oracle provides binary answers to queries about element pairs (e.g., "are these two elements in the same cluster?"), with the constraint that each query involves at most a constant number of elements. They establish a novel equivalence between this clustering problem and locally encodable source coding, a concept previously mentioned only in [23] but not thoroughly explored. This connection enables them to derive information-theoretic lower bounds on query complexity, as well as propose a new AND query strategy that outperforms traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery.

The paper builds on recent work in crowdsourced clustering [33, 30, 8, 21], particularly the query complexity analysis in [4] for k-means clustering. However, it makes a significant conceptual leap by framing the problem through the lens of information theory, rather than focusing solely on algorithmic design. This perspective allows them to derive fundamental limits on query complexity that depend on the prior label distribution, not just the number of elements. For the case of two clusters, they provide both upper and lower bounds showing that the number of queries required can be strictly less than n (the naive bound) when leveraging the prior distribution. Their AND query strategy proves particularly effective for approximate recovery, as it requires fewer queries than "same cluster" queries to achieve the same error rate.

Strengths: The paper's main contribution is the novel theoretical connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding, which provides a fresh perspective on query complexity. The information-theoretic approach is elegant and leads to meaningful lower bounds that are nontrivial and better than naive bounds for certain parameter ranges. The authors provide both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation through a real crowdsourcing experiment on movie genre classification, which strengthens their claims. The AND query strategy is well-motivated and demonstrates clear practical advantages over existing approaches.

Weaknesses: While the theoretical results are sound, the paper could better connect with the broader machine learning community. The focus on information-theoretic limits without addressing how these results might inform practical clustering algorithms is somewhat limiting. The paper also doesn't sufficiently compare with related work on query complexity in clustering (e.g., [21, 22]), which has developed sophisticated adaptive algorithms that might achieve better performance in practice. The experimental validation is limited to a single dataset with only 200 elements, which doesn't fully demonstrate the scalability of their approach.

Quality: The technical content is strong, with rigorous proofs of the main theorems. The information-theoretic analysis is sound, and the lower bounds are meaningful improvements over naive bounds. The experimental validation is appropriate for the theoretical nature of the paper, though the dataset size is small. The paper is careful to address both strengths and weaknesses of their approach, particularly noting that AND queries outperform "same cluster" queries but require different decoding strategies.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results to experimental validation. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the proofs are sufficiently detailed for experts. The real-world experiment provides a valuable bridge between theory and practice, making the paper more accessible to practitioners.

Originality: This is the paper's strongest aspect. The connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is novel, with no prior work establishing this equivalence. The AND query strategy is also original and represents a significant conceptual advance over existing work that has primarily focused on "same cluster" queries. The information-theoretic lower bounds for locally encodable source coding are new contributions to information theory.

Significance: While the results are primarily theoretical, they provide important fundamental insights into query complexity for clustering problems. The AND query strategy has clear practical implications for crowdsourcing applications, as demonstrated in the real-world experiment where it yielded fewer errors than "same cluster" queries. The paper advances our understanding of the fundamental limits of query-based clustering and opens new avenues for research at the intersection of information theory and machine learning.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Novel theoretical connection between clustering and information theory
- Strong information-theoretic lower bounds that improve upon naive bounds
- Practical AND query strategy with demonstrated advantages over existing approaches
- Real-world validation of theoretical claims
- Well-structured and clearly written paper

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Limited experimental validation (small dataset size)
- Limited connection to practical clustering algorithms beyond the theoretical bounds
- Some related work on query complexity for clustering is not sufficiently compared

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to understanding the fundamental limits of query-based clustering. The connection to locally encodable source coding is elegant and fruitful, and the AND query strategy represents a meaningful advancement over existing approaches. While the practical validation is limited, the theoretical results are strong and the paper would be a valuable contribution to NIPS. The paper's significance is enhanced by its potential to inform future work in crowdsourcing and query-based learning, making it a strong candidate for acceptance.
