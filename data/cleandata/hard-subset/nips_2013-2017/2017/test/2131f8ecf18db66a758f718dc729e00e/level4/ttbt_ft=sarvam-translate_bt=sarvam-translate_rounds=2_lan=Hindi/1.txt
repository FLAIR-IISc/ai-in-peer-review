The author studies a clustering model where the learner is allowed to ask an oracle a question and the goal is to find the clustering. The questions can be similar-cluster questions [or more generally, a fixed binary-valued function of the labels of input examples]. The author studies the query complexity of such models.

Query-based clustering models are a well-motivated and relatively recent research area, with applications in record-duplication, crowdsourcing, etc. The author studies the problem from an information-theoretical perspective. In particular, they provide upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required to fully (or approximately) recover the target clustering. They also consider the case that the oracle is noisy. The information-theoretical tools they use are interesting and new to the ML community.

Because no structural assumptions about the problem have been made (for example, a hypothesis class, etc.), the analysis is limited. In fact, the marginal distribution of labels is the main parameter on which the bounds are based (in addition to the noise level). Therefore, the setting studied is slightly different from the general settings studied in learning theory. However, this is a first step, and can help in understanding the information-theoretical aspects of query-based clustering, especially the relevant lower bounds.
