This paper explores a clustering problem focused on reconstructing true labels from potentially noisy, non-adaptive query answers, where each query involves only a few elements. It derives lower bounds on the number of queries needed for exact or partial reconstruction in various scenarios and proposes an "AND-queries" algorithm applicable to arbitrary k-cluster cases, evaluating its performance with a real dataset. While the paper aims to provide theoretical analysis and an algorithm with empirical validation, it suffers from issues in presentation, clarity of results, and comparison to related work. Significant revisions are likely needed for publication.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The paper establishes an equivalence between a recent model of semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding. This connection is used to derive lower bounds on the number of queries required to accurately label elements, given noisy answers to pairwise queries. The authors argue that ‘AND’ queries are more efficient than traditional ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries, particularly in approximate recovery scenarios. They present a querying scheme and algorithm based on AND queries, validated through experiments with Amazon Mechanical Turk data.

**Related Work & Context:**

The work builds on the growing area of crowdsourced clustering and entity resolution (e.g., [33, 30, 8, 20]). It appropriately positions itself within the context of information theory, specifically locally encodable and decodable source coding [23, 19, 16]. The connection to source coding is a novel and interesting perspective. The paper also correctly cites relevant work in crowdsourcing schemes [12, 13, 28, 34, 15] and query complexity [4, 22, 21].

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Connection:** Establishing the equivalence between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is a significant theoretical contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper attempts to provide rigorous theoretical analyses, including lower bounds on query complexity.
* **Practical Validation:** The inclusion of experiments using Amazon Mechanical Turk data provides some empirical support for the proposed approach.
* **Addressing Noise:** The consideration of noisy query answers is a realistic and important aspect of crowdsourcing scenarios.
* **AND Query Proposal:** The idea of using AND queries as an alternative to XOR queries is intriguing and potentially beneficial.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Restricted Model:** The constraint prohibiting repeated queries is limiting and requires stronger justification. The authors mention independence of answers as a motivation, but a comparison to algorithms designed for repeated queries (perhaps with mechanisms to handle dependence) would be beneficial. A clearer explanation of why repetition isn't feasible in the target applications is needed.
* **Clarity of Theorems:** The statements of Theorems 1, 3, and 4 are difficult to interpret, particularly regarding the concrete implications for query complexity (m) in relation to the number of elements (n) and the desired accuracy (δ). The relationship between the upper and lower bounds also needs more clarification.
* **Proof Readability:** The proofs are challenging to follow. More detailed explanations and intermediate steps are necessary for clarity.
* **Incomplete Comparisons:** Figures 2 and 5 lack critical comparisons to existing methods, especially the “same cluster” query approach. Simply stating that the proposed method outperforms the existing one isn't sufficient; a visual comparison showing the performance gap is crucial.
* **Practical Considerations:** The requirement for prior knowledge of the cluster sizes (p or relative sizes) is a practical limitation. The paper needs to discuss how this information can be estimated in real-world scenarios.
* **Algorithm Presentation:** Algorithm 1’s description is difficult to follow without a deep understanding of the supporting proof, which is itself complex. A more intuitive explanation of the algorithm’s steps would be helpful.
* **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experimental validation is limited. More extensive comparisons with state-of-the-art algorithms on various datasets would strengthen the conclusions.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The connection to locally encodable source coding and the proposal of AND queries represent genuinely new ideas.
* **Theoretical Contribution:** The derived lower bounds, even if initially unclear, contribute to the understanding of query complexity in this setting.
* **Potential Impact:** If the AND query approach demonstrably outperforms existing methods, it could have a significant impact on practical crowdsourced clustering applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Lack of Clarity:** The presentation and explanation of key results are often unclear and difficult to follow.
* **Incomplete Evaluation:** The lack of comprehensive comparisons to existing methods weakens the claims of superiority.
* **Practical Limitations:** The requirement for prior knowledge and the restriction on query repetition raise practical concerns.
* **Proof Complexity:** The complex proofs hinder the ability of reviewers to fully assess the validity of the theoretical results.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical framework for analyzing query complexity in semisupervised clustering. However, significant revisions are required to improve clarity, strengthen the empirical validation, and address the practical limitations. I currently lean towards **reject** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. The core ideas have merit, but the presentation and justification need substantial improvement.

**Specific Suggestions for Revision:**

* **Justify the Non-Repetition Constraint:** Provide a more compelling argument for this restriction or explore alternatives.
* **Clarify Theorem Statements:** Rewrite the theorems to clearly state the implications for query complexity (m) given n and δ.
* **Improve Proof Readability:** Add more intermediate steps and explanations to the proofs.
* **Add Comparative Results:** Include comprehensive comparisons to the “same cluster” query method in Figures 2 and 5.
* **Address Prior Knowledge:** Discuss practical methods for estimating the prior distribution or cluster sizes.
* **Simplify Algorithm Presentation:** Provide a more intuitive explanation of Algorithm 1.
* **Expand Experimental Validation:** Conduct experiments on a wider range of datasets and compare to more state-of-the-art algorithms.
