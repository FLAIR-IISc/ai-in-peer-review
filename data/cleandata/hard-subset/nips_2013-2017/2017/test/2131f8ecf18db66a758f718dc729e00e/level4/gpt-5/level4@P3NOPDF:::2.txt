Summary
The paper studies non-adaptive clustering from noisy queries that involve small subsets of items. It develops lower bounds on the number of queries needed for exact and partial recovery in several settings, proposes an AND-queries algorithm for general k, and evaluates it on a real dataset.

Strengths
- Tackles an important and timely problem: label recovery from limited, noisy, non-adaptive queries.
- Attempts a broad theoretical treatment (exact vs. partial recovery; varying noise and query structures).
- Proposes a concrete algorithm for arbitrary k and provides empirical results.

Main concerns and requests
1) Model restriction: no repeated queries
- The prohibition on repeating the same query feels restrictive and likely suboptimal under noise, since repetition is a natural variance-reduction device.
- Please justify this choice empirically and theoretically. At minimum:
  - Provide experiments comparing your method with and without allowing repeated queries, ideally including a baseline tailored to the repeat-allowed case.
  - If analysis concerns about dependence motivated the restriction, consider a setting where identical queries are posed to independent annotators to recover independence, or explicitly discuss why such a setting is impractical or does not help.
- If repeated queries do help, consider analyzing that more realistic model.

2) Theorem 1: discrepancy with Ahn–Lee–Suh (Allerton ’16)
- In the noiseless, exact-recovery setting, Ahn–Lee–Suh study same-cluster (XOR) queries under non-adaptive measurements. Your bound appears to differ. For example, when p = 1/2 and Δ is constant, your Theorem 1 suggests m = n/ log 2 (i.e., Θ(n)), whereas Ahn–Lee–Suh report m = Θ(n log n / Δ).
- Please reconcile these results by clarifying all modeling differences: query arity, operation (XOR vs. AND), noise/noiseless assumptions, cluster-size balance, and recovery criteria. If your setting is strictly easier, explain precisely why and restate the theorem accordingly.
- The proof of Theorem 1 is hard to follow. Please provide a detailed proof roadmap, explicitly state all assumptions and notational conventions, and streamline or relocate technical lemmas to an appendix while preserving clarity in the main text.

3) Theorem 3: interpretability of the lower bound
- The bound on δ is difficult to parse. Please invert or restate the result to express m as a function of n and δ, including leading-order terms or tight asymptotics in meaningful regimes (e.g., small δ, large k, balanced vs. imbalanced clusters).
- Provide intuition for how relaxation (δ > 0) reduces query complexity relative to exact recovery and quantify the improvement.

4) Theorem 4: gap to lower bounds and proof clarity
- As with Theorem 3, please present the upper bound in terms of m = m(n, δ, …) with clear asymptotics and regimes.
- Quantify the gap between this upper bound and the lower bound of Theorem 3. Is the gap constant, logarithmic, or can it be unbounded in some regimes? If the bounds are order-tight only under extra conditions, state those explicitly.
- The proof is also difficult to follow. A clearer structure and more explicit dependencies on problem parameters would help.

5) Figure 2: missing algorithmic baselines and claims
- The figure compares to a lower bound for same-cluster queries but not to an actual same-cluster algorithm. To support the claim that AND-queries outperform same-cluster schemes, include a strong same-cluster baseline implementation under matched budgets and noise.
- If the curves cross, provide intuition and a principled explanation (e.g., information content per query vs. robustness to noise and imbalance).

6) Algorithm 1: practicality and presentation
- The algorithm appears to require knowledge of p or relative cluster sizes (Theorem 6). How are these obtained in practice?
  - Consider including an estimation/calibration phase, a plug-in estimator with performance guarantees, or sensitivity analyses showing robustness to misspecification.
- The pseudocode is difficult to follow without the full proof of Theorem 5. Please add a high-level description of the algorithm’s stages, assumptions, complexity, and stopping criteria, making the procedure self-contained.

7) Figures 3–5: comparisons to state of the art
- Add quantitative comparisons to established baselines (e.g., same-cluster query methods and other non-adaptive/noisy clustering algorithms), with consistent query budgets, noise parameters, and k.
- Report variance (error bars) and include ablations (e.g., effect of allowing repeats; query arity; imbalance).

Additional presentation suggestions
- Clearly define all notation on first use (e.g., p, Δ, δ, k, query arity) and maintain consistency.
- State the main theorems informally before the formal versions to convey intuition and regimes.
- Where possible, provide simple special cases and back-of-the-envelope scaling to aid interpretation.

Post-rebuttal comments
- I appreciate the responses and found several points addressed. Two issues remain:
  1) Usefulness of repeating the same query: It is still unclear whether repetition is provably unhelpful in your model. If a prior work (the “Nature” paper mentioned in the rebuttal) establishes that repeats do not help, please cite it precisely, state the assumptions under which the result holds, and explain whether those assumptions match yours. Otherwise, please clarify empirically and/or theoretically in the revision.
  2) Comparison to the same-cluster method: The verbal clarifications in the response were helpful; please incorporate them into the paper with concrete experiments and discussion so that readers can fully assess the trade-offs.

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important problem and offers promising ideas. However, key theoretical claims need clearer statements and reconciliations with related work, the proofs require substantial tightening, and the empirical validation must include stronger baselines and practical considerations. With significant revisions along the lines above, the work could become a valuable contribution.
