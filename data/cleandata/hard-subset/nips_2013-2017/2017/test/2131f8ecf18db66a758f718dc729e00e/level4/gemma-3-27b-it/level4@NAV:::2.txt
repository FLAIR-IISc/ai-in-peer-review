This paper investigates a clustering problem focused on reconstructing true labels from potentially noisy and non-adaptive query responses, where each query involves only a small subset of elements. The work establishes lower bounds on the number of queries needed for exact or partial ground-truth label recovery across various scenarios, and introduces an “AND-queries” algorithm applicable to arbitrary k-cluster cases. The algorithm’s performance is evaluated using a real dataset.

While the paper presents theoretical analyses for several scenarios and proposes an algorithm with empirical evaluation, the presentation and clarity of the results, along with a comparison to relevant prior work, require significant improvement. Substantial revisions and potentially further development will be necessary for publication. Specific comments are detailed below:

1. The constraint prohibiting repeated queries appears restrictive and may hinder performance, as allowing repetition is a natural approach to mitigating noise. Justification for this restriction requires comprehensive real-data simulations comparing the proposed algorithm to one designed for the unrestricted case, demonstrating a performance benefit. If no such benefit exists, exploring the unrestricted case would be more appropriate. The authors’ concern regarding independence versus dependence of multiple answers to the same query can be addressed by framing the problem with distinct annotators, assuming independent responses.

2. Theorem 1’s noiseless, exact recovery setting is also investigated in [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which considers XOR-operation ('same-cluster') queries under a non-adaptive measurement setting. However, the result in Theorem 1 does not align with that presented in [Ahn-Lee-Suh]. For example, with p=1/2 and a constant Δ, Theorem 1 yields a query complexity of m=n/log2, while [Ahn-Lee-Suh] provides m=nlogn/Δ. A detailed clarification is required. Furthermore, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow.

3. The expression for the lower bound on δ in Theorem 3 is difficult to interpret, making it challenging to infer the corresponding bound on m in terms of n and δ, and thus assess the performance improvement resulting from the relaxed constraint reflected in δ.

4. The same comment applies to Theorem 4. Additionally, what is the relationship between the upper and lower bounds presented in Theorem 3? Could this gap be unbounded? The proof is also difficult to follow.

5. Figure 2 lacks a comparison to the same-cluster query method, presenting only a comparison to the same-cluster-query lower bound. It is unclear if a crossover point exists when compared to the same-cluster-query method, and if so, what the underlying intuition might be. The paper’s claim of outperforming the same-cluster query scheme is not supported by the presented Figure 2.

6. Algorithm 1 requires prior knowledge of p or the relative sizes of the clusters (as highlighted by Theorem 6). Practical methods for obtaining this information are not discussed, and a justification for the algorithm’s practicality is needed. The pseudo-code is difficult to follow without referencing the complex proof of Theorem 5.

7. Figures 3, 4, and 5 do not include comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, such as the same-cluster-query method, which is essential for supporting the claim that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme.

[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
