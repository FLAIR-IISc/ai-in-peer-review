This is an interesting paper, and I enjoyed reading it. The authors address the problem of clustering data points using crowdsourcing, specifically focusing on recovering the original labels of data points from responses to similarity-based cluster queries. They derive bounds on the number of queries required to recover these labels, considering both noisy and noiseless responses. The paper's main contribution is theoretical, supported by empirical simulations. It is well-written and clearly explains the theoretical contributions, making it accessible to a broad machine learning audience. The connection to Information Theory, used to derive the bounds, is particularly well-explained.

I have the following concerns that I believe would strengthen the paper:

(1) While the theoretical use of AND queries is interesting, it seems to deviate from the typical motivation for crowdsourcing. An AND query effectively reveals the cluster label as part of the answer, rather than simply asking whether data points of interest belong to the same cluster. If my interpretation is correct, this raises the question of why one would need to pose these similarity queries at all – why not directly ask for the label of a data point? This is my primary point of confusion regarding the paper’s contributions.

(2) Is it possible to simplify the expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1, 2, 3, and 4 without sacrificing the core results?

(3) The experiments appear to focus exclusively on AND queries. If this is the case, the validity of the experimental study is questionable unless the issue raised in point (1) is adequately addressed.

I am satisfied with the authors’ responses to my questions. Therefore, I vote to accept this paper.
