This paper studies clustering with query access to an oracle, focusing on the number of oracle queries needed to recover the target clustering. The queries include same-cluster questions, and more generally any fixed binary-valued function of the labels. The authors analyze this problem from an information-theoretic standpoint and derive upper and lower bounds on the query complexity for both exact and approximate recovery. They also consider a noisy oracle. The information-theoretic techniques appear novel to the ML audience and are well-chosen for this setting.

Strengths:
- The query-based clustering framework is timely and well-motivated, with clear applications in record linkage/deduplication and crowdsourcing.
- The paper takes a clean information-theoretic perspective and provides explicit query-complexity bounds, including lower bounds that clarify inherent limits independent of algorithmic details.
- The treatment includes both perfect and approximate recovery as well as a noisy-oracle model, making the results more broadly applicable.
- The use of information-theoretic tools is a positive contribution that could stimulate further work at the intersection of active learning and clustering.

Limitations and concerns:
- The analysis makes essentially no structural assumptions about the data or the hypothesis class underlying the clustering. As a result, the bounds depend largely on the marginal distribution of the labels (and the oracle’s noise level), rather than on geometric or probabilistic structure commonly studied in learning theory (e.g., separability assumptions, mixture models, cluster margin conditions).
- Because of this generality, the setting is somewhat removed from standard ML models where structural assumptions enable sharper, instance-dependent guarantees. The resulting bounds, while informative, may be loose or difficult to interpret for practical scenarios without additional modeling assumptions.
- It would be helpful to better articulate where the upper and lower bounds match (or nearly match), and to highlight regimes of parameters (e.g., class imbalance, noise rates) where the results are tight versus conservative.

Clarity and presentation:
- The core problem and motivation are clear, and the connection to applications is convincing.
- The role of the marginal label distribution as the key parameter could be emphasized earlier, to set expectations about the scope of the results.
- For accessibility, consider briefly summarizing the main information-theoretic tools and their intuition, and how they differ from common techniques in clustering and active learning.

Overall assessment:
This is a solid and timely first step toward an information-theoretic understanding of query-based clustering. The inclusion of both upper and lower bounds, and the extension to noisy oracles, are valuable. The main limitation is the lack of structural assumptions, which constrains the specificity and practical relevance of the results. Nonetheless, the paper advances our understanding of fundamental limits in this area and should help guide future work that introduces realistic structure (e.g., mixture models, metric assumptions, stability/margin conditions) to obtain sharper, instance-dependent bounds and algorithmic insights.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clearly delineate the tightness of the bounds across parameter regimes; add a concise table or summary if space permits.
- Discuss how the results might specialize under common structural assumptions (even at a high level), to bridge toward more applied settings.
- Provide a short comparison to related work in active learning, crowdsourced clustering, and record linkage that uses same-cluster queries, to position the contribution within the broader literature.
- If possible, include simple illustrative examples that show how the marginal label distribution and noise influence the query complexity.
