### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of semi-supervised clustering, where the goal is to recover the true labels of `n` elements using a minimal number of non-adaptive queries to an oracle (e.g., a crowd worker). The authors establish a novel and insightful equivalence between this problem and the information-theoretic concept of locally encodable source coding, where each compressed bit depends on a small, constant number of input bits.

Leveraging this connection, the paper derives several theoretical results, including information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery of labels. A key contribution is the analysis of different query types. The authors demonstrate the limitations of standard pairwise "same-cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery and propose a new type of query, the "AND-query," which is shown to be provably more efficient in certain regimes. The analysis is extended to handle noisy oracle responses, and an algorithm is presented that achieves approximate recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on a real crowdsourced dataset.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is the novel connection it establishes between semi-supervised clustering with local queries and locally encodable source coding. This is a significant conceptual contribution that provides a new theoretical framework and a powerful set of tools for analyzing query complexity in this domain. This framing allows the authors to derive new fundamental limits that were not previously understood.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. It provides rigorous theoretical analysis, including new upper and lower bounds on the number of queries for various settings (noiseless/noisy, exact/approximate recovery). The use of information-theoretic tools, such as rate-distortion theory and coding theory arguments (e.g., expurgation), to analyze the clustering problem is impressive and well-executed.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the information-theoretic viewpoint and making the technical contributions accessible to a broader machine learning audience. The problem setup, motivations, and main results are presented clearly.

4.  **Novel Query Type:** The introduction and analysis of AND-queries is a valuable contribution. The paper not only proposes this new query type but also provides both a theoretical justification for its superiority over "same-cluster" queries in the approximate recovery setting and empirical evidence suggesting that human workers may find them easier to answer, leading to lower error rates.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification (Arguments Against Acceptance)**

1.  **Practical Motivation of AND-Queries:** My main concern relates to the practical motivation behind the AND-query. The query, as formulated in the experiments ("Are both the movies ‘action’ movies?"), requires the oracle/worker to know the specific label ("action") to provide a positive answer. This seems to circumvent the core challenge of clustering, which is to discover group membership without knowing the labels beforehand. If a worker can answer this query, it implies they already possess label information. A natural question arises: why not simply ask for the label of a single data point directly ("Is this movie an 'action' movie?")? The introduction briefly argues against direct label queries, but a more thorough discussion comparing the cognitive load, error models, and information content of a direct label query versus a pairwise AND-query would significantly strengthen the paper's practical relevance and motivation.

2.  **Clarity of Theoretical Bounds:** While the derivations are rigorous, the final expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1-4 are quite complex. For broader accessibility and impact, it would be beneficial if the authors could provide simplified versions, asymptotic analyses, or more intuitive interpretations of these bounds. This would help the reader to more easily grasp the key takeaways regarding the trade-offs between parameters like `p`, `δ`, `∆`, and the number of queries `m`.

3.  **Experimental Comparison:** The experimental section provides a good proof-of-concept for the AND-query algorithm on a real dataset. However, the comparison with the traditional "same-cluster" queries could be strengthened. The authors note that "same-cluster" queries on the same pairs resulted in more than double the errors (234 vs. 105), which is a compelling finding that supports the usability of AND-queries. This point deserves more emphasis. The main performance comparison (Figure 6) is then conducted on synthetic data. A direct comparison of the recovery algorithms for both query types on the real-world dataset would provide a more powerful empirical conclusion, even if it requires special handling or discussion of the different noise levels.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper that introduces a novel and valuable perspective on semi-supervised clustering. The connection to locally encodable codes is elegant and leads to new insights and rigorous bounds. While the practical motivation for the proposed AND-queries could be further clarified and the experimental comparison strengthened, the theoretical contributions are significant and of high quality. I believe the paper makes a solid contribution to the field. I am currently leaning towards acceptance, and my score would likely increase if the authors can satisfactorily address the practical motivation of AND-queries in their rebuttal.
