This paper investigates a clustering model where the learner actively queries an oracle to identify the correct clustering. These queries assess whether pairs of instances belong to the same cluster – effectively, a binary function of the instance labels. The work focuses on determining the query complexity required to achieve accurate clustering, both in perfect and approximate scenarios, and extends to consider noisy oracle responses. 

The query-based clustering paradigm is a promising and developing area of research, with practical relevance in fields like record linkage and crowdsourcing. The authors approach this problem through an information-theoretic lens, establishing both upper and lower bounds on query requirements. The information-theoretic techniques employed represent a novel contribution to the machine learning literature. 

A key characteristic of this work is its lack of structural assumptions – it doesn't rely on concepts like hypothesis classes. Consequently, the analysis is largely driven by the marginal distribution of the labels and the level of noise. While this places the study somewhat outside traditional learning theory frameworks, it represents an important initial step towards understanding the fundamental information-theoretic limits of query-based clustering, particularly regarding achievable lower bounds.
