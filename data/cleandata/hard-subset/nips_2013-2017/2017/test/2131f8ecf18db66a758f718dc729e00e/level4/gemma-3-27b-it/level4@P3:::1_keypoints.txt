- Investigates query complexity of semi-supervised clustering by framing it as a locally encodable source coding problem.  
- Establishes connections between oracle queries for cluster assignments and information-theoretic limits.  
- Provides upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed for accurate label recovery.  
- Extends analysis to noisy oracle responses and gives bounds in that setting.  
- Motivated by practical applications in crowdsourcing and entity resolution.  
- Novel contribution: linking semi-supervised clustering to locally encodable source coding.  
- Theoretical rigor: formal information-theoretic lower bounds on query complexity.  
- Practical relevance: real-world applicability to crowdsourcing/entity resolution tasks.  
- Explores AND queries as a potentially better alternative to “same cluster” queries.  
- Includes experimental validation on real datasets, adding credibility to theoretical claims.  
- Clarity issues: dense writing and notation that hinder accessibility.  
- Proofs often lack intuitive explanations, making the core ideas hard to follow.  
- Key design choices (e.g., using a d-regular graph in noisy setting) need stronger justification.  
- Experimental section lacks sufficient detail about data, crowdworkers, and implementation.  
- Limited discussion of assumptions (e.g., known prior distribution) and their implications.  
- Connections to standard learning theory are acknowledged but underdeveloped.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, provides new insights and preliminary experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: presentation clarity, limited experimental validation, and strong/unexamined assumptions.  
- Overall recommendation: accept if authors improve clarity, add intuitive proof explanations, justify design choices, and expand experimental details.
