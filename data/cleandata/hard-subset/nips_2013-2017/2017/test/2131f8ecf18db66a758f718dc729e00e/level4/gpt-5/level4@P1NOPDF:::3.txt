This is an interesting and well-written paper. The authors study clustering via crowdsourcing: recovering the original labels of data points from answers to same-cluster queries. They derive bounds on the number of queries needed to recover the labels under both noiseless and noisy settings, and support the theory with simulations. The main contribution is theoretical, and the information-theoretic connections used to derive the bounds are explained clearly for a general machine learning audience.

I have the following concerns and suggestions for improvement:
1) While the idea of AND queries is theoretically interesting, it seems at odds with the core motivation for crowdsourcing. In an AND query, the answer effectively reveals the cluster label as part of the response, rather than only indicating whether two points belong to the same cluster. If my interpretation is correct, why issue similarity queries at all? Why not directly ask for the label of a single data point? This is my primary source of confusion regarding the paper’s contribution and its practical relevance.
2) It would be helpful to simplify the expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1–4 while preserving their essence. More compact or interpretable forms (e.g., highlighting leading terms and dependencies on key parameters) would improve readability and aid comparison across settings.
3) In the experiments, it appears that only AND queries are used. If so, the validity of the empirical study hinges on clarification of point (1). Without a clear practical justification for AND queries, the experimental setup may not reflect realistic crowdsourcing scenarios.

I am satisfied with the authors’ responses to these points. I therefore vote to accept this paper.
