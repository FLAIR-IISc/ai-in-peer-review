This paper addresses the problem of semi-supervised clustering, where the goal is to recover the ground-truth labels of `n` elements using a limited budget of non-adaptive queries. The key contribution is to establish a novel connection between this problem and the information-theoretic concept of locally encodable source coding, where each compressed bit (query answer) depends on only a small, constant number of input bits (element labels).

Building on this framework, the paper derives information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery of labels. A significant part of the work is dedicated to proposing and analyzing a new type of query, the "AND query," which is shown to be more efficient than the commonly used "same-cluster" (or XOR) query, particularly for approximate recovery in noisy settings. The paper presents algorithms based on AND queries for the `k`-cluster case and validates its theoretical claims with an experiment on a real crowdsourced dataset.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Theoretical Framework:** The connection drawn between semi-supervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is original and insightful. This framing provides a new theoretical lens through which to analyze the fundamental limits of query-based clustering and is a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Introduction of AND Queries:** The proposal of AND queries as an alternative to standard same-cluster queries is a simple yet powerful idea. The paper provides a theoretical basis for why AND queries can outperform XOR queries, especially in breaking symmetry and handling noise, which is a valuable contribution to the field of crowdsourcing and active learning.

3.  **Breadth of Analysis:** The paper commendably attempts a comprehensive analysis across various settings: noiseless and noisy queries, exact and approximate recovery, and general `k`-cluster scenarios. This breadth showcases the ambition of the work and the potential utility of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper, while containing promising ideas, has several weaknesses in its current form related to clarity, technical soundness, and the validation of its claims.

1.  **Clarity and Verification of Theoretical Results:**
    *   **Theorem 1:** The query complexity derived for exact recovery with XOR queries appears to contradict the results in [Ahn et al., 2016], which studied a very similar setting. For instance, for `p=1/2` and constant `∆`, Theorem 1 suggests `m = O(n)` queries, while [Ahn et al.] requires `m = O(n log n)`. This discrepancy is significant and needs to be addressed directly. The proof sketch provided is too brief and difficult to follow, preventing a clear verification of the result. A more detailed derivation and a discussion of the differences with related work are necessary.
    *   **Theorems 3 & 4:** The expressions for the lower bound on the error `δ` (Theorem 3) and the achievable error (Theorem 4) are complex and difficult to interpret. For the results to be impactful, it would be much clearer to re-express them to show the required number of queries `m` as a function of `n` and `δ`. This would allow for a more direct comparison between the upper and lower bounds and an understanding of the performance gains from the relaxed (approximate) recovery constraint. Furthermore, the paper does not discuss the tightness of these bounds, leaving the reader to wonder how large the gap between them might be.

2.  **Insufficient Empirical Comparison:**
    *   The central claim of the paper is that AND queries are superior to same-cluster queries. However, the experimental evidence provided is not sufficient to fully support this. In Figure 2, the performance of the AND query scheme is compared only to a *lower bound* for same-cluster queries, not to a competitive algorithm that *uses* same-cluster queries (e.g., spectral clustering on the graph of query answers). To make a convincing case, a direct comparison against such a baseline is essential. It is possible that an actual same-cluster method performs better than the AND query method in some regimes, even if it does not meet the theoretical lower bound.
    *   Similarly, the experiments in the noisy setting (Figures 3, 4, 5) and the real-data experiment lack a comparison to state-of-the-art baselines. Demonstrating that Algorithm 1 outperforms existing methods for noisy query-based clustering would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

3.  **Practicality and Modeling Assumptions:**
    *   **No Query Repetition:** The model's constraint that the same query cannot be asked multiple times seems restrictive. The justification provided is that this avoids dealing with potentially dependent errors from the same worker. However, a common and practical model in crowdsourcing is to ask the same question to *different* workers, whose errors can be reasonably assumed to be independent. This is a standard technique for noise reduction. The authors should provide a stronger justification for this modeling choice, perhaps by clarifying in the revision how existing literature (e.g., the cited Nature paper) demonstrates that repetition is not beneficial in this context.
    *   **Knowledge of Priors:** Algorithm 1 requires knowledge of the cluster prior probabilities `p` (or their relative sizes). While Theorem 6 suggests this can be handled by using empirical estimates, the practicality of this approach is not fully explored. How is this information obtained in a real-world scenario where no labels are known? A brief discussion on robust estimation of these parameters or an analysis of the algorithm's sensitivity to errors in the estimated priors would enhance the practical relevance of the work.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper introduces a novel and promising information-theoretic perspective on semi-supervised clustering and proposes the interesting concept of AND queries. However, the manuscript suffers from significant issues in clarity, the substantiation of its theoretical claims, and a lack of convincing empirical comparisons to establish the superiority of the proposed methods. The apparent conflict with prior work on a key result is a serious concern.

Given these issues, I believe the paper is not yet ready for publication. Substantial revisions are needed to clarify the theoretical results, resolve contradictions with existing literature, and provide a more rigorous empirical validation against appropriate baselines.

**Arguments for Acceptance (after major revision):**
*   The novel connection to locally encodable source coding is a strong conceptual contribution.
*   AND queries are an interesting and potentially impactful alternative to standard queries in crowdsourcing.

**Arguments against Acceptance (in its current state):**
*   Key theoretical claims (Theorem 1) appear to conflict with prior work and are not clearly proven.
*   Central claims about the superiority of AND queries are not adequately supported by direct experimental comparisons with relevant baselines.
*   Clarity of theoretical results is low, making them difficult to interpret and assess.
*   Modeling assumptions (e.g., no query repetition) may limit the practical relevance without stronger justification.
