This paper explores a clustering problem with the goal of reconstructing true labels of elements from possibly noisy and non-adaptive query answers, each associating only a few elements. The authors derive lower bounds on the number of queries required to reconstruct the ground-truth labels exactly or partially in various scenarios. They also propose the "AND-queries" algorithm, applicable to the arbitrary k-cluster case, and evaluate its performance on a real dataset.

While the paper attempts to provide theoretical analyses for different scenarios and develops an algorithm with real-data simulation, it has limitations in presentation, clarity of the claimed results, and comparison to related works. Significant changes and possibly new developments are needed for publication. See below for detailed comments.

1. The constraint of not repeating the same question appears restrictive. This restriction may degrade performance compared to the no-restriction case, as allowing the same questions is a natural way to combat the noise effect. A proper justification for this restricted model could be provided by presenting an extensive set of real-data simulations comparing the proposed algorithm to one tailored for the no-restriction case, potentially showing better performance. If not, investigating the no-restriction case may be more plausible. The authors seem concerned about the independence-vs-dependence issue for multiple answers to the same query, which may make analysis challenging. However, this issue can be resolved by focusing on a setting where the same query is given to distinct annotators, assuming the answers from annotators are independent.

2. The noiseless setting with exact recovery was also studied in a recent work by Ahn-Lee-Suh, which considers the XOR-operation ('same-cluster' queries) under a non-adaptive measurement setting. However, the result in Ahn-Lee-Suh does not coincide with the one claimed in Theorem 1. For instance, when p=1/2 and \Delta is a constant, the query complexity in Theorem 1 reads m=n/log2, while the one in Ahn-Lee-Suh reads m=nlogn/\Delta. This discrepancy needs to be clarified in detail. Moreover, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow.

3. The expression of the lower bound on \delta in Theorem 3 is hard to interpret. Specifically, it is challenging to infer the bound on m in terms of n and \delta, which would directly show the performance improvement due to the relaxed constraint reflected in \delta.

4. The same comment applies to Theorem 4 as to Theorem 3. Additionally, how different is the upper bound from the lower bound in Theorem 3? Can the gap be unbounded? Again, the proof is hard to follow.

5. Figure 2 only compares the proposed approach to the same-cluster-query 'lower' bound, without comparing it to the same-cluster-query 'method'. It is unclear if there is a crossing point when compared to the same-cluster-query 'method'. If so, what is the intuition behind this? The paper claims that the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster query scheme, but this cannot be supported by Figure 2.

6. The proposed algorithm (Algorithm 1) requires prior knowledge of p or the relative sizes of clusters (in view of Theorem 6). It is unclear how to obtain such information in practice. A proper justification might validate the practicality of the algorithm. Also, the pseudo-code is difficult to follow without relying on the detailed proof of Theorem 5, which is also challenging to grasp.

7. Figures 3, 4, and 5 do not compare the proposed algorithm to state-of-the-art methods like the same-cluster-query method, which is crucial to support the claim that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme.

Reference:
[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
