The authors analyze a clustering model in which a learner can query an oracle with the goal of recovering the ground-truth clustering. The queries consist of same-cluster questions—or, more generally, any fixed binary-valued function of the instance labels. The paper focuses on the query complexity of this framework.

This query-driven clustering setting is well motivated and relatively recent, with applications in record deduplication, crowdsourcing, and related areas. The authors adopt an information-theoretic perspective, providing upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required to exactly (or approximately) recover the target clustering, and they also handle the case of a noisy oracle. The information-theoretic tools they employ are interesting and, to the ML community, largely novel.

Because the analysis makes no structural assumptions about the problem (e.g., no specified hypothesis class), its scope is limited. In particular, aside from the noise level, the bounds are driven primarily by the marginal distribution of the labels. Consequently, the setting departs from standard formulations in learning theory. Nonetheless, this is a useful first step toward understanding the information-theoretic aspects of query-based clustering, especially the associated lower bounds.
