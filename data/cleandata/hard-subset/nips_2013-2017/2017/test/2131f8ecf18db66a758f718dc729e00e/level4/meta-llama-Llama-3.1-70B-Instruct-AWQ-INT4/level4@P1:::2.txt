This paper explores a clustering problem where the goal is to reconstruct the true labels of elements from possibly noisy and non-adaptive query answers, each associating only a few elements. It derives lower bounds on the number of queries required to reconstruct the ground-truth labels exactly or partially in a variety of scenarios. It also proposes a so-called "AND-queries" algorithm that applies to arbitrary k-cluster cases and evaluates the performance of the algorithm under a real data set.

While the paper attempts to provide theoretical analyses for various scenarios as well as develops an algorithm together with a real-data simulation, it comes with limitations in presentation, clarity of the claimed results, and comparison to related works. Perhaps significant changes and possibly new developments are to be made for publication.

1. The constraint that repeating the same questions is not allowed looks a bit restricted. This constraint may degrade the performance compared to the no-restriction case, as allowing the same questions is one natural way to combat the noise effect. One proper way to justify the restricted model might be providing an extensive set of real-data simulations with a comparison to an algorithm tailored for the no-restriction case and possibly showing a better performance of the proposed algorithm. If that is not the case, the no-restriction case is more plausible to investigate instead. The authors seem to concern about independence-vs-dependence issue for multiple answers w.r.t the same query, which might make analysis challenging. However, this issue can be resolved by focusing on a setting in which the same query is given to distinct annotators and therefore the answers from the annotators are assumed to be independent.

2. The noiseless setting with exact recovery was also studied in a recent work [Ahn-Lee-Suh] which also considers the XOR-operation ('same-cluster' queries) under a non-adaptive measurement setting. However, the result in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] does not coincide with the one claimed in Theorem 1. For instance, when p=1/2 and Δ is a constant, the query complexity in Theorem 1 reads m=n/log2, while the one in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] reads m=nlogn/Δ. This needs to be clarified in details. Moreover, the proof of Theorem 1 is hard to follow and requires further clarification.

3. The expression of the lower bound on δ in Theorem 3 is difficult to interpret. In particular, it is difficult to infer the bound on m in terms of n and δ, from which one can directly see the performance improvement due to the relaxed constraint, reflected in δ. A clearer explanation of this theorem and its implications would be beneficial.

4. The same comment given w.r.t. Theorem 3 applies to Theorem 4. In addition, how different is the upper bound from the lower bound in Theorem 3? Can the gap be unbounded? Again, the proof is hard to follow and requires further clarification.

5. Figure 2 only compares the proposed approach to the same-cluster-query 'lower' bound. However, it would be more informative to include a comparison to the same-cluster-query 'method'. Wondering if there is a crossing point also when compared to the same-cluster-query 'method'. If that is the case, any intuition behind that? Actually, the paper claims that the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster query scheme, which cannot be supported from Figure 2.

6. Algorithm 1 requires the prior knowledge on p or the relative sizes of clusters (in view of Theorem 6). How to obtain such information in practice? A proper justification might validate the practicality of the algorithm. Also, the pseudo-code is hard to follow without relying on the detailed proof of Theorem 5, which is also difficult to grasp.

7. Figures 3, 4, and 5 do not include a comparison to the state of the arts such as the same-cluster-query method, which is crucial to support the claim made in several places: the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme.

In conclusion, while the paper provides some interesting theoretical analyses and develops an algorithm for a clustering problem, it requires significant improvements in presentation, clarity, and comparison to related works to be considered for publication.

Strengths:

* The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the clustering problem under various scenarios.
* It develops a novel algorithm, "AND-queries," that applies to arbitrary k-cluster cases.
* The paper includes a real-data simulation to evaluate the performance of the algorithm.

Weaknesses:

* The presentation and clarity of the paper need significant improvement.
* The comparison to related works is limited and requires further clarification.
* The proofs of some theorems are hard to follow and require further clarification.
* The practicality of the algorithm and the prior knowledge required need further justification.

Recommendations:

* Improve the presentation and clarity of the paper.
* Include a more comprehensive comparison to related works.
* Clarify the proofs of the theorems and provide more intuitive explanations.
* Justify the practicality of the algorithm and the prior knowledge required.
* Include more simulations and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods to support the claims made in the paper.
