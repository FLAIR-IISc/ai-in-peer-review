This is an interesting and well-written paper. I enjoyed reading it and found the theoretical contributions particularly accessible to a general machine learning audience. The authors address the problem of clustering data points using crowdsourcing, specifically focusing on recovering original labels from responses to similarity queries. They provide theoretical bounds on the number of queries needed to achieve this, accounting for both noisy and noiseless responses, and support these bounds with empirical simulations. The connection to Information Theory, used to derive these bounds, is clearly explained.

However, I have several concerns that could strengthen the paper. 
(1) The use of AND queries, while theoretically intriguing, appears to deviate from the typical crowdsourcing paradigm. An AND query seems to reveal the cluster label directly, rather than simply assessing whether data points belong to the same cluster. If this interpretation is correct, it raises the question of why similarity queries are necessary at all; directly querying for the label of a data point would seem more efficient. This is my primary point of confusion regarding the paper’s contributions.
(2) Could the expressions for the bounds presented in Theorems 1, 2, 3, and 4 be simplified without sacrificing their core meaning?
(3) The experimental evaluation appears to focus solely on AND queries. If this is the case, the validity of the experimental study is questionable until the concerns raised in point (1) are addressed.

I am satisfied with the authors’ responses to my questions and therefore recommend accepting this paper.
