This paper presents an interesting connection between semisupervised clustering via crowdsourcing and locally encodable source coding. The authors investigate the query complexity of recovering labels for unlabeled data points, considering both noisy and noiseless oracle responses to pairwise queries. A key contribution is the introduction of AND queries as an alternative to traditional “same cluster” queries, with theoretical benefits in certain scenarios. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the theoretical foundations, making it accessible to a broad machine learning audience. The link to information theory for deriving bounds is particularly well-articulated.

I have several suggestions that could strengthen the paper. 

First, the motivation for AND queries warrants further clarification. While theoretically compelling, the mechanism of an AND query – effectively soliciting a label *as part* of the similarity check – seems to deviate from the typical crowdsourcing paradigm, where the goal is to avoid directly requesting labels and instead infer them from pairwise comparisons. The paper should address whether this is a valid interpretation and, if so, justify why this approach is still valuable within a crowdsourcing context. Specifically, why would one prefer this to directly querying for a label? This is a critical point for understanding the practical relevance of the proposed method.

Second, the mathematical expressions for the bounds presented in Theorems 1, 2, 3, and 4 are quite complex. While rigor is important, exploring potential simplifications—without sacrificing the core insights—could improve readability and facilitate broader understanding of the results. Consider highlighting the key dependencies and providing intuitive interpretations of the parameters.

Third, the experimental evaluation focuses solely on AND queries. Given the central claim that AND queries outperform “same cluster” queries, it's crucial to directly compare their performance in the experiments. Without this comparison, the experimental validation feels incomplete and raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings. Including a baseline comparison with “same cluster” queries, even within the limitations of the crowdsourcing setup, would significantly strengthen the empirical support for the proposed method.

Overall, the paper presents a solid theoretical contribution with a potentially impactful idea. Addressing the concerns above, particularly clarifying the motivation and experimental validation of the AND query approach, would significantly enhance its clarity and impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper establishes a novel and theoretically sound connection between semisupervised clustering and information theory.
*   The analysis provides meaningful lower bounds on query complexity.
*   The introduction of AND queries offers a potentially valuable alternative to traditional querying strategies.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The practical motivation for AND queries, given the context of crowdsourcing, is not fully convincing and requires further explanation.
*   The complexity of the theoretical expressions may limit accessibility.
*   The experimental evaluation is incomplete without a direct comparison to “same cluster” queries.
