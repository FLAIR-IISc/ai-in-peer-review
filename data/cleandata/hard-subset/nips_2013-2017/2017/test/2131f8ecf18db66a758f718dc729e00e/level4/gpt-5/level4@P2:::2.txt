Paper summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies non-adaptive, few-element queries for multiclass labeling (semisupervised clustering) under possible noise. It connects this setting to locally encodable source coding, where each compressed bit depends on only a small number of source bits. Leveraging this equivalence, the paper derives information-theoretic lower bounds on the query complexity for exact and approximate recovery, and argues that standard pairwise “same cluster” (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery. It proposes pairwise AND queries, proving improved tradeoffs in several regimes, and gives an algorithm for the noisy k-class case that achieves O(n log(k/δ)) non-adaptive queries for (1 − δ)-accurate recovery. A small crowdsourcing experiment is used to validate aspects of the theory and to compare error rates of AND vs. same-cluster queries.

This work relates to prior NIPS and information-theoretic literature on query complexity for clustering, crowdsourcing with noisy answers, and coding-theoretic formulations of learning and compression under locality constraints. Notable connections include the “same cluster” query model, rate–distortion theory, locally decodable/encodable codes, and recent work on community recovery in hypergraphs (Ahn–Lee–Suh, Allerton’16), which also studies XOR-like measurements in a non-adaptive regime.

Strengths
- A novel and potentially fruitful equivalence between non-adaptive semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, which yields new converse bounds.
- Clear identification of a limitation of XOR (same-cluster) queries for approximate recovery and a simple alternative (AND) with provable advantages.
- An algorithm for noisy k-class labeling with near-linear query complexity in n and logarithmic dependence on k/δ, plus a concentration-based correctness argument.
- An initial real-data experiment suggesting that AND queries may be more reliable for crowd workers than same-cluster queries.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
1) Prohibition on repeated queries. Disallowing repeated questions is restrictive and may worsen performance under noise, since repetition is a standard tool for error reduction. The paper motivates this choice via independence concerns and anecdotally cites cases where repetition does not help, but the justification remains weak. A stronger case would:
- Provide a thorough empirical comparison against algorithms that allow repetition (e.g., independent annotators with majority vote), including realistic worker error models.
- Alternatively, analyze the repeated-query setting under an independence assumption across annotators (as is common in the crowdsourcing literature), and clarify when repetition helps or provably does not. If the Prelec–Seung–McCoy (Nature 2017) insight is relevant, make explicit what it implies here.

2) Theorem 1 (noiseless, exact recovery) and relation to Ahn–Lee–Suh. The stated sample complexity with XOR-type queries appears inconsistent with [Ahn–Lee–Suh], which obtains m = Θ((n log n)/Δ) for p = 1/2 and constant Δ, whereas Theorem 1 suggests m ≈ n/log 2. Please reconcile the regimes, assumptions (e.g., typical set restriction, decoding model, expurgation), and definitions of success probability. The proof is difficult to follow; a high-level proof sketch, explicit constants, and careful comparison to existing results are needed.

3) Theorem 3 (approximate recovery lower bound). The bound on δ is hard to interpret, and it is not straightforward to invert the expression to obtain m as a function of n and δ. Please:
- Provide a corollary that isolates m/n as an explicit function of p, Δ, and δ (even if approximate/asymptotic).
- Include plots of achievable δ versus m/n for fixed p and Δ, highlighting the improvement over the exact-recovery threshold.

4) Theorem 4 (AND-query achievability). Similar interpretability issues arise. How does this upper bound compare numerically and asymptotically to the lower bound in Theorem 3? Can the gap be unbounded? The proof is also dense; a simplified derivation, intuition (e.g., two-hop neighborhood coverage), and approximations for large n would help.

5) Figure 2 and claims vs. same-cluster methods. The comparison in Figure 2 is against a same-cluster lower bound, not an implementable same-cluster algorithm. To substantiate the claim that AND queries outperform same-cluster schemes, please:
- Compare to concrete same-cluster baselines (e.g., non-adaptive designs plus ML/SDP decoding, or simple practical heuristics) across a range of m and δ.
- Discuss whether there are regimes where XOR methods outperform AND, and provide intuition for any crossings.

6) Algorithm 1 and prior knowledge. The method requires the prior p or accurate cluster-size estimates (Theorem 6). Please clarify:
- How p (or ni) is obtained in practice, including the sampling budget and its effect on total query complexity and accuracy.
- Robustness to misspecification of p, and whether thresholds Ci can be adapted from data.
- Improve the pseudocode so it is self-contained and comprehensible without the full proof (define thresholds, provide parameter guidance, and add complexity/runtime notes).

7) Experimental evaluation (Figures 3–5). The real-data experiment is a useful start, but it lacks comparisons to state-of-the-art same-cluster methods, which are essential to support the central claim. Please add:
- Head-to-head comparisons on identical query budgets with standard same-cluster pipelines (e.g., spectral clustering with normalized cuts, SDP-based methods).
- Sensitivity analyses over p, q, and d, with confidence intervals.
- Clarity on worker assignment (independence across annotators) and quality controls.

Quality
Technically promising with interesting bounds and an algorithmic contribution, but several proofs are hard to parse, some claims appear to conflict with prior work, and empirical validation is limited.

Clarity
The exposition needs significant tightening: clearer statements of regimes and assumptions; intuitive proof sketches; direct, interpretable corollaries; and expanded experimental comparisons.

Originality
The locally encodable source-coding perspective and the AND-query insight are original and interesting.

Significance
Potentially significant if the theoretical claims are clarified and the empirical comparisons substantiate the practical benefits over same-cluster methods.

After rebuttal
- The benefit (or lack thereof) of repeated queries remains unclear; please explicitly state whether existing evidence shows repetition is unhelpful in this setting, and under what models.
- Please incorporate the detailed comparison to same-cluster methods from the response into the paper.

Pros
- Novel theoretical framing and new lower bounds.
- Simple, scalable algorithm with near-linear query complexity.
- Practical insight that AND queries may be easier and more reliable for crowd workers.

Cons
- Inconsistent or unclear comparison with prior work (e.g., Ahn–Lee–Suh).
- Difficult-to-interpret bounds for approximate recovery.
- Limited experimental comparisons to strong same-cluster baselines.
- Reliance on known priors with limited guidance on estimation and robustness.

Overall
The paper has promising ideas but requires substantial clarification of theoretical claims, stronger empirical comparisons, and improved presentation to meet the NIPS standards.
