This paper presents an interesting theoretical analysis of crowdsourced clustering. The authors address the problem of recovering the true labels of data points by asking crowd workers 'same-cluster' queries. The main contribution is the derivation of information-theoretic bounds on the number of queries required to recover the labels, considering both noiseless and noisy settings. These theoretical contributions are supported by empirical simulations. The paper is well-written, and its arguments are presented clearly, making the information-theoretic analysis accessible to a general machine learning audience. In particular, the connection to information theory used to derive the bounds is explained very well.

I have the following concerns and questions, which I hope will help improve the paper:

(1) While the idea of 'AND' queries is theoretically interesting, it seems to conflict with the practical motivation for crowdsourcing. As I understand it, a positive response to an 'AND' query on a set of items not only confirms that they belong to the same cluster but also effectively reveals the cluster's label. If this interpretation is correct, it raises a question about the motivation for this query type. Why would one use such similarity queries instead of directly asking for an item's label? Clarification on this point would be helpful, as this is my primary concern regarding the paper's contributions.

(2) The bounds presented in Theorems 1-4 are central to the paper. Is it possible to simplify these expressions to provide more direct intuition, perhaps without sacrificing their essential theoretical message? A simplified form or a discussion of the asymptotic behavior could make the results more interpretable.

(3) The experiments appear to focus exclusively on 'AND' queries. If this is the case, the relevance of the experimental validation hinges on the clarification requested in point (1) regarding the motivation for this specific query type.

[Post-Rebuttal Update]
The authors have provided a satisfactory response to the questions above. Based on their clarifications and the overall strength of the paper's contributions, I recommend acceptance.
