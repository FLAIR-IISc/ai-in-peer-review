This paper investigates a clustering model in which a learner interacts with an oracle through queries to identify a target clustering. The queries considered are same-cluster queries, generalized to any fixed binary-valued function of the instance labels. The work's central focus is the analysis of this model's query complexity.

The paradigm of query-based clustering is a well-motivated and contemporary area of research, with practical applications in fields such as record de-duplication and crowdsourcing. The authors analyze the problem from an information-theoretic perspective, establishing upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed to either perfectly or approximately recover the ground-truth clustering. Their framework also accommodates the presence of a noisy oracle. The information-theoretic techniques utilized are noteworthy and represent a novel contribution to the machine learning community.

The analysis is somewhat constrained by the lack of structural assumptions on the problem, such as a specified hypothesis class. As a result, the derived bounds are primarily dependent on the marginal distribution of the labels, alongside the noise level. This positions the setting somewhat apart from those conventionally explored in learning theory. Nevertheless, this work serves as an important initial step, contributing to a deeper understanding of the information-theoretic aspects of query-based clustering, particularly concerning its fundamental lower bounds.
