This paper explores a clustering problem that aims to reconstruct the true labels of elements from possibly noisy and non-adaptive query-answers, each associating only a few elements. The authors derive lower bounds on the number of queries required to reconstruct the ground-truth labels exactly or partially in various scenarios. Additionally, they propose a so-called "AND-queries" algorithm that applies to the arbitrary k-cluster case and evaluate its performance using a real data set.

While the paper attempts to provide theoretical analyses for various scenarios and develops an algorithm along with real-data simulations, it has limitations in presentation, clarity of the claimed results, and comparison to related works. Significant changes and possibly new developments are needed for publication. See below for detailed comments.

1. The constraint of not repeating the same questions seems overly restrictive. This limitation may degrade the performance compared to the no-restriction case, as allowing the same questions is a natural way to combat the noise effect. To justify this restricted model, the authors could provide an extensive set of real-data simulations comparing their algorithm to one tailored for the no-restriction case, potentially showing better performance of the proposed algorithm. If not, investigating the no-restriction case might be more plausible. The authors seem concerned about the independence-vs-dependence issue for multiple answers regarding the same query, which could make analysis challenging. However, this issue can be resolved by focusing on a setting where the same query is given to distinct annotators, assuming the answers from the annotators are independent.

2. The noiseless setting with exact recovery was also studied in a recent work [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which considers the XOR-operation ('same-cluster' queries) under a non-adaptive measurement setting. However, the result in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] does not coincide with the one claimed in Theorem 1. For instance, when p=1/2 and Δ is a constant, the query complexity in Theorem 1 reads m=n/log2, while the one in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] reads m=nlogn/Δ. This discrepancy needs clarification. Furthermore, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow.

3. The expression of the lower bound on δ in Theorem 3 is hard to interpret. Specifically, it is challenging to infer the bound on m in terms of n and δ, which would directly show the performance improvement due to the relaxed constraint reflected in δ.

4. The same comment applies to Theorem 4. Additionally, how different is the upper bound from the lower bound in Theorem 3? Can the gap be unbounded? Again, the proof is hard to follow.

5. Figure 2 lacks a comparison to the same-cluster query method; there is only a comparison to the same-cluster-query 'lower' bound. It would be interesting to see if there is a crossing point when compared to the same-cluster-query 'method'. If so, what is the intuition behind it? The paper claims that the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster query scheme, which cannot be supported by Figure 2.

6. The proposed algorithm (Algorithm 1) requires prior knowledge of p or the relative sizes of clusters (in view of Theorem 6). It is unclear how to obtain such information in practice. A proper justification might validate the practicality of the algorithm. Also, the pseudo-code is difficult to follow without relying on the detailed proof of Theorem 5, which is also challenging to grasp.

7. Figures 3, 4, and 5 lack a comparison to state-of-the-art methods, such as the same-cluster-query method, which is crucial to support the claim made in several places: the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme.

[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
