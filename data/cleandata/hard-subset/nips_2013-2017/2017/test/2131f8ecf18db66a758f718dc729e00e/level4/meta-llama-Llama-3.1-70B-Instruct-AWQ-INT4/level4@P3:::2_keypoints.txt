- Studies reconstruction of true labels from noisy, non-adaptive few-element queries and derives query complexity bounds for exact and partial recovery.  
- Frames the problem as a locally encodable source coding task, connecting to budgeted crowdsourcing and information-theory literature.  
- Main technical contribution: new information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries for exact and approximate label recovery.  
- Proposes an AND-queries algorithm for arbitrary k-cluster settings and evaluates it on real data.  
- Claims AND-queries can outperform binary same-cluster queries in many regimes.  
- Strength: solid theoretical analysis and novel lower bounds advancing clustering/information-theory connections.  
- Strength: AND-queries algorithm generalizes to k clusters and shows promising empirical performance.  
- Presentation and clarity are lacking in places, making some claimed results hard to follow.  
- The "no repeated question" constraint is restrictive and needs justification and comparison to the unrestricted setting.  
- Proof of Theorem 1 is hard to follow and appears inconsistent with recent work by Ahn-Lee-Suh; authors should clarify the discrepancy.  
- Theorem 3's lower-bound expression for δ is opaque and makes it difficult to extract m as a function of n and δ.  
- Empirical claim that AND-queries outperform same-cluster queries is not supported by Figure 2; a direct comparison and more intuition are needed.  
- Algorithm requires prior knowledge of p or cluster size ratios; authors should justify how this is obtained in practice.  
- Pseudocode is unclear and depends heavily on the detailed proof of Theorem 5, reducing reproducibility.  
- Experimental evaluation should include comparisons to state-of-the-art methods (e.g., same-cluster queries) to substantiate performance claims.  
- Recommendation summary: significant theoretical contributions but needs clearer presentation, stronger proofs, and improved empirical comparisons before acceptance.  
- Review score: 6/10, reviewer confidence: 3/5.
