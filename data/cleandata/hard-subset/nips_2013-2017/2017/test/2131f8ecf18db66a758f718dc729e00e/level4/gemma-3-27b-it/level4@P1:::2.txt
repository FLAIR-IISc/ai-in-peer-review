This paper explores a clustering problem where the goal is to reconstruct the true labels of elements from potentially noisy, non-adaptive query answers, each involving only a few elements. It derives lower bounds on the number of queries required to reconstruct the ground-truth labels exactly or partially in a variety of scenarios. It also proposes a so-called "AND-queries" algorithm applicable to arbitrary k-cluster cases and evaluates its performance using a real dataset.

While the paper attempts to provide theoretical analyses for various scenarios and develops an algorithm with a real-data simulation, it suffers from limitations in presentation, clarity of results, and comparison to related works. Significant revisions and potentially further development are needed for publication. See below for detailed comments.

1. **(Restriction on Repeating Queries):** This constraint seems restrictive. Relaxing it could potentially improve performance by mitigating the noise effect. A strong justification for this restriction is needed, ideally with extensive real-data simulations comparing the proposed algorithm to one tailored for a setting where repeating queries *is* allowed, demonstrating superior performance. The authors’ concern about independence vs. dependence of multiple answers to the same query can be addressed by considering distinct annotators for each query, assuming independence between their answers.

2. **(Theorem 1):** The noiseless setting with exact recovery has been previously studied in [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which also considers XOR-operation ('same-cluster' queries) under a non-adaptive measurement setting. However, the result in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] does not coincide with that claimed in Theorem 1. For instance, when p=1/2 and Δ is constant, Theorem 1 yields a query complexity of m=n/log2, while [Ahn-Lee-Suh] gives m=nlog(n)/Δ. This discrepancy needs clarification. Furthermore, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow.

3. **(Theorem 3):** The expression for the lower bound on δ is difficult to interpret. It is challenging to infer the bound on *m* in terms of *n* and δ, which would allow a direct assessment of the performance improvement due to the relaxed constraint.

4. **(Theorem 4):** The same comment applies to Theorem 3 regarding difficulty of interpretation. Additionally, what is the relationship between the upper bound in Theorem 4 and the lower bound in Theorem 3? Can the gap between them be unbounded? The proof is also challenging to follow.

5. **(Figure 2):** The figure lacks a comparison to the standard ‘same cluster’ query *method* – it only shows a comparison to the same-cluster-query *lower bound*. Is there a crossover point when comparing the proposed approach to the ‘same cluster’ query method? The paper claims that the proposed approach outperforms the ‘same cluster’ query scheme, but this is not demonstrably supported by Figure 2.

6. **(Algorithm 1):** The proposed algorithm requires prior knowledge of *p* or the relative sizes of clusters (as highlighted in Theorem 6). How can this information be obtained in practice? A robust justification of the practicality of the algorithm is necessary. The pseudo-code is difficult to follow without a deep understanding of the detailed proof of Theorem 5, which is itself complex.

7. **(Figures 3, 4 & 5):** There is no comparison to state-of-the-art methods like the ‘same-cluster’ query method, which is crucial to substantiate the claims made throughout the paper – specifically, that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the ‘same-cluster’ query scheme.

[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," *Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing*, 2016.
