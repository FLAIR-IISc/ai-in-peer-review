The authors study a clustering model where the learner is allowed to ask queries from an oracle and the goal is to find the target clustering. The queries can be same-cluster queries [or more generally, a fixed binary-valued function of the label of the input instances]. The authors study the query complexity of such models.

The query-based clustering model is a well-motivated and relatively recent research area, with applications in record-deduplication, crowdsourcing, etc. The authors study the problem from the information theoretic perspective. In particular, they provide upper and lower bounds on the number of required queries to perfectly (or approximately) recover the target clustering. They also consider the case that the oracle is noisy. The information-theoretic tools that they use are interesting and new to ML community.

The main contributions of the paper are as follows:
- The authors show that a recently popular model of semisupervised clustering is equivalent to locally encodable source coding. This equivalence leads to new information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required in various scenarios.
- They propose pairwise AND queries, which provably perform better than same-cluster queries in many situations.
- The authors provide new information-theoretic results on fundamental limits of locally encodable source coding.
- They also perform a real crowdsourcing experiment to validate their algorithm.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem and the related work. The technical contributions are clearly explained, and the proofs are well-organized and easy to follow.

The paper has several strengths:
- The authors study a well-motivated problem with many applications.

- The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
