

This paper establishes a novel connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, offering a fresh information-theoretic perspective on query-based clustering problems. The authors consider a model where an oracle provides binary answers to queries about element labels, with each query involving at most Δ elements (typically Δ = 2 for pairwise queries). The central contribution is recognizing that this clustering setup is equivalent to locally encodable source coding, where each compressed bit depends on only a constant number of input bits. This equivalence allows them to derive tight information-theoretic bounds on query complexity for both exact and approximate recovery, and to propose a superior query strategy using AND operations instead of the commonly used "same cluster" (XOR) queries.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, for noiseless exact recovery with two clusters, they establish a new lower bound that improves upon the naive nH(p) bound for certain label distributions. Second, for approximate recovery, they prove that "same cluster" queries are fundamentally limited and demonstrate that AND queries substantially outperform them, requiring exponentially fewer queries to achieve the same recovery accuracy. Third, for noisy queries with k ≥ 2 clusters, they develop an efficient algorithm using AND queries that achieves (1-δ)-good recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries, validated through a real crowdsourcing experiment. The theoretical results are supported by a thorough analysis of the fundamental limits of locally encodable source coding.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The information-theoretic approach is both novel and well-executed, providing rigorous lower bounds that advance our understanding of query complexity in clustering. The connection to source coding is particularly insightful and has not been previously recognized in the ML literature. The theoretical results are complemented by a carefully designed real-world experiment that validates the AND query approach in practice, showing a 55% reduction in errors compared to "same cluster" queries. The paper is technically sound, with clear theorems, proofs, and empirical validation. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the assumption of independent errors across queries, and they appropriately contextualize their work within related literature.

The paper's main weakness lies in its somewhat narrow scope. While the authors extend their results to k ≥ 2 clusters in the noisy setting, the core theoretical contributions focus primarily on the binary case (k=2). This limitation is understandable given the complexity of the analysis, but it somewhat restricts the immediate applicability of the results to multi-class clustering problems, which are common in practice. Additionally, the paper assumes knowledge of the prior label distribution p, which might not always be available in real-world applications. However, the authors do address this by showing that the algorithm can work with just relative cluster sizes, which is a reasonable practical assumption.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a significant contribution by establishing the connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding. While locally decodable codes have been studied in information theory, this is the first work to apply this concept to clustering query complexity. The authors thoroughly compare their approach with prior work on query-based clustering, including the recent NIPS 2017 paper "Clustering with same-cluster queries" by Ashtiani et al., demonstrating clear theoretical and practical advantages of their AND query approach.

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides fundamental insights into the information-theoretic limits of query-based clustering, which is a critical problem in crowdsourcing and entity resolution. The practical implications are clear: by using AND queries instead of "same cluster" queries, practitioners can significantly reduce the number of queries needed to achieve high-quality clustering results. The 55% error reduction demonstrated in their real experiment is particularly compelling. The paper advances the state of the art by providing both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation of a superior querying strategy.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, though some of the technical details in the proofs could benefit from additional intuition. The authors effectively use figures to illustrate their theoretical bounds and experimental results, making the key insights accessible to readers. The connection to source coding is elegantly explained, and the paper makes a strong case for why this perspective is valuable.

In summary, this paper makes an important theoretical contribution by establishing a novel connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, with clear practical implications for query-based clustering applications. The authors provide tight information-theoretic bounds, a superior query strategy using AND operations, and empirical validation that demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods. While the focus on the binary case and the need for prior knowledge of label distributions are minor limitations, these are well-acknowledged and the paper's contributions are substantial. I believe this work would be a valuable addition to NIPS 2017, advancing both theoretical understanding and practical approaches to query-based clustering.
