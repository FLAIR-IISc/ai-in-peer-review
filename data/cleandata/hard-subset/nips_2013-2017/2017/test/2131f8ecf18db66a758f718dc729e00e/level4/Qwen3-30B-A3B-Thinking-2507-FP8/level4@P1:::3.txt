

This paper establishes a novel connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, providing theoretical bounds on query complexity for clustering via crowdsourcing. The authors consider recovering cluster labels for n elements using binary queries with at most ∆ elements per query (a small constant), demonstrating that pairwise "same cluster" queries (equivalent to XOR) have fundamental limitations for approximate recovery. They propose AND queries as a more efficient alternative, providing tight lower bounds for exact recovery, improved bounds for approximate recovery, and an algorithm for noisy queries with theoretical guarantees. The work builds on recent NIPS contributions in clustering with side information [4, 21, 33] and connects to information theory literature on locally encodable source coding [19, 23].

The paper's strengths are substantial. First, the theoretical contributions are significant and well-motivated. The connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding is genuinely novel, offering fresh insights into query complexity. The authors provide tight lower bounds (Theorems 1 and 2) that improve upon prior work for specific parameter regimes, and they rigorously demonstrate that AND queries outperform "same cluster" queries for approximate recovery (Theorems 3 and 4). The noisy query analysis (Theorem 5) is particularly valuable for practical applications. Second, the paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of theoretical contributions accessible to a machine learning audience. The information-theoretic perspective is especially well-explained. Third, the empirical validation in Section 4 provides compelling evidence for the practical relevance of their theoretical claims, showing that AND queries yield lower error rates than "same cluster" queries in a real crowdsourcing experiment.

The primary concern relates to the practicality of AND queries. The authors claim that "for a correct 'no' answer in an AND query, a worker just needs to know one of the labels in the pair," but this seems to contradict the fundamental motivation for crowdsourcing, where workers should not have prior knowledge of the cluster labels. If workers need to know the label space to answer AND queries (e.g., "Are both movies action movies?"), this fundamentally changes the problem setup. Why would one need to ask similarity queries at all if workers could simply answer direct label queries? The paper should clarify how workers would realistically answer AND queries without prior knowledge of the label space. This is a critical point that needs explicit discussion to establish the practical relevance of the approach.

The paper could be strengthened by simplifying the expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1-4 while preserving technical precision. While the current expressions are mathematically sound, they could be made more accessible without losing rigor. The authors might provide intuitive interpretations of the bounds for specific parameter values or include visualizations to help readers grasp the trade-offs.

The experimental section is well-executed but requires clarification regarding the AND query feasibility. The authors report that AND queries yielded 105 erroneous responses compared to 234 for "same cluster" queries. However, this comparison is only meaningful if AND queries are actually feasible for crowd workers. The paper should explicitly address how workers would answer AND queries without prior knowledge of the cluster labels, as this is essential for validating the practical value of their approach.

Assessing the four criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The authors carefully discuss both strengths and limitations of their approach.
- Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear explanations of complex theoretical concepts. The connection to information theory is particularly well-articulated.
- Originality: The connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding is genuinely novel, and the AND query approach represents a significant conceptual advance over existing work on pairwise queries.
- Significance: The results have clear implications for practical crowdsourcing applications, and the theoretical advances contribute meaningfully to our understanding of query complexity in clustering.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions that are well-motivated and well-explained. The main concern regarding the practicality of AND queries should be addressed to strengthen the paper's impact. With this clarification, the paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the practicality concern about AND queries.
