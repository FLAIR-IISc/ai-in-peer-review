This paper frames the problem of semi-supervised clustering from non-adaptive queries as one of locally encodable source coding. This information-theoretic perspective is used to derive lower bounds on the query complexity required for both exact and approximate recovery of ground-truth labels under various settings, including noiseless and noisy oracle responses. A key contribution is the proposal of "AND-queries" as an alternative to standard "same-cluster" queries, which the authors argue is more efficient, particularly for approximate recovery. The paper supports its theoretical findings with an algorithm for the multi-cluster case and validates it through experiments on a real crowdsourced dataset.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality:** The connection drawn between clustering with local queries and the information-theoretic concept of locally encodable source coding is novel and provides a valuable new perspective on the problem.
*   **Significance:** The paper tackles the important and practical challenge of minimizing query costs in crowdsourcing and active learning settings. The introduction of AND-queries as a potentially more robust and efficient alternative to XOR-based "same-cluster" queries is an interesting and promising idea.
*   **Scope:** The work is comprehensive, addressing multiple scenarios: exact vs. approximate recovery, noiseless vs. noisy oracles, and binary vs. multi-class clustering.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**
The paper presents several interesting ideas, but its claims are weakened by issues related to clarity, comparison with prior work, and experimental validation.

1.  **Clarity of Theoretical Results:** Several of the main theorems are presented in a way that is difficult to interpret, hindering an assessment of their impact.
    *   **Theorem 1:** The proof is condensed and challenging to follow. Furthermore, the claimed query complexity (m=n/log2 for p=1/2) appears to conflict with results in related work [Ahn et al., 2016], which finds a complexity of m=nlogn/Δ. This discrepancy needs to be explicitly addressed and clarified.
    *   **Theorems 3 & 4:** The expressions for the lower bound on error (δ) in Theorem 3 and the achievable error in Theorem 4 are complex. It is difficult to extract an intuitive understanding of the relationship between query budget (`m`) and achievable error (`δ`), which is crucial for appreciating the results. A discussion of the gap between this lower bound and the achievable upper bound would also be valuable.

2.  **Problem Formulation:** The assumption that a single query cannot be repeated seems restrictive. In practical crowdsourcing, asking the same question to multiple workers is a standard technique for noise reduction. While the authors allude to issues with answer independence, a more robust justification for this constraint is needed. For instance, the analysis could focus on a model where queries are repeated but posed to distinct, independent workers.

3.  **Insufficient Comparisons:** The paper's central claim—that AND-queries outperform same-cluster queries—is not adequately supported by the provided comparisons.
    *   **Figure 2:** The performance of the AND-query scheme is compared against a *lower bound* for same-cluster queries, not against the performance of a competitive *algorithm* using same-cluster queries. To make a compelling case, a direct comparison against an achievable scheme is necessary.
    *   **Figures 3, 4, & 5:** The experimental results demonstrate the performance of Algorithm 1 in isolation but lack a baseline comparison against state-of-the-art methods, including those based on same-cluster queries. This makes it difficult to gauge the practical significance of the proposed algorithm.

4.  **Practicality and Algorithm Clarity:**
    *   **Algorithm 1:** The proposed algorithm relies on prior knowledge of cluster sizes (`p`). The paper would be stronger if it included a more detailed discussion of how this prior information can be accurately estimated in real-world scenarios.
    *   The pseudocode for Algorithm 1 is not self-contained and is difficult to understand without referring to the proof of Theorem 5, which itself is challenging to parse.

In summary, while the paper introduces a novel theoretical framework and a promising new query mechanism, significant revisions are needed to improve the clarity of the results, strengthen the justification for its modeling choices, and provide more rigorous and convincing comparisons to existing work.
