This is an interesting and well-written paper that establishes a connection between semisupervised clustering via crowdsourcing and locally encodable source coding. The authors derive theoretical bounds on the number of queries required to recover labels, considering both noiseless and noisy query responses. The paper is clearly organized and explains the information-theoretic aspects accessibly to a general machine learning audience. The connection to locally encodable codes is a novel and valuable perspective.

However, the core contribution – the introduction of AND queries – requires significant clarification. The initial motivation for crowdsourcing is to elicit simple pairwise similarity judgments (“do these belong to the same cluster?”). The AND query, as presented, appears to request more information – effectively a conjunction of the labels themselves – which seems to deviate from the original crowdsourcing paradigm. The paper doesn’t adequately justify *why* a worker would be more willing to answer an AND query (requiring knowledge of both labels) than a standard ‘same cluster’ query, or why it aligns with realistic crowdsourcing constraints.  The authors briefly touch on this in the experiments and related work, but a more thorough discussion is needed.  Specifically, the paper needs to address why asking for a combined label (via AND) is preferable to directly asking for individual labels, a point that fundamentally questions the premise of this approach.

Regarding the theoretical results, while rigorous, the expressions for the bounds (Theorems 1, 2, 3, and 4) are quite complex. While complete simplification might not be possible, exploring whether the bounds can be expressed in a more intuitive and easily interpretable form would significantly enhance the paper's impact.  Providing some illustrative examples demonstrating the practical implications of these bounds would also be beneficial.

The experimental validation is somewhat limited. The authors primarily focus on AND queries, and the justification for excluding a comparison with standard 'same cluster' queries (beyond the theoretical advantages presented) is not entirely convincing, particularly given the initial question raised above.  A more comprehensive experimental evaluation, including a comparison of the error rates for both query types under realistic crowdsourcing conditions, would strengthen the empirical support for the proposed approach. The connection between the real crowdsourcing experiment and the specific theoretical results (e.g., the scaling of queries with δ) could also be made more explicit. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Establishing the link between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding provides a fresh perspective on the problem.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The derived bounds are mathematically sound and represent a significant theoretical contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and explains complex concepts in an accessible manner.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Inclusion of real-world experiments, even if limited, provides some support for the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Motivation for AND Queries:** The rationale for introducing AND queries in a crowdsourcing context is not fully convincing and requires further justification.
*   **Complexity of Bounds:** The theoretical bounds are complex and could benefit from simplification or more intuitive interpretation.
*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental setup focuses primarily on AND queries and lacks a direct comparison with standard approaches.
*   **Lack of discussion about computational cost:** The computational cost associated with the algorithm is not discussed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to semisupervised clustering.
*   The connection to locally encodable source coding is insightful and may open new avenues for research.
*   The authors address a relevant problem in machine learning and crowdsourcing.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The core contribution – AND queries – lacks a strong practical justification and may not align with the typical constraints of crowdsourcing.
*   The complexity of the theoretical results might limit their practical applicability.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited and does not fully validate the proposed approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a significant theoretical contribution. However, the practical relevance of the proposed AND queries is questionable and needs to be addressed more convincingly.  I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors: (1) providing a clearer and more compelling justification for the use of AND queries in a crowdsourcing setting, addressing the concerns about its alignment with typical crowdsourcing constraints; (2) exploring potential simplifications of the theoretical bounds; and (3) including a more comprehensive experimental evaluation that compares AND queries with standard ‘same cluster’ queries.
