

The authors investigate a query-based clustering model wherein a learner interacts with an oracle via same-cluster queries (or more generally, a fixed binary-valued function of the labels of input instances) to identify the target clustering. They analyze the query complexity required for exact (or approximate) recovery of the clustering, adopting an information-theoretic perspective. The work provides both upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed, extends the analysis to settings with a noisy oracle, and employs information-theoretic tools that are novel within the machine learning community.  

While the query-based clustering model is well-motivated and relevant to applications such as record deduplication and crowdsourcing, the analysis is constrained by the absence of structural assumptions about the problem (e.g., no hypothesis class or underlying data structure is specified). Consequently, the bounds depend solely on the marginal distribution of the labels (and the noise level), limiting the model's applicability to standard learning-theoretic frameworks. Nevertheless, this work represents a valuable first step toward understanding the information-theoretic foundations of query-based clustering, particularly in establishing lower bounds for such problems.
