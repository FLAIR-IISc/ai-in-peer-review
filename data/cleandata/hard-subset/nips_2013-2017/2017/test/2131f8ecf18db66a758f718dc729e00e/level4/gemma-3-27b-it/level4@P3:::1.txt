This paper investigates the query complexity of a semisupervised clustering problem, framing it as a locally encodable source coding problem. The authors establish connections between querying oracles for cluster assignments and fundamental limits in information theory, providing both upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed for accurate label recovery, even in the presence of noisy oracle responses. The work is motivated by recent applications of crowdsourcing for entity resolution and clustering.

This paper tackles a compelling and increasingly relevant problem – efficient clustering with limited access to ground truth via queries. The connection to locally encodable source coding is a novel and insightful perspective, leveraging tools from information theory to derive meaningful lower bounds. The exploration of both noiseless and noisy oracle scenarios is a strength, as is the consideration of AND queries as a potentially superior alternative to traditional ‘same cluster’ queries. The experimental validation on real datasets, while limited, adds credibility to the theoretical findings. The paper is well-aware of related work in crowdsourcing, entity resolution, and information theory, appropriately citing key papers in these areas (e.g., [4, 8, 14, 23]). 

However, the paper suffers from some clarity issues and a lack of detailed explanation in certain sections. The introduction, while broad, could be more focused, immediately highlighting the key contributions and their significance. The notation is sometimes dense and could benefit from more intuitive definitions. The proofs, while likely correct, are often presented with limited intuition, making it difficult to grasp the core ideas without significant effort. For example, the justification for choosing a d-regular graph in the noisy query setting could be elaborated upon. Furthermore, the discussion of the experimental setup feels somewhat cursory; more details regarding the data, the crowdworkers, and the specific implementation would be valuable. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is a unique and valuable contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides formal lower bounds on query complexity, grounded in information theory.
* **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is motivated by real-world applications in crowdsourcing and entity resolution.
* **Exploration of Alternatives:** Investigating AND queries as an alternative to ‘same cluster’ queries demonstrates a proactive approach to problem-solving.
* **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of experiments, even if limited, strengthens the claims.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity:** The writing is sometimes dense and lacks intuitive explanations, particularly in the proofs.
* **Detailed Explanation:** Several aspects, such as the choice of graph structure in the noisy setting, require more thorough justification.
* **Experimental Detail:** The experimental section lacks sufficient detail regarding the data, participants, and implementation.
* **Limited Scope Discussion:** While acknowledged, a more in-depth discussion of the assumptions made (e.g., known prior distribution) and their limitations would be beneficial.
* **Connections to Learning Theory:** The paper briefly acknowledges the difference from typical learning theory settings but could expand on how these results relate or contrast with existing learning guarantees.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a relevant problem.
* The connection to information theory provides valuable insights and lower bounds.
* The exploration of alternative query strategies is promising.
* The experimental results offer preliminary validation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The clarity of the presentation could be significantly improved.
* The lack of detailed explanation in certain sections hinders understanding.
* The experimental validation is limited in scope.
* The strong assumptions made about the problem setting should be more thoroughly discussed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to the field of query-based clustering. While the clarity and detail could be improved, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor, and the potential practical impact make it a worthwhile contribution. I recommend **acceptance**, contingent upon the authors addressing the clarity and explanation issues raised in the review. Specifically, I suggest adding more intuitive explanations to the proofs, elaborating on key design choices, and providing more detailed information about the experimental setup.
