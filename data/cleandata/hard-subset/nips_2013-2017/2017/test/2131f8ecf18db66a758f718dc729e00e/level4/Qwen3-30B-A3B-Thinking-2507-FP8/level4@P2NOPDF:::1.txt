

The paper investigates query-based clustering, where a learner identifies a target clustering by querying an oracle with same-cluster (or more generally, binary-valued function) queries. The authors analyze the information-theoretic query complexity for exact or approximate recovery, including scenarios with noisy oracles. Their work addresses a well-motivated problem with applications in record deduplication and crowdsourcing, offering novel insights through information-theoretic bounds that are particularly valuable to the machine learning community.

The analysis is rigorous and presents tight upper and lower bounds, with the label distribution (beyond noise level) as the primary parameter governing query complexity. While the absence of structural assumptions (e.g., hypothesis classes) is a deliberate choice to isolate information-theoretic limits, it also restricts applicability to standard learning-theoretic frameworks. Consequently, the model operates somewhat removed from typical clustering settings, where structural constraints often drive efficient algorithms. Nevertheless, this foundational work provides a crucial theoretical baseline for understanding the inherent information requirements of query-based clustering, especially regarding lower-bound techniques. The authors' novel use of information-theoretic tools represents a significant contribution, even if the setting remains abstract. This paper serves as an important first step toward characterizing the fundamental limits of interactive clustering, offering a clear pathway for future work that may incorporate structural assumptions while building on these theoretical bounds.
