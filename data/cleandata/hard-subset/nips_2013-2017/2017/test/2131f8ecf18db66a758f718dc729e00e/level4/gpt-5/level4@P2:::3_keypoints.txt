- Paper studies semi-supervised clustering with nonadaptive Δ-local queries (binary or k-ary) and links the problem to locally encodable source coding.  
- Noiseless exact recovery (k=2): XOR (“same-cluster”) queries can recover labels with < n queries for many priors p; authors give improved information-theoretic lower bounds beyond nH(p) in some regimes.  
- Noiseless approximate recovery: new lower bounds for recovering (1−δ) fraction of labels; XOR queries are inherently suboptimal for approximate recovery.  
- AND-based pairwise queries are proposed and shown to yield a better query–accuracy trade-off for approximate recovery.  
- Noisy approximate recovery (k≥2): using k-ary AND-style queries and a simple threshold decoder on a d-regular design, they recover (1−δ) fraction with m = O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive queries under per-query noise.  
- Small crowdsourcing experiment on movie genres shows AND queries have lower human error than same-cluster queries, consistent with theory.  
- Novelty: introduces a locally encodable source-coding perspective to clustering-with-queries, yielding sharper lower bounds and new algorithmic insights.  
- Strength: clear, well-organized exposition accessible to a general ML audience; information-theoretic framing is well motivated.  
- Strength: technical contributions include new lower bounds, demonstration of XOR limitations, AND-based schemes, and noisy k-class extension with near-optimal complexity.  
- Strength: empirical evidence (limited) supports human-factors claim favoring AND queries.  
- Concern: Practicality of AND queries — they require label-specific predicates, raising the question why not ask per-item labels; authors partially addressed this but should clarify scenarios where AND is preferable.  
- Concern: Theorem statements (1–4) use heavy notation; recommend simpler corollaries or asymptotic forms for common p, Δ, δ to improve readability.  
- Concern: Experiments are small-scale; should add baselines (per-item labeling under same budget/error), sensitivity analyses (q, class imbalance), and larger-scale validation.  
- Quality: Theoretical results appear sound with appropriate proofs and standard concentration arguments; assumptions (independent noise, nonadaptive design) are clearly stated.  
- Clarity: Generally well written; main theorem statements could be streamlined and key takeaways (e.g., m = O(n log(k/δ))) highlighted in the main text.  
- Significance: Advances fundamental understanding of query complexity in crowdsourced clustering and has likely broader interest; results should influence practical query scheme design.  
- Recommendation: Accept — paper makes strong, novel theoretical contributions and authors addressed main concerns.
