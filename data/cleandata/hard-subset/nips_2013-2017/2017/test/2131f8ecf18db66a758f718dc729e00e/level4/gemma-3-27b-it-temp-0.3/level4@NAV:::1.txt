This paper investigates a clustering model where a learner actively queries an oracle to identify the underlying cluster structure. The oracle responds to queries about whether instances belong to the same cluster – or, more generally, evaluates a binary function of instance labels. The central focus is characterizing the query complexity required to accurately recover the target clustering, both in perfect and approximate settings, and under conditions of oracle noise.

The query-based clustering framework is a promising and emerging area with practical relevance to problems like record deduplication and crowdsourcing. This work adopts an information-theoretic approach to analyze the problem, establishing both upper and lower bounds on query requirements. The authors employ novel information-theoretic techniques, which represent a valuable contribution to the machine learning literature. 

The analysis is constrained by the lack of structural assumptions about the problem – notably, the absence of a predefined hypothesis class. Consequently, the bounds primarily depend on the marginal distribution of labels and the level of noise. This places the work somewhat outside the scope of typical learning theory investigations. Nevertheless, this represents an important initial step towards understanding the fundamental information-theoretic limits of query-based clustering, particularly concerning the derived lower bounds.
