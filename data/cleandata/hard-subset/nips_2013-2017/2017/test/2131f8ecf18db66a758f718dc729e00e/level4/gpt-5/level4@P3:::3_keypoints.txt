- Establishes an equivalence between nonadaptive local-query clustering and locally encodable source coding, enabling new information-theoretic analyses.  
- Derives refined lower bounds under query-locality and proposes near-matching nonadaptive schemes.  
- For k=2, shows exact recovery with noiseless pairwise XOR queries can use < n queries for certain priors and improves on the classical nh(p) lower bound.  
- Proves XOR-based schemes are intrinsically limited for approximate recovery and connects this to linear rate–distortion code limits.  
- Introduces pairwise AND queries that yield strictly better query–accuracy tradeoffs for approximate recovery.  
- In the noisy, k≥2 setting, gives a simple thresholding algorithm on d-regular graphs that recovers (1−δ) labels with O(n log(k/δ)) AND-type queries.  
- Presents a small crowdsourcing experiment showing AND queries produce fewer annotation errors than same-cluster (XOR) queries.  
- Builds on prior work in same-cluster queries, noisy oracles, crowdsourcing, and locally decodable/smooth compression, with the locally encodable viewpoint as a novel contribution.  
- Technical contributions and proofs (expurgation, rate–distortion, Chernoff analyses) are solid and well-argued.  
- Theoretical claims are clearly stated with supporting proofs or sketches; empirical evidence aligns with theory though limited in scope.  
- Paper is well written and accessible; the information-theoretic mapping and XOR vs AND roles are clearly explained.  
- Novelty: locally encodable coding perspective and the demonstration that AND queries can outperform XOR are insightful and nontrivial.  
- Significance: advances fundamental limits for query-efficient clustering and gives practical guidance for crowdsourcing query design.  
- Practicality concern: authors justify AND queries as simple pairwise predicates that can be cognitively easier and more query-efficient than direct labels.  
- Suggest simplifying theorem statements with leading-order asymptotics and adding numerical instantiations/plots for intuition.  
- Recommend expanding experiments (more datasets, noise regimes, baselines) and analyzing robustness to prior misspecification and heterogeneous worker noise.  
- Pros: novel theoretical viewpoint, clear XOR/AND separation, scalable noisy algorithm, and practical crowdsourcing insights.  
- Cons: limited experimental scale/variety, some bounds could use cleaner summaries, and reliance on known priors.  
- Recommendation: Accept — technically sound, novel, and likely to influence future work.
