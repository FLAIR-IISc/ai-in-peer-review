This paper studies clustering with interactive oracle queries, aiming to recover the target partition. The allowed queries include same-cluster questions and, more generally, evaluations of a fixed binary function of the instance labels. The focus is on the query complexity required to achieve recovery.

The topic is well motivated, with applications in record deduplication and crowdsourcing. The authors take an information-theoretic perspective, deriving upper and lower bounds on the number of oracle queries needed for exact or approximate recovery, and analyzing the setting with a noisy oracle. The information-theoretic tools employed appear novel to the ML community and are of independent interest.

A key limitation is the absence of structural assumptions (e.g., a hypothesis class or a generative model), which constrains the scope and practical interpretability of the results. The bounds depend mainly on the marginal label distribution (and the noise level), placing the setting somewhat outside standard learning-theoretic frameworks. Nevertheless, as an initial step, the work helps clarify fundamental information-theoretic limits of query-based clustering—particularly the lower bounds—and can guide future research that incorporates richer structural assumptions.
