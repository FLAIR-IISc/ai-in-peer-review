This paper presents a compelling investigation into crowdsourced clustering. The central task is to recover the ground-truth labels for a set of data points by utilizing responses to same-cluster queries. The primary contribution is theoretical, providing bounds on the query complexity needed for label recovery under both noisy and noiseless settings, which is validated by empirical simulations. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and accessible to a broad machine learning audience. The authors are to be commended for the clarity with which they explain the derivation of the bounds, particularly the elegant connection to information-theoretic principles.

To further strengthen the paper, I would like to raise the following points:

(1) The concept of an "AND query" presents a potential conceptual issue. While theoretically sound, it appears to contradict the core motivation of crowdsourcing for clustering. My understanding is that an AND query response effectively reveals the cluster's label, not just the co-membership of the queried points. If this interpretation is correct, it raises the question of why similarity queries are necessary at all, as one could presumably query for an item's label directly. This point is my main source of confusion regarding the paper's framework.

(2) Could the mathematical expressions for the bounds presented in Theorems 1-4 be simplified or presented in a more intuitive form? While technically correct, a more streamlined representation might enhance the accessibility of these key results.

(3) The experiments appear to focus exclusively on the AND query setting. Consequently, the validity of the empirical study is contingent on a satisfactory resolution to the first point raised above.

Following the author's response, my concerns have been fully addressed. I am pleased to recommend this paper for acceptance.
