This is an interesting paper, and I enjoyed reading it. The authors consider the problem of clustering data points using crowdsourcing, specifically focusing on recovering original labels based on responses to similarity queries. They propose bounds on the number of queries required to recover these labels, accounting for both noisy and noiseless answers. The primary contribution of this work is theoretical, supported by empirical simulations. The paper is well-written and presents the theoretical contributions in a manner accessible to a broad machine learning audience; the connection to Information Theory in deriving the bounds is particularly well explained.

However, I have the following concerns that could strengthen the paper:

(1) While the theoretical use of AND queries is intriguing, it appears to conflict with the typical motivations behind crowdsourcing for clustering. An AND query effectively reveals the label of the cluster itself, in addition to indicating whether data points belong to the same cluster. If this interpretation is correct, as I understand it from the paper, it raises the question of why similarity queries would be necessary – why not directly solicit labels for individual data points? This is my central point of confusion regarding the paper’s contributions.

(2) Is it possible to simplify the expressions for the bounds presented in Theorems 1, 2, 3, and 4, while still retaining their core meaning and implications?

(3) The experiments appear to focus exclusively on AND queries. If this is the case, the validity of the experimental study is questionable unless the issue raised in point (1) is adequately addressed.

I am satisfied with the authors’ responses to my questions. Therefore, I vote to accept this paper.
