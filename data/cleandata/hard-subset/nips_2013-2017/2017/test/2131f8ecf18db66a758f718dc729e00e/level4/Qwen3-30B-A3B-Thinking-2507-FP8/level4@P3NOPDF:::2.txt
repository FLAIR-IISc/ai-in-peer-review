

This paper investigates a non-adaptive clustering problem where the goal is to recover true labels from noisy pairwise query answers, each involving only a few elements. It establishes theoretical lower bounds for exact and partial recovery under various scenarios and proposes an "AND-queries" algorithm for general k-clustering, validated on real data. While the work addresses an interesting problem and combines theoretical analysis with empirical evaluation, significant revisions are needed to clarify key contributions, justify assumptions, and substantiate claims. The current presentation lacks sufficient rigor in theoretical arguments and comparative evaluation, undermining the paper’s main assertions. Below are specific concerns requiring resolution.

**Theoretical Contributions and Justifications**  
The restriction against repeating queries (Section 3) appears unnecessarily limiting without justification. Allowing repeated queries could naturally mitigate noise (e.g., via majority voting across independent annotators), yet the paper does not address this. The authors cite an independence-dependence concern for repeated queries, but this is easily resolved by assuming distinct annotators for each repetition, yielding independent answers. A compelling justification would require empirical comparisons against a no-restriction baseline—showing that the proposed model *outperforms* the unrestricted case. Without this, the restricted model lacks practical motivation.  

Theorem 1 (noiseless exact recovery) conflicts with prior work [Ahn-Lee-Suh] (Allerton 2016), which also studies XOR-based queries in non-adaptive settings. For $p=1/2$ and constant $\Delta$, Theorem 1 claims $m = n / \log_2 n$, while [Ahn-Lee-Suh] reports $m = \Theta(n \log n / \Delta)$. This discrepancy must be clarified—e.g., by identifying differing problem formulations or assumptions. The proof of Theorem 1 is also opaque and requires substantial elaboration.  

Theorems 3 and 4 present lower/upper bounds on $\delta$ (the relaxed constraint parameter), but the bounds are impractical for interpretation. Specifically, the relationship between $m$ and $n$ (e.g., how $m$ scales with $n$ for fixed $\delta$) is unclear, making it impossible to assess the *degree* of improvement from relaxing constraints. The gap between Theorems 3 and 4’s bounds is also uncharacterized—could it be unbounded? The proofs lack sufficient detail for verification, reducing confidence in the results.

**Experimental Evaluation and Claims**  
Figure 2 claims the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster-query method but only compares against the *lower bound* of the latter, not the method itself. Without a direct comparison to the same-cluster-query *algorithm* (e.g., via the same implementation or baseline), the claim remains unsubstantiated. The figure should include a crossing point analysis to clarify when AND-queries dominate. The rebuttal mentions addressing this, but the revised figure must explicitly show this comparison.  

Figures 3–5 similarly omit comparisons to state-of-the-art methods (e.g., same-cluster-query algorithms), despite the paper repeatedly asserting superior performance. To support such claims, these figures must include baselines from relevant literature. Without this, the empirical results fail to validate the paper’s core contribution.  

**Algorithm Practicality and Clarity**  
Algorithm 1 requires prior knowledge of $p$ (noise rate) or cluster size ratios (per Theorem 6), yet the paper does not discuss how this is obtained in practice. This is a critical gap: if these parameters are unknown, the algorithm’s applicability collapses. The authors should either propose a practical estimation method or acknowledge this as a limitation. The pseudocode also relies heavily on Theorem 5’s proof, which is difficult to follow, making the algorithm’s implementation unclear without deep theoretical engagement.

**Additional Notes on Rebuttal**  
The rebuttal clarified that repeating queries *is* ineffective (as argued in the paper), but this requires stronger evidence beyond the authors’ assertion. A brief empirical demonstration (e.g., a small simulation showing no gain from repetition) would solidify this point. Regarding the same-cluster-query comparison, the rebuttal’s promised discussion must be integrated into the main text, not relegated to a footnote.

**Recommendation**  
This paper tackles a relevant problem but currently lacks sufficient rigor to warrant publication. The theoretical claims require substantial clarification (especially regarding [Ahn-Lee-Suh]), the experimental evaluation must include direct comparisons to baselines, and the algorithm’s practicality must be justified. If the authors address these points—particularly by:  
(1) Resolving the Theorem 1 vs. [Ahn-Lee-Suh] conflict,  
(2) Adding comparative experiments against same-cluster-query methods,  
(3) Clarifying the interpretation of bounds in Theorems 3–4,  
(4) Justifying the need for prior knowledge in Algorithm 1,  
the work could be a strong contribution. As-is, the paper’s claims are unsupported, and the restricted model’s rationale remains unconvincing.  

*Revisions required before acceptance: Theoretical clarity, empirical validation, and justification of key assumptions. Without these, the paper’s contribution is not sufficiently demonstrated.*
