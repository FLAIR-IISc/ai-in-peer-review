This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with access to an oracle. The authors study a model where a learner aims to recover a target clustering by posing queries, such as "same-cluster" queries that determine if two instances belong to the same partition. This framework is also generalized to accommodate any fixed, binary-valued function of the instance labels.

The problem of query-based clustering is well-motivated and topical, with clear applications in areas like record deduplication and crowdsourcing. The paper's primary contribution is a rigorous, information-theoretic analysis of this problem. The authors derive both upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required to achieve perfect or approximate recovery of the ground-truth clustering. A key strength of the work is its consideration of noisy oracles, which reflects a more realistic setting. Furthermore, the information-theoretic tools employed are interesting and introduce a novel perspective to the machine learning community for analyzing this class of problems.

The primary limitation of the analysis stems from its generality. By forgoing structural assumptions about the problem (e.g., defining a hypothesis class for the clusterings), the scope of the results is inherently constrained. Consequently, the derived bounds depend almost exclusively on the marginal distribution of the labels and the oracle's noise level. This makes the framework distinct from typical settings in computational learning theory, where prior knowledge is often leveraged.

Despite this limitation, the paper represents a valuable and foundational contribution. It serves as an important first step in characterizing the fundamental information-theoretic limits of query-based clustering. The lower bounds, in particular, provide a crucial benchmark for future work in this domain that may incorporate more structural assumptions.
