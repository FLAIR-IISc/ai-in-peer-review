The authors study a clustering model where a learner can ask queries to an oracle to determine the target clustering. These queries can be “same-cluster” queries, or more generally, any fixed binary-valued function of the input instances’ labels. The paper investigates the query complexity of such models, providing upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required for perfect (or approximate) recovery of the clustering, and also considers the scenario where the oracle provides noisy answers. The authors leverage information-theoretic tools, presenting a novel perspective within the machine learning community.

This query-based clustering model is well-motivated and a relatively recent area of research, with applications in record deduplication, crowdsourcing, and entity resolution. The authors’ approach, framing the problem through an information-theoretic lens, is a valuable contribution. Their reduction to locally encodable source coding provides a strong foundation for deriving lower bounds and designing efficient querying schemes. The introduction of AND queries as an alternative to traditional ‘same cluster’ queries is particularly interesting, and the theoretical and empirical results supporting their superior performance are compelling. The careful consideration of noisy oracle responses is also a strength, as it reflects real-world constraints of crowdsourcing scenarios.

The paper is technically sound, with clear theoretical derivations and justifications for the proposed algorithms. The connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is insightful and well-explained. The experimental validation on real datasets, while not extensive, provides further support for the theoretical findings. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, though some sections could benefit from more concise phrasing. 

However, the analysis operates under relatively general assumptions, lacking specific structural constraints on the problem (like a hypothesis class). Consequently, the bounds primarily depend on the marginal distribution of the labels (and the noise level), placing it somewhat apart from typical learning theory settings. While acknowledging this limitation, the authors correctly position their work as a foundational step toward understanding the information-theoretic limits of query-based clustering. 

**Strengths:**

*   Novel connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding.
*   Informative theoretical bounds on query complexity.
*   Introduction of AND queries as a potentially more efficient alternative to ‘same cluster’ queries.
*   Consideration of noisy oracle responses, relevant to real-world applications.
*   Experimental validation on real datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The lack of structural assumptions leads to bounds that are heavily reliant on the label distribution.
*   The theoretical focus might limit the immediate applicability to specific learning scenarios.
*   The experiments, while helpful, are somewhat limited in scope.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound theoretical analysis of a relevant problem.
*   The insights gained from the information-theoretic perspective are valuable to the community.
*   The proposed AND query strategy offers a practical improvement over existing methods.
*   The work is clearly written and well-organized, despite some minor areas for improvement.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The generality of the assumptions may limit the direct applicability of the results.
*   The experimental validation, while supportive, could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field. While the generality of the assumptions is a limitation, the novel theoretical insights and practical implications of the proposed AND queries make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance, contingent on minor revisions to improve clarity and conciseness in certain sections. I would encourage the authors to consider exploring more specific application scenarios in future work.
