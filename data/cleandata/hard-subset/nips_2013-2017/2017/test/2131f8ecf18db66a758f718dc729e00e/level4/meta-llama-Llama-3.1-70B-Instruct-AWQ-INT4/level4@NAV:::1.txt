The authors investigate a query-based clustering framework where a learner can pose queries to an oracle to identify the target clustering. These queries can be same-cluster queries or, more broadly, a fixed binary-valued function of the input instances' labels. The primary focus of the study is on the query complexity of this model.

This research area, which explores query-based clustering, is both well-motivated and relatively new, boasting applications in domains such as record-deduplication and crowdsourcing. The authors tackle the problem from an information-theoretic standpoint, providing upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required to perfectly or approximately recover the target clustering. They also delve into scenarios involving a noisy oracle. The information-theoretic tools employed in this study are novel and of interest to the machine learning community.

However, the analysis is somewhat limited due to the absence of structural assumptions about the problem, such as the presence of a hypothesis class. Consequently, the bounds are primarily based on the marginal distribution of the labels, aside from the noise level. This setting diverges from the conventional settings typically studied in learning theory. Nevertheless, this research represents a crucial first step, offering valuable insights into the information-theoretic aspects of query-based clustering, particularly in regards to the associated lower bounds.
