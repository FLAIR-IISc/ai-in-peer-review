

The authors investigate a clustering framework where the learner can pose queries to an oracle to identify the target clustering, with queries constrained to be binary-valued functions of the labels (e.g., "same cluster" or AND queries). They analyze the query complexity of this problem from an information-theoretic perspective, establishing both upper and lower bounds for perfect and approximate recovery, including scenarios with noisy oracle responses. Their key insight is the equivalence between this clustering model and locally encodable source coding, which enables them to derive novel information-theoretic bounds that are not previously known in the literature.

The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that pairwise "same cluster" queries are fundamentally limited for approximate recovery, proposing AND queries as a superior alternative that achieves substantially better performance with fewer queries. The theoretical results are supported by a real-world crowdsourcing experiment validating their approach, showing that AND queries yield lower error rates than "same cluster" queries. The information-theoretic approach is novel to the machine learning community and provides valuable insights into the fundamental limits of query-based clustering.

The work is technically sound, with well-supported theoretical claims and a clear connection to information theory. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The main limitation is the reliance on label distribution parameters without structural assumptions about the problem (e.g., hypothesis classes), which makes the setting somewhat distinct from standard learning theory frameworks. However, this is a valuable first step that advances our understanding of information-theoretic aspects of query-based clustering, particularly regarding lower bounds. The significance is high as the findings have direct implications for efficient crowdsourcing systems and entity resolution applications, with potential for practitioners to implement their AND query approach to reduce query costs while improving accuracy.
