This paper studies query-based clustering/labeling: given n items with k possible labels, the learner issues a set of simple, nonadaptive queries to an oracle, each involving at most Δ items and returning a (possibly noisy) discrete answer. The central contribution is to frame this setting as locally encodable source coding: each “compressed” bit (query answer) depends on only a constant number of source bits (labels). This perspective yields information-theoretic lower bounds and near-matching upper bounds on the number of queries needed for exact and approximate recovery. A key insight is that pairwise “same-cluster” (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery, while pairwise AND queries admit strictly better query–accuracy trade-offs in several regimes. The paper covers (i) noiseless exact recovery for k=2 with pairwise XOR queries and new lower bounds that improve on nH(p) for some p, (ii) noiseless approximate recovery where rate–distortion arguments plus local-encoding constraints give new lower bounds, and an AND-query scheme achieves better accuracy than XOR, and (iii) noisy approximate recovery for general k using k-ary AND-like queries on a d-regular graph, with a simple thresholding decoder achieving (1−δ) accuracy using O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive queries. A small crowdsourcing experiment suggests AND queries have lower human error than same-cluster queries.

Relation to prior work: The model builds on the recent “same-cluster” query line (e.g., Ashtiani et al., NIPS 2016/2017) and broader crowdsourcing/ER literature (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah; Vesdapunt et al.; Wang et al.). On the information-theory side, it connects to locally decodable/repairable compression and smooth codes (Makhdoumi et al.; Mazumdar–Chandar–Wornell; Montanari–Mossel) and uses rate–distortion and linear-code limitations (Massey; Ancheta–Massey). The noisy setting is akin to joint source–channel coding under locality constraints. To my knowledge, the explicit equivalence to locally encodable source coding, the new lower bounds under locality, and the role of AND queries in this clustering context are novel to the NIPS community.

Quality:
- Theory: Theorems provide clear achievability and lower bounds. The exact recovery result for k=2 under XOR with m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) is interesting, and the lower bound that beats nH(p) in a regime of p strengthens intuition about locality constraints. The approximate recovery lower bound under Δ-local encoding and the achievability via AND queries are technically plausible; however, one comparison relies on a conjectured (though partially known) limitation of linear/XOR schemes, which weakens the definitiveness of the “XOR is suboptimal” claim. The noisy k-ary AND scheme with O(n log(k/δ)) queries and a simple threshold decoder is elegant; the concentration-based analysis appears sound, and the extension to unknown priors via observed class sizes is helpful.
- Assumptions: Labels are independent with known priors p (or accurate estimates), queries are nonadaptive, and noise is i.i.d. across queries with a simple symmetric model. These are standard in information-theoretic treatments but limit applicability when correlations/side-information or adversarial noise matter. The no-repetition constraint is motivated but may be restrictive in some platforms.
- Empirics: The small-scale user study (200 movies, 10 volunteers) supports the claim that AND questions are easier and yield fewer errors than same-cluster questions; the results track the theory qualitatively. Nevertheless, the scale is modest, and comparisons to stronger baselines under equal query budgets (e.g., adaptive schemes or optimized XOR-based decoders) are limited.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized by regimes (exact/noiseless; approximate/noiseless; noisy/approximate). Some notation is heavy; consolidating the equivalence to source coding and the role of Δ early would aid readability. The XOR⇔same-cluster and AND definitions could be foregrounded sooner. A brief, high-level intuition for why XOR under locality is inferior to AND for approximate recovery would help readers unfamiliar with rate–distortion/linearity limits. Minor typos and footnote placement can be cleaned up.

Originality:
- The locally encodable source coding formulation for query-based clustering and the derived lower bounds under locality appear new. The identification and analysis of AND queries as a strictly better primitive than same-cluster (in several regimes) is a fresh and practically meaningful idea. The O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive scheme under noise with a simple decoder contributes a clean algorithmic takeaway.

Significance:
- The results sharpen our understanding of the fundamental query complexity for clustering with simple oracles, highlighting the cost of locality and the (in)efficiency of commonly used same-cluster queries. The nonadaptive, near-linear-query algorithms are attractive for crowdsourcing pipelines. While the independence and noise assumptions limit immediate applicability in complex domains, the work sets a theoretical baseline and suggests better query primitives for practice.

Pros:
- Clear information-theoretic framework with new lower bounds under locality constraints.
- Simple, effective AND-query schemes outperform XOR in approximate regimes.
- Nonadaptive noisy algorithm with O(n log(k/δ)) queries and easy decoding.
- Initial user study indicates AND queries reduce human error.

Cons:
- Several assumptions (independent labels, known/accurate priors, i.i.d. noise, no repetition) may not hold broadly.
- Some comparative claims rely on a conjectured limitation of XOR/linear schemes.
- Empirical validation is small-scale; limited comparison with strong baselines under matched budgets.
- Focused on two-query (Δ=2) primitives; extensions to higher-arity queries and adaptivity are only briefly discussed.

Suggestions and questions:
- Robustness to prior misspecification: how sensitive are bounds/algorithms to errors in p? Can priors be estimated from the same query budget with guarantees?
- Constants in the O(n log(k/δ)) bound: provide explicit dependencies on q, p, and desired δ.
- Compare against adaptive strategies and other primitives (e.g., triangle queries) under equal budgets.
- Explore correlated/noise models or worker heterogeneity and aggregation beyond single-shot queries.
- Provide a clearer, self-contained argument (beyond conjecture) for XOR limitations where possible, or narrow the claim.

Overall, this is a theoretically solid and original contribution that connects query-based clustering to locally encodable coding, delivers informative lower bounds, and proposes practically motivated AND queries with strong guarantees. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying the status of the XOR lower bounds would further improve the paper.
