This paper investigates a clustering problem focused on reconstructing ground-truth labels from potentially noisy and non-adaptive query responses, where each response concerns only a limited number of elements. The work establishes lower bounds on the number of queries needed for exact or partial label recovery across various scenarios, and introduces an “AND-queries” algorithm applicable to arbitrary k-cluster problems. The algorithm’s performance is evaluated using a real-world dataset.

While the paper presents theoretical analyses for multiple scenarios and develops an accompanying algorithm with empirical validation, it suffers from shortcomings in presentation, clarity of results, and comparison to existing literature. Substantial revisions and potential further development are likely necessary before publication. Detailed comments are provided below.

1. **Query Restriction:** The constraint prohibiting repeated queries appears restrictive and may negatively impact performance, as allowing repetition is a natural strategy for mitigating noise. A strong justification for this restriction requires comprehensive real-data simulations comparing the proposed algorithm to one designed for unrestricted queries, demonstrating superior performance. Alternatively, exploring the unrestricted case may be more fruitful. The authors’ concern regarding dependence between multiple answers to the same query could be addressed by considering a setting where distinct annotators answer the same query, ensuring answer independence.

2. **Theorem 1:** The noiseless, exact recovery setting is addressed in recent work [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which also utilizes XOR-operation ('same-cluster' queries) within a non-adaptive measurement framework. However, the result in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] does not align with the query complexity claimed in Theorem 1. Specifically, for p=1/2 and a constant Δ, Theorem 1 states m=n/log2, while [Ahn-Lee-Suh] indicates m=nlogn/Δ. This discrepancy requires detailed clarification. Furthermore, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow.

3. **Theorem 3:** The expression for the lower bound on δ lacks clear interpretability. It is challenging to derive a bound on m (in terms of n and δ) that directly demonstrates the performance improvement resulting from the relaxed constraint, as reflected in δ.

4. **Theorem 4:** The same interpretability issue applies to the lower bound expression in Theorem 4. Additionally, what is the relationship between the upper and lower bounds presented in Theorem 3? Is the gap between them potentially unbounded? The proof is also difficult to follow.

5. **Figure 2:** The figure lacks a comparison to the same-cluster query method, presenting only a comparison to the same-cluster-query lower bound. Is there a crossover point when compared to the same-cluster-query method itself? If so, what is the underlying intuition? The paper’s claim that the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster query scheme is not supported by the data in Figure 2.

6. **Algorithm 1:** The proposed algorithm requires prior knowledge of p or the relative sizes of the clusters (as indicated by Theorem 6). How can this information be obtained in a practical setting? A justification of the algorithm’s practicality is needed. The pseudo-code is difficult to understand without referencing the detailed (and also challenging) proof of Theorem 5.

7. **Figures 3, 4 & 5:** These figures do not include comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, such as the same-cluster-query method, which is crucial for supporting the claim that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme.

[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
