

This paper establishes an intriguing connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding in information theory. The authors consider the problem of recovering cluster labels for n elements using nonadaptive queries to an oracle, where each query can involve at most Δ elements and returns a binary answer. The main contribution is showing that "same cluster" (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery, and proposing AND queries that achieve significantly better performance. The paper provides theoretical bounds on query complexity for exact and approximate recovery in both noiseless and noisy settings, and validates the approach with real crowdsourcing experiments.

The work builds on recent NIPS contributions to clustering with side information, particularly [4, 21, 22] which considered pairwise "same cluster" queries. It extends the theoretical framework of [4] by introducing the locally encodable source coding perspective, which allows the authors to derive new information-theoretic lower bounds. The connection to information theory is particularly elegant, as the authors show that the clustering problem can be viewed as a constrained source coding problem where each compressed bit depends on only a constant number of source bits.

Strengths:
1. Theoretical contributions are strong and novel. The authors establish new lower bounds on query complexity that are strictly better than the naive n bound for certain parameter regimes (Theorem 2). The connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding is insightful and well-motivated.

2. The paper provides a compelling theoretical argument for AND queries over "same cluster" queries. The authors demonstrate that AND queries achieve (1-δ)-recovery with O(n log k/δ) queries, while "same cluster" queries require significantly more queries for the same recovery guarantee (Theorem 4 and Figure 2). The explanation for why AND queries work better (workers only need to know one label to answer correctly) is particularly convincing.

3. The experimental validation with real crowdsourcing data is well-executed and effectively supports the theoretical claims. The results show that AND queries produce lower error rates than "same cluster" queries (105 vs. 234 errors out of 1000 queries), which directly validates the theoretical advantage.

4. The paper is well-written with clear explanations of technical concepts. The information-theoretic perspective is particularly accessible to a machine learning audience, making the theoretical contributions broadly understandable.

Weaknesses:
1. The authors could better clarify the practical motivation for AND queries. As noted in the draft review, the primary concern is whether AND queries align with the original crowdsourcing motivation. The paper states that "for a correct 'no' answer in an AND query, a worker just needs to know one of the labels," but doesn't sufficiently address why workers would be able to provide this information without knowing the labels directly. The paper should clarify whether AND queries require workers to have knowledge of the label space or whether the query design makes the AND operation more intuitive than "same cluster" queries.

2. Some of the theoretical expressions could be simplified without losing rigor. For instance, the expressions in Theorems 1 and 2 contain complex terms like α = 1/2(1 + (1 - 4p(1-p))^Δ) that could be presented more intuitively. While the paper is technically precise, simplifying these expressions would enhance readability without compromising the theoretical contributions.

3. The experimental section only evaluates AND queries, which makes the comparison with "same cluster" queries somewhat limited. While the paper does include a separate comparison showing higher error rates for "same cluster" queries (234 vs. 105 errors), a more comprehensive experiment comparing the two query types under identical conditions would strengthen the empirical claims.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The proofs are well-constructed, and the experimental validation provides good support for the theoretical claims. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and limitations of their approach.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The connection between clustering and information theory is explained with sufficient detail for a general machine learning audience. The figures effectively illustrate the key theoretical results.

Originality: The connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is novel. The AND query approach represents a significant theoretical advance over existing work on crowdsourcing-based clustering, as it provides a provably better method for approximate recovery.

Significance: The results have both theoretical and practical significance. The information-theoretic bounds establish fundamental limits on query complexity for clustering, while the AND query approach offers a practical improvement for crowdsourcing applications. The paper's bridge between information theory and machine learning is particularly valuable.

Pro and Con acceptance arguments:

Pro:
- Establishes a novel connection between clustering and information theory
- Provides the first theoretical justification for AND queries over "same cluster" queries
- Derives new information-theoretic lower bounds that improve upon previous results
- Provides strong theoretical and empirical validation of the AND query approach
- Well-written with clear explanations accessible to a broad audience

Con:
- Limited explanation of why AND queries are practically feasible in crowdsourcing
- Some theoretical expressions could be simplified for better readability
- Experimental comparison between query types is somewhat limited

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes a meaningful advance in understanding the fundamental limits of clustering with side information. The paper successfully bridges information theory and machine learning, and the practical implications for crowdsourcing are well-supported by both theory and experiment. The concerns raised are minor and could be addressed in a camera-ready version. I recommend acceptance.
