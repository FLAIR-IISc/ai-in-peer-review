This paper studies a clustering problem whose goal is to recover true labels from possibly noisy, non-adaptive query answers, each involving only a small subset of elements. It derives lower bounds on the number of queries needed for exact or partial recovery across several settings. The paper also proposes an “AND-queries” algorithm applicable to arbitrary k-cluster scenarios and evaluates its performance on a real dataset.
While the paper aims to provide theoretical analyses across multiple scenarios and develops an algorithm with a real-data evaluation, it has limitations in presentation, clarity of the claimed results, and comparison to related work. Substantial revisions—and possibly new developments—appear necessary for publication. Detailed comments follow.
1. (No repeated questions): This constraint seems overly restrictive and may hurt performance relative to the unrestricted setting, since repeating queries is a natural way to mitigate noise. To justify this modeling choice, it would help to provide extensive real-data simulations comparing against an algorithm designed for the unrestricted setting, ideally demonstrating superior performance of the proposed approach. If such an advantage does not hold, the unrestricted setting seems more appropriate to study. The authors appear concerned about dependence across repeated answers to the same query, which may complicate analysis. This concern can be addressed by assigning the same query to distinct annotators and assuming independence across annotators.
2. (Theorem 1): The noiseless, exact-recovery setting was studied in [Ahn–Lee–Suh], which also considers XOR-based (“same-cluster”) queries in a non-adaptive regime. However, its result does not match Theorem 1. For example, when p = 1/2 and Δ is a constant, Theorem 1 implies m = n / log 2, whereas [Ahn–Lee–Suh] gives m = n log n / Δ. This discrepancy needs a detailed clarification. In addition, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow.
3. (Theorem 3): The expression for the lower bound on δ is hard to interpret. In particular, it is difficult to invert the result to obtain a bound on m in terms of n and δ, which would make the performance gains from the relaxed constraint (captured by δ) transparent.
4. (Theorem 4): The same concern as for Theorem 3 applies. In addition, how close is this upper bound to the lower bound in Theorem 3? Can the gap be unbounded? Again, the proof is hard to follow.
5. (Figure 2): There is no comparison against the same-cluster-query method—only against its lower bound. Is there a crossing point when compared to the actual same-cluster-query method? If so, what is the intuition? The paper claims the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme, but Figure 2 does not support this claim.
6. (Algorithm 1): The algorithm appears to require prior knowledge of p or of the relative cluster sizes (per Theorem 6). How would this information be obtained in practice? Providing a justification would help establish practicality. Moreover, the pseudocode is hard to follow without relying on the detailed proof of Theorem 5, which itself is difficult to parse.
7. (Figures 3, 4, and 5): There is no comparison against state-of-the-art baselines such as the same-cluster-query method. Such comparisons are essential to substantiate the repeated claim that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme.
[Ahn–Lee–Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, “Community recovery in hypergraphs,” Proceedings of the Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
