This paper explores the problem of semi-supervised clustering, framed as recovering ground-truth labels for a set of `n` elements by asking a series of non-adaptive queries. The central contribution is the establishment of a novel equivalence between this clustering model and the information-theoretic problem of locally encodable source coding, where each compressed bit depends on a small, constant number of input bits. Leveraging this connection, the authors derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate label recovery. A key finding is that traditional pairwise 'same cluster' (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery. To address this, the paper introduces and analyzes 'AND' queries, demonstrating their superior performance in both noiseless and noisy settings, supported by theoretical analysis and a real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Theoretical Framework:** The connection drawn between semi-supervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is insightful and original. It provides a powerful and principled framework for analyzing the fundamental limits of query-based clustering and is a significant contribution to the field.
*   **Introduction of AND Queries:** The paper makes a compelling case for moving beyond standard 'same cluster' queries. The proposal and analysis of AND queries are well-motivated, and the theoretical results convincingly demonstrate their advantages for approximate recovery, which is a practical and important setting.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors effectively communicate complex information-theoretic concepts, making the theoretical contributions accessible to a broader machine learning audience. The problem setup and motivations are clearly articulated.
*   **Comprehensive Analysis:** The work thoroughly investigates multiple facets of the problem, providing theoretical bounds for exact and approximate recovery under both noiseless and noisy query models. This comprehensive treatment strengthens the paper's overall contribution.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Practical Motivation for AND Queries:** The motivation for using AND queries in a crowdsourcing context could be clarified. For instance, a query like "Are both movies 'action' movies?" requires the worker to know the specific label ('action') to provide an answer. This seems to imply that the worker could just be asked for the label of a single item. While the paper touches upon the benefits of combined-label queries (e.g., for error reduction and sub-linear query complexity), a more detailed discussion on the practical scenarios where an AND query is preferable to a direct single-item label query would strengthen the paper's premise.
*   **Interpretability of Theoretical Bounds:** Some of the main theoretical results, particularly the bounds in Theorems 1-4, are presented as complex mathematical expressions. While precise, this makes it challenging for the reader to build intuition about the key dependencies and trade-offs. The paper would benefit from providing simplified or asymptotic versions of these bounds, or at least a more detailed discussion interpreting their meaning and implications.
*   **Experimental Comparison:** The experimental section provides a good validation for the proposed algorithm using AND queries. However, a direct empirical comparison with a baseline using 'same cluster' queries on the same dataset would be more impactful. The paper mentions that 'same cluster' queries were collected and had a higher error rate; showing the performance of a standard algorithm (like the spectral clustering mentioned later) on these noisy 'same cluster' answers within Figure 5 would provide a powerful empirical demonstration of the proposed method's superiority.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper with a novel perspective on query-based clustering. The introduction and analysis of AND queries are significant. Addressing the points above, particularly regarding the practical motivation for AND queries and adding a comparative baseline to the experiments, would further enhance the impact of this excellent work.
