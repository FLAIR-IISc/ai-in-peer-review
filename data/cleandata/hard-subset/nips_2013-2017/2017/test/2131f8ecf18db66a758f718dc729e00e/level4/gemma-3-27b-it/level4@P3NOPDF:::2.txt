This paper investigates a clustering problem where the goal is to reconstruct true labels from noisy, non-adaptive query-answers, each involving only a few elements. The authors derive lower bounds on the number of queries needed for exact or partial recovery of ground-truth labels across various scenarios and propose an “AND-queries” algorithm applicable to arbitrary k-cluster cases, evaluating its performance on a real dataset. 

While the paper attempts to combine theoretical analysis with algorithmic development and empirical evaluation, significant improvements are needed regarding presentation, clarity of results, and comparison to related work before it would be suitable for publication. The current presentation hinders a clear understanding of the contributions and their significance.

**1. Query Restriction:** The constraint prohibiting repeated queries appears unnecessarily restrictive and potentially detrimental to performance, particularly when combating noise. Allowing repeated queries represents a natural approach to noise mitigation. Justifying this restriction requires a comprehensive real-data simulation comparing the proposed algorithm to one designed for the unrestricted case, demonstrating superior performance despite the limitation. If such performance gains aren't evident, exploring the unrestricted case seems more plausible. The authors’ concern regarding independence versus dependence of answers to the same query could be addressed by framing the scenario with distinct annotators providing independent answers to identical queries.

**2. Theorem 1 and Related Work:** Theorem 1’s result in the noiseless setting with exact recovery appears to contradict findings in recent work by Ahn, Lee, and Suh (2016) [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which also considers XOR-operation queries under non-adaptive measurement. Specifically, when p=1/2 and Δ is constant, Theorem 1 predicts a query complexity of m=n/log2, while [Ahn-Lee-Suh] predicts m=nlogn/Δ. This discrepancy requires detailed clarification. Furthermore, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow and needs substantial simplification and explanation.

**3. Theorems 3 & 4: Interpretation of Bounds:** The expressions for the lower bounds on δ in Theorems 3 and 4 are difficult to interpret. It is challenging to derive bounds on *m* (number of queries) in terms of *n* (number of elements) and δ, which would directly reveal the performance improvement stemming from the relaxed constraint reflected in δ.  The proof for both theorems is similarly difficult to follow and requires clarification.

**4. Gap Between Bounds (Theorem 4):** In relation to Theorem 4, the gap between the proposed upper and lower bounds should be addressed. Could this gap be unbounded? A discussion of this potential gap and its implications is necessary.  Again, the proof is challenging to follow.

**5. Figure 2: Incomplete Comparison:** Figure 2 only compares the proposed approach to the lower bound for the same-cluster query method; it lacks a comparison to the same-cluster query *method* itself. Determining if a crossover point exists when compared to the same-cluster query method is critical. If such a crossover exists, providing an intuition for its occurrence would be valuable. The paper claims the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster query scheme, but Figure 2 does not support this claim.

**6. Algorithm 1: Practical Considerations:** Algorithm 1 requires prior knowledge of either *p* (query probability) or the relative sizes of the clusters (as indicated in Theorem 6).  A clear justification for how this information could be obtained in practical applications is essential to validate the algorithm's practicality. The pseudo-code is also difficult to understand without referencing the detailed (and itself challenging) proof of Theorem 5.

**7. Figures 3, 4 & 5: Missing State-of-the-Art Comparison:** These figures lack comparison to state-of-the-art methods, such as the same-cluster query method.  Such a comparison is crucial to substantiate the frequently stated claim that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a potentially interesting research direction, but its current form requires substantial revision. The theoretical results need further clarification, particularly concerning the comparison to existing work and the interpretability of the derived bounds. The algorithmic presentation needs to be made more accessible, and a more thorough empirical evaluation, including comparison to relevant baselines, is necessary to demonstrate the practical value of the proposed approach.



**Reference:**

[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
