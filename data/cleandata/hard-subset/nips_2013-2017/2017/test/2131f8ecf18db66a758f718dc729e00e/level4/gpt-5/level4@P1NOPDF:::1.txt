The authors study a clustering model in which a learner can query an oracle and the goal is to recover a target clustering. The queries include same-cluster questions (i.e., whether two instances belong to the same cluster) or, more generally, evaluations of a fixed binary-valued function of the instances’ labels. The paper investigates the query complexity of this setting.

This query-based clustering framework is well motivated and relatively recent, with applications in record deduplication, crowdsourcing, and related domains. The authors analyze the problem from an information-theoretic perspective, providing upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed to achieve exact or approximate recovery of the target clustering. They also consider the case of a noisy oracle. The information-theoretic techniques used are interesting and, to my knowledge, novel to the machine learning community.

However, because the analysis makes no structural assumptions about the data or hypothesis space (e.g., no geometric constraints, no parametric family, no explicit hypothesis class), its scope is necessarily limited. In effect, the bounds depend primarily on the marginal distribution of the labels, together with the noise level. As a result, the setting departs from standard formulations in learning theory, where structure typically enables sharper or more interpretable guarantees.

That said, this work represents a useful first step toward understanding the information-theoretic limits of query-based clustering, especially in establishing fundamental lower bounds.
