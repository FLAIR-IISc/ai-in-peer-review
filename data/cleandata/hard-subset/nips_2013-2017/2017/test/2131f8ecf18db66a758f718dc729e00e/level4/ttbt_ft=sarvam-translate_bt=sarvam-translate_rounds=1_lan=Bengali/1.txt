The authors have studied a clustering model where a student is allowed to ask a question from an oracle, and the goal is to find the target clustering. The questions can be from the same cluster [or more generally, a specific binary-valued function of the labels of the input instances]. The authors study the complexity of questions in such models.

The question-based clustering model is a well-motivated and relatively recent research area, used in record- deduplication, crowdsourcing, etc. The authors study the problem from a data theoretical perspective. In particular, they provide upper and lower bounds on the number of questions required to accurately (or almost accurately) recover the target clustering. They also consider a situation where the oracle is noisy. The data-theoretical tools they use are interesting and new to the ML community.

Since no structural assumptions have been made about the problem (e.g., having a hypothesis class, etc.), the analysis is limited. In fact, the marginal distribution of the labels is the main parameter on which the bounds are determined (except for the noise level). Therefore, the context discussed is somewhat distant from the general context studied in learning theory. However, it is a first step, and may help to understand the data-theoretical aspects of question-based clustering, especially the related lower bounds.
