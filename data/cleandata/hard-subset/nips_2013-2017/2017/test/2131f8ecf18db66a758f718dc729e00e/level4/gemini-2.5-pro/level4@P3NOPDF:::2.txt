This paper addresses the problem of clustering items based on noisy, non-adaptive queries, where each query involves a small subset of items. The objective is to reconstruct the ground-truth cluster labels. The authors contribute theoretical lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and partial recovery under various noise models. They also introduce a novel "AND-queries" algorithm for the general k-cluster case and provide an empirical evaluation on a real-world dataset.

The paper tackles an interesting and relevant problem in active clustering and information-theoretic analysis. The combination of theoretical lower bounds with a practical algorithm and real-data experiments is a notable strength. However, the paper suffers from significant issues in its current form, primarily concerning clarity, the justification of its problem setup, and an inadequate comparison to existing work. The theoretical results are difficult to interpret, and the empirical evidence provided is insufficient to support the paper's central claims of superiority. Consequently, the paper requires a major revision before it can be considered for publication.

**Detailed Comments:**

1.  **Justification of the Query Model:** The constraint that queries cannot be repeated seems overly restrictive. In noisy settings, repeating queries is a natural and common strategy to improve signal quality and combat noise. The paper should provide a stronger justification for this modeling choice. If the motivation is to avoid analytical challenges related to dependent answers, this should be stated explicitly. An alternative, and arguably more practical, model would be to assume repeated queries are posed to different annotators, thus preserving independence. The authors should clarify whether prior work has definitively shown that repeating queries is suboptimal in this context. Without a compelling justification, the standard model allowing repeated queries seems more relevant.

2.  **Comparison of Theorem 1 with Prior Work:** The result for the noiseless, exact recovery case in Theorem 1 appears to contradict findings in recent related work, specifically [Ahn-Lee-Suh, 2016]. For 'same-cluster' (XOR) queries in a non-adaptive setting, the query complexities differ. For example, with p=1/2 and constant Δ, Theorem 1 suggests a query complexity of m = n/log2, whereas [Ahn-Lee-Suh] indicates m = n*log(n)/Δ. This discrepancy must be addressed and clarified in detail. Furthermore, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow, which hinders verification of the result.

3.  **Clarity and Interpretability of Theoretical Bounds (Theorems 3 & 4):** The expressions for the lower bound (Theorem 3) and the upper bound (Theorem 4) are presented in a way that makes them very difficult to interpret. Specifically, the relationship between the number of queries (m) and the key parameters (n, δ) is not explicit. The bounds should be reformulated to make this dependence clear, allowing the reader to understand the trade-offs and see how performance improves with relaxed error constraints (δ). Additionally, the relationship between the upper and lower bounds is not discussed. Is the gap between them bounded? If so, by what factors? As with Theorem 1, the proofs for these results are challenging to follow.

4.  **Insufficient Empirical Comparison:** A major weakness of the paper is the lack of direct empirical comparison with relevant state-of-the-art methods. Throughout the experiments (Figures 2, 3, 4, and 5), the proposed AND-queries algorithm is primarily compared against a theoretical lower bound for the 'same-cluster query' scheme, not against an actual implementation of that scheme. Claims of empirical superiority cannot be substantiated by comparing an algorithm's performance to a theoretical bound. To validate the claim that the AND-queries algorithm is superior, the authors must include direct performance comparisons against competing algorithms (such as one based on same-cluster queries). It is recommended that the detailed discussion of this comparison be explicitly included in a revised manuscript.

5.  **Practicality and Clarity of Algorithm 1:** The proposed algorithm appears to require prior knowledge of cluster properties, such as the relative cluster sizes (p) mentioned in Theorem 6. This assumption may limit the algorithm's practical applicability, as such information is often unavailable. The authors should discuss how these parameters could be estimated in practice or analyze the algorithm's sensitivity to their misspecification. Furthermore, the pseudocode for Algorithm 1 is difficult to understand in isolation, forcing the reader to rely on the proof of Theorem 5, which itself is not presented with sufficient clarity.
