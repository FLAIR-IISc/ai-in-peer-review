The authors investigate a clustering model in which the learner is permitted to query an oracle, with the objective of identifying the target clustering. These queries can take the form of same-cluster queries or, more generally, a fixed binary-valued function of the labels associated with the input instances. The authors focus on analyzing the query complexity of such models.

The query-based clustering model represents a well-motivated and relatively recent area of research, with applications in record deduplication, crowdsourcing, and other domains. The authors approach the problem from an information-theoretic perspective, providing upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required to perfectly or approximately recover the target clustering. Additionally, they consider the scenario in which the oracle is noisy. The information-theoretic tools employed by the authors are interesting and novel to the machine learning community.

However, the analysis is limited by the absence of structural assumptions about the problem, such as the presence of a hypothesis class. Consequently, the bounds are primarily based on the marginal distribution of the labels, aside from the noise level. As a result, the studied setting diverges from the typical settings examined in learning theory. Nevertheless, this work constitutes a crucial first step, offering insights into the information-theoretic aspects of query-based clustering, particularly with regards to the associated lower bounds.
