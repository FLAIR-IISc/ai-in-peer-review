This paper studies a query-based clustering model in which a learner may ask an oracle binary queries—such as same-cluster queries, or more generally, any fixed binary-valued function of the (latent) labels of the input instances—with the goal of recovering the target clustering. The authors analyze the query complexity of this model from an information-theoretic perspective, providing upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed to recover the clustering exactly or approximately. They also treat the setting with a noisy oracle. The information-theoretic tools employed are interesting and appear novel to the ML community.

The problem is well motivated, with clear connections to record deduplication, crowdsourcing, and related applications. The information-theoretic framing is natural for studying fundamental limits, and the inclusion of both noiseless and noisy oracles is valuable.

A limitation is that the analysis makes essentially no structural assumptions about the problem (e.g., no hypothesis class or geometric constraints), which restricts the strength and specificity of the results. In particular, aside from the noise level, the bounds primarily depend on the marginal distribution of the labels. This places the setting somewhat outside the usual frameworks in learning theory and may limit direct comparability to standard results.

Overall, while the setting is intentionally abstract and the analysis correspondingly limited, this work represents a useful first step toward understanding the information-theoretic aspects of query-based clustering, especially in establishing lower bounds.
