- Studies query-based clustering with binary label functions, deriving information-theoretic upper and lower bounds on query complexity for exact and approximate recovery.  
- Extends analysis to a noisy oracle model, broadening applicability.  
- Uses novel information-theoretic techniques that are well-chosen and likely of interest to the ML community.  
- Framework is timely and well-motivated with clear applications in record linkage, deduplication, and crowdsourcing.  
- Provides explicit query-complexity bounds and lower bounds that clarify inherent limits independent of algorithms.  
- Lack of structural assumptions about data/hypothesis class means bounds depend mainly on marginal label distribution and oracle noise.  
- Generality removes connection to common ML models (e.g., separability, mixture models), potentially yielding loose or hard-to-interpret bounds for practical instances.  
- Paper should better specify where upper and lower bounds match and identify parameter regimes (class imbalance, noise rates) with tight vs. conservative results.  
- Core problem, motivation, and application connections are clear and convincing.  
- Recommend emphasizing the role of the marginal label distribution earlier to set reader expectations.  
- Suggest briefly summarizing the main information-theoretic tools and intuitions, contrasting them with standard clustering/active-learning techniques.  
- Overall: solid and timely first step that advances fundamental limits but needs structural assumptions in future work for sharper, instance-dependent guarantees.  
- Recommendations: add a concise summary/table of bound tightness, discuss specialization under common structural assumptions, compare to related work, and include simple examples showing effects of label distribution and noise.
