This paper investigates a clustering problem where the objective is to recover the true partition of a set of elements based on noisy, non-adaptive queries, each involving a small subset of elements. The authors derive lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and partial recovery under several models. Furthermore, they propose an algorithm for the general k-cluster case, termed "AND-queries," and evaluate its performance on a real-world dataset.

While the paper tackles an important problem and attempts to provide a comprehensive analysis with both theoretical and empirical contributions, the current manuscript has several significant limitations in its presentation, the clarity of its theoretical results, and its comparison to related work. Substantial revisions and potentially new developments may be required for publication.

Detailed comments are provided below.

1.  **On the Constraint of Not Allowing Repeated Queries:** The model's constraint against repeating queries seems restrictive. Allowing repeated queries is a natural and common strategy for mitigating the effects of noise. A stronger justification for this modeling choice is needed. While the authors may be concerned about analytical challenges arising from dependent answers to the same query, this issue can be readily addressed by considering a setting where the same query is posed to distinct annotators, whose answers can then be assumed to be independent. To validate the proposed restricted model, the authors could provide an extensive set of simulations comparing their algorithm to one tailored for the unrestricted case. If the proposed algorithm does not show a clear advantage, the unrestricted setting may be a more plausible and relevant model to investigate.

2.  **Discrepancy in Theorem 1:** The noiseless, exact recovery setting was also analyzed in the recent work [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which considers 'same-cluster' (XOR-operation) queries in a non-adaptive setting. The result claimed in Theorem 1 does not appear to coincide with the findings in [Ahn-Lee-Suh]. For instance, for `k=2` clusters of equal size (`p=1/2`) and a constant `Δ`, the query complexity in Theorem 1 is `m = n/log(2)`, whereas the result in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] is `m = n*log(n)/Δ`. This significant discrepancy needs to be clarified in detail. Additionally, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow, which hinders verification of the result.

3.  **Clarity of Theorem 3:** The expression for the lower bound on the error `δ` is difficult to interpret. As presented, it is not straightforward to infer the corresponding bound on the number of queries `m` as a function of `n` and `δ`. This makes it hard to appreciate the performance improvements gained from the relaxed (partial) recovery constraint.

4.  **Clarity of Theorem 4:** The same comment regarding interpretability applies to the upper bound in Theorem 4. Furthermore, it is unclear how this upper bound relates to the lower bound in Theorem 3. The potential gap between the bounds is not discussed and could be unbounded. As with other theoretical results, the proof is difficult to follow.

5.  **Evaluation in Figure 2:** The empirical comparison in Figure 2 is potentially misleading. The proposed algorithm is compared against a theoretical *lower bound* for the same-cluster query method, not its actual performance. To substantiate the paper's claim of superior performance, a direct comparison with an implementation of the same-cluster query *algorithm* is necessary. It would be interesting to see if there is a crossover point in performance when comparing the two methods directly, and if so, what intuition might explain it.

6.  **Practicality of Algorithm 1:** The proposed algorithm appears to have practical limitations, as it requires prior knowledge of the relative cluster sizes (`p`, as per Theorem 6). The paper should provide justification for how this information would be obtained in a real-world application. The pseudocode for the algorithm is also difficult to understand without relying heavily on the proof of Theorem 5, which itself is challenging to parse.

7.  **Lack of Comparative Baselines in Figures 3, 4, & 5:** A major weakness of the experimental evaluation is the absence of comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, particularly the same-cluster-query scheme. This omission is critical, as the paper repeatedly claims that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms this baseline. Without direct empirical comparisons, this central claim remains unsupported.

**Comments After Rebuttal:**

Some concerns were properly addressed in the author response. For further clarification in a revised manuscript:

1.  Regarding the constraint against repeating queries, it remains unclear whether this is a beneficial or necessary restriction. The authors should clarify in the revision if the cited work demonstrates empirically or theoretically that repeating queries is not useful in their setting.

2.  Concerning the comparison to the same-cluster query method, the detailed discussion and arguments provided in the author response should be incorporated directly into the main text of the revised manuscript to ensure clarity for all readers.
