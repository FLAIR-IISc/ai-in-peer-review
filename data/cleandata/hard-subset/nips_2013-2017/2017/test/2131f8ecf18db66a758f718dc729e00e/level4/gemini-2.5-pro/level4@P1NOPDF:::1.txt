This paper studies a clustering model in which a learner queries an oracle with the goal of recovering a target clustering. The queries are of the "same-cluster" type or, more generally, any fixed binary-valued function of the instances' labels. The authors' primary focus is on the query complexity of such models.

Query-based clustering is a well-motivated and relatively recent research area, with applications in fields such as record de-duplication and crowdsourcing. The authors approach this problem from an information-theoretic perspective, providing upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required to perfectly or approximately recover the target clustering. Their analysis also extends to the case of a noisy oracle. The information-theoretic tools employed are interesting and represent a novel contribution to the machine learning community.

The analysis is limited by the absence of structural assumptions, such as a restricted hypothesis class, which are common in statistical learning theory. As a result, the derived bounds depend almost entirely on the marginal distribution of the labels and the noise level, rather than on geometric or other properties of the data. Consequently, the setting under consideration is somewhat removed from the scenarios typically studied in machine learning. Nevertheless, this work should be viewed as an important foundational step. It provides valuable insights into the information-theoretic limits of query-based clustering, and the techniques used to establish the lower bounds are particularly noteworthy.
