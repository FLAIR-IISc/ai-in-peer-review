- Paper frames non-adaptive, local-query multiclass labeling as locally encodable source coding and studies noisy binary/k-ary answers.  
- Derives information-theoretic lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery under locality constraints.  
- Argues pairwise AND queries outperform pairwise XOR/same-cluster queries for approximate recovery.  
- Proposes a simple non-adaptive AND-query algorithm that achieves (1−δ)-accurate recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries under an independent noise model.  
- Presents a small crowdsourcing pilot (movie-genre) suggesting AND queries are empirically easier for workers than same-cluster queries.  
- Connects to prior work on locally decodable/smooth compression and rate–distortion/Massey’s limits.  
- Relates to crowdsourcing, entity resolution, and noisy query literature.  
- Notes related results for non-adaptive XOR queries and hypergraph/community recovery (Ahn–Lee–Suh, Allerton’16).  
- Empirical comparison complements observations that majority voting can fail in practice.  
- Strength: novel conceptual bridge between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding.  
- Strength: new locality-constrained lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery.  
- Strength: simple, scalable AND-query algorithm with concentration-based analysis and attractive O(n log(k/δ)) scaling.  
- Strength: real-data pilot indicating practical advantages of AND queries.  
- Weakness: several results, theorems, and proofs lack clarity and sometimes appear inconsistent with related literature.  
- Weakness: prohibition on repeating queries is insufficiently justified and departs from common practice.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation lacks strong same-cluster baselines and ablation studies to support claimed superiority of AND queries.  
- Weakness: algorithm heavily relies on prior knowledge of cluster sizes without a practical estimation protocol.  
- Detailed concern 1: need stronger justification or empirical evidence about banning repeated queries and independence assumptions across annotators.  
- Detailed concern 2: Theorem 1 conflicts with related XOR-type results (Ahn–Lee–Suh); requires reconciliation of assumptions and clearer proofs.  
- Detailed concern 3: Theorem 3’s approximate-recovery lower bound is opaque; needs interpretable forms, plots, and comparison to unconstrained rate–distortion limits.  
- Detailed concern 4: Theorem 4’s AND-achievability bound lacks interpretability and tightness comparison to Theorem 3; proof is hard to follow.  
- Detailed concern 5: Figure 2 compares AND achievability only to a same-cluster lower bound, not to concrete same-cluster algorithms; add direct baselines.  
- Detailed concern 6: Algorithm 1 requires knowing p or cluster sizes; specify practical estimation method, sample complexity, and threshold robustness.  
- Detailed concern 7: Figures 3–5 need comparisons to state-of-the-art same-cluster baselines across noise levels and budgets.  
- After rebuttal: repeating queries remains unclear; either make connection to cited work precise or analyze independent repetition explicitly.  
- After rebuttal: incorporate quantitative comparisons and the detailed AND vs. same-cluster discussion into the paper.  
- Qualitative: promising ideas and bounds but technical and presentational issues need substantial revision.  
- Qualitative: theorems and proofs require rewriting for readability; figures need stronger baselines and clearer takeaways.  
- Qualitative: locally encodable angle and AND-query design are novel and potentially significant if clarified.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel theoretical connection, scalable AND algorithm with attractive asymptotics, and practical crowd-worker insight.  
- Cons for acceptance: inconsistency with prior XOR results, limited empirical validation and baselines, and insufficient justification for restrictive assumptions.  
- Overall: substantial revisions required to clarify theory, reconcile with prior work, and add stronger empirical comparisons before publication.
