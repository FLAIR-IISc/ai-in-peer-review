The authors study a clustering model where the learner is allowed to ask queries from an oracle and the goal is to find the target clustering. The queries can be same-cluster queries [or more generally, a fixed binary-valued function of the label of the input instances]. The authors study the query complexity of such models.

The query-based clustering model is a well-motivated and relatively recent research area, with applications in record-deduplication, crowdsourcing, etc. The authors study the problem from the information theoretic perspective. In particular, they provide upper and lower bounds on the number of required queries to perfectly (or approximately) recover the target clustering. They also consider the case that the oracle is noisy.

The information-theoretic tools that they use are interesting and new to ML community. By casting the problem as a locally encodable source coding problem, the authors are able to derive novel bounds on the number of queries required. The use of AND queries as an alternative to same-cluster queries is also a significant contribution, as it leads to improved performance in certain scenarios.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The theoretical results are supported by experiments on real and synthetic datasets, which helps to validate the authors' claims.

One potential limitation of the paper is that the analysis is limited to the case where no structural assumption is made about the problem (e.g., having a hypothesis class, etc.). As a result, the bounds are primarily based on the marginal distribution of the labels, aside from the noise level. However, this is a first step, and can help in understanding the information-theoretic aspects of query-based clustering, especially the related lower-bounds.

Overall, the paper is a significant contribution to the field of query-based clustering, and the authors' use of information-theoretic tools provides a new perspective on the problem. The results are well-presented and supported by experiments, making the paper a valuable addition to the literature.
