- Problem: Studies clustering where the learner can query an oracle about whether pairs of instances share a cluster (or evaluate a binary function of labels), focusing on query complexity to identify the target clustering.
- Relevance: The query-based clustering model is timely and well-motivated, with applications in record deduplication and crowdsourcing.
- Methodology: Approaches the problem using information-theoretic techniques.
- Results: Provides upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed for exact and approximate recovery.
- Noise handling: Extends the analysis to account for noisy oracle responses.
- Novelty: The information-theoretic tools are novel and constitute a valuable contribution to ML.
- Limitation: Analysis omits structural assumptions (e.g., a predefined hypothesis class), which restricts scope.
- Consequence: Derived bounds depend heavily on the marginal label distribution and noise level, making the setting somewhat removed from standard learning-theory frameworks.
- Significance: Represents an important first step toward understanding fundamental information-theoretic limits of query-based clustering, particularly notable for its lower bounds.
