This paper explores the problem of semi-supervised clustering, where the goal is to recover the ground-truth labels of `n` elements using answers from a set of `m` non-adaptive queries. Each query involves a small, constant number of elements and may receive noisy answers. The paper's primary contribution is establishing a novel connection between this clustering problem and the information-theoretic concept of locally encodable source coding. Using this framework, the authors derive lower bounds on the query complexity for both exact and approximate label recovery in noiseless settings. They also propose a new query type, termed "AND-queries," as an alternative to the more common "same-cluster" (or XOR) queries. The paper argues that AND-queries can be more efficient, particularly for approximate recovery in noisy scenarios. An algorithm based on this query type is presented for the general k-cluster case and validated through a real-data crowdsourcing experiment.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality:** The connection drawn between semi-supervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is novel and provides a powerful theoretical framework for analyzing the problem.
*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper offers significant theoretical results, including new information-theoretic lower bounds on the query complexity required for both exact and approximate recovery.
*   **Novel Approach:** The introduction and analysis of AND-queries is a valuable and original contribution. The paper provides compelling arguments for why this query type may be superior to standard "same-cluster" queries, especially in noisy, practical settings where human annotators may find them easier to answer correctly.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The inclusion of a real-data crowdsourcing experiment to validate the proposed algorithm is a welcome addition that strengthens the paper's claims and demonstrates its potential practical relevance.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

While the paper presents several interesting ideas, it has significant limitations in its current form, primarily concerning clarity, comparison with related work, and the justification of key modeling assumptions. Substantial revisions are needed to address these issues.

1.  **Clarity of Theoretical Results:** The presentation of the main theorems is often difficult to interpret, which obscures their impact.
    *   **Theorem 1 & 2:** The proof of the upper bound in Theorem 1 is hard to follow. More importantly, the result appears to contradict the query complexity derived in [Ahn et al., 2016] for the same setting (e.g., for p=1/2, Theorem 1 suggests m=O(n) while [Ahn et al.] suggests m=O(n log n / Δ)). This discrepancy needs to be explicitly addressed and clarified.
    *   **Theorem 3 & 4:** The expressions for the lower bound on the error `δ` (Theorem 3) and the achievable error (Theorem 4) are complex. It is difficult to directly infer the required number of queries `m` as a function of `n` and `δ`, or to assess the tightness of the bounds. The presentation would be much clearer if the authors provided simplified corollaries that highlight the asymptotic scaling and make the performance trade-offs more transparent.
    *   **Algorithm 1:** The pseudo-code for Algorithm 1 is not self-contained. Understanding the choice of thresholds `C_i` and the overall logic requires a detailed reading of the proof of Theorem 5, which is also challenging to parse.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Baselines:** The paper claims that AND-queries outperform "same-cluster" queries, but the evidence provided is incomplete and not fully convincing.
    *   **Theoretical Comparison (Figure 2):** The performance of the proposed AND-query scheme is compared only to a *lower bound* for "same-cluster" queries, not to the performance of a concrete, achievable algorithm using such queries. A fair comparison requires benchmarking against an actual method to demonstrate a tangible advantage.
    *   **Experimental Comparison (Figures 3, 4, 5):** The experiments lack a baseline comparison against any state-of-the-art methods, including those based on "same-cluster" queries. To substantiate the paper's central claim of superiority, it is crucial to include such a comparison in the experimental evaluation.

3.  **Modeling Assumptions:** The paper relies on certain assumptions that may limit the practical applicability of the work and require stronger justification.
    *   **No Repeated Queries:** The model explicitly disallows repeating the same query. This is a restrictive assumption, as query repetition is a natural and widely used technique to combat noise in crowdsourcing. The justification provided is brief. The paper would be stronger if it provided more compelling evidence for this modeling choice (e.g., by citing work showing repetition is ineffective) or included a comparison to an algorithm that is allowed to repeat queries.
    *   **Prior Knowledge of Cluster Sizes:** Algorithm 1 and its analysis require prior knowledge of the relative cluster sizes (`p_i`). While Theorem 6 suggests that these can be estimated from the data, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion on the algorithm's sensitivity to estimation errors and the practical feasibility of obtaining this information accurately.

In summary, the paper introduces a promising direction by linking clustering with source coding and proposing the novel use of AND-queries. However, the claims of superiority are not yet fully supported, and the paper suffers from significant clarity issues. For the paper to be acceptable, the authors would need to substantially improve the clarity of their theoretical results, provide direct and fair comparisons to existing "same-cluster" query methods, and better justify their modeling choices.
