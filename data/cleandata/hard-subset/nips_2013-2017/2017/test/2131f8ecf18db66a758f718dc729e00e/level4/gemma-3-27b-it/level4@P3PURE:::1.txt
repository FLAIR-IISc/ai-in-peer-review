The authors investigate a clustering model where the learner can actively query an oracle to determine if pairs of instances belong to the same cluster, with the ultimate goal of recovering the target clustering. More generally, the authors consider queries based on a fixed binary-valued function of instance labels. This work focuses on analyzing the query complexity of such models.

Query-based clustering is a well-motivated and relatively recent research area with practical applications in areas such as record deduplication and crowdsourcing. The authors approach this problem from an information-theoretic perspective, providing upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required to perfectly (or approximately) recover the target clustering, and extending their analysis to the case of noisy oracles. The information-theoretic tools employed are interesting and represent a novel contribution to the machine learning community. 

A limitation of this work stems from the absence of structural assumptions regarding the problem – such as a predefined hypothesis class – which constrains the scope of the analysis. Consequently, the bounds derived are primarily dependent on the marginal distribution of the labels (and the noise level). This makes the setting somewhat distant from typical learning theory paradigms. However, this work represents an important first step towards understanding the fundamental information-theoretic limits of query-based clustering, particularly establishing relevant lower bounds.
