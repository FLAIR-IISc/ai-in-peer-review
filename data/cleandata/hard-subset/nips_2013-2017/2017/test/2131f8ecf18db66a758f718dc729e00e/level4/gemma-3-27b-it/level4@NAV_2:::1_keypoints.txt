- Problem studied: query-based clustering where a learner asks binary queries (e.g., ‘same cluster’ or arbitrary label-based functions) to recover cluster structure.  
- Main goal: characterize query complexity (number of queries) needed for exact and approximate clustering, including with noisy oracle responses.  
- Approach: information-theoretic analysis framing query complexity via locally encodable source coding.  
- Theoretical contributions: rigorous upper and lower bounds on query complexity, offering fundamental limits.  
- Novelty: introduction of locally encodable source coding as a framework for analyzing querying complexity.  
- Practical algorithmic insight: proposed AND query strategy shown to outperform traditional ‘same cluster’ queries.  
- Robustness: treatment of noisy oracle responses and development of algorithms that handle noise.  
- Empirical validation: initial real crowdsourcing experiment supporting theoretical findings.  
- Strength: strong theoretical rigor and clear presentation of information-theoretic connections.  
- Limitation: very general model relying mainly on label marginals and noise level, which may yield loose bounds in structured scenarios.  
- Limitation: focus on non-adaptive queries may miss efficiency gains from adaptive strategies.  
- Limitation: experimental evaluation is limited and would benefit from more datasets and noise-level variations.  
- Overall verdict: solid theoretical contribution that advances understanding of query-based clustering and is worthy of acceptance.
