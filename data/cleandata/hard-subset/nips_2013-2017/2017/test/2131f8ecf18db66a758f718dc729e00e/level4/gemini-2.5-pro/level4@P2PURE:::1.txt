This paper investigates a clustering model in which a learner queries an oracle to recover a target clustering. The queries ask whether two instances belong to the same cluster (or, more generally, are a fixed binary-valued function of the instance labels). The paper's primary focus is on the query complexity of this model.

Query-based clustering is a well-motivated and relatively recent area of research, with applications in tasks such as record de-duplication and crowdsourcing. The authors approach this problem from an information-theoretic perspective, deriving upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required for both perfect and approximate recovery of the target clustering. The analysis also considers the case where the oracle's responses may be noisy. The information-theoretic tools employed are interesting and appear to be novel to the machine learning community.

A limitation of the analysis is the absence of structural assumptions about the problem, such as a restricted hypothesis class. Consequently, the derived bounds depend almost exclusively on the marginal distribution of the labels and the noise level. This formulation makes the setting distinct from those typically studied in learning theory, which often incorporate richer structural information. Nevertheless, this work represents a valuable first step toward understanding the information-theoretic limits of query-based clustering, with the lower bounds being a particularly insightful contribution.
