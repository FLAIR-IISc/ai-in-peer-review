### Review

**Summary**

This paper investigates the problem of semi-supervised clustering where a learner aims to discover a ground-truth labeling of `n` items into `k` clusters. The learning process is based on non-adaptive queries made to an oracle. Each query involves a small, constant number of items (∆), and the oracle returns a binary (or k-ary) answer. The central contribution of this work is the novel connection it establishes between this query-based clustering model and the information-theoretic problem of locally encodable source coding.

Leveraging this framework, the authors derive several key results on the query complexity of the problem under different settings:
1.  **Noiseless Exact Recovery:** For `k=2` clusters, they provide upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required for perfect label recovery, demonstrating that with knowledge of the label priors, fewer than `n` queries can suffice.
2.  **Noiseless Approximate Recovery:** They establish a new lower bound for recovering a `(1-δ)` fraction of labels correctly. They highlight the limitations of standard "same-cluster" (XOR) queries in this regime and propose "AND" queries, proving they can achieve better performance with a smaller query budget.
3.  **Noisy Approximate Recovery:** In the more realistic setting where the oracle's answers can be erroneous, they present an algorithm based on AND-type queries that recovers a `(1-δ)` fraction of labels with `O(n log(k/δ))` queries. This result is supported by a real crowdsourcing experiment.

The paper situates its work within the context of recent research on crowdsourced clustering and entity resolution but distinguishes itself by applying a purely information-theoretic lens, which yields fundamental limits and insights into optimal query design.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and insightful framing of query-based clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem. This connection brings a new set of powerful theoretical tools to the machine learning community and allows for the derivation of fundamental performance limits that were not previously known. This fresh perspective is a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Rigorous Theoretical Results:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, covering exact and approximate recovery in both noiseless and noisy settings. The derived lower bounds are particularly valuable, as they establish hard limits on what any algorithm can achieve under this model, which helps to benchmark existing and future methods.

3.  **Novel Query Design:** The proposal and analysis of AND queries is a key practical and theoretical contribution. The authors not only suggest an alternative to the commonly used "same-cluster" queries but also provide a clear theoretical justification (and experimental evidence) for why AND queries can be more efficient, especially for approximate recovery and in noisy environments. The intuition that AND queries are easier for human workers to answer correctly is compelling and backed by their experiment.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the problem setting is clearly defined. The information-theoretic viewpoint is explained well, making the concepts accessible even to readers not deeply familiar with source coding theory.

**Weaknesses (Suggestions for Improvement)**

1.  **Strong Modeling Assumptions:** The analysis relies heavily on the assumption that item labels are drawn i.i.d. from a known prior distribution `p`. This assumption sidesteps any inherent structure in the data itself (e.g., feature vectors, geometric relationships), which is central to most traditional clustering problems. While this makes the information-theoretic analysis tractable, it also distances the setting from many practical applications where such structure is available and crucial. The paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of the scenarios where this assumption is most applicable (e.g., pure entity resolution with no side information) versus where it might be a limitation.

2.  **Computational Complexity of Decoding:** The paper focuses primarily on query complexity (the `m` parameter). However, some of the proposed recovery schemes, such as the Maximum Likelihood decoding required for Theorem 1, can be computationally intractable for large `n`. While Algorithm 1 for the noisy case is efficient, a broader discussion on the computational complexity of the *decoding* phase for all settings would provide a more complete picture of the overall cost of the proposed methods.

3.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** The inclusion of a real crowdsourcing experiment is commendable. However, the scale is relatively small (200 items, 1000 queries, 10 volunteers). While it serves as a good proof-of-concept for the claim about the lower error rate of AND queries, the paper's empirical validation would be more convincing if it were conducted on larger, standard benchmark datasets from the entity resolution or crowdsourcing literature.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a strong, primarily theoretical paper that introduces a novel and valuable perspective to the problem of query-based clustering. The connection to locally encodable source coding is original and leads to important new insights and fundamental limits. The analysis of AND queries as a superior alternative to same-cluster queries is a notable contribution. While the i.i.d. assumption on labels may limit its direct applicability in some settings, the work provides a foundational understanding of the information-theoretic trade-offs in this problem space. The strengths in originality and theoretical depth outweigh the limitations. The paper is a solid contribution to the field and is likely to inspire follow-up research at the intersection of machine learning and information theory.
