This paper explores a clustering problem that aims to reconstruct true labels of elements from possibly noisy and non-adaptive query-answers, each associating only a few elements. The authors derive lower bounds on the number of queries required to reconstruct the ground-truth labels exactly or partially in various scenarios. They also propose a so-called "AND-queries" algorithm that applies to the arbitrary k-cluster case and evaluate its performance on a real data set.

While the paper attempts to provide theoretical analyses for various scenarios and develop an algorithm along with a real-data simulation, it has limitations in presentation, clarity of the claimed results, and comparison to related works. Significant changes and possibly new developments are required for publication. Below are detailed comments to address these concerns.

1. The constraint of not repeating the same questions seems restrictive. This restriction may degrade performance compared to the no-restriction case, as allowing the same questions is a natural way to combat the noise effect. A possible way to justify this restricted model is by providing an extensive set of real-data simulations that compare the proposed algorithm to an algorithm tailored for the no-restriction case, potentially showing better performance of the proposed algorithm. If this is not feasible, it may be more plausible to investigate the no-restriction case instead. The authors' concern about the independence-vs-dependence issue for multiple answers to the same query can be resolved by focusing on a setting where the same query is given to distinct annotators, assuming the answers from the annotators are independent.

2. The noiseless setting with exact recovery was also studied in a recent work [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which considers the XOR-operation ('same-cluster' queries) under a non-adaptive measurement setting. However, the result in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] does not coincide with the one claimed in Theorem 1. For instance, when p=1/2 and \Delta is a constant, the query complexity in Theorem 1 reads m=n/log2, while the one in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] reads m=nlogn/\Delta. This discrepancy needs to be clarified in detail. Furthermore, the proof of Theorem 1 is hard to follow and requires improvement.

3. The expression of the lower bound on \delta in Theorem 3 is difficult to interpret. Specifically, it is challenging to infer the bound on m in terms of n and \delta, which would allow for a direct comparison of the performance improvement due to the relaxed constraint reflected in \delta.

4. The same comment regarding Theorem 3 applies to Theorem 4. Additionally, it is unclear how different the upper bound is from the lower bound in Theorem 3. Can the gap be unbounded? The proof of Theorem 4 is also hard to follow and requires improvement.

5. Figure 2 lacks a comparison to the same-cluster query method; instead, it only compares to the same-cluster-query 'lower' bound. It would be beneficial to investigate if there is a crossing point when compared to the same-cluster-query 'method.' If such a point exists, it would be helpful to provide intuition behind it. The paper claims that the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster query scheme, but this claim cannot be supported by Figure 2 alone.

6. Algorithm 1 requires prior knowledge of p or the relative sizes of clusters (in view of Theorem 6). It is unclear how to obtain such information in practice, and a proper justification is needed to validate the practicality of the algorithm. The pseudo-code is also hard to follow without relying on the detailed proof of Theorem 5, which is difficult to grasp.

7. Figures 3, 4, and 5 lack a comparison to the state-of-the-art methods, such as the same-cluster-query method, which is crucial to support the claim that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme.

[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.

Additional comments:

After reviewing the rebuttal, some comments have been properly addressed. However, further clarification is needed on the following points:

1. It is still unclear whether repeating the same question is not helpful. Did the Nature paper show that it is not useful? This point should be clarified in a revision.

2. Regarding the comparison to the same-query method, it would be clearer to include a detailed discussion in the paper, as addressed in the response.

By addressing these concerns and improving the presentation, clarity, and comparison to related works, the paper can be significantly enhanced and prepared for publication.
