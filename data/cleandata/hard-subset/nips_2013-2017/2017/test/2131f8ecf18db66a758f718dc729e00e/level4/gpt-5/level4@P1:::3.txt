This paper studies semisupervised clustering via nonadaptive, low-arity queries to an oracle, with the goal of recovering the labels of n items from m binary (or k-ary) answers, possibly corrupted by noise. The central conceptual contribution is to recast this crowdsourced clustering problem as locally encodable source coding: each query answer is a compressed bit (or symbol) that depends on only a constant number Δ of labels. This equivalence enables information-theoretic lower bounds on the required number of queries for exact and approximate recovery, and motivates new query designs beyond the standard “same-cluster” (XOR) queries.

Main ideas and relation to prior work:
- The paper builds on the recent literature that uses pairwise “same-cluster” queries for clustering and entity resolution, and on work in locally decodable/“smooth” source coding. The authors draw a novel connection to locally encodable source coding, which has received little attention.
- Noiseless setting (k = 2): They give achievability for exact recovery with m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries using Δ-ary XOR queries with α = 1/2(1 + (1 − 4p(1 − p))^Δ), which is strictly below n for a range of p even with Δ = 2. They also derive a lower bound that can improve on the naive n h(p) bound when Δ is constrained.
- Noiseless approximate recovery: Using rate–distortion insights, they obtain lower bounds when each query depends on at most Δ labels. A key observation is that “same-cluster” (XOR) queries are inherently limited for approximate recovery (in line with classical limitations of linear codes for rate–distortion). They propose AND queries, analyze a simple decoder on a random bipartite graph, and show strictly better query–error trade-offs than XOR for a range of parameters.
- Noisy setting (k ≥ 2): They introduce a k-ary generalization of AND queries that outputs i only when both inputs equal i, else 0. With a d-regular query graph and simple thresholding, they prove that O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive queries suffice to recover a (1 − δ) fraction of the labels under independent per-query noise. They also argue why XOR queries are less suitable here due to symmetry that eliminates the expectation gap exploited by their algorithm.
- Experiments: A small crowdsourcing experiment on movie genres supports the claim that AND-type queries yield lower error than same-cluster queries and that error decreases with more queries, consistent with the theory.

Strengths:
- Conceptually novel and well-executed link to locally encodable source coding.
- Clear and rigorous information-theoretic bounds in both noiseless and noisy regimes.
- Insightful demonstration of the limitations of XOR queries and the benefits of AND queries for approximate recovery.
- Writing is clear and accessible for a broad ML audience; proofs and techniques are well motivated.
- Empirical results, though small-scale, align with the theoretical predictions and provide a practical rationale for AND-type queries in crowdsourcing.

Concerns and requests for clarification:
1) Practical meaning of AND queries: Initially, AND queries appeared to leak label information (e.g., “both are class i”), which seems at odds with the original motivation for similarity-only queries. If one can ask such questions, why not ask for item labels directly? This was my primary confusion about the contribution and its applicability.
2) Presentation: The expressions in Theorems 1–4 are quite involved. It would help to simplify or refactor them to highlight the main dependencies and constants while preserving correctness.
3) Experiments: The experimental section focuses on AND queries. The study is appropriate if point (1) is addressed, but otherwise the rationale for evaluating only AND queries needs to be explicit.

I am satisfied with the authors’ response to the above points and their clarification of the role and practicality of AND-type queries in the considered crowdsourcing model. With these clarifications, the approach is coherent and the contributions are convincing.

Evaluation:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound, with careful statements and proofs; the algorithms and bounds are well supported.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized; simplifying some formulae would further improve readability.
- Originality: High. The locally encodable source coding perspective and the systematic analysis of AND vs. XOR queries are novel.
- Significance: The results advance understanding of query complexity for crowdsourced clustering, offer near-optimal schemes, and provide practically relevant guidance on query design under noise.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong theoretical contributions with a clear new perspective.
- Meaningful implications for practical query design in crowdsourcing.
- Solid connection to and differentiation from prior NIPS work on query-based clustering.

Cons:
- Some formulas are heavy and could be streamlined.
- Experiments are modest in scale.

Recommendation: Accept.
