This paper investigates a semisupervised clustering problem where the goal is to reconstruct the true labels of elements from the answers to non-adaptive queries. Each query involves a small number of elements, and the answers may be noisy. The authors establish lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery of the ground-truth labels in various scenarios. They also propose an "AND-query" based algorithm, applicable to the general k-cluster case, and evaluate its performance on a real-world dataset.

While the paper addresses an interesting problem and provides a mix of theoretical analysis and experimental validation, it has several significant weaknesses in its current form. Major revisions are needed to improve the justification of the problem setup, the clarity of the theoretical results, and the thoroughness of the experimental comparisons.

Detailed comments are provided below:

1.  **Justification of the Query Model:** The assumption that the same query cannot be repeated seems overly restrictive. Repeating queries is a natural and common strategy for mitigating noise in crowdsourcing. To justify this model, the authors should provide a stronger rationale. For example, they could provide extensive real-data simulations comparing their approach to an algorithm designed for the unrestricted case (where queries can be repeated) to demonstrate a performance advantage. The authors appear concerned about the statistical dependence between multiple answers to the same query, but this can be addressed by considering a practical setting where the same query is posed to different, independent annotators.

2.  **Clarity of Theorem 1 and Comparison to Prior Work:** The noiseless, exact recovery setting has been studied in prior work, notably [Ahn-Lee-Suh, 2016], which also considers non-adaptive "same-cluster" (XOR) queries. The results presented here do not appear to align with this previous work. For instance, when p=1/2 and Δ is a constant, Theorem 1 suggests a query complexity of m=O(n), whereas [Ahn-Lee-Suh] reports m=O(n log n / Δ). This discrepancy must be addressed and clarified in detail. Furthermore, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow.

3.  **Interpretability of Theorem 3:** The expression for the lower bound on the error rate δ in Theorem 3 is difficult to interpret. Specifically, it is not straightforward to invert the relationship to understand the required number of queries (m) as a function of n and δ. A more direct formulation would make it easier to quantify the performance improvement gained from the relaxed recovery constraint.

4.  **Interpretability of Theorem 4 and Gap Analysis:** The same criticism regarding interpretability applies to the upper bound in Theorem 4. A key question is not addressed: how large is the gap between this upper bound (achievability) and the lower bound from Theorem 3 (impossibility)? Is this gap bounded? Additionally, the proof of Theorem 4 is difficult to follow.

5.  **Insufficient Comparison in Figure 2:** In Figure 2, the proposed AND-query method is compared only to the theoretical *lower bound* for same-cluster queries, not to the performance of an actual *method* using same-cluster queries. To support the paper's claim of superiority, the AND-query scheme should be benchmarked against an achievable same-cluster query algorithm. As it stands, the claim that the AND-query approach outperforms the same-cluster scheme is not substantiated by this figure.

6.  **Practicality and Clarity of Algorithm 1:** The proposed algorithm requires prior knowledge of the label distribution (p) or the relative cluster sizes. The paper should discuss how this information could be obtained in a practical setting, as this assumption is critical to the algorithm's applicability. Furthermore, the pseudo-code for Algorithm 1 is difficult to understand on its own, and the corresponding proof of Theorem 5 also lacks clarity, making it hard to grasp the algorithm's mechanics.

7.  **Insufficient Experimental Comparisons:** The experiments in Figures 3, 4, and 5 are missing a crucial component: a comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly those based on the widely-used same-cluster query model. Without this baseline, it is impossible to empirically validate the paper's primary claim that the proposed AND-queries algorithm offers superior performance.

**Additional Comments (Post-Rebuttal):**

After reading the author response, some concerns have been addressed. However, further clarification is needed on the following points:

1.  It remains unclear why repeating the same query is not considered a helpful strategy in the authors' model. The rebuttal mentions a Nature paper, but it is not specified whether that work demonstrates that repeating queries is not useful. This point should be clarified in a revised manuscript.
2.  Regarding the comparison to same-cluster query methods, the authors should incorporate the detailed discussion from their rebuttal into the main paper to improve clarity and strengthen their claims.
