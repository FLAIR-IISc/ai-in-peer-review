

This paper presents a theoretically grounded approach to clustering via crowdsourcing, focusing on recovering true cluster labels from similarity queries. The authors derive information-theoretic bounds on the number of queries required for exact recovery under noisy and noiseless settings, complemented by empirical validation. The theoretical contribution is well-motivated and clearly articulated, with a particularly strong exposition of the information-theoretic foundation. The writing is accessible to a general ML audience, making the technical core comprehensible without oversimplification. The paper represents a solid theoretical contribution to the growing literature on crowdsourced clustering.

However, I have one major concern that significantly impacts the paper’s practical relevance and must be addressed before acceptance. The core innovation relies on "AND queries" (e.g., "Do points A, B, and C belong to the same cluster?"). While theoretically interesting, this model conflicts with the fundamental motivation of crowdsourcing for clustering. In standard crowdsourcing, workers provide *similarity judgments* (e.g., "Are A and B in the same cluster?"), not cluster labels. Crucially, an AND query *requires the worker to verify the cluster membership of multiple points simultaneously*, which effectively *reveals the cluster label* for those points (if the answer is "yes," they must all share a label; if "no," at least one differs). This is fundamentally different from the standard similarity query model. If the worker can determine the cluster label directly via such a query, the problem reduces to labeling individual points (e.g., "What is the label of point A?"), making the entire clustering query framework unnecessary. The paper does not clarify *why* such a complex AND query mechanism is preferable to direct labeling, which would require fewer queries and avoid the ambiguity in the worker’s task. This critical gap undermines the paper’s practical motivation and must be resolved. The authors should either: (a) explicitly justify why AND queries are more feasible or accurate than direct labeling in real crowdsourcing scenarios, or (b) revise the model to align with standard similarity queries (e.g., pairwise or triplet queries) and re-derive the bounds accordingly. Without this, the work’s contribution to the *crowdsourcing* literature is questionable.

Regarding minor points:
1.  **Theoretical Simplification:** The bounds in Theorems 1–4 are mathematically precise but complex. The authors should explore whether the expressions can be simplified (e.g., via asymptotic analysis or tighter approximations) while preserving the core insights, to enhance readability and interpretability for the broader audience.
2.  **Experimental Scope:** The experiments only evaluate AND queries (Section 5). Given the ambiguity in point (1), this renders the empirical results inconclusive unless the practical justification for AND queries is established. If the authors intend to focus solely on AND queries, the experimental section must explicitly state this limitation and clarify how it aligns with the paper’s stated goal of "clustering via crowdsourcing."

I am confident the authors can address the primary concern (point 1) with a clear theoretical or practical justification. If they provide such a clarification—e.g., by demonstrating that AND queries are *necessary* (e.g., due to worker constraints or specific task design) or by repositioning the model to match standard crowdsourcing paradigms—the paper would make a valuable contribution. The theoretical framework itself is sound and well-presented, and the minor suggestions (simplification, experimental scope) are readily addressable.

In summary, this is a technically strong paper with a clear theoretical contribution. However, the fundamental disconnect between the proposed AND query model and the practical context of crowdsourced clustering is a critical issue that must be resolved. I recommend **acceptance pending satisfactory revision addressing this concern**, as the theoretical results are otherwise compelling and the paper is well-written. The authors should explicitly clarify the role and necessity of AND queries in the context of real-world crowdsourcing tasks.
