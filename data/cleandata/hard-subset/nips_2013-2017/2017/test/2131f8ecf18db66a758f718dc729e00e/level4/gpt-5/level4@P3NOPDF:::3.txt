Summary:
This paper studies crowdsourced clustering via query-based supervision, focusing on recovering the true labels of data points from answers to similarity-style queries. The main contribution is a set of information-theoretic bounds on the number of queries needed for exact recovery under both noiseless and noisy settings, complemented by simulations. The paper is clearly written and accessible to a general machine learning audience; the information-theoretic perspective underpinning the bounds is especially well motivated and explained.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical framing with principled use of information theory to derive upper and lower bounds.
- Coverage of both noiseless and noisy regimes, with explicit dependence on key quantities.
- Clean exposition and notation that make the results broadly understandable.
- Simulations that align with the theory and illustrate the scaling trends.

Concerns and clarifications:
1) Role of AND queries and relation to crowdsourcing motivation:
- Initial concern: AND queries appear to reveal cluster labels directly, seemingly obviating the need for similarity queries; why not query labels for individual points instead?
- The authors’ rebuttal clarified that AND queries do not trivially disclose labels; rather, they encode a constrained, composite question whose answer structure fits the proposed noise model and can be elicited more reliably or cheaply than full labels in some realistic settings. They also clarified use cases where direct label queries are unavailable, too costly, or less reliable than structured comparisons. This addresses my core confusion about the conceptual motivation.

2) Complexity of bound expressions:
- The bounds in Theorems 1–4 are correct but sometimes difficult to parse. While the full expressions are necessary for sharp constants and generality, presentation could be improved by adding simplified corollaries that foreground the dependence on key parameters (e.g., k, n, noise parameters) and by providing big-O/Ω summaries in the main text, with detailed constants relegated to the appendix.

3) Experimental scope:
- The experiments focus on AND queries. Initially this raised concerns given (1). With the authors’ clarification about the practical role and availability of AND queries, this choice is defensible. Still, the empirical section would be stronger with a comparison to alternative query types (e.g., pairwise same-cluster queries) under matched budgets, or at least an ablation that varies the assumed query type or cost model to demonstrate robustness.

Technical quality and significance:
- The theoretical results are sound and appear to be novel in the context of query-based clustering under crowdsourced noise models. The connection to information-theoretic limits is valuable and helps position the work relative to existing literature on active clustering and noisy comparison queries.
- The inclusion of both upper and lower bounds highlights the near-optimality of the proposed strategies in certain regimes, which is a meaningful contribution.
- The work is likely to be of interest to researchers in interactive learning, crowdsourcing, and clustering.

Clarity and presentation:
- The writing is generally strong, with clear statements of assumptions, definitions, and results.
- To further improve readability:
  - Provide intuition immediately after each theorem (what drives the dependence on each parameter).
  - Aggregate the main scaling takeaways into a concise table or bullet list in the main text.
  - State the noise models up front and reference them consistently; include a brief discussion contrasting stochastic vs. adversarial noise (if considered).
  - Add concrete examples of how AND queries would be instantiated in real tasks, including interface considerations for crowd workers and potential cognitive load.

Experiments:
- The simulations illustrate the theoretical trends, but a stronger empirical narrative would include:
  - Comparisons across query types under a common cost/accuracy budget.
  - Sensitivity analyses to varying noise levels and class imbalance.
  - A brief discussion of query latency, worker error rates, and practical annotation pipelines, even if only via synthetic proxies.

Suggestions:
- Add simplified corollaries or big-Θ statements that capture the essential scaling for Theorems 1–4.
- Provide a short “practical guidance” subsection summarizing when to prefer AND queries versus other query types, based on model assumptions and budget.
- Clarify the cost model for different query types and motivate the empirical focus on AND queries with either empirical evidence (pilot study) or citations indicating their feasibility.

Overall assessment:
This is a well-executed theoretical paper with clear information-theoretic insights into query complexity for crowdsourced clustering. My initial concerns about the interpretation and practicality of AND queries were satisfactorily addressed in the rebuttal, and the authors’ clarifications render the experimental design appropriate for the stated model. With minor revisions to streamline the presentation of bounds and to broaden or better motivate the empirical choices, the paper will make a solid contribution.

Recommendation: Accept.
