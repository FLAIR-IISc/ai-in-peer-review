This paper investigates a clustering problem where the goal is to reconstruct true labels from potentially noisy and non-adaptive query-answers, with each query involving only a small subset of elements. The authors derive lower bounds on the number of queries needed for exact or partial label recovery across different scenarios and propose an "AND-queries" algorithm applicable to arbitrary k-cluster cases. The algorithm’s performance is evaluated using a real dataset.

While the paper presents theoretical analyses and an algorithm with empirical evaluation, significant improvements are needed in presentation, clarity of results, and comparison to related work before it would be suitable for publication. Major revisions and potential new developments are recommended. Specific concerns are outlined below.

1. **Query Restriction:** The constraint prohibiting repeated queries appears restrictive. It may even reduce performance compared to allowing repetition, as repeated queries offer a natural way to mitigate noise. A strong justification for this restriction is required, ideally through extensive real-data simulations demonstrating superior performance over an algorithm designed for unrestricted queries. If such performance gains aren’t demonstrable, exploring the unrestricted case seems more compelling. The authors’ concern regarding dependence between multiple answers to the same query is valid, but could be addressed by considering a scenario where the same query is presented to independent annotators.

2. **Theorem 1 & Related Work:** The noiseless, exact recovery setting addressed in Theorem 1 has been previously studied in [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which considers XOR-operation ('same-cluster') queries under a non-adaptive setting. However, the query complexity result in Theorem 1 (m=n/log2 when p=1/2 and Δ is constant) does not align with that of [Ahn-Lee-Suh] (m=nlogn/Δ). This discrepancy needs detailed clarification. Furthermore, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow and requires substantial improvement in readability.

3. **Theorems 3 & 4: Interpretability of Bounds:** The expressions for the lower bounds on δ in Theorems 3 and 4 are difficult to interpret. It is challenging to extract the bound on *m* (number of queries) in terms of *n* (number of elements) and δ, preventing a clear understanding of the performance improvement afforded by the relaxed constraint reflected in δ. The proofs for both theorems are also difficult to follow. 

4. **Gap Between Bounds:** For Theorem 4, what is the relationship between the derived upper and lower bounds from Theorem 3? Is the gap between them potentially unbounded?  Again, improving the clarity of the proof is essential.

5. **Figure 2: Incomplete Comparison:** Figure 2 only compares the proposed approach to the lower bound of the same-cluster query method, not to the method itself. It is unclear if a crossover point exists when comparing to the actual same-cluster query performance. If so, providing an intuition for this behavior would be valuable. The claim that the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster query scheme is not supported by the data presented in Figure 2.

6. **Algorithm 1: Practicality and Clarity:** The proposed algorithm requires prior knowledge of *p* (query probability) or the relative sizes of the clusters (as indicated by Theorem 6). How can this information be obtained in a practical setting? Justification of the algorithm’s practicality is necessary. The pseudo-code is also difficult to understand without a thorough grasp of the complex proof of Theorem 5, which itself requires simplification.

7. **Figures 3, 4, & 5: Lack of State-of-the-Art Comparison:** These figures lack comparison to relevant state-of-the-art methods, specifically the same-cluster-query method. Such a comparison is crucial to substantiate the authors’ claims that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms this scheme in various scenarios.
