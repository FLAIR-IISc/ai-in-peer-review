This paper investigates a clustering problem where the goal is to reconstruct true labels from potentially noisy and non-adaptive query-answers, with each query involving only a few elements. The authors derive lower bounds on the number of queries required for exact or partial reconstruction of ground-truth labels across various scenarios, and propose an "AND-queries" algorithm applicable to arbitrary k-cluster cases, evaluating its performance on a real dataset.

While the paper attempts to provide theoretical analyses alongside an algorithm and real-data simulation, it suffers from limitations in presentation, clarity of results, and comparison to related work. Substantial revisions and potential further development are needed for publication. Detailed comments follow.

1. The constraint forbidding repeated queries appears restrictive and may even degrade performance, as allowing repetition is a natural strategy for mitigating noise. A strong justification for this restriction is needed, ideally through extensive real-data simulations comparing the proposed algorithm to one designed for the unrestricted case, demonstrating superior performance. If such performance gains are not observed, exploring the unrestricted case would be more compelling. The authors express concern regarding independence versus dependence issues with multiple answers to the same query, which complicates analysis. However, this can be addressed by considering a setting where the same query is presented to distinct annotators, assuming independence of their responses.

2. Theorem 1’s result in the noiseless, exact recovery setting overlaps with prior work by Ahn, Lee, and Suh [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which also examines XOR-operation ('same-cluster') queries under a non-adaptive measurement setting. However, the query complexity presented in Theorem 1 (m=n/log2 when p=1/2 and Δ is constant) does not align with the result in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] (m=nlogn/Δ). This discrepancy requires detailed clarification. Furthermore, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow.

3. The expression for the lower bound on δ in Theorem 3 is challenging to interpret, and it is difficult to readily infer the bound on m (number of queries) in terms of n (number of elements) and δ, preventing a clear understanding of the performance improvement stemming from the relaxed constraint reflected in δ.

4. The same comment applies to Theorem 4 regarding the difficulty in interpreting the lower bound on δ and its relation to m. Additionally, what is the gap between the upper and lower bounds in Theorem 3, and can this gap be unbounded? The proof is also difficult to follow.

5. Figure 2 lacks a comparison to the same-cluster query *method*, presenting only a comparison to the same-cluster-query *lower bound*. It is unclear if a crossover point exists when comparing to the method itself, and if so, what intuition explains it. The paper’s claim of outperforming the same-cluster query scheme is not supported by Figure 2.

6. Algorithm 1 requires prior knowledge of p or the relative sizes of the clusters (as indicated by Theorem 6). How can this information be obtained in practice? A justification for the algorithm's practicality is necessary. The pseudo-code is difficult to follow without referencing the detailed (and challenging) proof of Theorem 5.

7. Figures 3, 4, and 5 do not include comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, such as the same-cluster-query method. This comparison is crucial to substantiate the claim, made repeatedly throughout the paper, that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme.

[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
