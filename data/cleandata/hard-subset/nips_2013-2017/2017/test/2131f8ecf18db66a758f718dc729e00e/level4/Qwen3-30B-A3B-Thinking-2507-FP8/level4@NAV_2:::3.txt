

This paper establishes a novel connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding in information theory. The authors demonstrate that the problem of recovering cluster labels via pairwise queries to an oracle is equivalent to locally encodable source coding, where each compressed bit depends on only a constant number of input bits. This equivalence enables the authors to derive information-theoretic lower bounds on query complexity for both noiseless and noisy settings, and to propose AND queries as a superior alternative to traditional "same cluster" queries. The work extends previous NIPS contributions on clustering with queries [4, 21, 22, 33], while addressing a key gap in the literature regarding query efficiency for approximate recovery.

The paper excels in theoretical quality, providing rigorous bounds for exact and approximate recovery in both noiseless and noisy regimes. The connection to information theory is particularly compelling, with the authors deriving nontrivial lower bounds that improve upon naive entropy-based bounds. The technical content is sound, with clear proofs for all main theorems and a thorough comparison of AND queries against the standard XOR ("same cluster") queries. The clarity of presentation is excellent, with well-structured arguments that make the theoretical contributions accessible to a general machine learning audience. The significance of the work is strong, as it provides both theoretical foundations and practical improvements for crowdsourcing applications, with empirical validation on real crowdsourced datasets.

The main strengths of the paper include the novel theoretical connection between clustering and information theory, the rigorous analysis of query complexity with both exact and approximate recovery guarantees, and the practical validation through crowdsourcing experiments. The AND query approach represents a meaningful contribution that demonstrates how simple query modifications can substantially improve performance.

The primary weakness concerns the practical motivation for AND queries, which appears to contradict the standard crowdsourcing paradigm. As the paper states, "For a correct 'no' answer in an AND query, a worker just need to know one of the labels in the pair. For a 'same cluster' query, both the labels must be known to the worker for any correct answer." However, this seems to suggest that the AND query inherently requires the worker to know the label of at least one element (which would be the very information the paper aims to recover), potentially making the query less practical than claimed. The authors should clarify whether the worker is expected to have prior knowledge of labels (which would make the query redundant) or how the worker would determine the label of one element without prior knowledge.

Another minor concern is that the expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1-4 could be simplified without losing technical content, particularly for the practical application of the results. The current expressions, while precise, may hinder adoption by practitioners.

The experimental section is well-designed but would be strengthened by including a direct comparison between AND queries and "same cluster" queries, as the paper claims AND queries outperform the standard approach. The current experiments only validate the AND query approach, leaving the comparative advantage unverified in the experimental section.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical implications for crowdsourcing applications. The paper makes a significant advance in understanding query complexity for clustering, and the AND query approach represents a meaningful improvement over existing methods. The theoretical results are well-supported, the writing is clear, and the experimental validation provides important real-world relevance. I recommend acceptance with the minor clarification regarding the practical motivation for AND queries.
