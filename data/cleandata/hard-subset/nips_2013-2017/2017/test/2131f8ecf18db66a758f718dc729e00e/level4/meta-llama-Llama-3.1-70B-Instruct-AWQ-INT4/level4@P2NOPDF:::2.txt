This paper explores a clustering problem aimed at reconstructing true labels of elements from possibly noisy and non-adaptive query-answers, each associating only a few elements. The authors derive lower bounds on the number of queries required to reconstruct the ground-truth labels exactly or partially in various scenarios. They also propose the "AND-queries" algorithm, applicable to arbitrary k-cluster cases, and evaluate its performance using a real data set.

While the paper attempts to provide theoretical analyses for different scenarios and develops an algorithm with real-data simulations, it has limitations in presentation, clarity of the claimed results, and comparison to related works. Significant changes and possibly new developments may be necessary for publication. Below are detailed comments.

1. The constraint of not repeating the same question seems restrictive and may degrade performance compared to the no-restriction case. Allowing the same questions is a natural way to combat the noise effect. To justify the restricted model, the authors could provide extensive real-data simulations comparing their algorithm to one tailored for the no-restriction case, demonstrating better performance. If not, investigating the no-restriction case might be more plausible. The authors' concern about independence versus dependence for multiple answers to the same query can be addressed by assuming independent answers from distinct annotators.

2. The noiseless setting with exact recovery was studied in a recent work [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which also considers XOR-operation ('same-cluster' queries) under a non-adaptive measurement setting. However, the result in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] does not coincide with Theorem 1. For instance, when p=1/2 and \Delta is a constant, the query complexity in Theorem 1 is m=n/log2, while in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] it is m=nlogn/\Delta. This discrepancy needs clarification. Furthermore, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow.

3. The expression of the lower bound on \delta in Theorem 3 is hard to interpret, making it challenging to infer the bound on m in terms of n and \delta, which would directly show the performance improvement due to the relaxed constraint.

4. The same comment applies to Theorem 4, and the proof is also hard to follow. Additionally, the gap between the upper bound and the lower bound in Theorem 3 is unclear. Can the gap be unbounded?

5. Figure 2 lacks a comparison to the same-cluster query method, only comparing to the same-cluster-query 'lower' bound. It would be interesting to see if there is a crossing point when compared to the same-cluster-query 'method'. The paper claims that the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster query scheme, but this is not supported by Figure 2.

6. Algorithm 1 requires prior knowledge of p or the relative sizes of clusters (as seen in Theorem 6). A proper justification for obtaining such information in practice is necessary to validate the algorithm's practicality. The pseudo-code is also difficult to follow without relying on the detailed proof of Theorem 5, which is also challenging to grasp.

7. Figures 3, 4, and 5 lack a comparison to state-of-the-art methods, such as the same-cluster-query method, which is crucial to support the claim that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme.

Reference:
[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
