- Paper addresses semi-supervised clustering with non-adaptive, noisy constant-size queries; main contributions are linking the problem to locally encodable source coding, deriving information-theoretic lower bounds, proposing AND-queries, and presenting an algorithm validated on crowdsourced data.

- Strength — Originality: Novel connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding.

- Strength — Theoretical contribution: New information-theoretic lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery.

- Strength — Novel approach: Introduction and analysis of AND-queries as an alternative to same-cluster queries, argued to be more practical in noisy settings.

- Strength — Practical relevance: Real-data crowdsourcing experiment provided to validate the algorithm.

- Weakness — Clarity of theoretical results: Main theorems are hard to interpret and their impact is obscured.

- Weakness — Theorem 1 & 2: Upper-bound proof is difficult to follow and appears to contradict prior results (e.g., Ahn et al., 2016) for certain parameter regimes.

- Weakness — Theorem 3 & 4: Lower/achievable error expressions are complex; asymptotic scaling m as a function of n and δ is not clearly stated or simplified.

- Weakness — Algorithm 1: Pseudocode is not self-contained; threshold choices C_i and overall logic require opaque proof details to understand.

- Weakness — Insufficient theoretical comparison: AND-query performance is compared only to a lower bound for same-cluster queries, not to an actual achievable same-cluster algorithm.

- Weakness — Insufficient experimental baselines: Experiments lack comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, including same-cluster query algorithms.

- Weakness — Modeling assumptions — No repeated queries: Banning repeated queries is restrictive and inadequately justified given its common use to combat noise.

- Weakness — Modeling assumptions — Prior cluster-size knowledge: Algorithm requires known p_i; estimation sensitivity and practical feasibility are not thoroughly discussed.

- Overall assessment: Promising and original work but currently unsupported claims and major clarity/comparison/modeling issues require substantial revision.
