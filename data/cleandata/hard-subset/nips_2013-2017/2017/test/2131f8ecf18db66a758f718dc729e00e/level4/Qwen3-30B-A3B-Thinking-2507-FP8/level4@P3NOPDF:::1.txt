

Review:  
This paper investigates query-based clustering, where a learner identifies an unknown target clustering by posing queries to an oracle (e.g., same-cluster or binary-valued label queries), and analyzes the information-theoretic query complexity required for exact or approximate recovery. The authors establish tight upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed, accounting for both noiseless and noisy oracles. The work is well-motivated, addressing a growing area with practical relevance to record deduplication and crowdsourced labeling, and contributes novel information-theoretic techniques to the machine learning community.  

The paper’s strengths lie in its clean theoretical framework and rigorous analysis. By focusing solely on the information-theoretic limits without imposing structural assumptions (e.g., on cluster structure or data distribution), the authors isolate the fundamental role of the label distribution in determining query complexity. This approach yields precise bounds that depend primarily on the entropy or size of the underlying cluster assignment distribution and the noise level—a departure from typical learning-theoretic settings that often rely on hypothesis classes or geometric constraints. The derivation of matching upper and lower bounds for both exact and approximate recovery is particularly compelling, demonstrating how the query complexity scales with problem parameters. The inclusion of a noisy oracle model further enhances practical relevance, with the authors providing nuanced bounds that capture the interplay between noise and recovery guarantees.  

However, the paper’s generality also constitutes its primary limitation. By eschewing structural assumptions about the data or clustering mechanism, the analysis becomes highly abstract and disconnected from typical learning-theoretic paradigms. The bounds depend exclusively on the marginal distribution of cluster labels (e.g., its entropy or cardinality), rendering the results insensitive to the intrinsic geometry or representational properties of the data. While this abstraction is valuable for establishing foundational limits, it raises questions about applicability: for instance, how do these bounds translate to real-world scenarios where data might exhibit low-dimensional structure or cluster separability? The paper does not address whether the derived query complexity could be reduced under mild structural assumptions (e.g., clusters being separable in feature space), which might be critical for practical deployment.  

This divergence from standard learning theory is not a flaw but a deliberate design choice. The authors explicitly position their work as a first step toward understanding the information-theoretic *minimum* query requirements for clustering, independent of data structure. While this makes the results less immediately actionable for applied settings, it provides a crucial benchmark for evaluating future algorithms. The work thus serves as a necessary theoretical baseline, highlighting the inherent information cost of clustering without data assumptions. The authors’ emphasis on deriving tight bounds—rather than proposing algorithms—aligns with this goal, though it leaves open the question of whether practical query strategies can approach these limits.  

The paper’s contribution is significant for the theoretical understanding of interactive learning. By framing clustering as an information-theoretic optimization problem, it bridges a gap between query learning and clustering, offering tools that could inform future work on adaptive clustering algorithms. The noisy oracle analysis is particularly timely, given the prevalence of human-in-the-loop systems where label noise is unavoidable. The clarity of the exposition and the novelty of the tools (e.g., leveraging entropy-based arguments for query complexity) make the paper accessible to ML researchers unfamiliar with information-theoretic approaches to clustering.  

In summary, this is a rigorous, well-motivated study that successfully establishes fundamental information-theoretic limits for query-based clustering. While the absence of structural assumptions limits direct applicability, it is a necessary step toward understanding the problem’s core complexity. The work provides a valuable foundation for future research that might explore how structural priors could reduce query complexity without sacrificing theoretical guarantees. For a conference like NeurIPS, this paper offers a clear, principled contribution to the theoretical underpinnings of interactive learning, making it suitable for publication with minor revisions to clarify the scope and limitations of the model.
