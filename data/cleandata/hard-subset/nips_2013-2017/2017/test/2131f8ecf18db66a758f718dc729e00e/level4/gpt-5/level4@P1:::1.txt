This paper studies a clustering/labeling model in which a learner may query an oracle and must reconstruct the true clustering from the oracle’s (possibly noisy) binary answers. Queries can be same-cluster queries or, more generally, any fixed binary-valued function applied to a small subset of elements (typically pairs). The work analyzes the query complexity of this setting from an information-theoretic perspective. It provides upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed to exactly or approximately recover the labels, and it also treats noisy oracles. The tools and viewpoint are information-theoretic and, to my knowledge, relatively new to the ML community in this context. The model and applications (e.g., crowdsourcing and entity resolution) are well motivated.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to recent work on query-based clustering and entity resolution using same-cluster queries, as well as to crowdsourcing models. The information-theoretic framing (e.g., rate/distortion-style reasoning for approximate recovery and analysis under noisy responses) complements algorithmic and statistical perspectives explored previously at NIPS and elsewhere. The paper’s focus on binary queries and small-size interactions with the oracle is aligned with practical constraints in crowdsourcing.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation from crowdsourcing/entity resolution where query cost is critical.
- An information-theoretic analysis that yields both upper and lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery, including a noisy-oracle model.
- The information-theoretic techniques are novel for this community and help clarify fundamental limits.

Weaknesses:
- The setup makes no structural assumptions (e.g., no hypothesis class or generative model beyond label marginals), which limits the scope of the analysis. Most bounds depend primarily on the marginal label distribution (and the noise level), making the setting somewhat distant from standard learning-theoretic frameworks.
- As a result, practical guidance for algorithm design in structured clustering settings (e.g., geometric or graph-based assumptions) is limited. The results are best viewed as baseline limits rather than end-to-end algorithms under common ML assumptions.

Quality: The technical claims appear sound within the stated model, and the use of information-theoretic tools is appropriate and insightful. Because the model abstracts away structure, the generality is both a strength (clean limits) and a limitation (restricted applicability). The noisy-oracle analysis is a valuable addition.

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized and written. The main problem and results are stated clearly, and the information-theoretic viewpoint is accessible to readers with a standard background.

Originality: The perspective and tools are original in this specific query-based clustering context, especially the combination of upper and lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery and the noisy setting.

Significance: The results illuminate fundamental query-complexity limits in an increasingly relevant model. While the lack of structural assumptions narrows immediate applicability, the work is a useful first step that can inform future papers incorporating structure and adapting these bounds.

Pros:
- Timely and well-motivated problem with crowdsourcing relevance.
- Solid information-theoretic treatment with bounds for both exact and approximate recovery.
- Inclusion of a noisy-oracle model and discussion of implications for practice.
- Techniques and framing are likely to be of interest to the NIPS audience.

Cons:
- Limited by the unstructured setting; bounds depend primarily on label marginals and noise.
- Weaker connections to standard learning-theoretic analyses that assume structure or a hypothesis class.
- Practical impact may require additional modeling assumptions and algorithmic development.

Overall, this paper offers a clean and informative information-theoretic study of query-based clustering. Despite the limited modeling assumptions, it provides useful baselines and lower bounds that can guide subsequent work in more structured settings.
