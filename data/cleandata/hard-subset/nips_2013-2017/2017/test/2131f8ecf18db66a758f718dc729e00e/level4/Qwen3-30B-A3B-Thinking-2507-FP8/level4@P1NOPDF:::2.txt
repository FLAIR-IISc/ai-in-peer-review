

This paper investigates a clustering problem where the goal is to reconstruct true cluster labels from noisy, non-adaptive queries involving only a few elements. It establishes lower bounds for exact or partial label recovery under various scenarios and proposes an "AND-query" algorithm applicable to arbitrary k-cluster settings, evaluating it on real data. While the work combines theoretical analysis, algorithm design, and empirical validation, it suffers from significant limitations in presentation, clarity of results, and comparison to related work. Major revisions would be required for publication.  

1. **Query Restriction Justification**: The prohibition against repeating queries appears unnecessarily restrictive. Allowing repeated queries is a natural approach to mitigate noise, and the authors should justify why this restriction is necessary. A compelling justification would require extensive real-data simulations comparing the proposed method to a tailored algorithm for the unrestricted case, ideally demonstrating superior performance. The authors cite independence vs. dependence concerns for repeated queries, but this can be resolved by assuming distinct annotators for repeated queries, ensuring independent answers. Without such evidence, the unrestricted case is more plausible to study.  

2. **Theorem 1 vs. Prior Work**: The noiseless exact recovery result in Theorem 1 conflicts with [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which also studies XOR-based "same-cluster" queries under non-adaptive settings. For instance, when $p=1/2$ and $\Delta$ is constant, Theorem 1 claims $m = n / \log 2$, whereas [Ahn-Lee-Suh] reports $m = \Theta(n \log n / \Delta)$. This discrepancy must be clarified. Additionally, the proof of Theorem 1 is unclear and requires significant revision.  

3. **Theorem 3 Interpretation**: The lower bound on $\delta$ in Theorem 3 lacks intuitive interpretation. It is difficult to derive a clean bound on $m$ in terms of $n$ and $\delta$, which would reveal how the relaxed constraint (via $\delta$) improves performance. The current formulation obscures the practical implications.  

4. **Theorem 4 and Gap Analysis**: The same interpretability issues apply to Theorem 4. Crucially, the gap between the upper bound here and the lower bound in Theorem 3 is unaddressed. Is this gap bounded or unbounded? The proof is also difficult to follow.  

5. **Figure 2 Misleading Claim**: The paper claims the AND-query method outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme, but Figure 2 only compares to the *lower bound* of the same-cluster approach, not the method itself. Without a direct comparison to the same-cluster-query *algorithm*, the claim lacks support. It is unclear whether a crossing point exists when comparing to the method (not just its bound), and the paper provides no intuition for the observed trends.  

6. **Algorithm 1 Practicality**: Algorithm 1 requires prior knowledge of $p$ (noise rate) or relative cluster sizes (per Theorem 6). The authors do not explain how this information is obtained in practice. A compelling justification for the algorithm’s practicality is missing. Furthermore, the pseudocode is difficult to follow without understanding Theorem 5’s proof, which is itself unclear.  

7. **Missing State-of-the-Art Comparison**: Figures 3–5 compare only to theoretical bounds, not to existing methods like the same-cluster-query approach. This omission undermines the paper’s central claim that AND-queries outperform prior schemes. A comparison to state-of-the-art methods is essential to validate this claim.  

**Additional Comments**:  
The authors addressed some points in their rebuttal, but two critical issues remain unresolved:  
1. The utility of prohibiting repeated queries is still unclear. The authors should clarify whether prior work (e.g., the "Nature paper" referenced) demonstrates that repeated queries are unhelpful, or if this restriction lacks justification.  
2. The comparison to the same-cluster-query method requires explicit discussion in the revision, as outlined in the authors’ response.  

In summary, the paper’s core contributions are overshadowed by presentation flaws, unaddressed technical inconsistencies, and insufficient comparisons. The theoretical results require rigorous clarification, the empirical validation is incomplete, and the practical relevance of the algorithm is unproven. Significant revisions—including resolving the Theorem 1 conflict, adding direct method comparisons, and justifying the query restriction—are necessary for consideration.
