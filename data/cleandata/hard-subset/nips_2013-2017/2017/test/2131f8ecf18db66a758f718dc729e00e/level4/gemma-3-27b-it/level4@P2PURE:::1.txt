The authors investigate a clustering model where the learner can query an oracle to determine if pairs of instances belong to the same cluster – or, more generally, to evaluate a binary function of instance labels. Their focus is on the query complexity required to identify the target clustering. 

This query-based clustering model is a recent and well-justified area of research, with potential applications in areas such as record deduplication and crowdsourcing. The authors approach the problem using information-theoretic techniques, providing both upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed to perfectly, or approximately, recover the target clustering, and extending their analysis to handle noisy oracle responses. The information-theoretic tools employed are novel and represent a valuable contribution to the machine learning community.

The absence of structural assumptions about the problem – such as a predefined hypothesis class – limits the scope of the analysis. Consequently, the bounds largely depend on the marginal distribution of the labels and the level of noise. This setting is therefore somewhat removed from typical learning theory frameworks. Nevertheless, this work represents an important first step in understanding the fundamental information-theoretic limits of query-based clustering, particularly concerning the derived lower bounds.
