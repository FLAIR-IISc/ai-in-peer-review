Summary
This paper studies nonadaptive, query-based semi-supervised clustering (multiclass labeling) through the lens of information theory. Each query is a simple function of at most Δ elements with a binary (or k-ary) answer, potentially corrupted by independent noise. The central insight is an equivalence between this querying model and locally encodable source coding, where each compressed bit depends on only a constant number of source bits. Leveraging this equivalence, the authors derive information-theoretic lower bounds and propose near-matching nonadaptive schemes.

For k = 2, they show that exact recovery with noiseless pairwise XOR (“same-cluster”) queries can be achieved with strictly fewer than n queries for a range of priors p, and they provide a lower bound that improves over the classical nh(p). For approximate recovery, they prove that XOR-based schemes are intrinsically limited (connecting to classical results on linear rate–distortion codes) and introduce AND queries, which yield strictly better query–accuracy tradeoffs. In the noisy setting with k ≥ 2 and k-ary outputs, they propose a simple thresholding algorithm operating on a d-regular graph that, with O(n log(k/δ)) pairwise AND-type queries, recovers a (1−δ) fraction of labels with high probability. The paper also reports a small crowdsourcing experiment (movies labeled as action vs romantic) that supports the theoretical predictions and suggests that AND queries are easier for human annotators than same-cluster queries.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on and extends a line of NeurIPS research on clustering with same-cluster queries and noisy oracles (e.g., Ashtiani et al.; Mazumdar & Saha), and connects to crowdsourcing models and algorithms (e.g., Karger-Oh-Shah). On the information-theoretic side, it relates to locally decodable/smooth compression (Makhdoumi et al.; Montanari & Mossel) and to rate–distortion limits and linear code limitations (Massey; Ancheta & Massey). The locally encodable viewpoint appears to be novel and yields new lower bounds tailored to local-query constraints.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The reductions to locally encodable source coding are well argued, and the bounds are carefully derived. The expurgation-style proof for exact recovery and the refined lower bound considering query locality are technically solid. The rate–distortion and Chernoff-based analyses for approximate/noisy cases are appropriate and connect well to the algorithm design.
- Claims vs. evidence: The theoretical claims are clearly stated, with proofs or proof sketches. The empirical section is small but aligns with the theory (including a useful comparison showing AND queries produce fewer annotation errors than same-cluster queries for humans).
- Assumptions: The independence of query noise and the non-repetition constraint are standard in the literature; the paper motivates them and discusses practical relevance.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and accessible to a broad ML audience. The information-theoretic mapping is explained clearly, and the roles of XOR vs AND queries are well motivated. A few places could benefit from streamlining (see suggestions below), but overall organization and notation are good.

Originality
The locally encodable source coding perspective for query-efficient clustering appears new, and the lower bounds under locality constraints are nontrivial. The demonstration that pairwise AND queries outperform XOR for approximate recovery—both theoretically and empirically—is an insightful contribution that challenges the prevailing emphasis on same-cluster queries.

Significance
The results advance our understanding of fundamental limits for semi-supervised clustering with local queries and provide practical guidance for query design in crowdsourcing. The O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive scheme with noisy answers is a useful benchmark. The observation that AND queries can be cognitively easier and statistically superior in certain regimes is likely to influence future systems and algorithms.

Responses to key concerns
1) Practicality of AND queries vs. asking direct labels: The authors clarified that an AND query elicits a simple predicate on a pair (e.g., “Are both movies action?”), not the individual labels. This has two advantages: (i) For approximate recovery, combining labels enables sublinear-in-n overhead beyond n only by O(n log(1/δ)) under noise, whereas asking direct labels requires at least (1−δ)n individual labels to achieve comparable accuracy. (ii) AND queries can be easier for annotators than same-cluster queries, as a correct “no” requires knowing only one label; empirically, AND queries had a substantially lower error rate. Thus, AND queries remain faithful to the crowdsourcing motivation while offering statistical and cognitive benefits over both XOR and direct labels.

2) Simplifying expressions: While the current statements are precise, several expressions (e.g., Theorems 1–4) could be summarized with leading-order asymptotics and simple corollaries that highlight dependence on p, Δ, q, and δ, relegating combinatorial terms to the appendix. Providing numerical instantiations and plots (some already included) helps convey the scaling more intuitively.

3) Experiments focusing on AND queries: The authors did collect both AND and same-cluster responses on the same pairs and observed substantially fewer errors with AND. They also compared to a spectral clustering baseline under same-cluster queries on synthetic data. Expanding this comparison (e.g., multiple datasets, varying noise regimes, and more baselines) would strengthen the empirical case, but the current evidence supports the paper’s main claims.

Pros
- Novel equivalence to locally encodable source coding with new lower bounds.
- Clear separation between XOR and AND queries for approximate recovery, supported by theory and human studies.
- Simple, scalable, nonadaptive algorithm achieving O(n log(k/δ)) queries under noise.
- Good clarity and connection to prior NeurIPS work; useful practical insights for crowdsourcing query design.

Cons
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scale and variety; more datasets and baselines would be valuable.
- Some proofs and bounds could be presented with cleaner, more intuitive summaries.
- Assumes knowledge (or accurate estimation) of priors; discussion of robustness to prior misspecification could be expanded.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide simplified corollaries and numerical examples for the main theorems, emphasizing dependence on Δ, p, q, k, and δ.
- Clarify early that AND queries ask pairwise predicates without revealing individual labels and summarize when direct labels are inferior in query complexity.
- Consider reporting robustness to prior estimation errors and heterogeneous worker noise.
- Expand empirical comparisons (if space allows) to include more datasets, crowdsourcing platforms, and same-cluster pipelines.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a fresh and rigorous perspective that advances both the theory and practice of query-efficient clustering with noisy oracles. The technical contributions are sound and clearly presented, and the insights about query design are likely to influence future research and applications. The author feedback satisfactorily addressed my initial concerns, particularly regarding the practicality of AND queries.
