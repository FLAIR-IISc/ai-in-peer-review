The authors investigate a clustering model in which the learner is permitted to pose queries to an oracle, with the objective of identifying the target clustering. These queries can take the form of same-cluster queries or, more generally, fixed binary-valued functions of the labels associated with the input instances. The authors' primary focus is on examining the query complexity of such models.

Query-based clustering is a well-motivated and relatively recent research area, boasting applications in record deduplication, crowdsourcing, and other domains. The authors approach this problem from an information-theoretic perspective, providing upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required to perfectly or approximately recover the target clustering. Additionally, they consider the scenario in which the oracle is noisy. The information-theoretic tools employed in this study are not only interesting but also novel to the machine learning community.

Notably, the analysis is not predicated on any structural assumptions regarding the problem, such as the presence of a hypothesis class. Consequently, the analysis is somewhat limited. In fact, the marginal distribution of the labels serves as the primary parameter underlying the bounds, aside from the noise level. As a result, the setting examined in this study diverges from the typical settings encountered in learning theory. Nevertheless, this research represents a crucial first step, offering valuable insights into the information-theoretic aspects of query-based clustering, particularly with respect to the associated lower bounds.
