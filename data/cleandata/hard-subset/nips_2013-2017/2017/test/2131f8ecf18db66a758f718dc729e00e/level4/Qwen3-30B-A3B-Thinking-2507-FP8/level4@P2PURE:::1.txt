

The authors investigate a clustering framework in which the learner interacts with an oracle via queries to identify the target clustering, where queries consist of same-cluster checks (or, more generally, a fixed binary-valued function of instance labels). They analyze the query complexity required for perfect or approximate recovery of the clustering, adopting an information-theoretic perspective. The work establishes upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed, extends to scenarios with a noisy oracle, and employs information-theoretic techniques that are novel to the machine learning community.  

While the query-based clustering model is well-motivated and relevant to applications like record deduplication and crowdsourcing, the analysis lacks structural assumptions typically present in learning theory (e.g., a hypothesis class). Consequently, the bounds depend primarily on the marginal distribution of the labels (alongside the noise level), rendering the setting significantly distinct from standard learning-theoretic frameworks. Nevertheless, this constitutes a valuable first step toward understanding the information-theoretic foundations of query-based clustering, particularly regarding lower-bound techniques.
