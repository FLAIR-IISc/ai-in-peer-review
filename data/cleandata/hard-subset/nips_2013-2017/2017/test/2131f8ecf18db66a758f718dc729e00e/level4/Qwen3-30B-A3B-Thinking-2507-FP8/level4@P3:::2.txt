

This paper establishes an intriguing connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, proposing a novel approach to query-based clustering using "AND queries" instead of traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries. The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves better query complexity for approximate recovery, particularly in noisy settings, and provide theoretical lower bounds and a real-world crowdsourcing validation. The work bridges information theory and clustering, offering new insights into the fundamental limits of query-based learning.

**Strengths:**
The paper's most compelling contribution is the theoretical framework linking clustering to locally encodable source coding, which yields nontrivial lower bounds on query complexity that surpass previous results for certain parameter regimes. The authors provide clear theoretical guarantees for both noiseless and noisy settings, with Theorem 5 establishing a significant O(n log k/δ) query complexity for approximate recovery with k clusters. The real-world crowdsourcing experiment (Section 4) effectively validates the theoretical claims, showing that AND queries achieve substantially lower error rates (10.5% vs. 23.4% for "same cluster" queries) in practice, which is a strong empirical contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Restrictive query model justification:** The paper assumes that repeating the same query is not permitted, which appears unnecessarily restrictive. The authors acknowledge that in practice, multiple workers could answer the same question independently, yielding independent noisy responses (as in [10]). This constraint significantly impacts the practical applicability of the results. The authors should provide a thorough comparison with the more natural model where queries can be repeated (with independent worker responses), demonstrating whether their approach remains superior or if the restriction artificially limits performance.

2. **Inadequate comparison with existing methods:** The paper claims that AND queries "provenly perform better" than "same cluster" queries but fails to provide a meaningful comparison with the best existing methods. Figure 2 only compares against the lower bound for "same cluster" queries, not against a practical implementation of that method. The authors should include a direct comparison with the best-known "same cluster" query algorithms (e.g., those from [4, 21]) to substantiate their claims. Without this, the superiority of their approach remains theoretical rather than demonstrated.

3. **Theoretical results lack interpretability:** The lower bounds in Theorems 3 and 4 are mathematically complex and difficult to interpret. The expression for δ in Theorem 3 is particularly opaque, making it challenging to see how the query complexity scales with n and δ. The authors should provide simplified asymptotic analyses or numerical examples to clarify the practical implications of these bounds. Similarly, Theorem 4's upper bound requires clarification on how it compares to the lower bound from Theorem 3—whether the gap is bounded or unbounded.

4. **Practical applicability concerns:** The proposed Algorithm 1 requires precise knowledge of cluster size priors (p), which may be difficult to obtain in practice. While Theorem 6 shows robustness to approximate priors, the authors should discuss practical methods for estimating these priors (e.g., through small subsampling as suggested in Section 2) and evaluate how estimation errors impact performance. Without addressing this, the practical utility of the algorithm remains questionable.

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by connecting clustering to source coding and introducing AND queries, which yield better theoretical guarantees than existing approaches. However, the restrictive query model, insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and lack of interpretability for key theoretical results significantly undermine the paper's impact. The real-world experiment is a strong point, but it doesn't fully compensate for the theoretical and practical gaps.

**Quality:** The theoretical results are sound but lack sufficient contextualization against existing work. The experimental validation is strong but limited to a single dataset.

**Clarity:** The paper is technically sophisticated but suffers from poor exposition of key results (Theorems 3-4), making the core contributions difficult to grasp.

**Originality:** The connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding is novel, and the AND query approach represents a meaningful advance over existing "same cluster" queries.

**Significance:** The theoretical results could inform future work on query-efficient clustering, but the restricted model and lack of comparison with existing methods limit their immediate practical significance.

**Recommendation:** The paper has merit but requires substantial revision to address the weaknesses noted above. The authors should (1) justify the non-repetition constraint thoroughly or adopt the more natural repeated-query model, (2) provide a direct comparison with existing methods (not just lower bounds), (3) simplify and clarify key theoretical results, and (4) discuss practical methods for obtaining cluster priors. Without these changes, the paper's claims about the superiority of AND queries remain unsubstantiated.
