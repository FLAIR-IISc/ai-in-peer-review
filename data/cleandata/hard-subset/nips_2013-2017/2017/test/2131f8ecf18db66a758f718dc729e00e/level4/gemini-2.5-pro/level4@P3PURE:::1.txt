This paper investigates the query complexity of a clustering model where a learner actively queries an oracle to recover a target clustering. The queries can be same-cluster queries or, more generally, any fixed binary-valued function of the instance labels.

Query-based clustering is a well-motivated and relatively recent research area with applications in domains such as record deduplication and crowdsourcing. The authors approach this problem from an information-theoretic perspective, providing upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required to perfectly or approximately recover the target clustering. Furthermore, they extend their analysis to the setting where the oracle's responses may be noisy. The information-theoretic tools employed in the analysis are interesting and novel to the machine learning community.

A limitation of the analysis is that it makes no structural assumptions, such as the existence of a specific hypothesis class. Consequently, the derived bounds depend primarily on the marginal distribution of the labels and the oracle's noise level. This makes the setting under consideration distinct from standard settings in learning theory, which typically leverage such structural assumptions. Nevertheless, this work represents an important first step and provides valuable insights into the information-theoretic limits of query-based clustering, particularly through its lower-bound analysis.
