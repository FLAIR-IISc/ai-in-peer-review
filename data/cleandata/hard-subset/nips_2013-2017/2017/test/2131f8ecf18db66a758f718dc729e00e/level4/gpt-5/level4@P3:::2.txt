Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies non-adaptive, budgeted querying for multiclass labeling/clustering where each query involves at most Δ elements and returns a simple (often binary) function of their labels. The authors draw a connection to locally encodable source coding, derive information-theoretic lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery, and argue that commonly used “same cluster” (XOR) queries can be suboptimal for approximate recovery. They propose AND queries as an alternative and analyze a simple decoder that, in the noisy setting with k classes, aims to recover a (1−δ) fraction of labels with O(n log(k/δ)) queries. They also report a small crowdsourcing experiment suggesting AND queries are easier for workers than “same cluster” queries.

The perspective of locally encodable source coding complements prior work on crowdsourced clustering and query complexity (e.g., Ashtiani et al., Karger-Oh-Shah) and on locality in coding (locally decodable/smooth codes). The paper is also related to community recovery with parity-type measurements (e.g., Ahn-Lee-Suh), and to classical limitations of linear codes for rate–distortion (Ancheta–Massey).

Strengths
- A unifying information-theoretic view (local encodability) that yields clean lower bounds and clarifies limitations of XOR queries.
- An appealing, simple primitive (AND queries) and a decoder that admits analysis in the noisy k-class setting with near-linear query complexity.
- Empirical observation that AND queries may be more reliable for human annotators than “same cluster” questions.
- The paper touches multiple regimes: exact/approximate recovery, noiseless/noisy queries.

Weaknesses
- Several key results (Theorems 1, 3, 4) are hard to parse and, in places, appear inconsistent with related work; proofs and assumptions are not sufficiently transparent.
- The restriction against repeating queries is strong and insufficiently justified; empirical comparisons to repetition-based baselines are missing.
- For approximate recovery, the bounds are difficult to interpret; inversion to obtain m as a function of (n, δ) is not explicit, and the gap between achievable and converse bounds is unclear.
- Experiments lack comparisons to strong baselines using “same cluster” queries; Figure 2 compares to a conjectured/known lower bound rather than to an implemented method.

Detailed comments and questions
1) Model restriction on repeated queries. Disallowing repetition is a significant constraint. While I appreciate the independence rationale and the note that majority voting may fail in practice, the paper should: (i) compare to a repetition-based baseline that queries the same pair multiple times to reduce noise (e.g., majority vote or more sophisticated aggregation); (ii) discuss whether repetition is provably unhelpful for the proposed AND strategy; and (iii) clarify the role of the “single-question crowd wisdom” results (e.g., Prelec et al.) in supporting the claim that repetition is not beneficial. After rebuttal: it remains unclear whether repetition offers no benefit under your assumptions; please clarify this explicitly and, if possible, provide a counterexample or a bound.

2) Theorem 1 vs prior work. The non-adaptive, noiseless, exact-recovery claim with XOR queries (Δ constant) yields m ≈ n / log 2 at p = 1/2, whereas Ahn–Lee–Suh report m ≈ (n log n)/Δ in a related non-adaptive XOR setting. Please reconcile these results by making the assumptions precise: independence/priors, target (labels vs clustering up to permutation), typical-set expurgation vs worst-case guarantees, linear vs nonlinear query designs, and whether your decoder leverages knowledge of p. A clear comparison table would help. The proof itself is currently hard to follow (e.g., expurgation over the typical set under Δ-sparse query matrix); more detail and intuition would improve readability.

3) Theorem 3 (approximate recovery converse). The bound on δ is difficult to interpret operationally. Please (a) provide an explicit inversion yielding m as a function of n, δ, p, Δ; (b) plot m/n vs δ for representative p and Δ to illustrate scaling; and (c) discuss the tightness regime and how locality (Δ) degrades the classical rate–distortion limit.

4) Theorem 4 (AND achievability). As with Theorem 3, present the bound as δ(m/n, p) in a simplified, interpretable form (e.g., Poisson approximation leading to δ ≈ p exp(−2m/n) + lower-order terms), and quantify the gap to the converse in Theorem 3. Is the gap bounded for fixed Δ and p? Can it be closed via degree-optimized graph designs or improved decoding?

5) Figure 2 and claims about outperforming “same cluster.” The figure compares to a lower bound for XOR, not to an implemented XOR-based method. Please add comparisons to strong XOR baselines (e.g., spectral clustering on the labeled graph, ML decoding on random XOR queries, or the best-known non-adaptive scheme), and identify whether/where AND curves cross them. Provide intuition for any crossings and discuss dependency on p and Δ.

6) Algorithm 1 and prior knowledge. The algorithm assumes knowledge of p (or exact cluster sizes; Theorem 6). Please explain how p is estimated in practice (e.g., subsampling plus exhaustive labeling on a small subset) and analyze robustness to misspecification. Also, the pseudocode is terse; define the thresholds Ci, clarify the graph construction, and give the constants in the Chernoff bounds used to set d as a function of (k, q, δ, p).

7) Experiments (Figures 3–5). The crowdsourcing experiment is nice but limited. Please add: (i) a head-to-head comparison to “same cluster” baselines on the same data at matched budgets; (ii) sensitivity to p, q, and d; (iii) robustness to misestimated priors; and (iv) larger-scale or synthetic experiments that explore the theory’s predicted O(n log(k/δ)) scaling. Quantify worker error rates and show that the AND-vs-XOR “ease” translates into better label recovery under comparable budgets.

Quality
The technical ideas are promising, but several proofs are hard to follow, key assumptions are not always explicit, and important comparisons are missing. Clarifications and additional experiments are needed to fully validate the claims.

Clarity
Overall organization is reasonable, but key theorems and proofs require significant rewriting for accessibility. Clearly state assumptions, separate noiseless/noisy and exact/approximate regimes, and provide interpretable corollaries and plots.

Originality
The locally encodable coding perspective and the systematic advocacy for AND queries in this context are novel and interesting. The lower bounds and the O(n log(k/δ)) non-adaptive algorithm are potentially impactful.

Significance
If clarified and validated against strong baselines, the results could inform the design of practical, parallelizable crowdsourcing schemes and spur further work on locally encodable coding. As written, the significance is limited by unclear gaps to prior art and by the experimental evaluation.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel conceptual bridge to locally encodable source coding.
- Insightful identification of XOR limitations and a simple, analyzable AND-based alternative.
- Potentially near-optimal non-adaptive query complexity for noisy, multiclass approximate recovery.

Cons for acceptance
- Inconsistencies/uncertainties relative to prior XOR results; proofs are difficult to verify.
- Approximate recovery bounds are hard to interpret; achievability–converse gap not quantified.
- Experimental evaluation lacks comparisons to strong “same cluster” baselines.

After rebuttal
Some issues were addressed, but two points remain: (i) clarify whether and when repetition is unhelpful (and whether Nature [Prelec et al.] supports that claim in this setting); (ii) include a detailed, quantitative comparison to “same cluster” methods as discussed in your response.

Overall, I find the paper promising but recommend substantial revisions before acceptance.
