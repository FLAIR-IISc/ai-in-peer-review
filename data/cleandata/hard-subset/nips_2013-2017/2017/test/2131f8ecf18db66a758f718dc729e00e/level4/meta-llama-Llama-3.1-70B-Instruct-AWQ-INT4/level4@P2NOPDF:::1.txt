The authors investigate a clustering model where a learner can query an oracle to find the target clustering. The queries can be same-cluster queries or, more generally, a fixed binary-valued function of the labels of the input instances. The primary focus of this work is on analyzing the query complexity of such models.

The query-based clustering model is a well-motivated and relatively recent research area, with practical applications in record-deduplication and crowdsourcing. The authors approach this problem from an information-theoretic perspective, deriving upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required to perfectly or approximately recover the target clustering. Additionally, they consider the scenario where the oracle is noisy. The information-theoretic tools employed in this work are novel and contribute to the machine learning community.

A notable aspect of this work is that it does not make any structural assumptions about the problem, such as assuming a hypothesis class. As a result, the analysis is limited, primarily relying on the marginal distribution of the labels as the key parameter for the bounds, aside from the noise level. Consequently, the setting studied in this work deviates from the typical settings explored in learning theory. Nevertheless, this research serves as a crucial first step towards understanding the information-theoretic aspects of query-based clustering, particularly in establishing related lower bounds.
