This paper studies a clustering problem whose goal is to recover the true labels of items from possibly noisy, non-adaptive queries, each involving only a small subset of items. It derives lower bounds on the number of queries needed for exact or partial recovery across several settings, proposes an “AND-queries” algorithm for general k, and evaluates it on a real dataset. While the scope is ambitious, the paper has issues in presentation, clarity of the claims, and positioning relative to prior work. Significant revisions, and possibly new developments, appear necessary. Detailed comments follow.

1. Prohibition on repeated queries. Forbidding repetition seems overly restrictive and may hurt performance, since repeating the same query is a natural way to mitigate noise. To justify this model choice, please provide extensive real-data experiments comparing against a method that can repeat queries, and demonstrate a clear advantage for the proposed approach; otherwise, analyzing the no-restriction setting seems more compelling. The stated concern about dependence across repeated answers can be addressed by querying different annotators for the same question and assuming independent noise across annotators.

2. Theorem 1 (noiseless exact recovery). A recent non-adaptive result with same-cluster (XOR) queries by Ahn–Lee–Suh appears inconsistent with Theorem 1. For example, when p = 1/2 and Δ is constant, Theorem 1 suggests m = n / log 2, whereas [Ahn–Lee–Suh] reports m = n log n / Δ. Please reconcile the assumptions, models, and regimes to explain this discrepancy (e.g., hypergraph vs graph, query arity, adversarial vs random design, constants vs asymptotics), and clarify which result dominates in overlapping settings. The proof of Theorem 1 is also hard to follow; a clearer roadmap, streamlined notation, and explicit statement of key lemmas would help.

3. Theorem 3 (partial recovery lower bound). The expression for the lower bound on δ is difficult to interpret. It would be much clearer to invert the relation and present an explicit or asymptotic bound on m as a function of n and δ, so the gains from allowing a δ fraction of errors are immediately visible. Providing simplified forms in canonical regimes (e.g., balanced clusters, sparse vs dense queries) would aid readability.

4. Theorem 4 (upper bound). The interpretability issues from Theorem 3 carry over. In addition, please quantify the gap between this upper bound and the lower bound of Theorem 3 across regimes: is it constant, logarithmic, or can it be unbounded? If the gap can diverge, characterize when and why. The proof is again difficult to parse; consider isolating the main technical ideas and moving technical details to an appendix.

5. Figure 2 (comparisons). The figure compares to a lower bound for same-cluster queries but not to an implemented same-cluster algorithm. The paper claims that AND-queries outperform same-cluster methods, but Fig. 2 does not substantiate this. Please include a same-cluster algorithmic baseline, report any crossover points, and provide intuition for when and why AND-queries win or lose.

6. Algorithm 1 (assumptions and clarity). The algorithm appears to require prior knowledge of p or relative cluster sizes (per Theorem 6). How are these obtained in practice? Please propose estimators or an adaptive procedure, analyze robustness to misspecification, and report sensitivity experiments. The pseudocode is also hard to follow without consulting Theorem 5’s proof; making the algorithm self-contained with clear inputs/outputs, complexity, and a brief correctness intuition would improve accessibility.

7. Figures 3–5 (baselines). The experiments lack comparisons to state-of-the-art baselines, especially same-cluster-query methods. Such comparisons are essential to support the repeated claim that AND-queries outperform same-cluster approaches. Please include these baselines and ablations isolating the impact of key design choices.

Reference:
[Ahn–Lee–Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, “Community recovery in hypergraphs,” Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.

Additional comments after rebuttal:
1. It remains unclear whether forbidding repeated questions is beneficial. Did the referenced Nature paper actually show that repetition is not useful? Please clarify the formal conditions under which repeats provably do or do not help, and relate them to your model.
2. Regarding the comparison to same-cluster methods, please incorporate into the main text the detailed discussion provided in the response, including experimental comparisons and a theoretical positioning statement.
