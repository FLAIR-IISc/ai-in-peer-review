The authors investigate a clustering model where the learner is permitted to query an oracle to discover the target clustering. These queries can be same-cluster queries or, more generally, a fixed binary-valued function of the label of the input instances. The primary focus of the study is on the query complexity of such models.

The query-based clustering model is a well-motivated and relatively recent research area, with applications in record-deduplication and crowdsourcing, among others. The authors approach the problem from an information-theoretic perspective, providing upper and lower bounds on the number of required queries to perfectly or approximately recover the target clustering. They also consider the case where the oracle is noisy, introducing an additional layer of complexity. The information-theoretic tools employed in this study are interesting and novel to the machine learning community.

One notable aspect of this work is the absence of structural assumptions about the problem, such as a predefined hypothesis class. Consequently, the analysis is limited, with the marginal distribution of the labels being the primary parameter on which the bounds are based, aside from the noise level. As a result, the studied setting diverges from the typical settings explored in learning theory. Nevertheless, this study represents a crucial first step towards understanding the information-theoretic aspects of query-based clustering, particularly in regards to the related lower bounds.

The authors' work provides valuable insights into the query complexity of clustering models, shedding light on the fundamental limits of such models. While the lack of structural assumptions may limit the analysis, the study's focus on information-theoretic aspects and the provision of novel tools make it a valuable contribution to the field. The exploration of noisy oracles adds a layer of realism, as real-world applications often involve imperfect information.

In conclusion, the authors' investigation of query-based clustering models from an information-theoretic perspective is a welcome addition to the field. The study's limitations are acknowledged, but its contributions, including the novel tools and bounds, make it a worthwhile read for researchers interested in clustering models and information theory.
