This paper explores a clustering problem aimed at reconstructing the true labels of elements from potentially noisy and non-adaptive query-response data, where each only associates a few elements. It derives a lower bound on the number of queries required to reconstruct the ground-truth labels accurately or partially in various scenarios. It also proposes a so-called "end-query" algorithm that applies to the arbitrary k-cluster case, and evaluates the algorithm's performance under real data sets.

While this paper attempts to provide theoretical analysis for various scenarios and develop an algorithm with real-data simulations, it comes with limitations compared to the claimed results and clarity of the related work. Important changes and possibly new developments are likely to be made for publication. See below for detailed comments.

1. (It is not allowed to repeat the same question): This constraint seems a bit restrictive. Also, it may reduce performance compared to the no-constraint case, as allowing the same query is a natural way to deal with noise effects. A reasonable way to justify the restricted model might be to provide a broader set of real-data simulations with comparisons to an algorithm designed for the no-constraint case, and possibly show better performance of the proposed algorithm. If not, it is more appropriate to examine the no-constraint case instead. The authors are concerned about the independence-vs-dependence issue for multiple answers regarding the same query, which can make the analysis challenging. But this issue can be resolved by focusing on a setting in which the same query is given to different annotators and therefore the answers are considered independent of the annotators.

2. (Theorem 1): A noise-free setting with accurate retrieval was also recently studied in a work [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which also considers XOR-operations ('same-cluster' query) under a non-adaptive measurement setting. But the result in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] does not match the result claimed in Theorem 1. For example, when p=1/2 and \Delta is a constant, the query complexity in Theorem 1 reads m=n/log2, while in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] it reads m=nlogn/\Delta. This needs to be clarified in detail. Furthermore, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to understand.

3. (Theorem 3): It is difficult to interpret the expression of the lower bound on \delta. In particular, it is difficult to estimate the limit on m in terms of n and \delta, which one can see directly due to the improvement in performance caused by the relaxed constraint reflected in \delta.

4. (Theorem 4): The same comment given in relation to Theorem 3 applies here. Furthermore, how different is the upper bound from the lower bound in Theorem 3? Can the difference be unlimited? Again, the proof is difficult to understand.

5. (Figure 2): No comparison has been made regarding the same-cluster query method - only the same-cluster-query is compared to the 'lower' bound. Thinking about whether there is even a crossing point compared to the same-cluster-query 'method'. If so, is there any intuition behind it? In fact, the paper claims that the proposed approach performs better than the same-cluster query scheme, which cannot be supported by Figure 2.

6. (Algorithm 1): The proposed algorithm requires prior knowledge of the p or relative sizes of the clusters (from the perspective of Theorem 6). How to obtain such information in practice? A reasonable justification can validate the practicality of the algorithm. Also, the pseudo-code is difficult to understand without relying on a detailed proof of Theorem 5, which is also difficult to understand.

7. (Figures 3 and 4 and 5): No comparison has been made regarding the state of arts like the same-cluster-query method, which is important to support the claim made in several places: the end-query algorithm performs better than the same-cluster-query scheme.

[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community Retrieval in Hypergraphs," Proceedings of the Elerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
