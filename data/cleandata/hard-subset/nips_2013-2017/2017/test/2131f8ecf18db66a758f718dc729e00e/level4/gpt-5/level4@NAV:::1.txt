The paper investigates a clustering framework in which a learner can issue queries to an oracle with the goal of recovering the ground-truth clustering. The queries may be same-cluster questions (or, more generally, a fixed binary-valued function of the instance labels). The authors analyze the query complexity of this model.

Query-driven clustering is a well-motivated and relatively recent line of work, with applications in record deduplication, crowdsourcing, and related areas. The authors approach the problem from an information-theoretic perspective. In particular, they derive upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required to exactly (or approximately) recover the target clustering, and they also treat the case of a noisy oracle. The information-theoretic techniques deployed are interesting and relatively new to the ML community.

Because no structural assumptions are imposed on the problem (e.g., no explicit hypothesis class), the analysis is limited. In effect, aside from the noise level, the bounds primarily depend on the marginal distribution of the labels. Thus, the setting is somewhat removed from the standard learning-theoretic frameworks. Even so, this represents an important first step toward understanding the information-theoretic aspects of query-based clustering, especially the corresponding lower bounds.
