

This paper presents an interesting approach to clustering via crowdsourcing, focusing on recovering original data point labels from answers to similarity queries. The authors establish theoretical bounds on the number of queries required for label recovery under both noisy and noiseless settings, complemented by empirical validation. The paper is well-written and accessible to a general machine learning audience, with a particularly clear exposition of the information-theoretic connections underlying the bounds.

I have the following concerns that should be addressed to strengthen the paper:

(1) The use of AND queries appears to conflict with the core motivation of crowdsourcing for label recovery. In an AND query, the worker must know the cluster label to answer "yes" (e.g., "are these points in cluster C?"), rather than merely indicating whether the points belong to the same cluster. If the cluster label is revealed to the worker via the query structure, the need for similarity queries is undermined—why not directly ask for the label of a data point? This fundamental issue must be clarified to justify the proposed query model.

(2) The expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1–4 could be simplified while retaining their theoretical essence, enhancing readability without sacrificing rigor.

(3) The experimental section exclusively evaluates AND queries. This is problematic unless the clarification in point (1) resolves the conceptual conflict, as the experimental validity depends on the query model’s justification.

I am satisfied with the authors' response to these concerns and recommend acceptance.
