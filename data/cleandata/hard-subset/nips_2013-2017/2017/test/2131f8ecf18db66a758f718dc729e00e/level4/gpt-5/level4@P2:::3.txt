This paper studies semisupervised clustering via crowdsourcing under a query model and establishes a tight connection to locally encodable source coding. The setting allows nonadaptive queries that touch at most a constant number Δ of elements and return binary (or k-ary) answers, possibly with noise. The authors show:

- Noiseless, exact recovery (k = 2): with pairwise XOR (“same-cluster”) queries viewed through a local encoding lens, one can recover all labels with high probability using fewer than n queries for a range of priors p, and they give an information-theoretic lower bound that improves on the classical nH(p) bound in some regimes.

- Noiseless, approximate recovery: they derive new lower bounds when only a (1 − δ) fraction of labels must be correct, and prove that XOR queries are fundamentally suboptimal for this goal. They propose pairwise AND queries that achieve a better query–accuracy trade-off.

- Noisy, approximate recovery (k ≥ 2): with a k-ary AND-style query and a simple thresholding decoder on a d-regular design, they recover a (1 − δ) fraction of labels with m = O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive queries under independent per-query noise.

They also report a small crowdsourcing experiment (movie genres) indicating that AND queries are easier for workers (lower error) than same-cluster queries, aligning with their theory.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on and contrasts with recent work on clustering with same-cluster queries (e.g., Ashtiani et al., NIPS), on noisy crowdsourcing models, and on local coding/decoding and rate–distortion. The locally encodable source-coding perspective appears novel for this problem and yields sharper lower bounds and new algorithmic insights.

Strengths
- Clear and well-organized exposition for a general ML audience; the information-theoretic framing is well motivated and instructive.
- Conceptual novelty: the equivalence to locally encodable source coding and the use of rate–distortion insights to bound approximate recovery.
- Technical contributions: new lower bounds beyond nH(p); identification of XOR limitations; AND-based schemes with provable advantages; extension to noisy, k-class settings with near-optimal query complexity O(n log(k/δ)).
- Empirical evidence (albeit limited) that AND queries are more reliable for human workers than XOR same-cluster questions.

Concerns and suggestions
1) Practicality of AND queries for crowdsourcing: An AND query implicitly reveals and requires the semantic label (e.g., “are both action?”), whereas same-cluster queries ask only about similarity. If workers can name labels, why not ask for each item’s label directly? The paper should articulate when AND queries are preferable to per-item labeling (e.g., budget constraints, better human accuracy on pairwise predicates, or when repeat queries are disallowed and aggregation is limited). After the rebuttal, I am satisfied with the authors’ justification that AND queries can yield fewer total interactions and lower error than direct labeling in their noise model and budget setting.

2) Complexity of expressions: Some bounds (Theorems 1–4) are presented in heavy notation. It would help to simplify or provide cleaner corollaries (e.g., explicit constants/ranges, asymptotic forms for common p, Δ, and δ) to improve readability without losing essence.

3) Experiments: Initially it appeared only AND queries were evaluated. The authors clarified the comparison and provided additional context on worker error rates for same-cluster queries and a synthetic comparison. The experimental section remains small in scale; adding baselines with per-item labeling under the same budget and error model, plus sensitivity to q and class imbalance, would further strengthen the case. Nonetheless, the current results are consistent with the theory.

Quality
- The theoretical results are carefully motivated and appear sound; key proofs are sketched with details in the appendix. The noisy-case analysis and concentration arguments are standard and appropriate. Assumptions (independent query noise, nonadaptive design, no repeated queries) are clearly stated and tied to the application.

Clarity
- Generally well written and accessible. Consider streamlining the statements of main theorems and highlighting key takeaways (e.g., m = O(n log(k/δ)) with constants) in the main text.

Originality
- High. The locally encodable source-coding perspective and the identification of AND queries as superior to XOR for approximate recovery under local constraints are new and insightful.

Significance
- The results advance understanding of the fundamental query complexity of crowdsourced clustering and should influence the design of practical query schemes. The methods and lower bounds are likely to be of interest beyond this specific problem.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel theoretical framing and results; clear advances over XOR-based schemes.
- Addresses both noiseless and noisy settings; provides near-optimal nonadaptive algorithms.
- Empirical evidence supports the human-factors motivation for AND queries.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental validation is limited in scale and breadth.
- Practical trade-off vs. direct labeling could be discussed more thoroughly.
- Some bounds could be simplified for readability.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper makes strong, novel theoretical contributions with clear implications for crowdsourced clustering, and the authors have addressed the main concerns raised.
