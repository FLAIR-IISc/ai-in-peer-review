This paper studies a clustering problem whose goal is to reconstruct the true labels of elements from possibly noisy, non-adaptive queries, each involving only a few elements. It derives lower bounds on the number of queries required for exact or partial recovery across several scenarios. It also proposes an “AND-queries” algorithm applicable to the general k-cluster case and evaluates its performance on a real dataset.

While the paper targets a broad set of scenarios and includes both theory and an algorithm with experiments, it has notable limitations in presentation, clarity of the claimed results, and comparison to related work. Substantial revisions, and possibly additional developments, are needed for publication. Detailed comments follow.

1. Prohibition on repeating the same questions: This constraint seems overly restrictive and may worsen performance relative to the unrestricted case, since repeating queries is a natural way to mitigate noise. A proper justification would include extensive real-data simulations comparing against an algorithm that allows repetition, potentially showing the proposed algorithm performs better. If not, the unrestricted model seems more appropriate to study. The authors appear concerned about dependence across multiple answers to the same query, which complicates analysis. This can be addressed by assigning the same query to distinct annotators and assuming independent answers across annotators.

2. Theorem 1: The noiseless, exact-recovery setting was also studied in [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which considers XOR (“same-cluster”) queries under a non-adaptive measurement setting. The result in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] does not match Theorem 1. For example, when p = 1/2 and Δ is constant, Theorem 1 yields query complexity m = n / log 2, whereas [Ahn-Lee-Suh] gives m = n log n / Δ. This discrepancy needs a detailed clarification (including assumptions, models, and constants). Moreover, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow.

3. Theorem 3: The expression of the lower bound on δ is hard to interpret. In particular, it is difficult to invert the result to obtain a bound on m in terms of n and δ, which would directly reveal the performance gain from the relaxed constraint (captured by δ).

4. Theorem 4: The same interpretability concern as in Theorem 3 applies here. In addition, how does this upper bound compare with the lower bound in Theorem 3? Can the gap be unbounded? Again, the proof is difficult to follow.

5. Figure 2: There is no comparison to the same-cluster query method—only to its lower bound. It would be important to see whether there is also a crossing point when comparing to the same-cluster query method itself; if so, please provide intuition. As it stands, the paper claims the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster query scheme, but Figure 2 does not support this claim.

6. Algorithm 1: The algorithm appears to require prior knowledge of p or the relative cluster sizes (per Theorem 6). How would such information be obtained in practice? A clear justification is needed to establish practicality. In addition, the pseudocode is hard to follow without relying on the detailed proof of Theorem 5, which is itself challenging to digest.

7. Figures 3–5: There are no comparisons to state-of-the-art baselines such as the same-cluster query method, which is essential to support the repeated claim that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster query scheme.

[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, “Community recovery in hypergraphs,” Proceedings of the Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
