This paper explores a clustering problem where the goal is to reconstruct true labels from potentially noisy and non-adaptive query-answers, with each query associating only a few elements. The paper derives lower bounds on the number of queries required to reconstruct ground-truth labels exactly or partially, across a variety of scenarios. It also proposes an algorithm based on “AND-queries” applicable to arbitrary k-cluster cases, and evaluates its performance using a real dataset.

While the paper attempts to provide theoretical analyses for various scenarios and develops an algorithm with real-data simulation, it suffers from limitations in presentation, clarity of the claimed results, and comparison to related work. Significant revisions, and potentially further development, are needed before publication. Detailed comments are provided below.

1. (Query Repetition): The restriction against repeating queries appears somewhat limiting. It may also degrade performance compared to allowing repetition, as repeating questions is a natural way to mitigate noise. A strong justification for this restriction would be an extensive set of real-data simulations comparing the proposed algorithm to one designed for the unrestricted case, demonstrating superior performance. If that is not achievable, investigating the unrestricted case may be more fruitful. The authors express concern about the independence-vs-dependence issue for multiple answers to the same query, which complicates analysis. However, this could be addressed by focusing on a setting where the same query is presented to distinct annotators, assuming their responses are independent.

2. (Theorem 1): The noiseless setting with exact recovery is also studied in a recent work [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which considers XOR-operation ('same-cluster') queries under a non-adaptive measurement setting. However, the result in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] does not align with the claim in Theorem 1. For example, when p=1/2 and Δ is a constant, Theorem 1 states a query complexity of m=n/log2, while [Ahn-Lee-Suh] states m=nlogn/Δ. This discrepancy requires detailed clarification. Furthermore, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow.

3. (Theorem 3): The expression for the lower bound on δ is difficult to interpret. Specifically, it’s challenging to infer the bound on m in terms of n and δ, which would directly illustrate the performance improvement resulting from the relaxed constraint, represented by δ.

4. (Theorem 4): The same comment regarding the interpretability of the bound applies here as with Theorem 3. Additionally, what is the gap between the upper and lower bounds in Theorem 3? Can this gap be unbounded? Again, the proof is difficult to follow.

5. (Figure 2): The figure lacks a comparison to the same-cluster query method; it only compares to the same-cluster-query lower bound. Is there a crossover point when compared to the same-cluster-query *method*? If so, what is the intuition behind it? The paper claims the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster query scheme, but this claim is not supported by Figure 2.

6. (Algorithm 1): The proposed algorithm requires prior knowledge of p or the relative sizes of the clusters (as noted in relation to Theorem 6). How can this information be obtained in practice? Justification of the algorithm’s practicality is needed. The pseudo-code is also difficult to follow without detailed understanding of the proof of Theorem 5, which is itself challenging to grasp.

7. (Figures 3, 4 & 5): No comparison is made to state-of-the-art methods, such as the same-cluster-query method. This comparison is crucial for supporting the claim, repeated throughout the paper, that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme.

[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
