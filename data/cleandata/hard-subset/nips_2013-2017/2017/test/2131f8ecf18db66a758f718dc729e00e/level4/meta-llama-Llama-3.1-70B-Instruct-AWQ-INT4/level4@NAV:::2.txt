This paper addresses a clustering problem that aims to recover the true labels of elements from potentially noisy and non-adaptive query-answers, each of which involves only a few elements. It establishes lower bounds on the number of queries required to reconstruct the ground-truth labels exactly or partially in various scenarios. Additionally, it proposes an "AND-queries" algorithm that can be applied to the general k-cluster case and evaluates the algorithm's performance using a real data set.

While the paper provides theoretical analyses for multiple scenarios and develops an algorithm with real-data simulation, it has limitations in terms of presentation, clarity of the results, and comparison to related works. Significant revisions and potential new developments are necessary for publication. The following detailed comments are provided:

1. The constraint of not repeating the same questions appears restrictive and may degrade performance compared to the unrestricted case, as allowing repeated questions is a natural way to mitigate the noise effect. To justify this restricted model, the authors could provide an extensive set of real-data simulations comparing the proposed algorithm to one tailored for the unrestricted case, demonstrating the proposed algorithm's superior performance. Alternatively, investigating the unrestricted case may be more plausible. The authors' concern about the independence vs. dependence issue for multiple answers to the same query can be addressed by focusing on a setting where the same query is given to distinct annotators, assuming independent answers.

2. The noiseless setting with exact recovery was also explored in a recent work [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which considers the XOR-operation ('same-cluster' queries) under a non-adaptive measurement setting. However, the result in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] does not align with the one claimed in Theorem 1. For instance, when p=1/2 and Δ is a constant, the query complexity in Theorem 1 is m=n/log2, whereas the one in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] is m=nlogn/Δ. This discrepancy requires clarification. Furthermore, the proof of Theorem 1 is challenging to follow.

3. The expression of the lower bound on δ in Theorem 3 is difficult to interpret, making it hard to infer the bound on m in terms of n and δ. This hinders the direct observation of performance improvement due to the relaxed constraint, reflected in δ.

4. The same comment regarding Theorem 3 applies to Theorem 4. Additionally, the gap between the upper bound and the lower bound in Theorem 3 can be unbounded. The proof is also difficult to follow.

5. Figure 2 lacks a comparison to the same-cluster query method, only comparing to the same-cluster-query 'lower' bound. It is unclear whether there is a crossing point when compared to the same-cluster-query 'method'. The paper claims that the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster query scheme, but this cannot be supported by Figure 2.

6. Algorithm 1 requires prior knowledge of p or the relative sizes of clusters (in view of Theorem 6). It is unclear how to obtain such information in practice, and a proper justification is needed to validate the algorithm's practicality. The pseudo-code is also challenging to follow without relying on the detailed proof of Theorem 5, which is also difficult to grasp.

7. Figures 3, 4, and 5 lack a comparison to state-of-the-art methods, such as the same-cluster-query method, which is crucial to support the claim that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme.

Reference:
[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
