

This paper establishes a novel connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding in information theory. The authors consider a crowdsourcing-based clustering problem where an oracle provides answers to queries about element relationships, with the goal of recovering true cluster labels using minimal queries. They demonstrate that traditional "same cluster" queries have fundamental limitations, and propose AND queries as a superior alternative that achieves better query complexity bounds for both noiseless and noisy settings. The paper provides theoretical bounds and validates them through a real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

The paper excels in clarity and presentation. The connection between clustering and information theory is elegantly explained, making sophisticated theoretical concepts accessible to a broad machine learning audience. The authors carefully situate their work within the NIPS literature on clustering with side information (e.g., [4, 8, 20, 21, 33]) and relate it to the underexplored area of locally encodable source coding in information theory (notably building on [23] while distinguishing from locally decodable codes [19, 16]). The problem setup, theoretical results, and experimental design are logically organized and well-motivated.

In terms of quality, the theoretical contributions are sound and well-supported. The authors provide both upper and lower bounds for query complexity across multiple scenarios (exact recovery, approximate recovery, noisy settings), and the experimental validation with real crowdsourcing data strengthens their theoretical claims. The paper carefully addresses limitations and provides detailed analysis of both the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.

The originality is strong. While crowdsourcing-based clustering has been studied in NIPS (e.g., [4, 21, 33]), the connection to locally encodable source coding and the introduction of AND queries as a fundamental improvement over traditional "same cluster" queries represent a significant theoretical advance. The paper properly contextualizes its contributions against related work in both machine learning and information theory.

The significance is demonstrated through substantial query complexity improvements (e.g., reducing from O(n log n) to O(n log 1/δ) queries) and practical validation. The work advances the state of the art by showing how information theory can provide fundamental limits for crowdsourcing-based clustering, with clear implications for real-world applications.

I have three specific concerns that would further strengthen the paper:

1. The motivation for AND queries requires clarification. The paper claims AND queries outperform "same cluster" queries, but it's unclear why workers would be willing to answer AND queries (which require knowledge of both labels) when they could simply be asked for labels directly. This is a critical point that needs elaboration to justify the approach. The authors should explain why asking for labels directly isn't sufficient, how AND queries provide advantages that outweigh the cognitive cost, and why workers would find AND queries more feasible than label queries.

2. The theoretical bounds (Theorems 1-4) contain complex expressions that could be simplified while preserving their essential meaning. While mathematically precise, the current formulations make it difficult to grasp practical implications. The authors could provide asymptotic simplifications or more intuitive explanations of the key dependencies.

3. The experimental validation focuses exclusively on AND queries without comparing to "same cluster" queries. Given the authors' claim that AND queries are superior, the experiments should include a direct comparison between the two query types to validate this advantage. The current experiment with real crowdsourcing only tests AND queries, which doesn't address the comparative claim.

These concerns can be addressed with relatively minor revisions. The paper is already strong in theoretical contribution and presentation, and the experimental results are promising. With clarification on the AND query motivation and a more comprehensive experimental comparison, the paper would be even stronger.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes a meaningful connection between clustering and information theory. The work is technically sound, well-written, and represents a significant advance in understanding query complexity for clustering with side information. I recommend acceptance.
