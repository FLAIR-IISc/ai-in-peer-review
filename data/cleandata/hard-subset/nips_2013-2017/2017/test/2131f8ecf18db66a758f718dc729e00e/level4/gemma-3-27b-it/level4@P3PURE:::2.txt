This paper explores a clustering problem where the goal is to reconstruct true labels from potentially noisy and non-adaptive query-answers, each associating only a few elements. It derives lower bounds on the number of queries required to reconstruct ground-truth labels exactly or partially in various scenarios. The paper also proposes an “AND-queries” algorithm applicable to arbitrary k-cluster cases and evaluates its performance on a real dataset.

While the paper attempts to provide theoretical analyses for various scenarios alongside an algorithm and real-data simulation, it suffers from limitations in presentation, clarity of results, and comparison to related works. Significant revisions and potentially further developments are needed for publication. Detailed comments are provided below.

1. (Query Restriction): The constraint prohibiting repeated questions appears restrictive. It may degrade performance compared to allowing repetitions, as repeating questions is a natural way to mitigate noise. A strong justification for this restriction requires an extensive set of real-data simulations comparing the proposed algorithm to one tailored for the no-restriction case, demonstrating a performance advantage. If such an advantage isn’t shown, the no-restriction case is more compelling to investigate. The authors express concern regarding independence versus dependence issues with multiple answers to the same query, which complicates analysis. However, this issue could be addressed by focusing on a setting where the same query is posed to distinct annotators, assuming independence of their responses.

2. (Theorem 1): The noiseless setting with exact recovery is also considered in a recent work [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which uses the XOR-operation ('same-cluster' queries) under a non-adaptive measurement setting. However, the result in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] does not align with the result claimed in Theorem 1. For example, when p=1/2 and Δ is a constant, Theorem 1 states a query complexity of m=n/log2, while [Ahn-Lee-Suh] states m=n log n/Δ. This discrepancy requires detailed clarification. Furthermore, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow.

3. (Theorem 3): The expression for the lower bound on δ is difficult to interpret. Specifically, it’s challenging to infer the bound on m (number of queries) in terms of n (number of elements) and δ, which would directly reveal the performance improvement resulting from the relaxed constraint, reflected in δ.

4. (Theorem 4): The same comment applies to the interpretation of the lower bound in Theorem 4. Additionally, what is the gap between the upper and lower bounds in Theorem 3? Can this gap be unbounded? The proof is also difficult to follow.

5. (Figure 2): The figure lacks a comparison to the same-cluster query method; it only compares to the same-cluster-query lower bound. Is there a crossing point when compared to the same-cluster-query *method*? If so, what is the intuition behind it? The paper claims the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster query scheme, but this is not supported by Figure 2.

6. (Algorithm 1): The proposed algorithm requires prior knowledge of p or the relative sizes of clusters (as indicated by Theorem 6). How can this information be obtained in practice? Justification of the algorithm’s practicality is needed. Moreover, the pseudo-code is difficult to understand without referring to the detailed (and also difficult to grasp) proof of Theorem 5.

7. (Figures 3, 4 & 5): No comparison is made to state-of-the-art methods, such as the same-cluster-query method, which is crucial for supporting the claim – made in several places – that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme.

[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
