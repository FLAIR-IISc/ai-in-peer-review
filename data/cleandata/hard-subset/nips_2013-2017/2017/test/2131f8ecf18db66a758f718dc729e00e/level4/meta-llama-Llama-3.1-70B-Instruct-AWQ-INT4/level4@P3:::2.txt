This paper explores a clustering problem where the goal is to reconstruct the true labels of elements from possibly noisy and non-adaptive query answers, each associating only a few elements. The authors derive lower bounds on the number of queries required to reconstruct the ground-truth labels exactly or partially in a variety of scenarios. They also propose a so-called "AND-queries" algorithm that applies to the arbitrary k-cluster case and evaluates the performance of the algorithm under a real data set.

The paper relates to previous work in clustering, crowdsourcing, and information theory. The problem setup is similar to that of budgeted crowdsourcing problems, which can be viewed as a canonical source-coding or source-channel coding problem from information theory. The authors' main contribution is to view this as a locally encodable source coding problem, where each compressed bit depends only on a constant number of input bits. This terminology is analogous to the recently developed literature on locally decodable source coding.

The authors provide new information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required to reconstruct the labels exactly and approximately. They also show that even within queries with binary answers, 'same cluster' queries may not be the best possible choice. Instead, they propose using an AND query, which performs substantially better in many situations.

The paper's main strength is its theoretical contribution to the field of clustering and information theory. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem and derive new lower bounds on the number of queries required to reconstruct the labels. The proposed AND-queries algorithm is also a significant contribution, as it applies to the arbitrary k-cluster case and has been shown to perform well in practice.

However, the paper has some limitations in presentation, clarity of the claimed results, and comparison to related works. The constraint of not repeating the same question seems a bit restricted and may degrade the performance compared to the no-restriction case. The authors should provide more justification for this constraint and possibly compare their algorithm to one tailored for the no-restriction case.

The proof of Theorem 1 is hard to follow, and the result does not coincide with that of a recent work [Ahn-Lee-Suh]. The authors should clarify this discrepancy and provide more details on the proof. The expression of the lower bound on δ in Theorem 3 is also difficult to interpret, and it is hard to infer the bound on m in terms of n and δ.

The paper claims that the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster query scheme, but this is not supported by Figure 2. The authors should include a comparison to the same-cluster query method in the figure and provide more intuition behind the results.

The proposed algorithm requires prior knowledge on p or the relative sizes of clusters. The authors should provide more justification for this requirement and explain how to obtain such information in practice. The pseudo-code is also hard to follow without relying on the detailed proof of Theorem 5.

The paper's experimental results are promising, but the authors should include more comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, such as the same-cluster query method. This would help to support the claim that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms other methods.

In conclusion, while the paper makes significant theoretical contributions to the field of clustering and information theory, it has some limitations in presentation and comparison to related works. The authors should address these issues in a revision to make the paper more convincing and clear.
