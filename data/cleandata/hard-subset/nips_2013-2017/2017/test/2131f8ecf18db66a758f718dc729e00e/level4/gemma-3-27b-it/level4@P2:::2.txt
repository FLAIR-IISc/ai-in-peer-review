This paper investigates a clustering problem where the goal is to reconstruct true labels from potentially noisy, non-adaptive query answers, each involving a limited number of elements. It derives lower bounds on the number of queries needed for exact or partial reconstruction and proposes an "AND-queries" algorithm applicable to arbitrary k-cluster cases, validating its performance with a real-world dataset.

While the paper attempts theoretical analysis and algorithm development with empirical validation, it suffers from issues in presentation, clarity of results, and comparison to existing work. Significant revisions, and potentially further development, are likely needed for publication.

The core idea – framing semisupervised clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem – is interesting and potentially valuable. However, the presentation makes it difficult to fully assess the contribution. The paper is dense and lacks intuitive explanations for several key claims.

**Summary of the Approach:** The paper establishes a connection between semisupervised clustering with limited queries and locally encodable source coding. This connection allows for the derivation of information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries needed for label recovery. The authors propose using AND queries as an alternative to standard "same cluster" (XOR) queries, arguing that AND queries offer advantages, particularly in noisy scenarios. They validate this claim through experiments on a crowdsourced dataset.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Connection:** The framing of the problem as locally encodable source coding is a novel and insightful connection.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper attempts to provide rigorous theoretical analysis, deriving lower bounds on query complexity.
*   **Real-World Validation:** The authors include experiments with real data collected from Amazon Mechanical Turk, demonstrating the practical relevance of their approach.
*   **AND-Query Proposal:** The proposal of AND queries as a potentially more efficient alternative to XOR queries is a promising direction.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Restricted Model:** The constraint prohibiting repetition of queries is arguably limiting. While justified by avoiding complexities with independent answer analysis, a comparison to algorithms allowing repetition would strengthen the work. The claim that this approach is beneficial in noisy settings without comparative evidence is weak.
*   **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper is difficult to follow. Many claims are made without sufficient explanation or intuition. Proofs are dense and lack sufficient detail, making it hard to assess their correctness.
*   **Comparison to Related Work:** The comparison to existing work is insufficient. The paper acknowledges related work (e.g., [Ahn-Lee-Suh]) but does not adequately address discrepancies in results or clearly position the contribution relative to the state-of-the-art. The authors should explicitly compare their method with existing clustering algorithms, especially those using 'same-cluster' queries, both theoretically and empirically.
*   **Result Interpretation:** The interpretation of results, particularly regarding the lower bounds (Theorems 3 & 4), is unclear. It is difficult to extract concrete implications about query complexity and performance gains.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The requirement for prior knowledge of cluster sizes (or relative label frequencies) poses a practical challenge. The paper needs to discuss how this information can be obtained in real-world scenarios.
*   **Missing Baseline:** The experimental results lack a crucial baseline: a comparison to a well-implemented ‘same cluster’ query-based clustering algorithm (e.g., spectral clustering) on the same dataset. This makes it difficult to evaluate the effectiveness of the AND-query approach.

**Specific Comments & Questions:**

*   **Theorem 1:** The discrepancy with the results from [Ahn-Lee-Suh] needs to be thoroughly investigated and explained. The proof requires significant clarification.
*   **Theorems 3 & 4:** The presented lower bounds are difficult to interpret in terms of practical query complexity.  A clearer connection between the derived bounds and achievable query numbers is needed.  What is the gap between the upper and lower bounds?
*   **Figure 2:** The figure lacks a comparison to the performance of the 'same cluster' query *method*, not just the lower bound. This omission weakens the claim that the proposed method outperforms the XOR approach.
*   **Algorithm 1:** The algorithm's reliance on prior knowledge and the process of obtaining that knowledge needs to be discussed in more detail. A more readable pseudo-code would improve understanding.
*   **Figures 3, 4, & 5:** The absence of a comparison to state-of-the-art clustering methods (e.g., spectral clustering using 'same cluster' queries) severely limits the impact of these results.
*   **Nature Paper Reference:** The claim regarding the Nature paper's findings on query repetition needs further clarification and a proper citation.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is a novel and interesting insight.
*   The paper demonstrates a theoretical understanding of the problem and attempts to derive meaningful lower bounds.
*   The experimental validation, while limited, provides some evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The presentation is unclear, and the proofs are difficult to follow.
*   The comparison to related work is insufficient.
*   The experimental evaluation lacks a crucial baseline, making it difficult to assess the practical benefits of the proposed method.
*   The restriction on query repetition is not adequately justified.
*   The reliance on prior knowledge about cluster sizes is a practical limitation that needs to be addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field, but it requires significant revisions. The authors need to address the clarity issues, provide a more comprehensive comparison to related work, and strengthen the experimental validation. Without these improvements, the paper falls short of NIPS standards. I would recommend **Reject** with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. The core ideas are promising, but the current presentation and lack of thorough evaluation hinder a positive assessment.
