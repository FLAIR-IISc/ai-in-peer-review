Review - This paper studies a clustering problem whose goal is to recover the true labels of elements from potentially noisy, non-adaptive query responses, where each query involves only a few elements. It establishes lower bounds on the number of queries required for exact or partial recovery across several scenarios. It also introduces an “AND-queries” algorithm for arbitrary k-cluster settings and evaluates its performance on a real dataset.
While the paper aims to provide theoretical analyses under various settings and develops an algorithm with real-data experiments, it has limitations in presentation, clarity of the claims, and comparisons to related work. Significant revisions—and possibly new developments—appear necessary for publication. Detailed comments follow.
1. (Repeating the same questions is not allowed): This constraint seems overly restrictive. It may also degrade performance relative to the unrestricted setting, since allowing repeated queries is a natural way to combat noise. A persuasive justification would be to present extensive real-data simulations comparing against an algorithm tailored to the unrestricted case, ideally showing better performance of the proposed method. If not, the unrestricted model seems more appropriate to study. The authors seem concerned with independence-versus-dependence for multiple answers to the same query, which may complicate the analysis, but this can be addressed by posing identical queries to distinct annotators so the responses can be assumed independent.
2. (Theorem 1): Exact recovery in the noiseless regime was also studied in [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which considers XOR (“same-cluster”) queries under a non-adaptive measurement setting. However, the result there does not match Theorem 1. For example, when p=1/2 and Δ is a constant, Theorem 1 gives m = n/log 2, whereas [Ahn-Lee-Suh] gives m = n log n / Δ. This discrepancy needs a detailed explanation. Moreover, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow.
3. (Theorem 3): The lower bound on δ is presented in a form that is hard to interpret. In particular, it is difficult to extract a bound on m in terms of n and δ, which would make the performance gains from relaxing the constraint (via δ) immediately apparent.
4. (Theorem 4): The same interpretability issue as in Theorem 3 applies here. In addition, how does this upper bound compare with the lower bound in Theorem 3? Can the gap be unbounded? Again, the proof is hard to follow.
5. (Figure 2): There is no comparison to an actual same-cluster query method—only to a same-cluster-query lower bound. Is there also a crossing point when compared to a concrete same-cluster-query method? If so, what is the intuition? As presented, the claim that the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster scheme is not supported by Figure 2.
6. (Algorithm 1): The algorithm appears to require prior knowledge of p or the relative cluster sizes (per Theorem 6). How would this information be obtained in practice? A clear justification would help establish practicality. Also, the pseudocode is hard to interpret without relying on the detailed proof of Theorem 5, which is itself challenging to digest.
7. (Figure 3 & 4 & 5): There is no comparison to state-of-the-art baselines such as a same-cluster-query method, which is essential to support the repeated claim that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms same-cluster-query schemes.
[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, “Community recovery in hypergraphs,” Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
