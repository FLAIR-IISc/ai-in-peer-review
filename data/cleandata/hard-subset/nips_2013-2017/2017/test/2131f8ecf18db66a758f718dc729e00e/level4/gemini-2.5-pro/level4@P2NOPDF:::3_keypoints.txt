- Paper studies crowdsourced clustering and derives query complexity bounds for recovering true labels using similarity queries (noiseless and noisy).
- Primary contribution is theoretical, supported by empirical simulations.
- Well-written with clear exposition and a strong connection to information-theoretic analysis.
- Major concern: practical motivation for "AND" queries is unclear—an affirmative AND response seems to reveal cluster identity, undermining the crowdsourcing premise.
- If workers can identify clusters (as implied by AND), asking for single-point labels would be simpler—need real-world scenarios where AND queries are natural.
- Theoretical bounds (Theorems 1–4) are complex and lack intuition; request simplified/asymptotic corollaries that highlight dominant terms and parameter dependencies.
- Experiments use only simulated AND queries, so empirical validation depends on justifying the AND model’s practical relevance.
- Reviewer leans toward acceptance but final decision hinges on the authors convincingly addressing the AND-query motivation and clarifying theoretical/empirical clarity.
