Summary
This paper studies a clustering problem in which the goal is to recover true labels from possibly noisy, non-adaptive queries, each involving only a small number of elements. It derives lower bounds on the number of queries required for exact or partial recovery across several scenarios, proposes an “AND-queries” algorithm applicable to arbitrary k, and evaluates its performance on a real dataset.

While the paper aims to offer broad theoretical analysis alongside an algorithm and empirical study, it currently has significant issues with presentation, clarity of claims, and comparison to related work. Substantial revisions—and possibly additional results—are needed. Detailed comments follow.

Major comments
1. Model restriction on repeating queries
- The prohibition on repeating the same query feels overly restrictive and likely suboptimal in the presence of noise—repetition is a natural tool for denoising.
- To justify this restricted model, the paper should present extensive real-data experiments comparing the proposed method against algorithms that allow repeated queries. If the proposed approach still performs better, that would support the modeling choice; otherwise, analyzing the no-restriction case seems more compelling.
- The stated concern about dependence across repeated answers complicating analysis can be mitigated by a setting where the same query is posed to independent annotators, rendering the responses independent.

2. Theorem 1 (noiseless exact recovery) and relation to prior work
- The noiseless, exact-recovery setting with non-adaptive, “same-cluster” (XOR) queries was also analyzed in [Ahn–Lee–Suh]. The result claimed in Theorem 1 does not appear to match theirs. For example, when p = 1/2 and Δ is constant, Theorem 1 seems to imply m = n / log 2, whereas [Ahn–Lee–Suh] gives m = n log n / Δ. This discrepancy needs a careful reconciliation: specify assumptions, models, and query types precisely, and explain why the bounds differ.
- The proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow as written. A clearer proof outline and a more transparent connection to existing literature would help.

3. Theorem 3 (lower bound with partial recovery)
- The expression for the lower bound on δ is hard to interpret. It should be reparameterized to give a clean bound on m in terms of n and δ, making it easy to see how allowing a fraction δ of errors translates into reduced query complexity.
- More intuition and a compact, user-facing statement (e.g., “to achieve error rate δ, m must scale at least as ...”) would significantly improve readability.

4. Theorem 4 (upper bound) and gap to Theorem 3
- The same interpretability issue as in Theorem 3 applies: present the upper bound explicitly as m in terms of n and δ, so the benefit of relaxing exact recovery is transparent.
- Quantify the gap between the upper bound in Theorem 4 and the lower bound in Theorem 3. Is the gap bounded or can it be arbitrarily large? If large gaps exist, what are the dominant sources (constants, log factors, mismatch in regimes)?
- As with Theorem 1, the proof is challenging to parse. A streamlined proof sketch and a summary of the key technical ideas would be valuable.

5. Figure 2 (comparison to same-cluster queries)
- The figure compares the proposed method to a lower bound for same-cluster queries, not to an actual same-cluster-query algorithm. Thus, it cannot substantiate the claim that the proposed approach outperforms the same-cluster scheme.
- Please include a direct comparison to a concrete same-cluster-query method. Is there a crossing point in performance curves? If so, provide intuition for when and why the proposed approach wins or loses.
- As stated, the paper’s claim of outperforming same-cluster queries is not supported by Figure 2.

6. Algorithm 1 (assumptions and clarity)
- The algorithm appears to require prior knowledge of p or of the relative cluster sizes (cf. Theorem 6). How would one obtain this information in practice? Please provide a practical procedure or justification to make the method usable.
- The pseudocode is difficult to follow without studying the proof of Theorem 5, which itself is hard to digest. Improve the algorithmic description so it is self-contained and implementable without delving into the full proof.

7. Figures 3, 4, and 5 (empirical validation)
- There is no empirical comparison to state-of-the-art baselines such as the same-cluster-query method. Such comparisons are essential to support repeated claims that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms same-cluster queries.
- Please add head-to-head evaluations under matched budgets and noise levels, with clear reporting of the regimes where each method excels.

Reference
[Ahn–Lee–Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, “Community recovery in hypergraphs,” Proceedings of the Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
