This paper investigates a clustering problem focused on recovering the ground-truth labels of items using potentially erroneous, non-adaptive query responses, where each query involves a small subset of items. The authors derive theoretical lower bounds on the required number of queries for both exact and partial label reconstruction across several scenarios. They also introduce an "AND-queries" algorithm, applicable to the general k-cluster case, and evaluate its performance on a real-world dataset.

While the work ambitiously combines theoretical analysis, algorithmic development, and empirical evaluation, it suffers from significant shortcomings in its presentation, the clarity of its claimed results, and its comparison to existing literature. Substantial revisions and potentially new contributions may be necessary before this work is suitable for publication. Detailed comments follow.

1.  **(Prohibition on repeating queries):** This constraint appears overly restrictive. Allowing query repetition is a natural strategy to mitigate the effects of noise, and this restriction might degrade performance. To validate this model, the authors should provide comprehensive simulations comparing their algorithm to one that permits repeated queries, demonstrating a clear performance advantage. If this is not possible, the unrestricted model appears more compelling for investigation. The authors seem concerned about dependencies between multiple answers to the same query; however, this can be addressed by considering a setting where each repeated query is posed to a different, independent annotator.

2.  **(Theorem 1):** The result for the noiseless, exact recovery setting appears to conflict with the findings in a recent work by [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which also studied "same-cluster" (XOR) queries in a non-adaptive framework. For instance, when p=1/2 and Δ is a constant, Theorem 1 suggests a query complexity of m=n/log2, whereas [Ahn-Lee-Suh] reports m=nlogn/Δ. This significant discrepancy requires a detailed clarification. Furthermore, the provided proof for Theorem 1 is difficult to follow.

3.  **(Theorem 3):** The lower bound on δ is expressed in a form that is challenging to interpret. Specifically, it is not straightforward to derive the corresponding bound on the number of queries (m) as a function of n and δ. Such a relationship is essential for understanding the performance improvements gained from the relaxed partial recovery constraint (represented by δ).

4.  **(Theorem 4):** The same comment regarding interpretability for Theorem 3 also applies to the upper bound in Theorem 4. Additionally, the relationship between this upper bound and the lower bound from Theorem 3 is unclear. Is the gap between them bounded, or can it be arbitrarily large? As with previous results, the proof is hard to follow.

5.  **(Figure 2):** The comparison in this figure is inadequate. The proposed method is compared against a *lower bound* for the same-cluster query approach, rather than an actual implementation of that method. This does not support the paper's claim of outperforming the same-cluster query scheme. It would be interesting to see if a crossover point exists when comparing against the same-cluster query *method* itself and, if so, what the intuition behind this might be. As it stands, Figure 2 fails to substantiate the claim of superiority.

6.  **(Algorithm 1):** The proposed algorithm appears to have practical limitations, as it requires prior knowledge of p or the relative cluster sizes (per Theorem 6). The authors should provide a justification for how this information could be obtained in a real-world scenario to validate the algorithm's practicality. The pseudocode is also difficult to understand without deeply referencing the proof of Theorem 5, which itself is challenging to parse.

7.  **(Figures 3, 4, & 5):** The experimental results presented in these figures are missing essential comparisons to state-of-the-art approaches, particularly the same-cluster-query method. To substantiate the paper's central claims, these figures must include such benchmarks. Without these comparisons, the assertion that the AND-queries algorithm is superior remains unsupported.

[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.

**(Additional comments)**

Following the rebuttal, some concerns have been addressed, but further clarification is needed on the following points:

1.  It remains unclear why repeating the same query is not considered beneficial. If the authors' argument relies on the cited Nature paper, they should explicitly clarify whether that work demonstrates that repetition is not useful in this context.

2.  Regarding the comparison with the same-cluster query method, the detailed discussion provided in the author response was helpful. This analysis should be incorporated directly into the revised manuscript for clarity.
