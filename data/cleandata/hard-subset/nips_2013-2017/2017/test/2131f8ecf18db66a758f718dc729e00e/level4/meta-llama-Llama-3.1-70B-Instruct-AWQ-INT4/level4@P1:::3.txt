This paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution to the task of clustering data points via crowdsourcing. The authors consider the problem of recovering original labels of data points based on the answers to similar cluster queries, proposing bounds on the number of queries required to recover the labels considering noisy as well as noiseless answers for the queries. The connection to information theory for deriving the bounds is explained nicely, making the paper accessible to a general machine learning audience.

However, I have some concerns that need to be addressed to improve the paper.

Firstly, the idea of AND queries seems to conflict with the original motivation for crowdsourcing. In the case of an AND query, effectively, the label of the cluster is also given as part of the answer rather than just answering if the data points of interest belong to the same cluster or not. If this is the case indeed, as I interpret from the paper, why would one even need to ask those similarity queries? Why not ask the label for a data point itself? This is my primary confusion regarding the contributions in this paper.

Secondly, the expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1, 2, 3, and 4 appear complex. Is it possible to simplify these expressions while keeping the essence of the results? Simplifying the expressions would make the paper more readable and easier to understand.

Lastly, the experiments seem to only consider AND queries. If so, the experimental study is in question unless the above point regarding the motivation for AND queries is clarified.

Despite these concerns, I found the paper to be well-written and enjoyable to read. The authors' response to these questions will be crucial in determining the paper's acceptance.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution to the task of clustering data points via crowdsourcing.
* The connection to information theory for deriving the bounds is explained nicely.
* The paper is well-written and accessible to a general machine learning audience.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The idea of AND queries seems to conflict with the original motivation for crowdsourcing.
* The expressions for the bounds appear complex and could be simplified.
* The experimental study is limited to AND queries, which may not be sufficient to support the paper's claims.
