This paper investigates the query complexity of a clustering problem where a learner interacts with an oracle to determine element labels. The learner poses queries (e.g., "do these two elements belong to the same cluster?") and aims to reconstruct the complete labeling with minimal queries. The authors connect this problem to locally encodable source coding from information theory, providing both upper and lower bounds on the necessary query count for perfect and approximate recovery, and extending the analysis to noisy oracle responses.

This work addresses a relevant and increasingly studied model of clustering, motivated by applications in areas like record deduplication and crowdsourcing. Framing the problem through the lens of information theory—specifically, locally encodable source coding—offers a novel perspective and allows the derivation of fundamental limits. The authors build upon recent work on querying for clustering (e.g., [4, 8, 20, 33]) and contribute new theoretical insights into the tradeoffs between query complexity, prior label distributions, and oracle noise. The investigation of AND queries as an alternative to traditional ‘same cluster’ queries is particularly interesting, and the initial experimental validation is a positive sign.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Connection:** The equivalence established between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is a key strength, bringing tools from information theory to bear on a machine learning problem.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, deriving lower bounds and establishing the performance of proposed querying schemes.
*   **AND Query Proposal:** The introduction of AND queries as a potential improvement over XOR/‘same cluster’ queries demonstrates insightful thinking and provides a concrete alternative.
*   **Noise Consideration:** Analyzing the noisy oracle setting adds practical relevance to the work.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of a real-world crowdsourcing experiment, even if preliminary, provides some empirical support for the theoretical findings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Generality vs. Specificity:** While the theoretical results are valuable, the analysis relies heavily on the prior distribution of labels. This limits the direct applicability to scenarios where such information is unavailable or unreliable. The model's reliance on this distribution, and the lack of assumptions about the underlying data structure, makes it somewhat distant from many standard learning theory settings.
*   **Practicality of AND Queries:** While the theoretical benefits of AND queries are demonstrated, the practical implications require further investigation. The experiment is a good start but needs to be expanded to fully assess their performance in more complex datasets and scenarios. The experiment’s setup, using a d-regular graph, isn’t fully explained or justified and would benefit from a more thorough discussion.
*   **Related Work Coverage:** While the authors cite relevant work, the discussion of related areas, such as active learning and submodular optimization (techniques often used for query selection), could be expanded. For example, the work of [12, 13, 15, 28, 34] could be more thoroughly integrated into the discussion.
* **Clarity:** The paper is dense and requires careful reading. Some sections could benefit from clearer explanations and more intuitive motivations. The notation can be heavy, and defining key concepts more explicitly would improve accessibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to a relevant problem.
*   The connection to locally encodable source coding is a significant contribution.
*   The theoretical results provide fundamental limits on query complexity.
*   The introduction of AND queries opens up new avenues for research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong reliance on prior knowledge about label distributions limits practical applicability.
*   The experimental validation is preliminary and needs to be strengthened.
*   The paper is dense and could benefit from improved clarity and exposition.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of query-based clustering. While the reliance on strong assumptions and the need for more extensive experimental validation are weaknesses, the novelty of the approach, the rigor of the analysis, and the potential impact of the findings outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper is **borderline accept/reject** but leaning towards **accept**, conditional on the authors addressing the concerns regarding clarity and expanding the experimental evaluation. I encourage the authors to consider streamlining the presentation and providing a more comprehensive discussion of the practical implications of their results.
