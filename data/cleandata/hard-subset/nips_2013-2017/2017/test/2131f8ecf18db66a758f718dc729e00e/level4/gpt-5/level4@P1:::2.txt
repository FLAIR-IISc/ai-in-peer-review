This paper studies multiclass labeling (clustering) from non-adaptive, local (constant-arity) queries that return possibly noisy binary or k-ary answers. It frames the problem as locally encodable source coding, derives information-theoretic lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery, and argues that pairwise “same-cluster” (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery compared to pairwise AND queries. It proposes a simple non-adaptive algorithm based on AND-type queries that, under an independent noise model, achieves (1−δ)-accurate recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries, and provides a small-scale crowdsourcing experiment (movie genre) suggesting that AND queries are empirically easier for workers than same-cluster queries.

Relation to prior work:
- The locally encodable perspective connects to locally decodable and smooth compression (e.g., [16, 19, 23, 26]) and leverages rate–distortion and Massey’s limits on linear codes [17].
- The crowdsourcing angle relates to entity resolution and noisy query models [8–15, 20–22, 27–31].
- For non-adaptive pairwise XOR queries, there is related work on hypergraph/community recovery (e.g., [Ahn–Lee–Suh], Allerton’16), as well as prior bounds for noisy pairwise clustering [21].
- The paper’s empirical comparison of AND vs. same-cluster queries is a useful complement to recent observations that majority voting can fail in practice [27].

Strengths:
- An interesting conceptual bridge between non-adaptive query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding.
- New lower bounds under locality constraints for exact and approximate recovery.
- A simple, scalable AND-query-based algorithm with clean concentration-based analysis, and an appealing O(n log(k/δ)) scaling for noisy approximate recovery.
- Real-data pilot study suggesting practical advantages of AND queries for crowdsourcing.

Weaknesses:
- Several results are difficult to parse or verify; key theorems and proofs lack clarity and sometimes appear inconsistent with related literature.
- The restriction against repeating queries is insufficiently justified and departs from common practitioner models.
- Empirical evaluation does not include strong baselines (e.g., same-cluster-based methods with robust decoders) or ablations to support claimed superiority of AND queries across regimes.
- Heavy reliance on prior knowledge (or exact relative sizes) of clusters without a clear, practical estimation protocol.

Detailed comments and questions:
1) Repetition of the same query. The prohibition on repeated queries is restrictive and may degrade performance under noise, since repetition is a standard way to reduce error. A more convincing justification would include extensive real-data comparisons against methods that allow multiple independent annotators per query (ensuring independence across repetitions). If repeated queries are truly unhelpful, please provide stronger empirical evidence and a clear argument (the current citation to a Nature paper appears insufficient to establish that repetition is generally not useful).

2) Theorem 1 (noiseless, exact recovery). Non-adaptive XOR-type queries with Δ-locality were also studied in [Ahn–Lee–Suh], which derived a different query complexity. For example, at p = 1/2 and constant Δ, Theorem 1 here seems to imply m = n/log 2, whereas [Ahn–Lee–Suh] gives m = (n log n)/Δ. This discrepancy needs to be reconciled carefully: clarify assumptions (e.g., distributional vs. worst-case labelings, exact vs. high-probability decoding, choice of query ensemble, decoder, typicality, etc.), and provide a clean, complete proof or a precise statement of differences in model or guarantees. As written, the proof is hard to follow.

3) Theorem 3 (approximate recovery lower bound). The expression for the lower bound on δ is opaque; it is hard to invert to obtain m as a function of n and δ and thus to see the benefit of allowing δ > 0. Please (i) restate the bound in a more interpretable form (e.g., regimes, simplifications, asymptotics), (ii) provide plots translating δ to query complexity m, and (iii) compare to the unconstrained rate–distortion limit to quantify the penalty due to local encodability.

4) Theorem 4 (AND queries, achievability). Similar interpretability issues arise here. How tight is this upper bound relative to the lower bound in Theorem 3? Can the gap be unbounded for some (p, δ, Δ) regimes? The proof is also difficult to follow (Poissonization, two-hop neighbor counting, and f(k, d) could be summarized more transparently, with intermediate steps or references).

5) Figure 2 (comparison). The figure compares AND-query achievability only against a same-cluster lower bound, not against an actual same-cluster method. The paper asserts that the proposed approach outperforms same-cluster schemes, but Figure 2 cannot support that claim. Please add a direct comparison with a concrete same-cluster baseline (e.g., non-adaptive XOR queries with ML or a strong spectral/SDP decoder) under the same sampling budget and noise.

6) Algorithm 1 (need for priors). The algorithm requires knowledge of p (or exact relative cluster sizes; Theorem 6). How are these obtained in practice? The paper briefly mentions estimating from a small sample; please specify the procedure, sample size, and the robustness of thresholds Ci to estimation error. The pseudo-code is terse and difficult to understand without the proof; adding comments that explain the choice of thresholds and the role of the “gap” exploited in the Chernoff analysis would help.

7) Figures 3–5 (evaluation). There is no comparison to state-of-the-art same-cluster baselines, which is crucial to substantiate the claim that AND queries outperform same-cluster approaches. Please include baselines (e.g., spectral clustering on same-cluster graphs, SDP-based recovery, or ML decoding where feasible), report performance across noise levels and budgets, and discuss when each method wins.

Additional comments after rebuttal:
- It remains unclear whether repeating the same query is indeed unhelpful. If the Nature paper is meant to show this, please make the connection precise; otherwise, consider incorporating independent repetition across annotators and analyzing the trade-off explicitly.
- Please incorporate the detailed discussion comparing the AND approach to same-cluster methods (as provided in the response) directly into the paper, with quantitative experiments.

Qualitative evaluation:
- Quality: Promising ideas and bounds, but several technical and presentational issues need substantial revision and clearer connections to related results.
- Clarity: Theorems and proofs require significant rewriting for readability; figures need stronger baselines and clearer takeaways.
- Originality: The locally encodable angle and the AND-query design are interesting and appear novel in this context.
- Significance: Potentially impactful if the theory is clarified and the empirical advantages over strong same-cluster baselines are convincingly demonstrated.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel connection to locally encodable source coding and informative lower bounds.
- Simple, scalable AND-query algorithm with attractive asymptotics.
- Practical insight that AND queries may be easier for crowd workers.

Cons for acceptance:
- Apparent inconsistency with related non-adaptive XOR results; proof clarity issues.
- Limited empirical validation and lack of strong baselines.
- Restrictive assumptions (no repetition, known priors) insufficiently justified.

Overall, substantial revisions—clarifying theoretical claims, reconciling with prior work, and adding stronger empirical comparisons—are needed before publication.
