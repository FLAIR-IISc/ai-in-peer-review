This paper addresses the problem of clustering elements based on noisy, non-adaptive queries, where each query involves a small subset of elements. The authors derive theoretical lower bounds on the query complexity required for both exact and partial recovery of the ground-truth labels under various settings. They also propose a novel 'AND-queries' algorithm for the general k-cluster case and evaluate its performance on a real-world dataset.

While the paper's scope is ambitious, encompassing theoretical analysis, algorithm design, and empirical evaluation, it suffers from significant limitations in its presentation, the clarity of its theoretical claims, and its engagement with related literature. In its current form, the paper requires substantial revision to meet the standards for publication. The concerns detailed below touch upon core aspects of the model, theoretical results, and experimental validation.

**Detailed Comments:**

1.  **Modeling Constraint (No Repeated Queries):** The model's constraint that queries cannot be repeated seems overly restrictive. Intuitively, repeating queries is a natural mechanism to combat noise, so this restriction may degrade performance compared to an unrestricted model. The authors' motivation for this choice—avoiding analytical challenges with dependent answers—could be addressed by considering a more practical setting where repeated queries are posed to different, independent annotators. To justify this modeling choice, the authors should provide a stronger rationale or demonstrate empirically that their approach offers a tangible advantage over algorithms that do allow query repetition. The rebuttal points to a Nature paper, but it remains unclear if that work explicitly demonstrates that repeating queries is not beneficial in this context. This point should be clarified in a revised manuscript.

2.  **Theorem 1 (Noiseless Exact Recovery):** A significant concern arises with Theorem 1. The derived query complexity does not align with the results in [Ahn-Lee-Suh], a recent work that studies a similar problem ('same-cluster' or XOR queries) in a non-adaptive setting. For instance, for p=1/2 and constant Δ, Theorem 1 suggests a query complexity of m=n/log2, whereas [Ahn-Lee-Suh] reports m=nlogn/Δ. This discrepancy must be addressed and explained in detail. Furthermore, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow, hindering verification of the result.

3.  **Theorem 3 (Lower Bound Interpretability):** The lower bound on the error rate δ in Theorem 3 is presented in a form that is difficult to interpret. It is not straightforward to rearrange the expression to understand the scaling of the required number of queries (m) as a function of the number of elements (n) and the desired error rate (δ). A more transparent formulation would allow readers to see how the query complexity improves as the recovery requirement is relaxed from exact (δ=0) to partial recovery.

4.  **Theorem 4 (Upper Bound and Gap):** Similar clarity issues affect Theorem 4. The upper bound is also difficult to parse, which makes it challenging to assess the tightness of the analysis. A crucial missing piece of the discussion is the gap between the lower bound of Theorem 3 and the upper bound of Theorem 4. Is this gap bounded, or can it be arbitrarily large? As with other results, the proof is not presented with sufficient clarity.

5.  **Figure 2 (Insufficient Comparison):** The experimental comparison in Figure 2 is insufficient to support the paper's claims of superiority. The proposed algorithm is compared only to a theoretical *lower bound* for the same-cluster query method, not an actual implementation of that method. This comparison is misleading, as an algorithm's performance can be far from its theoretical lower bound. The paper's claim that the AND-queries approach "outperforms the same-cluster query scheme" is therefore unsubstantiated by the provided evidence. It would be interesting to see if a performance crossover point exists when comparing against the actual same-cluster query *method* and to understand the intuition behind it.

6.  **Algorithm 1 (Practicality and Clarity):** The practical applicability of Algorithm 1 is questionable, as it appears to require prior knowledge of the noise parameter `p` or the relative sizes of the clusters (as per Theorem 6). The paper should provide justification for this assumption or propose methods for estimating these parameters in a real-world scenario. Additionally, the pseudocode for the algorithm is opaque and difficult to understand without a deep dive into the proof of Theorem 5, which itself is challenging to follow.

7.  **Figures 3, 4, & 5 (Lack of Baselines):** The lack of comparison to baseline methods extends to Figures 3, 4, and 5. These experiments are presented in a vacuum, without benchmarking against state-of-the-art approaches, including the widely-used same-cluster-query method. This is a critical omission. As acknowledged in the author response, a more detailed discussion and direct empirical comparison are needed to substantiate the repeated claims that the AND-queries algorithm is superior. The revised manuscript should include these direct comparisons.
