This paper discusses a clustering problem aimed at reconstructing the true labels of elements from potentially noisy and non-optimized query-response data, where each label associates only a few elements. It also establishes a lower bound on the number of queries required to correctly or partially reconstruct ground-truth labels in various situations. It also proposes a so-called "end-query" algorithm that is applicable to arbitrary k-clusters and evaluates the algorithm's performance under a real data set.

Although this paper attempts to provide theoretical analysis for various scenarios and creates an algorithm with a real-data simulation, there are some limitations in terms of presentation, the clarity of the claimed results, and comparison with related work. Significant changes and possibly new developments may be required for publication. See below for detailed comments.

1. (The same question cannot be repeated): This limitation seems somewhat limiting. Also, if there were no limitation, it could reduce performance, as repeating the same questions is a natural way to deal with the effects of noise. A suitable way to support the limiting model could be to provide a wide range of real-data simulations and compare the algorithm created for a non-limiting model, and possibly show better performance of the proposed algorithm. If not, it is more reasonable to investigate the non-limiting model. The authors seem concerned about the freedom-versus-dependence problem of multiple answers in the same query case, which could complicate the analysis. However, this problem can be solved by focusing on a situation where the same query is given to different commentators, and therefore the commentators' answers are assumed to be independent.

2. (Thesis 1): Noise-free scenarios with perfect recovery have also been studied recently in a work by [Ahn-Lee-Suh], which considers XOR operations ('same-cluster' queries) under a non-optimized measurement setting. But [Ahn-Lee-Suh]'s results do not match the results claimed in Thesis 1. For example, when p=1/2 and \Delta is a constant, the query complexity in Thesis 1 is m=n/log2, whereas in [Ahn-Lee-Suh]'s work it is m=nlogn/\Delta. This needs to be clarified in detail. Moreover, the proof in Thesis 1 is difficult to follow.

3. (Thesis 3): It is difficult to explain the expression of the lower bound of \delta. In particular, it is difficult to estimate the bound on m in terms of n and \delta, from which the direct visible performance improvement in \delta is reflected due to the relaxed limitation.

4. (Thesis 4): The same comments related to Thesis 3 apply here. Also, how different is the upper bound from the lower bound in Thesis 3? Can the interval be infinite? Again, the proof is difficult to follow.

5. (Figure 2): No comparison has been made with the same cluster query method - only the same-cluster-query 'lower' bound has been compared. I am wondering if there is any connection point when comparing with the same-cluster-query 'method'. If there is, is there any idea behind it? In fact, the paper claims that the proposed method surpasses the same-cluster query scheme, which cannot be supported from Figure 2.

6. (Algorithm 1): The proposed algorithm requires prior knowledge of the relative size of p or clusters (in terms of Thesis 6). How is such information actually obtained? A suitable justification could prove the practicality of the algorithm. Also, it is difficult to follow the pseudo-code without relying on the detailed proof in Thesis 5, which is difficult to understand.

7. (Figures 3, 4, and 5): No comparison has been made with the state of the art of the same-cluster-query method, which is extremely important to support the claims made in several places: the end-query algorithm surpasses the same-cluster-query scheme.

[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.
