This paper tackles clustering via crowdsourcing, aiming to recover the true labels of data points from responses to similarity-style queries. The authors provide bounds on the number of queries needed to recover labels under both noiseless and noisy oracles, with the primary contribution being theoretical and complemented by simulations. The paper is clearly written and accessible to a general machine learning audience. In particular, the information-theoretic framework used to derive the bounds is well motivated and clearly explained.

I have several comments intended to improve the paper:

1) Interpretation and practicality of AND queries: While AND queries are theoretically interesting, they seem to conflict with the original motivation for crowdsourcing. As presented, an AND query appears to reveal not just whether two items are in the same cluster but also the cluster label itself. If the oracle can directly provide cluster labels, why not ask for labels of individual points instead of (or in addition to) similarity queries? Please clarify the practical setting, cost model, and feasibility of AND queries relative to standard same-cluster queries. This is my primary source of confusion regarding the paper’s contributions and applicability.

2) Presentation of bounds: The statements of Theorems 1–4 contain fairly complex expressions. It would help readers if the bounds were simplified while preserving their essence—for example, by isolating the dominant terms, consolidating constants, or providing equivalent big-O/Theta forms alongside the exact expressions—together with brief intuition about how each parameter affects query complexity.

3) Experiments: The experiments appear to focus exclusively on AND queries. If so, and unless point (1) is fully addressed, the empirical evaluation may not align with realistic crowdsourcing conditions. I encourage the authors to (a) justify the AND-query model empirically (e.g., through cost, reliability, or availability arguments), and/or (b) include results for standard same-cluster queries or mixed-query regimes under comparable budgets and noise models.

The authors’ response addressed my concerns to my satisfaction. I recommend acceptance.
