The authors investigate a clustering model where a learner interacts with an oracle via queries to determine the underlying cluster structure. These queries can take the form of ‘same cluster’ questions or, more generally, any fixed binary-valued function applied to instance labels. The central focus is on establishing the query complexity – the number of queries needed – to accurately recover the target clustering, both in perfect and approximate settings, and under conditions of noisy oracle responses. This work frames the problem through an information-theoretic lens.

The query-based clustering paradigm is a rapidly developing area, driven by applications in areas like record linkage and crowdsourcing. The authors’ approach of leveraging information theory to analyze this problem is a compelling contribution, providing fundamental bounds on query complexity. They present both upper and lower bounds, offering a rigorous understanding of the inherent limitations and possibilities within this model. The introduction of locally encodable source coding as a framework for analyzing querying complexity is a novel and insightful connection, and the derived information-theoretic results are potentially valuable to the machine learning community.

The work’s strength lies in its theoretical rigor and the clear presentation of information-theoretic connections. The proposed AND query strategy and its demonstrated advantages over ‘same cluster’ queries represent a significant finding. The consideration of noisy oracle responses and the development of algorithms that perform well in such scenarios adds to the practical relevance of the research. The validation of theoretical findings with a real crowdsourcing experiment provides further support for the proposed methodology.

However, the analysis assumes a relatively general setting with few structural assumptions on the problem, relying heavily on the marginal distribution of labels and noise level. This broadness, while enabling strong general bounds, may limit the applicability to specific scenarios where stronger assumptions could lead to tighter results. The focus on non-adaptive queries, while simplifying the analysis, potentially sacrifices the efficiency gains achievable through adaptive strategies. Furthermore, while the experimental results are encouraging, a more comprehensive evaluation across diverse datasets and oracle noise levels would strengthen the claims. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information-theoretic tools to a relevant and growing area of machine learning (query-based clustering).
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear upper and lower bounds on query complexity.
*   Demonstrated improvement over existing query strategies (AND queries vs. ‘same cluster’ queries).
*   Consideration of noisy oracle responses and corresponding algorithmic development.
*   Initial validation through a real crowdsourcing experiment.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The generality of the model may lead to bounds that are loose in specific scenarios.
*   Focus on non-adaptive queries potentially limits practical efficiency.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.



Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution that provides a valuable foundation for understanding the limits of query-based clustering. While further work exploring specific applications and adaptive querying strategies would be beneficial, the current paper represents a significant step forward in the field and is worthy of acceptance.
