This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering when a learner can actively query an oracle to determine if two instances belong to the same cluster. The model allows for generalizations beyond pairwise comparisons, utilizing any fixed binary function of instance labels as a query. The authors provide information-theoretic upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed to achieve perfect or approximate recovery of the target clustering, and extend their analysis to scenarios with a noisy oracle.

The query-based clustering model is a compelling and relatively new research direction, with practical relevance to applications like record deduplication and crowdsourcing. The authors’ information-theoretic approach offers valuable insights into the fundamental limits of this problem. Their use of these tools is both interesting and a potentially valuable contribution to the machine learning community. 

A key characteristic, and limitation, of this work is the complete absence of structural assumptions about the underlying clustering problem. Unlike many learning theory settings, there's no hypothesis class or other constraints imposed. Consequently, the bounds derived depend primarily on the marginal distribution of the labels and the oracle's noise level. This makes the studied setting somewhat abstract compared to more traditional learning scenarios. However, this work represents an important initial step towards understanding the inherent information-theoretic constraints of query-based clustering, particularly establishing lower bounds that highlight the unavoidable cost of learning in this framework. 

The analysis provides a solid foundation for future research that might incorporate more realistic assumptions or explore the impact of specific data distributions.
