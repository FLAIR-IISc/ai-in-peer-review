This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering when the learner can actively query an oracle to determine if pairs of instances belong to the same cluster. The model allows for a general binary-valued oracle function, effectively encompassing same-cluster queries as a special case. The authors provide information-theoretic upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required to perfectly or approximately recover a target clustering, and extend their analysis to scenarios with a noisy oracle.

The query-based clustering model is a compelling and relatively new area of research, with direct relevance to practical problems like record deduplication and active learning via crowdsourcing. This work offers a valuable contribution by framing the problem through an information-theoretic lens, a perspective that provides fundamental limits on achievable performance. The presented bounds, both upper and lower, are rigorously derived and represent a significant step towards understanding the inherent difficulty of this clustering task. The authors appropriately highlight the connection between query complexity and the marginal distribution of labels, alongside the level of oracle noise. Furthermore, the application of information-theoretic tools, while standard in related fields, is relatively novel within the machine learning community and provides a fresh approach to analyzing clustering algorithms.

The primary limitation of the analysis stems from the lack of structural assumptions on the problem. Unlike traditional learning theory, this work does not operate within a hypothesis class or impose constraints on the cluster structure. Consequently, the bounds heavily rely on the entropy of the label distribution, making them somewhat sensitive to the characteristics of this distribution. While understandable given the goal of establishing fundamental limits, this dependence means the bounds may be loose or less informative in settings where stronger assumptions *can* be made about the underlying data or clustering process. This results in a setting that feels somewhat distanced from the more common, parameterized learning scenarios typically considered in theoretical machine learning.

However, this is a natural consequence of pursuing a purely information-theoretic analysis, and the authors are clear about this trade-off. The work’s value lies precisely in establishing these baseline limits – providing a foundational understanding of the information-theoretic requirements for query-based clustering, and particularly, for deriving lower bounds. The insights gained can inform the design of more efficient algorithms and help determine when further structural assumptions are necessary to achieve practical performance. 

**Strengths:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation based on information theory.
*   Clearly defined problem and rigorous analysis.
*   Relevant and well-motivated application area.
*   Novel application of information-theoretic tools to clustering.
*   Establishment of both upper and lower bounds.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Bounds are heavily dependent on the entropy of the label distribution.
*   Lack of structural assumptions may limit practical applicability of the bounds.
*   The setting feels somewhat abstract compared to typical learning theory frameworks.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid theoretical contribution that provides important insights into the fundamental limits of query-based clustering. While the lack of structural assumptions results in bounds that are sensitive to the label distribution, this is a deliberate choice made to explore the inherent information-theoretic constraints. This work lays a valuable foundation for future research in this area and is a worthwhile addition to the NIPS conference. I recommend acceptance, with the understanding that the results represent a first step towards a deeper understanding of the complexities involved in query-based clustering.
