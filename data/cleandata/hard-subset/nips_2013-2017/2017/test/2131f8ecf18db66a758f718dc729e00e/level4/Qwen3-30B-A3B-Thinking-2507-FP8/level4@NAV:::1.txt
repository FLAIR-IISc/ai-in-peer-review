

The authors investigate a clustering framework where a learner interacts with an oracle via queries to identify the target clustering, with queries restricted to same-cluster checks (or more generally, a fixed binary function of instance labels). They analyze the query complexity required for perfect or approximate recovery.  

This query-based clustering model is well-motivated and emerging, with applications in record deduplication and crowdsourcing. The authors adopt an information-theoretic approach, establishing tight upper and lower bounds on the necessary queries, including scenarios with noisy oracles. The information-theoretic techniques employed are novel within the machine learning community.  

However, the absence of structural assumptions (e.g., a hypothesis class) constrains the analysis, which primarily depends on the marginal label distribution (beyond noise parameters). Consequently, the setting is somewhat distinct from standard learning theory frameworks. Despite this, the work represents a valuable first step toward elucidating the information-theoretic foundations of query-based clustering, particularly regarding lower-bound techniques.
