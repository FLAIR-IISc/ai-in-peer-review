

This paper investigates a clustering problem where the objective is to reconstruct true element labels from noisy, non-adaptive query-answer pairs, each involving only a few elements. It establishes lower bounds on the number of queries required for exact or partial ground-truth label reconstruction across various scenarios and proposes an "AND-queries" algorithm applicable to arbitrary k-cluster settings, evaluating its performance on real data.  

While the paper provides theoretical analyses for multiple scenarios and develops an algorithm with real-data validation, it suffers from limitations in presentation, clarity of claimed results, and comparison to related work. Substantial revisions, potentially including new theoretical developments, are necessary for publication. Detailed comments follow:  

1.  **Restricted Query Model:** The constraint against repeating queries appears unnecessarily restrictive. Allowing repeated queries is a natural noise-combating strategy and may improve performance relative to the restricted model. The authors should justify this restriction by demonstrating (via extensive real-data simulations) that their algorithm outperforms a tailored version for the unrestricted case. If no such advantage exists, the unrestricted model is more plausible. The authors' concern about independence vs. dependence in multiple answers to the same query could be resolved by assuming distinct annotators for repeated queries, ensuring independent answers.  

2.  **Theorem 1 (Noiseless Exact Recovery):** The noiseless setting with exact recovery was also studied in [Ahn-Lee-Suh] under non-adaptive XOR ("same-cluster") queries. However, Theorem 1's claim (e.g., query complexity $m = n / \log 2$ when $p=1/2$ and $\Delta$ is constant) conflicts with [Ahn-Lee-Suh]'s result ($m = n \log n / \Delta$). This discrepancy must be clarified. Additionally, the proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow.  

3.  **Theorem 3:** The lower bound expression for $\delta$ is difficult to interpret. Specifically, it is unclear how to derive a bound on $m$ in terms of $n$ and $\delta$, which is necessary to directly assess performance gains from the relaxed constraint ($\delta$).  

4.  **Theorem 4:** The same interpretability issue as in Theorem 3 applies. Furthermore, the gap between the upper bound in Theorem 4 and the lower bound in Theorem 3 is not quantified—could it be unbounded? The proof is also difficult to follow.  

5.  **Figure 2:** The figure compares the proposed method only to the "lower bound" of the same-cluster-query method, not to the method itself. It is unclear whether a crossing point exists when comparing to the same-cluster-query *method* (not just its lower bound). The paper claims the AND-queries algorithm outperforms the same-cluster-query scheme, but Figure 2 does not support this claim.  

6.  **Algorithm 1:** The algorithm requires prior knowledge of $p$ or cluster size ratios (as per Theorem 6). The practical means of obtaining this information are not justified, undermining the algorithm's applicability. Additionally, the pseudo-code is hard to follow without the detailed proof of Theorem 5, which is also difficult to grasp.  

7.  **Figures 3–5:** No comparison is made against state-of-the-art methods like the same-cluster-query scheme, which is essential to support the paper's repeated claim that the AND-queries algorithm outperforms this scheme.  

[Ahn-Lee-Suh] K. Ahn, K. Lee, and C. Suh, "Community recovery in hypergraphs," Proceedings of Allerton Conference on Communication, Control, and Computing, 2016.  

**Additional Comments:**  
1.  The justification for prohibiting repeated queries remains unclear. Did the referenced "Nature paper" demonstrate it is unhelpful? This requires clarification in revision.  
2.  Regarding comparison to the same-cluster-query method, a detailed discussion as addressed in the rebuttal response would significantly improve clarity.
