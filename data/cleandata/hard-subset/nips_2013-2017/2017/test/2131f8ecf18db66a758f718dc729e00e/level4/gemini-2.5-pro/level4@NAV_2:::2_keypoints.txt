- Novel framing: maps semi-supervised clustering with non-adaptive queries to locally encodable source coding, offering a new information-theoretic perspective.  
- Key idea: proposes AND-queries as an alternative to same-cluster (XOR) queries, claimed to be more efficient especially for approximate recovery.  
- Broad scope: analyzes exact vs. approximate recovery, noiseless vs. noisy oracles, and binary vs. multi-class clustering.  
- Experimental validation: presents an algorithm for the multi-cluster case and tests it on a real crowdsourced dataset.  

- Theoretical clarity: main theorems are hard to interpret; proofs (e.g., Theorem 1) are condensed and difficult to follow.  
- Potential contradiction: claimed query complexity (m = n / log2 for p = 1/2) appears to conflict with prior work (Ahn et al., 2016: m = n log n / Δ) and needs explicit reconciliation.  
- Lack of intuition: Theorems 3 & 4 give complex error bounds without clear intuition relating query budget m to achievable error δ, and the gap between lower and upper bounds is not discussed.  
- Restrictive modeling: forbidding repeated queries is unrealistic for crowdsourcing; the paper needs stronger justification or an alternative model allowing repeats to independent workers.  
- Weak comparisons: central claim that AND-queries outperform same-cluster queries is not convincingly supported—Figure 2 compares to a lower bound for same-cluster queries rather than an actual algorithmic baseline.  
- Missing baselines: Figures 3–5 report Algorithm 1 in isolation without direct comparisons to state-of-the-art or same-cluster-based methods, reducing practical significance.  
- Practicality concerns: Algorithm 1 requires prior knowledge of cluster sizes p without discussion of how to estimate this reliably in practice.  
- Presentation of algorithm: pseudocode is not self-contained and depends on complex proofs (Theorem 5) for understanding; overall algorithm description needs clarification.
