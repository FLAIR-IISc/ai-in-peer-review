This paper presents a fascinating connection between semisupervised clustering with binary queries and locally encodable source coding. The authors leverage information-theoretic tools to derive lower bounds on the number of queries required for accurate clustering, and propose a novel querying scheme based on AND operations that demonstrably outperforms traditional 'same cluster' (XOR) queries in certain scenarios. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and makes a significant contribution to understanding the fundamental limits of querying-based clustering, particularly in the context of crowdsourcing.

**Summary & Relation to Previous Work:**

The core idea is to recast the problem of learning cluster assignments from pairwise queries as a source coding problem where the label vector is the 'source' and the query answers form the 'compressed' representation. The locality constraint inherent in locally encodable codes – where each compressed bit depends on only a few source bits – directly maps to the limited scope of pairwise queries. This connection allows the authors to apply known results from information theory to obtain lower bounds on query complexity.  Prior work in crowdsourced clustering (e.g., [4, 8, 20, 33]) largely focuses on algorithmic design and empirical evaluation. This paper distinguishes itself by providing rigorous theoretical foundations, building upon the emerging connection between crowdsourcing and information theory as highlighted in [14].  The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of locally decodable codes [16, 18, 19, 26] and smooth compression [23], clearly differentiating their focus on *encodability* rather than *decodability*.  The connection to Massey's work on rate-distortion [17] regarding the limitations of linear codes is insightful and crucial for motivating the AND query approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The equivalence between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is a novel and insightful observation.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is grounded in solid information-theoretic principles, and the derived lower bounds provide fundamental limits on query complexity.  The proofs appear careful and well-structured.
* **Practical Relevance:** The proposed AND query strategy offers a practical improvement over traditional XOR queries, particularly in noisy environments, and is supported by experimental validation.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The notation is consistent, and the concepts are explained clearly.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of relevant literature in both the clustering/crowdsourcing and information theory communities.
* **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of a crowdsourcing experiment, albeit on a relatively small scale, lends credibility to the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The assumption of independent errors in query answers, while common, is a simplification. Real-world crowdsourcing scenarios often exhibit correlated errors (e.g., systematic biases among workers).  The analysis doesn’t fully address the impact of such correlations.
* **Scalability of Experiments:** The crowdsourcing experiment is limited in scope (10 workers, relatively few queries).  Scaling up the experiment to a larger number of elements and workers would provide stronger evidence for the practical benefits of the AND query approach.
* **Complexity of Algorithm 1:**  While theoretically sound, Algorithm 1 appears computationally intensive, particularly for large-scale datasets.  Further investigation into the practical efficiency of the algorithm would be valuable. The discussion on the practical implications of the d-regular graph construction and its impact on query cost is lacking.
* **Limited Exploration of k > 2:** The majority of the theoretical results focus on the k=2 case. While the authors state that their results extend to larger k, the specific implications and limitations for larger values of k are not fully explored.  The experimental validation is only presented for k=2.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** While generally well-written, some sections (e.g., the proof of Theorem 2) could benefit from more detailed explanations of intermediate steps.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a fundamentally new perspective on querying-based clustering, linking it to a well-established area of information theory.
* The theoretical results are significant and provide valuable insights into the limitations of existing approaches.
* The proposed AND query strategy is a practical improvement that is supported by experimental evidence.
* The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The assumptions underlying the theoretical analysis (e.g., independent errors) may not hold in real-world scenarios.
* The scalability of the proposed algorithm and the experimental validation are limited.
* The analysis is largely focused on the k=2 case, and the implications for larger k are not fully explored.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is novel and insightful, and the theoretical results are both rigorous and relevant. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically:

1. **Address the limitations of the independent error assumption:**  Discuss the potential impact of correlated errors and suggest possible approaches for mitigating their effects.
2. **Expand the experimental validation:** Conduct experiments with a larger number of elements and workers.  Consider evaluating the performance of Algorithm 1 on publicly available datasets.
3. **Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of Algorithm 1:**  Analyze the runtime and memory requirements of the algorithm and identify potential optimizations.
4. **Explore the implications for k > 2:** Provide more detailed analysis and/or experimental results for larger values of k.
