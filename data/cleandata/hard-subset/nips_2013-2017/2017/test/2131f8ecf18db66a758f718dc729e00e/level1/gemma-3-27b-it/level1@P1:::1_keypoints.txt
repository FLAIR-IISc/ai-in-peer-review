- Novelty: Establishes a surprising, valuable equivalence between semi-supervised clustering with binary queries and locally encodable source coding.  
- Theoretical rigor: Provides clear definitions, rigorous proofs, and well-motivated theorems, including lower bounds on query complexity.  
- Practical scheme: Proposes an AND-query scheme that can outperform standard “same-cluster” queries and analyzes noisy oracle responses.  
- Thoroughness: Covers multiple scenarios (noiseless, approximate recovery, noisy queries) and connects to related coding concepts.  
- Experimental validation: Includes an Amazon Mechanical Turk experiment giving some empirical support.  
- Related work positioning: Situates work well within crowdsourced clustering literature and cites key prior works on locally encodable coding.  
- Clarity & organization: Paper is dense and occasionally hard to follow; introduction and some discussions (e.g., encodable vs decodable codes) need tighter focus.  
- Limited experiments: Crowdsourcing study is small in scope, uses limited datasets/participants, and parameter choices (e.g., d=10) are not fully justified.  
- Cost analysis missing: Lacks detailed comparison of practical costs between AND and Same Cluster queries.  
- Practical challenges underexplored: Does not sufficiently address worker bias, spamming, UI design, or other crowdsourcing realities.  
- Strong assumptions: Relies on independent error models and a known prior distribution p without exploring sensitivity to violations.  
- Referencing gap: Could cite more work on active learning given connections to adaptive query selection, despite focus on non-adaptive queries.  
- Acceptance arguments: Novel theoretical connection, solid mathematical analysis, and a promising practical scheme justify acceptance.  
- Rejection concerns: Dense presentation, limited empirical evidence, and inadequate discussion of practical limitations could hinder impact.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—improve clarity, expand and justify experiments (including cost analysis), discuss practical limitations, and slightly reorganize for better flow.
