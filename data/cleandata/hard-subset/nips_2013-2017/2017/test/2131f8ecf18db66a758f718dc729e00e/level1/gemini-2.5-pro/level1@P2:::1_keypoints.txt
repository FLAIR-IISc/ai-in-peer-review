- Introduces a novel connection between semisupervised query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding, enabling new information-theoretic analysis.  
- Problem: non-adaptive queries on n items into k clusters with each query involving at most Δ items; goal is label recovery from possibly noisy answers.  
- Reframes queries as locally encodable compression, deriving new fundamental lower bounds on query complexity (Theorems 2 and 3).  
- Proposes AND queries as a practical and theoretically superior alternative to "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery.  
- Shows via rate-distortion arguments that XOR queries are suboptimal and AND queries can provably perform better.  
- Provides strong theoretical results, notably a noisy approximate-recovery algorithm that achieves (1−δ)-fraction recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) non-adaptive queries.  
- Analysis leverages tools from information theory, coding theory, and probability and appears technically sound.  
- Includes a small real crowdsourcing experiment showing empirical alignment with theory and lower error rates for AND queries versus same-cluster queries.  
- Paper is technically dense and may be hard to follow for readers without information-theory background; more intuition would help accessibility.  
- Empirical study is limited in scale (200 items, 10 workers) and lacks broader comparisons to state-of-the-art on real data.  
- Analysis assumes known prior label distribution p or cluster sizes; robustness to estimation errors is not fully addressed.  
- Overall evaluation: significant, original contribution with strong theory and promising practical evidence; recommended for acceptance.
