- Establishes a novel equivalence between semisupervised clustering (with oracle queries) and locally encodable source coding in information theory.
- Frames pairwise-query cluster-label learning as a source coding problem where each compressed bit depends on a constant Δ input bits.
- Derives information-theoretic lower bounds on query complexity and provides matching/near-matching upper bounds (Theorems 1–5).
- Shows AND queries substantially outperform "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery, both theoretically and empirically.
- Presents rigorous proofs leveraging information-theoretic concepts and novel lower bounds (Theorems 2, 3).
- Experimental validation on crowdsourcing data supports theoretical claims (AND: 105 errors vs XOR: 234 errors).
- Addresses noisy-query scenarios with clear theoretical guarantees.
- Connects to prior work on clustering and crowdsourcing but provides a fundamentally different, unifying information-theoretic framework.
- Highlights practical significance: AND queries can reduce query complexity from O(n log n) to O(n log 1/δ) for (1−δ)-recovery.
- Well-written and organized, with clear problem setup and motivated connections among fields.
- Could give more intuition (especially why AND outperforms XOR) for readers unfamiliar with rate-distortion theory.
- Assumes known cluster-size priors (p) and independent errors, which may limit applicability; practical estimation of p is under-addressed.
- Experimental section lacks details on volunteer demographics and potential crowdsourcing biases and is limited to a single dataset (IMDB).
- Related-work connections to recent NIPS papers could be more explicitly drawn.
- Strengths: novel theoretical connection, rigorous bounds, practical AND query proposal, experimental support, and applicability to noisy settings.
- Weaknesses: need for more intuition, restrictive prior assumptions, limited experimental detail and dataset scope.
- Overall recommendation: accept — technically strong, novel, practically relevant, and minor issues are addressable.
