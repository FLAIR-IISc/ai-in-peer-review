

# Review: Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding

This paper establishes a novel connection between semisupervised clustering with pairwise queries and locally encodable source coding in information theory. The authors model the problem where an oracle (e.g., crowd worker) answers queries about whether pairs of elements belong to the same cluster (XOR/same cluster queries) or whether both elements share a specific label (AND queries). The goal is to recover the cluster labels with minimal queries, with the key contribution being the equivalence to locally encodable source coding, which enables information-theoretic lower bounds and the introduction of AND queries as a superior alternative to traditional XOR queries.

The paper builds on recent NIPS work on clustering with queries (e.g., Ashtiani et al., 2016; Mazumdar & Saha, 2017), but makes a significant theoretical contribution by framing the problem through the lens of locally encodable source coding. This perspective, while not directly studied in information theory, enables new insights about query complexity. The authors show that AND queries outperform XOR queries in both noiseless and noisy settings, particularly for approximate recovery where they achieve (1-δ)-good recovery with O(n log k/δ) queries versus the O(n log n) required for XOR queries.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides novel information-theoretic lower bounds for both exact and approximate recovery that are strictly better than existing results. The connection to locally encodable source coding is conceptually elegant and leads to meaningful bounds that had not been previously established.

2. **Practical Insight:** The authors demonstrate that AND queries are more efficient than XOR queries because they require less knowledge from crowd workers (only one label needs to be known for a "no" answer, whereas both labels are needed for a correct "same cluster" answer). This is validated in their real-world crowdsourcing experiment where AND queries yielded significantly fewer errors (105 vs. 234 erroneous answers).

3. **Algorithmic Results:** The proposed Algorithm 1 for noisy queries is simple yet effective, and the authors prove it achieves near-optimal query complexity. The theoretical results are well-supported by experiments on a real crowdsourcing dataset.

4. **Clarity of Core Contribution:** The distinction between XOR and AND queries is clearly explained, and the paper effectively demonstrates why AND queries are superior for approximate recovery.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Crowdsourcing Application:** The paper focuses exclusively on non-adaptive query schemes, which may not be practical for real-world crowdsourcing where adaptive querying can lead to better results with fewer queries. Recent work (e.g., Ashtiani et al., 2016) has shown adaptive querying can achieve better performance.

2. **Prior Knowledge Assumption:** The theoretical analysis assumes knowledge of the prior distribution p, which is often not available in practice. While the authors address this in Theorem 6 by using cluster size ratios, the practical implementation of this approach requires additional assumptions about cluster sizes.

3. **Experimental Validation:** The real-world experiment is limited to a single dataset of 200 movies, which is small for a crowdsourcing study. More comprehensive experiments across multiple datasets would strengthen the practical claims.

4. **Connection to Existing Work:** While the paper cites relevant NIPS work, it could better distinguish its contribution from recent advances in query-based clustering. For instance, the paper does not adequately address how its results compare to the adaptive query schemes in recent NIPS papers.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- The paper establishes a novel theoretical connection between clustering with queries and information theory that leads to meaningful insights.
- The AND query model is theoretically and practically superior to existing approaches, with clear experimental validation.
- The information-theoretic lower bounds are significant contributions that advance the understanding of query complexity in clustering.

*Con:*
- The focus on non-adaptive queries limits the practical applicability of the results.
- The assumption of known prior distribution p is a strong limitation for real-world applications.
- The experimental validation is limited in scope and scale.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of query-based clustering by establishing a novel connection to locally encodable source coding and demonstrating the superiority of AND queries over traditional XOR queries. The theoretical results are sound, and the experimental validation provides meaningful evidence for the practical advantages of the proposed approach.

While the focus on non-adaptive queries and the assumption of known priors limit the immediate practical applicability, these are common challenges in theoretical work that can be addressed in future extensions. The paper's core contribution—establishing the AND query model as theoretically and practically superior—represents a meaningful advance in the field.

The paper is well-written, clearly presents its theoretical contributions, and provides valuable insights that will likely influence future work in query-based clustering and crowdsourcing. The connection to information theory is particularly elegant and should be of interest to both theoretical and applied researchers in machine learning.

Given the significance of the theoretical contribution, the novelty of the AND query model, and the supporting experimental validation, I recommend acceptance for NIPS 2017. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the limitations of non-adaptive querying and the prior knowledge assumption in future work, but these do not detract from the current contribution.
