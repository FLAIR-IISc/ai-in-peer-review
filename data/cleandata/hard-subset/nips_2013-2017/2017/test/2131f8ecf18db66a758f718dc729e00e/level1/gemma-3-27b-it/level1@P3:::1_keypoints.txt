- Novel connection: Recasts semisupervised clustering with binary queries as a locally encodable source coding problem, mapping pairwise queries to local encoding constraints.  
- Theoretical contribution: Uses information-theoretic tools to derive lower bounds on required queries and provides rigorous proofs.  
- AND-query scheme: Proposes a novel AND-based querying strategy motivated by limitations of linear codes, which can outperform traditional XOR ('same cluster') queries in certain settings.  
- Relation to prior work: Distinguishes itself from prior crowdsourcing/clustering work by providing theoretical foundations and linking to locally decodable codes and rate-distortion results.  
- Practical relevance: AND queries show empirical benefits and a small crowdsourcing experiment provides supporting evidence.  
- Writing and presentation: Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and uses consistent notation.  
- Literature coverage: Comprehensive and well-positioned with respect to both clustering/crowdsourcing and information theory literatures.  
- Experimental limitations: Crowdsourcing experiment is small-scale (10 workers, few queries), limiting practical evidence.  
- Error model limitation: Analysis assumes independent query errors, ignoring realistic correlated/systematic worker biases.  
- Algorithmic scalability: Algorithm 1 appears computationally intensive and lacks detailed discussion of runtime, memory, and d-regular graph query cost.  
- Limited k>2 exploration: Main theoretical and experimental results focus on k=2; extensions to larger k are claimed but not fully explored.  
- Minor clarity issues: Some proofs (e.g., Theorem 2) need more detailed intermediate explanations.  
- Recommendation: Strong paper with significant contributions; reviewer recommends acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: Address correlated errors, expand experiments to larger datasets/workers, analyze Algorithm 1 complexity and optimizations, and provide more detailed treatment/experiments for k>2.
