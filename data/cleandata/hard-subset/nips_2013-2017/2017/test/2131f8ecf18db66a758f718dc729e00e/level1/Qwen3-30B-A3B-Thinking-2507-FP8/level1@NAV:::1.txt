

This paper establishes a novel connection between semisupervised clustering with pairwise queries and locally encodable source coding in information theory. The authors show that the problem of clustering elements using "same cluster" (XOR) queries can be viewed as a locally encodable source coding problem, where each compressed bit depends on only a constant number of input bits. This perspective leads to new information-theoretic lower bounds on query complexity and motivates the use of AND queries, which the authors prove outperform XOR queries for approximate recovery.

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) establishing the theoretical equivalence between clustering and locally encodable source coding; (2) deriving improved lower bounds for query complexity; (3) proposing AND queries as a superior alternative to "same cluster" queries; and (4) validating their approach with a real crowdsourcing experiment. The work builds on recent NIPS papers in crowdsourcing-based clustering (e.g., [4, 21]) but introduces a fundamentally different theoretical framework that addresses gaps in the existing literature on query complexity.

Strengths include strong theoretical foundations with rigorous proofs for both noiseless and noisy settings, clear presentation of the AND query approach's advantages over XOR queries, and compelling empirical validation through a real-world experiment showing reduced error rates with AND queries. The paper also provides practical insights for crowdsourcing applications, noting that workers find AND queries easier to answer than "same cluster" queries.

Weaknesses include some technical depth that may challenge readers unfamiliar with information theory concepts, and limited discussion of broader applications beyond the specific clustering problem. The connection to NIPS-specific work could be more explicitly articulated, though the paper does reference relevant prior work.

Pro: Novel theoretical framework, strong empirical validation, significant improvement over existing query strategies. Con: Some technical barriers for non-specialists, limited discussion of wider implications.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances both the clustering and information theory communities. The paper is well-written, carefully argued, and makes a clear distinction from previous work. The results have practical significance for crowdsourcing applications, making this an important contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
