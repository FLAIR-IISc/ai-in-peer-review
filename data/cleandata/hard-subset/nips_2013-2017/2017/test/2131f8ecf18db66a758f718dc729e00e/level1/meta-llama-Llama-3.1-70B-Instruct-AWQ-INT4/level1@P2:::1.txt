This paper presents a novel approach to semisupervised clustering by formulating it as a locally encodable source coding problem. The authors establish an equivalence between this model and a recently popular model of semisupervised clustering, where the task is to perform multiclass labeling of unlabeled elements. They show that this equivalence leads to information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required in various scenarios. The paper also proposes pairwise AND queries, which provably perform better than 'same cluster' queries in many situations.

The strengths of the paper are:

1. Novel formulation: The authors provide a fresh perspective on semisupervised clustering by formulating it as a locally encodable source coding problem. This formulation allows them to leverage information-theoretic tools to establish lower bounds on the number of queries required.
2. Theoretical contributions: The paper provides several theoretical contributions, including a lower bound on the number of queries required for exact recovery, a lower bound on the number of queries required for approximate recovery, and an upper bound on the number of queries required for approximate recovery using AND queries.
3. Algorithmic contributions: The authors propose a querying scheme using AND queries, which provably performs better than 'same cluster' queries in many situations. They also provide an algorithm for approximate recovery from noisy queries, which is shown to be (1-δ)-good.

The weaknesses of the paper are:

1. Limited experimental evaluation: While the authors provide some experimental results on a real dataset, the evaluation is limited, and more extensive experiments would be necessary to fully validate the theoretical results.
2. Complexity of the proofs: Some of the proofs, particularly those involving information-theoretic arguments, may be challenging for non-experts to follow.
3. Limited discussion of related work: While the authors mention some related work, a more comprehensive discussion of the existing literature on semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding would be helpful.

In terms of the reviewing criteria, the paper scores well on:

1. Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results.
2. Originality: The formulation of semisupervised clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem is novel and original.
3. Significance: The paper provides significant contributions to the field of semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, and the results have the potential to impact the design of clustering algorithms and crowdsourcing systems.

However, the paper could be improved in terms of:

1. Clarity: Some of the proofs and technical arguments could be clarified and simplified to make the paper more accessible to a broader audience.
2. Related work: A more comprehensive discussion of related work would help to situate the contributions of the paper within the broader context of the field.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper for publication, subject to revisions that address the weaknesses mentioned above.
