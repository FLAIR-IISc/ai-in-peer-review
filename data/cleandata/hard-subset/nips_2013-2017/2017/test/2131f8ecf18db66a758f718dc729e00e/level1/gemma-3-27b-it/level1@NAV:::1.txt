## Review of “Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding”

This paper establishes a surprising connection between semisupervised clustering with pairwise queries and locally encodable source coding. The core idea is to frame the clustering problem as a compression task where queries represent compressed bits, and labels are the original data. This allows the authors to leverage information-theoretic tools to derive lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering, and to propose a novel querying strategy using AND queries that demonstrably outperforms traditional ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries in several scenarios, particularly when dealing with noisy oracle responses.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the recent surge in interest in clustering via crowdsourcing and the associated cost of queries. It builds upon prior work in crowdsourcing (e.g., [4, 33, 30]) and information theory ([14, 23, 19]), explicitly positioning its contributions within these contexts. The theoretical results, especially the lower bounds on query complexity and the analysis of AND queries, are the paper’s strengths. The proof techniques appear rigorous, and the comparison to existing work highlights the advancements made. The experimental validation, although limited, provides some support for the theoretical findings.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the technical details are present, the connection to locally encodable source coding feels somewhat tenuous. The potential benefits of viewing the problem through this lens aren't fully realized beyond allowing the application of information-theoretic lower bounds. The presentation could be streamlined; the paper is quite dense, and certain sections, such as the discussion of locally decodable codes, feel somewhat tangential. The experimental setup is basic and could be significantly strengthened with more extensive evaluation on a wider range of datasets and oracle error rates. The analysis of the AND queries, while promising, could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of *why* they perform better, beyond just demonstrating that the lower bounds are tighter.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding.
*   Derivation of new lower bounds on query complexity.
*   Introduction of AND queries as a more efficient alternative to ‘same cluster’ queries.
*   Sound theoretical analysis and (limited) experimental validation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The connection to locally encodable source coding feels somewhat superficial.
*   The paper is dense and could benefit from improved clarity and conciseness.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
*   The intuitive explanation of the AND query advantage could be improved.

**Overall:**

Despite some weaknesses, I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field. The connection between clustering and information theory is intriguing, and the proposed AND query strategy has the potential to be impactful. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity and expand the experimental evaluation.
