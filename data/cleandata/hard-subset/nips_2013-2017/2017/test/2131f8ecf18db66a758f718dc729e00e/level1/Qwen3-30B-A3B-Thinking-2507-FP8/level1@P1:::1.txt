

# Peer Review

This paper establishes a novel equivalence between semisupervised clustering (via queries to an oracle) and locally encodable source coding in information theory. The authors demonstrate that the problem of learning cluster labels through pairwise queries can be framed as a source coding problem where each compressed bit depends on only a constant number of input bits (Δ). This perspective enables them to derive information-theoretic lower bounds on query complexity and propose superior query strategies, particularly AND queries, compared to the commonly used "same cluster" (XOR) queries. The work connects to recent NIPS papers on clustering with side information (e.g., Ashtiani et al. 2016, Mazumdar & Saha 2017) and crowdsourcing (e.g., Karger et al. 2014, Gruenheid et al. 2015), but provides a fundamentally different theoretical framework that unifies these approaches under information-theoretic principles.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors provide rigorous theoretical analyses including novel lower bounds (Theorems 2, 3) and upper bounds (Theorems 1, 4, 5) for various query models. The proofs are well-structured and leverage established information theory concepts appropriately. The experimental validation in Section 4 provides concrete evidence supporting the theoretical claims, showing that AND queries outperform "same cluster" queries in real crowdsourcing scenarios (105 vs. 234 errors in their experiment). The authors also thoughtfully address limitations of their approach (e.g., the need for prior knowledge of cluster sizes, the assumption of independent errors). The work is complete and not merely a position paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, with clear explanations of the problem setup and theoretical results. The connections between clustering, source coding, and query complexity are well-motivated. However, some sections could benefit from more detailed explanations of the intuition behind certain results. For instance, the explanation of why AND queries outperform XOR queries (Section 3.2) could be made more accessible to readers unfamiliar with rate-distortion theory. The experimental setup description is clear, though more details about the volunteer demographics and potential biases in the crowdsourcing experiment would strengthen the validation.

**Originality:** The paper makes significant original contributions. The equivalence between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is novel and provides a fresh perspective on a problem that has been studied extensively in the context of crowdsourcing and clustering. The authors' key insight that AND queries substantially outperform "same cluster" queries for approximate recovery is particularly noteworthy. While related concepts like locally decodable codes (Makhdoumi et al. 2015) and smooth compression (Montanari & Mossel 2008) exist, the authors establish a new connection to clustering that hasn't been previously explored. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work, particularly the NIPS 2017 paper by Mazumdar & Saha on clustering with noisy queries.

**Significance:** The results have clear significance for both theory and practice. The theoretical insights advance our understanding of fundamental limits in clustering with side information. The practical implications are substantial: the authors demonstrate that using AND queries instead of "same cluster" queries can reduce the number of required queries by orders of magnitude in many scenarios (e.g., for (1-δ)-recovery with δ=0.05, AND queries require O(n log 1/δ) queries versus O(n log n) for "same cluster" queries). The real-world crowdsourcing experiment provides compelling evidence that this theoretical advantage translates to practical benefits. The work also opens new research directions in information-theoretic approaches to clustering.

**Strengths:**
- Novel theoretical connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding
- Rigorous information-theoretic lower and upper bounds for query complexity
- Practical proposal of AND queries as superior to standard "same cluster" queries
- Strong experimental validation in real crowdsourcing settings
- Clear theoretical guarantees for noisy query scenarios
- Well-motivated and well-structured presentation

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could provide more intuition for why AND queries outperform XOR queries (beyond the technical rate-distortion argument)
- The assumption of knowing cluster size priors (p) may be restrictive in some applications; the paper acknowledges this but could better address how to estimate p in practice
- The experimental section, while useful, could include more details about the volunteer demographics and potential biases
- The connection to other NIPS papers could be more explicitly drawn in the related work section

**Pro/Con Arguments:**

**Pro:**
- The paper establishes a fundamentally new perspective on clustering with side information that has both theoretical depth and practical implications
- The AND query approach provides a substantial theoretical and practical improvement over existing methods
- The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs and experimental validation
- The work directly addresses a practical problem in crowdsourcing with clear real-world applications

**Con:**
- The assumption of known cluster size priors (p) may limit applicability in some scenarios
- The paper could better explain why AND queries are easier for crowd workers to answer (beyond the theoretical argument)
- The experimental validation, while promising, is limited to a single dataset (IMDB movies)

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a strong contribution that advances our understanding of semisupervised clustering from an information-theoretic perspective. The theoretical insights are novel, the results are well-supported by rigorous analysis, and the practical validation demonstrates clear benefits over existing approaches. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a significant problem with practical relevance to crowdsourcing applications. The paper is highly relevant to NIPS as it bridges theoretical information theory with practical machine learning applications.

I recommend acceptance. The paper's novel theoretical framework and practical improvements make it a valuable contribution to the field. The minor weaknesses noted are easily addressable in revision and do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.
