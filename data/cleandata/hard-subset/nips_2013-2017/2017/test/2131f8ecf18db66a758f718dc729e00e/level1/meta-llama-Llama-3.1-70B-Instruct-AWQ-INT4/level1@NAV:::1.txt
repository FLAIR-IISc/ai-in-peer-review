**Summary of Main Ideas**

This paper introduces a novel approach to semisupervised clustering by formulating it as a locally encodable source coding problem. The authors show that this formulation leads to new information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required to recover the correct labeling. They also propose a new querying strategy based on AND operations that performs better than traditional 'same cluster' queries. The paper further extends the results to the case of noisy queries and approximate recovery.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds upon previous work on semisupervised clustering, crowdsourcing, and information theory. The authors cite several papers on clustering with noisy queries, entity resolution, and locally decodable source coding. The paper's contribution is to bridge the gap between these areas by formulating semisupervised clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel formulation**: The paper introduces a new and innovative way of formulating semisupervised clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem.
2. **New information-theoretic lower bounds**: The authors derive new lower bounds on the number of queries required to recover the correct labeling, which are tighter than previous bounds.
3. **Improved querying strategy**: The paper proposes a new querying strategy based on AND operations that performs better than traditional 'same cluster' queries.
4. **Theoretical and experimental validation**: The authors provide both theoretical and experimental validation of their results, including a real crowdsourcing experiment.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited scope**: The paper focuses primarily on the theoretical aspects of semisupervised clustering and may not provide a comprehensive solution for practical applications.
2. **Assumptions**: The authors make several assumptions, such as the independence of query answers and the use of a specific prior distribution, which may not hold in all scenarios.
3. **Complexity**: The paper's theoretical results and proofs may be challenging to follow for non-experts in information theory and coding theory.

**Originality**

The paper's formulation of semisupervised clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem is novel and original. The authors' use of information-theoretic tools to derive new lower bounds and propose an improved querying strategy is also innovative.

**Significance**

The paper's results have significant implications for semisupervised clustering and crowdsourcing applications. The authors' improved querying strategy and information-theoretic lower bounds can help optimize the number of queries required to recover the correct labeling, leading to more efficient and effective clustering algorithms.
