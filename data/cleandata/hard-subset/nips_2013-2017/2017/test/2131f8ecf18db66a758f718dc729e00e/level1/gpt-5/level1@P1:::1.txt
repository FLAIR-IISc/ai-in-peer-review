Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies semisupervised clustering with limited, parallelizable queries to an oracle, and shows a tight connection with locally encodable source coding: each query answer is a compressed bit that depends on at most Δ input labels. This perspective yields information-theoretic lower bounds and suggests query designs beyond the ubiquitous “same-cluster?” (XOR) queries. The authors introduce pairwise AND queries and prove they can substantially reduce query complexity for approximate recovery, particularly under noise. They further propose a simple nonadaptive algorithm for k classes that achieves (1−δ)-accurate labeling with O(n log(k/δ)) pairwise queries under an independent noise model. Empirical evidence from a small crowdsourcing experiment suggests AND queries are not only more sample-efficient but also easier for workers.

The work relates to NIPS literature on clustering with queries and crowdsourcing (e.g., Ashtiani et al. 2016; Karger-Oh-Shah 2011–2014; Vinayak-Hassibi 2016; entity-resolution works), as well as to information-theoretic notions of local decodability/smooth compression (Makhdoumi et al.; Montanari-Mossel; Chandar et al.). The “locally encodable source coding” viewpoint appears novel and conceptually clarifying.

Quality
- Theory: The paper provides upper and lower bounds for exact recovery (noiseless, k=2) with Δ-local queries using random sparse XOR encodings and ML decoding, showing sublinear-in-n per-element query rate improvements over naïve schemes when p is imbalanced. The lower bound (Thm 2) leverages an averaging argument on per-item query participation and volume bounds. For approximate recovery, a general lower bound (Thm 3) under Δ-local constraints is stated (proof deferred), and an achievability via pairwise AND queries is analyzed (Thm 4) using a Poisson-degree approximation. For noisy queries and k>2, the thresholding algorithm (Alg. 1) on a d-regular graph achieves O(n log(k/δ)) queries (Thm 5), with concentration handled via dependency graph bounds; Thm 6 relaxes the need for priors to knowledge of cluster sizes.

- Soundness and gaps:
  - XOR achievability (Thm 1) uses typicality and expurgation with ML decoding on a random Δ-sparse linear code. This is information-theoretically sound, though ML is computationally intractable; this is acknowledged implicitly but should be noted when discussing practicality.
  - The lower bound (Thm 2) is interesting and improves over nH(p) in some regimes. However, notation is confusing: products vs exponents with Δ (e.g., “r(p)∆” vs “(1−2r(p))^Δ”) and the exact argument of the binary entropy function should be clarified to avoid dimensional inconsistencies.
  - The Δ-local approximate lower bound (Thm 3) is a key contribution but the main paper omits proof and intuition; at least a sketch would help assess tightness.
  - The XOR suboptimality for approximate recovery leans on a Massey conjecture (proved for p=1/2) to argue lower bounds for linear (XOR-based) schemes. While the empirical and alternative arguments (e.g., O(n log n) for random XOR with ML) support the claim, explicit formal separations independent of the conjecture would strengthen the paper.
  - In the noisy k-ary AND scheme, the query count is repeatedly written as “nd2” while the construction uses a d-regular graph whose number of edges is nd/2. The experiment with n=200, d=10 uses 1000 queries, consistent with nd/2. This typo recurs (Alg. 1 title, Sec. 3.3) and should be corrected throughout; otherwise the scaling statement is ambiguous.
  - The dependency bound in the concentration step (Sec. 3.3 end) underestimates the number of dependent variables: 2-hop neighborhoods in a d-regular graph are O(d^2), not “d + d”. This likely affects constants in the tail bounds but not the asymptotic O(n log(k/δ)) scaling; still, it should be fixed.
  - Independence assumptions: answers to different queries are assumed independent; this is standard in the literature but nontrivial in practice. The paper is careful about not allowing repeated identical queries, which is reasonable.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, with good motivation and links to prior work. However, several notational issues hamper readability:
  - Consistent use of “nd/2” vs “nd^2” for the number of queries.
  - Clarify whether “Δ” denotes multiplication or exponent in several formulas; define α and r(p) consistently.
  - Provide at least a proof sketch or intuition for Thm 3 in the main text.
  - State clearly the computational status of the ML decoders (infeasible vs conceptual optimum) vs the efficient thresholding algorithm.

Originality
- The locally encodable source coding perspective for query-based clustering appears novel and fruitful.
- The identification and analysis of AND queries as superior to XOR for approximate recovery (and under noise) is a fresh and practically relevant insight.
- New lower bounds under Δ-local constraints and an efficient O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive algorithm with pairwise queries contribute beyond prior work on same-cluster queries and triangle queries.

Significance
- The theoretical bounds help chart fundamental limits when per-query locality is constrained.
- The AND-query idea is actionable for crowdsourcing, where binary answers are desirable and different questions incur different cognitive loads; the small experiment indicating lower worker error for AND vs XOR is encouraging.
- The algorithm’s simplicity and linear-time decoding make it a plausible baseline for practitioners.

Pros
- Conceptually novel connection to local encoding; comprehensive theoretical treatment (noiseless/noisy; exact/approximate).
- Clear identification of limitations of same-cluster (XOR) queries and a principled alternative (AND).
- Simple, scalable algorithm with near-optimal query complexity for approximate recovery under noise.
- Empirical validation (albeit small) illustrating both sample efficiency and reduced worker error for AND queries.

Cons
- Notational/typographical inconsistencies (query counts; Δ usage) and a likely error in dependency counting.
- Reliance on a conjectured lower bound for linear/XOR schemes to argue a clean separation.
- ML-based achievability for XOR is not computationally feasible; practical algorithms for exact recovery are not provided.
- Experiments are limited in scale and baselines; only one domain and a small number of volunteers.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix all instances of the query count and dependency neighborhood sizes; clarify Δ’s role in formulas.
- Add a proof sketch and intuition for Thm 3 in the main paper; quantify its tightness vs achievability.
- Provide an unconditional lower bound showing suboptimality of XOR for approximate recovery in at least some regimes (beyond the p=1/2 case).
- Expand empirical evaluation: include synthetic studies across p, q, k with larger n; compare to majority-vote label queries, triangle queries, and existing same-cluster algorithms; report runtimes.
- Discuss worker interface design for k-ary AND queries and cost per query vs binary.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a compelling theoretical framework and practical insight (AND queries) with provable gains, despite clarity issues and limited experiments. Addressing the noted inconsistencies and strengthening the empirical/linear-vs-nonlinear separation would make it a strong accept.
