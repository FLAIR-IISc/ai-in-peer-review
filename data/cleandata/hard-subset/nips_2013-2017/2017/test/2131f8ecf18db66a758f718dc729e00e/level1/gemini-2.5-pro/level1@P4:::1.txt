### Paper Summary

This paper investigates the problem of semisupervised clustering, where the goal is to determine the correct labels for `n` items belonging to `k` clusters by making a minimum number of nonadaptive queries. The key contribution is the establishment of a formal equivalence between this clustering problem and the information-theoretic problem of locally encodable source coding, where each compressed bit (a query answer) depends on a limited number of input bits (a small subset of `Δ` labels). This novel framework allows the authors to derive fundamental limits and design efficient querying schemes.

The authors analyze three primary scenarios:
1.  **Noiseless Queries for Exact Recovery:** For the binary case (k=2), the paper provides upper and lower bounds on the number of pairwise 'same cluster' (XOR) queries required for perfect label recovery. The achievability scheme demonstrates that for certain prior label probabilities, fewer than `n` queries are sufficient, and the new lower bound improves upon the standard entropy-based bound `nh(p)` in some regimes.
2.  **Noiseless Queries for Approximate Recovery:** Here, the goal is to correctly recover a `(1-δ)` fraction of the labels. The paper presents a new lower bound for this setting and argues that 'same cluster' (XOR) queries are fundamentally suboptimal, drawing on classical results from information theory. As a constructive alternative, the authors propose pairwise 'AND' queries and show they can achieve a better trade-off between the number of queries and the recovery error `δ`.
3.  **Noisy Queries for Approximate Recovery:** This section presents the paper's main algorithmic contribution. For the general `k`-cluster case with noisy query answers, the authors generalize the AND query and propose an algorithm based on querying edges of a `d`-regular graph. The algorithm uses a simple thresholding decoder and is shown to be `(1-δ)`-good, requiring only `O(n log(k/δ))` queries. This significantly improves upon the `O(n log n)` complexity of previous approaches for this setting.

The theoretical results are supported by a real-world crowdsourcing experiment on movie genre classification, which validates the performance of the proposed algorithm and empirically confirms the claim that AND queries can have lower error rates than 'same cluster' queries.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the problem as locally encodable source coding is rigorous and provides a solid foundation for the subsequent analysis. The derivations of the lower and upper bounds in Sections 3.1 and 3.2 appear correct, leveraging established tools from information and coding theory. The analysis of Algorithm 1 in Section 3.3, which relies on Chernoff bounds to prove the performance guarantee, is a standard and well-executed approach. The claims are well-supported by theoretical proofs, providing a complete piece of work.

A minor weakness is that some proofs rely on common but simplifying assumptions. For instance, the proof of Theorem 4 models node degrees with a Poisson distribution, which is an approximation for the true distribution in the random graph model used. Additionally, the final step in the proof of Theorem 5, which uses a result from Alon and Spencer to show concentration of the total error, could benefit from a more detailed explanation of why the conditions of the cited corollary are met.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates it within the literature on crowdsourcing and clustering. The progression from the noiseless, exact recovery case to the more general noisy, approximate recovery setting is easy to follow. The information-theoretic perspective is introduced clearly in Section 2.

However, there are areas where clarity could be improved. The proof of Theorem 1 is quite dense and assumes significant familiarity with concepts like "typical sets" and "expurgation," which may be challenging for readers outside of information theory. A more intuitive explanation alongside the formal proof would enhance accessibility. A significant clarity issue exists in the description of Algorithm 1. The condition `if Nu,i ≥ dCie` in the text is confusing; the symbol `e` is undefined and its role is unclear. This appears to be a typo that hinders the reader's ability to fully understand and reproduce the algorithm's thresholding step. Finally, the figure captions could be more descriptive; for example, Figure 1 should explicitly label the upper and lower bound curves.

**Originality:** The paper's primary contribution is highly original. The connection drawn between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding is a novel conceptual leap that opens up a new avenue for analysis. Previous work, such as that by Mazumdar and Saha (2017), has studied noisy queries, but this paper provides a more foundational, information-theoretic perspective. The identification of the sub-optimality of standard 'same cluster' queries for approximate recovery and the proposal of 'AND' queries as a provably better alternative is a significant and novel idea. The resulting query complexity of `O(n log(k/δ))` for the noisy, approximate setting is a substantial theoretical improvement over existing results.

**Significance:** The results are important for both researchers and practitioners in areas like crowdsourcing, active learning, and entity resolution. The paper offers a new theoretical framework that can be used to analyze a wide range of query-based learning problems. The practical takeaway—that the choice of query type (e.g., AND vs. XOR) has a fundamental impact on performance—is a valuable insight that could guide the design of future crowdsourcing systems. The proposed algorithm for the noisy setting provides a state-of-the-art result in terms of query complexity, addressing a difficult and practical problem in a more efficient way than previous research. This work is likely to be built upon by others studying the fundamental limits of interactive and nonadaptive learning.

### Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance

**Pro:**
1.  **Novel Framework:** Establishes a novel and powerful connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Results:** Provides new lower bounds and efficient achievability schemes, significantly advancing the theoretical understanding of the problem.
3.  **Impactful Contribution:** The demonstration that 'same cluster' queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery and that 'AND' queries perform better is a key insight with practical implications.
4.  **State-of-the-Art Complexity:** The `O(n log(k/δ))` query complexity for the noisy, approximate recovery setting is a substantial improvement over prior work.
5.  **Experimental Validation:** The theoretical claims are supported by a real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

**Con:**
1.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** The paper contains some dense theoretical explanations and a potentially confusing typo in the description of its main algorithm (Algorithm 1), which could be clarified.
2.  **Practical Assumptions:** The main algorithm's analysis assumes knowledge of the prior label distribution and the oracle's noise rate, which may not always be readily available in practice.
