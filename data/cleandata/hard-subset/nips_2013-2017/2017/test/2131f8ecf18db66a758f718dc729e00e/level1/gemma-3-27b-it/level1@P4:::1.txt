This paper establishes an intriguing connection between semisupervised clustering with binary queries and locally encodable source coding. The core idea—modeling the querying process as a compression problem—allows the authors to derive lower bounds on the number of queries needed for clustering and to propose a new querying strategy (AND queries) that outperforms traditional ‘same cluster’ queries in certain scenarios. The paper is well-motivated, tackling a relevant problem in crowdsourcing and entity resolution.

**Summary of Contributions:** The authors demonstrate the equivalence between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding. They leverage this equivalence to establish information-theoretic lower bounds on query complexity. Furthermore, they introduce AND queries as an alternative to traditional ‘same cluster’ queries, proving their superior performance in approximate recovery scenarios. They also analyze the problem under noisy oracle responses and provide a scheme with logarithmic query complexity.  Finally, experimental validation on real datasets supports their theoretical findings.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Connection:** The linkage between clustering and locally encodable source coding is a novel and insightful contribution.  It opens up new avenues for applying tools from information theory to clustering problems.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides theoretical guarantees, including lower bounds and algorithmic analysis. The proofs appear well-structured and logically sound (though detailed verification requires further scrutiny of the supplementary material).
* **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed – minimizing query cost in crowdsourced labeling – is highly relevant in many applications. The proposed AND query strategy offers a potential practical improvement.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:** The authors consider noisy query responses, a realistic scenario in crowdsourcing, and develop algorithms to address this challenge.
* **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of experiments, even with a limited scope, adds credibility to the theoretical results. The observation that AND queries perform better in practice is encouraging.
* **Clear Structure:** The paper is generally well-organized, with a clear introduction of the problem, related work, and the main contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are a positive aspect, they are relatively small-scale.  A more extensive evaluation on diverse datasets would strengthen the claims. The description of the crowdsourcing setup is also somewhat brief and could benefit from more detail.
* **Assumptions on Oracle Noise:**  The assumption of independent errors for different queries, while common, may not hold in all real-world scenarios. Exploring the impact of correlated errors could be valuable.
* **Complexity of Theorems:** Some of the theorems, particularly those involving noisy queries, are presented with a high level of mathematical notation, potentially hindering accessibility for readers less familiar with information theory.
* **Relationship to Existing Work:** While the authors cite related work, a more in-depth discussion of how this approach differs from existing active learning strategies (beyond just noting the non-adaptive nature of the queries) would be beneficial.
* **Minor Clarifications Needed:** The explanation of why repeating queries is disallowed (avoiding theoretical uninteresting results) feels slightly weak. A stronger justification might improve the argument.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound connection between two seemingly disparate fields (clustering and source coding).
* The theoretical results—lower bounds and algorithmic analysis—are significant and contribute to a better understanding of the query complexity of clustering.
* The proposed AND query strategy is a practical improvement over existing methods, as demonstrated by the experimental results.
* The work is well-written and clearly structured, making it accessible to a broad audience of NIPS attendees.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental validation is limited and could be more comprehensive.
* The high level of mathematical notation in some sections may make the paper less accessible.
* While novel, the practical impact beyond the theoretical insights remains to be fully demonstrated.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. The novel connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding is particularly compelling. While the experimental validation could be more extensive, the theoretical results and the potential for practical improvements make this a worthwhile contribution to the NIPS community.

**Recommendation:**  **Accept** (with minor revisions addressing the points raised in the weaknesses section, particularly expanding the experimental evaluation).
