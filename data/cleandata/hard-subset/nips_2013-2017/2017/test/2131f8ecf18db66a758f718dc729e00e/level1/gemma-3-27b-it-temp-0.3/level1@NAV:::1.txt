## Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper establishes a surprising connection between semisupervised clustering with pairwise queries and locally encodable source coding. The core idea is to frame the problem of learning cluster labels from noisy oracle responses as a compression problem where each compressed bit depends on a limited number of input bits (the labels of queried elements). This connection allows the authors to leverage information-theoretic tools, deriving lower bounds on the number of queries required for clustering and demonstrating the limitations of using simple ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries. They propose AND queries as a potentially more efficient alternative, particularly in scenarios with noisy oracles, and validate this with experiments on real datasets.

The paper builds upon recent work in crowdsourcing for clustering (e.g., [4, 33, 30]) and connects it to the less-explored area of locally encodable source coding [23]. It appropriately positions itself within the broader context of information theory, referencing relevant concepts like rate-distortion theory [7] and locally decodable codes [19]. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with clear statements of theorems and proofs (some details relegated to the supplementary material). The introduction of AND queries as a potentially superior alternative to XOR queries is a novel contribution, and the experimental validation adds credibility to the claims.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Connection:** The link between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is a significant and insightful contribution.
*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper provides strong theoretical foundations for its claims, with well-defined theorems and proofs.
*   **Practical Implications:** The proposal of AND queries offers a potentially more efficient approach to clustering with noisy oracles.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on real datasets provide evidence supporting the theoretical findings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** The theoretical analysis is dense and may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with information theory.
*   **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are valuable, they are limited in scope. Further investigation with larger datasets and different noise models would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of independent errors in query answers, while common, may not always hold in real-world crowdsourcing scenarios.
*   **Clarity:** Some sections, particularly the introduction, could benefit from more concise and focused writing.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound connection between two seemingly disparate fields.
*   The proposed AND queries offer a potentially significant improvement over existing approaches.
*   The experimental results provide preliminary evidence supporting the theoretical claims.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical complexity may limit the paper's accessibility to a broader audience.
*   The experimental validation is limited in scope.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel contribution and rigorous analysis. While the theoretical complexity and limited experimental scope are minor concerns, the potential impact of the findings justifies acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper to NIPS.
