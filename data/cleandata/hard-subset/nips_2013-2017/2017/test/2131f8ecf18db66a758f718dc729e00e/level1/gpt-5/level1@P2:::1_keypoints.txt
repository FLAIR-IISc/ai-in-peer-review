- Paper formulates non-adaptive semi-supervised clustering with Δ-local oracle queries as locally encodable source coding, yielding information-theoretic bounds and query schemes.
- For k=2: shows exact recovery can require strictly fewer than n pairwise XOR queries for some priors via random sparse parity encoding and ML decoding.
- Proves XOR (“same-cluster”) queries are provably suboptimal for approximate recovery and proposes AND queries with better query–error trade-offs.
- For noisy answers and k≥2, gives a simple pairwise AND-like k-ary query on a degree-d regular graph with a trivial decoder achieving (1−δ)-accuracy using m = O(n log(k/δ)) queries.
- Validates noisy algorithm on a small crowdsourcing experiment and reports fewer errors for AND vs same-cluster.
- Theoretical derivations are generally sound, with Theorems 1–6 covering achievability, converses, and noisy analysis using typicality, Poissonization, inclusion–exclusion, and concentration/graph dependency arguments.
- Main caveat: exact-recovery achievability is existential using ML decoding over a sparse linear code—no efficient decoder provided.
- Caveat: noisy k≥2 scheme assumes k-ary outputs, which departs from earlier binary-answer practicality claims and lacks thorough human-feasibility comparison.
- Caveat: several O(·) bounds and thresholds omit explicit constants, complicating practical parameter selection.
- Caveat: assumptions of independent query errors and no repeated queries are strong and not compared to redundancy/worker-quality methods like Dawid–Skene.
- Paper clarity: well organized and readable overall, but contains typos, notational inconsistency, and some deferred proofs; implementability/complexity discussion could be clearer.
- Originality: locally encodable coding formulation and the Massey-based argument showing XOR limitations, plus proposing AND queries, are novel and insightful.
- Significance: sharpens theoretical understanding of locality-constrained query complexity and offers an actionable AND-vs-XOR message, but practical impact limited by small-scale experiments.
- Pros: principled information-theoretic framework, novel converse bounds, important insight that XOR is limited for approximate recovery, and a simple scalable noisy algorithm.
- Cons: existential (exponential-time) exact-recovery result, no comparison to adaptive or redundancy-based crowdsourcing methods, practicality concerns over k-ary queries, and limited experimental evaluation.
- Suggestions: clarify decoding complexity and explore efficient decoders (e.g., belief propagation), provide explicit constants for practical use, expand experiments with larger datasets and worker models, and discuss mapping k-ary answers to binary schemes.
- Recommendation: Weak accept — meaningful theoretical contributions and useful AND-query insight, tempered by limited empirical validation and lack of efficient exact-recovery decoding; confidence: medium.
