This paper establishes a surprising connection between semisupervised clustering with binary queries and locally encodable source coding. The authors leverage this equivalence to derive lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering, propose an AND-query scheme that outperforms standard ‘same cluster’ queries in many scenarios, and analyze the impact of noisy oracle responses. The work is mathematically rigorous, builds on concepts from information theory, and attempts validation through a crowdsourcing experiment.

**Summary of Related Work:** The paper correctly positions its work within the recent surge of interest in clustering via crowdsourcing (references [4, 33, 30, 8, 20]). It appropriately connects the problem to source coding and highlights the relatively unexplored area of locally encodable source coding, citing [23] as a key prior work. The authors demonstrate awareness of related concepts like locally decodable codes [19, 16] and smooth compression [23, 26]. The discussion of prior work regarding query complexity (e.g., [4, 21]) and crowdsourcing schemes ([12, 13, 28, 34, 15]) is comprehensive.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Connection:** The core contribution – establishing the equivalence between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding – is genuinely novel and insightful. This connection allows the authors to import tools and techniques from information theory to address the clustering problem.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is mathematically well-grounded, with clear definitions, rigorous proofs, and well-motivated theorems. The derivations of lower bounds and the analysis of the AND-query scheme are particularly strong.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The proposed AND-query scheme offers a potential improvement over existing approaches, and the analysis of noisy queries is crucial for real-world applications of crowdsourcing.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The authors attempt to validate their theoretical results through a crowdsourcing experiment using Amazon Mechanical Turk, providing some empirical support for their claims.
*   **Thoroughness:** The paper explores various scenarios, including noiseless queries, approximate recovery, and noisy queries, demonstrating a comprehensive treatment of the problem.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Organization:** While technically sound, the paper is dense and at times difficult to follow. The introduction could be more concise and focused on motivating the key contributions. The organization feels somewhat disjointed; for example, the discussion of locally encodable vs. locally decodable codes, while relevant, feels a little tangential.
*   **Experimental Setup:** The crowdsourcing experiment is limited in scope. While it provides some evidence, it would benefit from a more extensive evaluation on diverse datasets and with a larger number of participants. The parameter choices (e.g., d = 10) aren’t fully justified and could influence the results.  A more detailed comparison of the costs associated with the different query types (AND vs. Same Cluster) would strengthen the practical impact.
*   **Limited Discussion of Practical Challenges:** While the authors acknowledge the potential for errors in query answers, they don’t delve deeply into other practical challenges of crowdsourcing, such as worker bias, spamming, or the difficulty of designing effective query interfaces.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of independent errors in query answers (even with repetition prohibited) may not always hold in real-world scenarios. Exploring alternative error models could be valuable. The use of a known prior distribution (p) while presented as often easily estimated, is still a strong assumption.  How sensitive are the results to inaccuracies in the estimated prior?
*   **Referencing:** The referencing is generally good, but a more thorough citation of work on active learning could be relevant, given the adaptive query selection aspects (though the paper focuses on non-adaptive queries).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically significant connection between two seemingly disparate fields.
*   The rigorous mathematical analysis and derived lower bounds contribute valuable insights to the understanding of query complexity in clustering.
*   The proposed AND-query scheme has the potential to improve the efficiency of crowdsourced clustering.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The paper's density and lack of clarity may make it difficult for some readers to fully grasp the contributions.
*   The experimental validation is limited and doesn’t provide strong evidence of practical effectiveness.
*   The lack of discussion of practical challenges and potential limitations weakens the impact of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field, despite its weaknesses. The novelty of the core idea and the rigor of the analysis outweigh the concerns regarding clarity and experimental validation. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  The authors should focus on improving the clarity of the presentation, providing a more detailed discussion of the experimental setup and results, and addressing the limitations outlined above. Specifically, expanding the experimental section and providing a more in-depth cost analysis would be beneficial.  A slight reorganization to improve flow and clarity of presentation would also be appreciated.
