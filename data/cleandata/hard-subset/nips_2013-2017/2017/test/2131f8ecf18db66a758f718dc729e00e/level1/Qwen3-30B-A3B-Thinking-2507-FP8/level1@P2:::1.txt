

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper establishes a novel equivalence between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, providing theoretical insights and practical improvements for query-based clustering. The authors show that the problem of recovering cluster labels via a limited number of simple queries (with possible errors) is equivalent to a locally encodable source coding problem, where each compressed bit depends on only a constant number of input bits. This connection allows them to derive information-theoretic lower bounds and propose improved query strategies, specifically advocating for AND queries over the commonly used 'same cluster' (XOR) queries.

The paper builds on recent work in crowdsourcing-based clustering (e.g., Ashtiani et al., NIPS 2016; Mazumdar & Saha, NIPS 2017) but makes a significant theoretical leap by connecting this problem to information theory. While locally decodable source coding has been studied (e.g., Makhdoumi et al., ISIT 2015), the authors are the first to establish the equivalence to semisupervised clustering and develop a comprehensive theory of locally encodable source coding for this specific application. The paper also extends prior work on query complexity for clustering (e.g., Ashtiani et al., NIPS 2016; Karger et al., NIPS 2014) by showing fundamental limitations of pairwise 'same cluster' queries and proposing a superior alternative.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide both lower bounds and matching upper bounds for various scenarios (exact recovery, approximate recovery with noise), and the proofs are well-structured and complete. The real-world crowdsourcing experiment validates the theoretical claims, demonstrating that AND queries yield significantly fewer errors (105 vs. 234) than 'same cluster' queries on a real dataset. The paper is careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the need for prior knowledge of cluster sizes.

*Clarity:* The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem setup is thoroughly explained, and the connection to source coding is developed logically. The theorems and proofs are presented with sufficient detail, and the experimental section clearly describes the methodology and results. The figures effectively illustrate the theoretical results and experimental validation.

*Originality:* The core contribution—establishing the equivalence between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding—is highly original. The authors demonstrate that AND queries outperform 'same cluster' queries in multiple scenarios, which is a significant practical contribution. The paper extends the theory of locally encodable source coding, a topic that has received little attention in information theory (only mentioned in Montanari & Mossel, ISIT 2008). The work clearly differentiates itself from prior NIPS papers on clustering with queries (e.g., Ashtiani et al., NIPS 2016; Mazumdar & Saha, NIPS 2017) by providing a fundamental theoretical framework.

*Significance:* The paper addresses a practical problem in crowdsourcing (minimizing query costs while maintaining accuracy) with a theoretically grounded solution. The AND query approach has been validated with real crowdsourcing experiments, showing substantial improvements in error rates. The theoretical insights into locally encodable source coding have potential applications beyond clustering, potentially benefiting other problems with similar query constraints. The paper provides a new perspective on query design that could significantly impact practical implementations of crowdsourced clustering.

**Weaknesses:**

*Quality:* While the theoretical results are strong, the paper assumes that the prior distribution of labels (or relative cluster sizes) is known, which may not always be the case in practice. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not explore how to estimate these priors from limited data. The paper also does not thoroughly address the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms (beyond asymptotic analysis), which could be a practical concern for large-scale applications.

*Clarity:* The technical depth of the proofs (particularly in the supplementary material) may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with information theory. Some of the more complex theorems could benefit from more intuitive explanations or simplified versions for the main paper.

*Originality:* While the equivalence between clustering and source coding is novel, the specific use of AND queries as a replacement for 'same cluster' queries may seem somewhat incremental to some readers. However, the authors convincingly demonstrate that this is not merely a minor improvement but a fundamental shift in query strategy with significant theoretical and practical benefits.

*Significance:* The paper's significance is primarily theoretical with practical implications. While the crowdsourcing experiment validates the approach, the paper doesn't fully explore how this approach scales to extremely large datasets or how it would integrate with existing large-scale entity resolution systems.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Groundbreaking theoretical connection between clustering and source coding
- Clear demonstration of AND queries' superiority over standard approaches
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with matching lower and upper bounds
- Validated with real crowdsourcing experiment showing practical benefits
- Well-written and well-structured paper

*Con:*
- Assumption of known prior distributions may limit practical applicability
- Limited discussion of computational complexity for large-scale applications
- Some technical depth may be challenging for non-specialists

Overall, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that bridges information theory and machine learning, with clear practical implications for crowdsourced clustering. The authors have established a new framework for analyzing query-based clustering that is both theoretically sound and practically validated. The paper is of high quality, original, and significant, and it makes a clear contribution to the field of machine learning. I recommend acceptance.
