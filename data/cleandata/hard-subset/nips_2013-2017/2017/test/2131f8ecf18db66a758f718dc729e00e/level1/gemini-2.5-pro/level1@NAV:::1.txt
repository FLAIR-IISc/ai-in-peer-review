This paper establishes a novel and insightful connection between semi-supervised clustering with non-adaptive queries and the information theory problem of locally encodable source coding. The authors consider the problem of recovering `n` labels from `k` classes by asking a minimal number of queries, where each query involves a small, constant number of elements. This setup is well-motivated by crowdsourcing applications. By framing the problem through this information-theoretic lens, the authors derive new lower bounds on the required number of queries for both exact and approximate recovery. A key contribution is the proposal of "AND queries," which are shown to be provably more efficient than the commonly used "same-cluster" (XOR) queries, particularly for approximate recovery. The main algorithmic result is an efficient scheme for the noisy oracle setting that recovers labels with (1-δ) accuracy using O(n log(k/δ)) queries, a significant improvement over prior work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength is its highly original formulation of the query-based clustering problem in the language of locally encodable source coding. This is a significant conceptual contribution that opens up new analytical avenues. The introduction and analysis of AND queries is another major strength, providing a practical and demonstrably superior alternative to standard query types. The results significantly advance the state of the art in query complexity for this problem.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound and comprehensive. The authors provide a rigorous analysis with both upper and lower bounds for multiple settings (noiseless/noisy, exact/approximate). The claims are well-supported not only by theoretical proofs but also by a real-world crowdsourcing experiment, which compellingly validates the theoretical advantage of AND queries and the proposed algorithm's performance.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the contributions are laid out explicitly, making the paper easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The analysis relies on standard assumptions, such as i.i.d. priors for labels and a symmetric noise model for the oracle. While common in theoretical work, exploring robustness to more complex, structured data or adversarial noise could be a valuable extension.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The novel connection to source coding provides a new and powerful framework for a relevant problem.
*   The paper introduces a superior query type with strong theoretical and experimental backing.
*   The O(n log(k/δ)) query complexity for the noisy case is a substantial improvement.

This is a high-quality paper with significant contributions in originality, technical depth, and potential impact. It bridges ideas from information theory and machine learning in a compelling way. I strongly recommend acceptance.
