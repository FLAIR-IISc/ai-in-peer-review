### Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper establishes a novel and insightful connection between the problem of non-adaptive, query-based clustering and the information-theoretic concept of locally encodable source coding. The problem setup involves determining the true labels of `n` items, belonging to one of `k` classes, by asking a batch of non-adaptive queries. Each query involves a small, constant number of items (∆), and an oracle provides a (potentially noisy) answer. The goal is to minimize the number of queries required for either exact or approximate recovery of all labels.

The authors' key conceptual contribution is to frame this as data compression: the vector of labels is the source `X` to be compressed, and the vector of query answers is the compressed representation `Y`. The constraint that each query involves only ∆ items means each bit of `Y` depends on only ∆ bits of `X`, which defines a locally encodable source code. This framework allows the authors to derive information-theoretic lower bounds and develop near-optimal querying schemes.

The main contributions are:
1.  **Exact Recovery (Noiseless):** For `k=2` clusters, the paper analyzes standard 'same-cluster' (XOR) queries. It provides an upper bound on the number of queries that is provably less than `n` for certain priors, and a new lower bound that improves upon the standard entropy bound `nH(p)` in some regimes.
2.  **Approximate Recovery (Noiseless):** The paper argues that 'same-cluster' queries are suboptimal for recovering a `(1-δ)` fraction of labels correctly. It introduces 'AND' queries (`Q(u,v)=1` iff both `u,v` are in cluster 1) and shows they can achieve a better distortion-rate tradeoff than is possible with XOR queries.
3.  **Approximate Recovery (Noisy):** This section presents the main algorithmic result. The authors propose a generalized AND query for `k` classes and a simple, efficient decoding algorithm. They prove that with `m = O(n log(k/δ))` queries, their method correctly recovers `(1-δ)` of the labels, even when query answers are noisy. This result is validated with a real crowdsourcing experiment.

This work connects to several lines of research, including active clustering and crowdsourcing [4, 21], where the goal is to minimize queries, and information theory, particularly the less-studied area of locally encodable codes [23], in contrast to the more common locally decodable codes.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Conceptual Contribution:** The connection drawn between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding is highly original and powerful. It provides a new theoretical lens through which to analyze this problem and allows for the derivation of fundamental limits. This is a significant intellectual contribution that bridges two distinct fields.

2.  **Novel Query Type and Justification:** The paper makes a compelling case for moving beyond the standard 'same-cluster' (XOR) query. The introduction of the 'AND' query is well-motivated, both theoretically (showing its superiority for approximate recovery based on rate-distortion arguments) and practically (arguing it is easier for human annotators, supported by experimental data on error rates). This is a valuable and actionable insight.

3.  **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper is technically dense and provides a range of novel theoretical results. The lower bound for exact recovery (Theorem 2), the lower bound for approximate recovery with local queries (Theorem 3), and especially the achievability result for the noisy case (Theorem 5) are all strong contributions. The `O(n log(k/δ))` query complexity for noisy, approximate recovery is a particularly significant result, demonstrating that a good approximation is possible with a number of queries linear in `n` but only logarithmic in the desired precision `1/δ`.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the information-theoretic framework is explained well, and the main results are stated formally.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Asymmetry of the Proposed AND Query:** The main algorithmic result (Sec 3.3) relies on a query that is asymmetric: `Q(X, X') = i` if `X = X' = i`, and `0` otherwise. This implicitly treats class `0` as a default or 'negative' class. This is suitable for binary classification (e.g., 'action movie' vs. 'not action movie'), but its applicability to general multi-class clustering with `k` symmetric classes is unclear. Would one need to perform `k-1` separate rounds of queries, one for each non-default class? If so, this would multiply the query complexity by `(k-1)`, a significant factor that should be discussed.

2.  **Practicality of Assumptions:** The analysis assumes the prior probabilities `p` (or relative cluster sizes) are known. While the authors suggest these can be estimated, the effect of estimation error on the performance of the proposed algorithms (which use `p` to set thresholds) is not analyzed. Furthermore, the noise model is a simple binary symmetric channel, while real-world crowd noise can be more complex (e.g., adversarial or dependent on the specific items being queried).

3.  **Comparison to Baselines:** For the main result on noisy approximate recovery, the comparison is primarily against theoretical lower bounds for 'same-cluster' queries. A more direct comparison to practical algorithms would strengthen the paper. For instance, one could use noisy 'same-cluster' queries to construct a weighted graph and then apply a robust community detection algorithm (like spectral clustering, which was used in the synthetic experiment but not discussed in the main text). Showing that Algorithm 1 outperforms such a baseline under the same query budget would make the claims more convincing.

4.  **Technical Details in Proofs:** Some of the proof sketches rely on approximations or specific assumptions whose validity could be further clarified. For example, the proof of Theorem 4 uses a Poisson approximation for the node degrees in a random graph. The proof of Theorem 5 relies on a concentration inequality that holds for specific random graph models; it would be helpful to clarify if the d-regular graph construction used in the experiments satisfies the necessary conditions for the theoretical guarantees to hold.

**Decision**

Overall, this is a very strong paper that presents a highly original and technically deep contribution. The novel information-theoretic framework and the introduction of AND queries are significant advances for the field of query-based clustering. While there are some limitations regarding the practical assumptions and the generality of the proposed query type, the strengths in originality and theoretical depth far outweigh these weaknesses. The work is likely to inspire follow-up research at the intersection of machine learning and information theory. I strongly recommend acceptance.
