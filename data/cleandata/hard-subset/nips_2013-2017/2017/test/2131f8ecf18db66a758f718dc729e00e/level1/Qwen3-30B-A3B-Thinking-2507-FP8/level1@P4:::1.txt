

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper establishes a novel equivalence between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, providing theoretical insights into query complexity for clustering problems. The authors consider a setting where an oracle provides binary answers to queries about element labels, with the goal of minimizing the number of queries required to recover the labels. They show that pairwise "same cluster" (XOR) queries have fundamental limitations, and propose AND queries as a superior alternative that achieves better query complexity for approximate recovery.

The paper builds on prior NIPS work on clustering with side information (e.g., Ashtiani et al., "Clustering with same-cluster queries," NIPS 2016) but extends it significantly by establishing the connection to locally encodable source coding, a concept not previously studied in the information theory context for this problem. The authors provide both theoretical bounds and practical algorithms, with a real-world crowdsourcing experiment validating their approach.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical results. The authors provide lower bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) and upper bounds (Theorems 1, 4, 5) for various query settings (exact and approximate recovery, noiseless and noisy). The proofs are well-structured, with appropriate use of information-theoretic arguments and probabilistic analysis. The real crowdsourcing experiment (Section 4) effectively validates the theoretical claims, showing that AND queries achieve lower error rates (105 errors vs. 234 for "same cluster" queries) in practice. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the focus on pairwise queries and the assumption of independent errors.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem setup (Section 2) is concise and well-motivated, and the main results (Section 3) are presented with appropriate mathematical rigor. The theoretical results are supported by informative figures (Figures 1-5) that effectively illustrate the query-performance trade-offs. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithm 1) are precise and implementable. The only minor clarity issue is that the connection to information theory (locally encodable source coding) is introduced somewhat abruptly, though the authors do provide sufficient context.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant contribution by establishing the equivalence between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding. The authors' key insight that AND queries outperform XOR ("same cluster") queries for approximate recovery is novel and well-supported. While there is related work on clustering with side information (e.g., Ashtiani et al., NIPS 2016; Mazumdar & Saha, NIPS 2017), this paper's information-theoretic perspective and specific query design (AND queries) represent a meaningful advance beyond prior work. The paper properly cites relevant prior work, including recent NIPS papers on clustering with side information.

**Significance:** The results have both theoretical and practical significance. Theoretically, the paper establishes new information-theoretic bounds for locally encodable source coding, which is a well-defined but underexplored area. Practically, the AND query approach could substantially reduce the number of queries required in crowdsourcing applications, lowering costs and improving efficiency. The real crowdsourcing experiment demonstrates the practical viability of the approach, showing that AND queries are easier for workers to answer (105 errors vs. 234 for "same cluster" queries). The paper's results could guide future work in designing more efficient crowdsourcing schemes for clustering problems.

**Strengths:**
- Novel theoretical connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding
- Rigorous theoretical results with matching lower and upper bounds
- Practical AND query approach that outperforms traditional "same cluster" queries
- Validated by real crowdsourcing experiment
- Clear organization and well-motivated problem setup

**Weaknesses:**
- Focus on pairwise queries may limit applicability to scenarios where larger queries are feasible
- Requires knowledge of label prior distribution, which might not always be available
- Theoretical results for noisy case assume a specific query design (AND queries), which might not generalize to all clustering problems
- Limited discussion of how the results compare to other information-theoretic approaches to clustering

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that bridges information theory and clustering, with clear practical implications for crowdsourcing applications. The theoretical results are rigorous, the experimental validation is convincing, and the paper advances the state of the art in query-based clustering. The novelty is significant, the quality is high, and the results are important for both theoretical understanding and practical applications. The paper is well-written and clearly communicates its contributions.

The main limitation is the focus on pairwise queries, but the authors acknowledge this and provide a clear rationale for their choice. The paper's contributions are sufficiently distinct from prior NIPS work to warrant publication. The theoretical results and practical validation together provide a compelling case for the superiority of AND queries in this setting.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper makes a significant contribution to the field, with strong theoretical results and practical validation. The work is original, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in clustering with side information. The connection to information theory is novel and well-executed, and the experimental results provide compelling evidence of the practical utility of the approach.
