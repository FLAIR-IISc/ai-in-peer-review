Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonadaptive semi-supervised clustering via query-efficient crowdsourcing and establishes an equivalence to “locally encodable” source coding: each query/encoded bit is a function of at most Δ source labels. This perspective yields new information-theoretic lower bounds and query schemes. The authors argue that the widely used pairwise “same-cluster” (SC) query (binary XOR for k=2) is suboptimal for approximate recovery and propose pairwise AND queries, showing improved sample complexity for both noiseless and noisy settings.

Main technical contributions:
- Noiseless exact recovery (k=2): An achievability using random Δ-sparse XOR queries and ML decoding with m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries, α = 1/2(1 + (1 − 4p(1 − p))^Δ). A lower bound exploiting locality yields improvements over the classical nh(p) for certain p.
- Noiseless approximate recovery: A locality-aware lower bound (Theorem 3) augments the rate-distortion limit; SC (linear) queries are argued to be inherently suboptimal via Ancheta-Massey’s linear code limitations. A simple AND-based scheme with right-degree 2 and a per-node OR decoder achieves strictly better δ–m trade-offs than SC in plots.
- Noisy approximate recovery, general k: A k-ary AND-type query returning i if both endpoints have label i and 0 otherwise, with symmetric noise. With a d-regular query graph and a simple per-node thresholding rule, the algorithm achieves (1−δ)-recovery with m = O(n log(k/δ)) queries using Chernoff bounds and a dependency graph argument (Alon–Spencer). Small real and synthetic experiments support the claims and suggest AND is easier for workers than SC in practice.

The work builds on and contrasts with NIPS results on SC-based clustering and crowdsourcing [Ashtiani et al. (NIPS’16), Karger–Oh–Shah], smooth compression and local (de)coding [Montanari–Mossel, Makhdoumi et al.], and entity-resolution with noisy oracles. The connection to locally encodable source coding appears novel.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: The locally encodable source coding lens is fresh and fruitful. It clarifies how locality constraints fundamentally raise sample complexity beyond classical entropy/rate-distortion limits.
- New lower bounds: Theorems 2 and 3 quantify locality penalties; even when rough, they move beyond nh(p) and standard rate-distortion, and are of independent interest to information theory.
- Positive algorithmic result: The AND-based scheme in the noisy, multi-class setting achieves m=O(n log(k/δ)) with a simple linear-time decoder and clean concentration guarantees. This substantially improves on O(n log n) behaviors seen with SC under noise.
- Practical insight: The observation that AND is easier for workers than SC is supported by a small but suggestive user study; the asymmetry of AND provides a “gap” exploited by the decoder.
- Relation to prior work: The paper situates the results vis-à-vis SC-based clustering, crowdsourcing aggregation, and local coding/smooth compression.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Rigor of Theorem 1: The expurgation argument over “typical sequences” is loose. You argue existence of a query matrix with fewer than T collisions and then “throw out” source sequences; but the decoding task must succeed for (almost all) draws X∼p without expurgating possible X. Please reframe as a standard random-coding argument bounding the ML error probability over X in the typical set via a union bound over pairs, avoiding expurgation of source sequences. Also clarify where the probability is taken (over random queries and X).
- Computational issues for exact recovery: The ML decoder implied by Theorem 1 is exponential-time. Either temper the claim to information-theoretic achievability or provide an efficient decoder (even heuristic) with guarantees.
- Theorem 2 proof sketch: The “Hamming ball around Y0” argument assumes independence and typical distances; formalize the counting/entropy step and the choice of ρ. A more explicit Fano-type bound conditioned on low-degree elements would strengthen the result.
- Theorem 3 is central but its proof is deferred and the statement is hard to parse (missing parentheses and constants). Given its importance, include a clear, self-contained proof or at least a detailed sketch explaining where locality enters (e.g., via hypercontractivity/influence or constrained mutual information per query).
- Comparison to best SC baselines: The empirical comparison uses spectral clustering on SC answers; under your noise model and nonadaptive sampling, stronger SC baselines exist (e.g., likelihood-based inference, SDP relaxations, or message passing), including approximate recovery with side information. A fair comparison should use competitive SC decoders and report m vs δ under matched budgets.
- Noise/prior estimation: Algorithm 1 assumes known p and q (or cluster sizes). In practice these are unknown; please discuss consistent estimation (e.g., method-of-moments on counts Nu,i) and robustness. Theorem 6 addresses known class sizes asymptotically; provide finite-sample guidance.
- Scope of experiments: The real study is small (n=200) with a single domain and fixed d; error rates are reported but not sensitivity to k>2 or to different q. Include experiments for k>2 and ablations varying d, graph design, and thresholds.
- Related directions: The AND/OR structure is reminiscent of nonadaptive group testing and sparse-graph codes; a discussion of similarities/differences (e.g., superimposed codes vs your per-node thresholds) would help position the work.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: Theoretical contributions are interesting and largely sound, though some proofs are sketched and the exact-recovery achievability relies on intractable decoding. The noisy approximate recovery result is clean and convincing.
- Clarity: Generally readable, but notation and some derivations are terse. Fix typos, define all symbols (r(p), δ̃) at first use, and tighten the exposition of proofs.
- Originality: High. The locally encodable source coding viewpoint and the systematic study of AND queries in this context are novel.
- Significance: Good. The results inform the design of nonadaptive query schemes and set baselines for locality-constrained compression. The AND idea could influence practical crowdsourcing system design.

Pros for acceptance
- New conceptual bridge between clustering with queries and local source coding.
- Nontrivial lower bounds capturing locality effects.
- Simple, effective AND-based algorithm with near-linear query complexity under noise.
- Practical insight about query design corroborated by user data.

Cons for acceptance
- Some theoretical arguments (Theorems 1–3) need tightening; reliance on conjectured SC lower bound for parts of the narrative.
- Exact-recovery decoding is not computationally tractable as presented.
- Limited empirical evaluation and incomplete comparison to strong SC baselines.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Provide a rigorous error analysis for Theorem 1 without expurgating source sequences; clarify randomness and success probability.
- Include a detailed proof (or extended sketch) of Theorem 3 and clean up its statement.
- Add SC baselines with state-of-the-art decoders under the same noise model and sampling budget.
- Discuss estimation of p and q and provide robustness experiments.
- Expand experiments to k>2 and different graph constructions; report constants in O(n log(k/δ)).

Overall: This is a thoughtful and original paper with solid theoretical contributions and a compelling practical message about query design. With improved rigor and broader empirical validation, it would make a strong NIPS contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
