This paper presents an interesting connection between semisupervised clustering with binary queries and locally encodable source coding. The core idea – framing the querying problem as a compression problem where each compressed bit depends on only a few input bits – is novel and allows the authors to leverage tools from information theory to derive lower bounds on the number of queries required.  The proposal of AND queries as an alternative to ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries is also a compelling contribution, backed by theoretical analysis and a small-scale experiment.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The paper tackles the problem of clustering/labeling *n* elements into *k* clusters, given access to an oracle that can answer pairwise queries about cluster membership.  Instead of directly designing adaptive query strategies, the authors demonstrate an equivalence to locally encodable source coding. This allows them to apply information-theoretic bounds to the querying problem. They identify limitations of using only ‘same cluster’ queries and propose AND queries as a more efficient alternative, particularly when approximate recovery is sufficient. The work considers both noiseless and noisy oracle settings. The authors also present a real-world crowdsourcing experiment to validate their theoretical findings.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Insightful Connection:** The connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is a significant insight. This allows the application of a completely different set of tools and perspectives to a relatively well-studied problem.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is heavily grounded in information theory, with clear derivations of lower bounds and analysis of the proposed AND query strategy.  The proofs appear sound (though a deeper dive would be needed for complete verification).
* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The problem setup is clearly defined, and the authors make a strong case for the relevance of the non-adaptive querying setting to practical applications like crowdsourcing.
* **Practical Validation:** The inclusion of a crowdsourcing experiment, even on a small scale, lends credibility to the theoretical results and demonstrates the potential benefit of the AND query approach.
* **Consideration of Noise:** Addressing the noisy oracle setting adds realism and makes the results more applicable to real-world scenarios.
* **Comparison to Prior Work:** The paper adequately references related work in clustering, crowdsourcing, and information theory.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity in Presentation:** While the ideas are strong, the paper is dense and often difficult to follow. The notation is heavy, and the transitions between different parts of the analysis are sometimes abrupt.  More intuitive explanations and illustrations would improve readability. For example, explaining *why* AND queries are expected to perform better beyond simply stating it would be helpful.
* **Experiment Scale:** The crowdsourcing experiment is relatively small.  While it provides initial validation, a larger and more rigorous experimental evaluation is needed to convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the AND query approach, and comparison to baselines like spectral clustering would be valuable.
* **Limited Scope of Experiment:** The experiments seem primarily focused on validating the theoretical results for the noisy oracle setting. Exploring the performance of AND queries compared to "same cluster" queries on more diverse datasets, and under different noise models, would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Locally Encodable Source Coding Background:** The paper acknowledges the limited attention given to locally encodable source coding itself, and the connection feels somewhat underexplored.  A deeper discussion of existing work (even if minimal) in this area would be beneficial.  Expanding on potential applications of the locally encodable source coding perspective beyond the clustering problem could also strengthen the contribution.
* **Lack of Discussion on Query Selection:** The authors mention d-regular graph construction for query selection in the noisy case, but don't elaborate on the impact of the graph structure on performance. Investigating different graph constructions could lead to further improvements.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* **Novel and significant connection:** The link between clustering and locally encodable source coding is a valuable contribution to the field.
* **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis and derives meaningful lower bounds.
* **Potential for impact:** The proposed AND query strategy offers a potentially more efficient approach to clustering, especially in noisy environments.
* **Addresses a relevant problem:** Semisupervised clustering and crowdsourcing are important research areas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Clarity and readability:** The paper is dense and requires significant effort to understand.
* **Limited experimental validation:** The small-scale experiment doesn't provide strong evidence of practical superiority.
* **Underexplored areas:** The connection to locally encodable source coding and the query selection process could be further developed.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses in clarity and experimental validation, I believe this paper represents a worthwhile contribution to the field. The core idea is novel and insightful, the theoretical analysis is strong, and the potential impact of the AND query strategy is promising. I recommend **acceptance**, with a strong encouragement to the authors to improve the clarity of the presentation and conduct more extensive experimental evaluation.  Addressing the weaknesses related to readability and expanding the empirical validation would substantially strengthen the paper.
