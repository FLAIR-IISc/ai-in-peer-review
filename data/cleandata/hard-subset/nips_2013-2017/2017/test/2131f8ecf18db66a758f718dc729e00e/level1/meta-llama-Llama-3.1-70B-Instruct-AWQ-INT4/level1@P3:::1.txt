**Summary of Main Ideas**

This paper introduces a novel approach to semisupervised clustering by formulating it as a locally encodable source coding problem. The authors show that this formulation leads to new information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required to recover the correct labeling. They also propose a new querying strategy based on AND operations that performs better than traditional 'same cluster' queries. The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the problem, including noiseless and noisy query scenarios, exact and approximate recovery, and theoretical and experimental results.

**Related Work**

The paper is well-positioned in the context of recent work on crowdsourcing and clustering. The authors cite relevant papers on entity resolution, clustering with noisy queries, and locally decodable source coding. However, they could have further emphasized the connections to other areas, such as active learning and query-efficient algorithms.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel formulation**: The paper introduces a fresh perspective on semisupervised clustering by casting it as a locally encodable source coding problem. This formulation leads to new insights and results.
2. **Theoretical contributions**: The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem, including lower bounds, querying schemes, and theoretical guarantees. These contributions are well-motivated and carefully presented.
3. **Experimental validation**: The paper includes a real crowdsourcing experiment that validates the theoretical results. This adds credibility to the proposed approach and demonstrates its practical relevance.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited scope**: The paper focuses primarily on the theoretical aspects of the problem, with less emphasis on practical applications and real-world implications. While the experimental validation is a step in the right direction, more attention to practical considerations would strengthen the paper.
2. **Technical presentation**: Some of the technical sections, particularly the proofs, are dense and may be challenging for non-experts to follow. The authors could consider adding more explanatory text or breaking up the proofs into smaller, more manageable pieces.
3. **Comparison to existing methods**: While the paper compares the proposed approach to traditional 'same cluster' queries, a more comprehensive comparison to other existing methods, such as spectral clustering or active learning algorithms, would be beneficial.

**Originality**

The paper's formulation of semisupervised clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem is novel and original. The proposed AND-based querying strategy and the theoretical analysis that follows are also new contributions.

**Significance**

The paper's results have implications for various applications, including entity resolution, clustering, and crowdsourcing. The proposed approach has the potential to improve the efficiency and accuracy of these tasks, particularly in scenarios where query complexity is a concern.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written, with clear explanations of the problem, formulation, and theoretical results. However, some technical sections could benefit from additional explanatory text or clearer notation.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper introduces a novel and original formulation of semisupervised clustering.
* The theoretical contributions are well-motivated and carefully presented.
* The experimental validation adds credibility to the proposed approach.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper's scope is limited, with less emphasis on practical applications and real-world implications.
* Some technical sections are dense and may be challenging for non-experts to follow.
* A more comprehensive comparison to existing methods would be beneficial.

**Recommendation**

I recommend accepting the paper, pending minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above. The paper's originality, theoretical contributions, and experimental validation make it a valuable addition to the conference program.
