This paper introduces a novel and powerful connection between semisupervised clustering via queries and the information-theoretic problem of locally encodable source coding. The problem setup considers recovering a ground-truth labeling of `n` elements into `k` clusters. The learning algorithm is non-adaptive: it designs a set of `m` queries in advance, receives (possibly noisy) answers from an oracle, and then reconstructs the labels. A key constraint, motivated by practical crowdsourcing scenarios, is that each query can only involve a small, constant number (`Δ`) of elements.

The authors frame this task as data compression where each compressed bit (a query answer) depends on only a few input bits (the true labels). This perspective allows them to derive new information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required. A central contribution is the proposal of "AND" queries as a superior alternative to the commonly used "same cluster" (or XOR) queries. The paper analyzes the problem in three settings: (1) noiseless queries for exact recovery, (2) noiseless queries for approximate recovery, and (3) noisy queries for approximate recovery. For the noisy setting, they provide an algorithm that recovers a `(1-δ)` fraction of labels correctly using only `O(n log(k/δ))` queries, a significant improvement over prior work. The theoretical claims are supported by a small-scale, real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

### Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is the novel conceptual bridge it builds between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding. This is not merely a reframing; the information-theoretic viewpoint allows the authors to derive new fundamental lower bounds on the query complexity (Theorems 2 and 3), providing deeper insight into the problem's limits. This framework is a significant contribution that future work can build upon.
*   **Novel and Practical Query Type:** The introduction and analysis of "AND" queries is a major contribution. The paper provides a compelling theoretical argument, rooted in rate-distortion theory, for why "same cluster" (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery. It then demonstrates that the proposed AND queries can provably perform better. This is a practical and impactful insight, as the authors also show experimentally that AND queries can have a lower error rate in real crowdsourcing tasks, likely because they are cognitively simpler to answer correctly.
*   **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper presents several strong technical results. The main algorithmic result (Theorem 5) for the noisy, approximate recovery setting is particularly impressive. The ability to recover most labels with only `O(n log(k/δ))` non-adaptive queries is a substantial advance over previous results like `O(n log n)`. The analysis appears technically sound and leverages tools from information theory, coding theory, and probability in a sophisticated manner.
*   **Empirical Validation:** While primarily a theoretical paper, the inclusion of a real crowdsourcing experiment (Section 4) is a commendable strength. This experiment, though small, provides concrete evidence for two key claims: (1) the proposed algorithm's performance tracks the theoretical predictions, and (2) AND queries indeed elicited fewer errors from human workers than 'same cluster' queries on the same pairs. This helps ground the theory in the practical context that motivates the work.

### Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance / Suggestions for Improvement)

*   **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is technically dense and may be challenging for readers not well-versed in information theory. Concepts like typical sets, expurgation, and rate-distortion theory are used with minimal introduction. For instance, the proof sketch of Theorem 1 is quite compact. While this is understandable given space constraints, a bit more intuition in the main text could significantly broaden the paper's audience and impact.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The experiment is a valuable proof of concept but is limited in scale (200 items, 10 volunteers). A more comprehensive evaluation against other state-of-the-art query-based clustering algorithms on this real dataset (rather than synthetic data for the 'same cluster' baseline) would have made the empirical case more compelling. The comparison against spectral clustering is mentioned but the results are deferred, weakening the direct comparison in the main text.
*   **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on the knowledge of the prior label distribution `p` (or known cluster sizes `n_i`). While the authors argue this is a common assumption and can be estimated, the practicality of the proposed algorithms hinges on it. A brief discussion on the robustness of the methods to errors in the estimated prior would strengthen the paper.

### Overall Evaluation

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on clustering with queries. The connection to locally encodable source coding is insightful and opens up a new avenue for theoretical analysis. The introduction of AND queries is a simple but powerful idea with both theoretical and practical benefits. The paper is technically strong, advancing the state of the art with a new algorithm that has a substantially better query complexity for the noisy, approximate recovery setting. The weaknesses, primarily related to the density of the presentation and the limited scope of the experiments, are minor compared to the strengths of the core contributions. The paper is well within the scope of NIPS and is likely to be of high interest to researchers in machine learning, information theory, and crowdsourcing.

**Recommendation: Accept.**
