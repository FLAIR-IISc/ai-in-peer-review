Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies non-adaptive, semisupervised clustering with a limited budget of oracle queries that each touch at most Δ items, under both noiseless and noisy responses. It draws a clean equivalence to locally encodable source coding (compressed bits each depend on only a few source bits), a notion much less explored than local decodability and smooth compression. This perspective yields information-theoretic lower bounds and query schemes. For k=2, the paper shows: (i) exact recovery is possible with strictly fewer than n pairwise “same-cluster” (XOR) queries for certain priors p, via a random sparse parity encoding and ML decoding; (ii) “same-cluster” queries are provably suboptimal for approximate recovery (building on the classical Massey/Ancheta limitation of linear codes for rate-distortion), and a simple AND-query scheme with local threshold decoding achieves better query–error trade-offs; (iii) with noisy answers and k≥2 labels, a pairwise AND-like k-ary query and a degree-d regular graph yield a computationally trivial decoder achieving (1−δ)-accuracy with m=O(n log(k/δ)) queries. The authors validate the noisy algorithm on a small crowdsourcing experiment and compare AND vs “same-cluster,” finding fewer errors for AND.

This connects to: crowdsourced clustering/entity resolution with “same-cluster” queries (Ashtiani et al., Firmani et al., Vesdapunt et al.), query complexity for k-means with pairwise queries, and coding-theoretic work on local decodability/smooth compression (Makhdoumi et al., Montanari–Mossel, Mazumdar–Chandar–Wornell). The novelty is in formulating and analyzing locally encodable source coding for this problem and showing fundamental limits/advantages of AND queries.

Quality:
- Theory: The derivations are generally sound and clearly motivated. Theorem 1 uses typicality and expurgation over random Δ-sparse parity matrices to get m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) sufficient queries for exact recovery; Theorem 2 gives a nontrivial converse leveraging uneven query coverage; Theorem 3 lower bounds approximate recovery under local encodability; Theorem 4 analyzes a very simple local decoder for AND with a careful Poissonization and inclusion–exclusion; Theorem 5 establishes the noisy guarantee with concentration bounds and dependency graph arguments; Theorem 6 discusses replacing priors by empirical sizes with randomization.
- Caveats: (a) The exact-recovery achievability relies on ML decoding over a random sparse linear code, which is exponential time; no efficient decoder is provided, so the result is existential. (b) For the noisy k≥2 case, the output alphabet is k-ary. While this is natural for the proposed query, it is a departure from the binary-answer constraint emphasized as “most practical” earlier, and the human feasibility of k-ary AND-style answers is not thoroughly compared to binary schemes. (c) Several constants/gaps in the O(·) bounds and thresholds are not fully quantified, making practical parameter selection less direct. (d) Independence of query errors and prohibition on repeated queries are standard but nontrivial assumptions for real crowdsourcing; comparisons to redundancy/worker-quality estimation methods (e.g., Dawid–Skene variants) are not explored.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written and organized, with a clear separation of noiseless/noisy and exact/approximate settings. The information-theoretic lens is explained well. Some typos and notational inconsistencies exist, and a few key proofs are deferred (e.g., Theorem 3), but the main ideas are understandable. Complexity/implementability discussions for exact-recovery decoding could be more explicit.

Originality:
- The locally encodable source coding formulation and the consequent converse bounds appear novel. Demonstrating the fundamental suboptimality of XOR queries for approximate recovery by invoking Massey’s limitation of linear codes in rate-distortion and proposing AND queries with provably superior trade-offs is original and insightful. The simple O(n log(k/δ)) non-adaptive noisy scheme with a local threshold decoder is elegant.

Significance:
- On the theory side, the results sharpen our understanding of query complexity under locality constraints and connect clustering-by-queries to coding theory in a useful way. On the practical side, the AND-vs-XOR message is actionable for designing crowd queries, and the noisy algorithm is simple and scalable. However, empirical validation is limited (small movie dataset, modest synthetic comparisons) and lacks broader baselines; stronger experiments could substantially elevate impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled information-theoretic framework (locally encodable coding) with new lower bounds.
- Conceptually important insight: pairwise XOR “same-cluster” queries are limited for approximate recovery; AND queries can do better.
- Simple, non-adaptive, computationally light algorithm achieving O(n log(k/δ)) queries under noise.
- Initial empirical evidence that AND queries are easier for humans and yield fewer errors.

Cons against acceptance:
- Exact-recovery achievability is existential (ML decoding), with no efficient decoder.
- No direct comparison to adaptive strategies or to crowdsourcing methods using redundancy/worker models; no exploration of how allowing limited repetition would change bounds.
- k-ary query assumption in the noisy section may be less practical than binary answers; the paper does not quantify human cost for larger k.
- Experimental evaluation is small-scale and limited in scope; stronger benchmarks and datasets are needed.

Suggestions:
- Clarify decoding complexity and, if possible, propose efficient decoders (e.g., belief propagation on the sparse factor graph) and evaluate.
- Provide explicit constants and parameter choices for Theorem 5 to aid practical use.
- Expand experiments: larger datasets, varied k, worker models, and comparisons to majority-vote/redundancy, Dawid–Skene, and spectral/SDP baselines for “same-cluster” queries.
- Discuss feasibility of mapping k-ary answers to binary decompositions and the trade-offs.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contributions and the AND-query insight are meaningful and timely, and the simple noisy algorithm is appealing. The main weaknesses are empirical breadth and the lack of efficient exact-recovery decoding, but overall the paper advances the state of understanding in query-based clustering and its connection to coding theory. Confidence: medium.
