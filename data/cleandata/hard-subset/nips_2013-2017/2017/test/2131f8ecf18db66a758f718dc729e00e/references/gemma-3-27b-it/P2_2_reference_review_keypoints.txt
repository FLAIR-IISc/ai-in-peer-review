- Frames semisupervised clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem, enabling information-theoretic analysis.  
- Focuses on non-adaptive query complexity to minimize oracle/crowd queries.  
- Shows XOR (“same cluster”) queries are not always optimal and proposes AND queries as a better alternative, especially in noise.  
- Provides new information-theoretic lower bounds for query complexity in noiseless and noisy settings.  
- Theorem 5 gives a concrete guarantee for AND queries in noisy environments.  
- Argues AND queries are easier for crowd workers and supports this with a small Amazon Mechanical Turk experiment.  
- Uses a rigorous mathematical framework with detailed proofs.  
- Paper is generally well-organized and logically structured.  
- Theoretical sections are dense and assume strong information-theory background, limiting accessibility.  
- Crowdsourcing experiment is small-scale, task-specific (movie clustering), and lacks broader dataset/task evaluation.  
- Missing comparisons to other clustering algorithms beyond spectral clustering and limited demonstration of clustering accuracy gains.  
- Assumes independent query errors, which may not hold in real-world crowdsourcing; dependency discussion is limited.  
- Some contributions (e.g., exact recovery results) feel incremental and build on existing work.  
- Notational overhead (many symbols) makes the paper harder to follow.  
- Quality: technically sound and complete (score 4/5).  
- Clarity: generally clear but dense theoretical sections reduce clarity (score 3/5).  
- Originality: novel framing and AND-query analysis but builds on prior foundations (score 3.5/5).  
- Significance: potentially valuable for costly oracle-query applications but experimental impact is limited (score 3.5/5).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel perspective, rigorous analysis, new lower bounds, practical AND-query improvement with experimental support.  
- Arguments against acceptance: dense theory limiting accessibility, limited experimental validation, incremental aspects, and heavy notation.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept with Minor Revisions.  
- Recommended revisions: simplify theoretical presentation with intuitive explanations/examples, expand experimental evaluation and algorithmic comparisons, and discuss potential dependencies in query errors.
