This paper investigates the problem of semi-supervised clustering via querying a noisy oracle (e.g., crowd workers). It frames this as a locally encodable source coding problem and leverages information-theoretic tools to derive lower bounds on the query complexity and to design near-optimal non-adaptive querying schemes. A key contribution is the demonstration that AND queries can outperform “same cluster” (XOR) queries, especially in approximate recovery scenarios. The paper also presents experimental results on real datasets using Amazon Mechanical Turk to validate the proposed approach.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practically relevant problem in crowdsourcing and entity resolution. The connection to locally encodable source coding is a novel and interesting perspective, providing a strong theoretical foundation for analyzing query complexity. The information-theoretic lower bounds are significant and the introduction of AND queries as a potentially more efficient alternative to XOR queries is a valuable contribution. The experimental validation, despite being limited in scope, lends further credence to the theoretical findings. The paper’s strength lies in its rigorous mathematical analysis and clear presentation of complex concepts. The extensive referencing to related work demonstrates a strong understanding of the field.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the practical impact feels somewhat limited. The gains from using AND queries over XOR queries appear marginal in the presented experiments, and the scalability of the proposed algorithms to very large datasets isn't thoroughly addressed. The discussion around the experimental setup, specifically the d-regular graph construction and the choice of thresholds, could be more detailed. The comparison with existing state-of-the-art clustering algorithms, beyond the spectral clustering benchmark, is lacking. The supplementary material is extensive, indicating a complexity that might be off-putting to some readers. Furthermore, some of the claims are presented without sufficient explanation – for example, the justification for not allowing repeated queries.

Specifically, the motivation for not allowing the repetition of queries feels somewhat weak. While acknowledging theoretical trivialities, a discussion on the practical trade-offs (e.g., cost, worker fatigue) would strengthen the argument. The description of the “typical set” and its role in the proofs could be made more accessible. The experimental section would benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the error rates and a discussion of the potential biases introduced by the choice of movies and workers. The link between the theoretical analysis and the practical algorithm is not always clear.  For example, the discussion of how the theoretical bounds translate to practical parameter settings is missing.  Also, the experimental validation feels somewhat cursory and a more thorough investigation of the real-world performance is warranted.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel framing of the problem as locally encodable source coding.
*   Rigorous information-theoretic analysis and derived lower bounds.
*   Demonstration of the potential benefits of AND queries over XOR queries.
*   Experimental validation on real datasets.
*   Extensive and relevant referencing.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Practical impact feels limited.
*   Experimental results show marginal gains from AND queries.
*   Scalability to large datasets is not thoroughly addressed.
*   Some claims lack sufficient explanation.
*   The complexity of the theoretical exposition could be a barrier to understanding.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to the field of semi-supervised clustering and crowdsourcing. While the practical implications are not immediately obvious, the rigorous analysis and novel perspective make it a valuable addition to the literature. However, the lack of a more substantial experimental evaluation and the complexity of the presentation prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Reject with encouragement to revise and resubmit.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Expand the experimental evaluation:** Conduct more extensive experiments on larger datasets and compare the performance of the proposed algorithm with other state-of-the-art clustering methods.
2.  **Provide a more detailed justification for the design choices:** Elaborate on the rationale behind not allowing repeated queries and provide a more in-depth discussion of the d-regular graph construction.
3.  **Improve the accessibility of the theoretical exposition:** Clarify the explanations of key concepts and provide more intuition behind the mathematical derivations.
4.  **Address the scalability concerns:** Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm and explore potential techniques for scaling it to large datasets.
5.  **Strengthen the connection between theory and practice:** Provide guidance on how the theoretical results can be used to tune the parameters of the algorithm in practical applications.
6.  **Consider a more detailed discussion of the error model and its limitations.**
