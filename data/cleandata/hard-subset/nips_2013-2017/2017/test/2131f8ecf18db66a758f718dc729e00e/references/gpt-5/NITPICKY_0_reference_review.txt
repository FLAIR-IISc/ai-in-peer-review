Summary
This paper studies nonadaptive query-based clustering/label recovery through an information-theoretic lens, introducing “locally encodable source coding” where each query (compressed bit) depends on at most Δ source symbols. Main claims: (i) for noiseless exact recovery with k=2, XOR/same-cluster queries with Δ≥2 can beat n queries by exploiting label priors, with achievability near n h(p)/log(1/α); (ii) for noiseless approximate recovery, XOR queries are inherently limited (via Massey’s linear rate-distortion limitations), while AND queries with a simple decoder achieve better query-accuracy tradeoffs; (iii) for noisy queries, a k-ary generalization of AND on a d-regular graph recovers a (1−δ) fraction of labels with O(n log(k/δ)) queries, using a thresholding decoder; (iv) a small crowdsourcing experiment suggests AND queries are easier for humans than same-cluster queries.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to crowdsourced clustering and query complexity (e.g., Ashtiani et al., Karger-Oh-Shah line, Mazumdar-Saha), to rate-distortion/source coding (Cover-Thomas), and to locally decodable/“smooth” codes (Makhdoumi et al., Montanari-Mossel). The locally encodable viewpoint is interesting, though [23] (smooth/robust compression) already touches nearby ideas. Claims about linear codes and approximate recovery echo known limitations (Massey/Ancheta). The paper’s novelty lies in formalizing local encodability constraints for queries, a lower bound under those constraints, and advocating AND queries.

Strengths
- Interesting and timely connection between query-based clustering and local encoding constraints.
- The insight that AND queries can outperform XOR for approximate recovery (and are easier for workers) is compelling.
- Lower bounds that incorporate locality are potentially useful (if made precise).
- Attempt to address noise and k>2, with a simple thresholding algorithm and a dependency-graph concentration argument.
- Empirical evidence (albeit small-scale) that AND queries yield lower error from humans.

Weaknesses and concerns (technical correctness, clarity, consistency)
Major inconsistencies and likely errors:
1) Counting queries in the noisy-k setting (Sec. 3.3): The paper repeatedly states m = n d^2 queries for a d-regular graph on n nodes (Algorithm 1 header and Theorem 5). This is incorrect: a simple d-regular graph has |E| = nd/2 edges/queries. The experiments use n=200, d=10, and 1000 queries, which equals nd/2, not nd^2. This discrepancy propagates into the claimed O(n log(k/δ)) bound: with their own (incorrect) m=nd^2 and d=Θ(log(k/δ)), one would get m=Θ(n log^2(k/δ)), not Θ(n log(k/δ)). This is a critical flaw that affects the main contribution.

2) Thresholds in Algorithm 1: The threshold is written as Ci = d q/(k−1) + d p_i/2 (1 − q k/(k−1)), then Nu,i ≥ ⌈d Ci⌉. Ci already scales with d; multiplying by d again seems wrong. The expression also misplaces factors of k and lacks derivation. Without a correct, consistent threshold definition and gap analysis, the Chernoff bounds and scaling claims are not credible.

3) Chernoff exponents and dependencies: The proof sketch yields Pi ≤ c k e^{−2 d^2}. Typically, with d samples and a constant gap, error decays like e^{−Θ(d)} (not e^{−Θ(d^2)}). The subsequent concentration argument bounds dependence by “d + d,” ignoring that 2-hop neighborhoods in a d-regular graph have Θ(d^2) nodes. Using Alon–Spencer Cor. 4.3.5 requires a correct dependency degree; the stated ∆* bound is off by a factor Θ(d).

4) Theorem 1 (noiseless exact recovery): The statement m = n(h(p)+o(1)) log2(1/α) is ambiguous; the proof clearly implies division by log(1/α), not multiplication. There are also inconsistencies between base-2 and natural logs, and the expurgation/counting argument uses T instead of (T choose 2), etc. The ML decoding over GF(2) cosets with a biased Bernoulli prior is nontrivial; the proof glosses over the decoder and typical set handling.

5) Theorem 2 (lower bound): Dimensionally inconsistent. The proof suggests m h(…) ≥ n(1−ρ) h(p); the statement reads “≥ n h(p)·max{1, max_ρ (1−ρ)/h(…)}”, which multiplies by nh(p) rather than dividing by h(…). Please fix the expression and specify the maximization domain for ρ.

6) Theorem 3 (locality-aware rate-distortion lower bound): The bound δ ≥ δ̃(m/n) + [h(p)−h(δ̃)]/[h′(δ̃)(1 + e^{Δ h′(δ̃)})] is intriguing but unsupported (proof deferred), and the formula as typeset is ambiguous. Given the centrality of locality constraints, this result needs a clean, correct, self-contained argument or an extended appendix; as is, it’s hard to verify.

7) AND vs XOR claims rely partly on Conjecture 1 and plots (Figure 2) not available here. For XOR lower bounds beyond p=1/2, the conjecture should be clearly stated with precise conditions; otherwise, the comparison risks overstating.

8) k>2 generalization: The query Q returns i only if both endpoints have label i, else 0. This reduces multi-class to k−1 one-vs-baseline tests but ignores symmetry across classes (why is 0 special?). It also makes the decoder asymmetric and threshold choice dependent on arbitrary indexing. A more principled multinomial model and analysis would help.

9) Experiments: Very small scale (n=200), non-replicated, and not compared to strong baselines (e.g., algorithms from [21] or other robust query strategies). The majority-vote and spectral clustering baselines are insufficient; the “too many errors” rationale to switch to synthetic undermines the empirical case.

10) Clarity/grammar/formatting: Numerous typos (“to to,” “st Conference,” “will going to”), inconsistent notation (∆ n?), dangling footnotes (4), and poorly formatted/missing citations (years/venues missing, duplicates [9]/[10]). Figures referenced (1–6) are not shown; claims cannot be verified.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Conceptually interesting “locally encodable” lens with potential to inform query design.
- Insight that AND queries can be more effective and human-friendly than XOR in approximate recovery.
- Some lower-bound ideas that take locality into account.

Cons:
- Major technical inconsistencies (m=nd^2 vs nd/2; scaling of d; thresholds; dependency counts) that undermine the main theorems.
- Ambiguous/incorrect formulas and sloppy derivations in multiple results (Theorems 1–3, 5).
- Weak, small-scale experiments and limited baselines.
- Significant clarity and presentation issues, including references.

Questions for authors
- Please correct the counting of queries for d-regular graphs and reconcile the scaling m vs d throughout. What is the correct dependence of m on d and on (k, δ, q)?
- Provide a precise, derived threshold Ci and a clean Chernoff-style bound with correct exponents.
- Fix Theorem 1 statement (division vs multiplication) and base of logs; provide a rigorous ML decoder argument tailored to Bernoulli(p) priors.
- Clarify Theorem 2’s statement and units; specify the range of ρ and provide the maximizing value numerically for ∆=2.
- Include a full proof (appendix) for Theorem 3, with clear assumptions.
- For k>2, can you avoid privileging label 0 and treat all classes symmetrically?
- Expand experiments with stronger baselines and larger n.

Minor comments and typos (non-exhaustive)
- “to to graph clustering”; “st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems”; “∆ n” likely “∆ ≪ n”; inconsistent use of log/log2; “will going to recover”.
- Footnotes embedded awkwardly (“4”).
- Many references lack years/venues; duplicates (e.g., [9]/[10]), and inconsistent titles.

Recommendation
Reject. The core idea is promising and relevant, but the paper has substantial technical and presentational flaws that currently undermine its correctness and impact. A thorough revision addressing the above issues, with corrected analyses and stronger experiments, would be necessary for consideration.
