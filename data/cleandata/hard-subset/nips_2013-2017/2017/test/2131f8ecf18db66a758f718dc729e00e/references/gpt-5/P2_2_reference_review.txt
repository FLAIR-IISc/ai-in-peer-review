Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonadaptive, budgeted crowdsourcing for clustering/labeling through an information-theoretic lens. It formalizes query-based labeling as “locally encodable” source coding: each query (encoded bit) depends on only Δ labels. Main contributions:
- Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2): Using Δ-wise XOR queries (Δ=2 gives same-cluster queries), the authors prove sufficiency m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) with α depending on p,Δ, yielding m < n for a range of p; and a new converse exploiting locality, improving on the naive n h(p) bound for some p. 
- Noiseless, approximate recovery (k=2): A new lower bound for any Δ-local scheme, and an argument (via known limitations of linear codes as rate–distortion codes) that XOR/same-cluster queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery. They propose pairwise AND queries and analyze a simple decoder, showing superior query–accuracy trade-offs.
- Noisy, approximate recovery (general k): With a pairwise “k-ary AND” query (outputs i if both labels equal i, else 0), a simple thresholding algorithm on a d-regular graph recovers a (1−δ) fraction with m=O(n log(k/δ)) queries under independent per-query noise. They also handle the case where only cluster sizes (not priors) are known via random permutation. 
- A small real crowdsourcing experiment suggests AND queries yield fewer worker errors than same-cluster queries.

This connects classical source coding, local (en/de)coding, and joint source-channel coding to crowdsourced clustering, extending prior query-complexity work on same-cluster queries and noisy crowdsourcing (e.g., Ashtiani et al.; Mazumdar & Saha; Karger–Oh–Shah) and relating to smooth/local coding work.

Quality
- Theory: The sufficiency (Theorem 1) and converse (Theorem 2) in the exact/noiseless case use standard random coding/expurgation and locality arguments; they look technically sound and interesting, though the converse statement is terse and would benefit from clearer presentation of the maximization over ρ and the dependence on Δ, r(p). The approximate/noiseless lower bound (Theorem 3) is deferred and described as “quite involved”; without a sketch, it’s difficult to fully assess. The AND achievability (Theorem 4) is carefully analyzed with a Poisson-approximation argument and inclusion–exclusion over two-hop neighborhoods; the decoder is simple and plausible.
- Noisy setting: The m=O(n log(k/δ)) guarantee (Theorem 5) is supported via Chernoff bounds and a dependency graph concentration argument. The analysis is clean and the gap-based thresholding argument is convincing. Constants and the exact dependence on p,q,k are deferred; including at least one explicit instantiation would strengthen the result.
- Use of Massey’s limitation: The claim that same-cluster/XOR is fundamentally inferior for approximate recovery leans partly on a conjecture generalizing known results (proven at p=1/2). The empirical superiority of AND helps, but a fully proven lower bound specific to XOR under locality would be stronger.
- Experiments: The real experiment is small (200 movies, 10 volunteers, 1000 queries) but does support the claim that AND is easier for workers (105 vs 234 errors). The synthetic comparison against spectral clustering on same-cluster data is informative but limited. Given the paper’s theoretical focus, this is acceptable, though more comprehensive evaluation (varying k, q, d, n; baselines like majority voting with different aggregation; and worker load effects) would improve confidence.

Clarity
- The paper is generally understandable but has numerous typos/formatting glitches (footnotes misplaced, incomplete sentences, figure references without figures in the main text). Some theorem statements (e.g., Theorem 2, 3) and variable definitions could be clearer, and the role of priors vs known cluster sizes is scattered. Algorithm 1 is simple and clear. I recommend careful proofreading, clarifying the converse expressions, and adding brief proof sketches for deferred results.

Originality
- Viewing nonadaptive query design as locally encodable source coding and deriving locality-aware converses is novel and interesting. The key insight that AND queries yield a useful expectation gap enabling simple decoding (and outperform XOR for approximate recovery) appears new in this literature. Extending to noisy queries with O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive complexity is also a meaningful advance.

Significance
- The results advance our understanding of the fundamental limits of crowdsourced clustering under practical constraints (nonadaptive, small-arity queries, worker noise). The theoretical insights (locality converses; AND vs XOR) are likely to influence future query design. Practically, the k-ary AND requires non-binary answers for k>2, which may modestly increase worker cognitive load, but for k=2 it remains binary and appealing. The experimental validation is promising but small; broader empirical evidence would help impact.

Pros
- Conceptually fresh connection to locally encodable source coding; new locality-aware lower bounds.
- Simple, practical AND-based schemes with rigorous guarantees; clean O(n log(k/δ)) result under noise.
- Evidence that AND is easier for workers than same-cluster queries.

Cons
- Some key proofs deferred; reliance on a conjecture for parts of the XOR lower bound narrative.
- Limited experimental evaluation and baselines; small-scale crowdsourcing with volunteers.
- Clarity/typos and presentation issues; some theorem statements hard to parse.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Provide a proof sketch or more intuition for Theorem 3; clarify the exact statement of Theorem 2 (parentheses, ranges of ρ).
- Can you give a formal lower bound specific to XOR under Δ-locality for approximate recovery beyond p=1/2?
- For k>2, how sensitive is worker accuracy to k-ary responses? Any pilot evidence?
- Include explicit constants in Theorem 5 for a concrete parameter setting.
- Compare to triangle queries or other small-arity non-binary functions, and to adaptive schemes.

Recommendation
- Weak accept. The paper offers novel theoretical insights and a simple, effective algorithmic primitive (AND queries) with clear advantages over widely used same-cluster/XOR queries, plus new locality-aware converses. Presentation and empirical evaluation can be improved, but the contribution is substantial and relevant to NeurIPS. Confidence: medium.
