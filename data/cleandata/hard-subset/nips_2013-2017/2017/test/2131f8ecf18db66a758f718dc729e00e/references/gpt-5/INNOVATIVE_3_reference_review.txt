Summary and key ideas
The paper studies non-adaptive, budgeted crowdsourced clustering/labeling through an information-theoretic lens. It makes three intertwined contributions:

1) Locally encodable source coding viewpoint: Each query/measurement depends on at most Δ labels, which is framed as a “locally encodable” source code. This yields new lower bounds on the number of queries for exact and approximate recovery, beyond classical entropy/rate-distortion limits that assume fully global encoders.

2) AND vs same-cluster (XOR) queries: For k=2, the paper shows that “same-cluster” queries (XOR) are suboptimal for approximate recovery and introduces pairwise AND queries that achieve substantially better tradeoffs. Theoretical analysis (Theorem 4) gives the error rate under random sparse bipartite designs and a simple decoder, and connections to Massey/Ancheta show inherent limitations of linear/XOR-based schemes for lossy recovery.

3) Noisy, multi-class approximate recovery: For k≥2 and per-query noise q (independent across queries), the paper proposes a non-adaptive scheme based on pairwise AND-like queries generalized to k classes (return i if both labels equal i, else 0), with a thresholding decoder on a d-regular graph. It proves that O(n log(k/δ)) queries suffice to recover a (1−δ) fraction of labels with high probability (Theorem 5), and extends to the case where only cluster sizes (not priors) are known (Theorem 6).

Additional contributions include: (i) for exact recovery with noiseless queries, an upper bound m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) with α depending on Δ, implying m<n for a range of p even with Δ=2; (ii) a stronger necessary bound than n h(p) for some p (Theorem 2); (iii) a lower bound for approximate recovery under local encodability (Theorem 3). The paper also presents a small real crowdsourcing experiment (binary action/romance movie labels) showing AND queries produce fewer errors than same-cluster questions, and synthetic evidence comparing AND to spectral clustering on noisy same-cluster answers.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on same-cluster query complexity (Ashtiani et al.) and crowdsourcing algorithms (Karger–Oh–Shah, Vesdapunt et al.), as well as local decoding/smooth compression (Makhdoumi et al., Montanari–Mossel), and classical source coding/rate-distortion (Cover–Thomas). The observation that XOR is linear and thus limited for lossy compression traces to Massey/Ancheta. The “locally encodable” angle appears novel relative to the better-studied “locally decodable/smooth” bodies of work.

Strengths
- Novel conceptual framing: Casting non-adaptive query design as locally encodable source coding is fresh and potentially impactful. It cleanly explains why certain query primitives underperform under locality.
- New limits and achievability: The paper derives nontrivial lower bounds specific to local encodability (Theorems 2–3) and provides simple, analyzable schemes, notably the AND-based construction with a very simple decoder that achieves O(n log(k/δ)) queries for approximate recovery under noise.
- Practical insight: The argument and evidence that AND queries are easier for workers and more robust than same-cluster questions is compelling and actionable for crowdsourcing design.
- Direction-setting: The work opens a path for designing non-adaptive, low-cognitive-load queries tailored to approximate recovery objectives, beyond the prevailing XOR/same-cluster paradigm.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Query count inconsistency in Section 3.3: The paper repeatedly states “nd^2 queries” for a d-regular graph yet then treats m as scaling like n d, concluding m=O(n log(k/δ)). A d-regular graph on n nodes has |E|=nd/2 edges/queries. Please fix the notation and ensure all bounds use the correct m.
- Practicality of k-ary outputs: The k-class generalization returns “i if both labels equal i, else 0.” For k>2, this asks workers to choose among k+1 options per pair. Is this realistic at scale? The experiments are for k=2; please discuss cognitive load and feasibility for k>2 and whether multiple binary ANDs per class could be used instead.
- Clarity and typos: There are several notational glitches (e.g., the threshold Ci formula in Algorithm 1 has ambiguous terms like qk/(k−1); logs without bases; missing parentheses). Theorems 1–3 rely on typical-set/expurgation and a complex approximate lower bound; clearer statements and proof sketches would help.
- Efficiency vs existence: The exact recovery scheme (Theorem 1) uses ML decoding over random XOR measurements and typical-set expurgation; it is non-constructive and computationally intractable. This is acceptable as an information-theoretic upper bound, but the contrast with practical algorithms should be explicit.
- Strength of “XOR is bad” claim: For k=2, the argument partly leans on a conjecture (true at p=0.5) to lower bound XOR-based lossy performance. It would strengthen the paper to provide unconditional bounds (beyond conjecture) where possible, or to more precisely delimit the regimes where AND provably dominates.
- Noise model assumptions: Errors across different queries are assumed independent; in crowdsourcing, annotator- and item-specific biases induce dependence. Some robustness discussion or experiments with correlated noise would be valuable.
- Limited empirical evaluation: The real dataset is small, with q≈0.105 inferred from responses. It would help to (i) test on k>2, (ii) include baselines designed for noisy pairwise tasks (e.g., iterative inference over worker reliabilities), and (iii) report sensitivity to p, q, d, δ.

Quality
The theoretical development is largely sound and interesting, though parts are terse and some proofs are deferred. The d-regular counting issue should be corrected. The empirical section illustrates feasibility but is not comprehensive.

Clarity
Readable and generally well-organized, but polish is needed: fix notation errors, standardize log bases, tighten algorithm thresholds, and better separate information-theoretic existence results from efficient algorithms.

Originality
High. The locally encodable source coding framing and the emphasis on AND queries for approximate recovery under local constraints are novel and thought-provoking.

Significance
High potential impact. The work suggests better query primitives and provides bounds/guidance for non-adaptive crowdsourced clustering under realistic constraints. It can influence both theory (local encodability) and practice (task design).

Pros for acceptance
- Strong conceptual novelty with new bounds tailored to locality
- Simple, practical AND-based scheme with provable O(n log(k/δ)) scaling under noise
- Actionable insight for crowdsourcing; initial real-world validation
- Bridges information theory and learning/crowdsourcing communities

Cons against acceptance
- Notational/technical inconsistencies (nd^2 vs nd) and minor typos
- Limited empirical scope; k>2 practicality not demonstrated
- Some reliance on conjectured limitations of XOR for parts of the argument
- Exact recovery achievability is non-constructive

Recommendation
Accept (leaning toward weak accept for current form). The paper advances a bold, novel direction with solid theory and practical implications. With fixes to the noted inconsistencies and improved clarity, it would be a valuable NeurIPS contribution.
