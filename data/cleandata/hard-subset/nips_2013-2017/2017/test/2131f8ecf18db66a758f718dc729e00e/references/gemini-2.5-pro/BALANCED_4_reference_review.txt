This paper presents a novel information-theoretic perspective on the problem of non-adaptive, query-based clustering. The central idea is to frame the problem as one of "locally encodable source coding," where the goal is to recover a label vector (the source) from a set of query answers (the compressed encoding), with the constraint that each query answer can only depend on a small number (∆) of labels. This framework is used to analyze the query complexity required for both exact and approximate recovery of cluster labels, with and without noise in the query answers.

The paper connects to a significant body of work on active clustering and crowdsourcing (e.g., [4, 21, 30, 33]), which aims to minimize the number of queries needed to determine a clustering. Unlike much of this work, the focus here is strictly on non-adaptive querying, which is beneficial for parallelization. The key innovation is the formal connection to source coding theory, specifically introducing the under-studied concept of locally encodable codes. This allows the authors to derive new information-theoretic lower bounds on the query complexity. A major contribution is the proposal and analysis of "AND queries" as an alternative to the standard "same-cluster" (equivalent to XOR) queries. The authors demonstrate, both theoretically and through a small experiment, that AND queries can be more efficient, especially for approximate recovery and in the presence of noise.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Insightful Framework:** The formulation of query-based clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem is the paper's primary strength. It provides a principled and powerful theoretical lens for analyzing the fundamental limits of the problem. This fresh perspective is a significant conceptual contribution that could inspire further research at the intersection of active learning, clustering, and information theory.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contributions:** The paper delivers a solid set of theoretical results. It provides both achievability bounds (querying schemes) and converse bounds (lower bounds) for several important settings:
    *   For exact recovery (Sec 3.1), it presents a scheme that requires fewer than `n-1` queries for `k=2` by leveraging the prior distribution of cluster sizes, and provides a new non-trivial lower bound.
    *   For approximate recovery (Sec 3.2), it establishes a new lower bound and convincingly argues for the sub-optimality of "same-cluster" (XOR) queries, citing classical results [17]. The proposed AND query scheme is shown to outperform this lower bound.
    *   For noisy, approximate recovery (Sec 3.3), the paper presents its main algorithmic result, showing that `O(n log(k/δ))` queries are sufficient to recover a `(1-δ)` fraction of labels. This is a strong result, improving upon the `O(n log n)` complexity of prior work for noisy settings.

3.  **Introduction and Analysis of AND Queries:** The proposal of AND queries is a key practical and theoretical insight. The paper provides clear intuition for why AND queries might be more robust to noise and more efficient for approximate recovery: for a "no" answer, a worker only needs to identify one element's label correctly. The experimental result showing fewer errors for AND queries than same-cluster queries on the same pairs (105 vs 234) provides compelling, albeit preliminary, evidence for this claim. This simple but effective idea could have a tangible impact on the design of real-world crowdsourcing systems.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The problem is clearly defined, and the information-theoretic viewpoint is introduced in an accessible manner. The progression from noiseless exact recovery to noisy approximate recovery is logical and builds the paper's argument effectively.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Recovery Algorithm Complexity:** The paper's analysis focuses almost exclusively on query complexity (the "encoding" cost). However, the computational complexity of the recovery algorithm (the "decoding" cost) is also crucial. For the scheme in Theorem 1, "Maximum Likelihood decoding" is invoked, which for a general linear code is computationally hard. While the proposed random code structure may admit efficient decoding, this is not discussed. This is an important omission, as the `n-1` query baseline (comparing all elements to a single pivot) has a trivial `O(n)` recovery algorithm. A discussion on the trade-offs between query complexity and decoding complexity would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Limited Experimental Validation:** While the inclusion of a real-world crowdsourcing experiment is commendable, its scale is quite small (200 items, d=10, 10 volunteers). It serves as a good proof-of-concept but does not provide a comprehensive validation. The comparison with spectral clustering on synthetic data is useful, but a direct comparison on the real-world dataset would have been more powerful. The interesting finding that human error rates were lower for AND queries than for XOR queries is a key piece of evidence supporting the paper's claims and could be highlighted more, perhaps with more detailed analysis.

3.  **Asymmetry in k-ary AND Query:** For the general `k`-cluster case in Section 3.3, the proposed query `Q(X,X') = i if X = X' = i, 0 otherwise` treats the label '0' as a special default outcome. This asymmetry is reflected in the recovery algorithm (Algorithm 1), which also defaults to label '0'. This design choice needs more justification. Does it assume that `p_0` is much larger than other `p_i`? What happens if the cluster sizes are balanced? Clarifying the rationale behind this design and its implications would be helpful.

4.  **Clarity of Proofs:** The proof sketches in the main text are very high-level. For instance, the proof of Theorem 4 relies on a Poisson degree distribution assumption for a regular graph, and the proof of Theorem 5 uses Chernoff bounds without explicitly defining the parameters that determine the constants `c, ε, c', ε'`. While space is limited, adding a sentence or two of justification for these key steps would improve readability and make the arguments in the main paper more self-contained and convincing.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Presents a novel and powerful information-theoretic framework for query-based clustering.
    *   Provides strong theoretical results, including new lower bounds and efficient achievable schemes.
    *   Introduces the insightful and practically relevant concept of AND queries, demonstrating their advantages over standard same-cluster queries.
    *   Addresses the important and practical setting of noisy queries and approximate recovery.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The computational complexity of the recovery algorithms is not adequately discussed.
    *   Experimental validation is limited in scale.
    *   Some design choices (e.g., the k-ary AND query) could be better motivated.

**Decision**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant novel contributions. The new perspective of locally encodable source coding and the introduction of AND queries are valuable additions to the literature. The theoretical results are strong and address an important problem. While there are some weaknesses regarding the discussion of decoding complexity and the scope of the experiments, they do not detract from the importance of the core ideas. The paper is well-suited for the NeurIPS audience and is likely to stimulate further work in this area. I recommend acceptance.
