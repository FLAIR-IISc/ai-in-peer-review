This paper tackles the problem of semi-supervised clustering via a novel information-theoretic lens, framing it as a locally encodable source coding problem. This is a bold and interesting move, opening up the possibility of leveraging tools from information theory to derive fundamental limits on query complexity. The introduction of AND queries as a potentially superior alternative to standard “same cluster” queries is particularly intriguing, and the initial experimental results supporting this claim are promising. The connection to locally decodable codes and smooth compression, while briefly mentioned, hints at a potentially rich research direction.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core idea of viewing crowdsourced clustering as locally encodable source coding is highly novel. This perspective provides a fresh approach to a well-studied problem.
*   **Theoretical Depth:** The paper rigorously connects the problem to information-theoretic bounds, offering theoretical guarantees on query complexity. The derivation of lower bounds, especially for approximate recovery, is valuable.
*   **Non-Standard Query Type:** The proposal of AND queries as an alternative to XOR queries is insightful and challenges conventional wisdom.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of a crowdsourcing experiment, even with its limitations, provides empirical support for the theoretical findings and demonstrates practical relevance.
*   **Well-defined Problem Setup:** The formulation of the problem, incorporating noisy oracles and the non-adaptive querying setting, is realistic and relevant to many applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Complexity:** The theoretical analysis, particularly in Sections 3.2 and 3.3, is quite dense and relies on several advanced concepts from information theory. While technically sound, it may be inaccessible to a broad NIPS audience.
*   **Experimental Scale:** The crowdsourcing experiment is relatively small-scale and limited to a specific dataset (movies). A more extensive evaluation on diverse datasets would strengthen the claims.
*   **Algorithm 1 Detail:** Algorithm 1’s practical implementation details are glossed over. Clarifying the precise parameter tuning (thresholds, regularization) and computational cost would be helpful.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to existing algorithms (especially those for noisy label learning) is somewhat limited. A more thorough benchmark against state-of-the-art methods would improve the paper’s impact.
*   **Locally Encodable Source Coding Background:** While the authors correctly identify the lack of prior work on locally encodable source coding, a slightly more detailed explanation of this concept and its potential implications would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel perspective on a classic problem.
*   The theoretical contributions are significant and provide valuable insights into the fundamental limits of crowdsourced clustering.
*   The introduction of AND queries is a compelling idea with preliminary experimental support.
*   The paper opens up exciting new research directions at the intersection of information theory and machine learning.
*   The minor weaknesses are outweighed by the originality and potential impact of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical complexity may limit the accessibility of the paper.
*   The experimental validation is limited in scope and scale.
*   The connection to practical applications beyond crowdsourcing is not fully explored.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. While not without its flaws, the novelty and theoretical depth of the work are compelling. The framing of crowdsourced clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem is a brilliant insight, and the proposed AND queries offer a promising avenue for improving query efficiency. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant theoretical concepts and provide initial experimental evidence to support their claims. I am willing to overlook the minor weaknesses in light of the paper’s originality and potential impact. 

**Recommendation:** Accept. I encourage the authors to consider clarifying the theoretical explanations and expanding the experimental evaluation in a future version. I also suggest providing more details on the practical implementation of Algorithm 1.
