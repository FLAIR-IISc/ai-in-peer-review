## NIPS 2017 Review: Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a theoretically interesting connection between crowdsourced clustering and locally encodable source coding. The authors provide insightful lower bounds and propose a novel querying strategy (AND queries) that outperforms traditional “same cluster” (XOR) queries in certain scenarios. The paper is mathematically rigorous and well-motivated. However, the experimental validation is limited, and the practical impact beyond the theoretical contributions remains somewhat unclear. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, leaning towards a strong accept.

**Summary:**

The paper tackles the problem of learning labels (or clustering) with a limited budget of queries to an oracle (e.g., crowd workers). It frames this problem as a locally encodable source coding problem, leveraging information-theoretic tools to derive lower bounds on query complexity.  Crucially, the authors demonstrate that traditional “same cluster” queries (XOR) are not optimal and propose utilizing AND queries as a superior alternative, particularly in noisy settings.  They provide theoretical guarantees for their AND-query based algorithm, demonstrating its effectiveness in recovering a significant proportion of labels with a reduced number of queries. The authors also consider the case of noisy oracles and show that simple majority voting can be suboptimal, suggesting the need for more sophisticated approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Connection:** The connection between crowdsourced clustering and locally encodable source coding is novel and provides a fresh perspective on this problem.  It’s a clever framing that allows the application of powerful information-theoretic tools.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is grounded in rigorous mathematical analysis. The lower bounds are well-derived and the analysis of the AND-query scheme appears sound.
* **Insights into Query Design:** The demonstration that AND queries can outperform XOR queries is a significant insight. This challenges the conventional wisdom and opens up new avenues for designing more efficient querying strategies.
* **Handling Noise:** The consideration of noisy oracle responses and the analysis of the proposed scheme in such settings are valuable, especially for real-world applications.
* **Real Experiment (albeit limited):** The crowdsourced experiment, while small-scale, provides some validation of the theoretical results and the efficacy of AND queries compared to simply observing higher error rates with XOR queries.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Experimental Validation:** The experiments are relatively limited in scope.  The dataset size is small (100 movies), and the analysis is primarily focused on validating the lower bound and comparing error rates, rather than a comprehensive performance evaluation of the algorithm. The comparison to existing baselines (beyond a brief mention of spectral clustering) is lacking.
* **Practical Implications:** While the theoretical results are compelling, the practical impact of the AND-query scheme isn’t fully explored. How much improvement can be expected in real-world scenarios, and what are the costs associated with implementing AND queries (e.g., increased cognitive load on workers)?
* **Complexity of the Noisy Case Analysis:**  The analysis in the noisy case is complex, and while it provides a theoretical guarantee, the constant factors and practical implications might be difficult to assess.
* **Repetition of Query Justification:** While the justification for not allowing repeated queries is reasonable, it feels somewhat reiterated throughout the paper.  A more concise explanation would suffice.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:**  The notation is dense, and some sections (e.g., the proof sketches) require careful reading to follow.

**Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 3.3 (Noisy Queries):**  The assumption of independent errors is standard but worth explicitly acknowledging limitations. Are there scenarios where this assumption is violated (e.g., systemic biases in the crowd workers)? How might the proposed algorithm be adapted to handle correlated errors?
* **Algorithm 1:**  The algorithm description seems somewhat informal. A pseudocode representation might improve clarity.
* **Experiments:**  It would be valuable to compare the performance of the AND-query scheme with other baseline algorithms for crowdsourced clustering, such as active learning approaches or those based on stochastic block models.  Could the authors explore larger datasets and more diverse query scenarios?
* **The Role of Priors:** The dependence on prior knowledge of cluster sizes (or their estimation) is mentioned. How sensitive is the performance of the algorithm to inaccuracies in the prior?
* **Local Decodability Connection:** The connection to locally decodable source coding is mentioned briefly. Could the authors elaborate on this relationship and explore potential synergies?




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to crowdsourced clustering.
* The demonstration that AND queries can outperform XOR queries is a significant contribution.
* The paper’s results have the potential to impact the design of more efficient crowdsourcing systems.
* The authors address a challenging problem with a sound methodology.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental validation is limited.
* The practical implications of the proposed algorithm are not fully explored.
* The mathematical analysis is complex, and the constant factors might be a concern.

**Overall Score:** 8.0 / 10

**Recommendation:**  Accept with minor revisions. I recommend the authors strengthen the experimental validation by including comparisons to more relevant baselines and exploring larger datasets.  Addressing the clarity concerns and providing a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the proposed algorithm would also be beneficial.
