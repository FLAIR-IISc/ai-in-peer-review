Summary
This paper studies nonadaptive, budgeted crowdsourced clustering/label recovery through an information-theoretic lens. The key idea is to cast query-based labeling as “locally encodable source coding,” where each query/encoded bit depends on only ∆ input labels. The authors provide:

- Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2): An achievability scheme using parity/XOR (i.e., “same-cluster”) queries on ∆-tuples shows exact recovery with m ≈ n(h(p)+o(1))/log(1/α) queries, α = 1/2(1+(1−4p(1−p))^∆). For ∆=2 this is strictly < n for a nontrivial range of p. A new lower bound improves on the naive n h(p) limit by incorporating locality.
- Noiseless, approximate recovery (k=2): A locality-aware converse strengthening rate–distortion gives a new lower bound on achievable distortion δ for a given m. The paper argues (using Massey/Ancheta insights on linear codes) that XOR queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery, and introduces pairwise AND queries with a simple threshold decoder whose performance beats the “same cluster” lower bound over a range of m, p.
- Noisy queries, approximate recovery (general k): With a k-ary “AND-like” query that returns i if both labels equal i and 0 otherwise (and independent per-query noise), a simple algorithm on a random d-regular graph recovers a (1−δ) fraction of labels with m = O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive pairwise queries. The analysis uses Chernoff bounds and a dependency graph concentration argument. A small crowdsourcing study (200 movies, 10 workers, 1000 queries) supports that AND queries are easier/less erroneous than “same cluster” queries and aligns with predicted trends.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to work on clustering with same-cluster queries (e.g., Ashtiani et al.), entity resolution with noisy crowds (Karger–Oh–Shah and follow-ups), and information-theoretic compression with locality constraints (locally decodable codes, smooth encoding). The notion of locally encodable source coding itself is underexplored; the authors cite the closest precursors (Mazumdar et al.; Montanari–Mossel) and use classical rate–distortion and linear-code limitations (Massey/Ancheta).

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Framing query-based labeling as locally encodable source coding is elegant and yields meaningful converses that explicitly capture locality constraints. This perspective should interest both ML and information theory communities.
- Theoretical contributions: 
  - An achievability for exact recovery with < n pairwise XOR queries (for some p) and a locality-aware converse improving on n h(p).
  - A locality-aware rate–distortion lower bound for approximate recovery.
  - A simple, effective AND-based algorithm achieving O(n log(k/δ)) queries under noise.
- Practical insight: AND queries can be easier and more reliable for workers than same-cluster XOR queries. The experiment, though small, supports this with significantly fewer annotation errors for AND.
- Algorithmic simplicity and robustness: The noisy setting algorithm is simple (count-and-threshold), adapts to unknown priors via cluster size estimates, and relies on mild independence assumptions.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Clarity and notation:
  - Several typos and notational inconsistencies impede readability. Most importantly, in Sec. 3.3 the number of queries for a d-regular graph should be m = n d/2 (not “nd2” as written multiple times). This matches the experiment (n=200, d=10 → 1000 queries). Please correct throughout, including Theorem 5 statements and figures.
  - The bases of logarithms (natural vs base-2) and constants in Theorem 1 (log(1/α) vs log2(1/α)) should be made consistent.
  - Theorem 2’s expression appears dimensionally inconsistent as written (“at least by nh(p)·max{…}”); please restate clearly as a bound on m with parameters ∆, ρ, p and specify the domain of ρ.
  - Theorem 3’s lower bound includes “e ∆ h′(·)” which looks like a typesetting error; clarify the exact expression and assumptions (proof deferred).
- Scope of queries in practice:
  - For k>2, the proposed k-ary AND-like query asks annotators to return i when both labels equal i. This may be less natural than binary questions and increases cognitive load. The practicality trade-off (versus, e.g., class-specific binary AND questions) should be discussed, and whether the O(n log(k/δ)) guarantee persists with purely binary queries.
- Experimental validation:
  - The empirical study is small (10 volunteers, 1000 queries) and uses a highly simplified task. While acceptable for a theory-focused paper, comparisons to stronger baselines are limited. Using spectral clustering on “same cluster” answers is not necessarily optimal under independent label noise; an MLE or probabilistic model for noisy same-cluster answers would be a fairer baseline.
  - For k=2, it would help to show end-to-end accuracy vs. m for AND vs XOR queries on larger synthetic graphs and a range of p, q, δ, including comparisons to the best-known XOR-based nonadaptive strategies.
- Dependence on priors: Although Theorem 6 addresses unknown priors via cluster sizes, the algorithm’s thresholds depend on p and q. Please clarify robustness to mis-specified priors and noise rates.
- Use of conjecture: The “same cluster” lower bound in Sec. 3.2 relies on a conjecture (except at p=1/2). The narrative should emphasize that the AND scheme’s advantage does not hinge on unproven conjectures, and clearly separate unconditional bounds from conjectural comparisons.

Quality
Overall technically sound with reasonable proof sketches where provided; the key ideas (random sparse designs, ML or threshold decoding, Chernoff bounds, dependency graph concentration) match the claims. Some theorems are deferred or tersely justified; polishing statements and correcting typos will help reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper would benefit from careful editing: fix the m = n d/2 vs “nd2” issue; unify notation; restate lower bounds precisely; include figure captions and axes clearly; and give more intuition for Theorem 3. A brief discussion comparing AND to OR/group-testing-style queries would also aid intuition.

Originality
High. The locally encodable source coding viewpoint and the AND-query paradigm for approximate recovery under noise appear novel and nontrivial.

Significance
Moderate to high. The results sharpen our understanding of the fundamental limits of nonadaptive, small-arity queries for clustering and introduce a practically motivated query primitive with provable gains. This can influence future work in crowdsourced clustering, active querying, and locality-constrained compression.

Pros
- Novel and useful theoretical framework (local encodability).
- New converses and achievability results; AND queries outperform XOR for approximate recovery.
- Simple, scalable algorithm with O(n log(k/δ)) queries under noise.
- Initial empirical evidence that AND queries reduce human error.

Cons
- Clarity/typo issues (notably m = n d/2) and some imprecise statements.
- Limited empirical evaluation and baseline comparisons; practicality for k>2 (k-ary answers) needs discussion.
- Some reliance on conjectural comparisons for XOR lower bounds.

Questions for authors
- Can the O(n log(k/δ)) guarantee be achieved with purely binary queries for k>2 (e.g., class-specific ANDs) without increasing constants significantly?
- How sensitive is Algorithm 1 to misspecified q/p? Can you adaptively estimate q from the data?
- Can you provide unconditional lower bounds that rule out XOR-based schemes in the approximate regime beyond p=1/2?

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers an interesting and original theoretical perspective with concrete algorithmic consequences. With improved clarity, corrected notation, and a strengthened empirical section (even in the supplement), it would make a solid NIPS contribution.
