

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for clustering via queries by framing the problem as locally encodable source coding. The authors demonstrate that AND queries outperform the commonly used "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery of labels, providing both theoretical guarantees and experimental validation. The work builds on prior research in clustering with queries [4, 8, 21, 33] but introduces a fresh information-theoretic perspective, connecting to locally decodable codes [19, 16, 26] and smooth compression [23] while focusing on the "encodable" rather than "decodable" aspect.

**Quality:** The paper presents technically sound theoretical results with rigorous proofs for both lower and upper bounds on query complexity. The authors provide comprehensive coverage of noiseless and noisy settings, exact and approximate recovery, and carefully distinguish between different query types. The real-world crowdsourcing experiment validates the theoretical claims, showing that AND queries produce fewer errors (105 vs. 234 errors in a 1000-query experiment) than "same cluster" queries. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., the extension to k > 2 is mentioned but not fully explored) and makes appropriate distinctions between different theoretical settings. The proofs for the main theorems are well-structured, though some details are deferred to the appendix.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the problem setup, making the connection between clustering and information theory accessible. Key concepts like "locally encodable source coding" are well-defined and motivated. The authors effectively use figures to illustrate theoretical bounds (Figures 1-4) and experimental results (Figures 5-7). The experimental section clearly describes the crowdsourcing methodology and results. Some technical sections (particularly the proofs in the appendix) may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with information theory, but the main results are presented accessibly.

**Originality:** The paper's core contribution is the novel framing of clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem, which the authors note is "not well-studied" in information theory. The key insight that AND queries can achieve better query complexity than XOR queries for approximate recovery is both theoretically novel and practically significant. The authors carefully distinguish their work from prior contributions: unlike [4] which focused on k-means clustering, this work focuses on label recovery; unlike [21] which showed O(n log n) queries are sufficient for two clusters, this paper demonstrates O(n log 1/δ) queries suffice with AND queries. The information-theoretic results on locally encodable source coding appear to be new contributions to the field.

**Significance:** The problem of efficient clustering with minimal queries is practically important for crowdsourcing applications, where each query incurs cost. The AND query approach provides both theoretical advantages (reduced query complexity) and practical benefits (easier for workers to answer, as shown in the experiment). The paper advances the state of the art by providing tighter query complexity bounds and a more efficient querying strategy. The results could directly impact the design of future crowdsourcing systems for entity resolution and clustering tasks. The connection to information theory provides a new theoretical lens for understanding query complexity in clustering problems.

**Strengths:** The paper's main strength is the novel theoretical framework that connects clustering with locally encodable source coding, leading to the key insight about AND queries. The comprehensive theoretical analysis covers multiple settings (exact/approximate recovery, noiseless/noisy) and provides tight bounds. The experimental validation on real crowdsourcing data strongly supports the theoretical claims. The paper clearly articulates how it differs from and improves upon prior work.

**Weaknesses:** The extension of results to k > 2 clusters is mentioned but not thoroughly explored, which limits the applicability of the results to more complex clustering scenarios. The paper states that "locally encodable source coding" is not well-studied, but the related notion of locally decodable codes is well-studied; a clearer distinction between these concepts would strengthen the theoretical contribution. Some of the proofs (particularly Theorem 3) are deferred to the appendix, which may hinder verification for some readers.

**Recommendation:** This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of query complexity in clustering. The novel perspective on locally encodable source coding, the key insight about AND queries, and the supporting experimental results make this a strong candidate for NIPS. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses an important practical problem with meaningful theoretical advances. The paper meets all four criteria (quality, clarity, originality, significance) at a high level.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it makes a substantial theoretical contribution to a problem with practical relevance in machine learning and data science. The authors have provided a complete piece of work with rigorous theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The results advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way, and the approach could be used by researchers and practitioners working on clustering and crowdsourcing systems.
