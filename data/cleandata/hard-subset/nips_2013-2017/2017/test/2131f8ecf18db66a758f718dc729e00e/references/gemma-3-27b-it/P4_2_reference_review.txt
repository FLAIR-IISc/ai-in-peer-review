This paper investigates the problem of semi-supervised clustering via querying a crowd-sourced oracle. The core idea is to frame this problem as a locally encodable source coding problem, which allows for the derivation of information-theoretic lower bounds on the query complexity.  A novel querying strategy based on AND queries is proposed, claimed to outperform traditional ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries, especially in the presence of noisy oracle responses.  The paper extends existing work on adaptive and non-adaptive querying schemes ([33, 30, 8, 22, 21]) by focusing on non-adaptive schemes and introducing a novel query type.  It builds upon the burgeoning area of crowdsourcing for entity resolution ([33, 30, 8, 20]) and connects it to fundamental concepts from information theory ([14, 23, 19, 18, 16, 26, 6, 25, 5, 32]).

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Framing:**  The formulation of the querying problem as a locally encodable source coding problem is a genuinely novel and insightful approach.  This connection allows the application of information-theoretic tools, offering a rigorous basis for analyzing query complexity.
* **AND Query Proposal:** The introduction of AND queries as a potentially superior alternative to XOR queries is a significant contribution. The theoretical analysis and experimental validation suggest that this can lead to improved performance.
* **Noise Handling:** The analysis of the noisy oracle setting is well-motivated and provides practical insights for real-world crowdsourcing applications.  The proposed algorithm for handling noisy queries appears promising.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper contains a substantial amount of theoretical analysis, including lower bounds and performance guarantees for the proposed algorithms.  The mathematical derivations appear generally sound.
* **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of a real crowdsourcing experiment, albeit on a limited scale, adds credibility to the findings and demonstrates the practical relevance of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Organization:** The paper is quite dense and the presentation can be challenging.  The constant referencing of previous work, while demonstrating awareness of the field, sometimes disrupts the flow of the main argument.  Sections 3.2 and 3.3, in particular, contain a lot of equations with limited immediate explanatory context.
* **Notational Overhead:**  The use of numerous variables and symbols without sufficient initial explanation can make it difficult to follow the derivations.
* **Experimental Scope:** While the experiment is valuable, it is relatively small-scale. Expanding the dataset and the number of participants would strengthen the conclusions. The description of the experimental setup could also be more detailed (e.g., selection of movies, precise instructions to workers).
* **Practical Considerations:** The assumption of non-repetition of queries, while justified, might be overly restrictive in some practical scenarios. Discussing the potential impact of allowing limited repetition could be beneficial.
* **Connection to Related Work:**  While related work is cited, the paper could more clearly articulate how the proposed approach differs from, and builds upon, existing techniques for locally decodable codes ([19, 16]) and smooth compression ([23, 26]).



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in semi-supervised learning and crowdsourcing.
* The framing as locally encodable source coding offers a fresh perspective and allows for the application of powerful information-theoretic tools.
* The proposed AND query strategy has the potential to improve the efficiency of querying in crowdsourcing applications.
* The experimental validation, though limited, provides supporting evidence for the theoretical findings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper's density and lack of clarity may hinder its accessibility to a broad audience.
* The experimental scope is relatively limited, and the results might not generalize to larger and more complex datasets.
* The strong assumptions (e.g., non-repetition of queries) may limit the practical applicability of the proposed approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically strong and conceptually novel contribution to the field of semi-supervised learning and crowdsourcing.  The connection to locally encodable source coding is a significant insight, and the proposed AND query strategy holds promise. However, the paper's clarity and experimental scope could be improved.  Considering the significant theoretical contributions, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions focusing on improving clarity and providing a more detailed discussion of the experimental setup and its limitations.  Addressing the notational overhead and providing more intuitive explanations of the key equations would also be beneficial.
