

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution by connecting clustering with query complexity to the underexplored area of locally encodable source coding in information theory. The authors frame the problem of learning cluster labels through queries as a source coding problem where each compressed bit depends on only a constant number of input bits. Their key insight is that AND queries outperform traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery, with theoretical guarantees and empirical validation.

**Strengths:**
1. **Novel theoretical connection:** The paper's core contribution is establishing the link between clustering with queries and locally encodable source coding, which is a fresh perspective not previously explored in NIPS literature. The authors provide new information-theoretic lower bounds (Theorems 1-2) that improve upon naive bounds for both exact and approximate recovery.

2. **Practical significance:** The AND query approach represents a meaningful conceptual advance over existing "same cluster" query methods. The theoretical results for approximate recovery (Theorem 4) demonstrate that AND queries require substantially fewer queries (O(n log 1/δ) vs O(n log n) for XOR queries) to achieve the same recovery quality, with real-world validation showing reduced error rates in crowdsourcing experiments.

3. **Rigorous analysis:** The paper provides detailed theoretical results with careful proofs for both noiseless and noisy settings. The experimental validation with real crowdsourcing data (Section 4) effectively supports the theoretical claims, showing that AND queries yield lower error rates (105 errors vs 234 for "same cluster" queries) in practice.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **NIPS relevance:** While the problem has applications in machine learning (crowdsourcing for entity resolution), the core contributions are primarily theoretical information theory results rather than machine learning advances. The paper would benefit from stronger connections to machine learning applications and clearer justification of why this work belongs in NIPS rather than an information theory venue.

2. **Comparison with related work:** The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from recent NIPS work on clustering with queries (e.g., [21, 22, 33]). For instance, the claim that "a smaller number of queries can be achieved for approximate recovery by using AND queries" needs to be compared with the best known algorithms from prior work (e.g., [21] which also achieves O(n log n) for two clusters).

3. **Limited experimental scope:** The crowdsourcing experiment involves only 200 movies and 10 volunteers, which is small-scale. The paper would be stronger with additional experiments on larger datasets or with different types of crowd workers.

4. **Superficial treatment of k > 2:** The paper claims results extend to k > 2, but the analysis for general k (Section 3.3) is less developed than the k=2 case. The connection between the AND query approach and general clustering with multiple clusters isn't fully justified.

**Constructive suggestions:**
1. Strengthen the connection to machine learning by discussing specific applications in unsupervised learning or entity resolution where these query strategies could be deployed, and how they compare to existing ML approaches for these problems.

2. Expand the comparison with related work in Section 2, particularly with recent NIPS papers on clustering with queries (e.g., [21, 22, 33]), to better establish the novelty and significance of the AND query approach.

3. Include additional experimental validation on larger or more diverse datasets to support the theoretical claims about AND queries being more effective for crowd workers.

4. Provide more detailed analysis for the k > 2 case, particularly explaining why AND queries generalize well to multiple clusters (Section 3.3).

**Overall assessment:**
This paper presents a theoretically sound and novel contribution that advances our understanding of query complexity in clustering. The AND query approach represents a meaningful improvement over existing methods for approximate recovery, with strong theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. However, the connection to machine learning and NIPS is somewhat tenuous, and the paper would benefit from stronger differentiation from related work and more comprehensive experimental validation.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of query complexity in clustering, but its relevance to NIPS would be substantially strengthened by better articulating the machine learning connections and providing more thorough comparisons with recent NIPS work in this area. With these improvements, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.

**Decision:** Weak Accept (borderline) - The paper is technically sound and makes a novel theoretical contribution, but the connection to NIPS is not sufficiently strong, and the experimental validation is limited. With the suggested revisions, this could be an excellent NIPS paper.
