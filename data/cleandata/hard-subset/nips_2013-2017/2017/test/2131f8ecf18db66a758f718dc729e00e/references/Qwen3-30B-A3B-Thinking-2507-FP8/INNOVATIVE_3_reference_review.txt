

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper presents a novel perspective on clustering by framing it as a locally encodable source coding problem, which is a fresh angle in information theory. The authors propose replacing traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries with AND queries, demonstrating theoretically and empirically that AND queries can significantly reduce the number of queries needed for approximate recovery of cluster labels. The core insight is that AND queries provide better information-theoretic efficiency for approximate recovery compared to XOR queries, which had been the standard in prior work on clustering with queries.

The paper's main contributions are threefold: (1) introducing the locally encodable source coding framework for clustering, (2) establishing that AND queries outperform XOR queries for approximate recovery (proving a lower bound showing XOR queries are fundamentally limited), and (3) providing a practical algorithm for noisy settings that achieves near-optimal query complexity. The theoretical results are well-supported with rigorous proofs, including new information-theoretic bounds for locally encodable source coding. The real-world crowdsourcing experiment validates the theoretical claims, showing AND queries yield lower error rates than XOR queries in practice.

This work stands out for its bold theoretical shift in perspective. While prior work in clustering with queries has focused on XOR operations (as in [4, 21, 33]), the authors' insight that AND queries could be more efficient is both surprising and powerful. The connection to information theory is particularly fresh—locally encodable source coding is not well-studied, and the authors make a clear case for why it's the right framework for this problem. Their lower bound in Section 3.2 (Theorem 3) showing the fundamental limitation of XOR queries for approximate recovery is especially compelling.

The significance of this work is substantial. Crowdsourcing applications for entity resolution and clustering are increasingly important, and reducing query complexity directly impacts cost and efficiency. The authors show that for (1-δ)-approximate recovery, AND queries require O(n log k/δ) queries, while XOR-based approaches require O(n log n) queries even for approximate recovery. This represents a significant theoretical improvement that could translate to real-world savings in crowdsourcing costs.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The connection between the information-theoretic framework and the practical crowdsourcing application is well-motivated. The real-world experiment with 100 action and 100 romantic movies is well-designed and effectively validates their theoretical claims—showing AND queries produced only 105 errors compared to 234 for XOR queries.

Minor weaknesses include:
1. The focus on binary labels (k=2) in most theoretical sections, though the authors do extend to k>2 in Section 3.3.
2. The theoretical analysis doesn't deeply explore computational complexity of their decoding algorithms, though this is reasonable for a theoretical paper.
3. The real-world experiment, while good, is limited to a single dataset with only 200 elements.

However, these are minor concerns that don't undermine the paper's core contribution. The paper's novelty is substantial—this is not an incremental improvement but a fundamentally new perspective on a well-studied problem. The authors clearly articulate how their work differs from prior contributions, and the related work is well-cited.

The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission. It's technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical results are rigorous, the experimental validation is appropriate, and the problem is both important and well-motivated.

This paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel theoretical work that NIPS values. While many papers focus on incremental improvements to existing methods, this work rethinks the fundamental problem from a fresh perspective, yielding significant theoretical and practical improvements. The authors' insight that AND queries could be more efficient than XOR queries for approximate recovery is counterintuitive and highly valuable.

I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper introduces a new conceptual framework that has the potential to influence future work in clustering, crowdsourcing, and information theory. The theoretical results are compelling, the experimental validation is convincing, and the paper is clearly written. This is a significant contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.
