

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper presents a novel information-theoretic perspective on semisupervised clustering through the lens of locally encodable source coding. The authors propose AND queries as a more efficient alternative to traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery of labels in crowdsourcing applications. The main contributions include theoretical bounds on query complexity for exact and approximate recovery, a new querying strategy using AND operations, and validation through real-world crowdsourcing experiments.

## Strengths

The paper makes several significant theoretical contributions. The connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding is novel and well-motivated, providing new information-theoretic lower bounds that improve upon previous work. The authors establish that AND queries outperform XOR ("same cluster") queries for approximate recovery, which is a key insight with practical implications. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with well-constructed proofs for the main theorems, and the experimental validation using crowdsourced data on movie classification provides compelling empirical support for the theoretical claims.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem setup to theoretical results to experimental validation. The authors properly contextualize their work within existing literature on crowdsourcing and clustering, citing relevant NIPS papers such as [4, 21, 30, 33]. The comparison between AND queries and "same cluster" queries is particularly strong, showing that AND queries achieve substantially better query complexity for approximate recovery.

## Weaknesses

While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper's connection to the NIPS community is somewhat tenuous. The primary contribution lies in information theory (locally encodable source coding), which is not the main focus of NIPS. The paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of how these results advance machine learning specifically, rather than just information theory.

The experimental section, while validating the main theoretical claims, is somewhat limited. The crowdsourcing experiment with 100 action and 100 romantic movies is small-scale, and the analysis could be strengthened with more detailed error analysis or comparison to additional baselines. The paper states that AND queries had 105 errors versus 234 for "same cluster" queries, but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for this difference.

The paper also makes several technical claims that could be better justified. For instance, the justification for not repeating queries (Section 2) is somewhat brief, and the connection between the theoretical model and practical crowdsourcing scenarios could be more thoroughly developed.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Strengthen the machine learning connection**: Explicitly discuss how the results can be applied to specific machine learning problems beyond clustering, such as semi-supervised learning or active learning, and how they advance these areas.

2. **Expand the experimental validation**: Include more detailed analysis of the crowdsourcing results, including statistical tests for the query performance difference, and consider additional datasets or problem settings.

3. **Clarify the practical relevance**: Provide more concrete guidance on how practitioners can implement the proposed AND query approach in real-world crowdsourcing systems, including potential challenges and limitations.

4. **Refine the theoretical discussion**: The connection to locally encodable source coding could be made more accessible to machine learning readers by providing more intuitive explanations and examples.

5. **Address the "noiseless" assumption**: While the paper acknowledges the noiseless case is idealized, it would be helpful to discuss how the results might extend to more realistic noise models beyond the binary symmetric channel.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the problem of query-efficient clustering, with novel insights about the superiority of AND queries over traditional approaches. The theoretical results are rigorous, and the experimental validation provides good empirical support. However, the primary contribution lies in information theory rather than machine learning, which makes it somewhat less aligned with NIPS' focus.

The paper's quality is high, and the technical contributions are sound. The originality is significant, as the AND query approach represents a clear departure from previous work. The significance is moderate, as the results primarily advance the theoretical understanding of query complexity rather than providing a practical breakthrough for machine learning practitioners.

The main concern is that the paper's contribution is more relevant to information theory than to machine learning, which is a key consideration for NIPS. While the problem (clustering with minimal queries) is relevant to machine learning, the primary contribution is framed through information theory, which may not align with NIPS' focus on machine learning advances.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak rejection**. The paper makes a genuine theoretical contribution to a relevant problem, but the framing and primary contribution are more aligned with information theory than with the machine learning focus of NIPS. The authors could significantly strengthen the paper by better connecting their results to machine learning applications and providing more detailed discussion of how their approach advances machine learning practice.

To improve the paper for a machine learning venue, the authors should:
1. Reframe the introduction to emphasize the machine learning applications and implications
2. Expand the discussion of how the results can be applied to specific machine learning problems
3. Include more detailed analysis of the experimental results in the context of machine learning
4. Strengthen the connection between the theoretical results and practical machine learning challenges

With these revisions, the paper would be a strong candidate for NIPS or a related machine learning venue. As it stands, the information-theoretic focus makes it less suitable for NIPS, though the technical quality and novelty of the results are commendable.
