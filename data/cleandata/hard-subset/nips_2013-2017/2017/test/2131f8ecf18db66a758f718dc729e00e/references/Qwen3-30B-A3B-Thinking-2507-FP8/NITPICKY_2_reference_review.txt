

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper presents an interesting connection between semisupervised clustering, AND-queries, and locally encodable source coding. The authors propose viewing clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem, which allows them to derive information-theoretic lower bounds and propose new querying strategies using AND operations instead of traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries. The theoretical results for noiseless and noisy approximate recovery with AND queries appear promising, and the experimental validation on a real crowdsourcing dataset adds practical relevance.

However, the paper contains numerous technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and unclear statements that significantly undermine its scientific rigor and presentation. While the core ideas may have merit, these issues make it difficult to assess the validity of the claims and the novelty of the contributions.

**Major Technical Issues:**

1. **Critical Notation Inconsistencies:** The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. For example, "qk−1" is used ambiguously to mean both q/(k-1) (in the noise model) and q^(k-1) (in other places). The definition of the AND query for k=2 in Section 3.3 contradicts the definition in Section 3.2, creating confusion about what the authors mean by "AND query."

2. **Mathematical Errors:** Theorem 1 contains a serious typo: "α = 12 (1 + (1 − 4p(1 − p)) ∆)" should be "α = 1/2 (1 + (1 − 4p(1 − p))^∆)". The missing space, missing slash, and missing exponent make the expression mathematically meaningless. Similarly, the expression in Theorem 4 is poorly formatted with missing parentheses and unclear summation limits.

3. **Inconsistent Reference Handling:** The paper states "Figure 1" is included but the figure is not present in the text. The paper references [31] as "recent work in [31] considers triangle queries" but [31] is not in the reference list. The reference to [27, Figure 1] lacks the full citation.

4. **Critical Experimental Error:** The paper claims "for d = 10, we have nd2 = 1000 queries" when n=100, but nd2 = 100*10/2 = 500, not 1000. This is a fundamental arithmetic error that undermines the experimental setup.

5. **Contradictory Claims:** The paper states "repeating the same question multiple times is not allowed" but later claims "the probability of error of a query answer may also be thought of as the aggregated answer after repeating the query several times" - these are directly contradictory.

6. **Reference Issues:** The references contain multiple formatting errors (e.g., extra commas, "st" instead of "31st" in conference names) and the reference [23] is mischaracterized as discussing locally encodable source coding when it's actually about smooth compression.

**Minor but Significant Issues:**

1. The paper claims "the only place where it is mentioned to the best of our knowledge is in [23]" regarding locally encodable source coding, but [23] is titled "Smooth compression" and the paper itself states "the notion of locally encodable source coding is also present where it was called robust compression" - this is a contradiction.

2. The paper states "For the special case of k = 2, we denote p0 ≡ 1 − p and p1 ≡ p" but later uses p to denote both the probability of label 1 and the vector p, creating confusion.

3. The paper claims "the best known algorithms need O(n log n) queries to perform the clustering with two clusters [21]" but doesn't explain why this is the case, making it difficult to assess the novelty of their results.

4. The paper refers to "Figure 2" and "Figure 3" but doesn't include these figures in the text, making it impossible to verify the claims about performance comparisons.

**Quality Assessment:**

The paper contains promising theoretical insights that connect clustering with information theory, but the numerous technical errors, inconsistent notation, and unclear explanations significantly weaken the scientific contribution. The paper appears to have been rushed without careful proofreading, and the errors are substantive enough to cast doubt on the validity of the claims.

The experimental section contains a critical arithmetic error (nd2 = 1000 instead of 500 for n=100, d=10) that undermines the entire experimental validation. The theoretical claims about the AND query scheme are difficult to verify due to the inconsistent notation and mathematical errors.

**Conclusion:**

While the paper addresses an interesting problem and makes some potentially valuable connections between clustering and information theory, the numerous technical errors, inconsistent notation, and formatting issues make it difficult to assess the validity of the claims. The paper fails to meet the basic standards of technical precision expected at NIPS. The authors should thoroughly revise the paper to correct all mathematical expressions, notation, and experimental details before resubmission.

I recommend rejection due to these substantial technical flaws that undermine the paper's scientific contribution. The core ideas may be promising, but the paper as written does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims.
