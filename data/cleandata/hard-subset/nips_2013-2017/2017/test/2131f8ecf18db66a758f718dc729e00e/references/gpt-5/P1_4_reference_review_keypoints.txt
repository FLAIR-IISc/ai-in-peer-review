- Paper frames nonadaptive, budgeted query-based clustering as "locally encodable source coding" and connects query design to information-theoretic limits.  
- Model: n i.i.d. labels in {0,…,k−1} from known prior p; fixed nonadaptive queries each depending on ≤Δ labels; goal exact/approximate recovery with minimal m in noiseless/noisy settings.

Main results:
- Noiseless exact recovery (k=2): using Δ-ary XOR queries, achievability m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) with α = 1/2(1+(1−4p(1−p))^Δ) via random constant-row-weight matrix and ML decoding; lower bound improves on trivial nh(p) for some p.  
- Noiseless approximate recovery (k=2): locality-aware lower bound for Hamming distortion δ; XOR/same-cluster queries are provably suboptimal for approximation; propose pairwise AND queries with simple decoder and error expression that outperforms XOR bounds in many regimes.  
- Noisy approximate recovery (general k): k-ary AND-like queries with answers flipped with prob q, using d-regular graph (m=nd/2) and per-node thresholding achieves (1−δ)-accuracy with m = O(n log(k/δ)); extends to known cluster sizes via random permutation.  
- Experiments: small crowdsourcing on 200 movies with 1000 pairwise queries shows AND queries produce substantially fewer errors (105 vs. 234) and align with theoretical predictions; synthetic comparisons to spectral clustering also provided.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty in introducing locally encodable source coding and deriving locality-aware lower bounds.  
- Clear message that XOR/same-cluster queries are inferior for approximate/noisy recovery and AND-type queries can be superior.  
- Strong theoretical contributions including sub-n achievability for exact recovery (k=2), nontrivial locality-aware lower bounds, and O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive noisy recovery.  
- Practical relevance: AND queries seem easier for workers and the scheme is parallelizable and scalable.  
- Some empirical validation including a real crowdsourcing study.

Weaknesses:
- Several results focus on k=2; k>2 treatment is limited to noisy setting and lacks empirical validation.  
- Exact-recovery achievability requires ML decoding over random XOR measurements; computational feasibility and robustness are not discussed.  
- Theorems and proofs are terse; key arguments (expurgation, lower bounds, Theorem 3) need fuller, self-contained exposition in main text.  
- The "same-cluster" lower bound is conjectural, and comparisons partly rely on it.  
- Notation/clarity issues (m=nd/2 typeset ambiguously as “nd2”), typos, missing equation numbers, and formatting errors hinder readability.  
- AND-scheme error expression (Theorem 4) is complicated and could use simpler interpretable bounds.  
- Experiments are small-scale, assume noise independence without verification, omit k>2 tests, and lack strong baselines (e.g., Dawid–Skene/EM, modern crowdsourcing methods); spectral clustering comparison may be insufficiently tuned.

Quality:
- Overall technically sound; noisy-scheme achievability and concentration arguments are convincing; exact-recovery XOR achievability is plausible; lower bounds are interesting but need more detail and tighter constants.

Clarity:
- Organization acceptable but notation, typos, and compressed proofs reduce clarity; needs clearer model statements, fixed notation, and intuitive proof sketches.

Originality:
- High; locally encodable source coding perspective and AND-query advantages appear novel for this community.

Significance:
- Moderate to high; potential influence on crowdsourcing query design and follow-up theoretical work on locality-aware query complexity.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel theory linking query-based clustering and local source coding.  
- Simple, strong algorithm with near-linear query complexity under noise.  
- Clear negative/positive results for XOR vs AND with both theory and empirical support.  
- Relevance to NeurIPS audiences across crowdsourcing, clustering, and information theory.

Cons for acceptance:
- Clarity and notation problems; key proofs relegated to appendix and hard to assess.  
- Weak and small-scale experiments lacking strong baselines and k>2 validation.  
- Exact-recovery scheme relies on ML decoding without computational discussion.  
- Some important claims (XOR lower bound) remain conjectural.

Questions and suggestions:
- Fix ambiguous m=nd/2 notation and define d clearly; reconcile with experiments.  
- Provide simpler/tighter bounds for Theorem 4 (make δ dependence explicit with constants).  
- Discuss computational alternatives or practicality for exact-recovery ML decoding (polynomial-time approximations).  
- Add baselines (Dawid–Skene/EM, majority vote with redundancy, coding-theoretic methods) and k>2 synthetic experiments under equal budgets.  
- Discuss robustness to correlated worker errors and prior misspecification.

Minor comments:
- Check references for duplicates and completeness (e.g., Ancheta–Massey).  
- Clean up footnotes, grammar, equation numbering, and formatting issues.
