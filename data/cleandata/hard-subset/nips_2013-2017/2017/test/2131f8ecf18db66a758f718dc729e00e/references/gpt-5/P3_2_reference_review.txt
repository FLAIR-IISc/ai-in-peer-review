Summary
The paper studies nonadaptive, query-efficient labeling/clustering with an oracle that answers simple functions of a small number (Δ) of items. It casts the problem as “locally encodable source coding,” where each compressed bit (query answer) depends on at most Δ input labels. The authors derive information-theoretic bounds and propose concrete querying/decoding schemes.

Key contributions:
- Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2): Using Δ-ary XOR (“same-cluster”) queries, a random sparse linear encoding plus ML decoding achieves exact recovery with m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries (α = 1/2·(1+(1−4p(1−p))^Δ)), which can be strictly less than n for certain p. A new converse exploiting locality improves on the naive n h(p) in a range of p.
- Noiseless, approximate recovery (k=2): A new converse under locality constraints and an argument (invoking Massey’s linear rate-distortion limitations) that XOR queries are inherently suboptimal for approximate recovery. The authors propose AND queries (pairwise Q(X,X′)=X∧X′) with a simple two-hop decoder on a random right-degree-2 bipartite graph; they analyze the error and show improved query–distortion trade-offs vs. XOR.
- Noisy, approximate recovery (general k): Define a pairwise k-ary query returning i if both endpoints have label i, else 0, passed through symmetric noise (correct with prob 1−q, otherwise uniform among wrong labels). On a random d-regular graph, a simple per-node thresholding decoder achieves (1−δ)-accuracy with m=O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive queries. A small crowdsourcing study suggests AND queries yield fewer human errors than “same-cluster” queries, and the empirical performance roughly follows the theory.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The “locally encodable source coding” lens is fresh and relevant, tying query design to information-theoretic limits under local encoding constraints. This perspective could inform future work beyond clustering.
- Provable achievability with simple, practical decoders: The AND-based schemes are nonadaptive, linear-time, and robust to noise, with clean Chernoff-style analyses and appealing O(n log(k/δ)) scaling for approximate recovery.
- Insight on query design: The observation (theoretical and empirical) that AND queries can outperform XOR/same-cluster queries for approximate recovery is valuable for crowdsourcing practice, where ease for workers matters.
- Some improved converses: The locality-aware lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery extend beyond classic source coding bounds and clarify inherent costs of local encoding.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Inconsistency in query count and notation: The noisy approximate section repeatedly states “nd^2 queries” for a d-regular graph, but a d-regular graph on n nodes has nd/2 edges/queries. The experiments (n=200, d=10 → 1000 queries) clearly follow nd/2. This is a significant inconsistency that must be fixed throughout (including Theorem 5 statement, proof, and captions).
- Proof completeness and rigor in the main text:
  - Theorem 1 uses a typical-set expurgation with a sparse random binary matrix to argue existence under ML decoding. The per-row “even-parity” probability is approximated as 1/2(1+(1−2r(p))^Δ); the passage from the exact hypergeometric probability to this bound is not justified in the text. Moreover, decoding is ML (NP-hard) with no tractable algorithm provided, so the result is existential.
  - Theorem 2 (converse for exact recovery) has a nontransparent expression and limited discussion; the derivation and units (e.g., the max over ρ expression) are difficult to parse and may contain typographical errors.
  - Theorem 3 (approximate recovery converse) presents a complicated lower bound without a self-contained proof; the dependence on Δ enters via an exponential term but the intuition and conditions are unclear in the main text.
  - Concentration in Theorem 5: The dependency degree for the number of errors Z is bounded as Δ* ≤ d + d; but in a d-regular graph, dependence propagates to two-hop neighbors, which are O(d^2), not O(d). This matters for tail bounds (still likely fine since d=Θ(log(k/δ)), but the bound and citation (Alon–Spencer Cor. 4.3.5) should be corrected).
- Lower bounds for XOR/same-cluster queries: The claim that XOR is suboptimal for approximate recovery relies on a conjecture (true at p=0.5) derived from Massey’s linear rate–distortion limitations. A firm lower bound for the class of XOR-only querying schemes (even in restricted ensembles) would substantially strengthen the message; as is, the comparison hinges on a conjecture and on trivial XOR baselines.
- Modeling assumptions and practicality:
  - The noisy k-ary response model assumes uniform confusion among wrong labels; the small crowdsourcing experiment does not validate this assumption (nor its independence), and no robustness analysis is provided.
  - The proposed k-ary query treats label 0 specially (everything “not both i” maps to 0). This asymmetry and the need for priors (or exact class sizes) should be discussed more: how sensitive is performance to prior misspecification? What if classes are highly imbalanced?
  - The paper initially motivates binary answers as most practical, yet the main noisy result uses k-ary outputs. It would help to explicitly discuss the binary-only version for k>2 (e.g., multiple AND bits) and its query complexity.
- Clarity and presentation: Numerous typos and formatting issues (inline footnotes, missing/unclear figure references, collisions of Δ for arity and dependency, misstatements like nd^2 queries) hinder readability. Several key proofs are deferred; the main text should provide clearer intuition and cleaned-up statements.

Relation to prior work
- The connection to locally decodable/smooth compression is appropriate and well cited. However, related literatures like group testing, sparse pooling, and one-bit sensing (which also consider simple Boolean queries and recovery under noise) should be acknowledged and contrasted.
- The crowdsourcing literature used as motivation is relevant; it would help to position the nonadaptive, constant-arity querying against adaptive strategies (e.g., Ashtiani et al.) in terms of regimes where nonadaptivity is preferable.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel and timely perspective (local encoding) with new bounds.
- Simple, analyzable algorithms with attractive scaling and some empirical support.
- Practical insight that AND queries can be easier and more informative than same-cluster queries.

Cons:
- Technical and presentation issues (query count inconsistency; dependence bound; limited rigor in main text).
- Key comparative claim (XOR inferiority) relies on a conjecture; converses are not tight.
- Limited experimental validation and mismatch with the assumed noise model.

Recommendation and suggestions
I lean toward rejection in the current form, mainly due to the inconsistencies and missing rigor in central parts of the theory and presentation. However, the ideas are promising. To strengthen the paper:
- Fix the query-count inconsistencies (nd/2 vs nd^2) and all affected statements/figures.
- Provide a cleaner, self-contained sketch for Theorems 1–3, correct the dependency analysis in Theorem 5, and clarify the exact achievable m for exact recovery with Δ=2 (with numerical ranges).
- Either prove a nontrivial lower bound for XOR-only schemes in the approximate regime or qualify the claims more cautiously.
- Discuss robustness to prior misspecification and to nonuniform noise; consider a binary-only design for k>2.
- Improve experiments (larger datasets, error bars, more baselines) and discuss cognitive load empirically.

With these revisions, the work could make a solid contribution to NeurIPS on the theory and practice of query-efficient clustering.
