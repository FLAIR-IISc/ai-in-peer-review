Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonadaptive, budgeted crowdsourced clustering/labeling through an information-theoretic lens. Each query is a small-arity function of a few labels (typically two), and the goal is to minimize the number of queries while achieving exact or approximate label recovery. The authors (i) formalize the problem as locally encodable source coding, where each compressed bit depends on at most Δ source bits; (ii) derive lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery under locality; (iii) show that “same-cluster” (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery and introduce AND-queries that yield better tradeoffs; (iv) propose a simple, nonadaptive algorithm for noisy queries using pairwise AND-type queries on a d-regular graph, attaining O(n log(k/δ)) queries for (1−δ)-approximate recovery; (v) provide a small real crowdsourcing experiment and some synthetic comparisons.

This connects to prior work on clustering with queries (e.g., Ashtiani et al., Mazumdar & Saha), budget-optimal crowdsourcing (Karger-Oh-Shah), and smooth/locally decodable compression (Montanari–Mossel; Makhdoumi et al.). The locally encodable source coding viewpoint targets encoder locality, which is less studied than decoder locality, and the authors leverage rate-distortion and a result by Massey/Ancheta on linear code limitations to critique XOR-based schemes.

Strengths
- Conceptual framing: Casting nonadaptive query design as locally encodable source coding is appealing and yields natural information-theoretic lower bounds under locality constraints.
- Theory: 
  - Exact recovery (k=2): Existence result achieving m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) with Δ-ary XOR queries, giving strictly fewer than n queries for some p, along with an improved necessary bound that can beat the naive n h(p).
  - Approximate recovery (k=2): A locality-aware lower bound and a rigorous analysis of a simple AND-based estimator over random graphs.
  - Noisy setting (general k): A transparent counting/thresholding algorithm with Chernoff analysis giving m = O(n log(k/δ)). The dependency-graph concentration argument is appropriate for the nonindependent errors across neighboring nodes.
- Practical insight: AND-queries can be easier for workers than “same-cluster” queries; the pilot experiment suggests lower error rates with AND.
- Relevance: Nonadaptive schemes are attractive for parallelization in real crowdsourcing pipelines.

Weaknesses
- Practicality of the k-ary AND query model: The proposed Q(X,X′) returns i if both labels equal i, else 0. It is unclear how a crowd worker can answer this as a single k-ary response without being asked k different binary questions (“Are both items class i?” for each i), which would inflate the effective query budget by a factor of k. If instead a single question is asked, how does the worker decide which i to return without checking all options? The paper should clarify the interface and the counting of cost when k>2.
- Reliance on priors/cluster sizes: Several results assume known p or exact cluster sizes n_i. Estimating these reliably without incurring additional budget (beyond o(n)) is nontrivial in practice; the proposed O(log n) subsampling approach requires a complete clustering within the subsample, which itself may be costly and nontrivial for k>2. The robustness of Algorithm 1 to errors in p or n_i is not evaluated.
- Exact recovery scheme (Theorem 1) lacks an efficient decoder: The ML/typical-set decoder is computationally infeasible. Thus, while the existence result is theoretically interesting, it offers limited algorithmic value unless accompanied by a polynomial-time decoder or at least a tractable relaxation with guarantees.
- Lower bounds vs XOR: The critique of “same-cluster” queries for approximate recovery leans on a Massey/Ancheta conjecture except at p=0.5. The unconditional, XOR-specific lower bound remains incomplete. The presented general locality-aware lower bound (Theorem 3) is valuable but does not isolate XOR’s limitations without additional assumptions.
- Experimental validation is thin: The real experiment is small (200 items, 1000 pairwise queries, 10 volunteers) and does not compare against standard aggregators (e.g., Dawid–Skene, GLAD) or crowdsourcing baselines under the same budget. Worker heterogeneity and adversarial behaviors are not modeled. The synthetic comparison uses spectral clustering for XOR but omits stronger baselines tailored to noisy pairwise comparisons. Overall, the empirical section does not yet meet the rigor commensurate with the theoretical claims.
- Clarity/presentation: The manuscript has notational overload, typos, and some unclear steps (e.g., special treatment of label “0”; missing constants and exact conditions for Theorem 5; definition and practicality of k-ary AND queries; some proofs deferred and hard to parse). Several references are incomplete.

Quality
The theoretical components are mostly sound under the stated independence/noise models and nonadaptive design, though some proofs are sketched or deferred. The existence result for exact recovery and the approximate/noisy analysis are plausible. However, the experimental support is limited, and important modeling choices (k-ary AND feasibility, prior estimation) are not adequately stress-tested.

Clarity
Readable at a high level but needs significant polishing. Please clean notation (especially special role of label 0), fully specify Algorithm 1 with all constants and thresholds, and clearly define the worker-facing query for k>2, including how budget scales.

Originality
Moderately novel. The locally encodable source coding perspective and associated lower bounds are a fresh angle, and the emphasis on AND queries for approximate recovery seems new in this literature. However, the work builds on established tools (rate-distortion, Chernoff, dependency graphs) and prior query-clustering formulations.

Significance
Potentially significant if the AND-query model can be made practical and if the O(n log(k/δ)) guarantee translates to real crowdsourcing under realistic worker models. As it stands, the practical impact is uncertain due to the query model ambiguity and limited empirical validation.

Pros
- Interesting theoretical framing and lower bounds under encoder locality.
- Simple, scalable algorithm with clear intuition and provable guarantees in the noisy setting.
- Empirical indication that AND queries may be easier for workers.

Cons
- Query model for k>2 is not convincingly practical; budget accounting may be optimistic.
- No efficient algorithm for the exact recovery result; XOR lower bounds partly conjectural.
- Sparse and limited experiments; weak baseline comparisons; no worker-heterogeneity modeling.
- Clarity issues and missing details.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Precisely define how a single k-ary AND response is elicited from a worker; if multiple binary sub-queries are needed, reflect this in the query budget and theory.
- Provide robustness analysis to mis-specified priors/cluster sizes; can Algorithm 1 estimate thresholds adaptively?
- Add efficient algorithmic counterparts (or relaxations) for the exact recovery setting.
- Strengthen experiments: compare against Dawid–Skene/GLAD and recent query-based clustering methods under equal budgets; include worker heterogeneity; report cost and time.
- Clarify the scope of Theorem 3 and provide more intuition; tighten XOR-specific lower bounds beyond the conjecture if possible.
- Improve presentation, notation, and complete references.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The theoretical ideas are promising, but the practicality of the k-ary AND query, lack of efficient exact-recovery algorithms, and limited empirical validation against strong baselines make me hesitant to endorse at this time.

Confidence: High.
