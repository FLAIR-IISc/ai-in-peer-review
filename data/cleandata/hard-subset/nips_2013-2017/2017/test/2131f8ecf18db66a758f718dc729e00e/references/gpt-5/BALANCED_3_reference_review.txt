Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies non-adaptive, budgeted crowdsourcing for clustering/labeling via pairwise (and small-arity) queries, emphasizing that “same-cluster” (XOR) queries are not necessarily optimal. It reframes the problem as locally encodable source coding—each query output depends on at most Δ labels—contrasting with classical source coding (global) and locally decodable/smooth compression (e.g., Montanari & Mossel; Makhdoumi et al.). For k=2 and noiseless oracles, the authors give an achievability scheme using random parity (XOR) queries to achieve exact recovery with m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries for certain p<1/2 and Δ≥2, strictly below n, and a lower bound that can improve upon the naive n h(p). For approximate recovery, they derive a locality-aware information-theoretic lower bound and argue (via a conjecture related to Massey’s limitation of linear codes for lossy compression) that XOR is suboptimal. They propose an AND-based scheme and show improved trade-offs (particularly when p≠1/2) under noiseless queries. In the noisy setting with k classes and k-ary responses, they introduce a simple non-adaptive scheme on a d-regular graph with threshold decoding and prove that O(n log(k/δ)) queries suffice for (1−δ) recovery under i.i.d. noise. A small crowdsourcing experiment (binary genre labeling of movies) suggests AND queries yield fewer human errors than same-cluster queries, with trends matching theory.

Quality:
- Positive: The information-theoretic framing is sound and insightful. The achievability using random sparse parity checks for exact recovery (Theorem 1) is plausible via standard typicality/expurgation arguments. The noisy approximate scheme (Algorithm 1) is simple and the idea of exploiting an expectation gap under AND-type queries is compelling; the use of Chernoff bounds is standard. The human study, though small, is a useful sanity check supporting the claim that AND is easier for workers.
- Concerns:
  - Query counting and concentration appear inconsistent. In Sec. 3.3, the paper repeatedly states m = n d^2 queries for a d-regular graph, but querying each edge once yields m = |E| = nd/2, not nd^2. The proof of Theorem 5 uses sums over d neighbors, so the natural sample size per node is d; the Chernoff exponent should scale with d (up to a factor depending on the gap), not d^2 as written (the text states errors ~ e^{-2 d^2}). This mismatch affects the claimed O(n log(k/δ)) complexity. Please correct the counting and exponents and align claims accordingly.
  - The exact-recovery achievability (Theorem 1) relies on ML on a random sparse parity matrix and expurgation; the decoder is exponential and the existence proof omits finite-length constants. The lower bound (Theorem 2) is interesting but sketchy, and it would benefit from a clearer, fully specified counting argument (precise dependence on Δ, r(p), and ρ).
  - The approximate-recovery lower bound (Theorem 3) is deferred and hard to assess. The claim about XOR’s suboptimality partly leans on a conjecture (Ancheta/Massey) that is only known at p = 1/2; more unconditional evidence (or bounds specific to XOR with locality) would strengthen the case.
  - Noise model assumes independent per-query errors and known priors; the algorithm uses thresholds derived from p and q. Robustness to model misspecification and heterogeneous workers is not addressed.
  - Experiments are limited (200 items, 10 volunteers, 1000 pairs). Same-cluster baselines are weak (spectral clustering on randomly sampled pairs), and more competitive inference methods (e.g., Dawid–Skene, EM/GLAD, Karger–Oh–Shah) are not compared.

Clarity:
The paper is readable but contains several typos, notational inconsistencies, and missing details:
- In Sec. 3.3, the m=nd^2 vs m=|E| inconsistency, and the incorrect Chernoff exponent, need fixing.
- Some formulas (e.g., α in Theorem 1) and bounds (Theorem 2) are hard to parse due to formatting; define all quantities cleanly once.
- Several figure references are crucial for interpretation; ensure they are clear and self-contained.
- References are incomplete or duplicated; please provide full bibliographic data.

Originality:
- The “locally encodable source coding” lens for crowdsourced clustering is novel and interesting. The identification of AND-type queries as better suited for approximate recovery (under certain priors and noise) is a valuable insight. The lower bounds tailored to local encoding constraints are a meaningful step beyond standard rate–distortion arguments.
- Some components (random parity achievability; Chernoff-gap arguments; graph-based non-adaptive designs) are standard, but the overall combination is original and timely.

Significance:
- Conceptually significant: highlights that the choice of query function matters and that non-linear queries like AND can offer strong advantages in budgeted crowdsourcing.
- Practical potential: simple, non-adaptive scheme; reduced cognitive load for workers. However, empirical validation is preliminary, and the practicality of k-ary “both-equals-i” questions for k>2 is debatable. Stronger empirical evidence and comparisons would increase impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Fresh and relevant theoretical perspective (local encodability) tying information theory and crowdsourcing.
- New lower bounds under locality constraints.
- Simple, intuitive AND-based algorithm with attractive asymptotics.
- Preliminary human study supporting the core intuition.

Cons for acceptance:
- Technical inconsistencies in Sec. 3.3 (query counting, exponents) that affect main claims.
- Some key results are conjectural or deferred; proofs lack full rigor and constants.
- Limited and non-comprehensive experiments; weak baselines.
- Clarity issues and incomplete references.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The ideas are promising and appropriate for NeurIPS, but the paper needs to correct technical inconsistencies, tighten proofs, and strengthen empirical evaluation.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Fix the m vs d accounting in Sec. 3.3; derive tight, non-asymptotic bounds with correct constants. If m=nd/2, show explicitly that d = Θ(log(k/δ)) suffices and adjust Theorem 5 accordingly.
- Provide a rigorous, self-contained proof of Theorem 3 (and, if possible, a lower bound specialized to XOR under locality without relying on the Massey conjecture).
- Give a matching (or near-matching) lower bound for pairwise AND in the approximate/noisy setting, to complement achievability.
- Clarify the decoder and computational complexity for exact recovery; discuss practical, polynomial-time decoders (e.g., belief propagation on sparse graphs) and how close they get to ML.
- Explore Δ>2 queries and their gains; relate to group testing and pooling designs, which are closely connected to AND/OR functions.
- Strengthen experiments: larger datasets; diverse tasks; AMT with quality control; heterogeneous worker models; comparisons with Dawid–Skene/GLAD/KOS; stronger same-cluster baselines with optimized query designs.
- Discuss robustness to unknown/misspecified p and q; incorporate estimation of p and data-driven thresholds.
- Improve presentation: consistent notation, fully specified figures and references.
