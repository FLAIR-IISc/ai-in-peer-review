Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies nonadaptive, budgeted querying for semisupervised clustering/label learning with a focus on pairwise queries that return simple functions of labels. It introduces the lens of “locally encodable source coding,” where each query (compressed bit) depends on at most Δ labels, drawing connections to source coding, locally decodable/“smooth” compression, and joint source–channel coding. The core contributions are:

1) Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2): For binary labels with prior p, using parity (XOR) of Δ labels as queries, the paper gives an achievability scheme with m ≈ n(h(p)+o(1)) / log2(1/α) queries (α = 1/2(1+(1−4p(1−p))^Δ)), employing ML decoding over a random Δ-regular query matrix. It also gives a lower bound that strictly improves on the classic nH(p) bound for some p by exploiting locality constraints.

2) Noiseless, approximate recovery (k=2): The paper provides a lower bound on achievable distortion when queries are constrained to act on at most Δ inputs, via rate–distortion reasoning under local encodability. It argues that XOR (“same-cluster”) queries are provably suboptimal for approximate recovery by invoking classical results on the limitations of linear rate–distortion codes (Ancheta–Massey). It proposes an AND-based pairwise query and a simple decoder (“1 if any incident answer is 1, else 0”), with an explicit error expression derived via a Poisson left-degree approximation. Empirically and in bounds, AND queries outperform XOR for approximate recovery.

3) Noisy approximate recovery (general k): For pairwise queries with k-ary outputs defined as Q(X,X′)=i if X=X′=i, else 0 (for k=2 this is AND), with independent per-query error rate q, the paper proposes a nonadaptive scheme using a d-regular graph and a threshold decoder (Algorithm 1). It proves that with d = Θ(log(k/δ)) (and hence m=nd/2 = O(n log(k/δ))), one can recover at least a (1−δ) fraction of labels w.h.p. The scheme only needs priors p (or, via Theorem 6, the relative cluster sizes). This improves over O(n log n) bounds known for exact recovery with noisy same-cluster queries (e.g., Mazumdar–Saha). Small-scale experiments with crowdsourced annotations (k=2) suggest that AND questions yield fewer label errors than same-cluster queries; additional synthetic experiments compare against spectral clustering on same-cluster responses.

The work relates to: query-based clustering (Ashtiani et al.; Mazumdar & Saha), crowdsourcing reliability and task design (Karger–Oh–Shah; Vempaty et al.), and local coding/decoding and smooth compression (Makhdoumi et al.; Montanari–Mossel; Chandar et al.). The explicit “locally encodable source coding” framing appears novel.

Quality (technical soundness):
- The exact-recovery achievability with parity queries under prior p leverages standard typical set and expurgation arguments for sparse linear maps; the per-row collision probability bound of 1/2(1+(1−2r(p))^Δ) is standard, and the ML-decoder achievability seems sound asymptotically (though computationally infeasible).
- The lower bound for exact recovery (Theorem 2) leverages a locality-constrained counting argument (elements touched by few queries) and Hamming-ball covering; the presentation is terse and would benefit from more rigorous handling of constants and typicality, but the approach is reasonable.
- The rate–distortion lower bound under local encodability (Theorem 3) is stated without proof in the main text; its expression is nontransparent and may need clearer derivation and conditions (e.g., the role of Δ and h′).
- The conjectured XOR (“same-cluster”) lower bound (Conjecture 1) relies on classical limitations of linear codes as rate–distortion codes; it is stated as a conjecture, known for p=1/2. This section appropriately distinguishes what is proven vs conjectured.
- The AND-query achievability analysis uses a Poisson left-degree approximation and a counting formula for distinct 2-hop neighbors. The resulting bound is explicit and methodologically reasonable.
- The noisy k-ary AND scheme (Algorithm 1) and its analysis via Chernoff bounds and dependency graphs are straightforward and technically sound; the m=O(n log(k/δ)) guarantee for (1−δ)-good recovery aligns with standard thresholding arguments on random regular graphs.
- Some steps (e.g., constants in thresholds Ci, finite-n behavior, explicit dependence on q, p, k) are deferred to the appendix; inclusion of key constants in the main text would improve rigor.

Clarity:
- The paper’s organization is generally logical, but clarity suffers from numerous typographical errors, inconsistent notation (Δ vs d for degrees; d used for degree and distortion), and occasional imprecise statements (e.g., “The compressed vector is not necessarily binary, nor it is necessarily smaller length”).
- Several key proofs are deferred; some theorems (e.g., Theorem 3, Theorem 6) require clearer statements of assumptions and exact dependencies.
- Figures are referenced but not shown here; their absence makes it harder to evaluate empirical claims.
- References are inconsistently formatted, with duplications and incomplete bibliographic details.

Originality:
- The locally encodable source coding viewpoint for query-based clustering appears novel and leads to new lower bounds under locality constraints.
- The emphasis on AND queries (and their k-ary generalization) as superior to XOR for approximate recovery, especially under noise, is a useful and arguably novel insight in the crowdsourcing context.
- Exact-recovery parity-query achievability with m<n for some p is a refined result under local constraints, though grounded in known information-theoretic tools.
- The conjectured XOR lower bound leverages classical results, appropriately credited.

Significance:
- The O(n log(k/δ)) query complexity for (1−δ)-accurate labeling under noise is practically meaningful for nonadaptive crowdsourcing and improves on existing noisy exact-recovery bounds when δ is constant.
- The AND-vs-XOR design insight can influence task design in practice, potentially reducing worker effort and error.
- The lower bounds under local encodability help delineate fundamental limits but fall short of full tightness across regimes; a matching lower bound for the noisy approximate setting is not provided.
- Empirical validation is limited in scale (10 workers, 200 items) and only for k=2; however, it supports the qualitative claim that AND questions are easier.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Introduces and fruitfully exploits the locally encodable source coding perspective to derive bounds under realistic locality constraints.
- Provides a simple, implementable nonadaptive algorithm with clear guarantees m=O(n log(k/δ)) for noisy approximate recovery.
- Offers a principled argument and empirical evidence that AND-type queries outperform XOR for approximate recovery and are easier for workers.
- Contains nontrivial information-theoretic bounds (including a lower bound that improves on nH(p) due to locality).

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Clarity and presentation issues (typos, inconsistent notation, missing proofs/figures) hinder accessibility and verification.
- The exact-recovery achievability relies on ML decoding and does not address computational tractability.
- The XOR suboptimality statement for approximate recovery partly rests on an unproven conjecture (beyond p=1/2).
- Experimental validation is small-scale and only for k=2; the practicality of k-ary outputs for k>2 (cognitive load of “return i if both are i”) is not empirically evaluated.
- No matching lower bounds are provided for the noisy approximate regime; optimality of O(n log(k/δ)) remains open.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify and unify notation (use distinct symbols for degree vs distortion), fix typos, and improve reference formatting.
- Move key elements of Theorem 3 and Theorem 6 proofs (or precise statements with conditions and constants) into the main text; detail constants in Algorithm 1’s thresholds.
- Discuss computational aspects of decoding in Theorem 1 and potential practical relaxations (e.g., message-passing decoders).
- Provide empirical evaluation for k>2 and assess worker burden for k-ary answers; compare with majority-voting baselines and adaptive strategies where relevant.
- Where possible, add lower bounds for the noisy approximate setting or discuss obstacles.

Overall, the paper offers interesting theoretical insights and a simple algorithm with meaningful performance guarantees under realistic nonadaptive, local-query constraints, but presentation issues and limited empirical validation temper its impact.
