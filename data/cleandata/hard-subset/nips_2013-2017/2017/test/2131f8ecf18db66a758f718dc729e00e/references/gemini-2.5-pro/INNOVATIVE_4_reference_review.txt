**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of semi-supervised clustering using a limited budget of non-adaptive queries to an oracle, a setting common in crowdsourcing. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they introduce a novel and powerful information-theoretic lens, framing the problem as one of *locally encodable source coding*. This allows them to derive new lower bounds on the required number of queries for both exact and approximate recovery of cluster labels. Second, and more significantly, they challenge the de-facto standard "same-cluster" (or XOR) query. They argue that this type of query is suboptimal for approximate recovery and propose a novel "AND-query" as a superior alternative. For the noisy setting with `k` clusters, they provide a non-adaptive querying scheme and a simple decoding algorithm using these AND-style queries that recovers `(1-δ)` of the labels correctly with only `O(n log(k/δ))` queries, a substantial improvement over prior art. The paper supports its theoretical claims with a small-scale but insightful real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The main strength of this paper lies in its exceptional originality and the boldness of its central ideas.

**Originality & Significance:**
The connection drawn to locally encodable source coding is, to my knowledge, entirely new in this context. It is a creative and powerful framing that brings a new theoretical toolbox to the active clustering community. While this theoretical contribution is valuable in itself, the paper's most exciting contribution is the introduction of the AND-query. The community has largely taken for granted that "same-cluster" queries are the natural and optimal primitive for this task. This paper frontally challenges that assumption, not with a minor tweak, but with a fundamentally different logical operation. The authors provide a compelling case—backed by rate-distortion arguments, new achievability bounds, and practical experiments—that this alternative query is not just viable, but significantly more efficient for approximate recovery in noisy settings.

The significance of this is high. The `O(n log(1/δ))` query complexity for approximate recovery is a strong theoretical result that advances the state of the art. More importantly, the paper opens up a new and fascinating direction for research: what is the *optimal* query function for a given crowdsourcing task? The experimental finding that human workers made fewer than half as many errors on AND-queries compared to same-cluster queries on the same pairs is a striking result. It suggests that the cognitive load of a query is a critical, and hitherto overlooked, design parameter. This insight alone could influence the design of future crowdsourcing systems and algorithms.

**Quality:**
The theoretical development is solid, providing new lower bounds and achievability results across several settings. The proposed algorithm for the noisy case is simple and elegant, exploiting a statistical gap that does not exist with symmetric XOR queries.

The primary weakness is that the work is not yet methodologically complete. The theoretical analysis in the main text relies on some approximations (e.g., a Poisson model for node degrees) and defers several proofs to an appendix. The upper and lower bounds for the exact, noiseless case are not tight. The real-world experiment, while providing a fantastic proof of concept, is small in scale (200 items, 10 workers). A more traditional reviewer might see these as significant flaws.

However, from my perspective, these are acceptable trade-offs for the sheer novelty of the contribution. The paper is not an incremental improvement on an established method; it is a bold proposal of a new direction. In such a case, a perfect, exhaustive validation is less critical than a clear and compelling demonstration of the core idea. The small experiment achieves exactly that, providing strong evidence for the practical utility of the proposed query type. The theoretical framework is sound, even if some of the bounds could be tightened in future work.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the shift from the standard source coding problem to the locally-constrained version, and clearly articulate the limitations of XOR queries that motivate the introduction of AND queries.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Highly Novel Conceptual Framing:** Introduces the "locally encodable source coding" perspective to the field.
*   **Bold and Counter-Intuitive Proposal:** Challenges the standard "same-cluster" query with the novel AND-query, opening a new line of research into optimal query design.
*   **Strong Theoretical Results:** Achieves a significantly improved query complexity of `O(n log(1/δ))` for noisy, approximate recovery.
*   **Compelling Practical Insight:** The experimental finding that AND-queries yield substantially lower human error rates is a significant and actionable result for the crowdsourcing community.
*   **Sparks New Ideas:** This is precisely the kind of thought-provoking paper that can inspire follow-up work in multiple directions.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Incomplete Validation:** The experimental evaluation is on a small scale.
*   **Minor Theoretical Gaps:** The analysis relies on some non-rigorous approximations in the main text, and the derived bounds are not always tight.

**Recommendation**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. While it may lack the methodological polish and exhaustive experimentation of more incremental work, its conceptual contributions are profound. NIPS should be the premier venue for bold, paradigm-shifting ideas, and this paper is a perfect example. It takes a familiar problem, looks at it through a completely new theoretical lens, and proposes a simple, non-obvious solution that is shown to be superior both theoretically and practically. The weaknesses are minor and easily addressable in future work, whereas the strengths represent a significant and exciting advance for the field. This paper will be read, cited, and built upon.
