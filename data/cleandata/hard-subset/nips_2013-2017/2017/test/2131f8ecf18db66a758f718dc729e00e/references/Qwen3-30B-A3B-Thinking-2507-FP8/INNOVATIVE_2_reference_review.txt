

# Review: Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding

This paper introduces a bold and novel approach to clustering problems by rethinking the fundamental question of what queries to use in crowdsourcing-based clustering. The authors propose using AND queries instead of the conventional "same cluster" (XOR) queries, demonstrating that this simple change leads to dramatically improved query complexity for approximate recovery. The paper's core insight—that AND queries can recover (1-δ) proportion of labels with O(n log(1/δ)) queries (compared to O(n log n) for traditional methods)—represents a significant theoretical advancement with practical implications.

The paper's novelty lies in three interconnected contributions: (1) reinterpreting clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem, (2) establishing that AND queries fundamentally outperform XOR queries for approximate recovery, and (3) providing a complete theoretical framework for this new approach. The authors successfully bridge information theory, clustering, and crowdsourcing in a way that challenges the field's conventional wisdom. Their theoretical results establish new lower bounds for locally encodable source coding and provide matching upper bounds for the AND query approach.

The most compelling aspect of this work is its clear demonstration that the standard "same cluster" query is fundamentally suboptimal for approximate recovery. The authors provide rigorous theoretical justification (using Massey's result on linear codes) and compelling experimental evidence showing that AND queries yield significantly lower error rates in real crowdsourcing tasks (105 errors vs. 234 for "same cluster" queries). This is not merely an incremental improvement—it represents a paradigm shift in how we think about query design for clustering.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with strong theoretical foundations supported by real-world validation. The theoretical analysis is sound, with careful attention to both noiseless and noisy settings, and the experimental results perfectly complement the theoretical claims. The authors also provide insightful analysis of why AND queries work better than XOR queries, noting that for AND queries a worker only needs to know one label to answer correctly for a "no" response, whereas XOR queries require knowledge of both labels.

While the paper primarily focuses on binary labels (k=2), the authors appropriately note that their results extend to larger k, and the real-world experiment with two movie genres validates their approach. The only minor limitation is the need for prior knowledge of cluster sizes, which is a common assumption in clustering literature (e.g., in stochastic block models) and can be estimated through subsampling.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper exceptionally compelling. It doesn't just improve upon existing methods—it challenges the foundational assumption of what constitutes a useful query for clustering. The paper's contribution is not merely a new algorithm but a new conceptual framework for thinking about clustering with human input. This represents a significant step forward in the field, with clear potential for impact in practical crowdsourcing applications.

The theoretical results are strong, the experimental validation is convincing, and the paper makes a clear case for why this approach is fundamentally better than existing methods. The authors have successfully bridged information theory and practical crowdsourcing in a way that advances both fields. The paper's core contribution—replacing XOR with AND queries—seems almost obvious in hindsight, but its novelty and impact are undeniable.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to NIPS, advancing our theoretical understanding of clustering with human input while providing practical improvements that could directly benefit real-world applications. The paper's bold rethinking of a fundamental assumption in the field is exactly the kind of innovative work that NIPS should prioritize.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Strengths:**
- Novel conceptual shift (AND vs. XOR queries)
- Strong theoretical foundation with new bounds
- Clear experimental validation in real crowdsourcing
- Practical implications for reducing crowdsourcing costs
- Clear explanation of why AND queries are superior

**Weaknesses:**
- Primarily focuses on binary labels (though extension to k>2 is noted)
- Requires prior knowledge of cluster sizes (common assumption in the field)
- Limited discussion of how to obtain the prior distribution in practice

Overall, this paper represents a significant advance in the field with strong theoretical and practical contributions. The novelty of the approach outweighs any minor methodological limitations, and the paper clearly advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.
