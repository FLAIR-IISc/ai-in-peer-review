

This paper introduces a novel perspective on clustering via queries by framing it as a locally encodable source coding problem in information theory. The authors consider non-adaptive querying schemes where each query involves at most Δ elements and returns a binary answer. They demonstrate that traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries are inefficient for approximate recovery, and propose AND queries as a superior alternative that achieves (1-δ)-good recovery with significantly fewer queries. The work includes theoretical bounds for exact and approximate recovery (both noiseless and noisy) and validates the approach with real crowdsourcing experiments.

The paper makes several technically sound contributions. For exact recovery with noiseless queries (k=2), the authors establish a new lower bound that improves upon the naive nH(p) bound for certain prior distributions and provide a querying scheme that achieves near-optimal query complexity. For approximate recovery, they rigorously prove that AND queries outperform XOR queries by leveraging a key insight from Massey's result on linear codes. The noisy query case (k≥2) features a main algorithmic contribution: a querying scheme with O(n log(k/δ)) queries that achieves (1-δ)-good recovery, with a detailed analysis of the error probability. The theoretical results are well-supported by proofs, and the real-world experiment with 200 movies validates the theoretical claims, showing that AND queries yield significantly fewer errors (105 vs. 234) than "same cluster" queries.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results to experimental validation. The contributions are properly contextualized within related work on crowdsourcing-based clustering [4, 21, 22] and locally decodable source coding [19, 16, 26]. The authors make a compelling case for the novelty of their approach, particularly regarding the locally encodable source coding perspective, which they note is not well-studied in information theory.

Strengths include the clear theoretical advances in query complexity bounds, the practical significance of reducing query counts in crowdsourcing applications, and the successful experimental validation. The paper makes a significant contribution to understanding the fundamental limits of query-based clustering. The real-world experiment with crowdsourced data provides strong empirical support for the theoretical claims, demonstrating that AND queries are easier for workers to answer and yield better results.

Weaknesses include the focus on k=2 for most theoretical results, with the k≥2 case presented as a natural extension. The lower bound in Theorem 3 relies on a conjecture (Conjecture 1) that is only proven for p=0.5, which slightly weakens the theoretical contribution. The real-world experiment uses a relatively small dataset (200 movies), which may limit the generalizability of the findings. While the paper cites related work on locally encodable source coding [23], it could more explicitly connect its results to this literature.

The paper's significance is substantial: it provides fundamental insights into query-based clustering that directly impact practical crowdsourcing applications. By showing that AND queries can reduce query counts by orders of magnitude for approximate recovery, the work has clear implications for reducing costs in crowdsourcing systems. The theoretical framework also opens new research directions in locally encodable source coding.

The paper meets all four review criteria: it is technically sound with rigorous proofs, clearly written with well-structured arguments, makes a novel contribution by introducing the locally encodable source coding perspective and AND queries, and has significant practical implications for crowdsourcing applications.

The main contributions are well-justified and the theoretical results are carefully derived. The experimental validation strengthens the paper's claims and demonstrates practical relevance. The authors provide a comprehensive treatment of the problem across multiple settings (exact/approximate recovery, noiseless/noisy queries), which adds to the paper's depth.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, as it combines theoretical insights from information theory with practical applications in machine learning. The work represents a significant advancement in the understanding of query complexity for clustering, with implications for semi-supervised learning and crowdsourcing systems.

The paper makes a strong case for acceptance at NIPS. The theoretical contributions are novel and well-supported, the experimental validation is appropriate and convincing, and the practical significance of the results is clear. The work advances the state of the art in query-based clustering and provides new theoretical tools for analyzing similar problems.

Pro: Novel theoretical perspective on clustering as locally encodable source coding; significant improvement over existing query strategies; rigorous theoretical analysis with well-supported bounds; successful experimental validation.

Con: Focus on k=2 for most theoretical results; reliance on a conjecture for one lower bound; relatively small-scale real-world experiment.
