This paper investigates semi-supervised clustering using queries to an oracle (e.g., crowd workers). It frames the problem as a locally encodable source coding problem and provides theoretical bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery of labels, even in the presence of noisy oracle responses. A key contribution is the introduction of AND queries, which are shown to potentially outperform ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries in certain scenarios. The paper also includes experimental validation on real datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon a growing body of work on crowdsourcing for clustering and entity resolution (e.g., [4, 8, 20, 33]). The connection to locally encodable source coding is a novel framing and allows the authors to leverage tools from information theory. The exploration of AND queries as an alternative to XOR queries is a potentially impactful idea. The theoretical results (Theorems 1-6) provide valuable insights into the fundamental limits of querying complexity. The inclusion of experiments, even with a limited scope, strengthens the paper.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Framing:**  The use of locally encodable source coding provides a fresh perspective on the querying problem, enabling the application of information-theoretic tools.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents several well-defined theorems with proofs, establishing lower and upper bounds on query complexity.
*   **AND Query Proposal:** The introduction of AND queries as a potential alternative to XOR queries is a significant contribution, with theoretical justification and empirical support.
*   **Noise Handling:** The analysis extends to the case of noisy oracle responses, a realistic scenario in crowdsourcing applications.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, while modest in scope, provide some evidence supporting the theoretical findings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Notation and Definitions:** Some notation is introduced without clear explanation initially (e.g., `nr(p)`).  A more thorough explanation of locally encodable source coding, beyond the initial mention, would be beneficial for readers unfamiliar with this concept.
*   **Complexity of Proofs:** The proofs are dense and rely on several technical results from information theory. While rigorous, they may be difficult for readers not specialized in this area to follow.  More intuitive explanations of the key ideas behind the proofs would improve accessibility.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited in scope.  The results are shown for specific parameters and datasets.  A more comprehensive evaluation with varying parameters and datasets would increase confidence in the proposed approach. The comparison with other state-of-the-art clustering algorithms is also missing.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The paper focuses primarily on theoretical aspects.  A discussion of practical challenges and considerations in implementing the proposed algorithms, such as query design and worker incentive mechanisms, would be valuable.
*   **Overly verbose introduction:** The introduction could be more concise. While providing context is important, the length and level of detail may deter some readers.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to semi-supervised clustering.
*   The connection to locally encodable source coding is a significant contribution.
*   The AND query proposal has the potential to improve query efficiency.
*   The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem with clear theoretical guarantees.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The complexity of the proofs and notation may limit accessibility.
*   The experimental validation is limited in scope.
*   The paper lacks a detailed discussion of practical considerations.
*   The introduction is overly verbose.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. The novel framing of the problem, the theoretical rigor, and the potential impact of the AND query proposal are compelling. While the paper has some weaknesses in terms of clarity and experimental scope, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the points raised in the weaknesses section, particularly regarding clarity of notation and expanding the experimental evaluation. A more concise introduction would also be beneficial.
