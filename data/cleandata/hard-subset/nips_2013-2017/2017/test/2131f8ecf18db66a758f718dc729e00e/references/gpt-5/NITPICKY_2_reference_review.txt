Summary
This paper studies nonadaptive, crowdsourced clustering/labeling via pairwise queries and frames it as locally encodable source coding: each query output depends on at most Δ labels. Contributions include (i) information-theoretic bounds for exact and approximate recovery; (ii) the observation that “same cluster” (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery, with a proposed alternative using AND queries; (iii) an algorithm for noisy queries over k classes that claims O(n log(k/δ)) queries suffice to recover a (1−δ)-fraction of labels; and (iv) a small-scale crowdsourcing experiment supporting the claim that AND queries are easier for workers and perform better than XOR in practice.

Relation to prior work is made to rate-distortion, locally decodable/“smooth” codes, and crowdsourcing for entity resolution. The AND-vs.-XOR comparison is tied to classical linear rate-distortion limitations (Ancheta-Massey).

Strengths
- Interesting perspective: casting limited-arity query design as “locally encodable” source coding is insightful and seems underexplored relative to local decoding/smooth compression.
- The observation that AND queries can exploit asymmetry to create a mean-gap under noise (enabling simple thresholding) is elegant and practically motivated.
- The approximate-recovery regime is the right target for budget-limited crowdsourcing. The aim of O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive queries (if correct) is compelling.
- The small crowdsourcing experiment, while limited, is aligned with the modeling intuition that AND is easier than XOR for workers.

Weaknesses and technical issues (many are substantial)
- Query-count inconsistency (major): In Sec. 3.3 and Algorithm 1 you repeatedly claim m = n d^2 queries for a d-regular graph, but a simple counting argument gives |E| = nd/2 queries. The proof of Theorem 5 and the experimental section also implicitly use nd/2 (e.g., n=200, d=10 ⇒ 1000 queries). This inconsistency propagates to the main scaling claim. If m = nd/2 and d = Θ(log(k/δ)), then m = Θ(n log(k/δ)); if m = nd^2, then m = Θ(n log^2(k/δ)). The paper currently claims O(n log(k/δ)) while repeatedly writing nd^2. This must be corrected and the bounds rechecked.
- Chernoff exponents and dependence (major): In Theorem 5 you bound per-node misclassification by c k e^{-2 d^2}, but Binomial(d,·) tail bounds scale like exp(−Θ(d)), not exp(−Θ(d^2)). This error also affects the union bounds and the “almost always” concentration. Additionally, you assert Δ* ≤ d + d for dependency (1- and 2-hop neighbors); in a d-regular graph this should be O(d^2), not O(d). These mistakes compromise the high-probability statement.
- Exact recovery section (Sec. 3.1) has correctness/clarity issues:
  - The expurgation argument “throwing out label vectors” is inappropriate in source coding unless you show the source lies in the retained set with high probability. The text hints at this (“take to be of the form n^{-β}”) but the variable is undefined and the argument is incomplete.
  - The random ensemble analysis uses “typical set” collisions but ultimately needs a careful conditioning on typicality and a union bound over typical pairs; the derivation is sketched and contains typos (e.g., r(p) defined twice; missing normalization signs).
  - Several algebraic slips (e.g., λ = 2m/n later written as 2mn; α reused ambiguously) make it hard to verify the bound.
- Lower bound in Theorem 2 is opaque and possibly incorrect as stated:
  - The formula m ≥ nh(p)·max{1, max_ρ (1−ρ)/h(((1−ρ) r(p) Δ)/ρ)} has dimensional and domain issues (argument of h(·) may exceed 1, base of logs unspecified). The derivation sketch suggests m ≥ n(1−ρ)h(p)/h( … ), but the final statement differs.
  - Without a precise and self-contained proof, it’s impossible to assess whether the bound improves on the classical nh(p) uniformly and under what parameter ranges.
- Approximate recovery lower bound (Theorem 3) is stated without proof and has an unusual form δ ≥ δ̃(m/n) + [h(p) − h(δ̃)]/[h′(δ̃)(1 + e^{Δ h′(δ̃)})]. It is unclear that the RHS stays within [0,1], what regime it strengthens the unconstrained rate-distortion bound, or how tight it is. Given that this is central to the “local encoding penalty,” a clear proof or at least intuition is needed in the main text.
- XOR suboptimality: The “Conjecture 1” is labeled a conjecture and only known for p=0.5. Yet the narrative leans on it to claim XOR is strictly worse. Please either (i) provide a concrete proven lower bound for XOR in your model and compare to your AND achievability, or (ii) carefully qualify the claim.
- Theorem 4 (AND achievability, noiseless) has several typos and notational inconsistencies. The complicated expression could be replaced by a simpler asymptotic bound in terms of m/n and p; as written it’s difficult to interpret. Also, the Poisson approximation line flips 2m/n to 2mn.
- Noise model and thresholds: In Sec. 3.3, the expectation E[Nu,i] expression is correct, but the thresholds Ci omit a discussion of how to set them when priors are unknown/noisy, and how to control the false positive rate across all i with a clean union bound. The claimed extension (Theorem 6) with known class sizes but adversarial labels needs a more careful argument (e.g., permutation-invariance and coupling to the i.i.d. prior).
- Experiments:
  - The “nd^2 queries” phrase recurs here but the actual number is nd/2. Please fix.
  - Only 10 volunteers, with 100 questions each, uncalibrated worker reliabilities, and no replication/aggregation modeling. Confidence intervals or bootstrap estimates would help.
  - XOR comparison uses a single spectral clustering baseline; please consider stronger baselines (e.g., correlation clustering with noisy must-link constraints) and report sensitivity to graph design and thresholds.
- Presentation:
  - Numerous typos/formatting issues (e.g., “st Conference…”, footnotes interleaved into text, missing figure panels, inconsistent notation such as h vs H, r(p) use, misplaced parentheses).
  - References are often incomplete or duplicated; some arXiv/preprint identifiers repeated; ensure precise citations (e.g., Ancheta-Massey result used).

Questions for the authors
1. Please resolve the m = nd^2 vs m = nd/2 inconsistency and restate Theorem 5 accordingly. Which scaling is actually proved?
2. Can you provide a clean, high-level proof sketch for Theorem 3 in the main text and indicate regimes where it significantly strengthens rate-distortion?
3. For XOR queries: can you state a rigorous lower bound (not conjectural) in your model and compare quantitatively to AND achievability for p=1/2?
4. In Theorem 4, can you provide a simpler explicit upper bound on δ as a function of m/n and p?
5. For Theorem 6, please formalize the random permutation argument and its coupling to the i.i.d. model.

Pros
- Fresh information-theoretic framing (locally encodable source coding).
- Insight that AND queries exploit asymmetry to beat XOR for approximate recovery.
- Simple, practical nonadaptive algorithm with promising scaling (if corrected).
- Initial empirical validation with real crowd answers.

Cons
- Major inconsistencies in query counting and exponents that undermine the main theorem’s scaling.
- Several proofs are sketchy or deferred; key statements have typos and unclear conditions.
- Experimental evaluation is too limited and the XOR baseline is weak.
- Many presentation/notation/formatting issues.

Recommendation
Reject. The core ideas are interesting and likely publishable, but the current manuscript has substantial technical and presentation flaws (notably the m = nd^2 vs nd/2 confusion and incorrect concentration exponents) that must be addressed to make the results reliable and reproducible. I encourage the authors to fix these issues, strengthen/clarify the proofs, and resubmit. Confidence: high.
