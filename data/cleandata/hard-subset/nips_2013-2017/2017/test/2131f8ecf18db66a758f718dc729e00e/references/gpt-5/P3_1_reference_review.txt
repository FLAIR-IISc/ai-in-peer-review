Summary
The paper studies non-adaptive, budgeted crowdsourced labeling/clustering through the lens of “locally encodable source coding”: each query/measurement is a simple function of at most Δ labels. The authors develop information-theoretic lower bounds and algorithms for three regimes:
- Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2): with Δ-ary XOR queries (pairwise XOR is “same-cluster”), ML decoding achieves exact recovery using m ≈ n h(p) / log(1/α) queries with α = 0.5(1+(1−4p(1−p))^Δ); a converse stronger than nh(p) is given for some p.
- Noiseless, approximate recovery (k=2): lower bounds under local encoding are derived; “same-cluster” (XOR) is argued to be suboptimal for lossy recovery via classical results on linear rate–distortion codes. A non-linear pairwise AND-query scheme with simple decoding is analyzed and shown to outperform XOR in this regime.
- Noisy, approximate recovery (general k): a k-ary AND-style query (“returns i if both inputs equal i, else 0”) with per-node thresholding on a d-regular graph yields (1−δ)-accurate recovery with m = O(n log(k/δ)) queries. A small crowdsourcing experiment suggests AND queries are easier for workers and empirically reduce error compared to same-cluster queries.

Relation to prior work is made to crowdsourced entity resolution and to locally decodable/smooth compression; the framing as locally encodable source coding and the systematic comparison of XOR vs AND queries appear novel.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: casting non-adaptive query design as locally encodable source coding is insightful and yields new lower bounds that reflect locality constraints.
- Technical contributions: (i) an achievability for exact recovery under sparse XOR queries that can beat n for certain priors; (ii) a converse that improves over nh(p) in some regimes; (iii) a clean, scalable AND-query algorithm with O(n log(k/δ)) queries under noise; (iv) a simple decoding/analysis (Chernoff + dependency graph) and robustness to unknown priors via estimated cluster sizes.
- Practical angle: AND queries are motivated as cognitively easier for workers; preliminary user study supports this.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Clarity and correctness issues:
  - m counting is inconsistent in Section 3.3 and experiments. For a d-regular graph on n nodes, |E|=nd/2, yet the paper repeatedly states m=nd^2. In the experiment with n=200, d=10, m is 1000, consistent with nd/2, not nd^2. This must be fixed throughout.
  - Section 3.1 achievability: the expurgation argument is not written cleanly. The text suggests “throwing out” label vectors to resolve collisions; for a probabilistic source this can be acceptable if the expurgated set has vanishing probability mass, but the argument needs to be explicit (expected number of collisions < 1 ensures existence of Q with injectivity on the typical set; otherwise, quantify failure probability over X drawn i.i.d. p). As written, it reads like designing an encoder that depends on the realized X, which is invalid.
  - Several definitions and symbols (e.g., r(p), α) appear without consistent notation or with typos; some footnotes leak into body text; figures are referenced but not shown; proofs of key bounds (Theorem 3) are deferred, limiting verifiability.
- Evidence and comparisons:
  - The empirical evaluation is small-scale (200 items, 10 users), uses hand-picked movie categories, and compares AND vs XOR under different error models and decoders (simple thresholding vs spectral clustering), making the comparison uneven. Stronger baselines (e.g., best-known non-adaptive XOR schemes for approximate recovery, or majority-vote/denoising pipelines from crowdsourcing literature) are not included.
  - The claim that AND strictly outperforms XOR for approximate recovery partly rests on a conjectured lower bound for linear (XOR) schemes; at p=0.5 the classical result establishes suboptimality of linear lossy codes, but the generality of the claim should be qualified and the exact statement/scope clarified.
- Positioning:
  - The AND query and thresholding on a sparse design connect to LDGM-based lossy compression and to group testing–style designs; these connections could be strengthened (e.g., Chandar’s thesis on sparse graph codes for compression; known algorithms/limits for LDGM lossy compression).
  - The noisy k-ary query model assumes symmetric confusion among incorrect outputs; this is convenient analytically but may be unrealistic. A brief robustness discussion would help.

Quality
- The high-level ideas and several analyses are sound, but the paper has technical slips (m miscounting, expurgation presentation) and key proofs relegated to the supplement. The noisy-approximate recovery analysis is clean and convincing. The exact-recovery achievability and improved converse are interesting but need clearer exposition.

Clarity
- The manuscript needs substantial polishing: consistent notation, correcting m, organizing proofs, cleaning footnotes, and improving figure references. Algorithm 1 should state the correct total query count and the relationship among d, m, δ, k, q, p precisely (the supplement presumably contains this; a summary belongs in the main text).

Originality
- The locally encodable source coding viewpoint and the advocacy for AND queries in this setting are, to my knowledge, novel. Lower bounds under locality constraints and the O(n log(k/δ)) noisy result add new angles to the literature.

Significance
- If clarified, the results could influence non-adaptive crowdsourcing and query design, showing that simple non-linear queries can be much more sample-efficient for approximate recovery, and stimulating further work on locally encodable coding limits. Practical impact hinges on broader empirical validation.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Fix the query-count inconsistency (nd^2 vs nd/2) and propagate the correction throughout, including Theorem 5 and experiments.
- Clarify the expurgation-based achievability in Theorem 1: formalize the probabilistic guarantee over X and existence of Q without “throwing out” realized sources.
- State Theorem 3 more explicitly in the main text with intuition for the extra term; provide a brief sketch if space allows.
- Calibrate the AND vs XOR comparison: include a strong non-adaptive XOR baseline for approximate recovery (e.g., optimized graph design + ML/Belief Propagation), and, if possible, test on larger datasets with crowdsourced labels.
- Discuss robustness to non-symmetric noise and to varying priors; can thresholds be learned from data?
- Better situate the work with LDGM lossy compression and group testing.

Pros
- Novel theoretical framing; meaningful lower bounds with locality.
- Simple, practical AND-based scheme with strong theoretical guarantees in the noisy approximate regime.
- Insightful observation that non-linear queries can beat XOR for lossy recovery; supported by a small user study.

Cons
- Significant clarity/notation errors; key proof details deferred.
- Empirical validation is limited and comparisons are uneven.
- Some claims about XOR suboptimality rely on conjectured bounds.

Recommendation
- Score: 5 (borderline reject). The ideas are interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper needs corrections and stronger empirical/analytic support to meet NeurIPS standards. I would encourage resubmission after addressing clarity issues, tightening proofs in the main text, and expanding the evaluation.
