

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper addresses the problem of clustering elements (or learning label vectors) with minimal queries to an oracle, framing it as a locally encodable source coding problem in information theory. The authors establish theoretical bounds on query complexity for exact and approximate recovery under noiseless and noisy settings, and demonstrate that AND queries significantly outperform traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery. The work connects clustering to information theory concepts like rate-distortion theory and locally encodable codes, with validation through real crowdsourcing experiments.

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) It establishes a novel theoretical framework by viewing clustering as locally encodable source coding; (2) It proves that AND queries require substantially fewer queries than "same cluster" queries for approximate recovery; (3) It provides tight theoretical bounds for query complexity in multiple scenarios; and (4) It validates the approach through real crowdsourcing experiments showing AND queries yield lower error rates than "same cluster" queries.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical results with both upper and lower bounds for query complexity. The proofs of Theorems 1-6 are technically sound, with clear connections to information theory concepts like rate-distortion theory and locally encodable codes. The authors appropriately reference foundational work (e.g., Massey's result on linear codes [17]) to establish their lower bounds.

2. **Novel perspective:** The framing of clustering as locally encodable source coding is a fresh perspective that connects two distinct areas (clustering and information theory). This conceptual contribution is well-motivated and provides new theoretical insights into query complexity.

3. **Practical validation:** The real-world crowdsourcing experiment with 100 action and 100 romantic movies demonstrates that AND queries yield significantly lower error rates (105 errors vs. 234 errors for "same cluster" queries), validating the theoretical claims in a practical setting.

4. **Clear separation of scenarios:** The paper distinguishes between exact recovery and approximate recovery, noiseless and noisy settings, and provides appropriate bounds for each case, making the results well-organized and comprehensive.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited generalization to k>2:** While the paper mentions generalization to k>2, most theoretical results focus on the binary case (k=2). The main algorithmic contribution (Theorem 5) and experiments are for k=2, with only brief discussion of the general case. This limits the broader applicability of the work.

2. **Supplementary material reliance:** Some key proofs (e.g., Theorem 3) are referenced to supplementary material, which may reduce accessibility for readers who don't consult the appendices.

3. **NIPS-related work connections:** While the paper cites relevant prior work on clustering with queries (e.g., [4], [8], [21]), it could more explicitly connect its theoretical framework to recent NIPS work on related topics like clustering with side information [22, 23].

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The proofs are rigorous, and the experimental validation complements the theoretical results. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and limitations of their approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem setup is well-defined, and the main contributions are clearly articulated. The figures effectively illustrate the theoretical results. The connection between information theory concepts and the clustering problem is well-explained.

**Originality:** The paper introduces a novel theoretical framework (locally encodable source coding) for the clustering query problem. The demonstration that AND queries outperform "same cluster" queries for approximate recovery is a significant insight not present in prior work. While related concepts like locally decodable codes have been studied [19, 16], the paper's specific application to clustering with queries is new.

**Significance:** The problem of minimizing query complexity is important for practical applications like crowdsourcing. The paper provides theoretically optimal or near-optimal query schemes with practical implications for reducing costs in entity resolution and clustering tasks. The real-world validation strengthens the significance, showing that the theoretical improvements translate to reduced error rates in practice.

**Recommendation:** This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that connects information theory to clustering with queries, with clear practical implications. While the focus on k=2 limits the generality, the core insights about AND queries versus "same cluster" queries are significant and well-supported. The paper advances the state of the art in query-efficient clustering and provides both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel theoretical framework connecting clustering to locally encodable source coding
- Rigorous theoretical bounds with both upper and lower bounds
- Clear demonstration that AND queries outperform traditional approaches
- Practical validation through real crowdsourcing experiments
- Well-written and well-organized presentation

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited generalization to k>2 (though the paper does mention extensions)
- Some proofs deferred to supplementary material
- Could more explicitly connect to recent NIPS work on clustering

Overall, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in query-efficient clustering with clear practical implications. The work is technically sound, well-written, and appropriately validated. It meets all NIPS quality standards and makes a meaningful contribution to the field.
