This paper presents a fascinating and ambitious approach to semi-supervised clustering by framing it as a locally encodable source coding problem. The core idea – leveraging information-theoretic bounds and specifically exploring AND queries as an alternative to standard ‘same cluster’ queries – is genuinely novel and represents a significant departure from existing work in crowdsourcing and clustering. While the paper is dense and could benefit from increased clarity in certain sections, the boldness of the approach and the potential for impactful theoretical results outweigh its shortcomings. I believe this work pushes the boundaries of the field and offers a valuable new perspective.

**Summary:** The authors tackle the problem of learning cluster labels with limited, costly queries to an oracle (e.g., crowd workers). They establish a connection to locally encodable source coding and derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries needed for exact and approximate recovery. Critically, they demonstrate that AND queries can outperform ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries in the approximate recovery setting, especially when dealing with noisy oracles. Real-world experiments with Amazon Mechanical Turk provide preliminary validation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The connection to locally encodable source coding is a key strength. This framing unlocks a powerful theoretical toolkit for analyzing query complexity. The proposal of AND queries as superior to XOR queries is also a novel and intriguing contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents several theorems and lower bounds, demonstrating a strong commitment to theoretical analysis. The exploration of noisy oracles adds to the robustness of the framework.
*   **Potential Impact:**  If the theoretical findings hold up and can be translated into practical algorithms, this work could significantly reduce the cost of obtaining labels via crowdsourcing, enabling scaling to larger datasets.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of a crowdsourcing experiment, even with its limitations, strengthens the credibility of the work. The observation that AND queries appear to perform better in practice is encouraging.
*   **Comprehensive Scope:** The authors tackle several variations of the problem (exact vs. approximate recovery, noisy vs. noiseless oracles), demonstrating the versatility of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Readability:** The paper is quite dense and assumes a high level of background knowledge in information theory. The notation is heavy, and the flow of ideas could be improved with more intuitive explanations and examples. For example, the motivation behind focusing on *nonadaptive* queries is not clearly articulated beyond a passing mention of parallelizability.
*   **Complexity of Proofs:** The proofs are complex and sometimes lack sufficient detail. While rigor is appreciated, increased accessibility would broaden the impact of the work.
*   **Experiment Scale:** The crowdsourcing experiment is relatively small-scale. While it provides preliminary evidence supporting the AND query approach, a more extensive evaluation with a larger number of participants and diverse datasets would be beneficial. The comparison with spectral clustering seems a bit hastily presented.
*   **Practical Algorithm Details:** While the theoretical results are strong, the practical implications for designing efficient decoding algorithms are not fully explored. Algorithm 1 is presented, but further elaboration on its performance and potential optimizations would be valuable.
*  **Limited Exploration of Query Types:** While AND queries are shown to be superior to XOR queries, the space of possible query functions is vast. The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of other potential query types and their theoretical properties.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and promising approach to semi-supervised clustering.
*   The theoretical results are significant and offer valuable insights into the fundamental limits of query complexity.
*   The experimental validation, while limited, supports the core claims of the paper.
*   The work has the potential to have a significant impact on the field of crowdsourcing and machine learning.
*   The authors tackle a challenging problem with a sophisticated and well-motivated methodology.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is dense and difficult to read, potentially limiting its accessibility to a wider audience.
*   The proofs are complex and may require significant effort to verify.
*   The experimental validation is relatively limited in scope.
*   The practical implications of the theoretical results are not fully explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its shortcomings, I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper represents a bold and innovative contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, the strength of the theoretical analysis, and the potential for significant impact outweigh the concerns regarding clarity and experimental scope. The authors have identified a compelling new research direction, and I am confident that this work will inspire further investigation in the area of information-theoretic approaches to crowdsourcing and clustering. Minor revisions to improve clarity and potentially expand the experimental evaluation would further strengthen the paper.



**Rating:** Accept (7/10)
