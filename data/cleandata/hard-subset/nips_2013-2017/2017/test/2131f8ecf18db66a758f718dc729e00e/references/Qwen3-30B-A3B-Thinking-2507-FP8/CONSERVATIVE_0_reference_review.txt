

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for clustering with crowdsourcing by introducing "AND-queries" as an alternative to traditional "same cluster" queries. The authors frame the problem as a locally encodable source coding task, providing theoretical bounds for exact and approximate recovery in noiseless and noisy settings. The main claim is that AND-queries require fewer queries for approximate recovery compared to "same cluster" queries, supported by a small-scale real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

## Strengths
The paper makes a technically sound contribution to information theory, with well-constructed proofs for the theoretical bounds. The information-theoretic perspective on the clustering problem is interesting, and the mathematical derivations for the AND-query scheme are rigorous. The real-world experiment, while limited, provides some empirical validation of the theoretical claims.

## Weaknesses
Despite the technical soundness of the theoretical work, the paper has significant methodological shortcomings that undermine its contribution:

1. **Overstated novelty**: The authors claim "locally encodable source coding" is a new concept, but the paper cites [23] as having previously mentioned the concept (calling it "robust compression"). The paper fails to clearly distinguish its contribution from existing work on locally decodable codes [19, 16, 26, 6], which is a well-established field.

2. **Weak justification for AND-queries**: The claim that AND-queries are easier for workers because "a worker just needs to know one of the labels" is misleading. For "same cluster" queries, workers could also determine the answer without knowing both labels (e.g., if they know the movies belong to the same genre). The paper provides no empirical evidence that AND-queries are actually easier for workers.

3. **Inadequate experimental validation**: The real-world experiment has severe limitations:
   - Only 100 action and 100 romantic movies were used (small dataset)
   - Only 10 volunteers participated (extremely small sample size)
   - No statistical significance testing for the error rate difference (10.5% vs 23.4%)
   - No comparison to established clustering algorithms that might use "same cluster" queries

4. **Unproven theoretical claims**: Conjecture 1 (the lower bound for "same cluster" queries) is only proven for p=0.5 (equal-sized clusters), yet the paper treats it as a general result. The paper claims "same cluster" queries are "highly inefficient" for approximate recovery but fails to provide a rigorous proof for general p.

5. **Limited scope**: The paper claims to address k-clusters but the main algorithm (Algorithm 1) and most results are for k=2. The extension to k>2 is not well-justified, and the paper fails to address whether the AND-query approach scales well to more than two clusters.

6. **Lack of comparison to established baselines**: The paper doesn't compare its AND-query approach to state-of-the-art clustering algorithms that might be used with "same cluster" queries. The comparison to "same cluster" queries is insufficiently rigorous.

7. **Misleading claims**: The paper states that "repeating the same question multiple times is not allowed" and that "majority voting could even increase errors," but this is not a strong argument against "same cluster" queries. In fact, majority voting is a standard technique for error reduction in crowdsourcing.

## Recommendation
While the theoretical results for the AND-query scheme are mathematically sound, the paper's claims are overstated and insufficiently validated. The main contribution is not truly novel, as the core concept of locally encodable source coding has been previously studied under different terminology. The experimental validation is inadequate to support the paper's claims about the practical superiority of AND-queries.

The paper would benefit from:
1. A more thorough comparison to existing work on locally decodable codes
2. A larger, statistically rigorous experiment with proper baseline comparisons
3. A clear explanation for why AND-queries are superior beyond the limited "one label" argument
4. Better justification for the claims about "same cluster" queries being inefficient

Given these significant weaknesses, particularly the lack of rigorous empirical validation and overstated novelty, I recommend rejection. The paper presents an interesting theoretical perspective but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims about the practical advantages of AND-queries over established methods. The field would benefit more from a more thorough validation of the proposed approach against established baselines rather than a theoretical paper with limited empirical support.
