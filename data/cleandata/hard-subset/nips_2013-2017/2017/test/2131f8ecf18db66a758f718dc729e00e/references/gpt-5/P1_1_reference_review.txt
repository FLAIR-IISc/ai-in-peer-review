Summary
The paper studies non-adaptive, budgeted crowdsourced clustering/labeling through an information-theoretic lens, introducing “locally encodable source coding,” where each query/encoded bit depends on at most Δ labels. It develops lower bounds and algorithms for both noiseless and noisy settings, highlighting that widely used “same-cluster” (XOR) queries can be suboptimal for approximate recovery, and proposing AND-type queries that achieve better query/accuracy trade-offs.

Main contributions:
- Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2): An achievability result using Δ-ary XOR queries with m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries (α = 1/2(1+(1−4p(1−p))^Δ)), decoded by ML over the typical set; a converse lower bound that improves on the naive n h(p) in some regimes.
- Noiseless, approximate recovery (k=2): A new lower bound for Δ-limited queries; an argument (building on Massey’s limits for linear rate-distortion codes) that XOR/same-cluster queries are intrinsically limited for approximate recovery; an AND-query scheme with simple local decoding and a tractable error expression that beats the XOR lower bound over a range of parameters (provably at p=0.5).
- Noisy, approximate recovery (general k): A pairwise k-ary “AND-like” query that returns i if both labels equal i, else 0, passed through a symmetric noise model (correct w.p. 1−q, otherwise one of the other k−1 labels). A simple thresholding decoder over a d-regular graph achieves (1−δ)-accurate recovery with m = O(n log(k/δ)) non-adaptive queries. An experiment with 200 movies suggests AND queries are easier for workers and achieve lower empirical error than same-cluster queries.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects crowdsourced clustering/query complexity (e.g., Ashtiani et al. NIPS’16; Mazumdar & Saha NIPS’17/AAAI’17) to source coding under locality constraints, complementing the literature on locally decodable codes and smooth compression. Compared to noisy crowd-query clustering that needs O(n log n) queries (e.g., [21]), the AND approach promises O(n log(1/δ)) for (1−δ)-approximate recovery. The link to Massey’s limits on linear rate-distortion underpins the argument against XOR.

Quality
- Theory: The high-level ideas are sound and interesting. The exact-recovery achievability (Theorem 1) is a standard random-ensemble/expurgation argument adapted to Δ-local XOR; it yields information-theoretic feasibility but requires intractable ML decoding. The converse in Theorem 2 uses degree/locality constraints to improve on n h(p) in some regimes. The approximate-recovery lower bound (Theorem 3) is deferred and nontrivial; as stated, it is hard to assess without the appendix. The AND achievability (Theorem 4) is clean and yields an explicit error expression via a Poisson degree model, and the noisy k-ary AND algorithm (Theorem 5) uses a simple threshold gap plus Chernoff bounds to get m = O(n log(k/δ)).
- A key issue: throughout Section 3.3 (and in experiments), the number of queries is repeatedly written as n d^2. For a d-regular graph, |E| = n d/2, so the total number of pairwise queries is nd/2, not nd^2. The experiments (n=200, d=10, “1000 queries”) confirm it should be nd/2. This inconsistency must be fixed; it affects statement and proof of Theorem 5 and figures.
- Another concern: claims that AND “beats” same-cluster rely partly on a conjectured XOR lower bound (true at p=0.5). The paper should temper claims accordingly and clearly separate rigorously proved regions from conjectured ones.
- Computational aspects: The exact-recovery result uses ML over the typical set and is not computationally efficient. This limitation should be acknowledged more explicitly.
- Assumptions: The independence of query errors and prohibition of repeated queries are reasonable and clearly stated. The noise model in Section 3.3 is specific (symmetric among wrong answers); please clarify if results extend to asymmetric noise.

Clarity
The paper’s presentation needs significant editing:
- Notational typos/ambiguities: “nd^2 queries” vs nd/2; qk−1 vs q/(k−1); inconsistent logs (log vs log2); occasional missing parentheses and exponents; “dq k−1” should be d q/(k−1); “qkk−1” appears to mean q·k/(k−1). These make several formulas hard to parse.
- Some figures are referenced but not visible here; ensure axes and baselines are clear and that curves corresponding to conjectured bounds are labeled as such.
- Proofs of Theorem 3 and Theorem 6 are deferred; a summary of the core ideas would help.
- A brief discussion of computational complexity (ML vs local decoding) would improve balance.

Originality
- The “locally encodable source coding” framing and derived bounds are novel and timely, complementing prior work on local decodability/smooth compression.
- The insight that AND queries can be markedly better than XOR for approximate recovery (both noiseless and noisy) is original and practically relevant.
- The simple, non-adaptive, linear-time decoder with m = O(n log(k/δ)) under noise is a useful contribution.

Significance
- The work advances our understanding of the query/accuracy trade-off under practical constraints (locality, non-adaptivity, noise). The AND-based design is likely to influence future query primitives for crowdsourcing and related interactive labeling systems.
- However, the exact-recovery part is mainly information-theoretic (intractable decoding), and the experimental validation is small-scale. Stronger empirical comparisons to existing baselines (e.g., majority vote repetition strategies, EM/Dawid–Skene-style aggregation, spectral methods under identical budgets) would enhance impact.

Pros
- Clear conceptual contribution (locally encodable view) with meaningful lower bounds.
- Novel and practically motivated AND-query primitive with simple, scalable algorithm.
- Improved query complexity for approximate recovery under noise.
- Some real-user validation showing AND questions are easier and less error-prone.

Cons
- Serious clarity/notation errors (nd^2 vs nd/2) and several ambiguous formulas.
- Some claims rely on a conjectured XOR bound; need to delineate rigor vs conjecture.
- Exact-recovery achievability is computationally intractable.
- Limited experiments; baselines and ablations are thin.

Questions and suggestions
- Please correct all occurrences of “nd^2 queries” to nd/2, and audit affected theorems and plots.
- State Theorem 5 with explicit constants and precise conditions on q, k, p; clarify dependence of d on these parameters and δ.
- Clearly separate proved superiority of AND over XOR from conjectured regions; for p≠0.5, can you provide unconditional comparisons (e.g., to the best-known XOR achievability)?
- Discuss computational complexity explicitly and the practicality of ML decoding in Sec. 3.1.
- Expand experimental evaluation: compare to same budget with repeated same-cluster queries + majority/EM aggregation, and to entity-resolution heuristics; report confidence intervals.

Overall evaluation
This paper contains interesting, original theory with a compelling practical takeaway (use AND-like queries). With the substantial clarity fixes and a more cautious positioning of XOR-vs-AND claims, I lean positive. As it stands, the notational errors and reliance on conjecture weaken the presentation; I recommend acceptance conditional on a careful revision that fixes the technical exposition and strengthens empirical comparisons.
