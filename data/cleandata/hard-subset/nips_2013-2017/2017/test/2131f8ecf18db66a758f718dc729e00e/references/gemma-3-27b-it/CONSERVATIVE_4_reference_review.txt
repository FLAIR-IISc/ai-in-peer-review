This paper proposes a novel framework for semi-supervised clustering, framing it as a locally encodable source coding problem and introducing AND queries as a potentially more efficient alternative to traditional ‘same cluster’ queries, particularly in the presence of noisy oracle responses. While the motivation—reducing query cost in crowdsourcing scenarios—is valid, the paper suffers from a significant lack of practical demonstration and relies heavily on theoretical results with limited direct connection to real-world applicability.

The core idea of leveraging locally encodable codes is interesting, and the attempt to connect this to querying strategies is a creative starting point. However, the paper feels excessively theoretical and lacks a compelling argument for why this approach surpasses existing, well-established methods. The authors acknowledge the significant body of work on crowdsourcing and clustering but don’t sufficiently position their contribution within it, beyond highlighting the potential for query reduction. The theoretical results, while mathematically rigorous, are presented with insufficient intuition and practical implications. The numerous theorems and their proofs dominate the paper, obscuring the key insights.

A major weakness is the limited experimental validation. The single crowdsourcing experiment, while presented, is more of a proof-of-concept than a thorough evaluation. The chosen dataset is small (100 movies each), and the experimental setup lacks detail regarding worker qualification, query presentation order, and potential biases. Comparing the AND query performance directly to well-known clustering algorithms (e.g., k-means with appropriate similarity measures derived from the oracle responses) is missing. The claim that AND queries are "easier to answer" is supported only by anecdotal evidence, and a more rigorous user study would be necessary to substantiate this.  Furthermore, the experiment doesn’t convincingly demonstrate that the theoretical gains from AND queries translate to tangible improvements in clustering accuracy in a real-world scenario.

The discussion of noisy queries is a positive aspect, but the proposed algorithm (Algorithm 1) and its associated theorems are complex and difficult to follow. The connection between the theoretical error bounds and practical performance remains unclear. The extension to non-prior knowledge (Theorem 6) is interesting, but again lacks empirical support.

The paper also suffers from a somewhat convoluted writing style and an overabundance of notation. The numerous references, while demonstrating awareness of the relevant literature, can be overwhelming and detract from the central message. The constant referencing of the authors' previous work, while not inherently negative, sometimes feels self-promotional. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel framing of semi-supervised clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem.
*   Theoretical exploration of AND queries as a potential alternative to ‘same cluster’ queries.
*   Consideration of noisy oracle responses.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Excessively theoretical with limited practical demonstration.
*   Insufficient experimental validation and comparison to established methods.
*   Complex presentation and convoluted writing style.
*   Limited clarity regarding the practical benefits of the proposed approach.
*   Lack of convincing evidence that the theoretical gains translate to real-world improvements.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical exploration but falls short of making a significant contribution to the field. The lack of robust experimental validation and the complex presentation hinder its impact. While the idea of leveraging locally encodable codes is intriguing, it requires significantly more practical investigation and comparison to existing approaches before it can be considered a valuable addition to the NIPS program. I recommend **rejection** at this time. The authors should focus on strengthening the experimental evaluation, simplifying the presentation, and clearly articulating the practical benefits of their approach. A more thorough comparison to well-established clustering techniques would also be crucial for a revised submission.
