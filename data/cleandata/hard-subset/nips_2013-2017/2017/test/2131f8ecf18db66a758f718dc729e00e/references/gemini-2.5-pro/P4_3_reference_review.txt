This paper studies the problem of semi-supervised clustering of `n` elements into `k` clusters using nonadaptive queries. The central and novel contribution is to frame this problem within the information-theoretic paradigm of "locally encodable source coding," where each query's output is an encoded bit that depends on a small number (`∆`) of input labels. This framework allows the authors to derive fundamental limits and design near-optimal query schemes. The paper addresses three primary settings: (1) noiseless queries for exact recovery, (2) noiseless queries for approximate recovery, and (3) noisy queries for approximate recovery. For exact recovery with two clusters, the authors show that for certain prior label distributions, `o(n)` pairwise 'same-cluster' (XOR) queries are sufficient, and they provide a new information-theoretic lower bound. For approximate recovery, the paper argues that XOR queries are suboptimal and proposes "AND queries" as a more efficient alternative, providing an achievable performance bound that surpasses the theoretical limits of XOR queries. The main algorithmic result is for the noisy, approximate recovery setting, where a scheme based on generalized AND queries on a d-regular graph achieves `(1-δ)` accuracy with `O(n log(k/δ))` queries, a significant improvement over the `O(n log n)` complexity required for exact recovery in prior work [21]. The theoretical findings are supported by a real crowdsourcing experiment.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of the paper is its novel connection between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding. This is a powerful conceptual reframing that brings new theoretical tools to a well-studied problem in the NIPS community. It allows for the derivation of non-trivial lower bounds on query complexity that go beyond standard entropy arguments.
2.  **Novel Query Type:** The introduction and analysis of the AND query is a significant contribution. The paper provides a compelling case, both theoretically (bypassing the limitations of linear codes for rate-distortion) and empirically (preliminary evidence suggests workers make fewer errors), for why this nonlinear query type can be more powerful than the standard 'same-cluster' (XOR) query. This insight is likely to be influential for future work in crowdsourcing and active learning.
3.  **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper presents a number of strong theoretical results. The `O(n log(k/δ))` query complexity for `(1-δ)`-approximate recovery in the noisy setting (Theorem 5) is a key result that demonstrates a clear advantage over existing methods aimed at exact recovery. The new lower bound for exact recovery (Theorem 2) and the rate-distortion-style lower bound for approximate recovery (Theorem 3) add to the theoretical completeness of the work.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. It begins with a thorough introduction and problem setup, then logically progresses through the three different settings (noiseless/exact, noiseless/approximate, noisy/approximate). This organization makes the contributions easy to follow and appreciate.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Technical Depth in Main Paper:** Several of the proofs in the main text are highly condensed or deferred to the appendix. For example, the proof of Theorem 1 relies on the "coding theoretic idea of expurgation" without sufficient explanation for a general audience, and the proof of the key lower bound in Theorem 3 is omitted entirely. While space constraints are understood, this makes it difficult to fully verify the technical soundness of some claims without recourse to the supplementary material.
2.  **Inconsistencies in Query Models:** The paper analyzes binary-output queries in Sections 3.1 and 3.2, but shifts to a k-ary output query for the main algorithmic result in Section 3.3. The motivation for this shift is not fully explained. While the k-ary query for `k=2` reduces to the binary AND query, it would strengthen the paper to discuss why this specific generalization was chosen and whether binary-output queries could achieve similar results in the `k>2` noisy setting.
3.  **Practicality of Algorithm 1:** The proposed algorithm for the noisy setting (Algorithm 1) requires knowledge of the prior probability vector `p` to set its thresholds. The paper suggests that `p` can be estimated, and Theorem 6 shows asymptotic correctness if relative cluster sizes are known. However, the analysis does not address the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to estimation errors in `p`, which is a critical practical concern, especially for finite `n`.
4.  **Limited Experimental Validation:** The crowdsourcing experiment provides a valuable proof of concept, but its scale is limited (200 items, 10 volunteers, one specific task). The d-regular graph was constructed in a specific manner (circular permutation), and it is unclear how robust the results are to other graph structures. A more comprehensive comparison against baseline methods (like spectral clustering on the noisy 'same-cluster' query results) on the real-world data, rather than just on synthetic data, would make the experimental validation more compelling.

In summary, this paper presents a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the field of semi-supervised learning and crowdsourcing. The information-theoretic perspective is novel and yields important theoretical insights and improved algorithms. The introduction of AND queries is a key takeaway that could have a lasting impact. The weaknesses identified are primarily areas for further refinement—such as expanding on technical details, justifying modeling choices, and conducting more extensive experiments—but they do not detract from the core value of the contributions. The work is technically sound and advances the state of the art in query-based clustering.
