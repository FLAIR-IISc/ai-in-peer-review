

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper investigates the problem of clustering elements using queries to an oracle, with a focus on minimizing the number of queries while achieving accurate recovery. The authors make a novel connection between clustering problems and locally encodable source coding from information theory. They introduce AND queries as a more efficient alternative to the commonly used "same cluster" (XOR) queries, providing theoretical guarantees for both noiseless and noisy settings. The work builds on recent NIPS papers on clustering with queries (e.g., [21, 22, 23]), but makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the limitations of linear queries and introducing a new class of queries that achieve better performance.

The paper makes several key theoretical contributions: (1) new lower bounds for locally encodable source coding, (2) a novel AND query scheme that outperforms "same cluster" queries for approximate recovery, and (3) an algorithm with theoretical guarantees for noisy queries that requires O(n log k/δ) queries. The authors also validate their theoretical results with a real-world crowdsourcing experiment on movie categorization.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Contributions and Novelty (Originality):** The paper's most significant contribution is the theoretical insight that "same cluster" queries (XOR) are fundamentally limited for approximate recovery, while AND queries provide substantially better performance. This is a novel perspective that hasn't been explored in prior work. The authors establish rigorous lower bounds and show that their AND query scheme achieves near-optimal performance, which is a substantial theoretical advance. The connection to locally encodable source coding is also novel and provides a fresh information-theoretic framework for analyzing clustering problems.

**Technical Quality and Rigor:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with well-constructed proofs for all main theorems. The theoretical analysis is thorough, with careful treatment of both noiseless and noisy settings. The authors provide both lower bounds and matching upper bounds for their schemes, which is a hallmark of strong theoretical work.

**Practical Validation:** The real-world crowdsourcing experiment provides valuable empirical validation of the theoretical results. The authors show that AND queries indeed produce lower error rates (105 errors vs. 234 for "same cluster" queries) in practice, which strongly supports their theoretical claims. This bridges the gap between theory and practice in a meaningful way.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Scope on General k:** While the paper addresses the two-cluster case (k=2) comprehensively, the general k-cluster case is only briefly covered in Section 3.3. The main theoretical contributions and the most compelling results (e.g., the AND query scheme) are primarily for k=2, which limits the broader applicability of the work. For a paper claiming to address clustering generally, this is a notable limitation.

**Practical Justification of AND Queries:** The paper states that "for a correct 'no' answer in an AND query, a worker just need to know one of the labels in the pair," but doesn't provide a clear explanation of why this makes the query easier for workers to answer. Without a more detailed explanation of the cognitive burden for workers, the practical advantage of AND queries remains somewhat speculative.

**Experimental Scale:** The real-world validation uses only 10 volunteers on a dataset of 200 movies, which is relatively small for a crowdsourcing study. While the results are consistent with theory, a larger-scale experiment would provide stronger empirical validation.

**Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper cites relevant prior work but doesn't directly compare with the most recent approaches to clustering with queries (e.g., [33, 30, 8, 20]). A more detailed comparison would strengthen the paper's claim that their approach is superior.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical paper with significant novelty in connecting clustering problems to locally encodable source coding. The authors establish a fundamental limitation of "same cluster" queries and propose a substantially better alternative with AND queries. The theoretical contributions are rigorous and the real-world validation provides important empirical support.

The paper's main limitation is the focus on k=2 in most of its theoretical contributions, though the general k case is addressed in Section 3.3. However, the core insights about the limitations of linear queries and the advantages of AND queries are likely to generalize to k>2, and the authors provide some discussion of this.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the theoretical understanding of clustering with queries, with clear practical implications for crowdsourcing applications. The work advances the state of the art in information-theoretic approaches to clustering and provides a new perspective that could influence future work in the area.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution with novel insights, rigorous analysis, and empirical validation. While there are some limitations in scope and experimental scale, these do not undermine the paper's core contributions. The work is of sufficient quality, originality, and significance to be of interest to the NIPS community, particularly to researchers working at the intersection of information theory, learning theory, and practical machine learning applications.

The paper's connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding is particularly compelling, and the AND query scheme represents a meaningful improvement over existing approaches. The authors have clearly demonstrated that their approach provides better theoretical guarantees and better performance in practice than the standard "same cluster" query method. This is a strong contribution to the field.
