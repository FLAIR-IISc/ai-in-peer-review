

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper proposes a novel approach to clustering by using AND queries instead of conventional "same cluster" (XOR) queries. The authors frame the problem as a locally encodable source coding problem and claim that AND queries can achieve better query complexity for approximate recovery than existing methods. The theoretical results include bounds for exact and approximate recovery in noiseless and noisy settings, with a real crowdsourcing experiment to validate the approach.

The paper's main strengths include a clear problem formulation and a well-organized presentation of theoretical results. The authors provide rigorous proofs for their theoretical claims and present a real-world experiment that somewhat supports their theoretical findings. The connection to information theory is well-motivated, and the experimental setup for the crowdsourcing validation is generally sound.

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses that undermine its contribution and credibility. First, the paper misrepresents the "locally encodable source coding" framework. The authors claim this concept is "not well-studied," but this conflates two fundamentally different concepts in information theory: locally encodable codes (where each encoded bit depends on few input bits) and locally decodable codes (where each decoded bit depends on few encoded bits). The authors cite works on locally decodable codes [19, 16, 26, 6] as relevant to their "locally encodable" framework, which is a serious conceptual error. This misapplication weakens the theoretical foundation of the paper.

Second, the claimed novelty of AND queries over XOR queries is overstated. The paper's main theoretical contribution is the claim that AND queries can achieve better performance than "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery. However, the justification for this claim relies on a misapplication of Massey's result [17] on linear codes as rate-distortion codes. The authors do not establish why their AND queries would overcome the limitations of linear codes in this context, and the theoretical results for AND queries are not convincingly shown to be superior to existing approaches.

Third, the experimental validation is limited and insufficient to support the paper's claims. The crowdsourcing experiment involves only 1000 queries with 10 volunteers, which is a very small sample size for statistical significance. The error rates for AND queries (105 errors out of 1000) versus "same cluster" queries (234 errors) do show a difference, but the paper fails to provide statistical tests to confirm this difference is meaningful. Moreover, the error rate for AND queries (10.5%) is still quite high, raising questions about practical utility.

The paper also makes several questionable assumptions. For instance, the authors assume that the prior distribution of labels (p) is known, which is often not the case in real-world clustering problems. While they claim this can be estimated by subsampling, they do not provide evidence that this estimation is robust in practice.

The theoretical results for the noisy query setting (Theorem 5) are interesting but not sufficiently novel. The performance guarantee for the AND query approach is O(n log(k/δ)) queries, which is similar to what can be achieved with standard approaches for approximate clustering. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate that this is a significant improvement over existing methods.

The most concerning issue is the paper's failure to adequately contextualize its contribution within the broader literature. The authors cite works on locally decodable codes as relevant to their "locally encodable" framework, but they do not acknowledge that these are fundamentally different concepts. They also do not sufficiently compare their approach to recent work on clustering with noisy queries [21, 22, 23], which has established more robust and practical approaches for crowdsourcing-based clustering.

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting idea, the misapplication of information theory concepts, limited experimental validation, and overstated claims of novelty make it difficult to recommend for acceptance at NIPS. The paper would benefit significantly from a more rigorous theoretical foundation, a more comprehensive comparison with existing approaches, and stronger experimental validation.

In summary, the paper's main weakness is its conceptual confusion between locally encodable and locally decodable source coding, which undermines its theoretical contribution. The experimental results are too preliminary to support the claimed advantages of AND queries. While the general idea of using different query types for clustering is interesting, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to justify the novelty or practical significance of its approach.

I recommend rejection. The paper would need to substantially revise its theoretical foundation, provide stronger experimental validation, and more carefully contextualize its contribution within the existing literature on clustering and information theory before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.
