## NIPS 2017 Review: Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and theoretically rich framework for analyzing and optimizing querying strategies in semisupervised clustering, particularly within the context of crowdsourcing. The connection to locally encodable source coding is insightful, and the introduction of AND-queries as a potentially superior alternative to standard "same cluster" queries is a significant contribution. While the mathematical exposition is dense, the results are substantial and well-supported. However, the extensive theoretical focus comes at the cost of limited empirical validation. I recommend **acceptance**, contingent on minor clarifications and a more thorough discussion of the practical implications of their findings.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The paper tackles the problem of learning cluster labels with a limited query budget, specifically focusing on scenarios where querying involves obtaining answers from an oracle (e.g., crowd workers). It frames this problem as a locally encodable source coding task – a less-explored area within information theory.  The core idea is to minimize the number of queries needed to reconstruct the labeling, given that each query can only access a small number of labels.  The authors demonstrate that traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries are not optimal and propose using "AND" queries, especially for approximate recovery, showing information-theoretic lower bounds and non-adaptive schemes that approach optimality. They also address the issue of noisy oracle responses and provide an algorithm with theoretical guarantees.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper positions itself well within the growing body of work on crowdsourced clustering and entity resolution (e.g., [33, 30, 8, 20]). It builds upon recent studies exploring query complexity for clustering with objectives like k-means [4] and optimal crowdsourcing schemes [12, 13, 28, 34, 15]. The connection to source coding and the invocation of locally encodable and decodable codes [19, 16, 23, 26] are appropriate and clearly explained.  The authors explicitly acknowledge and contrast their work with previous approaches, highlighting the novelty of their framing and the introduced AND-query strategy.  The references are comprehensive and relevant.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Framing:**  The connection to locally encodable source coding provides a powerful theoretical lens for analyzing the problem and deriving bounds.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is heavily grounded in information theory, with well-defined theorems, proofs, and lower bounds. The analysis is meticulous and convincing.
*   **Important Insight: AND-Queries:**  The demonstration that AND-queries can outperform XOR-queries, especially in approximate recovery, is a significant finding with potentially practical consequences.
*   **Handling Noisy Oracles:** The treatment of noisy query answers is a realistic and valuable addition, making the results more applicable to real-world crowdsourcing scenarios.
*   **Validation:** The limited experiment on real crowdsourced data, while modest, provides some support for the theoretical findings, showing a lower error rate with AND queries.
*   **Clear Exposition:** Despite the mathematical complexity, the paper is generally well-organized and the key ideas are clearly presented.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is relatively thin.  While the initial results are encouraging, a more extensive empirical study, possibly with a wider range of datasets and query parameters, would strengthen the claims.  The comparison to other clustering algorithms beyond the basic spectral clustering setup would also be valuable.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The paper focuses heavily on theoretical analysis. A deeper discussion of the practical challenges of implementing these algorithms in a real-world crowdsourcing system (e.g., query design, worker motivation, cost optimization) would be beneficial.  Specifically, the assumption of a perfectly d-regular graph seems potentially problematic and deserves further discussion.
*   **Mathematical Density:** The mathematical notation and derivations are dense and may be inaccessible to some readers.  Adding more intuitive explanations or examples could improve accessibility.
*   **Repetition of Query Limitation:** While the justification for not allowing the repetition of the *same* query is reasonable, it’s worth acknowledging the potential trade-offs in practice. Briefly discussing scenarios where repetition *might* be beneficial and how the analysis would change could add nuance.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in understanding the query complexity of semisupervised clustering.
*   The introduction of AND-queries is a novel and promising approach.
*   The treatment of noisy oracle responses adds to the practical relevance of the work.
*   The paper is well-written and thoroughly researched.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The limited empirical evaluation leaves some questions about the practical impact of the findings.
*   The theoretical focus may be too abstract for some readers interested in applied crowdsourcing.



**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the practical challenges of constructing a perfectly d-regular graph in a real crowdsourcing setting?
*   Could you discuss potential strategies for adapting the AND-query approach to handle scenarios where the oracle response is not binary (e.g., a Likert scale)?
*   How sensitive are your theoretical results to deviations from the assumption of independent query errors?
*   What are the computational costs associated with the proposed algorithms, particularly in terms of decoding complexity?
*   Can you expand on the implications of the locally encodable source coding framing for other related problems in machine learning or data mining?

**Overall, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further enhance its impact and solidify its place as a valuable addition to the NIPS literature.**
