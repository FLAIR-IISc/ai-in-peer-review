Summary
This paper studies nonadaptive, query-based clustering/label recovery under a probabilistic prior on labels and possibly noisy answers. The key conceptual move is to cast the problem as “locally encodable source coding,” where each query/encoded bit depends on a constant number Δ of labels. The paper provides:

- Information-theoretic lower bounds on queries needed for exact and approximate recovery under locality constraints.
- An achievability result for exact recovery (k=2) using parity/XOR-type queries and ML decoding with m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries, α depending on p and Δ.
- A claim that “same-cluster” (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery, leveraging limitations of linear codes (Massey/Ancheta), and proposing pairwise AND queries instead.
- For noisy answers and k≥2, a simple nonadaptive scheme using pairwise AND-like queries on a d-regular graph plus a thresholding decoder achieving (1−δ)-accuracy with m = O(n log(k/δ)) queries. 
- A small crowdsourcing experiment (200 movies, 10 participants, 1000 queries) showing lower raw error rates for AND-type questions than same-cluster questions.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to source coding, locally decodable/“smooth” codes, sparse-graph codes, group testing, and query-based clustering/entity resolution. Prior NIPS work on query complexity for clustering and crowdsourcing is cited. The “locally encodable” coding viewpoint is less explored than local decoding, and the use of AND queries relates to classical group testing (which uses OR queries) and coding-theoretic designs.

Strengths
- Interesting information-theoretic perspective (local encodability) with nontrivial lower bounds under locality constraints.
- Clear separation between exact vs approximate recovery and noiseless vs noisy settings; the O(n log(1/δ)) scaling for approximate recovery under noise is appealing.
- Simple, practical reconstruction rule (thresholding) on sparse graphs; analysis via Chernoff bounds and dependency graphs is standard and credible.
- The idea that certain query functions (AND) can be easier for crowd workers is plausible, and the small study is suggestive.

Weaknesses
- Computational tractability is largely ignored for the exact recovery result: achievability relies on ML over random parity-like queries; there is no efficient decoder or explicit construction. The result is primarily existential and thus of limited algorithmic value.
- The argument that “same-cluster queries (XOR)” are suboptimal relies in part on a conjectural lower bound (Massey’s conjecture), proven only at p=0.5. Claims that AND “beats the same-cluster lower bound” are tenuous if the bound is unproven. Moreover, a same-cluster query equals XNOR, not XOR; this mismatch should be clarified.
- Limited practical validation: the experiment is extremely small (10 volunteers, 1000 questions), on an artificial two-class movie split, without MTurk-scale variability or worker modeling. No comparison to established crowdsourcing baselines (e.g., Dawid–Skene, KOS) under a realistic repetition/aggregation setting. The “no repetition” assumption is atypical in practice and removes a key design lever used by many systems.
- The noisy model assumes independent BSC noise per query with parameter q and known priors/cluster sizes; robustness to model misspecification (unknown q, adversarial or worker-dependent noise) is not studied.
- Relation to group testing and sparse-graph coding is underdeveloped. The AND query is essentially the complement of OR; classical nonadaptive noisy group testing achieves similar O(n log(1/δ))-type behavior with explicit designs and decoders. A careful comparison (tight constants, regimes, ties to τ = prevalence) is missing.
- Presentation issues: numerous typos/formatting glitches (e.g., footnotes in text, inconsistent notation like “qkk−1”), figures and proofs deferred to the supplement, and uneven exposition make it hard to verify details. Some bounds are given in complicated forms without intuition or tightness discussion; the regimes where lower bounds beat nh(p) are narrow.

Quality
The theoretical components are mostly sound in technique (typicality/expurgation, Chernoff, dependency graphs), but the exact recovery achievability is nonconstructive, and some central claims hinge on a conjecture. The noisy approximate result’s constants and threshold selection are obscured by notation/typos, and comparisons to established theory (group testing) are insufficient. The empirical evidence is too limited to substantiate practical claims.

Clarity
The paper needs careful editing. Clarify whether “same-cluster” equals XOR or XNOR; fix notation (e.g., thresholds Ci), explain dependence of constants on q,p,k; provide intuition for lower bounds and their tightness; and include key proofs in the main text or give sharper summaries.

Originality
The local-encodability framing and focus on AND queries for crowdsourced clustering appear novel within this community, but several components overlap with known areas (group testing, sparse-graph codes). The paper would benefit from positioning against that literature more explicitly.

Significance
Potentially meaningful if it led to practical nonadaptive schemes outperforming standard pairwise queries under realistic noise/worker models. As written, the practical impact is unclear, and the main exact recovery result lacks an efficient algorithm.

Questions for authors
- Please clarify the “same-cluster” function: is it XOR or XNOR? Several arguments hinge on linearity.
- Can you provide an efficient decoder or explicit query design for exact recovery, or bounds ruling them out?
- How does your noisy approximate algorithm relate to classical noisy nonadaptive group testing (OR queries)? Are the guarantees strictly stronger in some regimes?
- How sensitive is Algorithm 1 to unknown q and mis-specified priors? Can you estimate thresholds adaptively?
- Can you present larger-scale experiments with diverse workers and comparisons to Dawid–Skene/KOS and to majority-vote baselines?

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting theoretical angle; nontrivial lower bounds under locality.
- Simple, analyzable algorithm with favorable asymptotics for approximate recovery.
- Insightful point that query design affects human error.

Cons for acceptance
- Nonconstructive exact recovery; reliance on conjecture for “same-cluster” inferiority.
- Weak empirical validation and unrealistic “no repetition” restriction.
- Missing comparisons to group testing/crowdsourcing baselines and lack of robustness analysis.
- Clarity and presentation issues.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper contains promising ideas, but it needs stronger theoretical positioning (especially re: group testing), an efficient exact-recovery approach or clearer limits, more robust/noise-agnostic analysis, and substantially more rigorous empirical validation before it can meet NIPS standards. Confidence: high.
