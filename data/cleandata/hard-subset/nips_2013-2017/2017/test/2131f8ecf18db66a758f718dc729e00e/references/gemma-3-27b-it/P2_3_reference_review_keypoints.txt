- Problem: Clustering/labeling using limited, possibly noisy, pairwise queries to an oracle (crowd), analyzed as a locally encodable source coding problem.  
- Contributions: Information-theoretic lower bounds for exact and approximate label recovery; demonstration that AND queries can outperform XOR (“same cluster”) queries, especially in noise.  
- Algorithm & validation: Practical AND-query-based algorithm presented and validated with theoretical analysis and a small crowdsourcing experiment.  
- Novelty: Framing via locally encodable source coding is a clever, novel bridge between information theory and crowdsourcing/clustering.  
- Theoretical rigor: Solid, rigorous derivations and lower bounds with reasonably clear proof techniques.  
- AND query insight: Significant, counterintuitive finding that AND queries yield practical and theoretical advantages for approximate/noisy recovery.  
- Empirical support: Crowdsourcing experiment provides proof-of-concept evidence and an interesting observation that majority voting can increase errors.  
- Scope of analysis: Paper treats both noiseless and noisy query settings and outlines extensions beyond k=2 clusters.  
- Motivation concern: The rationale for focusing on non-adaptive querying (vs. adaptive) is underdeveloped and the trade-offs need clearer justification.  
- Proof accessibility: Some proofs—especially for noisy queries—are dense and hard to follow; more intuition and intermediate steps are needed.  
- Experimental limitations: Empirical validation is limited in scale and parameter variation, weakening practical evidence.  
- Related work: Connections to active learning and query-selection literature are not fully articulated, making the paper’s relation to prior work unclear.  
- Practical issues: Real-world considerations such as query cost and worker fatigue for AND queries are not addressed.  
- Quality rating: Technically sound and complete; strengths outweigh weaknesses—overall strong.  
- Clarity rating: Generally clear writing but dense proofs hurt accessibility—good.  
- Originality rating: Novel framing and AND-query result—strong.  
- Significance rating: Potentially important practical and theoretical implications—good.  
- Pro-acceptance arguments: Novel, theoretically grounded approach; impactful AND-query insight; rigorous fundamental limits.  
- Con-acceptance arguments: Complex, potentially inaccessible proofs; limited experiments; insufficient discussion of related active-learning work.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong submission recommended for acceptance, with suggestions to clarify proofs and expand experimental validation.
