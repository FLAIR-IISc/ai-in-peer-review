- Overall: Strong, theoretically rich paper linking semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding; recommend acceptance with minor clarifications and more practical discussion.
- Main idea: Minimize queries to recover cluster labels under limited-query budget by framing the problem as locally encodable source coding.
- Core contribution: Show XOR ("same cluster") queries are suboptimal and propose AND-queries as a superior alternative for approximate recovery.
- Theoretical results: Provide information-theoretic lower bounds and non-adaptive schemes that approach optimality.
- Noise handling: Analyze noisy oracle responses and give an algorithm with theoretical guarantees.
- Empirical validation: Limited real-crowd experiment shows AND-queries can yield lower error but overall empirical evaluation is thin.
- Relation to prior work: Well-positioned within crowdsourced clustering, entity resolution, and locally encodable/decodable codes literature with comprehensive citations.
- Novel framing strength: The connection to locally encodable source coding offers a powerful new theoretical lens.
- Theoretical rigor strength: Detailed theorems, proofs, and lower bounds underpin the claims.
- AND-query insight strength: Demonstrates a significant, potentially practical advantage over XOR queries.
- Practical realism strength: Inclusion of noisy oracle modeling increases practical relevance.
- Presentation strength: Generally clear and well-organized despite complex math.
- Limited empirical evaluation weakness: Needs broader experiments across datasets and comparisons to more clustering baselines.
- Practical implementation weakness: Lacks discussion of real-world issues like constructing d-regular graphs, worker behavior, and cost trade-offs.
- Accessibility weakness: Dense mathematical notation and derivations may impede non-theory readers; more intuition/examples needed.
- Query repetition caveat: Paper forbids repeating identical queries but should acknowledge scenarios where repetition could help and how analysis would change.
- For acceptance: Significant theoretical advance, novel AND-query proposal, and treatment of noisy responses make it a valuable contribution.
- Against acceptance: Overly theoretical focus and limited empirical evidence raise questions about practical impact for applied crowdsourcing audiences.
- Authors questions: Explain practical challenges of d-regular graphs, adapt AND-queries to non-binary oracle responses, sensitivity to non-independent errors, decoding computational costs, and broader implications for ML/data mining.
