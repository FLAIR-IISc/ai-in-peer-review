This paper investigates semi-supervised clustering via a novel information-theoretic lens, framing the problem as a locally encodable source coding problem and introducing AND-queries as a potentially more efficient alternative to traditional ‘same cluster’ queries. While the central idea of connecting querying complexity to source coding is intriguing, the paper suffers from a significant number of shortcomings, ranging from stylistic issues and unsubstantiated claims to a lack of rigorous experimental validation and inconsistencies in the presentation of results.

**Summary & Relation to Prior Work:** The paper builds on recent work in crowdsourced clustering (e.g., [4, 33, 30]), aiming to minimize query complexity. The connection to locally encodable source coding, though conceptually interesting, isn't deeply explored; the authors merely state its existence and leverage it to derive bounds. The comparison to XOR queries and the introduction of AND queries represent the core contribution. Prior work on locally decodable codes [19, 16, 26] is acknowledged, but the novelty of applying this framework to *encodable* codes isn't clearly established.

**Strengths:** The core idea of viewing the querying problem through the lens of locally encodable source coding is potentially valuable. The theoretical exploration of AND-queries, particularly the claim of improved performance over XOR queries for approximate recovery, is the most promising aspect. The attempt to validate the approach with a real crowdsourcing experiment is also commendable, though ultimately flawed (discussed below).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Rigor & Clarity:** The mathematical arguments, particularly surrounding Theorems 1-6, are often presented without sufficient detail, making it difficult to fully assess their validity. For example, the jump from “bad event” analysis in Theorem 1 to the conclusion about perfect recovery lacks crucial steps. The explanation of how the d-regular graph construction in Section 3.3 contributes to the theoretical guarantees is also unclear.  The notation is inconsistent and sometimes undefined (e.g., "T" in Theorem 1). The definition of a "(1-δ)-good" algorithm is unclear as it refers to a distortion function and percentage of correct labels simultaneously.
* **Overstated Claims:** The claim of providing “new information-theoretic lower bounds” requires stronger justification. The authors reference Massey [17] but fail to articulate *how* their results surpass existing bounds in a meaningful way. The lower bound presented in Theorem 2, while potentially tighter for specific 'p' values, isn’t substantially better for typical scenarios.
* **Experimental Validation:** The crowdsourcing experiment is weak. The description of the survey setup lacks sufficient detail.  The claim that “the error rate of AND queries is lower” needs a statistical significance test and proper error bar representation. The authors acknowledge prior work showing majority voting *increasing* errors, yet proceed with a similar evaluation of AND queries without a comparable warning about potential biases or confounding factors. The assertion that the experimental results “validate” the theoretical findings is a significant overstatement given the experiment’s limitations.  Figure 5 lacks sufficient detail (axis labels, error bars).
* **Stylistic Issues and Redundancy:** The introduction is overly verbose and contains repetitive phrasing. The paper suffers from numerous grammatical errors and awkward sentence constructions. The same concepts are reiterated multiple times throughout the paper, creating a sense of redundancy.
* **Missing Related Work:** While the authors cite relevant papers, they miss crucial work on active learning and query selection strategies for clustering, which are highly relevant to the problem.  A more comprehensive literature review is needed.
* **Assumptions and Limitations:** The assumption of independent query errors is acknowledged, but its implications aren’t fully explored. The handling of k > 2 is also superficial; the algorithm primarily focuses on the k=2 case.
* **Inconsistent Results:** The statement that repetition of a query is theoretically uninteresting while simultaneously using majority voting in the experiment is contradictory.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**Against:**
* Lack of technical rigor and clarity in the theoretical derivations.
* Overstated claims about novelty and significance.
* Weak and poorly described experimental validation.
* Numerous stylistic issues and redundancies.
* Incomplete related work.

**For:**
* The core idea of framing the problem as locally encodable source coding is potentially insightful.
* The exploration of AND queries as an alternative to XOR queries is a worthwhile investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**  Despite the intriguing central idea, this paper falls short of NIPS standards. The technical weaknesses, overstated claims, and insufficient experimental validation outweigh the potential novelty of the approach. The paper feels incomplete and requires substantial revision to address the numerous flaws identified. I recommend **rejection**. A major revision addressing the points above would be necessary for reconsideration, and even then, a significantly more rigorous and well-validated presentation would be required.
