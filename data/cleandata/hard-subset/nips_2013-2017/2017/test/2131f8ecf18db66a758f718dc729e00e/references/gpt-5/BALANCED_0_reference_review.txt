Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonadaptive, budgeted crowdsourced clustering/label recovery under a prior p over k labels, casting query design as a “locally encodable source coding” problem: each query output Yj is a deterministic function of at most Δ labels. This perspective yields information-theoretic lower bounds and achievability schemes.

Noiseless exact recovery (k=2): With Δ-wise XOR (parity) queries (Δ=2 corresponds to “same-cluster”), the authors show an achievability bound using a random Δ-regular query matrix and ML decoding: m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α), α=0.5(1+(1−4p(1−p))^Δ), strictly below n for a range of p even with pairwise queries. A lower bound (via average per-item query degree and a packing argument over typical sequences) improves on n h(p) in some regimes.

Noiseless approximate recovery (k=2): They provide a lower bound for (1−δ)-good schemes with Δ-local encoding (proof deferred), and argue that XOR (“same cluster”) is fundamentally limited for approximate recovery using the classical Ancheta–Massey result on linear rate–distortion codes (precisely at p=0.5; they conjecture an extension to all p). They propose AND queries on pairs and a simple local decoder (label i as 1 if any neighboring AND returns 1), with a closed-form bound showing that AND queries achieve smaller error for a given m than what is known for XOR queries.

Noisy approximate recovery (general k): The main algorithmic contribution uses pairwise k-ary “AND-like” queries Q(X,X′)=i if X=X′=i, else 0, on a d-regular graph. A thresholding decoder at each node uses counts of neighbors returning label i. With an i.i.d. symmetric noise model over k outputs (correct with prob 1−q, otherwise uniform), they show (via Chernoff bounds and dependency graphs) that m=nd^2=O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive queries suffice for (1−δ)-accurate labeling. They also show how to replace priors by known cluster sizes.

Experiments: A small crowdsourcing study on 200 movies compares pairwise AND (“Are both action?”) vs same-cluster queries: AND exhibits fewer erroneous responses (105 vs 234 out of 1000). The algorithm’s error vs d tracks the theory; a synthetic comparison with spectral clustering for same-cluster queries is provided.

Strengths
- New perspective: Framing nonadaptive query design as locally encodable source coding is original and connects crowdsourced clustering to information theory beyond the more mature “local decodability” literature.
- Theory across regimes: The paper develops lower bounds (exact and approximate recovery) and matching/near-matching achievability in several settings; the AND vs XOR contrast is insightful and nonobvious.
- Simple, scalable algorithm: The noisy k-ary AND scheme with linear-time per-node thresholding and m=O(n log(k/δ)) is clean and practically attractive for approximate recovery.
- Empirical hint that AND queries are easier for workers than same-cluster queries, aligning with the modeling intuition (one affirmative cue suffices for a correct “no” in AND).

Weaknesses
- Practicality of k-ary queries: The k-ary AND query demands a single k-ary response per pair (“which class do both share, if any?”). For human workers, this can be harder than binary questions and may not keep per-query costs uniform across k; the paper does not discuss this trade-off or how to elicit such answers reliably. If decomposed into k binary queries, the query budget would inflate by k.
- Achievability via ML for XOR: The exact-recovery achievability (Theorem 1) uses ML decoding over a dense random query matrix; complexity is exponential. It clarifies information-theoretic limits but is not algorithmic. A discussion of efficient decoding (e.g., via sparse graphs/LPN-style decoders) or limitations would improve the contribution.
- Lower bound coverage and tightness: The improved necessary bound (Theorem 2) beats n h(p) only in a narrow regime (e.g., small p for Δ=2). The key approximate-recovery lower bound (Theorem 3) is deferred; without seeing it, it is hard to assess novelty, assumptions, and tightness. The argument that XOR is suboptimal for approximate recovery relies partly on a conjecture beyond p=0.5.
- Experimental validation is limited: The real-user study is small (10 participants, 1000 queries), includes potential confounds (task difficulty/order effects), and compares against a single spectral clustering baseline for XOR. No ablations (e.g., robustness to class imbalance, varying q, adaptive vs nonadaptive) or comparisons to recent query-efficient clustering methods with noisy oracles.
- Presentation issues: Several typos and formatting glitches; figures referenced but not visible; some proofs are sketchy; a few references are incomplete/duplicated. The practicality of estimating or knowing p and q in deployment is only briefly discussed.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The conceptual framing and several proofs appear sound, but key pieces are deferred. The noisy approximate recovery result is clean and well-supported. Some claims (XOR inferiority for all p) are conjectural.
- Clarity: Generally understandable but needs polishing; better exposition of the noisy k-ary query interface, and clearer comparisons to prior query-complexity results would help.
- Originality: High. The local encoding viewpoint and the AND-query design for approximate recovery seem novel within the ML/crowdsourcing literature.
- Significance: Potentially significant for nonadaptive, budgeted crowd labeling, and for information-theoretic understanding of local-encoding limits. The practical impact depends on feasibility of k-ary queries and stronger empirical validation.

Arguments for acceptance
- Introduces a fresh theoretical lens (locally encodable source coding) with nontrivial bounds.
- Demonstrates a simple, practical algorithm achieving O(n log(k/δ)) with noise and offers a compelling AND-vs-XOR insight.
- Bridges information theory and crowdsourcing/ER, likely to inspire follow-up work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practicality gap for k-ary queries; limited, small-scale empirical support and baseline coverage.
- Some theory relies on conjectures; some bounds are not tight or lack in-paper proofs.
- Exact-recovery achievability uses intractable decoding and may overstate algorithmic progress.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the k-ary query interface: how to pose it to workers; discuss cognitive load, error rates vs k; or propose a decomposition that preserves overall query complexity.
- Include the full proof of Theorem 3 (or at least a detailed sketch) and position it relative to smooth compression and local decodability results (e.g., Montanari–Mossel, Makhdoumi et al., Mazumdar et al.).
- Either avoid relying on the XOR lower bound conjecture or carefully delimit claims to p=0.5 where the result is known.
- Provide an efficient decoding alternative or negative complexity result for the XOR-based exact recovery to separate “info-theoretic” from “algorithmic.”
- Strengthen experiments: larger crowd study, balanced/randomized task assignment, more baselines (e.g., majority vote aggregation, state-of-the-art noisy-query clustering), varying k and class imbalance, and report per-query time/cost.
- Discuss estimating p and q in practice and robustness when priors are misspecified.
- Clean up writing, figures, and references; provide finite-sample constants for Theorem 5.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper contains interesting and original theoretical ideas, and the AND-query algorithm for noisy approximate recovery is elegant. However, the practicality of the k-ary query model, reliance on conjectural bounds for XOR, limited empirical validation, and presentation gaps make it borderline for NeurIPS. With stronger empirical evidence, clearer proofs, and a more convincing discussion of practical query design, this work could be a solid contribution.
