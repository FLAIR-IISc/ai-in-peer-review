**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of semi-supervised clustering, where the goal is to recover a ground-truth labeling of `n` items into `k` clusters by making queries to an oracle. The authors focus on the non-adaptive setting, where all queries are decided in advance. The primary contributions are twofold. First, the problem is framed from an information-theoretic perspective as "locally encodable source coding," where each query (an encoded bit) can only depend on a small, constant number of labels (source bits). Under this framework, the authors derive new upper and lower bounds on the number of queries for exact and approximate recovery in the noiseless, two-cluster case. Second, and more centrally, the paper argues that the standard "same-cluster" query (equivalent to XOR for binary labels) is suboptimal for approximate recovery, especially in the presence of noise. It proposes a novel "AND-query" and presents a simple algorithm that, under a noisy oracle model, can recover a `(1-δ)` fraction of labels correctly with `O(n log(k/δ))` queries. The theoretical claims are supported by a small-scale, real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Theoretical Framing:** The connection between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding is novel and provides a principled, information-theoretic lens through which to analyze the problem. This fresh perspective allows for the derivation of new fundamental limits (e.g., Theorems 2 and 3), which is a solid theoretical contribution.
2.  **Analysis of the Noisy Setting:** The main algorithmic result (Theorem 5 and Algorithm 1) for approximate recovery with noisy queries is well-developed. The analysis is clear and demonstrates that the proposed AND-query scheme can achieve good performance with a query complexity that scales favorably with the number of items (`n`) and the desired accuracy (`δ`).
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The problem setup, theoretical viewpoint, and main results are presented in a logical sequence.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Justification for a New Query Type:** The paper's central claim is that the proposed AND-query is superior to the well-established "same-cluster" (XOR) query. This is an unconventional and strong claim that requires exceptionally strong evidence. However, the justification provided is not compelling enough. While the authors' specific recovery algorithm (Algorithm 1) leverages an asymmetry in expectation that exists for AND-queries but not XOR-queries, this is a property of the *algorithm*, not necessarily the *query type*. Established methods for clustering with "same-cluster" queries, such as spectral clustering, are robust to noise and do not rely on this asymmetry. The paper dismisses XOR queries too hastily without a rigorous theoretical or empirical comparison against these state-of-the-art baselines.

2.  **Limited and Unconvincing Experimental Validation:** The empirical evidence is preliminary and falls short of what is needed to support the paper's claims.
    *   The primary experiment involves a single dataset (200 movies) with a binary classification task and 10 volunteers. This is a very small scale to draw general conclusions from.
    *   The main result in Figure 5 only compares Algorithm 1's performance against its own theoretical prediction under varying parameters. It does not include a comparison against a baseline method using standard "same-cluster" queries on the same real-world data. Without this direct comparison, it is impossible to assess whether the proposed method offers any practical advantage.
    *   The comparison against spectral clustering is relegated to synthetic data (Figure 6). A key contribution of the paper is its performance in a real-world noisy setting, yet the primary baseline is not evaluated in that setting.
    *   The claim that workers make fewer errors with AND-queries (105 errors vs. 234 for XOR) is an interesting observation, but it is based on a single, small experiment. Such a claim about human-computer interaction requires much more rigorous user studies across different tasks and interfaces to be considered a reliable finding. As it stands, it is anecdotal.

3.  **Methodological Concerns:** The choice of baseline for comparison is questionable. While spectral clustering is a reasonable choice, the paper should also consider other robust methods for noisy correlation clustering. Furthermore, the recovery algorithm for AND-queries is a simple thresholding scheme. It is unclear if this is the optimal decoder for the information provided by these queries. A more complete analysis would compare against more sophisticated decoding strategies and demonstrate that the performance gain is due to the query type itself, not an artifact of comparing a specialized algorithm for one query type against a general-purpose algorithm for another.

**Overall Assessment**

The paper introduces an interesting theoretical perspective on query-based clustering and presents a novel query type with a corresponding recovery algorithm. The theoretical analysis is sound and the ideas are original. However, the work is significantly hampered by its weak empirical validation. The core argument—that a new, unconventional query type should be preferred over the standard in the field—is not substantiated by the limited experiments and weak comparison to established baselines. The paper presents a promising idea but feels incomplete, more like a position paper with a proof-of-concept than a finished piece of work with thoroughly vetted results. Before endorsing such a fundamental departure from existing practice, the community would need to see much more extensive evidence of its superiority across multiple datasets and against a wider range of state-of-the-art methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel and interesting information-theoretic connection to locally encodable source coding.
*   Provides new theoretical bounds and a simple, efficient algorithm for a noisy, approximate recovery setting.
*   The idea of designing queries to be asymmetric for easier decoding is clever.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The central claim of the superiority of AND-queries over established same-cluster queries is not convincingly supported.
*   The experimental validation is too limited in scale and scope to be conclusive.
*   The comparison to established baselines is weak and not performed on the main real-world dataset, which undermines the paper's practical claims.
*   Claims about human performance are anecdotal and not backed by rigorous user studies.

**Decision:**
While the theoretical ideas are intriguing, the lack of rigorous empirical validation and a thorough comparison against established methods is a major flaw. The evidence presented is not sufficient to justify the paper's strong claims. Therefore, I lean towards rejection.
