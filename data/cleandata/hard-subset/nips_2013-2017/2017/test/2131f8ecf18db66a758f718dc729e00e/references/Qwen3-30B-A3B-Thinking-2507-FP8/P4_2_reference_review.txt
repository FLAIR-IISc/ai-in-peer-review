

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper introduces a novel information-theoretic framework for clustering via query-based approaches, viewing the problem through the lens of locally encodable source coding. The authors propose a new query type called "AND queries" that outperforms traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery of cluster labels. The paper establishes theoretical guarantees for both noiseless and noisy settings, with a focus on minimizing the number of nonadaptive queries required to recover cluster labels with high probability.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it introduces the concept of locally encodable source coding in the context of clustering, providing new information-theoretic lower and upper bounds. Second, it demonstrates that AND queries substantially outperform XOR queries for approximate recovery, with a theoretical justification based on Massey's result regarding linear codes. Third, it presents a novel algorithm for noisy query settings that achieves (1-δ)-good recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries. Finally, it validates the theoretical results with a real crowdsourcing experiment on movie classification.

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide clear proofs for their main theorems, establishing both lower and upper bounds for various settings. The theoretical results are well-supported by experiments on real crowdsourced data, which show that AND queries yield lower error rates than traditional same-cluster queries. The paper properly acknowledges limitations, such as the focus on binary labels (k=2) with extensions to larger k mentioned but not deeply explored.

The writing is clear and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem definition to theoretical results to experimental validation. The connection between clustering and information theory is well-explained, and the paper adequately relates to prior work in both information theory (e.g., locally decodable codes) and clustering (e.g., crowdsourcing approaches in NIPS 2017 and earlier).

The main strengths of the paper are its novel theoretical framework, the introduction of AND queries as a superior alternative to XOR queries, and the strong experimental validation. The paper's originality is demonstrated through its unique perspective on clustering as a source coding problem and the development of AND queries. This work clearly differs from previous NIPS contributions on clustering with queries, such as [4, 8, 20, 21, 22, 30, 33], which primarily focus on XOR queries or different query models.

The significance of the work is substantial for both theoretical and practical reasons. The problem of minimizing query costs in crowdsourced clustering is important for real-world applications, and the paper provides fundamental limits and practical algorithms that can reduce query costs significantly. The AND query approach is particularly valuable as it requires fewer queries than existing methods while maintaining accuracy, with experimental results validating the theoretical claims.

Potential weaknesses include the focus on k=2 (binary labels) with only brief mentions of extensions to larger k, and some technical details being deferred to the supplementary material. While the authors acknowledge the practical importance of their work, the paper could better elaborate on how their approach would scale to very large datasets or integrate with existing crowdsourcing platforms.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS scope, addressing a fundamental problem in machine learning with a novel theoretical perspective that has practical applications. The theoretical contributions are significant and advance the state of the art in understanding the fundamental limits of query-based clustering.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel theoretical framework connecting clustering to locally encodable source coding
- Introduction of AND queries as a superior alternative to standard XOR queries
- Rigorous theoretical guarantees for both exact and approximate recovery
- Strong experimental validation with real crowdsourcing data
- Clear presentation of theoretical results and practical implications

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited exploration of the k>2 case beyond brief mentions
- Some technical proofs deferred to supplementary material
- Limited discussion of how the approach would integrate with existing crowdsourcing systems

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with strong theoretical contributions and practical validation. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field of clustering with queries, particularly through the introduction of AND queries and the locally encodable source coding framework. The theoretical results are well-supported by experiments, and the work advances the state of the art in understanding the fundamental limits of query-based clustering. The paper meets all NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
