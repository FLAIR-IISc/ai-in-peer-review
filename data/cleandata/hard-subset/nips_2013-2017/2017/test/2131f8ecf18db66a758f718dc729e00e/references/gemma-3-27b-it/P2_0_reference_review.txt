## Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the problem of clustering with limited, costly pairwise queries to an oracle (e.g., crowd workers). It frames this as a locally encodable source coding problem and derives information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery of cluster labels. A key contribution is the proposal of using AND queries instead of traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries, particularly for approximate recovery, demonstrating improved performance both theoretically and empirically. The work also addresses the scenario of noisy oracle responses and proposes a scheme leveraging AND queries that achieves good performance even with imperfect answers.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Framing:**  The connection to locally encodable source coding provides a fresh perspective on the querying problem, opening the door to leveraging tools from information theory. This is a significant step beyond typical algorithmic approaches to crowdsourced clustering.
* **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper presents rigorous information-theoretic lower bounds, which are a valuable contribution to understanding the fundamental limits of the problem.  The lower bound for approximate recovery is particularly noteworthy.
* **AND Query Insight:**  The demonstration that AND queries can outperform XOR queries in certain scenarios is a practical and significant finding. This challenges the intuitive choice of XOR and offers a concrete improvement.
* **Handling Noisy Oracles:**  Addressing the realistic scenario of noisy oracle responses and proposing a scheme resilient to errors is crucial for practical applications.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments with Amazon Mechanical Turk provide support for the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the AND query approach.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, with a clear explanation of the problem setup and contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are valuable, they are relatively small-scale. The results could be strengthened by evaluating the algorithm on larger datasets and with more diverse query scenarios.  The connection to prior work in crowdsourcing, especially regarding the experimental setup and baselines, could also be strengthened.
* **Complexity of Theoretical Analysis:** Some of the theoretical derivations (especially those concerning noisy queries and approximate recovery) are quite involved and might be difficult for a general NIPS audience to fully grasp without significant background in information theory. While the core ideas are presented, the details could benefit from simplification or supplementary material.
* **Repetition of Motivation:** The introductory section reiterates the motivation for non-adaptive queries and minimizing query cost multiple times, making it somewhat redundant.
* **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:**  The comparison of AND queries to ‘same cluster’ queries is well-done, but a comparison to more sophisticated algorithms specifically designed for noisy clustering, even if they aren't crowdsourced, would strengthen the empirical validation.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical analysis and convincing empirical results. Claims are well-supported, and the authors are careful in their analysis. The work appears complete, though expanding the experimental evaluation would further bolster the conclusions.
* **Clarity:** The paper is mostly clear, but as noted, the theoretical sections could benefit from simplification. The notation is generally consistent.
* **Originality:** The framing of the problem as locally encodable source coding and the exploration of AND queries represent a novel combination of ideas. The work adequately references related research, but a more thorough comparison to the crowdsourcing literature would be beneficial.
* **Significance:** The results have the potential to be significant for the field of crowdsourced clustering and entity resolution. The insight that AND queries can be superior to XOR queries is a practical contribution.  The theoretical lower bounds provide a valuable benchmark for algorithm design.



**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel framing and insightful theoretical contributions.
* Practical improvement in query strategy (AND queries).
* Good empirical validation of the proposed algorithm.
* Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in the context of modern data analysis.

**Con:**

* Limited scope of the experiments.
* Theoretical analysis can be challenging to follow.
* Some redundancy in the introduction.
* Could benefit from more extensive comparison to related work in crowdsourcing.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, coupled with the strong theoretical analysis and practical validation, make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. While the experimental scope could be expanded, the current results are convincing. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, primarily focused on simplifying the presentation of the theoretical results and strengthening the connection to related work in crowdsourcing. Specifically, I suggest adding a more detailed comparison to existing clustering algorithms beyond ‘same cluster’ queries.
