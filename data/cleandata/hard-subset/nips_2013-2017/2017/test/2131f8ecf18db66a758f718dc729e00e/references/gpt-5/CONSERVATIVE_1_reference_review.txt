Summary
The paper studies non-adaptive, budgeted query-based labeling/clustering with k classes through an information-theoretic lens. It introduces “locally encodable source coding,” where each query (compressed bit) depends on at most Δ labels, and develops lower bounds and algorithms in this constrained setting. For k=2, it contrasts standard “same-cluster” (XOR) queries with AND queries. Main claims:
- Exact recovery with noiseless queries: For k=2 and Δ≥2, a random XOR-based scheme with Δ-sparse rows achieves m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries (α depends on p, Δ), strictly below n for some p, with a matching-style lower bound that improves on naive n h(p) in a regime.
- Approximate recovery with noiseless queries: A lower bound under local encodability is given (proof in appendix). The paper argues XOR queries are fundamentally limited for approximate recovery (citing linear rate–distortion limits) and proposes an AND-based scheme with a simple decoder, deriving an explicit distortion expression under a random graph model.
- Noisy queries and approximate recovery for general k≥2: It defines a k-ary pairwise query that returns i if both labels equal i, else 0, and assumes symmetric noise on the k outcomes. Using a d-regular graph and label-wise thresholding, it claims (1−δ)-accurate reconstruction with m=O(n log(k/δ)) queries.
- A small crowdsourcing experiment on movie genres suggests AND queries yield fewer labeling errors than “same-cluster” queries; additional synthetic results compare to a spectral clustering baseline.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on crowdsourced clustering/query complexity (e.g., Ashtiani et al.; Mazumdar & Saha), coding-theoretic perspectives on crowdsourcing, and local decoding/smooth compression. The locally encodable viewpoint appears less explored than locally decodable/smooth compression, and the AND-vs-XOR comparison is aligned with recent interest in query design beyond pairwise equality. However, several strong baselines in noisy crowdsourcing (Dawid–Skene, Karger–Oh–Shah, task allocation/EM approaches) are not empirically compared.

Strengths
- Interesting and timely perspective linking non-adaptive query-based clustering to locally encodable source coding; some new lower bounds under locality constraints.
- Clear articulation that XOR is linear and may be suboptimal for approximate recovery, with a simple, analyzable AND-based alternative and decoder.
- The noisy k-ary scheme and thresholding analysis give an appealing O(n log(k/δ)) scaling under the chosen noise model.
- The paper attempts both theory and practice, including a small user study and synthetic experiments.

Weaknesses
- Empirical validation is very limited and does not rigorously support several key claims:
  - The real experiment is small (200 items, 10 volunteers), lacks worker heterogeneity modeling, and does not compare against strong baselines (e.g., Dawid–Skene/KOS with repeated queries or alternative non-adaptive designs).
  - The “same-cluster” vs AND comparison is not controlled; the much higher error rate for same-cluster could be task difficulty or instruction effects. The authors then move to synthetic data to compare to spectral clustering, which is not the right baseline for noisy crowdsourcing aggregation.
  - No comparisons to other non-adaptive query primitives (triangles, ternary comparisons), nor to methods that learn worker reliabilities or use repeated queries.
- Several theoretical aspects are unclear or rely on conjectures:
  - The statement that XOR is “highly inefficient” for approximate recovery leans on a Massey-style linear rate–distortion limitation; the paper itself labels the central bound as a conjecture, known only at p=0.5. Claims that AND “beats the lower bound” are therefore misleading except in special cases.
  - Theorem 1’s expurgation argument is not fully rigorous as presented. The union bound over collisions among typical sequences and the expurgation over “label vectors” reads like a codebook expurgation; the derivation uses T instead of (T choose 2) at one point and would benefit from a clean statement about existence of a query matrix Q with injectivity over the typical set.
  - Theorem 3 (approximate recovery lower bound) is central but its proof is deferred and the bound is opaque; no empirical or analytical demonstration of tightness.
- Modeling and realism:
  - The noise model in Sec. 3.3 assumes independent symmetric k-ary channel noise, equal confusion across non-true outcomes. Real crowdsourcing errors are worker-dependent and correlated; independence across queries is assumed and untested.
  - The scheme assumes known priors p (or exact cluster sizes), using an O(log n) fully queried subsample. In many applications, exact priors are not known and subsampling with all-pairs might violate the non-repetition constraint the paper emphasizes elsewhere.
- Clarity/notation issues:
  - Repeated confusion between m = nd/2 (edges in a d-regular graph) and “nd2”; figures and text suggest “nd2” is a typesetting of nd/2 but this should be fixed.
  - Several typos and dense passages (e.g., thresholds Ci) with limited intuition; key figures and proofs are in the supplement.

Quality
Mixed. Some arguments (Chernoff-style thresholds in Sec. 3.3) are reasonable; others need tightening (exact-recovery expurgation, approximate lower bounds). The empirical section does not provide the breadth or depth needed to validate the practicality of the proposed querying primitive, nor does it compare to established baselines.

Clarity
Moderate. The paper is long and sometimes hard to follow; notation inconsistencies (nd/2 vs nd2) and reliance on the supplement hinder readability. The main ideas could be distilled more clearly, and claims tied tightly to proven statements.

Originality
The locally encodable framing and specific lower bounds seem novel. The AND vs XOR insight is simple but useful. However, parts of the argument against XOR rely on a conjecture.

Significance
Potentially interesting for designing non-adaptive crowdsourced queries, but current experimental evidence is insufficient to influence practice. Tightening the theory and adding strong empirical comparisons would improve impact.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Provide rigorous proofs (or tighter sketches) for Theorem 1 and Theorem 3; avoid relying on the Massey conjecture for central claims about XOR limitations.
- Clarify and fix all notation around m=nd/2; add intuition for thresholds Ci and constants in Theorem 5; report explicit constants in the O(·).
- Add empirical baselines: Dawid–Skene/KOS-style aggregation, majority vote with repeated queries, adaptive vs non-adaptive tradeoffs, triangle queries, and label-query baselines under the same budget.
- Evaluate robustness to misspecified priors and to heterogeneous worker reliabilities.
- Expand real-world experiments beyond a small convenience sample; include inter-annotator agreement and quality control.

Decision
Lean reject. The theoretical perspective is promising and some bounds/algorithms are interesting, but the paper overstates conclusions about XOR vs AND, relies on conjectural arguments for a key narrative, and lacks thorough empirical validation against established baselines. With strengthened proofs and significantly improved experiments, this work could be much more compelling. Confidence: high.
