

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper makes a compelling case for a fundamental shift in how we approach clustering with crowdsourced queries. The authors pose the problem through the lens of locally encodable source coding, a novel perspective that connects information theory with clustering. Their key insight is that AND queries (where the oracle returns "yes" only if both elements share the same label) outperform traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery, requiring significantly fewer queries. This is a bold and counterintuitive approach that challenges the conventional wisdom in the field.

The paper's most significant contribution is the introduction of AND queries as a superior alternative to XOR-based queries for clustering. The authors rigorously establish that "same cluster" queries are fundamentally limited for approximate recovery (Theorem 3 and Conjecture 1), while AND queries achieve substantially better performance (Theorem 4). For example, with p=0.5 (equal-sized clusters), the AND query scheme achieves δ=0.1 with about 500 queries, while XOR queries require nearly 1,000 queries for the same error rate. This is a clear, quantitative improvement that could have significant practical implications for crowdsourcing applications.

The theoretical framework connecting clustering to locally encodable source coding is particularly elegant and novel. While locally decodable codes have been studied, the authors make a compelling case for the importance of locally encodable codes (where each compressed bit depends on a constant number of input bits) in this context. They provide new information-theoretic lower bounds that are strictly better than previous results for certain parameter ranges (Theorem 2). This theoretical contribution is substantial and establishes a new foundation for understanding query complexity in clustering.

The paper's experimental validation is well-executed and directly supports their theoretical claims. The real crowdsourcing experiment with 100 action and 100 romantic movies demonstrates that AND queries yield 57% fewer errors than "same cluster" queries (105 vs. 234 errors), confirming their theoretical advantage. The results in Figure 5 show a clear match between theoretical predictions and experimental outcomes, which is particularly impressive given the noisy nature of crowdsourced data.

The paper also makes important progress on noisy query scenarios (Section 3.3), where they show that AND queries can achieve (1-δ)-good recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries even when oracle answers are noisy. This is a significant improvement over previous approaches that required O(n log n) queries for similar error rates.

While the paper is strong in its novelty and theoretical contributions, there are a few areas for improvement. The focus on the binary case (k=2) is understandable given the main contribution, but the generalization to k>2 could be more thoroughly explored. The experimental validation, while well-executed, is limited to a single movie dataset; additional validation on other datasets would strengthen the practical claims. The connection to prior work on crowdsourcing (e.g., [21, 22, 27]) is adequate but could be more comprehensive.

What makes this paper stand out is its bold rethinking of a well-studied problem. The authors don't just improve existing methods—they question the fundamental assumptions of the field and propose a completely different approach that turns out to be superior. This is precisely the kind of novel, paradigm-shifting work that NIPS should celebrate.

The paper's contribution is not incremental—it's a new direction for the field. The authors have identified a fundamental limitation in the standard approach (XOR queries) and provided a concrete, theoretically grounded alternative (AND queries) that outperforms existing methods. This is exactly the kind of work that advances the field in a demonstrable way.

In terms of significance, this work has the potential to change how we approach crowdsourced clustering, with immediate practical applications in entity resolution and other areas where human labeling is required. The theoretical framework they've developed also opens up new research directions in information theory.

Given my emphasis on novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, I believe this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance. The main contribution—introducing AND queries as a superior alternative to XOR queries—is sufficiently novel and impactful to outweigh any minor shortcomings in the experimental validation or theoretical generalizations. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

The authors have provided a compelling case for why AND queries are superior, with both theoretical and empirical evidence. This is the kind of paper that sparks new research directions and challenges the field to rethink its assumptions.

I recommend acceptance. This paper represents a significant step forward in the field of clustering with crowdsourced queries, and its novel approach to the problem should be shared with the NIPS community.
