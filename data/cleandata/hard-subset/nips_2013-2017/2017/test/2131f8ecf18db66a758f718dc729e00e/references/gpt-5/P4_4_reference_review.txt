Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonadaptive, budgeted semisupervised clustering/labeling with access to simple pairwise queries answered by an oracle (e.g., crowd workers). It recasts the problem as “locally encodable source coding,” where each query answer (encoded bit) depends on at most Δ labels, contrasting with classical source coding that allows global dependence. The work develops information-theoretic lower bounds under this locality constraint and proposes query/decoding schemes with near-optimal sample complexity.

Main technical contributions:
- Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2): Using parity (XOR) queries over Δ inputs (Δ=2 corresponds to “same-cluster” queries), the paper shows achievability with m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries for α = 0.5(1 + (1−4p(1−p))^Δ), via a random constant-weight query matrix and ML decoding over the typical set (Theorem 1). A new lower bound improves over the naive nh(p) bound for some p (Theorem 2).
- Noiseless, approximate recovery (k=2): Provides a locality-aware lower bound on achievable distortion δ for a given budget m (Theorem 3), and argues that “same-cluster” (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery, consistent with Massey’s limitation of linear rate–distortion codes. The paper proposes pairwise AND queries with a simple local decoder (output 1 if any incident AND answer is 1), and analyzes its error via a random bipartite graph with right-degree 2, showing improved δ–m trade-offs (Theorem 4).
- Noisy, approximate recovery (general k): Introduces k-ary AND queries that return label i if both endpoints have label i, else 0, under symmetric oracle noise (correct with prob 1−q, otherwise uniform over incorrect answers). On a random d-regular graph, a thresholding decoder per node attains (1−δ)-accurate recovery with m = O(n log(k/δ)) queries using Chernoff bounds and dependency-graph concentration (Theorem 5). A variant uses only relative cluster sizes (Theorem 6).
- Experiments: A small real crowdsourcing study (200 movies, d=10) shows lower observed error for AND queries than same-cluster queries and empirical trends consistent with the theory; additional synthetic comparisons are reported.

This connects to prior NeurIPS work on clustering with pairwise queries and noisy crowdsourcing [Ashtiani et al. (NIPS 2016); Mazumdar & Saha (NIPS 2017)], to locally decodable/smooth compression [Makhdoumi et al.; Montanari & Mossel], and to budget–fidelity tradeoffs in crowdsourcing [Lahouti & Hassibi]. The proposed AND primitive relates conceptually to sparse-graph coding and to ideas known in group testing/pooled testing (worth citing for completeness).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The achievability analyses for Theorems 1, 4, and 5 follow standard random coding/constant-weight designs, Chernoff bounds, and dependency-graph concentration; they are plausible. The exact recovery scheme relies on ML decoding over typical sequences and is information-theoretic (not computational). The lower bound in Theorem 2 uses a counting/Hamming ball argument under locality and improves on nh(p) for some regimes. Theorem 3’s locality-aware rate–distortion lower bound is central but only stated (proof in appendix) and the final expression could use more intuition and careful presentation. The analysis in the noisy k-ary case clearly identifies an expectation gap enabling thresholding; constants and precise thresholds are only sketched but can be made explicit.
- Correctness gaps/ambiguities to address:
  - Notation: m = nd/2 in the d-regular design is sometimes typeset as “nd2,” which is ambiguous. Clarify throughout.
  - Theorem 1 uses ML over the typical set; the decoder’s computational complexity is exponential and should be stated explicitly as a limitation for exact recovery.
  - Theorem 3: explain the role of Δ and the term involving exp(Δ h′(·)); provide a proof sketch and sanity checks against classical rate–distortion when Δ→∞.
  - The noisy model assumes independent errors across queries; discuss robustness to worker-dependent biases/correlations.
- Experiments: The crowdsourcing study is small but supports the claim that AND questions are easier for workers than same-cluster. Additional baselines (e.g., Dawid–Skene-style aggregators, spectral/SDP methods under noisy pairwise equality) and sensitivity to class imbalance and k>2 would strengthen the empirical section.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but several presentation issues hinder clarity:
  - Ambiguous “nd2” notation; use nd/2 for the number of queries in a d-regular graph.
  - Minor typos, footnote placement, and occasional inconsistent constants (e.g., threshold Ci includes a 1/2 that is not present in the expectations).
  - Figures are referenced to demonstrate gaps; ensure they are self-contained with axes/units and include comparisons to XOR-based baselines where possible.
  - Provide a high-level intuition for why locality increases distortion beyond classical rate–distortion.
  - Make explicit the computational nature of decoders: simple local thresholding for AND vs intractable ML for XOR exact recovery.

Originality
- The locality-constrained source coding view for clustering queries appears novel and interesting. The improved lower bounds under local encoding and the identification of linear/XOR limitations for approximate recovery are meaningful. The proposal and analysis of AND queries for nonadaptive, noisy approximate recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries and simple decoding is, to my knowledge, new in this context. Relating to group testing/pooled testing literature would better situate the AND-based design.

Significance
- The work advances our understanding of query design for crowdsourced clustering, showing that simple nonadaptive AND queries can substantially reduce query counts for approximate recovery and are empirically easier for workers. The locality-aware IT bounds can inform future designs. Impact is likely among researchers working on interactive clustering, crowdsourcing, and information-theoretic limits. Practical significance would be strengthened by broader empirical validation and comparisons to established inference methods under realistic noise.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled information-theoretic framing with new lower bounds under locality.
- Clear identification of a suboptimality of same-cluster (XOR) queries for approximate recovery.
- Simple, scalable AND-based algorithms achieving O(n log(k/δ)) with noise.
- Initial real-data evidence that AND queries reduce worker error.

Cons for acceptance
- Some core proofs (notably Theorem 3) are deferred and lack intuition in the main text.
- Exact recovery achievability relies on intractable ML decoding; computational aspects are not addressed.
- Notational/typographical issues (nd/2 vs nd2) and threshold expressions need cleanup.
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope and baselines; no k>2 real-data demonstration.

Scope
- The paper is in scope for NeurIPS (clustering with queries, crowdsourcing, information theory).

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify notation and thresholds; provide a concise proof sketch and intuition for Theorem 3.
- State computational complexity of decoders explicitly; discuss tractable alternatives for exact recovery.
- Add comparisons with standard crowdsourcing aggregators (e.g., Dawid–Skene, variational methods) and with XOR-based clustering (spectral/SDP) under the same noise.
- Discuss robustness to correlated errors and provide guidance for estimating p from data within the proposed pipeline.
- Cite related group testing literature and contrast AND with other primitives (OR, majority, triangle queries).
