

# Review

This paper introduces a bold and novel approach to clustering by reframing it as a locally encodable source coding problem, with the key innovation being the introduction of AND-queries as a superior alternative to traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries. The authors demonstrate that AND-queries can significantly reduce the number of required queries for label recovery, particularly in approximate recovery scenarios, while also providing theoretical guarantees and real-world validation.

The paper's most compelling contribution is its novel framing of clustering as locally encodable source coding, a concept that appears to be underexplored in information theory. This reframing leads directly to the discovery of AND-queries, which the authors prove can recover (1-δ) proportion of labels with O(n log(1/δ)) queries, compared to the O(n log n) queries required by existing methods using XOR queries. This represents a fundamental improvement over prior work, particularly for large-scale crowdsourcing applications where query costs are prohibitive.

The theoretical contributions are substantial, including new information-theoretic lower bounds that strictly improve upon previous results for certain parameter ranges. For exact recovery with two clusters, their upper bound is strictly less than n for all 0.0694 ≤ p < 0.5 when using pairwise queries, which is a significant improvement over the standard approach of querying every element against a reference element (requiring n-1 queries). For approximate recovery, they provide a rigorous lower bound showing that XOR queries are fundamentally limited, while their AND-query approach achieves substantially better performance.

The experimental validation is particularly convincing. The authors conducted a real crowdsourcing experiment using IMDB movie data, where AND-queries resulted in only 105 errors compared to 234 errors for traditional "same cluster" queries. This practical demonstration of the theoretical advantage—AND-queries being both more accurate and easier for crowd workers to answer—strongly supports their claims.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem formulation to theoretical results to experimental validation. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., they don't allow query repetition, which is justified in the introduction) and to properly contextualize their work within related literature.

The significance of this work is substantial. It addresses a practical problem (reducing query costs in crowdsourcing) with a theoretically grounded solution that is validated in practice. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a new direction for research in clustering with queries, rather than making incremental improvements to existing methods.

The only minor weakness is the limited focus on k > 2 clusters, though the authors mention their results extend to larger k. A more detailed discussion of this extension would strengthen the paper, but this does not detract from the core contribution.

This paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel work that NIPS values most highly. The authors didn't just optimize existing methods—they fundamentally reframed the problem in a way that led to a substantially better solution. The combination of theoretical rigor, practical validation, and conceptual novelty makes this a strong contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance. The paper introduces a significant new concept that advances the field in a demonstrable way, and the authors have provided both theoretical guarantees and real-world validation to support their claims. This is exactly the kind of work that should be published at NIPS.
