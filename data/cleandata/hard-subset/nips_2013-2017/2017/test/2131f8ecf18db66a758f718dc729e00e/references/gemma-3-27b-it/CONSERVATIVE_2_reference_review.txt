This paper explores the problem of semi-supervised clustering via a novel lens – locally encodable source coding – and proposes using AND-queries in addition to the standard ‘same cluster’ queries to improve querying efficiency. While the motivation of minimizing oracle queries in crowdsourcing is well-established (see, e.g., [4, 8, 20]), and the connection to information theory is reasonable ([14]), the work presents a significant challenge to justify its novelty and impact. My overall assessment is skeptical.

The core idea of framing the problem as locally encodable source coding feels like a somewhat forced application of an information-theoretic concept. The benefit of this framing isn't immediately clear, and the authors don't convincingly demonstrate that it unlocks significant new insights beyond what’s already known in the clustering/crowdsourcing literature. The authors acknowledge the relatedness to locally *decodable* codes, but don’t sufficiently differentiate their contribution in the locally *encodable* setting. The claims of new information theoretic results feel overstated without more substantial comparison to existing bounds.

The proposed AND-query approach is potentially interesting, but its advantages are presented as largely theoretical. The derivation of lower bounds and the demonstration of superiority over XOR queries are mathematically presented, however, the gap in performance is not compelling enough, especially considering the added complexity of using AND queries. The authors point to a real-world experiment, but the experimental setup is not sufficiently detailed and the results are not particularly striking. Simply observing lower error rates with AND queries in a single, limited experiment doesn't constitute strong validation. The explanation regarding why majority voting fails in real-world scenarios is interesting, but feels somewhat tangential to the main contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   The attempt to connect clustering with information-theoretic principles is a potentially fruitful direction.
*   The mathematical analysis of the querying complexity, while complex, is rigorous.
*   The consideration of noisy oracle responses is a practical and important addition.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The novelty of the locally encodable source coding framing feels limited. It isn't clear how this perspective leads to fundamentally new algorithms or insights.
*   The proposed AND-query scheme lacks a compelling practical advantage over established methods, particularly given its increased complexity. The gap is not large enough to justify the added complexity.
*   The experimental validation is weak. The experimental setup lacks detail, and the results are not conclusive. A comparison with strong baselines (e.g., active learning strategies or more sophisticated query selection methods) is missing.
*   The paper is dense and difficult to follow. The notation is heavy and the explanations are often terse.
*   There is a general lack of connection to the broader machine learning/clustering literature. For instance, the work does not discuss the relationship to spectral clustering or other widely used techniques.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a theoretically rigorous analysis of a relevant problem.
*   The AND-query approach, while not revolutionary, might offer incremental improvements in specific scenarios.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The novelty is limited and the contribution feels incremental.
*   The experimental validation is insufficient.
*   The paper lacks sufficient clarity and connection to the broader literature.
*   The benefits of the proposed method are not substantial enough to warrant inclusion in NIPS.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. The paper presents a mathematically sound, but ultimately unconvincing contribution. The claims of novelty are overstated, the experimental validation is weak, and the practical impact of the proposed methods is unclear. While the authors demonstrate technical competence, the work does not meet the high standards of originality and significance expected at NIPS. A significant revision with more compelling experimental results and a clearer articulation of the contribution would be needed for reconsideration.
