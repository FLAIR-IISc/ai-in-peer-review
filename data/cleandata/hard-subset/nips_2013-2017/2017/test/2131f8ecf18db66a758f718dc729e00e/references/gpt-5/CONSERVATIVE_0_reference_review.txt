Summary
The paper studies nonadaptive query-efficient labeling/clustering with an oracle, recasting it as “locally encodable source coding” where each query/compressed bit depends on at most Δ labels. For k=2, the authors analyze parity/XOR (“same-cluster”) queries and show an achievability bound for exact recovery with m ≈ n(h(p)+o(1))/log(1/α) queries, α = 1/2 · (1 − 4p(1−p))^Δ + 1/2, via a random Δ-sparse linear encoding and ML decoding over typical sequences. They give a lower bound exploiting that many items participate in few queries. For approximate recovery, they derive a lower bound under local encodability constraints (proof in supplement) and argue that XOR (linear) queries are suboptimal (invoking Massey/Ancheta results), proposing instead pairwise AND queries with a simple local decoder; they provide an explicit error expression and show improved tradeoffs. For noisy responses, they generalize AND to a k-ary pairwise query returning i only when both labels equal i, otherwise 0; with a d-regular design and thresholding (Algorithm 1), they claim (1−δ)-accurate recovery with m = O(n log(k/δ)) queries under independent per-query noise. A small crowdsourcing experiment on 200 movies suggests AND queries yield fewer errors than “same-cluster” queries, and a synthetic comparison to spectral clustering is reported.

Strengths
- Interesting information-theoretic viewpoint (locally encodable source coding) and new lower/upper bounds.
- The insight that non-linear AND queries can be substantially better than XOR for approximate/noisy settings is plausible and potentially impactful for crowdsourcing practice.
- Simple, local decoding rule for AND queries with clean Chernoff-style analysis; use of limited dependency to argue high-probability guarantees.
- Small empirical study suggests AND queries may be easier for workers than “same-cluster” queries.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Technical correctness/clarity: In Sec. 3.3 the paper repeatedly states m = nd^2 queries for a d-regular graph, but a d-regular undirected graph on n nodes has |E|=nd/2, i.e., m should be Θ(nd), consistent with the experiment (n=200, d=10 → 1000 queries). This inconsistency is serious and undermines the claimed O(n log(k/δ)) scaling (is it O(nd) or O(nd^2)?).
- Practicality of decoding: The exact-recovery achievability (Theorem 1) relies on ML decoding over the typical set for a random sparse linear code; the decoding complexity is exponential and not addressed. Thus the “below n” query gains are not algorithmic in a practical sense.
- Proof gaps/typos: Several key derivations are deferred to the supplement (Theorem 3). In Algorithm 1, the threshold Ci and expectations E[Nu,i] include terms like “1 − q k/(k−1)” that appear dimensionally inconsistent with the described noise model (correct with prob 1−q, otherwise uniform among k−1 wrong labels). The derivation of the expectation under conditioning on Xu=i should be carefully rechecked. The dependence of constants on q, p, k is not made explicit in the main text.
- Reliance on conjectural or specialized bounds: The argument that XOR is suboptimal for approximate recovery leans on linear code rate–distortion limitations and a conjecture (only proven at p=1/2). A direct, unconditional lower bound tailored to the query model would strengthen the claim.
- Query model practicality for k>2: The k-ary pairwise query that returns i only if both items are in class i otherwise 0 is unusual for crowdsourcing; it requires workers to recognize and consistently encode class indices. The empirical validation is only for k=2 (binary AND). There is no user study for k>2 or analysis of worker burden/cognitive load.
- Limited experiments and baselines: The real experiment uses only 10 volunteers and 200 items with instruction to answer without checking resources; error rates may not reflect realistic crowd workflows. Comparisons to established noisy crowdsourcing methods (e.g., Dawid–Skene, Karger–Oh–Shah) or to stronger nonadaptive baselines are missing. The synthetic comparison to spectral clustering on noisy “same-cluster” answers is limited and not necessarily the best way to use those answers.
- Missing related work: The AND-query design is closely related to group testing and pooled testing, including noisy approximate recovery with O(n log(1/δ)) tests; this literature is not cited or contrasted, although the problem setting differs (not sparse). Also, locally encodable/decodable compression relates to LDGM/LDPC-based lossy compression; some connections are cited but could be better positioned relative to the proposed schemes.

Quality
The theoretical direction is interesting, but the above inconsistencies (m=nd^2 vs nd/2), deferred proofs/typos, and omission of decoding complexity weaken soundness and reproducibility. The empirical section is too small-scale, lacks strong baselines, and does not validate the k>2 setting.

Clarity
The paper is dense with several typos and notational inconsistencies. Key results (Theorem 3, constants in Theorem 5) are not presented fully in the main text. The query-count inconsistency and threshold expressions should be corrected. A clearer description of the noise model and the exact expectations/thresholds is needed.

Originality
The “locally encodable source coding” lens for crowdsourced clustering is a nice conceptual contribution, and emphasizing AND over XOR in this context is novel to my knowledge. However, there is substantial overlap with prior sparse-graph compression and group-testing ideas that are not fully acknowledged.

Significance
If corrected and strengthened, the message that simple non-linear queries can outperform “same-cluster” in noisy/approximate regimes could influence practical crowdsourcing designs. As is, the gap between theory and practical, validated algorithms (especially for k>2) limits impact.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify the total number of queries in Sec. 3.3: is it m=nd/2 or nd (undirected vs directed), and where does nd^2 come from? The experiment suggests nd/2.
- Provide the full, correct expressions for E[Nu,i] and Ci under the stated noise model, and the exact constants in the Chernoff bounds and the dependence on q, p, k.
- What is the decoding complexity for Theorem 1? Can any polynomial-time decoder achieve similar query savings?
- How feasible is the k-ary pairwise query in practice? Have you tested k>2 with real users?
- Can you compare empirically against Dawid–Skene/Karger–Oh–Shah style methods and stronger baselines for “same-cluster” queries under equal query budgets?
- Please discuss connections and differences to noisy group testing.

Pros
- Interesting theoretical framing and bounds.
- Plausible and simple AND-based scheme with local decoding.
- Some empirical indication that AND queries are easier for workers.

Cons
- Apparent inconsistency in query counts; typos in key formulas.
- ML-based achievability not computationally feasible.
- Limited experiments, no strong baselines; k>2 not validated.
- Missing related work and unconditional lower bounds for XOR.

Recommendation: Reject
Confidence: High (4/5)
