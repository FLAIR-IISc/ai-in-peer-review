

# Peer Review: "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper introduces a novel perspective on clustering and label learning by reframing it as a locally encodable source coding problem. The authors argue that traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery, and propose using AND queries instead, which they demonstrate can achieve significantly better query complexity. The paper makes theoretical contributions to locally encodable source coding while providing practical insights for crowdsourcing applications.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper builds on recent work in query-based clustering [21, 33] but reframes the problem through information theory, specifically as a locally encodable source coding problem where each query depends on only a constant number of labels. While related to locally decodable codes [19, 16], this is the first to study locally encodable codes in the context of clustering. The authors also connect to source coding theory [7] but extend it to the constrained setting where each query depends on only Δ labels. The paper's key insight is that AND queries outperform XOR queries for approximate recovery, which is both theoretically and empirically validated.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty**: The paper establishes the first nontrivial information-theoretic bounds for locally encodable source coding. The proofs for Theorems 1 and 2 are elegant and demonstrate deep understanding of coding theory and information theory. The connection between clustering and source coding is particularly insightful.

2. **Practical Insights**: The paper provides compelling evidence that AND queries outperform XOR queries in practice, supported by both theoretical analysis and a real crowdsourcing experiment (105 errors vs. 234 for AND vs. "same cluster" queries). This is a valuable contribution to human-in-the-loop machine learning.

3. **Technical Rigor**: The theoretical results are well-supported with detailed proofs, and the experimental validation adds credibility. The noisy query analysis (Section 3.3) is particularly strong, with a clean proof of Theorem 5 showing O(n log(k/δ)) query complexity.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear definitions and logical flow. The connection between clustering and source coding is explained effectively, and the experimental section is well-structured.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Related Work Discussion**: The paper misses key recent works on query-based clustering, such as [22, 28] which also consider non-adaptive schemes. The authors should clarify how their approach differs from these works.

2. **Implementation Challenges**: The paper doesn't adequately address practical implementation challenges for AND queries. While claiming AND queries are easier for workers, the experiment only shows lower error rates without explaining *why* (e.g., "both are action" vs. "same genre" is more intuitive for some workers).

3. **Overstated Claims**: The paper claims AND queries are "better" without fully addressing when XOR queries might still be preferable (e.g., when p is highly skewed toward 0 or 1). The theoretical advantage only holds for certain prior distributions.

4. **Theoretical Limitations**: The k > 2 results (Section 3.3) assume the oracle provides k possible answers, which is a significant departure from typical binary answers in crowdsourcing. The practicality of this assumption needs more discussion.

## Significance and Originality

The paper makes a strong contribution to both theoretical and practical aspects of clustering with human-in-the-loop systems. The information-theoretic bounds for locally encodable source codes are novel and could interest the information theory community. The practical insights about query design could significantly impact future crowdsourcing system design.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution while providing practical insights. The main weaknesses are minor compared to the paper's substantial contributions. The theoretical results are well-supported, the experimental validation is convincing, and the writing is excellent.

## Specific Comments

1. In Section 3.2, Conjecture 1 about XOR query lower bounds is only proven at p=0.5. The paper should clarify how this affects the general case and whether the advantage of AND queries holds for all p.

2. The real crowdsourcing experiment would be strengthened by analyzing why AND queries have lower error rates (e.g., cognitive load differences between question types).

3. The paper should explicitly state conditions under which AND queries are better than XOR queries beyond the information-theoretic lower bounds.

4. The connection to smooth compression [23] is mentioned but not fully exploited; the authors could strengthen their theoretical contribution by drawing more explicit parallels.

## Recommendation

Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution with clear practical implications. The minor weaknesses noted do not undermine the core contributions. This work advances both the theoretical understanding of query-based clustering and provides actionable insights for practitioners designing crowdsourcing systems. The paper is technically sound, original, and significant for the NIPS community.

**Summary of Key Arguments:**
- *For acceptance*: Strong theoretical contribution to locally encodable source coding; compelling empirical validation; novel query design with practical impact.
- *Against acceptance*: Limited discussion of related work; insufficient exploration of practical implementation challenges for AND queries.

This paper represents an excellent contribution to the field, demonstrating how information theory can provide fundamental insights into practical machine learning problems.
