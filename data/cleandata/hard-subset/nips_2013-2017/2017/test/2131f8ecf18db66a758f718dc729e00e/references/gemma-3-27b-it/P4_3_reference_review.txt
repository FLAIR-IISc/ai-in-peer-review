This paper investigates the problem of semi-supervised clustering via querying a potentially noisy oracle (e.g., crowd workers). The authors formulate this as a locally encodable source coding problem and derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required for exact and approximate recovery of labels. A key contribution is showing that AND queries can outperform ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries, especially in the approximate recovery setting. The paper also presents a practical algorithm leveraging AND queries and validates it through experiments on real datasets obtained from Amazon Mechanical Turk.

The paper is well-structured and the introduction clearly motivates the problem and highlights the connections to prior work in crowdsourcing, clustering, and information theory. The problem setup is mathematically sound and the link to locally encodable source coding provides a novel perspective. The theoretical results, particularly the lower bounds and the analysis of AND queries, are significant. The experimental validation provides further credibility to the proposed approach, demonstrating that AND queries can indeed lead to lower error rates compared to ‘same cluster’ queries in a real-world scenario. The extensive referencing to related works is commendable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of the querying problem as a locally encodable source coding problem is a novel contribution, opening up avenues for applying information-theoretic tools to crowdsourced clustering.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides rigorous information-theoretic analysis, including lower bounds on query complexity and a detailed performance analysis of the proposed algorithm.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The paper addresses a practical problem in crowdsourcing and demonstrates the benefits of the proposed approach through real-world experiments.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the mathematical notation is clear. The motivation and intuition behind the key ideas are well-explained.
*   **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors thoroughly review related work in crowdsourcing, clustering, and information theory.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of Proofs:** While the theoretical results are significant, the proofs, particularly those in the appendix, could be more accessible. A more intuitive explanation of the key steps in the proofs would be helpful.
*   **Experimental Details:** The experimental section could benefit from more detailed information about the experimental setup, including the specific parameters used (e.g., graph regularity, survey design) and statistical significance tests to support the claims.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments focus primarily on movie classification. While this provides a proof-of-concept, evaluating the algorithm on a wider range of datasets and tasks would strengthen the conclusions.
*  **Discussion on Oracle Cost**: The paper mentions the cost of queries, but doesn’t explore more complex cost models. Some queries might take longer for workers to answer or may be more prone to error. A discussion of how the algorithm could be adapted for different cost structures would be valuable.



**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to semi-supervised clustering with noisy queries.
*   The information-theoretic lower bounds and the analysis of AND queries are significant contributions to the field.
*   The experimental validation provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and comprehensively references related work.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   The complexity of the proofs might limit the accessibility of the paper to a broader audience.
*   The experimental section could benefit from more details and a wider range of datasets.
*   The lack of discussion on different oracle cost models is a minor weakness.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of semi-supervised clustering and crowdsourcing. The novel formulation of the problem, the rigorous theoretical analysis, and the practical validation make it a valuable addition to the NIPS program. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not outweigh the strengths of the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve the clarity of the proofs and provide more detailed experimental results. Specifically, adding a small section describing the time taken to complete the crowdsourcing experiments and any quality control measures would strengthen the experimental section.
