Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies non-adaptive, budgeted query-based labeling/clustering of n items with k classes, framing queries as a form of locally encodable source coding: each binary query output depends on at most Δ labels. The work connects crowdsourced “same-cluster” (XOR) queries to linear operations and proposes AND-type queries as better primitives for approximate recovery. The authors derive information-theoretic lower bounds under locality constraints, give achievability schemes for exact recovery (noiseless) and approximate recovery (noisy and noiseless), and present a simple non-adaptive algorithm for noisy queries achieving (1−δ) accuracy with O(n log(k/δ)) queries. Small-scale crowdsourcing experiments (k=2) support that AND queries are easier and yield lower error than XOR (“same-cluster”), and synthetic experiments compare against spectral clustering.

This work builds on crowdsourced clustering/entity resolution with pairwise queries [4,8,20–22,31], coding-theoretic views of crowdsourcing [14,27,29], and locally decodable/smooth compression literature [16,18,19,23,26]. It leverages rate-distortion theory [7] and the classical limitation of linear codes for lossy compression (Ancheta–Massey [17]). For noisy models it relates to joint source–channel coding and prior bounds of O(n log n) queries for k=2 [21].

Quality
- Theoretical results:
  - Exact recovery, noiseless, k=2, Δ-local encoding: Theorem 1 shows achievability with m ≈ n h(p)/log2(1/α), α=1/2(1+(1−4p(1−p))^Δ), using random Δ-sparse parity queries (XOR across Δ labels) and expurgation over the typical set. This is plausible and standard in style; however, the statement and proof inconsistently toggle between multiplication and division by log(1/α). The correct dependence matches division per the proof.
  - Lower bound (Theorem 2): Uses that many elements participate in few queries (by averaging) and packs typical sequences differing on those positions, yielding m ≥ nh(p)·max{1, maxρ (1−ρ)/h(((1−ρ)r(p)Δ)/ρ)} with r(p)=2p(1−p). The argument is reasonable under the stated typicality but omits some combinatorial details; the improvement over the naive nh(p) for certain p and Δ is interesting.
  - Approximate recovery, noiseless (Theorem 3): Presents a locality-aware lower bound that augments the rate–distortion baseline δ̃(m/n) with an extra penalty depending on Δ and h′. The proof is deferred; in main text this result is hard to assess fully.
  - Limitations of XOR: The paper invokes Ancheta–Massey to argue that linear (XOR-based) schemes are suboptimal for lossy compression except at p=1/2, and states a conjectured lower bound δ ≥ p(1 − m/(n h(p))) for “same-cluster” queries, known at p=1/2. This is presented as a conjecture appropriately.
  - AND queries, noiseless (Theorem 4): Proposes a simple decoder (predict 1 if any incident AND answer is 1) and analyzes error for k=2 under a random right-regular (degree-2) bipartite graph. The Poisson approximation and occupancy-based inclusion–exclusion lead to a closed-form error expression; the approach is sound for asymptotics though technical details (independence, multigraph effects) are glossed over.
  - Noisy queries, approximate recovery, general k (Theorem 5): Uses a d-regular graph, k-ary AND-like query (outputs class i if both endpoints are i, else 0), and a per-node thresholding decoder. Chernoff bounds yield per-node errors ≲ exp(−Ω(d)), so choosing d = Θ(log(k/δ)) gives total errors ≤ δn with high probability; thus m = Θ(n log(k/δ)). The high-level argument is correct. However, there are notable inconsistencies and typos:
    - The paper repeatedly states the total number of queries as nd^2, but a d-regular graph on n nodes has |E| = nd/2, matching the experiments; the correct scaling is m=Θ(nd).
    - Tail bounds are written with e−2d^2 rather than the expected e−Ω(d).
    - The dependency-graph bound for concentration counts at most d + d dependent nodes; it should be O(d^2) (1- and 2-hop neighborhoods in a d-regular graph).
  - Theorem 6 (unknown priors, known cluster sizes): The claim is plausible by random permutation symmetry; details are deferred.

- Empirics: A small real experiment (200 movies, d up to 10, k=2) shows lower worker error for AND versus XOR, and the thresholding decoder tracks the predicted dependence on d. The comparison to spectral clustering on same-cluster queries uses synthetic data and is indicative. However, there are no experiments for k>2, and the real dataset is limited in size and scope.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-motivated, but clarity is hampered by several notational/arithmetical inconsistencies (e.g., m = nd^2 vs nd/2; inconsistent logs in Theorem 1; Chernoff exponents; dependence degree). Some derivations (Theorem 3) are deferred entirely; others rely on approximations without precise conditions. Algorithm 1’s thresholds Ci could be derived more clearly from the channel model. Figures are referenced but some axes/units are not described in the excerpt.

Originality
- Introducing and analyzing “locally encodable source coding” for query-based clustering is novel and connects two communities.
- The lower bounds incorporating locality constraints appear new.
- The use of AND queries (and k-ary generalization) to outperform XOR for approximate recovery in both noiseless and noisy settings is original and well-motivated by coding-theoretic insights.

Significance
- The results suggest a principled redesign of crowdsourcing queries to improve sample efficiency and robustness, with non-adaptive, parallelizable schemes and near-optimal query complexity for approximate recovery.
- The exact-recovery bounds below n for certain p and Δ highlight the value of priors.
- Practical impact depends on the feasibility of k-ary AND-style queries at scale and on extending the empirical validation beyond k=2.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Conceptually novel connection (locally encodable coding) yielding meaningful lower/upper bounds.
- Clear insight that XOR (“same-cluster”) can be inferior to AND for approximate recovery, supported theoretically and empirically.
- Simple, scalable algorithm with near-optimal O(n log(k/δ)) query complexity under noise.
- Non-adaptive design aligns with practical crowdsourcing constraints.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Multiple technical inconsistencies/typos that affect core quantities (query counts, exponents, dependency degree), reducing confidence in rigor.
- Some key results (Theorem 3) lack proofs in the main text; others rely on approximations without precise statements.
- Limited empirical evaluation (small k=2 study; no k>2 experiments; comparison to stronger baselines is minimal).
- The conjectured lower bound for XOR is only established at p=1/2; the narrative risks overstating.
- Practicality of k-ary queries in multi-class settings needs discussion and testing.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the query-count inconsistency: replace nd^2 by nd/2 wherever appropriate; correct Chernoff exponents and dependency-degree bounds.
- Provide a complete, self-contained proof sketch for Theorem 3 and tighten the derivations in Theorem 2 and Theorem 4 (e.g., via the configuration model).
- Clarify Theorem 1’s statement (division by log(1/α), log base) and clean up notation.
- Expand empirical evaluation: include k>2, compare against strong baselines (e.g., optimized same-cluster strategies, methods allowing soft priors), and report sensitivity to noise q and class imbalance.
- Discuss practical elicitation of the k-ary AND query, worker instructions, and UI; consider variants using multiple binary ANDs with limited overhead.
- Either prove a stronger lower bound for XOR beyond p=1/2 or temper claims and emphasize the conjectural status.

Scope
The topic is squarely within NeurIPS (learning theory, clustering, crowdsourcing, information-theoretic limits).

Overall assessment
The paper offers a compelling and original perspective with promising theoretical and practical implications. However, the current presentation contains several technical inconsistencies and gaps that should be addressed to ensure rigor and reproducibility. With these corrections and expanded validation, the contribution would be strong.
