

# Peer Review: "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel information-theoretic framework for semisupervised clustering using non-adaptive queries to a human oracle. The authors frame the clustering problem as a locally encodable source coding problem, where each query answer depends on only a constant number of input labels. The key innovation is the introduction of AND queries (where the oracle returns "1" only if both elements have label 1) as a more efficient alternative to the commonly used "same cluster" (XOR) queries. The paper provides theoretical bounds for exact and approximate recovery in noiseless and noisy settings, along with a real crowdsourcing experiment validating their approach. This work connects clustering to information theory in a fresh way, building on recent advances in locally decodable codes while addressing practical limitations of existing query-based clustering methods.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors establish new information-theoretic lower bounds for locally encodable source codes, which is a significant contribution to information theory itself. The proofs of all main theorems are well-structured and logically sound, with careful attention to the conditions under which the bounds hold. The experimental validation in Section 4 is well-designed, with a clear crowdsourcing experiment comparing AND queries to "same cluster" queries, showing a substantial reduction in error rates (105 vs. 234 errors in 1000 queries). The paper is careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the focus on binary labels in many sections, and discusses the tradeoffs between different query types. The theoretical results are well-supported by both mathematical analysis and empirical validation.

## Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, with a logical progression from problem setup to theoretical results to experimental validation. The connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding is clearly explained, making the information-theoretic perspective accessible. The figures (Figures 1-5) effectively illustrate the theoretical results and experimental findings. However, some technical sections (particularly Section 3.2) could benefit from additional intuition to help readers grasp why AND queries outperform XOR queries. The paper could also better explain why AND queries are easier for human workers (beyond the statement that "for a correct 'no' answer in an AND query, a worker just need to know one of the labels"). Overall, the paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results, though some readers may need familiarity with information theory concepts.

## Originality
This paper makes several significant original contributions. The framing of clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem is novel and provides a fresh perspective on query complexity in clustering. The introduction of AND queries as a superior alternative to XOR queries for approximate recovery is a major theoretical insight with practical implications. The paper also establishes new information-theoretic bounds for locally encodable codes, which is a previously unexplored area (as the authors note, "there was no nontrivial result known related to locally encodable codes in general"). The connection to crowdsourcing applications is not merely superficial but forms the core of the theoretical analysis, which is a substantial improvement over previous work on query-based clustering that typically focuses on adaptive algorithms without information-theoretic analysis.

## Significance
The results have considerable significance for both theoretical and applied machine learning. The theoretical insights about query complexity provide fundamental understanding of the limits of semisupervised clustering, while the practical contribution of AND queries could substantially reduce the cost of crowdsourcing for clustering tasks. The paper demonstrates that AND queries can recover (1-δ) proportion of labels with O(n log k/δ) queries, which is significantly better than the O(n log n) queries required for "same cluster" queries. The real-world experiment validating these results adds substantial credibility to the theoretical claims. This work could influence future research in crowdsourcing, entity resolution, and semisupervised learning by providing a more efficient query strategy. The paper addresses a difficult problem (query complexity in clustering) in a demonstrably better way than previous research, advancing the state of the art.

## Strengths and Weaknesses
**Strengths:**
- Novel theoretical framework connecting clustering to locally encodable source coding
- Introduction of AND queries as a superior alternative to XOR queries
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with both lower and upper bounds
- Well-designed real-world experiment validating theoretical claims
- Clear connection between theory and practical crowdsourcing applications

**Weaknesses:**
- Some technical sections would benefit from additional intuition
- Limited explanation of why AND queries are easier for human workers
- The paper focuses primarily on binary labels (k=2) in many sections, though it mentions generalization to k>2
- Could provide more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods beyond the crowdsourcing experiment

## Recommendation
This paper makes a strong contribution to both theoretical machine learning and practical crowdsourcing applications. The novel theoretical framework, combined with the experimental validation, demonstrates significant potential for real-world impact. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear advance over previous work. While there are minor areas for improvement, the paper's strengths far outweigh its weaknesses.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper addresses an important problem with a novel approach that advances both theoretical understanding and practical applications. The quality of the theoretical analysis, combined with the real-world validation, makes this a strong candidate for publication at NIPS. The results are significant for researchers working on semisupervised learning, crowdsourcing, and information theory, and could influence future work in these areas.
