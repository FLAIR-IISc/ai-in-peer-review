

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper studies the problem of clustering elements using queries to an oracle, with a focus on minimizing query complexity. The authors frame the problem as a locally encodable source coding problem in information theory, introducing a novel query type called "AND queries" that outperforms traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery. The work connects clustering to information theory, establishes theoretical bounds, proposes new querying schemes, and validates results through real crowdsourcing experiments.

The paper builds on prior work in clustering with queries (e.g., [4, 8, 21, 22, 33]) but introduces a novel theoretical perspective. Previous works primarily focused on XOR ("same cluster") queries, while this paper demonstrates that AND queries provide better performance guarantees for approximate recovery. The authors also contribute new information-theoretic results on locally encodable source coding, a concept mentioned but not well-studied in [23].

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis with well-constructed theorems and proofs for noiseless exact recovery, noiseless approximate recovery, and noisy approximate recovery. The authors establish both lower bounds and achievable upper bounds, demonstrating clear improvements over prior work. The information-theoretic framework is well-motivated and correctly applied.

2. **Novelty:** The introduction of AND queries as a superior alternative to XOR queries for approximate recovery represents a significant conceptual advance. The paper's framing of the problem as locally encodable source coding is novel within this context, and the authors provide the first non-trivial results for this problem. The connection to information theory offers a fresh perspective on a well-studied problem.

3. **Experimental Validation:** The paper includes a real-world crowdsourcing experiment on IMDB movie data that validates the theoretical results. The comparison between AND queries and "same cluster" queries shows that AND queries produce fewer errors (105 vs. 234 erroneous responses), supporting the theoretical claims. This practical validation strengthens the paper's contribution.

4. **Clarity of Main Results:** The key results (Theorems 1-5) are clearly stated and well-organized. The paper effectively demonstrates that AND queries outperform XOR queries for approximate recovery, with the theoretical guarantees matching the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Connection to Source Coding:** While the paper introduces "locally encodable source coding," the connection to information theory is not sufficiently elaborated. The authors cite [23] for related work on smooth compression, but do not clearly explain how their locally encodable framework differs from existing concepts in source coding. A more detailed explanation of why this particular source coding model is the right framework for the clustering problem would strengthen the paper.

2. **Limited Experimental Scope:** The real-world validation is limited to a single dataset (IMDB movies). While sufficient to support the main claims, more diverse experimental validation would strengthen the practical relevance. The paper could have included results on other datasets or more detailed analysis of the crowdsourcing experiment.

3. **Theoretical Complexity:** Some of the proofs (e.g., Theorem 2) are quite dense and may be difficult for readers to follow without additional intuition. The paper would benefit from more explanatory material to help readers grasp the key insights behind the theoretical results.

4. **Comparison with Prior Work:** While the paper cites relevant prior work, it could more explicitly contrast its results with the most similar works. For instance, the comparison with [21] (which also addresses noisy clustering) could be more detailed to highlight the specific advantages of the proposed AND query approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper introduces a novel query type (AND queries) that demonstrably outperforms existing methods for approximate clustering recovery.
- It provides the first non-trivial theoretical results on locally encodable source coding in the context of clustering.
- The information-theoretic framework offers a fresh perspective on a well-studied problem.
- The theoretical results are validated through real crowdsourcing experiments.
- The paper makes a clear contribution to the theoretical understanding of query complexity in clustering.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The connection to source coding could be more thoroughly explained and justified.
- The experimental validation is limited to a single dataset.
- Some of the theoretical proofs are dense and could be made more accessible.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the problem of clustering with limited queries. The introduction of AND queries as a superior alternative to traditional XOR queries for approximate recovery is novel and well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental validation. The paper's framing of the problem as locally encodable source coding provides a valuable new perspective that advances our understanding of the fundamental limits of clustering with queries.

The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs and clear theoretical guarantees. While the connection to source coding could be more explicitly explained, the core contributions are well-justified and meaningful. The experimental validation, though limited to one dataset, provides important practical support for the theoretical claims.

The significance of this work is high, as it addresses a fundamental problem in crowdsourcing and clustering with practical implications for reducing query costs. The results advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way, providing both theoretical guarantees and practical validation.

I recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS. The contributions are novel, technically sound, and well-validated, making this a valuable addition to the field.
