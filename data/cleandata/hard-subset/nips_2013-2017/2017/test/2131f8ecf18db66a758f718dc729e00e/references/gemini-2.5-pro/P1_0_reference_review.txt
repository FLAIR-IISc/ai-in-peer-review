### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of semi-supervised clustering (or label recovery) for `n` items into `k` clusters using non-adaptive pairwise queries. Motivated by crowdsourcing applications where query budget is limited, the primary goal is to minimize the total number of queries.

The authors make several key contributions:
1.  **A Novel Information-Theoretic Framework:** The paper compellingly frames the problem as one of "locally encodable source coding," where each compressed bit (query answer) depends on a small, constant number of input bits (labels). This perspective allows for the derivation of new information-theoretic lower bounds on the required number of queries.
2.  **Analysis of "Same-Cluster" (XOR) Queries:** For the noiseless, exact recovery case with k=2 clusters, the paper provides upper and lower bounds on the query complexity. It demonstrates that by leveraging prior knowledge of cluster sizes, it's possible to recover all labels with fewer than `n` queries, outperforming naive approaches.
3.  **Introduction of "AND" Queries:** A central contribution is the proposal of a new query type, the AND query ("Are both items in cluster 1?"). The authors argue, with theoretical backing, that this query is fundamentally better than the standard "same-cluster" (XOR) query for the task of *approximate recovery*, especially in the presence of noise.
4.  **An Efficient Algorithm for Noisy Queries:** The paper presents its main algorithmic result for the noisy, approximate recovery setting. It proposes an algorithm based on a generalized k-ary AND query and proves that it can recover a `(1-δ)` fraction of labels correctly with only `O(n log(k/δ))` non-adaptive queries. This is a significant improvement over the `O(n log n)` complexity required by previous methods in the noisy setting.
5.  **Experimental Validation:** The theoretical claims are supported by a small-scale, real-world crowdsourcing experiment involving movie genre classification, which shows that the proposed algorithm performs close to theoretical predictions and that human workers indeed make fewer errors on AND queries compared to "same-cluster" queries.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance of the Framework:** The connection drawn to locally encodable source coding is novel and powerful. It provides a principled, information-theoretic foundation for analyzing query-based clustering and allows the authors to derive new fundamental limits (lower bounds) for this problem. This perspective is a significant conceptual contribution that could inspire further research at the intersection of information theory and active learning/crowdsourcing.

2.  **Impact of AND Queries:** The introduction and analysis of the AND query is a standout contribution. The insight that the asymmetry of the AND operation can be exploited for more efficient recovery (especially in noisy settings) is clever and impactful. The argument against the limitations of linear queries (like XOR) for approximate recovery is well-grounded in rate-distortion theory. This finding has direct practical implications for designing better crowdsourcing tasks.

3.  **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper delivers strong theoretical guarantees. The `O(n log(k/δ))` query complexity for noisy, approximate recovery (Theorem 5) is a substantial improvement for a practical and challenging setting. The bounds for the noiseless case are also new and tighten our understanding of the problem's fundamental limits. The analysis appears technically sound, employing standard tools from information theory, coding theory, and probability in a skillful way.

4.  **Practical Relevance and Experimental Support:** Despite being heavily theoretical, the paper remains grounded in the practical problem of crowdsourcing. The algorithm for the noisy case is simple and implementable. The inclusion of a real-world experiment, while small, is a major strength. It not only validates the performance of the algorithm but also provides empirical evidence for a key hypothesis: that AND queries are cognitively easier for human workers, leading to lower error rates. This is a compelling result that bridges theory and practice.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Computational Complexity of Decoding:** For the noiseless, exact recovery setting (Section 3.1), the achievability result (Theorem 1) relies on a Maximum Likelihood (ML) decoder. ML decoding for a general random linear code is NP-hard. While the focus is on information-theoretic query complexity, the paper would be stronger if it discussed the computational feasibility of the decoding step. Are there structured query designs (e.g., based on LDPC codes) that would permit efficient decoding while maintaining near-optimal query complexity?

2.  **Clarity of Proof Sketches:** Some of the proof sketches are quite dense and rely on heuristic arguments. For example, the proof of Theorem 4 uses a Poisson approximation for the node degrees in the random query graph. While this is a standard and often accurate approximation, a more rigorous justification (e.g., by showing concentration) would solidify the claim. Given the space constraints, this is understandable, but the writing could be sharpened to make the assumptions more explicit.

3.  **Scope of Experimental Validation:** The experiment is a valuable proof of concept. However, with n=200 items and 10 volunteers, its scale is limited. The claims about experimental validation should be presented with this context in mind. The comparison with spectral clustering for "same-cluster" queries is mentioned as being in the supplement; it would be beneficial to briefly summarize the key takeaway from that comparison in the main text to better position the performance of the proposed algorithm.

4.  **Assumption of Known Priors:** The algorithms rely on knowledge of the prior probabilities `p` (or relative cluster sizes `n_i/n`). While the authors rightly point out this is a common assumption and can be estimated, it is still a limitation. Theorem 6, which addresses this by using a random permutation, is crucial for practical applicability, but its proof is deferred. A brief sketch of the intuition behind why randomization makes the algorithm robust to adversarial cluster assignments would be helpful for the reader.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes significant contributions to the fields of active learning, crowdsourcing, and semi-supervised clustering. The novel information-theoretic framing, the introduction of the superior AND query, and the highly efficient algorithm for the noisy setting are all important and original results. The work is technically strong, well-motivated, and its theoretical insights are complemented by practical validation. The weaknesses are minor and mostly relate to clarifying computational aspects and the scope of the experiments, which do not detract from the core contributions. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and is likely to be highly influential for researchers working on query-based learning and information-theoretic approaches to machine learning.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
