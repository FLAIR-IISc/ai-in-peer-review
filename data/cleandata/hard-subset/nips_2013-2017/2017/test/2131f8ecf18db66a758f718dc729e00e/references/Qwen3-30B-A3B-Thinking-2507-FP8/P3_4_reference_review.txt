

# Peer Review: "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

## Summary and Context

This paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for semisupervised clustering by modeling the problem as locally encodable source coding. The authors propose using AND queries instead of traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries, demonstrating that AND queries require significantly fewer queries for approximate recovery in both noiseless and noisy settings. The work connects clustering to information theory in a fresh way, with theoretical results supported by a real crowdsourcing experiment. This builds on prior work in crowdsourcing-based clustering (e.g., [33, 21]) but introduces a fundamentally different query mechanism with theoretical advantages.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Query Mechanism**: The paper's core contribution is the introduction of AND queries, which are theoretically superior to "same cluster" queries for approximate recovery. The authors prove that AND queries achieve better query complexity (O(n log(k/δ)) vs. O(n log n) for "same cluster" queries), with a compelling theoretical justification.

2. **Strong Theoretical Results**: The paper provides non-trivial information-theoretic lower bounds for locally encodable source coding (Theorems 2, 3), which is a well-motivated contribution to a relatively underexplored area. The exact recovery results (Theorem 1) for specific prior distributions are particularly interesting, showing that fewer than n queries suffice for certain p.

3. **Practical Validation**: The real-world crowdsourcing experiment with 200 movies validates the theoretical claims, showing that AND queries indeed have lower error rates (10.5% vs. 23.4% for "same cluster" queries). This practical relevance strengthens the paper's contribution.

4. **Clear Technical Contributions**: The paper makes a careful distinction between exact and approximate recovery, which is crucial for practical applications where perfect recovery may not be necessary.

## Weaknesses

1. **Conceptual Confusion Between Clustering and Labeling**: The paper repeatedly conflates clustering (grouping elements) with labeling (assigning specific cluster labels), which creates confusion. In clustering, the specific labels don't matter, only the grouping. The authors claim their algorithms are valid for clustering, but their algorithms explicitly require knowledge of the label values (e.g., "label 0" vs "label 1"), which is not required for clustering.

2. **Limited Generalization for k > 2**: While the paper mentions results extend to k > 2, the technical details for k > 2 are sparse. The definition of the k-ary query for k > 2 (Section 3.3) is confusingly presented as "exactly the same as binary AND," but this isn't clearly explained. The theoretical guarantees for k > 2 lack sufficient detail.

3. **Underdeveloped Justification for Worker Performance**: The claim that AND queries are easier for workers ("a worker just need to know one of the labels") isn't well-justified. In reality, an AND query's "no" answer could mean either label is 0 or both are 0, which might be more confusing than a simple "same cluster" answer.

4. **Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper claims "best known algorithms need O(n log n) queries" for two clusters, but doesn't provide a thorough comparison with recent works (e.g., [22, 21]) to establish the novelty of their O(n log(1/δ)) bound.

5. **Small-Scale Experiment**: The real-world experiment with only 200 movies (100 per cluster) is limited in scale and may not generalize to larger, more complex datasets common in practice.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing AND queries as a superior alternative to traditional "same cluster" queries for semisupervised clustering. The theoretical results are sound, and the experimental validation adds practical relevance. However, the conceptual confusion between clustering and labeling is a significant issue that weakens the paper's main claims. The limited discussion of k > 2 and the underdeveloped justification for worker performance are also notable weaknesses.

The paper's strength lies in its novel information-theoretic perspective on a practical crowdsourcing problem, but it would be strengthened by:
1. Clarifying the distinction between clustering and labeling throughout
2. Providing more detailed analysis for k > 2
3. Better justifying why AND queries are easier for workers
4. Including a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods

## Recommendation

**Accept** (with minor revisions)

The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a practical problem with strong experimental validation. While there are some conceptual and technical issues that need addressing, the core contribution is significant and represents a meaningful advancement in the field of crowdsourcing-based clustering. The paper would be strengthened by clarifying the clustering vs. labeling confusion and providing more detailed analysis for k > 2, but these are fixable issues rather than fundamental flaws.

The work is highly relevant to NeurIPS as it bridges information theory, machine learning, and practical crowdsourcing applications—exactly the type of interdisciplinary work the conference values. The theoretical insights have potential to influence future work in both information theory and practical data collection systems.
