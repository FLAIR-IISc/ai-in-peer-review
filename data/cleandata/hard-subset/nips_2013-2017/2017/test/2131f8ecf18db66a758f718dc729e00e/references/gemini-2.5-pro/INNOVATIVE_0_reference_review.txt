### Review

**Summary**
This paper establishes a novel and insightful connection between the problem of semi-supervised clustering via queries and the information-theoretic concept of locally encodable source coding. The authors frame the task of recovering `n` labels using `m` queries, each involving a small number (`Δ`) of elements, as a data compression problem where each compressed bit depends on a limited number of input bits. Using this framework, the paper derives new information-theoretic lower and upper bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery of cluster labels. A key contribution is the introduction and analysis of "AND-queries" as an alternative to the standard "same-cluster" (or XOR) queries. The authors argue, both theoretically and intuitively, that AND-queries are fundamentally more efficient for approximate recovery, especially in the presence of noise. They provide an efficient algorithm for the noisy, approximate recovery setting using AND-queries and validate its performance with a real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

**Strengths**
The primary strength of this paper is its outstanding novelty. The conceptual leap to frame query-based clustering as locally encodable source coding is a bold and powerful move. This is not an incremental improvement on an existing method; it is a fundamental reframing of the problem that opens it up to a new, powerful set of analytical tools from information theory. This perspective is a significant contribution in itself and is likely to inspire a new line of research.

A second major strength is the introduction of AND-queries. The vast majority of work in this area has focused on "same-cluster" queries. The authors make a compelling case that this is a suboptimal choice, particularly for approximate recovery, by connecting it to the limitations of linear codes in rate-distortion theory. The proposed AND-query is a simple, non-linear alternative that elegantly sidesteps these limitations. The theoretical analysis showing its superiority (Theorem 4 and Figure 2) is convincing, and the intuitive argument that AND-queries may be less error-prone for human annotators is insightful and supported by their experiment (105 errors for AND vs. 234 for XOR).

The theoretical results are strong and well-motivated. The paper provides new bounds for exact recovery that, for certain parameter regimes, show that fewer than `n` queries are sufficient (Theorem 1), which is a non-trivial result for non-adaptive queries. The algorithm for approximate recovery in the noisy setting (Algorithm 1) is simple, practical, and comes with strong theoretical guarantees (Theorem 5), demonstrating that a good fraction of labels can be recovered with a sub-linear (in `δ`) number of queries per element.

**Weaknesses**
The main weakness of the paper is the limited scope of its experimental validation. The crowdsourcing experiment is a welcome inclusion and serves as a valuable proof-of-concept. However, with 200 items and 10 volunteers, it is too small to be considered a comprehensive validation. A more thorough experimental section would compare the proposed algorithm against state-of-the-art clustering-with-queries methods on established, larger-scale benchmark datasets. While the paper's focus is clearly theoretical, a stronger empirical demonstration would have elevated the work even further.

Some of the theoretical assumptions, such as i.i.d. priors for labels and a simple symmetric noise model for queries, are simplifications of real-world scenarios. While these are standard assumptions in theoretical analyses and acceptable for a foundational paper like this, their impact on performance in more complex, structured settings is an open question. Similarly, the recovery scheme for the exact noiseless case relies on a Maximum Likelihood decoder, whose computational feasibility is not discussed and is likely prohibitive for large `n`.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a genuinely new perspective on a classic problem. The connection to locally encodable source coding and the introduction of AND-queries are major conceptual contributions that significantly advance our understanding and are likely to be highly influential.
2.  **Bold and Thought-Provoking:** It challenges the community's implicit assumption that "same-cluster" queries are the default choice, backing this challenge with solid information-theoretic arguments. This is precisely the kind of work that stimulates new discussions and research directions.
3.  **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is technically sound and presents a suite of new theoretical results (bounds and algorithms) that are interesting in their own right.

**Arguments against Acceptance**
1.  **Preliminary Experimental Results:** The empirical validation is more of an illustration than a rigorous evaluation against existing methods, leaving the practical performance benefits less established than the theoretical ones.

**Recommendation**
I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The novelty and significance of its core conceptual contributions are exceptional. The act of connecting two seemingly disparate fields—query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding—is a high-risk, high-reward approach that has paid off handsomely here. The paper introduces new ideas, challenges old assumptions, and provides a solid theoretical foundation for future work. While the experimental validation could be stronger, this is a forgivable flaw in a paper that is so rich with new concepts. This is exactly the type of bold, forward-looking research that NIPS should be showcasing.
