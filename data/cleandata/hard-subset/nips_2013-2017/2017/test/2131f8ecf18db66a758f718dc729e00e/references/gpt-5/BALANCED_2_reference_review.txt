Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonadaptive, budget-limited crowdsourced clustering/labeling via pairwise queries and frames it as a locally encodable source coding problem: each query (a compressed bit or symbol) depends on at most Δ labels. This lens yields new information-theoretic lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery, and motivates designing query functions beyond the standard “same-cluster” (XOR) query. The main technical claims are:
- Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2): With pairwise XOR (Δ=2) and a random constant-weight query matrix, one can recover all labels with high probability using m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries, where α = 1/2·(1 + (1−4p(1−p))^Δ). A converse lower bound stronger than n h(p) holds in a parameter range.
- Noiseless, approximate recovery (k=2): Lower bounds for (1−δ)-accurate schemes under locality constraints are given; XOR queries are provably suboptimal for approximation (in line with Massey’s results on linear codes and rate-distortion). A simple pairwise AND query with an elementary decoder achieves strictly better query–distortion trade-offs than XOR for a broad regime.
- Noisy queries, approximate recovery (general k): With a k-ary “AND-like” query that outputs i if both items are label i and 0 otherwise, and a d-regular query graph, a thresholding decoder achieves per-item error exp(−Ω(d)) and thus (1−δ) accuracy with m = Θ(n d) = O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive queries. A small crowdsourcing experiment and synthetic tests support the practicality: workers make fewer mistakes on AND-type questions than same-cluster ones.

The work connects to: nonadaptive clustering with same-cluster queries (e.g., Ashtiani et al., NIPS 2016), query complexity under noisy workers (Mazumdar & Saha, NIPS 2017), coding-theoretic approaches to crowdsourcing (Karger–Oh–Shah; Vempaty–Varshney), and locally decodable/smooth compression (Makhdoumi et al.; Montanari–Mossel; Mazumdar–Chandar–Wornell). The locally encodable source coding angle and the explicit superiority of AND over XOR for approximate recovery appear to be new.

Quality
- Theory: The achievability for exact recovery via random Δ-sparse XOR queries and expurgation is standard in flavor and technically sound in outline; the converse in Theorem 2 is interesting and stronger than the vanilla entropy bound in a regime (p small, Δ=2), though a more detailed derivation and tightness discussion would help. The lower bound for approximate recovery under locality (Theorem 3) is central but its proof is deferred; given its novelty, a clearer sketch in the main text (intuition, key steps) would strengthen confidence. The AND achievability (Theorem 4) is clean and uses standard Poissonization and inclusion–exclusion; the noisy-case analysis (Theorem 5) is plausible via Chernoff gaps on a d-regular graph.
- Notational issues: There is a recurring typesetting confusion “nd2” that is used to denote the number of queries; from the experiment (200 items, d=10 yields 1000 queries), it is clear the authors mean |E| = nd/2, not n·d^2. Please fix consistently; otherwise the stated O(n log(k/δ)) vs O(n log^2(k/δ)) could be misinterpreted. Also, in Theorem 5’s proof the tail bounds should yield e−Ω(d), not e−Ω(d^2).
- Modeling: The noise model assumes independent per-query errors and disallows repeated identical queries, which is standard and justified. The requirement of known priors p is reasonable; Theorem 6 addresses replacement by known cluster sizes.
- Experiments: The small-scale crowdsourcing test is useful qualitatively (AND questions are easier), but quantitative validation is limited (n=200, two classes). The “same-cluster” baseline via spectral clustering under noisy, non-Erdős–Rényi measurements is not necessarily competitive; alternative decoders (e.g., belief propagation/thresholding on the same graph) would be a fairer XOR baseline.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized with clear problem statements and intuition. However:
- Fix the “nd2” notation throughout, and verify all big-O claims match the actual edge count |E|=nd/2.
- The k-ary query definition for k>2 deserves a practical explanation: how is the single k-ary answer collected from workers? Is the interface “which label do both belong to (if any)?” or implemented as k separate binary “both i?” queries? Clarify, as the user burden differs.
- Provide a brief proof sketch of Theorem 3 in the main text (core inequality, dependence on Δ), and a statement clarifying the parameter regimes where Theorem 2 improves n h(p).
- Streamline typos/grammar.

Originality
- The locally encodable source coding framing and derived lower bounds under locality constraints are novel, to my knowledge.
- The identification of XOR’s limitations for approximate recovery via Massey’s linear rate–distortion barrier, and the simple AND-based nonadaptive scheme with provable O(n log(k/δ)) query complexity under noise, are new and conceptually interesting.
- The empirical observation that AND queries are easier for workers aligns with the theory and is a nice supporting point.

Significance
The results advance our understanding of query design in crowdsourced clustering: they show that simple, nonadaptive, pairwise AND queries can dramatically reduce query complexity for approximate recovery under noise, and they introduce a principled framework to derive locality-aware bounds. This has potential impact on practical system design (choosing query types) and on theory (locally encodable codes). The experimental section is modest; broader validation would enhance the practical significance.

Pros
- Clear theoretical contributions with new lower bounds and achievable schemes under locality.
- Insightful and persuasive case for AND queries over XOR for approximate recovery and noisy settings.
- Nonadaptive, scalable scheme with near-linear queries O(n log(k/δ)).
- Connection to coding theory is deep and may spur follow-up work on locally encodable codes.

Cons
- Some critical proofs are deferred; the main text could better convey the core ideas of Theorem 3.
- Notational/typographical issues (nd/2) risk confusion about the main complexity claim.
- Limited empirical evaluation; XOR baseline may be suboptimal; k>2 not validated empirically.
- Practicality of k-ary AND query interface for workers needs clearer justification.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix notation and tail bound exponents; explicitly state |E|=nd/2 and derive m=Θ(n d)=O(n log(k/δ)).
- Add a concise proof sketch for Theorem 3 and a discussion of tightness/gaps vs achievability.
- Clarify how k-ary answers are elicited in practice and, if implemented as multiple binaries, account for that in query complexity.
- Strengthen experiments: larger synthetic tests across p, q, k; compare AND vs XOR with matched decoders (e.g., thresholding or BP), and report constants in the O(·).
- Discuss robustness to prior misspecification and to correlated worker errors.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper offers novel and well-motivated theory with a simple, implementable algorithmic takeaway (ask AND-style queries), and connects two communities (crowdsourcing and information theory). With clarified notation and slightly expanded exposition of key proofs, this would be a solid NIPS contribution.
