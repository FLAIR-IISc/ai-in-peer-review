**Summary of the Paper:**

This paper reformulates the problem of semi-supervised clustering with pairwise queries as a locally encodable source coding problem. The authors analyze the query complexity required to recover n labels belonging to k clusters, where each query can only depend on a small number (Δ) of labels. The paper examines several settings: noiseless queries for exact recovery, noiseless queries for approximate recovery, and noisy queries for approximate recovery. A key contribution is the introduction of "AND" queries as an alternative to the more common "same-cluster" (or XOR) queries. The authors provide information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries and present non-adaptive querying schemes that are shown to be near-optimal in some regimes. For approximate recovery, particularly in the noisy setting, they argue that AND queries are substantially more efficient than XOR queries, requiring only O(n log(k/δ)) queries to recover a (1-δ) fraction of labels correctly. The theoretical claims are supported by a small-scale crowdsourcing experiment.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Theoretical Framing:** The connection between active clustering and locally encodable source coding is novel and provides an interesting information-theoretic perspective on the problem. This framing allows the authors to derive new lower bounds and analyze query types beyond the standard "same-cluster" query.
2.  **Introduction of AND Queries:** The proposal and analysis of AND queries is a significant contribution. The paper makes a compelling case, both theoretically and intuitively, for why AND queries can be more efficient and robust to noise than XOR queries for approximate recovery. The argument that AND queries are cognitively simpler for human workers, supported by their experimental data (105 errors vs. 234 for XOR), is a particularly strong point.
3.  **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper addresses a range of important settings, including exact vs. approximate recovery and noiseless vs. noisy oracles, which provides a fairly complete theoretical picture. The main algorithmic result for the noisy approximate recovery case (Theorem 5) is practical and the O(n log(k/δ)) query complexity is compelling.

**Weaknesses:**

This paper, despite its promising core ideas, is marred by a substantial number of flaws in its presentation, technical clarity, and experimental methodology. These issues, in aggregate, significantly undermine the quality of the submission.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is poorly organized and lacks careful proofreading.
    *   **Formatting:** There is a stray conference header ("1st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)...") at the bottom of the first page, indicating a copy-paste or template error.
    *   **Use of Footnotes:** Critical information is relegated to footnotes, hindering readability. For instance, the distinction between "clustering" and "learning labels" (footnote 1) and the definition of a "typical set" (footnote 4) are central to the paper's setup and proofs, and should be in the main text.
    *   **Citations:** There appear to be duplicate and potentially erroneous citations. References [9] and [10] ("Fault-tolerant entity resolution with the crowd") appear to be the same paper. Similarly, [21] and [22] ("Clustering with noisy queries" and "Query complexity of clustering with side information") seem to refer to the same work by the authors. This suggests a lack of care in preparing the manuscript.
    *   **Missing Figures:** The text references Figures 1, 2, 3, 6, and 7, none of which are included in the paper. This is a critical omission, as these figures are essential for visualizing the performance trade-offs between different query types and comparing bounds (e.g., Figure 2 is meant to show AND queries beating the XOR lower bound). The paper's claims cannot be fully evaluated without them.

2.  **Technical Rigor:** Many of the theoretical arguments are presented as high-level sketches that lack necessary detail for proper verification.
    *   The proof of Theorem 1 is hand-wavy. The inequality used to bound the probability of a "bad event" is not justified, and the expurgation argument is mentioned without any detail. The text "if we take to be of the form n−β for β > 0" is confusingly written and seems to contain a typo where an epsilon symbol was intended but is missing.
    *   The proof of Theorem 4 relies on modeling the node degree with a Poisson distribution. While this is a common approximation, it should be explicitly stated and justified as such.
    *   The proof of Theorem 5 relies on the Chernoff bound, but states the result in terms of undefined constants (c, c', ε, ε'), making it impossible to check the derivation. It then outsources a key concentration argument to a corollary in an external textbook [3] without stating the corollary or its conditions.
    *   The proof of the lower bound in Theorem 3 is deferred entirely to an appendix, which prevents a full assessment of this result.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiment is a small-scale proof-of-concept rather than a rigorous validation.
    *   The dataset is small (n=200) and the number of annotators is low (10 volunteers).
    *   The graph construction method (permuting nodes on a circle and connecting to neighbors) is deterministic given the permutation and may not be representative of a truly random regular graph. The properties of this graph construction are not discussed.
    *   The reported results in Figure 5 are difficult to interpret without the figure itself. The comparison with spectral clustering on synthetic data is mentioned but the corresponding Figure 6 is also missing.

**Decision:**

**Pros for acceptance:**
*   The connection to locally encodable source coding is original and elegant.
*   The introduction and analysis of AND queries is a strong conceptual contribution with practical implications.
*   The finding that human annotators make fewer errors on AND queries is an important empirical insight.

**Cons for acceptance:**
*   The paper suffers from numerous and significant presentation flaws, including formatting errors, missing figures, and careless citation management.
*   The theoretical proofs are sketched in a very high-level and often opaque manner, preventing proper verification of the claims.
*   The experimental validation is preliminary and lacks sufficient scale and rigor to be fully convincing.

While the core ideas in this paper are novel and significant, the execution is deeply flawed. The sheer number of errors, ranging from minor typos to critical omissions like missing figures and unverifiable proof sketches, suggests that the manuscript was prepared in a hurry and is not ready for publication at a top-tier conference like NIPS. The paper reads more like a draft or a technical report than a polished, complete piece of work. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors should be strongly encouraged to perform a major revision to address the clarity, rigor, and presentation issues before resubmitting to a future venue.
