This paper proposes a framework for semi-supervised clustering framed as a locally encodable source coding problem, introducing AND queries as a potentially more efficient alternative to standard ‘same cluster’ queries, particularly in noisy environments. While the motivation – minimizing query cost in crowdsourcing – is relevant, and the connection to information theory is intriguing, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling empirical evidence and a heavy reliance on theoretical results that, while mathematically sound, don’t fully translate into practical advantage without stronger validation.

The core idea of leveraging locally encodable codes for querying is reasonable. However, the paper feels overly focused on theoretical bounds and less on demonstrating a concrete, substantial benefit over existing approaches. The introduction positions the work against a substantial body of research on adaptive querying and crowdsourcing for clustering (references [4, 8, 20, 21, 33]), yet the practical comparison to these methods is limited. The claim that AND queries outperform ‘same cluster’ queries is presented with theoretical justification, but the experimental validation (Section 4) feels somewhat tacked on and doesn't offer a convincing demonstration of the claimed advantages, particularly outside of the synthetic settings.

The theoretical analysis is extensive, with several theorems presented. However, the assumptions underlying these theorems – particularly regarding the prior distribution *p* and the independence of query errors – are strong. The paper acknowledges these assumptions but doesn’t thoroughly explore their sensitivity or robustness. The connection to locally decodable source coding, while mentioned, doesn't seem to lead to any particularly novel insights beyond framing the problem in this context. The discussion of smooth compression feels similarly tangential. 

The clarity is mixed. The mathematical notation is generally standard, but the sheer number of theorems and technical details makes the paper dense and difficult to follow for a reader not already deeply familiar with information theory and source coding.  The motivation and intuition behind some of the choices (e.g., the choice of d-regular graphs in the experimental section) aren't always clearly explained.

The originality is moderate. The idea of using AND queries is novel, but the overall framework builds heavily on existing concepts from information theory and crowdsourcing.  The paper adequately references related work, though a more thorough discussion of existing adaptive querying algorithms would strengthen the positioning of the contribution.

The significance is questionable. While the theoretical results provide interesting bounds, it's not clear whether these bounds translate into practical improvements that would significantly impact real-world crowdsourcing applications.  The experimental results need to be more convincing to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing techniques. The argument that AND queries are ‘easier’ for workers to answer intuitively makes sense, but requires more rigorous support (beyond the observation of fewer errors in the small-scale experiment).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel framing of semi-supervised clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem.
*   Theoretical results providing lower bounds and suggesting the potential benefits of AND queries.
*   Connection to information theory concepts, potentially opening new avenues for research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical validation; the experiment is small-scale and doesn't convincingly demonstrate the advantages of AND queries.
*   Heavy reliance on theoretical assumptions that may not hold in real-world scenarios.
*   The paper feels overly theoretical and lacks a strong practical impact.
*   The advantage over existing clustering/querying algorithms isn’t sufficiently demonstrated. Existing techniques have established baselines that aren’t adequately addressed.
*   The connection to locally decodable source coding feels tenuous and doesn't contribute significantly to the core argument.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents a mathematically sound analysis and introduces a novel idea (AND queries), it lacks the strong empirical validation and practical impact necessary for acceptance at NIPS. The theoretical results are interesting, but the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate that they translate into a significant advantage over existing approaches. The authors need to provide more compelling evidence, through more extensive experiments and a more thorough comparison to relevant baselines, to support their claims. Furthermore, a simplification of the presentation and more focus on the practical implications of the work would improve its accessibility and impact.
