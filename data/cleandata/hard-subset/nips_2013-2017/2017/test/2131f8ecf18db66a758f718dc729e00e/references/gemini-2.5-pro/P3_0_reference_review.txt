**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of semi-supervised clustering, where the goal is to recover the true labels of `n` items using a series of non-adaptive queries to an oracle. The central and most novel contribution is the framing of this problem within information theory as "locally encodable source coding." In this view, the items' labels form a source vector, and the query answers form a compressed representation, where each compressed bit (query answer) depends on only a small, constant number (`Δ`) of source bits (labels).

The paper explores this framework across three settings:
1.  **Noiseless, Exact Recovery:** For binary clustering (k=2), it analyzes the performance of "same-cluster" (XOR) queries. It provides an upper bound on the number of queries needed, showing it can be less than `n` when the cluster sizes are unbalanced, and a new information-theoretic lower bound.
2.  **Noiseless, Approximate Recovery:** The paper argues that XOR queries are suboptimal for recovering a large fraction (1-δ) of labels. It introduces "AND queries" as an alternative and provides a scheme that outperforms the theoretical limits of XOR-based (linear) methods.
3.  **Noisy, Approximate Recovery:** This section presents the main algorithmic contribution. For general `k` clusters, it proposes a generalized AND query and provides a simple, efficient decoding algorithm (Algorithm 1). It proves that this non-adaptive scheme can recover a (1-δ) fraction of labels with `O(n log(k/δ))` queries, even when the oracle answers are noisy. This theoretical result is supported by a small-scale, real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

**Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance of Framing:** The connection to locally encodable source coding is a highly original and powerful perspective on query-based clustering. It moves the problem from ad-hoc algorithmic design to a principled, information-theoretic foundation, enabling the derivation of novel lower bounds and a clearer understanding of fundamental limits. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could influence future work in active learning and crowdsourcing.

2.  **Novelty of AND Queries:** The introduction of AND queries is a key insight. The paper provides compelling theoretical and intuitive arguments for why this non-linear query is superior to the standard "same-cluster" (XOR) query in approximate and noisy settings. The observation that AND queries are asymmetric and potentially easier for human annotators (as suggested by their experiment) is both clever and practical.

3.  **Strong Main Result:** The algorithm for the noisy, approximate recovery setting is a strong and practical result. Achieving `(1-δ)`-approximate recovery with `O(n log(k/δ))` non-adaptive queries is highly efficient, especially given the presence of noise. The simplicity of the decoding algorithm (Algorithm 1) is a major plus, making it readily implementable.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of a real crowdsourcing experiment, while small, adds significant weight to the paper's claims. It not only demonstrates that the proposed algorithm works in practice but also provides empirical evidence for the key hypothesis that AND queries may elicit lower error rates from human workers than "same-cluster" queries. This grounds the theoretical work in a practical reality.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Decoding Complexity for Exact Recovery:** In Section 3.1, the achievability result (Theorem 1) relies on a Maximum Likelihood (ML) decoder. For the proposed linear (XOR) code, ML decoding is equivalent to solving a system of linear equations or finding the nearest codeword, which can be computationally prohibitive (NP-hard in the general case of decoding linear codes). The paper does not discuss the algorithmic feasibility of this decoding step, which is a critical aspect for a practical algorithm. A discussion of polynomial-time decoders, even if they achieve slightly worse rates, would strengthen this section.

2.  **Clarity of Proof Sketches:** Some of the proof sketches are quite dense for a broad audience. For instance, the argument in Theorem 4 relies on a Poisson approximation for the node degrees in the random query graph. While this is a standard technique, a brief justification (e.g., citing relevant random graph theory) would make the argument more rigorous. Similarly, the proof of Theorem 5 relies on Chernoff bounds where the resulting error exponents depend on constants (`c, ε, c', ε'`) that are not explicitly defined in terms of the problem parameters (`p, q, k`), making it hard to fully grasp the dependencies.

3.  **Baseline for "Same-Cluster" Queries in the Noisy Setting:** The experimental comparison in Section 4 uses spectral clustering as the decoder for noisy "same-cluster" queries. While a reasonable choice, it's not explicitly designed to handle the specific noise model. A more direct comparison would be against an algorithm specifically tailored for noisy XOR queries, or a stronger justification for why spectral clustering is a fair and representative baseline. The reported error rate of 23.4% for "same-cluster" queries seems very high and might be more an artifact of the decoder than the query type itself.

4.  **Limited Discussion on Adaptivity:** The paper focuses exclusively on the non-adaptive setting, which is well-justified for parallelizable crowdsourcing. However, a brief discussion contextualizing the `O(n log(k/δ))` query complexity with what is achievable via adaptive querying in the same noisy/approximate setting would help readers appreciate the trade-offs more fully.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper with significant contributions in originality, technical depth, and potential impact. The novel formulation of query-based clustering as locally encodable source coding is a standout feature that provides new tools and insights. The introduction and analysis of AND queries offer a clear and practical improvement over standard approaches in the important noisy and approximate recovery settings. While there are some areas where clarity and completeness could be improved (notably the decoding complexity for the exact case), the core ideas are sound, interesting, and well-supported by both theory and a practical experiment. The paper addresses an important problem, advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, and is likely to be influential.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
