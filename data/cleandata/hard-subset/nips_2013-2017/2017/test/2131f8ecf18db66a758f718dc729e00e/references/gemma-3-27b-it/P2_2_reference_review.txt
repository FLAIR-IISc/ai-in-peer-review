## NIPS 2017 Review: Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the problem of semisupervised clustering, framing it as a locally encodable source coding problem. The authors explore query complexity, particularly focusing on non-adaptive querying schemes for minimizing the number of queries to an oracle (e.g., crowd workers). They demonstrate that ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries are not always optimal and propose using AND queries, which can achieve better performance, especially in noisy scenarios. They provide theoretical lower bounds and algorithmic results, supported by a real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Framing:** The connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is a novel and insightful perspective. This allows the authors to leverage tools and techniques from information theory to analyze the problem.
* **Theoretical Contributions:** The paper provides new information-theoretic lower bounds for query complexity, both in noiseless and noisy settings.  The analysis of AND queries compared to XOR queries is a significant theoretical contribution. Theorem 5 particularly provides a concrete guarantee for noisy environments.
* **Practical Relevance:** The work addresses a practical challenge in crowdsourcing – minimizing query costs. The argument for AND queries being easier for crowd workers to answer is well-reasoned and supported by their experiment.
* **Rigorous Analysis:**  The paper employs a solid mathematical framework and provides detailed proofs for their claims.
* **Experimental Validation:** While limited in scope, the experiment on Amazon Mechanical Turk provides some validation for the proposed AND query scheme.
* **Clear Organization:**  The paper is generally well-organized and the sections flow logically.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Presentation:** The theoretical sections are dense and require a strong background in information theory to fully grasp. This may limit accessibility for a broader NIPS audience. While detail is good, it borders on excessive in places.
* **Experiment Scope:** The crowdsourcing experiment is relatively small-scale and focuses on a specific task (movie clustering). It would be beneficial to see results on more diverse datasets and tasks.  The comparison to other clustering algorithms (beyond spectral clustering) is missing.  The experiment primarily serves to support the claim about worker preference for AND queries; demonstrating a significant advantage in clustering *accuracy* would strengthen the paper considerably.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of independent query errors, while common, may not always hold in real-world crowdsourcing scenarios. The paper acknowledges this, but a more nuanced discussion of potential dependencies would be helpful.
* **Incremental Nature:** While the results are solid, some of the contributions (e.g., the exact recovery results) feel somewhat incremental. The most significant contribution is the analysis and promotion of AND queries, but even this builds heavily on existing work in information theory.
* **Notational Overhead:** The use of multiple notations (X, Y, ∆, k, p, q) can sometimes make the paper difficult to follow.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by analysis and (to a lesser extent) experiments. It represents a complete piece of work within its scope. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations. (Score: 4/5)
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear, but the density of the theoretical sections and the notational overhead can make it challenging to follow.  More intuitive explanations of the information-theoretic concepts would improve clarity. (Score: 3/5)
* **Originality:** The framing of the problem as locally encodable source coding and the analysis of AND queries are original contributions. However, the work builds upon existing foundations in information theory and crowdsourcing. (Score: 3.5/5)
* **Significance:** The results are potentially significant for applications involving costly oracle queries, such as crowdsourcing and entity resolution. The potential for reducing query costs is valuable. The impact would be greater if the experimental validation were more compelling. (Score: 3.5/5)

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Presents a novel perspective on semisupervised clustering.
* Provides rigorous theoretical analysis and new lower bounds.
* Offers a practical improvement (AND queries) with supporting experimental evidence.
* Addresses a relevant problem in crowdsourcing and related fields.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical sections are dense and may limit accessibility.
* The experimental validation is limited in scope.
* Some contributions feel incremental.
* The notational complexity can hinder understanding.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, particularly in its theoretical analysis of query complexity and the promotion of AND queries as a more efficient alternative to XOR queries.  The paper is technically sound and well-motivated. However, I recommend the authors address the weaknesses outlined above by:

1.  Simplifying the presentation of the theoretical results, perhaps by adding more intuitive explanations and examples.
2.  Expanding the experimental evaluation with more diverse datasets and tasks, and including a comparison to other clustering algorithms.
3.  Considering a more nuanced discussion of potential dependencies in query errors.
