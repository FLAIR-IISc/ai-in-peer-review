- Paper frames query-based labeling as "locally encodable source coding," linking query locality to information-theoretic converses.
- For noiseless exact recovery (k=2), authors give an XOR/parity scheme on ∆-tuples achieving m ≈ n(h(p)+o(1))/log(1/α) and a new locality-aware lower bound improving on n h(p).
- For noiseless approximate recovery (k=2), a rate–distortion style converse yields lower bounds, and pairwise AND queries with threshold decoding outperform XOR-based bounds over some regimes.
- For noisy queries and general k, a k-ary AND-like query plus a simple algorithm on a random d-regular graph recovers (1−δ) fraction with m = O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive pairwise queries.
- Small crowdsourcing study (200 movies, 10 workers, 1000 queries) suggests AND queries produce fewer annotation errors than same-cluster (XOR) queries and align with predicted trends.
- Connections drawn to prior work on same-cluster queries, noisy crowdsourced entity resolution, and locality-constrained compression (locally decodable codes).
- Conceptual novelty is high: the locally encodable source coding viewpoint yields meaningful, locality-explicit converses.
- Theoretical contributions include achievability and new converses for exact/approximate recovery and a simple noisy-setting algorithm with provable guarantees.
- Practical insight: AND queries may be easier for human annotators than same-cluster queries; initial experiment supports this.
- Algorithmic simplicity and robustness: count-and-threshold algorithm adapts to unknown priors via cluster-size estimates and uses mild independence assumptions.
- Multiple clarity and notation issues impede readability and reproducibility (notably m = n d/2 miswritten as “nd2”).
- Inconsistencies in log bases and constants (e.g., log vs log2) need unification.
- Theorem 2 and Theorem 3 contain unclear or possibly mistyped expressions and should be restated precisely with parameter domains.
- Practicality concern: k-ary AND-like queries may be cognitively harder than binary questions; need discussion of trade-offs and whether binary variants maintain O(n log(k/δ)).
- Empirical validation is limited: small volunteer pool, simplified task, and suboptimal baselines (spectral clustering on noisy same-cluster answers) limit conclusions.
- Recommend additional experiments: larger synthetic studies for k=2 comparing end-to-end accuracy vs m for AND vs XOR and stronger baselines (MLE/probabilistic models).
- Dependence on priors/noise rates: clarify robustness to mis-specified p and q and whether q can be adaptively estimated from data.
- Some comparisons rely on an unproven conjecture (same-cluster lower bound except at p=1/2); separate unconditional results from conjectural claims.
- Overall technically sound with acceptable proof sketches, but some theorems deferred or terse; polishing statements and fixing typos needed.
- Paper is original and significant: sharpens limits of nonadaptive small-arity queries and introduces a practically motivated query primitive.
- Pros: novel framework, new converses/achievability, simple scalable algorithm, initial empirical support.
- Cons: clarity/typo issues, limited empirical evaluation and baselines, practicality concerns for k>2, some reliance on conjectural comparisons.
- Questions for authors include whether O(n log(k/δ)) holds with binary queries, sensitivity of Algorithm 1 to misspecified q/p and adaptive estimation of q, and provision of unconditional lower bounds ruling out XOR schemes beyond p=1/2.
- Recommendation: Weak accept, conditional on improved clarity, corrected notation, and strengthened empirical evaluation (possibly in supplement).
