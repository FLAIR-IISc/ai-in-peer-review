Summary
The paper studies non-adaptive query-based label learning/clustering with two main angles: (i) an “information-theoretic” framing as locally encodable source coding (each query depends on at most Δ labels), yielding lower bounds; and (ii) new non-adaptive querying/decoding schemes, arguing that pairwise AND queries outperform “same-cluster” (XOR) queries for approximate recovery, including under noise. Main claims: (a) for k=2 and noiseless queries, exact recovery with m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) using Δ-wise XOR queries and an ML decoder (Theorem 1), and a converse improving nh(p) for some p (Theorem 2); (b) for approximate recovery, lower bounds accounting for Δ (Theorem 3), and an AND-based scheme that dominates XOR-based approaches (Theorem 4); (c) with noise and k≥2, a degree-d regular query graph and a simple threshold decoder achieve (1−δ)-accurate recovery with m = O(n log(k/δ)) queries (Theorem 5). A small crowdsourcing experiment on movie genres supports the AND-query approach.

Strengths
- Brings a useful “local encodability” perspective that is natural in query-limited settings and suggests principled lower bounds.
- Highlights limitations of XOR (“same-cluster”) queries for approximate recovery and proposes simple AND-based decoders with intuitive gap-based analysis.
- Attempts to cover both noiseless and noisy settings, binary and multi-class, and provides an easy-to-implement, non-adaptive scheme.
- The small crowdsourcing study is well-aligned with the modeling choice and shows AND queries can be easier for humans.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
Major technical inconsistencies:
- Query count m for the d-regular graph: the paper repeatedly claims m = n d^2, but with pairwise queries on a d-regular graph, |E| = nd/2, hence m = Θ(nd), not Θ(nd^2). This error propagates into Theorem 5, its proof, and discussion (“the total number of queries is m = nd^2”). The experiments actually use m ≈ nd/2 (n=200, d=10 ⇒ m=1000), contradicting the stated nd^2. This is a critical flaw; the claimed O(n log(k/δ)) bound is consistent only if m = Θ(nd), not nd^2.
- Chernoff exponents in Theorem 5’s proof: the bound is written as c k e^{-2 d^2}, but standard concentration yields e^{-Ω(d)}, not e^{-Ω(d^2)}, since there are d independent trials. This appears to stem from the same m vs. d confusion.
- Algorithm 1 thresholds: the expected Nu,i when Xu=i is given as dq/(k−1) + d p_i (1 − qk/(k−1)), but the algorithm sets Ci = dq/(k−1) + (d p_i/2)(1 − qk/(k−1)). The factor 1/2 is unexplained and inconsistent with the expectation used in the proof.
- Section 3.1: base of logarithms is inconsistent (h uses log2; elsewhere natural log and e^· appear). α is defined as 1/2(1 + (1−4p(1−p))^Δ) with typos in superscripts/parentheses; later log2(1/α) vs. log(1/α) is mixed.
- Theorem 1 proof uses expurgation on the typical set; while standard in random coding, the text “throw out 1 label vector” is misleading without clearly tying to average error under the source distribution. A precise probability-of-error statement is needed (e.g., over random Q and random X from p).
- Theorem 2 lower bound expression is hard to parse and likely mis-typed: nh(p)·max{1, max_ρ (1−ρ)/h(((1−ρ) r(p) Δ)/ρ)}. Is r(p) multiplied by Δ or exponentiated? Are logs base 2? Please provide a clean, reproducible bound and derivation (current proof handwaves Hamming ball volumes without careful local-dependency accounting).
- Theorem 3 (approximate recovery LB): only stated, proof deferred; expression contains e^{Δ h′(·)} with unclear log base and missing parentheses. This is central to the “local encodability” message; without a rigorous proof in the main text and correct statement, the contribution is hard to assess.
- Theorem 4 (AND scheme): the final δ expression mixes d (left degree) with a sum over d=1..n and uses a Poisson parameter λ=2m/n but incorrectly enumerates degrees. The first term p e^{-2m/n} is ad hoc; the inclusion-exclusion f(k,d) part needs a clear derivation. As written, the formula is not reproducible; a simpler and correct bound (e.g., via collisions analysis and union bounds) would suffice.
- Noisy model for k=2: with a binary symmetric channel on query outputs, the “baseline” rate of spurious 1’s when the true outcome is 0 is q, which can swamp small thresholds; the reported “theoretical threshold T=2” for d=10 seems unjustified without a precise gap analysis. The text does not reconcile this with the earlier threshold formula.
- The argument that XOR is suboptimal relies on a conjecture (Massey’s) except at p=0.5; yet the narrative suggests a definitive impossibility. Please temper claims or provide a complete bound specific to the constrained local-encoding setting.

Clarity and presentation issues:
- Numerous typos/grammar (“to to,” “will going to recover,” “we emphasize on”), malformed footnotes, and missing/incorrect figure references. Figures 1–6 are mentioned but not present; several claims depend on them.
- Notation overload (k as both number of classes and an index; p vs. p_i; r(p) used with Δ ambiguously). Entropy/log bases are mixed.
- References are inconsistently formatted, some duplicated, some missing bibliographic details; several claims cite arXiv/preprints without precise pointers to the exact results used.
- Important proofs deferred to the supplement with only sketches here; for theoretical contributions, at least one central proof (e.g., Theorem 5 with correct m, d dependencies) should be self-contained.

Relation to prior work:
- The “AND” primitive is reminiscent of group testing and sparse-graph code ideas (e.g., OR/AND tests and peeling decoders). These literatures are not discussed, and drawing connections could strengthen both analysis and positioning.
- “Locally encodable source coding” is related to smooth/locally decodable compression; the distinctions are not made precise enough to identify genuinely new bounds vs. reapplications.

Significance
The topic is timely and relevant to NeurIPS (crowdsourcing, interactive clustering, information-theoretic limits). The idea that certain non-linear queries (AND) can outperform XOR under local constraints is plausible and interesting. However, the current version has substantial technical errors and presentation issues that prevent reliable validation of the key claims.

Pros
- Interesting problem formulation (local encodability) and motivation from crowdsourcing.
- Simple, implementable non-adaptive scheme with empirical support that AND queries are easier for humans.
- Potentially useful lower-bound direction accounting for locality.

Cons
- Critical errors in query complexity accounting (m = nd^2 vs nd/2), leading to incorrect exponents and bounds.
- Inconsistencies in Algorithm 1 and its proof (thresholds, Chernoff rates).
- Overstated claims about XOR limitations relying on conjecture.
- Clarity/notation/formatting problems throughout; missing figures; incomplete proofs in main text.
- Limited experimental validation (small dataset, no comparison to strong baselines beyond a spectral method, no confidence intervals).

Recommendation
Reject. The paper has promising ideas but requires substantial revision to correct technical errors (especially query counts and concentration exponents), provide rigorous and clearly stated proofs/bounds, fix threshold inconsistencies, clean up notation and presentation, and better position relative to group testing and coding literature. With these improvements and stronger experiments, it could be a valuable contribution. Confidence: high.
