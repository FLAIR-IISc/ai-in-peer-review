Summary
The paper studies nonadaptive, budgeted crowdsourced clustering/labeling through an information-theoretic lens. The core idea is to cast query-based labeling as locally encodable source coding: each query/encoded bit depends on at most Δ source bits (labels), unlike classical source coding where codewords can depend on all bits. This framing yields new lower bounds and guides algorithm design.

Contributions:
- Noiseless exact recovery (k=2): With parity/XOR queries on Δ labels (Δ=2 corresponds to “same-cluster”), a random Δ-sparse query matrix and ML decoding recovers all labels with high probability using m ≈ n h(p) / log(1/α) queries, where α depends on Δ and p. For some p, this is strictly below n with Δ=2. A new lower bound that reflects locality constraints can be stronger than n h(p).
- Noiseless approximate recovery (k=2): A locality-aware lower bound on achievable distortion vs. rate, and an argument (via Massey/Ancheta) that XOR (“same-cluster”) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery. A simple pairwise AND-query scheme with local decoding achieves significantly better query–distortion trade-offs than XOR.
- Noisy approximate recovery (general k): Generalizes AND to k-ary “label-AND” queries that output i if both endpoints have label i, otherwise 0. With a d-regular query graph and thresholding, the algorithm recovers a 1−δ fraction with m = O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive queries under an i.i.d. symmetric error model. The method also works when priors are replaced by known cluster sizes. A small crowdsourcing study suggests AND queries are easier for workers than same-cluster queries.

Relation to prior work: Builds on crowdsourced clustering with “same-cluster” queries, budget-optimal crowdsourcing, and local decoding/smooth compression literature. The locally encodable source coding viewpoint and the use of AND queries for improved approximate recovery appear novel. The results relate to classical information-theoretic limits (rate–distortion, Massey’s limitations of linear codes), sparse-graph coding, and entity resolution with noise.

Quality
- Theory: The statements are plausible and technically careful at a high level. Theorem 1 (XOR queries, typical-set plus expurgation) and Theorem 2 (locality-aware converse using participation-count and Hamming ball volume) reflect standard IT/coding proof techniques; they are consistent with intuition about local encoders. Theorem 3 (locality-aware rate–distortion lower bound) is deferred; if correct, it is a substantive contribution. Theorem 4’s AND achievability uses Poissonization and two-hop neighborhood counting; the derived bound matches the simple decoder and aligns with simulations. Theorem 5’s noisy result follows from Chernoff gaps in count statistics and dependency-limited concentration on a sparse graph; scaling m=O(n log(k/δ)) is reasonable and practically attractive.
- Claims vs. assumptions: The independence-of-errors assumption is standard in crowdsourcing models but fragile in practice; the small-scale experiment likely violates independence (10 workers answered many questions), though the findings about AND vs XOR difficulty are still informative. The k-ary symmetric noise model is an abstraction; whether workers make uniformly random mistakes over incorrect labels is debatable. The requirement of priors is mitigated by Theorem 6 (use known cluster sizes with random graph construction).
- Experiments: Limited in scope and scale. The real-data study is small (200 movies, 1000 pairs, 10 workers) and primarily qualitative (AND appears easier). The synthetic comparison with spectral clustering under same-cluster queries is reasonable but not exhaustive. The main value of the paper is theoretical; experiments illustrate rather than validate at scale.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized and readable for an IT/crowdsourcing audience. Some notation could be streamlined (e.g., consistent use of α, r(p), and careful definition of “typical set” without footnotes inside proofs). Several proofs are in the supplement; at least intuitive proof sketches for Theorems 2–3 in the main text would help. Minor typos and formatting issues exist, but do not impede understanding. The k-ary noisy query model should be motivated more explicitly for human computation.

Originality
- The locally encodable source coding lens for nonadaptive querying is original and timely. The locality-aware converse bounds and explicit demonstration that XOR (“same-cluster”) is suboptimal for approximate recovery, together with an AND-based algorithm that achieves O(n log(k/δ)) with noise, constitute novel contributions bridging information theory and crowdsourcing.

Significance
- The theoretical insights are meaningful: they refine fundamental limits for nonadaptive query-based labeling under locality, and they provide actionable guidance (prefer AND over XOR for approximate recovery; use sparse-graph, threshold decoders). For practitioners, the exact constant factors and robustness to realistic worker models remain to be demonstrated, but the direction is promising. The results are likely to stimulate follow-up work on local encoding/decoding, alternative query primitives, and more realistic noise/adversarial models.

Pros
- Clear, principled bridge between clustering-by-queries and locally encodable source coding.
- New locality-aware lower bounds; sublinear-in-n improvements over naive schemes in some regimes.
- Simple, practical AND-based querying and decoding with strong asymptotic guarantees under noise.
- Empirical observation that AND queries can be easier for workers.

Cons
- Experimental validation is small-scale; independence and noise model realism are questionable.
- Some key proofs (notably Theorem 3) are deferred; more main-text intuition would help.
- The k-ary noisy model and the specific query primitive might be restrictive in some applications.
- Exact recovery improvements for XOR depend on priors and are limited in regime.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide a concise proof sketch for Theorem 3 in the main paper and clarify tightness vs. known rate–distortion converse.
- Discuss robustness to nonuniform or worker-specific noise, and to correlated errors.
- Compare to adaptive schemes quantitatively; could a few adaptive rounds reduce constants?
- Explore hybrid query sets beyond AND/XOR, and triadic queries vs. pairwise local encoders.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Strong theoretical contributions with clear novelty and potential impact, albeit with limited empirical support. My confidence is medium-high.
