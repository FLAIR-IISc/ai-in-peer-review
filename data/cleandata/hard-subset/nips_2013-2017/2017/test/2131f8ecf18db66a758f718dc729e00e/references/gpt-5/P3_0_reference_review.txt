Summary
The paper studies non-adaptive crowdsourced clustering/labeling through the lens of locally encodable source coding. Each query is a simple function of at most Δ labels and returns a (mostly binary) response. The authors (i) derive information-theoretic lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery under locality constraints, (ii) show achievability for exact recovery below n queries for k=2 using Δ-ary XOR-type queries and ML decoding, (iii) argue that pairwise “same-cluster” (XNOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery and propose pairwise AND queries that achieve better query–accuracy trade-offs, and (iv) in the noisy setting with k classes, introduce a k-ary “AND-like” query and a simple thresholding decoder over a d-regular graph, achieving (1−δ)-accurate recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries. They complement the theory with a small crowdsourcing experiment (movie genres) indicating that workers make fewer mistakes on AND-type queries than on “same-cluster” queries.

Quality
- Theory: The exact-recovery achievability (Thm 1) uses a sparse random query matrix with Δ ones per row and ML decoding; the expurgation-style argument to obtain injectivity over typical sequences is standard and, under the random-source model, suffices to show recovery with high probability when m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α). The lower bound (Thm 2) leveraging participation counts and Hamming ball packing under locality is interesting and nontrivial; it improves on the naive nh(p) bound in a parameter range. The approximate recovery lower bound under locality (Thm 3) is potentially novel, but the statement is opaque and proof deferred; the expression contains typographical ambiguities (e.g., e^{Δ h′(⋅)}?) that make it hard to parse or verify.
- “Same-cluster” vs AND: The critique of linear (XOR/XNOR) queries for rate–distortion draws on the Ancheta–Massey result; formulating a conjectural lower bound (Conj 1) is fair, though only proven at p=0.5. The AND achievability (Thm 4) analysis on a random bipartite graph with right degree 2 and a simple OR-of-neighbors decoder is reasonable; the combinatorial term f(k,d) accounts for distinct neighbors, and the Poisson left-degree approximation is standard. This establishes a concrete trade-off beating the conjectured XOR bound for a range of parameters.
- Noisy k-class scheme: The query Q(u,v)=i if both endpoints have label i else 0 induces a gap in expected counts that the thresholding decoder exploits via Chernoff bounds. The high-probability bound for the number of errors via bounded dependency neighborhood is appropriate. However, there is a serious notational/technical inconsistency: the paper alternates between m=nd^2 and the correct edge count m=nd/2 for a d-regular graph on n vertices. The experiments (n=200,d=10,m=1000) reflect m=nd/2. The theorem statement and subsequent O(n log(k/δ)) claim are only consistent if m=Θ(nd), not nd^2. This must be fixed; otherwise the claimed scaling is off by a log factor. There are also repeated typos around q/(k−1) vs “qk−1”, and “log kδ” vs log(k/δ), which obstructs verification.
- Experiments: The crowdsourcing study is small but illustrative. However, the comparison between AND and “same-cluster” is confounded: the observed error rates differ drastically (105 vs 234 errors), so comparing downstream clustering accuracy is not an apples-to-apples test of the algorithms but of task difficulty. A fair comparison would match the noise level across query types (e.g., subsample so both have the same q) or simulate equal-q noise on both sets.

Clarity
- The manuscript has numerous typographical errors and notation ambiguities that impede understanding: same-cluster vs XOR conflation, missing fractions (q/(k−1)), missing parentheses in logs, “two-hop neighbors” terminology (really left–right–left paths), and the m=nd^2 vs nd/2 inconsistency. Figures are referenced but not always sufficiently explained. Proofs of key bounds (Thm 3) are deferred; at least a proof sketch is needed.
- The “locally encodable source coding” framing is compelling, but differences from smooth compression [Montanari–Mossel] and locally decodable codes should be more crisply delineated.

Originality
- The locality-constrained source-coding viewpoint for query design in crowdsourced clustering is novel and interesting. The exact-recovery achievability under Δ-local encoding with fewer than n queries (for certain p) and the refined lower bound (Thm 2) appear new. The argument that AND-type queries outperform XOR-type for approximate recovery, together with concrete schemes and analysis, is a useful insight. The noisy k-class thresholding scheme is simple and practical.
- Overlaps not discussed: The AND strategy for k=2 is closely related to non-adaptive group testing with very small pool size (Δ=2), and connections to that literature (e.g., Aldridge–Johnson–Scarlett survey; Chan–Tse) should be acknowledged, including known limits when pool sizes grow. Also relevant are parity-based compressive sensing over GF(2) and sparse-graph code decoding analogies.

Significance
- If corrected, the O(n log(k/δ)) query complexity with pairwise queries and a trivial decoder is practically attractive for large-scale crowdsourcing where parallelism and simplicity matter. The message that query design (AND vs same-cluster) materially affects sample complexity under noise is important for practitioners.
- The lower bounds under locality constraints are of theoretical interest beyond crowdsourcing, informing locally encodable compression limits.

Pros
- Fresh information-theoretic framing (locally encodable coding) with nontrivial bounds.
- Simple, non-adaptive, and practical schemes with provable guarantees; good intuition for why AND beats same-cluster under noise.
- Initial empirical validation and a clear motivation grounded in crowdsourcing practice.

Cons
- Critical inconsistency in query complexity (m=nd^2 vs nd/2) undermines the main scaling claim.
- Clarity issues, typos, and missing proof details (especially Thm 3).
- Experimental comparison between query types is confounded by differing noise levels; baselines are limited.
- Limited discussion of computational tractability for ML decoding in exact recovery.
- Underdeveloped connections to group testing and related literature.

Questions and suggestions
- Please resolve the m=nd/2 vs nd^2 discrepancy throughout (theory, theorem statements, plots, and experiments). With d=Θ(log(k/δ)), is the true query complexity Θ(n log(k/δ))?
- Clarify all notations: same-cluster is XNOR (not XOR), write q/(k−1) explicitly, and use consistent log notation.
- Provide a proof sketch for Thm 3 and clarify the role of e^{Δ h′(⋅)} in the bound.
- Strengthen empirical evaluation: (i) simulate both query types under matched noise q and compare accuracy; (ii) include additional baselines (e.g., majority vote over direct label queries under a budget, or improved decoders for same-cluster); (iii) vary p, q, k to validate predicted scalings.
- Discuss computational aspects: exact ML decoding for XOR-based exact recovery is intractable in general; can one use efficient decoders (e.g., message passing) with performance guarantees?
- Relate to group testing and compressive sensing with small pools; explain how Δ=2 changes the landscape and whether larger Δ yields better trade-offs.

Recommendation
Promising and potentially impactful, but the current version has significant clarity and correctness issues that must be addressed before acceptance.
