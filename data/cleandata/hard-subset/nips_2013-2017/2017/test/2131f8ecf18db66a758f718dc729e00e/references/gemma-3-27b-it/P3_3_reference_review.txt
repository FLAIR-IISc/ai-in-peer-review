## NIPS 2017 Review: Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a theoretically rich investigation into the problem of semisupervised clustering via querying, framing it as a locally encodable source coding problem. The work is strong on theoretical contributions, particularly in establishing lower bounds and demonstrating the advantages of AND-queries over traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries. However, the experimental validation feels somewhat limited and could be strengthened.  I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions, contingent on addressing some concerns outlined below.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of learning labels (or clustering) with limited query access to an oracle. It draws a compelling analogy to source coding, specifically *locally encodable* source coding, which hasn’t been extensively studied in information theory.  The authors correctly identify the recent surge in interest in this area, driven by applications like crowdsourcing and entity resolution (citing relevant work like [33, 30, 8, 20]). They build upon prior work on query complexity for clustering [4] and optimal crowdsourcing schemes [12, 13, 28, 34, 15].  A significant contribution is the critique of XOR queries, drawing on Massey’s work [17] to show their limitations and advocating for the use of AND queries for improved performance.  The consideration of noisy oracles is also a valuable addition, building on existing work on error modeling in crowdsourcing [10, 21, 27].

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novel Framing:**  The perspective of viewing querying as locally encodable source coding is insightful and opens up avenues for applying tools from information theory.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper contains several strong theoretical results, including novel lower bounds on query complexity and provable guarantees for the proposed AND-query based scheme. The proofs appear meticulous and well-structured.
* **AND-Query Insight:** Demonstrating the superiority of AND queries over XOR queries is a key contribution. This challenges conventional wisdom and provides a practical improvement for query design.
* **Noise Handling:**  Addressing the realistic scenario of noisy oracles is crucial and enhances the paper’s applicability. The proposed scheme for dealing with noisy answers and the associated analysis are commendable.
* **Real-world Validation:** The inclusion of a crowdsourcing experiment, even if limited in scope, provides some empirical support for the theoretical findings.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Scope:** The crowdsourcing experiment, while present, feels underdeveloped. The dataset size is relatively small (200 movies), and the experimental setup could be more comprehensive (e.g., comparing different oracle noise levels, varying the graph regularity *d*).  More detailed analysis of the experimental results would strengthen the validation.  The comparison to spectral clustering in Figure 7 (mentioned in the paper) isn't included within the main body, and should be brought up in the core discussion.
* **Complexity of Presentation:** The paper is dense and requires significant background knowledge in information theory and crowdsourcing. While thorough, some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations and examples.  Specifically, the connection between locally encodable source coding and the querying problem isn't always crystal clear.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of independent query errors, while common, might not always hold in practice.  Discussing potential correlations and their impact on the proposed scheme would be beneficial.
* **Lack of Discussion on Computational Complexity:** While the paper focuses on *query* complexity, it lacks discussion on the computational cost of decoding/reconstructing the labels from the query answers, particularly for larger datasets. This is a relevant consideration for practical implementation.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 2:**  The motivation for non-adaptive querying is well-articulated.
* **Section 3.1:** The theorem on exact recovery is interesting, but the practical impact is limited given the tight conditions for perfect oracle accuracy.
* **Section 3.2:** The derivation of the lower bound in Theorem 3 seems complex.  A more intuitive explanation of the key steps would be helpful.
* **Section 3.3:**  The choice of using a d-regular graph is a good one for facilitating parallel querying.  However, the impact of *d* on performance needs further discussion.
* **Experiments:**  What were the specific instructions given to the crowdworkers?  Were they provided with any examples or training?  How was the ground truth for the movie labels established? A more detailed description of the experimental setup would increase confidence in the results.



**5. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Significant theoretical contributions to the field of semisupervised clustering.
* Novel framing of the problem as locally encodable source coding.
* Insightful demonstration of the benefits of AND queries.
* Handles realistic scenario of noisy oracles.
* Contains some experimental validation.

**Con:**

* Experimental validation could be more robust and comprehensive.
* Paper is dense and may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with the field to fully grasp.
* Limited discussion of computational complexity and potential practical limitations.


**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The authors should:

1. **Expand the experimental section:**  Increase the dataset size, explore different noise levels, and provide a more detailed analysis of the results. Include the comparison to spectral clustering in the body of the text.
2. **Improve clarity:**  Provide more intuitive explanations and examples throughout the paper, particularly in sections 3.2 and 3.3.
3. **Discuss computational complexity:**  Add a section discussing the computational cost of decoding and its scalability.
4. **Address the assumption of independent query errors:** Discuss potential correlations and their impact.



This is a valuable contribution with the potential to influence research in semisupervised learning and crowdsourcing. Addressing the aforementioned concerns will further enhance its impact and solidify its position as a strong NIPS paper.
