

# Peer Review: "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of efficient clustering via query-based approaches, particularly in the context of crowdsourcing. The authors propose a novel information-theoretic framework by framing the problem as a locally encodable source coding task. Their main contribution is introducing AND queries as a more efficient alternative to the commonly used "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery. The paper establishes theoretical guarantees for exact and approximate recovery under noiseless and noisy query settings, and validates their approach through a real crowdsourcing experiment.

The work builds upon recent advances in query-based clustering [21, 30, 33] but makes a significant departure by questioning the conventional wisdom that "same cluster" queries are optimal. Instead, they demonstrate that AND queries can achieve substantially better performance, particularly for approximate recovery. The authors also provide new information-theoretic results on locally encodable source coding, which appears to be a novel angle in the information theory literature.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty and Soundness**: The paper makes several strong theoretical contributions. The lower bounds for exact recovery (Theorem 2) are non-trivial and improve upon the naive nH(p) bound for certain parameter ranges. The AND query approach for approximate recovery (Section 3.2) is theoretically well-justified and provides a clear improvement over existing "same cluster" query approaches.

2. **Practical Significance**: The authors convincingly demonstrate that AND queries are easier for crowd workers to answer than "same cluster" queries (105 errors vs. 234 errors in their experiment), which directly addresses a key practical concern in crowdsourcing applications.

3. **Comprehensive Analysis**: The paper covers multiple settings (exact vs. approximate recovery, noiseless vs. noisy queries) with appropriate theoretical analysis. The connection to information theory is well-motivated and provides valuable new insights.

4. **Empirical Validation**: The real-world crowdsourcing experiment with movie classification provides strong empirical support for the theoretical claims, which is particularly valuable in this domain.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable Novelty of "Locally Encodable Source Coding" Framing**: While the paper identifies a connection to locally encodable source coding, this appears to be more of a convenient rephrasing than a fundamentally new theoretical contribution. The authors cite [23] as the only prior mention of the concept, but the focus is primarily on query complexity rather than source coding per se. This framing might be slightly misleading given the minimal contribution to the source coding literature itself.

2. **Limited Scalability Discussion**: The paper doesn't address how their approach scales to very large datasets (n in millions), which is common in real-world clustering applications. The theoretical results are asymptotic, but practical implementation considerations for large-scale problems are not discussed.

3. **Experimental Scope**: The crowdsourcing experiment is limited to 200 movies (100 action, 100 romantic), which is relatively small for a real-world validation. While the results are promising, larger-scale experiments would strengthen the practical claims.

4. **Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper doesn't sufficiently compare with the most recent query-based clustering algorithms. For instance, the paper cites [21] as the best known algorithm for two-cluster clustering, but recent work by [22] and [21] has already established near-optimal query complexity for certain settings.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of query-based clustering by introducing AND queries as a more efficient alternative to the conventional "same cluster" queries. The theoretical results are sound, the practical validation is convincing, and the approach has clear potential for reducing costs in real-world crowdsourcing applications.

The paper's main weakness is the somewhat tenuous connection to "locally encodable source coding" as a theoretical contribution, which appears to be more of a convenient rephrasing than a substantial new insight to the information theory community. However, this doesn't detract from the core contribution of the AND query approach, which is novel and well-justified.

The significance of the work is high, as it directly addresses a practical problem in crowdsourcing with clear theoretical and empirical validation. The reduction from O(n log n) to O(n log(1/δ)) query complexity for approximate recovery is a substantial improvement, and the practical validation with real crowd workers is particularly compelling.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper at NeurIPS. The work presents a novel and practically significant approach to query-based clustering that makes meaningful theoretical contributions while providing strong empirical validation. While the connection to locally encodable source coding might be somewhat overstated, the core contribution of AND queries is well-motivated and impactful.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art query-based clustering algorithms and a discussion of scalability for large datasets, but these are minor concerns that don't undermine the paper's overall contribution.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field that advances the state-of-the-art in query-based clustering for crowdsourcing applications.
