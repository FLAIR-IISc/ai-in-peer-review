Summary
The paper studies nonadaptive, budgeted crowdsourced clustering/label recovery through an information-theoretic lens. The central idea is to cast query-based labeling as locally encodable source coding, where each query/encoded bit depends on only a small number Δ of labels. This viewpoint yields new lower bounds and motivates new query designs. The main algorithmic insight is that AND queries (return 1 only if both labels are 1) can be strictly better than standard “same-cluster” (XOR) queries for approximate recovery: with noise and k classes, a simple thresholding algorithm over a d-regular query graph recovers a (1−δ) fraction of labels with m = O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive pairwise queries. For noiseless binary labels, they also show exact recovery below n queries for a range of priors using random sparse XOR queries and ML decoding, together with a refined lower bound that can beat the classical nH(p) bound in a certain regime. They support the approach with a small real crowdsourcing experiment indicating that workers make fewer mistakes on AND queries than on same-cluster queries.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Framing query-based clustering as locally encodable source coding is elegant and new (to my knowledge). The paper connects active/crowdsourced clustering to local coding constraints and rate–distortion, and derives lower bounds that explicitly reflect local encodability.
- Bold, simple primitive: Promoting AND queries over same-cluster (XOR) for approximate recovery is a crisp, actionable idea with strong intuition (asymmetric evidence), theoretical support, and preliminary empirical validation.
- Improved query complexity: For noisy, multiclass labels, the nonadaptive AND-based scheme achieves m = O(n log(k/δ)) to recover (1−δ) of labels, improving over O(n log n) behaviors known for XOR in some settings, and providing a clear dependence on δ.
- Theoretical contributions: Multiple new bounds (exact and approximate settings; with/without noise), a discussion relating Massey/Ancheta limits for linear codes to XOR queries, and a decoding analysis over sparse random graphs.
- Practical relevance: The real crowdsourcing study (albeit small) supports the claim that AND queries are easier for workers and less noisy.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Notational inconsistency on query count: The paper repeatedly writes the number of queries as “nd2”. For a d-regular undirected graph queried on all edges, the number of queries is |E| = nd/2, not nd^2. The experiments with n=200, d=10 yield 1000 queries, matching nd/2. Please standardize notation throughout (m = nd/2), and ensure Theorem 5’s O(n log(k/δ)) follows from d = Θ(log(k/δ)).
- Rigor in Theorem 1 (expurgation): The argument “throwing out” label vectors in the typical set needs to be framed in terms of average probability of error over the source distribution (vanishing probability mass of the expurgated subset), not as eliminating codewords. A clearer statement/justification of how the decoder achieves vanishing error probability under the Bernoulli prior would help.
- Approximate recovery lower bound (Theorem 3): The result is central to the local-encodability story but the proof is deferred and the statement is hard to parse (and uses h′). A sketch with intuition and a discussion of its tightness versus standard rate–distortion would strengthen the paper.
- Dependence assumptions and noise model: The k-ary symmetric noise on the AND-like query may be unrealistic in some crowdsourcing settings (class-dependent confusion, adversarial outliers). A robustness discussion or simulations under alternative noise models would increase impact.
- XOR limitations rely partly on a conjecture: The lower bound for “same-cluster” (Conjecture 1) is not fully established except at p=0.5. The empirical/simulation comparison with strong decoders for XOR (beyond random queries and ML) would better substantiate the claim that AND dominates across regimes.
- Clarity/organization: There are several typos and footnote formatting issues; some definitions (e.g., “two-hop neighbor” in the AND analysis) could be made more precise. A brief “roadmap” and a consolidated notation table would help readability.

Quality
Overall technically solid with several nontrivial bounds and analyses. Some proofs are deferred; the main algorithm is simple and clearly analyzed. A few arguments (Theorem 1 expurgation, Theorem 3 placement) would benefit from tighter exposition and consistency in counting queries.

Clarity
Good high-level storytelling, but the paper would benefit from cleaner notation and correction of m = nd/2 vs nd^2. More intuition around key bounds (esp. Theorem 3) would aid readers.

Originality
High. The local encodability framing and the advocacy for AND queries as a primitive for crowdsourced clustering are fresh and impactful. The lower bounds under local constraints and the O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive scheme are significant.

Significance
High. This work can influence how practitioners design queries for crowdsourced clustering/ER, and opens a line of inquiry on locally encodable source coding. The simplicity of the proposed scheme and its robustness to noise are attractive.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Please correct and unify the counting of queries (m = nd/2) and verify all statements depending on m, d, and δ.
- Provide a concise proof sketch or additional intuition for Theorem 3 in the main text.
- Clarify the expurgation step in Theorem 1 in terms of average error under the source distribution.
- Discuss whether multiway (Δ>2) AND-like queries provide further gains, and how results compare to triangle queries [31].
- Explore non-symmetric/noisy worker models (e.g., varying expertise) and whether thresholds can be adapted.
- Expand the empirical section with simulations comparing AND vs XOR under matched budgets across a range of p, q, δ and graph designs.

Reasons to accept
- Novel conceptual bridge (locally encodable source coding).
- Clear, simple, and effective new query primitive (AND) with strong theoretical guarantees.
- Improved query complexity for approximate recovery under noise; nonadaptive and practical.
- Preliminary empirical support and good potential impact on crowdsourcing practice.

Reasons to reject
- Notational/confusion issues around query counts; some proofs deferred or not fully rigorous in presentation.
- Limited empirical validation; noise model may be simplistic.
