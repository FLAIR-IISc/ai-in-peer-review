Summary
The paper studies non-adaptive crowdsourced clustering/labeling through the lens of locally encodable source coding: each query is a small function of at most Δ labels with a binary or k-ary response. The authors (i) derive information-theoretic lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery under locality constraints, (ii) present near-matching achievability schemes, (iii) argue that “same-cluster”/XOR queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery, and (iv) propose AND-based pairwise queries that, together with a simple thresholding decoder on a sparse d-regular graph, achieve O(n log(k/δ)) queries to recover a (1−δ) fraction of labels even with symmetric noise. They also report a small crowdsourcing experiment where AND queries elicit fewer errors than same-cluster queries.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to entity resolution with oracle queries and clustering with pairwise “same-cluster” questions, locally decodable/“smooth” compression, and rate–distortion. The locally encodable viewpoint is natural and under-explored. The use of Massey/Ancheta bounds to critique XOR-based approximate recovery is appropriate, though the generalization beyond p=1/2 is treated as a conjecture. The work omits connections to group testing (non-adaptive tests with OR/AND-like primitives), which is highly relevant to their AND construction and analysis.

Quality and technical soundness
- Theorem 1 (noiseless, exact): The random Δ-sparse parity (XOR) measurement ensemble and ML over the typical set yields m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) with α=½(1+(1−4p(1−p))^Δ). The high-level approach (collision analysis + expurgation) is standard. However, the argument as written conflates existence via expurgation with performance under the true source distribution. To translate a bound on the number of collisions among typical sequences into a high-probability recovery guarantee, one needs a clear averaging over X~Bern(p)^n and careful control of atypical sequences. The “throw out 1 label vector per bad event” phrasing is not a valid decoding procedure—one cannot remove source sequences. The result is likely correct with a refined union bound/typicality argument, but the current proof sketch is imprecise.

- Theorem 2 (noiseless, exact lower bound under locality): The “low-degree fraction” argument and Hamming ball bound are interesting but underdeveloped. The bound on the radius ((1−ρ) r(p) Δ m / ρ) and subsequent entropy calculation require a more careful dependence analysis (which queries are affected by variations on S, double counting, constants). As presented, the rigor is insufficient.

- Theorem 3 (noiseless, approximate lower bound): The result is potentially important (locality-degraded rate–distortion), but the proof is deferred and the formula is not interpretable at a glance. A proof sketch and elucidation of the correction term would help.

- Conjecture 1 (XOR limitation): Using linear rate–distortion limitations to argue about same-cluster queries is compelling. However, several comparisons in the text/figures appear to rely on the conjecture; claims that XOR is strictly dominated should be qualified accordingly.

- Theorem 4 (noiseless, AND approximate): The construction (right-degree 2, random pairing; declare 1 if any incident query returns 1) is simple and appealing. The analysis (Poisson left degree, counting distinct two-hop neighbors via inclusion–exclusion f(k,d)) is reasonable; only false negatives occur. A simpler and more interpretable upper bound (avoiding the f(k,d) exact combinatorics) would be welcome.

- Theorem 5/Algorithm 1 (noisy, k-ary AND-generalization): The thresholding on a d-regular graph with Chernoff bounds yields node-wise error exp(−Ω(d)), so d=Θ(log(k/δ)) suffices. However, there are serious inconsistencies:
  - The paper repeatedly claims the number of queries is n d^2, while a d-regular simple graph on n vertices has |E|=nd/2, i.e., m=Θ(nd). The proof later sets m=nd^2=O(n log(k/δ)), which is dimensionally wrong. This must be corrected throughout (and will improve constants).
  - The dependency bound for concentration over all nodes uses “Δ* ≤ d + d” (1-hop and 2-hop neighbors), but 2-hop neighborhoods are O(d^2) in the worst case; the stated bound is incorrect. A standard dependency graph/Janson inequality with Δ*=O(d^2) still gives concentration, but the current text needs fixing.
  - Practicality: the k-ary query Q(X,X′)=i if both equal i (else 0) demands the worker identify which specific class both belong to. This contradicts the earlier claim that AND is easier because a “no” requires knowing only one label; in the k-ary setting, deciding i requires strong knowledge of both items. The empirical section evaluates only k=2.

Clarity
The manuscript has many typos, notation inconsistencies, and missing details:
- Logs sometimes base-2, sometimes unspecified; α’s definition is unclear at first mention.
- Inconsistent use of Δ vs d.
- The m=nd^2 vs nd/2 confusion appears multiple times.
- Several figures are referenced but not described sufficiently; proofs are deferred without a sketch.
- Footnotes and references have duplication/misformatting.

Originality
- The locally encodable source coding framing and locality-aware lower bounds for approximate recovery are interesting and, to my knowledge, novel in this precise context.
- The AND-query primitive and simple threshold decoder on sparse graphs is a neat twist with clear intuition.
- However, parts of the critique of XOR rely on a conjecture, and the lack of connection to group testing and sparse-graph coding literature for inference weakens the novelty framing.

Significance
The work advances our understanding of what non-adaptive, local queries can achieve for semisupervised clustering/labeling and suggests practically useful primitives (AND). If polished, the theory could influence the design of crowdsourced systems and inspire further locality-constrained information-theoretic results. As written, the impact is limited by technical gaps and presentation.

Pros
- Fresh “locally encodable” viewpoint; nontrivial lower bounds under locality.
- Simple, scalable AND-based algorithms with strong asymptotics for approximate recovery under noise.
- Empirical indication that AND questions may be easier for humans than “same-cluster.”

Cons
- Serious technical inconsistencies (m=nd^2 vs nd/2; dependency degree).
- Key claims about XOR limitations hinge on an unproven conjecture (beyond p=1/2).
- Several proofs are sketchy or deferred; rigor needs strengthening.
- Experiments are minimal, small-scale, and do not evaluate multi-class k>2.

Questions/requests for the authors
1) Fix the query-count throughout and re-derive m vs d vs δ clearly. Provide full proofs (or detailed sketches) for Theorems 1–3 and correct the dependency bound in Theorem 5.
2) Can you provide a rigorous XOR lower bound for approximate recovery without relying on Conjecture 1, at least in some regimes of p, δ?
3) Please discuss connections to group testing and whether OR-type queries would yield similar/better tradeoffs.
4) Clarify practicality of k-ary AND queries for k>2; any user study beyond binary?
5) Unify notation, log bases, and provide tighter, more interpretable bounds for Theorem 4.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The core ideas are promising and likely publishable with substantial revisions, but the current version has correctness issues, incomplete proofs, and clarity problems that prevent a confident acceptance.
