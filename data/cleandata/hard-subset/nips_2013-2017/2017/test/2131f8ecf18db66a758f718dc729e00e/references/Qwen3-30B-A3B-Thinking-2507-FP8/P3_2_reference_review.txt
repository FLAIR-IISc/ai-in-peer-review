

# Peer Review: "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper presents an intriguing theoretical framework for clustering problems by reframing them as locally encodable source coding problems. The authors make a novel connection between clustering with pairwise queries and information-theoretic concepts in source coding, introducing AND queries as a more efficient alternative to traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries. The work is well-motivated by practical crowdsourcing applications where query efficiency and worker accuracy are critical.

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

The paper's core insight is recognizing that clustering problems can be viewed as a special case of locally encodable source coding, where each compressed bit (query answer) depends on only a constant number of source bits (element labels). This perspective enables the authors to derive new information-theoretic bounds and propose more efficient querying strategies. While related work has studied clustering via pairwise queries (e.g., [4, 8, 21]), this paper uniquely identifies AND queries as superior to XOR queries for approximate recovery, which is a significant departure from existing approaches. The connection to source coding is particularly novel, as the authors note that "locally encodable source coding" has received little attention compared to locally decodable codes.

**Strengths**

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides rigorous information-theoretic bounds for both exact and approximate recovery. Theorem 1 establishes a new upper bound that is strictly better than the naive n queries for certain prior distributions (p ≥ 0.0694 when k=2). Theorem 2 provides a stronger lower bound than the standard entropy bound for small p. Most importantly, Theorem 4 demonstrates that AND queries can achieve significantly better performance than XOR queries for approximate recovery.

2. **Practical Insight**: The authors make a compelling case that AND queries are more intuitive for crowd workers than "same cluster" queries. Their experimental results (Figure 5) confirm this intuition, showing that AND queries yield fewer errors (105 vs. 234 errors) in their real-world experiment with IMDB movie labels.

3. **Algorithmic Contribution**: Algorithm 1 for noisy queries with k ≥ 2 clusters is elegant and efficient, requiring only O(n log k/δ) queries. The proof of Theorem 5 is particularly strong, using concentration bounds to establish the error probability scaling.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Experimental Validation**: While the real crowdsourcing experiment is a nice touch, it's limited to a single dataset (100 action/100 romantic movies) with only 10 volunteers. A more comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets and larger crowdsourcing samples would strengthen the practical claims. The comparison with "same cluster" queries in Figure 7 is promising but based on a synthetic dataset.

2. **Technical Accessibility**: The paper is quite dense, particularly in the proofs of Theorems 2-4. While the technical depth is appropriate for NIPS, some intuitive explanations of why AND queries outperform XOR queries would help readers grasp the core insight more quickly.

3. **Related Work**: The connection to "smooth compression" [23] is mentioned but not thoroughly explored. The authors should more explicitly contrast their locally encodable coding model with the well-studied locally decodable codes [19, 16] to clarify the novel contribution.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The paper assumes that the prior distribution p is known, which is reasonable for many applications, but doesn't address how to estimate p in practice. While they mention subsampling, a more detailed discussion of this step would be helpful.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by reframing clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem and demonstrating the superiority of AND queries over traditional approaches. The theoretical results are sound, the connection to practical crowdsourcing is well-motivated, and the experimental validation provides empirical support for the claims.

The work advances the state of the art in several ways: it provides new information-theoretic bounds for clustering with pairwise queries, introduces a more efficient query type (AND) that outperforms existing approaches, and establishes a novel connection between clustering and source coding. These results are important for both theoretical computer science and practical applications in crowdsourced data labeling.

While the experimental validation could be strengthened with larger-scale studies, the theoretical contribution is substantial and the practical implications are clear. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a genuine contribution to the field.

**Recommendation**: Accept. This paper presents a novel and significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of clustering with pairwise queries, with clear practical implications for crowdsourcing applications. The work is well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental validation, and makes a meaningful advance over previous approaches.
