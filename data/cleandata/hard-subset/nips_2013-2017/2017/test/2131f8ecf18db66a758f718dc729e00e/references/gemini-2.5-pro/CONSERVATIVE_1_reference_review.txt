This paper frames the problem of semi-supervised clustering via oracle queries within the information-theoretic context of source coding. Specifically, it introduces the notion of "locally encodable source coding," where each query (an encoded bit) depends on a limited number of labels (source bits). The paper analyzes this problem under three settings: noiseless queries for exact recovery, noiseless queries for approximate recovery, and noisy queries for approximate recovery. The primary contributions are a set of information-theoretic upper and lower bounds on the required number of queries and, most notably, the introduction of a novel "AND query" as an alternative to the standard "same-cluster" (or XOR) query. The authors argue that this AND query is theoretically and practically superior for approximate recovery, especially in the presence of noise, and propose an algorithm that achieves `(1-δ)` recovery with `O(n log(k/δ))` queries.

**Strengths:**
1.  The formulation of query-based clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem is a novel and potentially insightful perspective. It allows the authors to leverage information-theoretic tools to derive new lower bounds on query complexity, which advance the theoretical understanding of the problem.
2.  The paper correctly identifies a key limitation of standard "same-cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery, linking them to the known inefficiency of linear codes for rate-distortion problems. This provides a solid theoretical motivation for exploring alternative query types.
3.  The proposed algorithm for the noisy, approximate recovery setting (Algorithm 1) demonstrates a significant asymptotic improvement in query complexity (`O(n log(k/δ))`) over existing methods that require `O(n log n)` queries. This is the paper's strongest theoretical contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The primary weakness of this work lies in the empirical validation, which is not commensurate with the paper's strong theoretical claims. The real-world crowdsourcing experiment is conducted on a small scale (n=200, 10 volunteers). While commendable for attempting to ground the theory in reality, this scale is more akin to a preliminary pilot study than a robust validation suitable for a top-tier publication. Conclusions drawn from such limited data are necessarily tentative.
2.  **Unsupported Claims about Human Factors:** The paper makes the strong claim that AND queries are easier for crowd workers to answer than same-cluster queries, citing a lower error rate (105 vs. 234) in their small experiment. While the rationale provided is plausible, this is a cognitive claim that requires a carefully designed user study to substantiate. As presented, it remains an interesting but unproven observation. Without more rigorous evidence, the practical advantages of the AND query are speculative.
3.  **Reliance on Heuristics in Analysis:** The theoretical analysis, while providing valuable high-level insights, relies on approximations that are not fully justified. For example, the proof of Theorem 4 models the node degree in a random regular graph with a Poisson distribution. This is a well-known heuristic used for convenience, but it is not formally correct and the impact of this approximation on the tightness of the resulting bounds is not discussed. A more rigorous analysis would be required for the results to be considered fully sound.
4.  **Limited Practicality of the Noiseless Exact Recovery Scheme:** In Section 3.1, the proposed scheme for exact recovery using XOR queries requires Maximum Likelihood decoding, which is computationally intractable in general. Furthermore, it only outperforms the trivial `n-1` query baseline for a limited range of prior probabilities `p`. A simple, deterministic algorithm is often preferable to a complex randomized one with marginal and conditional performance gains.
5.  **Incomplete Experimental Comparisons:** The comparison against the spectral clustering baseline is performed on synthetic data. It is unclear why this comparison was not also performed on the real crowdsourced dataset the authors collected. A direct comparison on the same real-world data would have been much more compelling. Additionally, the performance of Algorithm 1 is shown for several thresholds in Figure 5, but the paper only discusses the theoretically derived threshold (T=2) and does not explain the performance of the others.

**Summary and Recommendation:**
This paper presents a theoretically interesting direction by connecting query-based clustering with source coding and proposing a novel query type. The asymptotic results for the noisy approximate recovery setting are promising. However, the work in its current form feels preliminary. The central, unconventional idea—the superiority of the AND query—is not supported by sufficient empirical evidence. The experimental validation is too limited in scale to be convincing, and some claims about practical usability are unsubstantiated. While the theoretical ideas have merit, the paper's contributions are undermined by a lack of methodological rigor in the experimental design and parts of the analysis.

For these reasons, I am hesitant to recommend acceptance. The paper introduces novel ideas, but they are not yet proven to the high standard expected at NIPS. The work would be significantly strengthened by: (1) a large-scale empirical study on multiple real-world datasets to robustly validate the proposed algorithm, (2) a formal user study to properly investigate the cognitive load and error profiles of AND vs. XOR queries, and (3) a more rigorous theoretical analysis that addresses the use of simplifying heuristics.
