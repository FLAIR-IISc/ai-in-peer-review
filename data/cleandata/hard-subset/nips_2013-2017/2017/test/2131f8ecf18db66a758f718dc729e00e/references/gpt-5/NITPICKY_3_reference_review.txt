Summary
This paper studies nonadaptive query-efficient learning of labels/clustering via a new information-theoretic lens: locally encodable source coding. The authors consider k-class labeling with priors, binary or k-ary queries on small tuples (mostly ∆=2), and independent per-query noise. Main claims:
- Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2): with ∆-XOR queries (same-cluster), one can recover with m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) < n for some p and ∆; lower bounds tighter than n h(p) in some regimes.
- Noiseless, approximate recovery: lower bounds under local encodability constraints; arg that XOR is suboptimal for approximate recovery (via Massey/Ancheta limitations on linear rate-distortion); propose AND queries; provide performance bound for a simple decoder.
- Noisy, approximate recovery (k ≥ 2): propose a k-ary AND-based query (return i iff both endpoints have label i, else 0) with symmetric noise; a local thresholding decoder on a d-regular graph achieves (1−δ)-accuracy with m = O(n log(k/δ)).
- A small crowdsourcing experiment suggests AND queries are easier and empirically achieve lower error than same-cluster queries, with a synthetic comparison to spectral clustering.

Strengths
- Fresh and interesting perspective connecting nonadaptive crowdsourced clustering to locally encodable source coding and rate–distortion.
- The intuition that AND queries can outperform XOR for approximate recovery (especially under noise) is compelling and practically motivated; simple local decoders are attractive.
- Attempts at both information-theoretic bounds and algorithms; combines theory with a small real-data test.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and rigor:
- Inconsistencies in query complexity: The noisy algorithm section repeatedly states “nd^2 queries” while the construction is a d-regular graph with queries on edges, which should yield m = |E| = nd/2, not nd^2. The title of Algorithm 1 and subsequent claims (e.g., Theorem 5 proof) depend on this. This must be corrected; as written the scaling is contradictory.
- Threshold expectation Ci in Algorithm 1 is derived incorrectly. Under the stated noise model (with true output preserved with prob 1−q and otherwise uniformly flipped among the other k−1 outputs), the expected increment for Nu,i when Xu=i should be d [ q/(k−1) + p_i ((1−q) − q/(k−1)) ], not the provided expression (which oddly contains a factor 1/2 and “1− q k/(k−1)” without clear parentheses). This error affects the gap analysis and the Chernoff bounds, hence Theorem 5.
- Theorem 5 proof is a sketch with problematic steps: assumes independence that does not hold (Nu,i counts share the same d edges for node u), uses vague constants “c, c′,” and concludes “Z = EZ almost always” from a dependency graph bound (should claim concentration around the mean). The dependence neighborhood bound “≤ d + d” is asserted with no justification; two-hop dependency needs care.
- Theorem 1 proof has a confusing “expurgation” argument. One should expurgate bad query matrices, not “expurgate label vectors.” As written: “for every ‘bad event’, we can throw out 1 label vector” is not meaningful (the source is not under our control). The typical-set collision probability calculation also includes notational inconsistencies (r(p), α), and the expected number of bad events is written incorrectly (missing a choose(T,2) factor).
- Theorem 2 (lower bound) is an imprecise sketch: choice of ρ, the projection argument, radius (1−ρ) r(p) ∆ m / ρ, and the entropy bound h((1−ρ) r(p) ∆ / ρ) lack derivation and conditions. Symbols and steps are introduced without precise definitions; the final bound’s optimization over ρ is not analyzed.
- Approximate recovery and XOR limitation: the claim relies on a “conjecture” attributed to Massey; the citation [17] is about joint source-channel coding and does not clearly support the stated lower bound. The known result at p=1/2 is referenced but not proved or cited precisely. Without a formal lower bound, the “XOR is suboptimal” claim remains largely heuristic.
- Theorem 4 (AND, noiseless): the Poissonization and two-hop neighbor counting use an occupancy-type formula f(k,d) but ignore dependencies from sampling pairs without replacement; the derivation is terse, and no finite-n or asymptotic error term control is provided. It reads more like a heuristic than a theorem-proof.

Clarity and presentation:
- Numerous formatting/typographical issues hinder readability: dangling sentences, inline footnotes (1,2,3) breaking flow, missing or inconsistent parentheses in formulas (e.g., “log2 1 α”, “1− qkk−1”), undefined symbols, inconsistent use of logs, and frequent notational changes (r(p) vs 2p(1−p)). Figures are heavily referenced but not present; several references are incomplete or duplicated; many lack years/venues.
- Algorithm 1: order of checking i=1..k−1 biases toward lower indices; tie-breaking is not specified. The requirement “d even” appears without rationale.
- Computational aspects of Theorem 1 (ML decoding over parity constraints) are ignored; ML is intractable in general. The paper should clarify whether computational efficiency is a goal.

Experimental evaluation:
- Very small and ad-hoc: 200 items, 10 volunteers, 1000 queries; no confidence intervals; thresholds tuned; and only one type of AND question (“both action?”) was used—this asymmetrically favors the positive class and may inflate perceived ease vs “same cluster.” For fairness, also ask “both romantic?” and/or balance pairs.
- The baseline for “same cluster” uses spectral clustering on a sparse, noisy graph; this is not a direct competitor to the proposed local decoder. More appropriate baselines: majority vote of direct label queries, Dawid–Skene-type models, or ER algorithms with noise-aware likelihoods. Also, note that direct label queries would require ≤ n queries, while the experiment uses 1000 queries, so the efficiency claim is not compelling in this setup.

Originality and significance:
- The locally encodable source-coding viewpoint for crowdsourced queries is interesting and potentially impactful.
- However, the central theoretical claims (sub-n XOR exact recovery, XOR lower-bound for approximate recovery, O(n log(k/δ)) noisy scheme) need clean, correct, and complete proofs; as written, multiple technical errors and inconsistencies undermine confidence. Without a solid XOR lower bound, the AND-vs-XOR superiority remains suggestive rather than definitive.

Pros
- Interesting and timely problem; novel framing.
- Simple, implementable AND-based nonadaptive scheme with intuitive advantage in noisy settings.
- Attempt to bridge information-theoretic limits and practical algorithms.

Cons
- Multiple technical errors (query count, thresholds, proofs) and unclear/incorrect derivations.
- Poor writing/formatting; inconsistent notation; incomplete references.
- Weak, small-scale experiments; limited and arguably unfair baselines.
- Computational intractability of ML decoder for exact recovery ignored.

Recommendation: Reject
The paper has promising ideas, but the accumulation of technical inaccuracies, inconsistencies (notably m=nd^2 vs nd/2 and incorrect expectations for Ci), and presentation issues prevents me from trusting the results. I encourage the authors to:
- Rigorously fix Theorems 1–5 (correct counts/thresholds, provide precise, dependency-aware proofs, and correct the expurgation argument).
- Either provide a formal XOR lower bound for approximate recovery or clearly limit claims.
- Improve clarity, notation, and references; present all figures; and include computational considerations.
- Strengthen and balance experiments with fair baselines and larger-scale tests.
With these addressed, the contribution could be significant for the community.
