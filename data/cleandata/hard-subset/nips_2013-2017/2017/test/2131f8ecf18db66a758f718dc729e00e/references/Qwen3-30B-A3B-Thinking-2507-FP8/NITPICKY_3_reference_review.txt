

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper explores the problem of clustering via pairwise queries, introducing AND queries as an alternative to traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries. The authors frame the problem as a locally encodable source coding problem, providing theoretical bounds and algorithms for exact and approximate recovery under noise.

## Strengths
The paper makes a novel connection between clustering with pairwise queries and locally encodable source coding. The theoretical results in Sections 3.1-3.2 are interesting, particularly the demonstration that AND queries can outperform XOR queries for approximate recovery. The experimental validation on a real crowdsourcing dataset, while limited, provides some empirical support for the theoretical claims.

## Major Weaknesses

### 1. Technical Inconsistencies and Errors
The paper contains numerous technical errors that undermine its credibility:

- Theorem 1 states "α = 12 (1 + (1 − 4p(1 − p)) ∆)" but uses "12" instead of "1/2" (a clear typesetting error).
- Theorem 2's lower bound has inconsistent notation: "h( (1−ρ)r(p)∆ ρ )" is missing parentheses around the argument of h.
- In Theorem 5's proof, the inequality "Pi ≤ cke−2d 2" has unclear notation - should be "e^(-2d^2)".
- Theorem 5's statement "d scales as O(log kδ )" should be "O(log(k/δ))".
- The paper states "the only thing that remains to be proved is that the number of incorrect labels is δn with high probability" but merely references "Corollary 4.3.5 in [3]" without providing the full citation or explanation.

### 2. Clarity and Explanatory Deficiencies
- The connection between "locally encodable source coding" and the clustering problem is not clearly articulated. The paper claims this notion is "not well-studied" but references [23] as mentioning it, without explaining how their work differs.
- The explanation for why AND queries have lower error rates than "same cluster" queries is misleading. The paper states "for a correct 'no' answer in an AND query, a worker just need to know one of the labels in the pair" - this is incorrect. To say "no" for an AND query (meaning not both are 1), the worker must know that at least one is 0, which may require knowing both labels if they're unknown.
- The typical set is defined in a footnote as "all such label vectors where the number of ones is between np− n2/3 and np+ n2/3" but this should be formally defined earlier in the paper.

### 3. Formatting and Presentation Issues
- "to to graph clustering" (double "to") in the first paragraph.
- "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems" should be "The Conference" or "NIPS".
- "The compressed vector is not necessarily binary, nor it is necessarily smaller length." (grammatical error - should be "nor is it necessarily smaller in length").
- The paper references "the appendix in the supplementary material" without mentioning supplementary material in the main text.

### 4. Experimental Limitations
- The real-world experiment uses only 10 volunteers on a small dataset (200 movies), which is insufficient for robust validation.
- The paper states "the gap between the theoretical result and the experimental results is small" but provides no quantitative measure of this gap.
- The comparison with "same cluster" queries on the synthetic dataset (Figure 6) lacks statistical significance testing.

## Minor but Notable Issues
- The paper claims "a smaller number of queries can be achieved for approximate recovery by using what we call an AND query" but doesn't clearly explain why AND queries would be better than other possible query types.
- The reference to [27, Figure 1] lacks a complete citation.
- The paper states "the probability of error of a query answer may also be thought of as the aggregated answer after repeating the query several times" but then claims "repeating the same question multiple times is not allowed" without sufficient justification.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper introduces an interesting theoretical angle on clustering with queries, the numerous technical errors, unclear explanations, and formatting issues significantly undermine its contribution. The paper would benefit from a thorough rewrite to address these issues before publication. The experimental validation is also insufficient to support the theoretical claims.

The paper's main contribution (AND queries outperforming XOR queries for approximate recovery) is not convincingly demonstrated. The theoretical justification is weak, and the experimental support is minimal. The paper also fails to clearly differentiate its work from related work in [23] on smooth compression.

Given the number of technical errors and the insufficient experimental validation, I recommend rejection. While the paper has some interesting ideas, the issues identified above prevent it from meeting NIPS's standards for technical soundness and clarity. The paper would require substantial revision to address these concerns.
