

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper introduces a novel information-theoretic framework for query-based clustering, framing the problem as locally encodable source coding. The authors propose AND queries as a superior alternative to traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries, demonstrating theoretically and empirically that AND queries require significantly fewer queries for approximate recovery in both noiseless and noisy settings. The paper makes connections to prior work in crowdsourcing (e.g., [21, 33]) and information theory (e.g., locally decodable codes [19, 16]), while establishing new theoretical bounds and a practical crowdsourcing validation.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical contributions. The authors establish non-trivial information-theoretic lower bounds for locally encodable source coding (Theorems 2, 3) that advance the field beyond prior work. The AND query approach (Theorems 4, 5) provides a clear theoretical advantage over traditional methods, with a well-constructed proof for Theorem 5 that handles noisy query scenarios. The real-world experiment validates the theoretical claims, showing that AND queries yield 55% fewer errors than "same cluster" queries in a crowdsourcing setting (105 vs. 234 errors out of 1000 queries). However, some proofs (e.g., Theorem 3) are deferred to supplementary materials, which slightly weakens the paper's self-contained quality. The error model assumes independent query errors, which may not perfectly capture real-world crowdsourcing dynamics where errors could be correlated.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem setup to theoretical results to experimental validation. The key contributions are clearly articulated, and the distinction between locally encodable and locally decodable source coding is well-explained. The figures (Figures 1-5) effectively illustrate the theoretical bounds and experimental results. The description of the experimental setup (Section 4) is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. One minor weakness is that the connection between the theoretical results and practical implementation details could be slightly more explicit for readers unfamiliar with information theory.

## Originality
This paper makes a significant original contribution by reframing query-based clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem. The introduction of AND queries as a superior alternative to "same cluster" queries is novel and well-motivated. The authors establish new information-theoretic results on locally encodable source coding that appear to be the first non-trivial results for this specific problem (as they note, "the only place where it is mentioned to the best of our knowledge is in [23]"). While related work on locally decodable codes exists, the paper clearly distinguishes between encodable and decodable settings, making this a meaningful theoretical advance.

## Significance
The results have clear significance for both theoretical and practical crowdsourcing applications. The paper demonstrates that AND queries can reduce query complexity from O(n log n) to O(n log(1/δ)) for (1-δ)-good approximate recovery, which is a substantial practical improvement. The real-world validation with crowdsourced movie labeling provides compelling evidence for the approach's practical utility. The paper also makes an important theoretical contribution by connecting crowdsourcing to information theory in a novel way. The results are likely to be influential for future work on query-based clustering and crowdsourcing.

## Strengths and Weaknesses
**Strengths:**
- Novel theoretical framework (locally encodable source coding) for query-based clustering
- AND queries provide a clear theoretical and empirical advantage over traditional approaches
- Strong experimental validation with real crowdsourcing data
- Clear distinction between locally encodable and locally decodable source coding
- Well-motivated connection to information theory

**Weaknesses:**
- Some key proofs deferred to supplementary materials
- Limited scaling analysis for k > 2 clusters in practical settings
- Small-scale real-world experiment (200 movies total)
- Assumption of independent query errors may not fully capture real-world dynamics
- Limited discussion of how the method would scale to very large datasets

## Recommendation
This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical implications. The novelty of the AND query approach and the information-theoretic framework is compelling, and the experimental validation provides convincing evidence for the approach's practical value. The paper advances our understanding of the fundamental limits of query-based clustering in a meaningful way.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the field. While the experimental validation is somewhat limited in scale, the theoretical results are strong and the approach is clearly superior to existing methods for the problem at hand. The paper's connection between crowdsourcing and information theory is particularly noteworthy.

I recommend acceptance, as this paper represents a substantial theoretical advance with clear practical implications for a problem of growing importance in machine learning and data science. The results would be of interest to both theoretical researchers and practitioners working on crowdsourcing and entity resolution.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Include the proof of Theorem 3 in the main paper to strengthen the technical contribution.
2. Expand the discussion on scaling to larger k (beyond k=2) and larger datasets.
3. Provide more detailed analysis of the error model's limitations in real-world crowdsourcing.
4. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the method would scale to very large datasets (e.g., millions of elements).
5. Include a comparison to recent work on query-based clustering that might use different query types.
