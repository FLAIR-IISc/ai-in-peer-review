## NIPS 2017 Review: Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of clustering with limited, potentially noisy, pairwise queries to an oracle (e.g., crowd workers). It frames this problem as a locally encodable source coding problem, leveraging information-theoretic tools to establish lower bounds on query complexity and propose new querying schemes. A key contribution is the introduction of “AND queries” as a potentially more efficient alternative to standard “same cluster” (XOR) queries, especially for approximate recovery. The authors also present a practical algorithm utilizing AND queries and validate its performance with real-world crowdsourcing experiments on movie genre classification.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon a growing body of work exploring crowdsourcing for clustering and entity resolution (e.g., [33, 30, 8, 20]). It connects this area to the well-established field of information theory, specifically source coding and locally decodable/encodable codes [19, 23]. It positions itself within the landscape of adaptive vs. non-adaptive querying schemes, focusing on the latter for its parallelizability. The connection to Massey’s work [17] on linear codes and rate-distortion is a crucial element in justifying the exploration of non-XOR queries.  The paper correctly identifies and differentiates itself from prior work on smooth compression [23] and locally decodable codes [19, 16], highlighting the novelty of focusing on *locally encodable* codes.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Framing:**  The formulation of the querying problem as locally encodable source coding provides a fresh perspective and opens the door to applying powerful theoretical tools.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a solid theoretical analysis, including lower bounds on query complexity and performance guarantees for the proposed AND-query scheme. The proofs, while dense, appear sound.
* **AND-Query Insight:** The introduction of AND queries as a potentially more efficient alternative to XOR queries is a significant contribution. The theoretical justification based on Massey’s work is compelling, and the experimental validation supports this claim.
* **Practical Validation:** The inclusion of a real-world crowdsourcing experiment with Amazon Mechanical Turk strengthens the paper and demonstrates the practical relevance of the proposed approach. The comparison with ‘same cluster’ queries in a real-world setting is especially valuable.
* **Handling Noisy Oracles:** The consideration of noisy query answers and the development of a scheme to mitigate errors, even acknowledging limitations of majority voting, is a practical and important addition.
* **Extension to k Clusters:**  The work extends beyond the binary clustering case (k=2), providing a more general framework.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Dense and Technical:** The paper is very dense and relies heavily on information-theoretic terminology, making it challenging for readers not deeply familiar with the field.  More intuitive explanations of the core concepts would improve accessibility.
* **Experimental Scope:** While the real-world experiment is valuable, it's limited in scope. Expanding the dataset, varying the query types (e.g., including triangle queries), and comparing against more sophisticated active learning algorithms would further strengthen the findings.  The experiment primarily validates the AND query in a noisy environment; more comprehensive evaluation of the theoretical bounds would be helpful.
* **Algorithm 1 Complexity:** The description of Algorithm 1, while mathematically sound, feels a bit abstract. A more detailed explanation of its implementation, and a discussion of its computational complexity, would be beneficial.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach. For example, how does the performance degrade with larger values of *k*? What are the practical challenges of implementing the d-regular graph construction in large-scale scenarios?
* **Redundancy:** Some sections repeat ideas already presented in the introduction, which feels repetitive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and original paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of crowdsourced clustering. The innovative framing of the problem as a locally encodable source coding problem, the theoretical rigor, the introduction of AND queries, and the practical validation all demonstrate a high level of technical competence and insight. While the paper is quite dense and could benefit from improved clarity and expanded experimental evaluation, the strengths clearly outweigh the weaknesses.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel theoretical framework with strong grounding in information theory.
* Significant contribution of AND queries as a potentially more efficient querying strategy.
* Practical validation with real-world crowdsourcing data.
* Addresses an important and increasingly relevant problem in data management and machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* High technical barrier to entry for readers unfamiliar with information theory.
* Limited scope of the experimental validation.
* Algorithm description could be more detailed and its computational complexity discussed.



**Rating:** **8/10 - Strong Accept**

I recommend this paper for acceptance to NIPS 2017. The contributions are substantial and the paper has the potential to influence future research in this area.  Addressing the minor weaknesses noted above would further enhance its impact. I suggest the authors focus on improving clarity and expanding the experimental evaluation.
