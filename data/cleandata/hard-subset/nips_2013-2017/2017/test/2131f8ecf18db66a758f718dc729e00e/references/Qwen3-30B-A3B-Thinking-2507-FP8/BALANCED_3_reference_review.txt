

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper introduces a novel information-theoretic framework for semisupervised clustering by framing the problem as locally encodable source coding. The authors demonstrate that AND queries significantly outperform traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery, providing theoretical guarantees and experimental validation. The work builds upon recent advances in crowdsourced clustering [4, 8, 21] but makes a fundamental conceptual shift by connecting query complexity to information-theoretic limits of locally encodable codes.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing new lower bounds on query complexity that are strictly better than existing results for certain parameter ranges (Theorem 2). The connection to locally encodable source coding is novel and well-motivated, addressing a gap in the information theory literature.

2. **Practical Insights**: The paper provides a compelling argument for using AND queries over traditional "same cluster" queries, demonstrating that AND queries require fewer queries for approximate recovery (Theorem 4). The experimental validation with real crowdsourcing data (Section 4) effectively supports the theoretical claims, showing AND queries yield 55% fewer errors than "same cluster" queries in practice.

3. **Comprehensive Analysis**: The paper thoroughly analyzes multiple settings: noiseless exact recovery, noiseless approximate recovery, and noisy approximate recovery. The authors provide both lower bounds and matching upper bounds for each setting, demonstrating a deep understanding of the problem space.

4. **Clear Experimental Validation**: The real-world crowdsourcing experiment with 100 action and 100 romantic movies provides compelling practical validation of the theoretical results. The results closely match theoretical predictions (Figure 5), strengthening the paper's claims.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Scope**: The real-world experiment is limited to 200 movies and 10 volunteers, which may not be sufficiently large to generalize the findings to more complex scenarios. The paper would benefit from additional experiments with larger datasets or different domains.

2. **Prior Distribution Assumption**: The paper assumes knowledge of the prior distribution p, which may be challenging to obtain in practice. While the authors acknowledge this limitation (Section 3.3), they don't sufficiently address how to handle cases where p is unknown or poorly estimated.

3. **Technical Depth in Key Proofs**: The proof of Theorem 3 (the rate-distortion lower bound for AND queries) is deferred to the appendix, which makes it difficult for reviewers to fully assess the technical soundness of this key result. A more detailed sketch of this proof in the main text would strengthen the paper.

4. **Connection to Existing Crowdsourcing Literature**: While the paper cites relevant works, it could better position itself within the broader literature on crowdsourced clustering. For example, the comparison with [21] (which also addresses query complexity) could be more thorough, particularly regarding how the AND query approach differs from their techniques.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide both lower and upper bounds for the key problems, and the experimental results validate the theoretical claims. The main weakness is the limited scope of the experimental validation, which might not fully support the broader claims about practical applicability.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical progression from problem setup to theoretical results to experimental validation. The connection between clustering and information theory is well-explained. However, some of the technical details (particularly in the proofs) may be dense for readers unfamiliar with information theory. The experimental section is particularly clear and well-documented.

## Originality

This paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by introducing the locally encodable source coding perspective to clustering. The insight that AND queries outperform XOR queries for approximate recovery is both counterintuitive and significant. While the paper builds on prior work in crowdsourced clustering, the information-theoretic framing and the specific query strategies represent a meaningful advance beyond existing approaches.

## Significance

The problem of query-efficient clustering is highly relevant to practical applications, particularly in crowdsourcing. The paper's theoretical results provide near-optimal query complexity guarantees, and the practical validation demonstrates real-world applicability. The finding that AND queries can reduce error rates by over 50% compared to traditional approaches has significant implications for practitioners.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the problem of query-efficient clustering. The novel information-theoretic perspective, the compelling empirical validation, and the practical insights about AND queries over traditional "same cluster" queries represent a meaningful advance in the field. 

The main weaknesses are the limited scope of the experimental validation and the assumption of known prior distribution p. However, these are reasonable limitations for a theoretical paper, and the authors have appropriately acknowledged them.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field that would be of interest to the NeurIPS community. With minor revisions addressing the experimental scope and clarifying the proof of Theorem 3 (if possible), this would be a strong contribution to the conference.

**Summary of Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel information-theoretic framing of clustering as locally encodable source coding
- Theoretical guarantees with tight lower and upper bounds
- Practical validation showing AND queries reduce errors by 55% in crowdsourcing
- Clear, well-written presentation of complex ideas
- Significant implications for practical crowdsourcing applications

**Summary of Arguments for Rejection (if applicable):**
- Limited experimental validation scope (200 movies, 10 volunteers)
- Assumption of known prior distribution p (though acknowledged as limitation)
- Key proof deferred to appendix (though common practice)
