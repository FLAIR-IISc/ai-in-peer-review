### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of semi-supervised clustering, framed as recovering a vector of `n` labels from `k` classes using a limited number of non-adaptive queries. The central and novel contribution is to view this problem through the lens of locally encodable source coding, where each compressed bit (query answer) depends on only a small, constant number of input bits (labels). This perspective allows the authors to derive new information-theoretic lower bounds and develop near-optimal non-adaptive querying schemes.

The paper's contributions are structured around three settings:
1.  **Noiseless Queries, Exact Recovery (k=2):** The authors analyze standard 'same-cluster' (XOR) queries. They provide an achievability result (Theorem 1) showing that for certain priors, exact recovery is possible with fewer than `n` queries, improving upon the simple baseline. They also derive a new information-theoretic lower bound (Theorem 2) on the number of queries, which is tighter than the standard entropy bound for some parameter regimes.
2.  **Noiseless Queries, Approximate Recovery (k=2):** The paper argues that XOR queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery, where the goal is to correctly label a `(1-δ)` fraction of items. It proposes a novel "AND query" (`Q(X, X') = X ∧ X'`) and provides a querying scheme (Theorem 4) that outperforms the theoretical limits of XOR queries in this setting.
3.  **Noisy Queries, Approximate Recovery (k≥2):** This section presents the main algorithmic contribution. The authors generalize the AND query for `k` classes and propose a simple, non-adaptive algorithm (Algorithm 1) based on querying pairs along the edges of a regular graph. The key result (Theorem 5) is that this algorithm achieves `(1-δ)`-approximate recovery with only `O(n log(k/δ))` queries, even when oracle answers are noisy. This significantly improves upon the `O(n log n)` complexity of prior work in the noisy setting.

The theoretical results are supported by a real-world crowdsourcing experiment on a movie genre classification task, which validates the performance of the proposed algorithm and provides evidence that AND queries may be less error-prone for human workers than 'same-cluster' queries.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel Conceptual Framework:** The connection drawn between active clustering and locally encodable source coding is highly original and insightful. This reframing is not merely a change in terminology; it provides powerful information-theoretic tools to analyze the problem, leading to new lower bounds and a deeper understanding of the fundamental limits of non-adaptive querying. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could inspire future work at the intersection of active learning, crowdsourcing, and information theory.

2.  **Innovative Query Design (AND Queries):** The paper makes a compelling case for the use of AND queries over the more conventional 'same-cluster' (XOR) queries. This idea is novel and well-motivated from multiple angles:
    *   **Theoretical:** The analysis shows that for approximate recovery, AND queries can bypass the rate-distortion limitations inherent to linear (XOR) queries.
    *   **Practical:** The proposed algorithm for the noisy setting relies on the asymmetry of the AND query, a property not shared by XOR.
    *   **Empirical:** The crowdsourcing experiment suggests that AND queries are cognitively simpler for human workers, leading to a substantially lower error rate. This is a valuable practical insight for designing real-world systems.

3.  **Strong and Comprehensive Theoretical Results:** The paper provides a rigorous and thorough theoretical treatment of the problem.
    *   The derivation of new lower bounds for exact (Theorem 2) and approximate (Theorem 3) recovery strengthens our understanding of the problem's fundamental difficulty.
    *   The achievability result for the noisy, approximate recovery setting (Theorem 5) is particularly strong. The query complexity of `O(n log(k/δ))` is highly efficient and represents a clear improvement over prior art. The algorithm itself is simple and non-adaptive, making it practical and parallelizable.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of a real-world crowdsourcing experiment is a major strength. It demonstrates that the proposed algorithm works in practice and that its performance closely tracks the theoretical predictions. The finding about the lower human error rate for AND queries further solidifies the paper's practical significance.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity of Theoretical Statements:** While the paper is generally well-written, some of the formal statements are dense and could be made more accessible. For instance, the expression for `δ` in Theorem 4 is complex, and the main text would benefit from a more intuitive summary of its asymptotic behavior or a plot illustrating its relationship with `m/n`. This would help readers grasp the takeaway message without needing to parse the full expression.

2.  **Assumption of Known Prior:** The analysis and algorithms rely on knowing the prior probability distribution `p` of the labels. Theorem 6 partially addresses this by showing that knowing the cluster sizes is sufficient. However, the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to errors in the estimation of these priors is not discussed. In many practical scenarios, these priors must be estimated from a small sample, and an analysis of the algorithm's robustness to such estimation errors would strengthen the paper's claims.

3.  **Experimental Scope:** The experiment provides a valuable proof-of-concept but is limited in scale (n=200, k=2, a single dataset). While the results are compelling, more extensive experiments on larger, standard datasets and for `k>2` would provide more conclusive evidence of the algorithm's general applicability and advantages. Furthermore, the main experimental comparison against spectral clustering with 'same-cluster' queries is mentioned for a synthetic dataset and detailed in the supplementary material. Including a summary of this comparison in the main paper would have more directly highlighted the proposed algorithm's superiority.

### Arguments for Acceptance
*   The novel framework connecting active clustering to locally encodable source coding opens up a new and promising research direction.
*   The introduction and analysis of AND queries is a significant and practical contribution, with both theoretical and empirical support.
*   The paper provides a highly efficient, non-adaptive algorithm for clustering with noisy queries, improving significantly on the state of the art.
*   The theoretical claims are rigorous and are convincingly validated with a real-world experiment.

### Arguments against Acceptance
*   The practical utility is somewhat constrained by the assumption of a known prior or known cluster sizes, and the robustness to estimation errors is not analyzed.
*   The experimental validation could be more extensive to fully demonstrate the method's advantages across different problem settings.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant contributions in terms of its conceptual framework, theoretical results, and practical insights. The strengths, particularly the originality of the approach and the strength of the results, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The work is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community.
