Summary
The paper studies nonadaptive, budgeted crowdsourced clustering/labeling through an information-theoretic lens. It frames query design as “locally encodable source coding,” where each query (a compressed bit) depends on at most Δ labels. The authors derive:
- Noiseless exact recovery (k=2): An upper bound using random parity (XOR) queries across Δ elements with ML decoding that beats n queries for certain priors p, and a lower bound exploiting locality constraints that improves over the naive nH(p) bound for some p.
- Noiseless approximate recovery (k=2): A lower bound for local-query schemes; arguments (invoking Massey/Ancheta) that XOR (“same-cluster”) queries are suboptimal for rate–distortion; and an AND-based scheme with a simple decoder that achieves favorable error–query tradeoffs.
- Noisy approximate recovery (k≥2): A pairwise “AND-like” query that outputs i only if both labels are i, otherwise 0, with symmetric noise. A simple thresholding algorithm over a d-regular graph yields (1−δ) recovery with m = O(n log(k/δ)) queries. A small crowdsourcing experiment on movie genres suggests that AND queries lead to fewer worker errors than same-cluster XOR.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of locality constraints in query-based labeling and a useful connection to information-theoretic bounds.
- The AND-query viewpoint is interesting and practically motivated; the simple decoder is appealing and its analysis via Chernoff-type bounds is accessible.
- Derivation of lower bounds that reflect local encoding constraints is valuable beyond this specific application.
- Focus on nonadaptive querying is relevant for parallel crowdsourcing.
- The small user study indicating AND queries may be easier for workers (fewer errors) is noteworthy and aligns with the algorithmic intuition.

Weaknesses
- Several correctness/clarity issues in the main algorithmic section: the number of queries for a d-regular graph is inconsistently stated as nd^2; it should be |E|=nd/2 (one query per edge). The experiments use 1000 queries for n=200, d=10 (consistent with nd/2), highlighting the inconsistency as a substantive error.
- The main “same-cluster is suboptimal” comparison relies heavily on a conjectured lower bound (only known at p=0.5). The empirical comparison is limited and not controlled for equal worker-error conditions or equal budgets; different query types elicited different error rates, confounding conclusions about fundamental query optimality.
- The ML-based achievability for XOR parity queries (Theorem 1) is purely information-theoretic; no efficient decoder is provided. Practicality in the crowdsourcing setting is thus unclear.
- The noisy scheme’s expectation/threshold formulas (Ci and E(Nu,i)) appear error-prone or poorly parenthesized, and the noise model’s impact on spurious “i” observations is not cleanly derived. This undermines confidence in the constants and thresholds.
- Limited experimental validation: one domain, small scale, no comparison to stronger baselines under matched budgets (e.g., methods from Karger–Oh–Shah; majority vote with learned worker reliabilities; existing nonadaptive schemes with same-cluster queries; group testing–style designs).
- Missing connections to closely related literatures: group testing and boolean compressed sensing (AND/OR channels with noise), which study nearly identical primitives and provide sharp bounds and designs. This weakens the positioning of novelty.
- Presentation is rough: numerous typos, incomplete/missing proofs in the main text, inconsistent notation (logs, bases), missing figures in the submission, and occasional imprecise statements.

Quality
- Some proofs are only sketched or deferred; the key local lower bounds look plausible but would benefit from a cleaner, self-contained treatment. The ML achievability is standard but impractical. The noisy algorithm’s analysis is essentially a Chernoff gap argument but the derivation of expectations/thresholds needs to be corrected/clarified. Empirical validation is insufficient to substantiate superiority over established baselines.

Clarity
- The manuscript needs significant editing. The nd^2 vs nd/2 inconsistency is critical. Several equations have ambiguous parentheses or typographical errors; the role of priors in thresholds must be derived cleanly. The relation between “approximate recovery” and standard rate–distortion (δ̃) is introduced but the locality penalty is not intuitively explained. Figures referenced are not present.

Originality
- The local encodability framing for query design is interesting, though related notions (smooth compression, local decodability) and group testing are close precedents. The AND-query decoder over a sparse graph is reminiscent of standard noisy classification over neighborhoods; novelty relative to group testing and nonadaptive crowdsourcing needs clearer differentiation. The lower bounds reflecting locality are a useful contribution.

Significance
- Potentially useful for designing practical nonadaptive query schemes with provable tradeoffs. However, the current theoretical/practical gaps (inefficient ML decoders, unclear constants/thresholds, limited experiments, reliance on conjectures for comparisons) limit immediate impact.

Pros
- Interesting information-theoretic framing and locality-aware lower bounds.
- Simple AND-based querying/decoding with clean scaling m = O(n log(k/δ)).
- Practical insight that AND queries may be easier for workers.

Cons
- Critical technical inconsistency in query counts; unclear/possibly incorrect formulas in the noisy-case analysis.
- Overstated comparison vs same-cluster queries; partial reliance on a conjecture; limited empirical support.
- No efficient algorithm for the XOR achievability; practical relevance uncertain.
- Missing related work (group testing), limited experiments, and rough presentation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the d-regular graph/query-count inconsistency throughout; reconcile analysis and experiments.
- Derive and present the exact expectations and thresholds for the noisy scheme with unambiguous notation and a careful noise model.
- Benchmark against strong baselines under matched budgets and matched worker-error models; include at least one larger dataset.
- Either provide an efficient decoder for the XOR scheme or clearly separate information-theoretic achievability from practical algorithms.
- Discuss connections to group testing and boolean compressed sensing, and clarify novelty relative to those literatures.
- Improve writing, include key proofs (or more detailed sketches) in the main paper, and ensure all figures are present.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper contains appealing ideas and potentially useful bounds, but the technical/presentation issues and limited validation make it premature for acceptance. With corrections, stronger empirical comparisons, and clearer positioning, it could be a solid contribution.
