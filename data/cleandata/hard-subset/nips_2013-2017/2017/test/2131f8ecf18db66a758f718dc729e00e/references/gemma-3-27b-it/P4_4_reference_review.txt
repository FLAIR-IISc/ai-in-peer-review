This paper investigates the problem of semi-supervised clustering via querying an oracle (e.g., crowd workers) to determine if two elements belong to the same cluster. The authors frame this as a locally encodable source coding problem and derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required for exact and approximate recovery of the labeling. A key contribution is the introduction of AND queries, which they show can outperform traditional 'same cluster' (XOR) queries for approximate recovery, especially in noisy settings. They also provide algorithms approaching these bounds and validate their results through experiments on real datasets.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents a complex problem with connections to multiple fields – clustering, crowdsourcing, information theory, and coding theory. The motivation is well-explained, and the authors effectively relate their work to prior research in these areas (e.g., [4, 14, 21]). The framing of the querying problem as a locally encodable source coding problem is novel and provides a powerful theoretical framework for analysis.  The derivations of the lower bounds and the proposed querying schemes appear mathematically sound, and the experimental validation provides some evidence of practical relevance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Framing the problem as locally encodable source coding is a genuinely novel approach. The use of AND queries as an alternative to XOR queries is also an interesting and potentially impactful contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:**  The theoretical analysis seems rigorous, utilizing established information-theoretic principles. The proofs, while perhaps not fully detailed in the main body (references to supplementary material are made), appear logically structured.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the concepts are explained clearly, despite the technical complexity. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the key contributions.
*   **Significance:** The potential to reduce the number of queries to crowd workers has practical implications for entity resolution and other crowdsourcing applications. The theoretical insights into the limitations of XOR queries and the advantages of AND queries are significant.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Detail in Proofs:** The paper relies heavily on references to a supplementary material for detailed proofs. While this is acceptable, it makes it difficult to fully assess the correctness and subtleties of the analysis without reviewing the supplement.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental section is relatively limited. While the results are encouraging, more extensive experiments with different datasets, oracle noise levels, and comparison with existing methods would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Assumptions:** The independence assumption of query errors (q) and the restriction against repeating queries, while justified, may not hold in all real-world scenarios. A discussion of the robustness of the results to violations of these assumptions would be valuable.
*   **Practicality of AND Queries:** The paper mentions that AND queries are easier for workers to answer. However, a more thorough exploration of the cognitive load and potential biases associated with AND queries versus XOR queries would be useful.  What happens if the worker doesn't understand the task or intentionally gives incorrect answers?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in clustering and crowdsourcing.
*   The novel framing of the problem as a locally encodable source coding problem provides a strong theoretical foundation.
*   The derived lower bounds and proposed querying schemes are significant contributions to the field.
*   The experimental validation, though limited, supports the theoretical results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of detailed proofs in the main body makes it difficult to fully assess the technical soundness.
*   The experimental section is relatively weak and could be strengthened with more extensive evaluation.
*   The assumptions made about query errors and query repetition may limit the practical applicability of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. The novelty of the approach and the potential for practical impact make it a valuable addition to the NIPS conference. While the lack of detail in the proofs and the limited experimental validation are weaknesses, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors address the reviewer's concerns by providing a thorough and accessible supplementary material. A minor revision to expand on the discussion of practicality of AND queries in real-world settings could also be beneficial.
