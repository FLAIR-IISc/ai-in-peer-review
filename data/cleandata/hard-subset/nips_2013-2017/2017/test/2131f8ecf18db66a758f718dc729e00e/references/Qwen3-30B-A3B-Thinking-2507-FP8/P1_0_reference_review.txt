

# Peer Review: "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel information-theoretic perspective on clustering problems by modeling them as locally encodable source coding problems. The authors demonstrate that standard "same cluster" (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery, and instead propose using AND queries, which significantly reduce the number of required queries. The paper makes several theoretical contributions: (1) new information-theoretic lower bounds for locally encodable source coding; (2) a querying scheme using AND queries that achieves (1-δ)-good recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries; and (3) a real-world crowdsourcing experiment validating their theoretical results. This work extends and improves upon recent work on query complexity for clustering [4, 21, 33].

## Strengths

**Originality (Strong):** The connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding is genuinely novel. While locally decodable source codes have been studied, the authors are the first to identify and analyze locally encodable source coding as a model for clustering with query constraints. Their key insight that AND queries outperform XOR ("same cluster") queries for approximate recovery is a significant theoretical contribution that challenges conventional wisdom in the field.

**Quality (Strong):** The paper presents rigorous theoretical results with well-constructed proofs. The authors provide both lower bounds and matching upper bounds, demonstrating tightness for their schemes. The real-world experiment effectively validates their theoretical claims, showing that AND queries produce lower error rates (105 errors vs. 234 for XOR queries) in practice. The paper carefully addresses the limitations of their approach and provides a thorough analysis of both noiseless and noisy settings.

**Significance (Strong):** The problem of minimizing query complexity in clustering is highly relevant to practical applications like crowdsourcing, where each query incurs cost. The authors demonstrate that their AND query approach reduces query complexity from O(n log n) to O(n log(1/δ)), which represents a substantial improvement. The theoretical results have clear practical implications for designing more efficient crowdsourcing systems.

**Clarity (Good):** The paper is well-organized with clear exposition. The problem setup is carefully defined, and the main contributions are well-motivated. Figures 1-4 effectively illustrate the theoretical results, and the experimental section provides a transparent account of the crowdsourcing setup and results.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Scope (Moderate):** While the paper makes strong theoretical contributions for k=2, the generalization to larger k is not fully explored in the experimental validation. The main algorithmic result (Theorem 5) handles general k, but the real-world experiment is limited to k=2. The paper would benefit from additional experimental validation for larger k or a more detailed discussion of how the approach scales.

**Prior Knowledge Assumption (Moderate):** The authors assume knowledge of the prior distribution p, which can be estimated from a small sample. While they acknowledge this, the paper could better discuss the implications when this prior is unknown or poorly estimated, as this is a common practical scenario.

**Comparison with Recent Work (Moderate):** The paper mentions recent work on clustering with queries [4, 21, 33] but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of their approach with the most recent state-of-the-art methods. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the significance claim.

## Specific Suggestions

1. The paper could better clarify the relationship between their work and existing literature on locally decodable source codes [19, 16, 26]. While they mention this briefly, a more detailed discussion would help contextualize their novel contribution.

2. The paper could provide more intuition about why AND queries outperform XOR queries. The explanation in Section 3.2 is somewhat technical; a more intuitive explanation would help readers grasp this key insight.

3. For the noisy query case (Section 3.3), the authors could briefly discuss how their approach compares to other error-correction methods used in crowdsourcing, such as the majority voting approach they mention.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes significant progress on the problem of minimizing query complexity for clustering. The paper's core insight—that AND queries outperform XOR queries for approximate recovery—represents a meaningful advancement over existing work. The theoretical results are rigorous, and the experimental validation provides strong empirical support for the claims. The paper is well-written and clearly organized.

The work is clearly within the scope of NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning (clustering) with novel theoretical insights that connect to information theory. The results have clear significance for practical applications in crowdsourcing, which is an active research area at NIPS.

## Recommendation
I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution with practical relevance, and the results are well-supported by both theory and experiment. While there are minor areas for improvement (primarily in expanding the experimental validation to larger k), these do not detract from the overall strength of the work. The paper advances the state-of-the-art in a demonstrable way and provides a unique theoretical approach to a challenging problem.

The key contributions—novel connection to locally encodable source coding, AND queries as superior to XOR queries, and the O(n log(1/δ)) query complexity for approximate recovery—represent a significant step forward in the field. This work has the potential to influence both theoretical and practical approaches to clustering with query constraints.

## Final Rating: Strong Accept
