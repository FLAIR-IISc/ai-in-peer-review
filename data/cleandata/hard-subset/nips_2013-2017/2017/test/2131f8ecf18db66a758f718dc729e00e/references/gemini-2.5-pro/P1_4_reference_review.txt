### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of semi-supervised clustering (or label learning) for `n` items into `k` clusters using a limited budget of non-adaptive queries. The central and most novel contribution is to frame this problem within the information-theoretic lens of "locally encodable source coding," where each compressed bit (query answer) depends on a small, constant number of input bits (labels).

The authors analyze the query complexity in three distinct settings:
1.  **Noiseless, Exact Recovery:** For `k=2` clusters, they provide an upper bound on the number of "same-cluster" (XOR) queries that is strictly less than `n` when the cluster sizes are unbalanced, improving upon simple baselines. They also derive a new information-theoretic lower bound.
2.  **Noiseless, Approximate Recovery:** The paper argues that XOR queries are fundamentally suboptimal for recovering a large fraction `(1-δ)` of labels correctly. It introduces "AND" queries as an alternative and demonstrates their superiority, providing an achievability result that surpasses the theoretical limits of XOR queries.
3.  **Noisy, Approximate Recovery:** This section presents the main algorithmic result. For general `k`, the authors propose a generalized AND query and a simple, efficient decoding algorithm. They prove that this scheme can recover a `(1-δ)` fraction of labels with only `O(n log(k/δ))` queries, even when query answers are noisy. This is a significant improvement over the `O(n log n)` complexity of previous methods for noisy queries.

Finally, the paper validates its theoretical claims with a real-world crowdsourcing experiment, which not only supports the performance of their algorithm but also provides compelling evidence for a key premise: human workers make fewer errors on AND queries than on "same-cluster" queries.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Insightful Framing:** The connection drawn between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding is novel and powerful. It provides a principled, information-theoretic foundation for analyzing query complexity and moves the field beyond problem-specific heuristics. This perspective is likely to inspire further research at the intersection of machine learning, crowdsourcing, and information theory.

2.  **Significant Theoretical Contributions:** The paper delivers strong theoretical results across multiple settings. The `O(n log(k/δ))` query complexity for the noisy, approximate recovery setting (Theorem 5) is a major contribution. It demonstrates that near-perfect recovery is possible with a query budget that scales only linearly with `n`, which was not known for the noisy, non-adaptive setting. The new lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery are also valuable contributions.

3.  **Practical and Important Insight on Query Design:** The paper's core argument—that "same-cluster" (XOR) queries are not always optimal and that simple "AND" queries can be significantly better—is a crucial insight for the crowdsourcing community. This is particularly relevant in the noisy setting, where the asymmetry of the AND query allows for a simple and robust decoding algorithm. The paper explains this intuition clearly.

4.  **Compelling Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of a real crowdsourcing experiment (not just a simulation) is a major strength. The experiment on movie genre classification provides excellent validation for two key claims: (a) the proposed algorithm performs well in practice and closely tracks the theoretical predictions, and (b) the error rate of human workers was substantially lower for AND queries than for "same-cluster" queries (10.5% vs 23.4%). This second finding provides strong empirical grounding for the theoretical motivation of the paper.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity on Decoder Complexity:** For the noiseless, exact recovery setting (Sec 3.1), the achievability proof relies on a Maximum Likelihood decoder over a random codebook. While standard for information-theoretic existence proofs, this implies a decoder with potentially exponential complexity. The paper focuses exclusively on query complexity (encoding) and should briefly acknowledge the computational cost of decoding in this specific setting. This is less of an issue for the approximate recovery settings, where the proposed decoders are simple and efficient.

2.  **Practicality of the k-ary AND Query:** The proposed query for `k>2` in Section 3.3 returns `i` if both items have label `i`, and `0` otherwise. This requires the crowd worker to not only know that the items are in the same cluster, but also to identify *which* cluster `i` it is. This may be a more difficult task than the binary AND query ("Are both items of type `i`?") or the "same-cluster" query. The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of this human-factors aspect for the `k>2` case.

3.  **Minor Lack of Detail in Proofs:** Some technical steps are abbreviated. For instance, the proof of Theorem 4 relies on a Poisson approximation for vertex degrees in a random graph, which is a reasonable and common step but should be stated more explicitly as an approximation. Similarly, the proof of Theorem 5 provides the core Chernoff bound argument but could benefit from a more explicit calculation showing how `d = O(log(k/δ))` is derived from the error probability expressions. These are minor points that can be easily addressed.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   The observation that human error rates are lower for AND queries is fascinating. The authors hypothesize this is because a correct 'no' answer only requires identifying one item's label. This is a great insight that strengthens the paper's entire premise.
*   In the experiment, the threshold `T=2` worked best and was close to the theoretical prediction. It would be interesting to see how sensitive the results are to the estimation of the noise parameter `q`.
*   Theorem 6 is a nice addition, showing that the algorithm works with estimated cluster sizes instead of a true prior, which enhances its practicality.

### **Overall Assessment and Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that makes significant contributions on both theoretical and practical fronts. It introduces a novel and fruitful perspective, delivers strong theoretical results that advance the state-of-the-art, and provides a simple, effective algorithm backed by compelling real-world experiments. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall impact of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and significant connection between semi-supervised clustering and locally encodable source coding.
*   Introduces the simple but powerful idea of using AND queries, showing their theoretical and practical advantages over standard "same-cluster" queries.
*   Provides a state-of-the-art result of `O(n log(k/δ))` query complexity for noisy, approximate recovery.
*   The claims are exceptionally well-supported by a combination of theoretical analysis, an efficient algorithm, and a real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The computational complexity of the decoder for the exact recovery case is not discussed.
*   The practical difficulty of the proposed k-ary query for `k>2` could be further explored.

The arguments for acceptance are overwhelmingly stronger. The paper is well-written, tackles an important problem, and offers novel insights that are likely to be influential.

**Recommendation: Accept**
