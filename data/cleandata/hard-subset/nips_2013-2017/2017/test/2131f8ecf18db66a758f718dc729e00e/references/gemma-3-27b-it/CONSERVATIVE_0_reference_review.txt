This paper proposes a novel framework for semi-supervised clustering, framing it as a locally encodable source coding problem and introducing the use of AND queries alongside traditional ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries. The authors aim to minimize the number of queries to a crowd oracle while accurately reconstructing cluster labels, even in the presence of noisy oracle responses. They present theoretical lower bounds and an algorithm leveraging AND queries that, under certain conditions, outperforms the theoretical limits for XOR queries. Experiments on real datasets purportedly validate these findings.

While the connection to locally encodable source coding is an interesting perspective, the paper suffers from a significant lack of clarity and, more importantly, insufficient empirical validation to justify the claims of substantial improvement over established methods. The theoretical contributions, while potentially valuable, are presented with a level of mathematical detail that obscures the core ideas and makes it difficult to assess their practical impact.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Framing:** The perspective of viewing crowdsourced clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem is a potentially fruitful avenue for research.
* **AND Query Exploration:** Investigating AND queries as an alternative to XOR queries is a worthwhile exploration, and the theoretical results suggest potential benefits.
* **Noisy Oracle Consideration:** Explicitly addressing the issue of noisy oracle responses is a realistic and important consideration for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is dense and mathematically involved. Key concepts like locally encodable source coding are introduced without sufficient context, making it challenging for readers unfamiliar with the field to grasp the core ideas. The notation is often cumbersome.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is weak. The setup with IMDB movies is simplistic and doesn’t convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method, especially given the complexities of real-world data and potential biases in crowdsourcing. The comparison to spectral clustering is mentioned but not detailed enough to assess its fairness. The claim of validation is overstated.
* **Overstated Claims:** The authors claim “substantial” improvements and “validate” their algorithm, yet the experimental evidence is limited and doesn't fully support these strong claims. The advantage of AND queries appears modest in the provided results.
* **Lack of Baseline Comparison:** There's a lack of rigorous comparison with well-established clustering and active learning algorithms. While XOR queries are discussed, a comparison with more sophisticated active learning strategies tailored for crowdsourcing is missing.
* **Theoretical Assumptions:** The theoretical results rely on several assumptions (e.g., specific noise models, prior knowledge of cluster sizes) that may not hold in practical scenarios. The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions is not adequately explored.
* **Redundancy and Repetition:** The paper contains significant redundancy, with many concepts and results being reiterated multiple times. This contributes to the overall lack of clarity.

**Specific Concerns:**

* **Figure 2:** The comparison of lower bounds for approximate recovery is difficult to interpret without clearer explanations of the assumptions and parameters used.
* **Algorithm 1:** The reconstruction algorithm lacks detail. It’s unclear how the threshold ‘T’ is determined and how robust the algorithm is to different threshold values.
* **Crowdsourcing Experiment:** The survey setup feels ad-hoc. The authors provide little detail about the quality control measures used to ensure the reliability of the crowdsourced answers beyond the error rate itself.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:**

* The paper lacks strong empirical validation. The experimental results are insufficient to support the claims of significant improvement.
* The paper is difficult to understand due to its density, mathematical complexity, and lack of clarity.
* The contribution feels incremental given the theoretical complexity and limited empirical impact.
* The comparison to existing methods is inadequate.

**For:**

* The framing of the problem as locally encodable source coding is a novel and potentially valuable idea.
* The exploration of AND queries as an alternative to XOR queries is interesting.
* The consideration of noisy oracle responses is a realistic and important aspect of crowdsourced clustering.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents an interesting theoretical perspective and explores a potentially useful alternative querying strategy, its lack of clarity, limited empirical validation, and overstated claims outweigh its strengths. The paper requires substantial revisions, including a more rigorous empirical evaluation, a clearer presentation of the theoretical results, and a more comprehensive comparison to existing methods, to be considered for NIPS. The current version reads more like a preliminary investigation than a complete and convincing contribution to the field. The authors would benefit from focusing on demonstrating the practical benefits of their approach on more complex and realistic datasets, alongside more detailed comparisons to established baselines.
