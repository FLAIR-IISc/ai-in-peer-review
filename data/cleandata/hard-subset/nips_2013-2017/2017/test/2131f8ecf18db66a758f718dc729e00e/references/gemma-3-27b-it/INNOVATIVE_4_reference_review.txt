This paper tackles the problem of semisupervised clustering via a novel and compelling lens: locally encodable source coding. The central idea – framing querying as a compression problem where each query response depends on only a constant number of labels – is both insightful and opens doors to applying information-theoretic tools. The authors establish lower bounds, propose an AND-query strategy challenging the conventional wisdom of using XOR queries, and provide experimental validation. This is a bold attempt to connect disparate fields and, despite some roughness, presents a genuinely novel perspective.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core idea of viewing crowdsourced clustering as locally encodable source coding is highly original. This perspective isn't readily apparent and allows for the application of a powerful, underutilized theoretical framework.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper includes several information-theoretic results, including lower bounds and analysis of the AND-query scheme. While the proofs appear dense and could benefit from more elaboration (see weaknesses), the mathematical foundation is clearly present.
* **Challenging Conventional Wisdom:** The demonstration that AND queries can outperform “same cluster” (XOR) queries is a significant result, especially in the context of potentially noisy oracle responses.
* **Experimental Validation:** The authors back up their theoretical claims with experiments on real datasets, lending credibility to the proposed approach. The observation that majority voting *increases* errors in their setup is a noteworthy empirical finding.
* **Connection to Related Work:** The paper appropriately acknowledges and positions itself within the existing literature on crowdsourcing, information theory, and clustering.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper is quite dense and challenging to follow. The notation is heavy, and the connections between different ideas aren't always explicitly articulated. The introduction could be more focused, and the motivation for considering non-adaptive queries more clearly explained.
* **Proof Detail:** While the paper claims several theoretical results, the proofs are often sketched and lack sufficient detail for complete verification. For example, the proof of Theorem 1 relies on several steps that require further elaboration. The use of "o(1)" and asymptotic notation without careful justification is frequent.
* **Experimental Details:** The experimental section is relatively brief. More details about the dataset, experimental setup, and statistical significance of the results would strengthen the conclusions. The comparison with spectral clustering feels somewhat superficial.
* **Scope & Generality:** The focus on the binary case (k=2) dominates much of the discussion. While extensions to larger k are mentioned, their practical implications aren’t fully explored. The discussion of prior knowledge is somewhat hand-wavy; how robust are the results to inaccurate prior estimates?
* **Practical Impact:** While the theoretical results are interesting, the practical gains of the AND-query approach over simpler methods (like repeated XOR queries with majority voting) aren't fully quantified in the experiments.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a genuinely novel perspective on crowdsourced clustering.
* The theoretical results, while potentially incomplete, are a significant step towards understanding the fundamental limits of this problem.
* The challenge to the conventional wisdom regarding query types is impactful.
* The experimental validation, though limited, supports the theoretical claims.
* The potential to leverage information-theoretic tools for designing more efficient crowdsourcing schemes is exciting.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The paper’s density and lack of clarity make it difficult to fully assess the validity of the claims.
* The proofs require more rigorous development and justification.
* The experimental section lacks sufficient detail and quantitative analysis.
* The practical benefits of the proposed approach are not definitively demonstrated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper is a high-risk, high-reward submission. It presents a bold and original idea with a strong theoretical foundation, but suffers from clarity and presentation issues.  I lean towards acceptance, prioritizing the novelty and potential impact of the work. The paper introduces a fundamentally new way of thinking about this problem, and I believe its long-term influence could be significant, even if the current presentation isn't perfect.  However, I strongly recommend the authors address the clarity and proof details concerns in a revised version. I would anticipate this paper stimulating further research in this area, and its contribution, despite the current imperfections, justifies acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Accept (with encouragement to address clarity and proof details).
