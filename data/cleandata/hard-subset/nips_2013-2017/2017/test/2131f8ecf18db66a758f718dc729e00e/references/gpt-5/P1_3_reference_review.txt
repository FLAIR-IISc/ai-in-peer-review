Summary
The paper studies non-adaptive, budgeted crowdsourced clustering/label learning through the lens of locally encodable source coding. Each query is a simple function of at most Δ labels; the compressed “codeword” consists of m query answers, from which the label vector X is reconstructed. The paper develops:

- Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2): Using parity/XOR queries over Δ labels, an existence result shows exact recovery with m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α), α = 1/2(1+(1−4p(1−p))^Δ), which is strictly less than n for a range of p even with Δ=2. A new converse (stronger than n h(p) for some p) exploits locality constraints.

- Noiseless, approximate recovery (k=2): A locality-aware lower bound on achievable distortion δ with m queries, and an argument that “same-cluster” (XOR) queries are fundamentally limited (Massey’s result on linear rate–distortion). The paper proposes an AND-based query and a simple decoder (label 1 if any adjacent AND-answer is 1), with an explicit error expression; empirically, this achieves a better m–δ trade-off than XOR.

- Noisy, approximate recovery (general k): Pairwise, k-ary “AND-like” queries that return i if both endpoints are i, else 0; the decoder thresholds per-class neighbor counts on a d-regular graph. With d = Θ(log(k/δ)), the algorithm achieves (1−δ)-accurate recovery with m = Θ(n log(k/δ)) queries under an i.i.d. symmetric noise model. The method can use only cluster sizes rather than priors p via randomization of the graph. A small human study (movie genres) and a synthetic comparison support the claims and suggest that AND-queries yield fewer human errors than “same-cluster” queries.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects crowdsourced clustering to information-theoretic source coding with local encoding constraints (a relatively unexplored complement to local decodability and smooth compression). It builds on prior crowdsourcing/query-complexity works ([4], [8], [21], etc.) and on classic information theory (rate–distortion, joint source–channel coding, local/smooth coding). The argument that XOR is suboptimal for approximate recovery leverages Massey/Ancheta’s limitations of linear codes as rate–distortion codes.

Quality
- Theory: The noiseless exact-recovery achievability via random Δ-sparse parity queries is sound as an existence/ML-decoding result; the converse exploiting per-symbol query budget (Theorem 2) is interesting and improves over n h(p) in certain regimes. The approximate-recovery lower bound with local encoding (Theorem 3) is potentially novel but the statement is opaque; proof is deferred. The AND-based achievability (Theorem 4) is clean and the Poissonization/balls-in-bins analysis is standard. The noisy k-ary result (Theorem 5) is technically straightforward (Chernoff gap plus dependency graph concentration) and leads to an appealing O(n log(k/δ)) complexity.

- Empirics: The small-scale user study (200 movies, d=10, 1000 queries) is consistent with the model and supports the claim that AND-queries are easier for workers than same-cluster queries. However, the experimental section is limited in scope and does not include stronger baselines or larger-scale evaluations.

- Caveats:
  - ML decoding for the parity-based scheme is computationally intractable in general; the paper provides no efficient decoder for exact recovery, so the result is existential.
  - Several formulas/notations appear inconsistent or possibly incorrect: e.g., in Section 3.3, the number of queries is repeatedly stated as n d^2 and Algorithm 1 is titled “with n d^2 queries,” while a d-regular graph yields m = n d/2 queries (the experiments use nd/2). This should be corrected throughout.
  - Some expressions (e.g., Theorem 2’s h((1−ρ) r(p) Δ / ρ), Theorem 3’s bound involving h′ and e^{Δ h′(·)}) would benefit from clearer statements, ranges ensuring arguments are in [0,1], and intuition.
  - The comparison to XOR in the approximate setting partly rests on a conjecture derived from linear rate–distortion limits; a fully rigorous lower bound for XOR-only querying under locality would strengthen the case.
  - The k-ary query model presumes workers can reliably output one of k categorical outcomes (“both endpoints are i”), which may be more demanding than binary answers. The paper mitigates this by focusing on k=2 in experiments; practical guidance for k>2 is needed.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-motivated and places the contribution within both crowdsourcing and information theory. However, clarity issues include:
- The m = n d^2 vs n d/2 inconsistency.
- Occasional typos and notational slips (e.g., logs, bases, use of r(p)^Δ vs Δ r(p), special cases omitted).
- Heavy reliance on deferred proofs; some key ideas (Theorem 3) deserve a schematic proof sketch.
- Figures are referenced to argue superiority over XOR; quantitative axes/parameters should be fully described in the text.

Originality
- Framing non-adaptive label querying as locally encodable source coding is fresh and interesting.
- The existence of sublinear-in-n gains for exact recovery under parity queries with Δ-local encoding and non-uniform priors is nontrivial.
- The AND-query perspective and the simple, robust O(n log(k/δ)) noisy scheme are conceptually simple yet appear novel within the crowdsourcing/query-complexity literature.

Significance
- For theory, the locality-aware lower/upper bounds advance understanding of query-limited clustering and locally encodable compression.
- For practice, AND-based queries and the simple thresholding algorithm provide a credible path toward more reliable non-adaptive crowdsourcing under noise, with concrete query–accuracy trade-offs. Broader empirical validation would increase impact.

Pros
- Clear and timely problem; non-adaptive focus is practical.
- New information-theoretic perspective and bounds under local encoding.
- Simple, scalable algorithm with O(n log(k/δ)) queries under noise.
- Insightful observation (and user evidence) that AND queries are easier than same-cluster.

Cons
- Exact-recovery achievability is non-constructive (ML decoding).
- Notational/formula inconsistencies (notably m = n d^2) and some unclear bounds.
- Dependence on a conjecture to argue XOR suboptimality in approximate recovery.
- Limited experimental validation and comparison to strong baselines.

Questions/requests for authors
1. Please correct and unify the query count in Section 3.3 and Algorithm 1 (should be m = n d/2).
2. Provide a brief proof sketch or tighter intuition for Theorem 3 and ensure all function arguments lie in valid ranges.
3. Can you state an unconditional lower bound for XOR-only queries under locality for approximate recovery, at least in some regimes, to remove reliance on the conjecture?
4. What are the computational properties of your ML decoder in Theorem 1? Any efficient relaxation?
5. For k>2, how would you implement the k-ary query with human workers? Would you instead ask k separate binary AND questions?

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a novel and valuable theoretical framing, nontrivial bounds, and a simple, practically relevant algorithm that improves the query–accuracy trade-off in the noisy, non-adaptive setting. However, the presentation needs cleanup (notational errors), and the empirical evaluation is modest. With revisions addressing clarity and strengthening the XOR comparison, this would be a solid NIPS contribution.
