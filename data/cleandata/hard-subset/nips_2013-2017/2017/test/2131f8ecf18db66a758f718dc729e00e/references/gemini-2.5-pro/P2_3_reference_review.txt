### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of semi-supervised clustering (or label recovery) for *n* items into *k* clusters using a limited budget of non-adaptive queries to an oracle, a common scenario in crowdsourcing. The central and most novel contribution is the framing of this problem through the lens of information theory, specifically as a locally encodable source coding problem. In this view, the labels are a source vector, and the query answers are the compressed bits, with the constraint that each compressed bit (query answer) can only depend on a small, constant number of source bits (labels).

Building on this framework, the paper delivers several key results across different settings:
1.  **Noiseless, Exact Recovery:** For two clusters, the authors provide a querying scheme using XOR ('same-cluster') queries that can recover all labels with fewer than *n* queries for a range of label priors, along with a new information-theoretic lower bound.
2.  **Noiseless, Approximate Recovery:** The paper argues that XOR queries are suboptimal for recovering a (1-δ) fraction of labels correctly. It introduces AND queries as a superior alternative and provides a scheme whose performance surpasses the theoretical limits of XOR-based (linear) schemes.
3.  **Noisy, Approximate Recovery:** This section presents the main algorithmic result. The authors generalize the AND query for *k* clusters and provide an algorithm that recovers a (1-δ) fraction of labels correctly with O(n log(k/δ)) queries, even when oracle answers are noisy. This result is validated with a small-scale, real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

### **Review**

This is a strong, well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution by bridging the fields of active clustering/crowdsourcing and information theory. The theoretical results are novel and the practical implications, particularly the introduction of AND queries, are compelling.

#### **Quality**
The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs or detailed proof sketches that employ standard and appropriate information-theoretic and probabilistic tools (e.g., typical sets, Chernoff bounds, random graph analysis). The work is comprehensive, systematically tackling noiseless/noisy and exact/approximate recovery scenarios, which provides a holistic view of the problem.

The authors are also careful to evaluate their contributions. The comparison between their proposed AND query scheme and the theoretical limitations of XOR queries (Conjecture 1) is particularly effective at highlighting the benefits of their approach. The inclusion of a real-world crowdsourcing experiment, while small, lends significant credibility to the theoretical claims, especially the crucial observation that AND queries yielded a much lower error rate from human annotators than 'same-cluster' queries. This provides a strong practical motivation for the theoretical development.

#### **Clarity**
The paper is generally well-written and structured. The introduction does an excellent job of motivating the problem, situating it within existing literature, and clearly outlining the paper's contributions. The information-theoretic framing is explained well, making the core idea accessible.

However, the paper is quite dense, and some sections could benefit from more intuition. For instance, the proof sketch for Theorem 4, which analyzes the performance of AND queries, moves quickly through a series of probabilistic arguments and approximations (e.g., modeling node degrees with a Poisson distribution). A little more exposition on why these steps are justified would improve readability for a broader audience. Similarly, the presentation of the lower bounds in Theorems 2 and 3 is highly technical; a more intuitive summary of what these complex expressions imply would be helpful.

#### **Originality**
The originality of this paper is its greatest strength.
1.  **Novel Framework:** The connection between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding is, to my knowledge, entirely new. This is not just a relabeling of an old problem but a conceptual leap that enables the derivation of new fundamental limits and inspires novel algorithm design.
2.  **Novel Query Type:** The proposal to use AND queries instead of the standard 'same-cluster' (XOR) queries is a simple yet powerful idea. While non-linear queries are not unheard of, the specific analysis and demonstration of their superiority for approximate recovery in this context is novel and impactful.
3.  **New Theoretical Results:** The paper provides new information-theoretic lower bounds for this specific problem setting, which appear to be new contributions not only to the machine learning literature but also to the nascent theory of locally encodable codes.

The authors do a good job of citing and differentiating their work from related research in crowdsourcing, active clustering, and information theory.

#### **Significance**
The results presented are highly significant for both researchers and practitioners.
*   **For Researchers:** The information-theoretic framework opens a new avenue for analyzing active and semi-supervised learning problems. It provides a principled way to reason about the fundamental trade-offs between query complexity, query locality (the number of items per query), noise, and recovery accuracy. Other researchers are likely to build upon this elegant and powerful connection.
*   **For Practitioners:** The O(n log(k/δ)) query complexity for noisy, approximate recovery is a very strong and practical result. It demonstrates that one can achieve high accuracy on large datasets with a number of queries that is linear in *n* and only logarithmic in the desired precision (1/δ). The insight that the *type* of query can dramatically affect both algorithmic performance and human error rates is a crucial, actionable takeaway for anyone designing real-world crowdsourcing systems. The proposed Algorithm 1 is simple to implement and appears robust.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**
1.  **Highly Original Framing:** The connection to locally encodable source coding is a powerful and novel conceptual contribution.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper provides new upper and lower bounds on query complexity, including a near-optimal O(n log(k/δ)) algorithm for the noisy, approximate case.
3.  **Novel and Practical Query Proposal:** The introduction and analysis of AND queries is a significant finding, with clear advantages over standard 'same-cluster' queries, supported by both theory and a real experiment.
4.  **Comprehensive Scope:** The work thoroughly investigates multiple facets of the problem (noiseless/noisy, exact/approximate), presenting a complete picture.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Dependence on Priors:** Algorithm 1 requires knowledge of the cluster priors *p*. While the authors argue these can be estimated (and Theorem 6 provides an asymptotic guarantee), the algorithm's sensitivity to estimation errors in the non-asymptotic regime is not analyzed, which may be a practical concern.
2.  **Small-Scale Experiment:** The experiment provides an excellent proof-of-concept, but its scale (200 items, 10 volunteers) is limited. While compelling, the quantitative results should be interpreted with caution.
3.  **Density of Presentation:** Some of the theoretical sections are dense and could be made more accessible with additional intuitive explanations.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel theoretical framework to a well-motivated practical problem, leading to significant new results and insights. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It is a clear accept for NIPS.
