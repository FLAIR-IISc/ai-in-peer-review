This paper tackles the problem of semi-supervised clustering via a novel information-theoretic lens, framing it as a locally encodable source coding problem. The central idea – exploring querying strategies beyond simple ‘same cluster’ queries, specifically proposing AND queries – is a significant departure from existing work and demonstrates a potentially fruitful direction. The authors connect their work to related areas like crowdsourcing, source coding, and locally decodable codes, providing a solid theoretical foundation. While the paper is dense and contains a significant amount of technical detail, the core contribution is compelling.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of the clustering problem as locally encodable source coding is genuinely novel. The exploration of AND queries as an alternative to XOR/‘same cluster’ queries is a clever insight.
*   **Theoretical Depth:** The paper is rigorously grounded in information theory, providing lower bounds and analyzing the efficiency of the proposed querying schemes. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant theoretical tools.
*   **Potential Impact:** If the theoretical advantages of AND queries translate to practical benefits in real-world crowdsourcing scenarios, this work could significantly impact the efficiency of entity resolution and other data labeling tasks.
*   **Connection to Existing Literature:** The paper carefully situates its work within the broader context of crowdsourcing, source coding, and related fields, acknowledging and building upon existing research.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of a crowdsourcing experiment, even if preliminary, adds credibility to the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is quite dense and requires significant effort from the reader to follow the technical details. The notation is heavy, and the connections between different sections could be made more explicit. A more intuitive explanation of locally encodable source coding and its relevance to the clustering problem would be beneficial.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The crowdsourcing experiment is somewhat limited in scope and could be strengthened by including a wider range of datasets and query types. A more detailed analysis of the experimental results, comparing the performance of AND queries to other strategies, would be valuable.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumptions about the noise model (independent errors, no repetition of queries) are clearly stated but might be overly restrictive in some practical settings. Addressing the robustness of the proposed schemes to violations of these assumptions would be useful.
*   **Incomplete Validation:** While theoretical guarantees are provided, the practical performance gains of AND queries are not decisively demonstrated. More extensive empirical evaluation is needed to confirm the advantages of this approach.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The authors briefly mention smooth compression but do not fully explore its connection to their work. A more thorough discussion of the relationship between locally encodable and smooth codes could be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea – framing clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem and exploring alternative query types – is genuinely novel and has the potential to significantly advance the field.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the fundamental limits of semi-supervised clustering.
*   The experimental results, while limited, provide preliminary evidence to support the proposed approach.
*   The paper is well-written and thoroughly researched, demonstrating a strong understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is quite dense and may be difficult for some readers to follow.
*   The experimental validation is limited, and the practical benefits of AND queries are not decisively demonstrated.
*   The assumptions about the noise model may be overly restrictive.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to semi-supervised clustering. While the paper has some weaknesses in terms of clarity and experimental validation, the originality and potential impact of the work outweigh these concerns. The paper introduces a significant new concept and direction that is likely to stimulate further research in this area. I believe that the paper deserves to be accepted, despite the minor flaws. I encourage the authors to address the clarity issues and expand the experimental evaluation in future work.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
