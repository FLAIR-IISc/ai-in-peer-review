

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper introduces a novel information-theoretic framework for clustering problems by framing them as locally encodable source coding problems. The authors propose using AND queries instead of the commonly used "same cluster" (XOR) queries, demonstrating theoretically and empirically that AND queries require significantly fewer queries to achieve comparable or better clustering accuracy.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide tight lower bounds and matching upper bounds for several scenarios:

1. For noiseless exact recovery with two clusters, they establish a lower bound strictly better than nH(p) for certain p values (Theorem 2), and prove the existence of a querying scheme that achieves this bound (Theorem 1).

2. For noiseless approximate recovery, they prove that "same cluster" queries are fundamentally limited (Theorem 3) and introduce the AND query approach that outperforms them (Theorem 4), providing a concrete error rate analysis.

3. For noisy queries with k≥2 clusters, they present a comprehensive algorithm (Algorithm 1) with theoretical guarantees (Theorem 5) and validate it with a real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

The theoretical results are well-supported with mathematical proofs, and the experimental validation on a real dataset (100 action movies and 100 romantic movies) provides strong empirical evidence for the theoretical claims. The paper carefully acknowledges limitations, such as the need for prior knowledge of cluster sizes, and the assumption that query answers are independent.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively sets up the problem, relates it to prior work, and states the main contributions. The problem setup section clearly defines the model and notation, which is crucial for a theoretical paper. The main results are presented in a logical progression from noiseless to noisy scenarios, with each section building on the previous one.

Figures 1-5 effectively illustrate key theoretical results and experimental findings. The supplementary material contains detailed proofs and additional results, which is appropriate for a theoretical paper. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implementation of AND queries in crowdsourcing, but this is a minor point given the focus on theoretical contributions.

## Originality

This paper makes several significant original contributions. The primary novelty is framing clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem, which is not well-studied in the information theory literature (the authors note this is only mentioned in [23] in passing). The AND query approach is a major conceptual advance over the standard "same cluster" queries, with the authors providing both theoretical justification (via Massey's result [17]) and empirical validation.

The paper's connection between clustering and information theory is particularly novel. Previous work on clustering with queries (e.g., [4], [21], [33]) has focused on algorithmic approaches without the information-theoretic perspective. The authors' insight that AND queries can achieve better query complexity than XOR queries for approximate recovery represents a significant conceptual shift.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. For crowdsourcing applications, reducing the number of queries directly translates to cost and time savings. The paper shows that AND queries can reduce the number of required queries from O(n log n) to O(n log(1/δ)) for (1-δ)-approximate recovery, which is a dramatic improvement.

The theoretical advances in locally encodable source coding are also significant. The paper provides the first nontrivial results on fundamental limits of locally encodable codes, which extends the literature on locally decodable codes [19, 16, 26, 6] and smooth compression [23, 26].

The real-world validation is particularly convincing, with the authors showing that AND queries yield 105 errors out of 1000 queries compared to 234 errors for "same cluster" queries, demonstrating a clear practical advantage.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with practical implications for crowdsourcing applications. The main strengths are the novel theoretical framework (locally encodable source coding), the AND query approach with its theoretical and empirical advantages over standard methods, and the comprehensive theoretical analysis supported by real-world validation.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to both information theory and machine learning. The only minor limitation is that the results are primarily for the case of k=2 clusters, with only limited extension to k>2. However, the authors note this is a special case they emphasize, and the theoretical framework naturally extends to larger k.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, addressing a fundamental problem in machine learning with a novel theoretical approach that has practical applications.

## Recommendation

This paper should be accepted. It makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of query complexity in clustering, with clear practical implications for crowdsourcing. The quality of the theoretical results is high, the approach is novel, and the significance is substantial. The real-world validation provides strong evidence for the practical relevance of the theoretical findings.

The paper would be particularly valuable to researchers working on crowdsourcing, clustering, and information theory, and it has the potential to influence both theoretical and applied work in these areas. The clear exposition and strong empirical validation make it accessible to a broad NIPS audience.

This is a well-executed, high-quality paper that advances the state of the art in a meaningful way.
