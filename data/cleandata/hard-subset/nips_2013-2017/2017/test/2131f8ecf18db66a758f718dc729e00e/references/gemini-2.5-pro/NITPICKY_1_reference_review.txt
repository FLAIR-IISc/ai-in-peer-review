**Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"**

**Summary**

This paper frames the problem of label recovery from pairwise queries, a form of semi-supervised clustering, within the information-theoretic paradigm of source coding. The authors introduce the constraint that each query (an encoded bit) can only depend on a small, constant number of elements (source bits), a setting they term "locally encodable source coding." The paper explores this problem in three settings: 1) noiseless queries for exact recovery, 2) noiseless queries for approximate recovery, and 3) noisy queries for approximate recovery. The main contributions include new information-theoretic upper and lower bounds on the required number of queries, and the introduction of a novel "AND query" type. The authors argue and provide theoretical and experimental evidence that these AND queries can be significantly more efficient than the standard 'same-cluster' (XOR) queries, particularly for approximate recovery in the presence of noise. For the noisy setting, they propose an algorithm that achieves `(1-δ)`-approximate recovery with `O(n log(k/δ))` queries, a notable improvement over previous `O(n log n)` results.

**Quality**

The paper is built on a strong and appealing theoretical foundation. However, the technical execution and presentation contain several flaws that undermine its quality and reproducibility.

-   **Technical Soundness:** The proofs provided in the main text are often sketchy and contain unclear or undefined notation.
    -   In the proof of Theorem 1, an undefined variable `epsilon` appears suddenly ("Now if we take to be of the form n−β"). This is a critical omission that makes the argument impossible to follow.
    -   The statement that `X1 + X2 mod 2` has `r(p) = 2p(1-p)` ones is incorrect; it should have approximately `n * r(p)` ones. While likely a typo, it reflects a lack of care.
    -   The proof of Theorem 5 is very high-level. It relies on Chernoff bounds with unspecified constants (`c`, `epsilon`) and invokes a result from [3] without sufficient detail to verify its application.
    -   Algorithm 1 contains a thresholding condition `Nu,i ≥ dCie`. The variable `e` is not defined. If it is the mathematical constant, its inclusion is unusual and unexplained. This makes the algorithm description ambiguous.
    -   The proof of Theorem 4 relies on a Poisson approximation for the vertex degrees, which should be more formally justified as it is an approximation of a binomial distribution in this context.

-   **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental validation, while a welcome addition, is preliminary.
    -   The experiment is conducted on a small dataset (200 items, 10 volunteers). Conclusions drawn from this, such as the claim that AND queries are "easier to answer for workers" due to a lower error rate (105 vs. 234), are anecdotal. This observation requires a more controlled study to be substantiated.
    -   The paper compares its algorithm against spectral clustering on a synthetic dataset but not on the real crowdsourced dataset where 'same-cluster' queries were also collected. This inconsistency is not explained.
    -   The results in Figure 5 show a noticeable gap between the theoretical curve and experimental results for thresholds other than the theoretically chosen T=2. This discrepancy is not adequately discussed.

**Clarity**

The paper suffers from numerous issues in clarity and formatting, which detract significantly from the reading experience and suggest a hasty preparation.

-   **Organization and Formatting:**
    -   Important definitional details, such as the distinction between clustering and label learning (Footnote 1), are relegated to footnotes, which is poor practice.
    -   The paper includes the NIPS 2017 template header on the first page, which should have been removed.
    -   The citation style uses numeric references (e.g., [4]) instead of the standard author-year format (e.g., (Ashtiani et al., 2016)) expected for NIPS, which is a major formatting error.
    -   Figure numbering and referencing are confusing. Section 3.3 refers to Figures 3 and 4, but the flow from the previous sections is unclear. The text refers to Figure 6, which is not provided in the copy I reviewed (only a caption for Figure 5 is present).

-   **Writing:** The language is often imprecise. The explanation of the expurgation argument in Theorem 1 is rushed. The justification for disallowing query repetitions as "theoretically not interesting" (Footnote 2) is overly dismissive of what could be a valid modeling direction.

**Originality**

The paper's core ideas are highly original.
-   The connection between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding is novel and provides a powerful new lens through which to analyze this problem.
-   The introduction of AND queries as an alternative to standard 'same-cluster' queries is a significant conceptual contribution. It challenges a default assumption in the field and demonstrates clear theoretical advantages.
-   The new lower bounds for this constrained source-coding problem (Theorems 2 and 3) are valuable additions to the information-theoretic literature.

**Significance**

The results presented are theoretically significant.
-   The `O(n log(k/δ))` query complexity for approximate recovery with noisy queries is a substantial improvement over prior art and is a strong headline result.
-   The work opens up a new research direction by linking crowdsourcing problems to specific, constrained coding theory problems, which could inspire further work in both communities.
-   While the practical validation is weak, the theoretical results suggest a promising new class of algorithms for efficient crowdsourcing that could have a real-world impact if developed further.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The information-theoretic framing is novel and elegant.
2.  The introduction of AND queries is a creative and impactful idea.
3.  The main theoretical result (`O(n log(k/δ))` complexity) represents a significant advance over prior work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The paper is marred by a substantial number of clarity issues, including formatting errors, typos, and confusing presentation, indicating a lack of careful preparation.
2.  Key technical arguments contain undefined variables and are presented in a sketchy, non-rigorous manner, making the results difficult to verify (e.g., proofs of Thm 1 & 5, Alg 1).
3.  The experimental validation is preliminary and does not robustly support some of the paper's stronger practical claims (e.g., about worker error models).

**Decision**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper introduces novel, significant, and exciting ideas, the execution falls short of the standards expected at NIPS. The numerous flaws in technical precision, clarity, and formatting are not merely cosmetic; they obscure the arguments and prevent a confident verification of the results. The combination of undefined variables in key theorems, ambiguous algorithm descriptions, and a high-level treatment of proofs constitutes a major weakness. The paper has the potential to be excellent, but it requires a thorough revision to address these shortcomings. I would be enthusiastic to see a more polished and rigorous version of this work in the future.
