This paper tackles the problem of semi-supervised clustering, or label recovery, using a fixed budget of non-adaptive queries. The central and novel contribution is to frame this problem within the information-theoretic language of locally encodable source coding, where each compressed bit (a query answer) depends on a small, constant number of source bits (the true labels). This perspective allows the authors to derive new information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery.

The paper investigates two main types of pairwise queries for the binary clustering case (k=2): the standard 'same-cluster' query (equivalent to XOR) and a newly proposed AND query. The authors provide an analysis for both noiseless and noisy query settings. For exact recovery with noiseless XOR queries, they provide an achievability result that requires fewer than `n` queries for certain label priors, along with a new lower bound. For approximate recovery, they argue that XOR queries are fundamentally limited and demonstrate that their proposed AND queries can achieve a better trade-off between the number of queries and the recovery error. This is extended to the noisy, multi-cluster (k>=2) setting, where a practical algorithm based on a generalized AND query is proposed and shown to recover a `(1-δ)` fraction of labels correctly with `O(n log(k/δ))` queries. The theoretical findings are supported by a small-scale real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Theoretical Framework:** The connection drawn between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding is insightful and original. This fresh perspective allows for the application of powerful information-theoretic tools, leading to new fundamental limits (lower bounds) for this problem, which is a significant theoretical contribution.

2.  **Introduction and Analysis of AND Queries:** The paper makes a compelling case that the conventional 'same-cluster' (XOR) query is not always optimal, particularly for approximate recovery. The introduction of the AND query is a key contribution. The theoretical analysis (Theorem 4, Figure 2) and the empirical evidence (lower error rates in the crowdsourcing experiment) strongly support the claim that AND queries can be more efficient and robust. This is an important, practical finding for designing crowdsourcing systems.

3.  **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper provides a broad theoretical treatment, covering multiple important scenarios: exact vs. approximate recovery, noiseless vs. noisy oracles, and binary vs. multi-cluster problems. Providing both achievability schemes (upper bounds) and fundamental limits (lower bounds) for these settings makes for a well-rounded and thorough investigation.

4.  **Practical Algorithm and Experimental Validation:** The proposed algorithm for the noisy, multi-cluster setting (Algorithm 1) is simple, non-adaptive, and efficient. The inclusion of a real-world crowdsourcing experiment, while modest in scale, is a major strength. It grounds the theoretical work and provides convincing evidence for the practical utility of the proposed AND queries, showing they are not only theoretically sound but may also be easier for human workers to answer correctly.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity on the Generalized AND Query:** In Section 3.3, the binary AND query is generalized for k clusters to a k-ary query: `Q(X,X') = i if X = X' = i, 0 otherwise`. This formulation is asymmetric, treating cluster 0 as a default. The motivation for this specific design over other possibilities (e.g., a query that returns `i` if `X=X'=i` and some other symbol otherwise) is not clearly explained. Why is this the natural generalization of AND? Clarifying the design choice and its implications would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Presentation and Flow:** The paper moves quickly between different problem settings (e.g., k=2, noiseless, exact recovery in 3.1; k=2, noiseless, approximate in 3.2; k>=2, noisy, approximate in 3.3). While the sections are well-defined, the overall narrative could be improved by better motivating these transitions. For instance, the sudden switch from binary-output queries to k-ary-output queries in Section 3.3 feels abrupt and should be justified more explicitly.

3.  **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on an i.i.d. prior for the labels. While this is a common assumption, real-world data often exhibits more structure. Theorem 6 partially addresses this by considering fixed cluster sizes, but the limitation of the i.i.d. model should be more prominently discussed in the main body. Similarly, the noise model is a simple symmetric channel, and a brief discussion of how the results might change under more complex, label-dependent noise models would be welcome.

4.  **Experimental Scale:** The experiment is a valuable proof of concept. However, its scale is limited (200 items, 10 volunteers). To further bolster the claims, a comparison on larger, standard benchmark datasets from the entity resolution literature would be beneficial. Furthermore, the comparison to spectral clustering mentioned at the end of Section 4 is crucial but the results are deferred to the supplementary material. Integrating this comparison more centrally into the main paper would provide a stronger empirical baseline.

**Summary of Pro/Con Arguments:**

*   **Pro:**
    *   Establishes a novel and fruitful connection between active clustering and locally encodable source coding.
    *   Introduces AND queries as a superior alternative to standard 'same-cluster' queries, supported by both theory and experiments.
    *   Provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis with both upper and lower bounds for multiple settings.
    *   Includes a practical algorithm and real-world experimental validation.

*   **Con:**
    *   Clarity could be improved regarding the motivation for the generalized k-ary query and the transitions between problem settings.
    *   The experimental evaluation is on a small scale and would benefit from comparison on larger, standard benchmarks.
    *   Relies on a simplified i.i.d. model for labels.

**Decision:**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions in originality, quality, and significance. The novel theoretical framing and the introduction of the more effective AND query are likely to influence future work in active learning and crowdsourcing. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to presentation and the scope of the experimental section, and they do not undermine the core contributions. The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of research. I recommend acceptance.
