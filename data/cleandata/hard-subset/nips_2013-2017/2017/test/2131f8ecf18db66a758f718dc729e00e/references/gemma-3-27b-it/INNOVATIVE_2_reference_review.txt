This paper presents a fascinating and ambitious attempt to frame a crowdsourcing query problem as a locally encodable source coding problem, leveraging information-theoretic principles to derive lower bounds and design efficient querying schemes. The core idea – shifting the perspective from direct label elicitation to function evaluation via AND queries – is genuinely novel and potentially impactful, particularly for approximate recovery in noisy settings. While the technical details are dense and the experimental validation is preliminary, the boldness of the approach and the potential for significant theoretical and practical implications strongly outweigh the current limitations.

**Summary:** The paper investigates minimizing queries to a crowd oracle for label learning (clustering). It frames this as locally encodable source coding, deriving lower bounds and proposing AND queries as an alternative to 'same cluster' (XOR) queries.  Crucially, it demonstrates that AND queries can achieve better performance, especially with noisy oracles, and presents a scheme with O(n log k/δ) queries for (1-δ)-approximate recovery with k clusters.  Experiments on real datasets with Amazon Mechanical Turk provide initial support.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The connection to locally encodable source coding is a genuinely new perspective on the crowdsourcing query problem. This is not a simple incremental improvement but a fundamentally different framing.
*   **Theoretical Depth:** The paper rigorously utilizes information theory to derive lower bounds and establish the optimality of the proposed AND-query scheme. The analysis, while complex, appears sound.
*   **Potential Impact:**  The AND-query approach offers a practical pathway to reduce querying costs in real-world crowdsourcing applications, especially where oracle errors are a concern.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly defines the problem, assumptions (noisy oracle, prior knowledge), and evaluation criteria.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of a real-world experiment, even with its limitations, is commendable and provides initial support for the theoretical findings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Technical Density:** The paper is mathematically dense, and the explanations of some concepts (e.g., locally encodable source coding) could be more accessible. The leap from information-theoretic bounds to practical algorithms isn’t always fully fleshed out.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The experimental validation is limited in scope. A more extensive evaluation on diverse datasets and with a larger number of crowd workers would significantly strengthen the claims. The comparison to other existing crowdsourcing algorithms isn't robust enough.
*   **Assumptions:** The independence assumption of query errors might not always hold in practice. Exploring the impact of correlated errors would be valuable.
*   **Algorithm 1 Complexity:** The implementation complexity of Algorithm 1 in the noisy query setting could be prohibitive for very large datasets, though the theoretical runtime is promising.
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:** Some notation is inconsistent or unexplained (e.g. definition of T in section 3.3)

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a highly novel and conceptually significant approach to a challenging problem.
*   The theoretical results are rigorous and offer valuable insights into the fundamental limits of querying with noisy oracles.
*   The AND-query scheme has the potential to significantly improve the efficiency of crowdsourcing applications.
*   The experimental validation, while preliminary, supports the theoretical findings.
*   The potential for future work extending this framework is substantial.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is technically demanding and may be inaccessible to a broad audience.
*   The experimental validation is limited and does not fully demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach.
*   The assumptions about query error independence may not hold in all real-world scenarios.
*   The complexity of Algorithm 1 needs further analysis.

**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend acceptance. While the paper has some weaknesses, its novelty, theoretical depth, and potential impact are compelling. The authors have tackled a difficult problem with a highly original and insightful approach. The experimental validation, while limited, is a positive step, and the potential for future work is significant. I believe that this paper represents a valuable contribution to the NIPS community and warrants publication despite the minor flaws. A minor revision addressing some of the clarity issues and further detailing the algorithmic complexity would be beneficial. I specifically suggest expanding the discussion on the assumption of query independence and potential avenues for mitigating its impact.
