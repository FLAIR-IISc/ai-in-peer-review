## NIPS 2017 Review: Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the problem of clustering/labeling with limited pairwise queries to an oracle (e.g., crowdworkers), focusing on non-adaptive querying schemes. The authors frame this as a locally encodable source coding problem, deriving information-theoretic lower bounds on query complexity.  A key contribution is demonstrating that "AND" queries can outperform traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries, especially in approximate recovery scenarios, and providing a practical algorithm leveraging this. They also address the setting with noisy oracle responses and validate their theoretical findings with a real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The work builds upon a growing body of research on clustering with queries, particularly in the context of crowdsourcing for entity resolution (references [4, 8, 20, 33]). It connects this to classical information theory, specifically source coding and, crucially, the less-explored area of locally encodable codes [23].  The paper acknowledges related concepts like locally decodable codes [19, 16] and smooth compression [23], distinguishing its focus on *encodability*. They also place their work in the context of existing algorithms for noisy queries [21].

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Framing:**  The perspective of framing querying as locally encodable source coding is insightful and unlocks the application of powerful information-theoretic tools.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides well-defined theoretical results, including lower bounds and performance guarantees for their proposed scheme. The proof structure appears sound (though detailed verification requires more time – see caveats below).
* **AND Query Insight:** The demonstration that AND queries can be superior to XOR queries for approximate recovery is a significant and counterintuitive finding.
* **Practical Validation:** The crowdsourcing experiment, while not extensive, offers preliminary evidence supporting the effectiveness of the AND query approach in a real-world setting.
* **Handling Noise:** Addressing the realistic scenario of noisy oracle responses adds practical relevance to the work.
* **Clear Connection to Existing Literature:** The authors do a good job of situating their work within relevant areas, citing a comprehensive list of related publications.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Proofs:**  The proofs appear dense and challenging to follow. A more detailed walkthrough of the key arguments (e.g., in the lower bound derivation) would greatly enhance understanding.  I couldn't fully verify all claims within the time constraints of the review.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of independent errors for queries, while common, might not always hold in practice.  The paper acknowledges this but doesn't deeply explore potential alternatives.
* **Experiment Scope:** The crowdsourcing experiment is relatively small-scale. Expanding it with larger datasets, more workers, and a wider range of parameters would strengthen the empirical validation. The choice of movies as a dataset, while a starting point, may not be representative of all clustering scenarios.
* **Algorithm 1’s Practicality:** While the theoretical guarantees for Algorithm 1 are strong, the computational cost of constructing the d-regular graph and implementing the decoding step might be substantial for very large datasets.  A discussion of scalability would be helpful.
* **Limited Discussion of Generalization:** While the authors mention that results extend to k > 2, the paper largely focuses on the k=2 case. Expanding the analysis and experimental validation to larger values of k would be valuable.




**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  The paper is technically sound, with a clear logical flow and well-supported claims *given the complexity of the proofs*.  The complete nature of the work is reasonable for a theoretical NIPS submission, though practical considerations (scalability) could be addressed more thoroughly. The authors acknowledge limitations and potential weaknesses.  (Score: 4/5)
* **Clarity:** The writing is generally clear, but the technical details of the proofs could be presented more accessibly. The organization is logical.  (Score: 3.5/5)
* **Originality:** The framing of the problem as locally encodable source coding and the finding regarding AND queries are novel and contribute meaningfully to the field.  (Score: 4/5)
* **Significance:** The results have the potential to be impactful in areas such as entity resolution, data integration, and crowdsourcing. The theoretical insights provide a fundamental understanding of query complexity, and the AND query approach offers a practical improvement over existing methods. (Score: 4/5)

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to a challenging problem.
* The AND query insight is significant and offers a practical advantage.
* The connection to information theory provides a valuable perspective.
* The empirical validation, while limited, is encouraging.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the proofs makes it difficult to fully assess their correctness.
* The scalability of the proposed algorithm needs further consideration.
* The experimental evaluation could be more extensive and representative.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The core ideas are strong and original, and the theoretical results are compelling. The authors should focus on improving the clarity of the proofs, addressing the scalability concerns, and expanding the experimental validation. A more detailed explanation of the decoding process in Algorithm 1 would also be beneficial. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.  A more detailed scrutiny of the proofs during rebuttal might be warranted.
