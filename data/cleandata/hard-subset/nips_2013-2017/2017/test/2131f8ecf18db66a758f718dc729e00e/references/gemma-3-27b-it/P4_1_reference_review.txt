This paper investigates the problem of semisupervised clustering via querying an oracle (e.g., crowd workers) to determine relationships between elements. It frames this as a locally encodable source coding problem, deriving information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required for exact and approximate recovery of labels, and proposes a novel querying scheme based on AND operations that outperforms traditional “same cluster” (XOR) queries, particularly in noisy settings.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly connecting clustering with information theory and crowdsourcing. The exploration of locally encodable source coding as a lens for analyzing this problem is a novel idea. The derivation of lower bounds and the introduction of AND queries offer potential improvements over existing approaches. The experimental validation on real datasets (using Amazon Mechanical Turk) is a valuable addition, demonstrating practical relevance.  The authors meticulously relate their work to existing literature in crowdsourcing, information theory, and clustering, highlighting their contributions within these fields.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of locally encodable source coding to the querying problem for clustering is a new perspective. The introduction of AND queries as a potential improvement over XOR queries is a significant idea.
* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis, utilizing information-theoretic concepts, appears rigorous. The proofs are outlined (with details deferred to supplementary material), and the connection to established results (e.g., Massey’s work) is clearly articulated.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The problem setup is clearly defined, and the main contributions are explicitly stated. The notation is consistent.
* **Significance:**  The potential for reducing the number of queries in crowdsourcing applications is substantial, as each query carries a cost (time, money). Improved query efficiency directly translates to cost savings and faster results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Proofs:** While the outlines of the proofs are provided, the complexity of the derivations may hinder full comprehension without detailed examination of the supplementary material.  This is somewhat unavoidable in a theoretical paper of this nature, but could be mitigated by providing more intuitive explanations of the key steps.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation focuses on validating the algorithm’s performance with AND queries. While this is valuable, it would be beneficial to include a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art clustering algorithms using “same cluster” queries on the same datasets, especially to demonstrate the practical advantage of the proposed approach under various conditions.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of independent errors in queries, even with repeated queries disallowed, is stated. While justified, the paper doesn’t thoroughly discuss the sensitivity of the results to deviations from this assumption.  Real-world scenarios might exhibit correlated errors.
* **Limited Exploration of k > 2:** While the paper claims the results extend to k > 2, the primary focus and the detailed analysis are centered around the k=2 case.  A more in-depth discussion of the challenges and potential modifications for larger values of k would strengthen the paper.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a relevant problem in clustering and crowdsourcing.
* The introduction of locally encodable source coding offers a fresh perspective and leads to concrete improvements in query efficiency.
* The experimental validation, although limited, supports the theoretical findings.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contributions.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The complexity of the proofs might limit the accessibility of the paper to a broader audience.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive to demonstrate a clear practical advantage over existing methods.
* The assumption of independent query errors may not hold in all real-world scenarios.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The novelty of the approach, the rigorous theoretical analysis, and the potential practical impact make it a worthwhile contribution. While the weaknesses mentioned above warrant consideration, they do not detract substantially from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors consider expanding the experimental evaluation and providing more intuitive explanations of the core theoretical results. I also suggest that the authors clarify the applicability of their results for larger values of k.
