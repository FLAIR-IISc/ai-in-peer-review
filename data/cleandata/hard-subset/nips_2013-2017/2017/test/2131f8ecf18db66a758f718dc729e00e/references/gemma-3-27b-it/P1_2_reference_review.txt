## NIPS 2017 Review: Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the problem of clustering/label learning from pairwise queries to an oracle (e.g., crowdworkers). It frames this problem as a locally encodable source coding problem, establishing information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries needed for both exact and approximate recovery of labels, considering both noiseless and noisy oracle responses. A key contribution is the demonstration that “AND” queries can outperform traditional “same cluster” (XOR) queries, particularly in the approximate recovery setting, and proposes an algorithm leveraging AND queries with strong theoretical guarantees. The work also includes validation via a small crowdsourcing experiment on movie genres.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon a growing body of work on clustering with queries, drawing connections to entity resolution, crowdsourcing, and information theory. It correctly positions itself within the context of recent work on adaptive querying schemes (e.g., [33, 30, 8, 22, 21]) and the use of crowdsourcing for clustering. The connection to source coding is insightful, and the framing as locally encodable source coding appropriately highlights its novelty, referencing the limited existing work in this area [23]. The paper effectively positions its findings in relation to other relevant areas like locally decodable codes [19, 16] and smooth compression [23].

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Framing:**  The formulation of the querying problem as locally encodable source coding provides a fresh perspective and enables the application of tools from information theory to derive meaningful bounds.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents rigorous theoretical analysis, deriving lower bounds on query complexity and establishing performance guarantees for the proposed AND-query-based algorithm.
* **AND Query Insight:** The demonstration that AND queries can outperform XOR queries for approximate recovery is a significant contribution. This is a non-trivial finding that challenges conventional approaches.
* **Noise Handling:** The consideration of noisy oracle responses is realistic and essential for practical applications. The algorithm designed for the noisy case appears well-motivated and provides practical improvements.
* **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of a crowdsourcing experiment, though small, provides some empirical support for the theoretical findings and demonstrates the potential real-world benefits of the proposed approach.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear progression of ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiment:** The crowdsourcing experiment is relatively small in scale. While it provides some validation, it would be strengthened by more extensive evaluation on diverse datasets and with a larger number of workers.
* **Algorithm Complexity:** While the theoretical guarantees are strong, the practical implementation complexity of the proposed algorithms (especially the noisy case) may be significant.  A more detailed discussion of computational costs would be helpful.
* **Prior Knowledge Assumption:** The algorithm relies on knowledge of the prior distribution of labels (p). While the authors acknowledge the feasibility of estimating this prior, the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to inaccuracies in this estimate is not thoroughly investigated.
* **Comparison to Existing Methods:** The paper briefly mentions existing clustering algorithms like spectral clustering. A more detailed and comparative analysis of the proposed method's performance against established clustering techniques, particularly in the noisy setting, would be valuable.
* **Presentation of Theorem 5:** While the theorem is significant, the proof sketch is quite dense. A clearer and more intuitive explanation of the core ideas behind the proof would enhance readability.



**Detailed Evaluation based on Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous analysis and well-supported claims. The proofs, while sometimes dense, appear correct. The work represents a complete investigation within its chosen scope.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear, but some sections (e.g., the proof of Theorem 5) could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The notation is consistent and well-defined.
* **Originality:** The framing of the problem as locally encodable source coding and the demonstration of the superiority of AND queries are novel contributions. The paper builds upon existing work but presents a unique and valuable perspective.
* **Significance:** The results have the potential to be impactful, particularly in scenarios where querying costs are high (e.g., crowdsourcing). The theoretical bounds provide a fundamental understanding of the trade-offs involved in querying-based clustering.  The practical implications of using AND queries could lead to more efficient and cost-effective crowdsourcing applications.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to querying-based clustering.
* The insights regarding AND queries offer a practical improvement over existing methods.
* The theoretical results provide fundamental limits on query complexity.
* The experimental validation, while limited, supports the theoretical findings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The crowdsourcing experiment is small and lacks extensive evaluation.
* The practical implementation complexity of the algorithm may be a concern.
* The assumption of prior knowledge could limit applicability in some scenarios.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field. The novel framing, rigorous theoretical analysis, and insightful results regarding AND queries make it a valuable addition to the NIPS program. While the experimental validation could be stronger and the implementation complexity deserves further consideration, the theoretical contributions are significant.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend accepting the paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Expand the discussion of the algorithm's computational complexity.
2.  Investigate the sensitivity of the algorithm to inaccuracies in the prior distribution.
3. Provide a more intuitive explanation of the proof of Theorem 5.
4.  Consider adding a more detailed comparison to existing clustering methods.
