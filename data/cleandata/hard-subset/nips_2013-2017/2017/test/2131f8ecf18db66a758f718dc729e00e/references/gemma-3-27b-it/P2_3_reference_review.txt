## NIPS 2017 Review: Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the problem of clustering/labeling with limited, potentially noisy, pairwise queries to an oracle (e.g., crowd workers). It frames this as a locally encodable source coding problem and derives information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries needed for exact and approximate recovery of labels. A key contribution is demonstrating that AND queries can outperform traditional “same cluster” (XOR) queries for approximate recovery, particularly in noisy settings. The authors also present a practical algorithm utilizing AND queries and validate it through both theoretical analysis and a small-scale crowdsourcing experiment.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Framing:** The connection to locally encodable source coding is a clever and insightful way to analyze the problem, bringing tools from information theory to bear on a crowdsourcing/clustering challenge.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides rigorous information-theoretic lower bounds, which are valuable for understanding the fundamental limits of the problem.  The proof techniques appear solid, and the derivations are presented with reasonable clarity.
* **AND Query Insight:** The demonstration that AND queries can be superior to XOR queries is a significant and counterintuitive finding. This offers a concrete algorithmic improvement for practical applications.
* **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of a crowdsourcing experiment, even on a relatively small scale, strengthens the paper by providing some evidence of the practical benefit of the proposed approach.  The observation that majority voting *increases* errors is interesting and reinforces the need for more sophisticated techniques.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper tackles both noiseless and noisy query scenarios, providing a more complete picture of the problem and its solutions.
* **Extension to k Clusters:**  While much of the analysis focuses on k=2, the extension to larger k is mentioned and the algorithm is presented as applicable to the general case.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity in Motivation:**  While the introduction lays out the problem, the motivation for focusing on *non-adaptive* querying is not fully convincing. The authors state it's for parallelizability, but a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs (vs. adaptive querying) would be helpful.
* **Complexity of Proofs:** The proofs, particularly those relating to the noisy query setting, are dense and challenging to follow in full detail. While the main ideas are conveyed, more intuition or intermediate steps could improve accessibility.  Referring to details in the supplementary material is helpful but doesn’t fully address this.
* **Limited Experimental Scope:** The crowdsourcing experiment is limited in scale and scope. While it provides a valuable proof-of-concept, more extensive experiments with varying parameters (e.g., oracle error rate, number of workers per query) would strengthen the empirical support.
* **Connection to Existing Work:**  While the paper references relevant work, the connection to the broader literature on active learning and query selection for clustering could be more explicitly discussed. It's not clear how this work builds upon or differs significantly from those areas.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper doesn’t address practical issues such as the cost of different types of queries or the potential for worker fatigue in responding to AND queries.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis. The work is reasonably complete, although the empirical evaluation could be stronger. The authors are careful in outlining the assumptions and limitations of their work. **(Strong)**
* **Clarity:**  The writing is generally clear, but some sections (especially the proofs) are dense.  Better organization and more intermediate steps would improve readability. **(Good)**
* **Originality:**  The framing of the problem as locally encodable source coding and the discovery of the benefits of AND queries are novel contributions. The paper builds upon existing work in information theory and crowdsourcing but offers a fresh perspective. **(Strong)**
* **Significance:**  The results are potentially significant, particularly the finding that AND queries can outperform XOR queries. This has practical implications for the design of crowdsourcing systems for clustering and entity resolution. The theoretical lower bounds also provide valuable insights into the fundamental limits of the problem. **(Good)**



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to a challenging problem.
* The AND query insight is a significant contribution that could lead to practical improvements in crowdsourcing applications.
* The theoretical results are rigorous and provide valuable insights into the problem's fundamental limits.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The proofs are complex and may be inaccessible to some readers.
* The experimental validation is limited in scope.
* The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work in active learning and query selection.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong submission** that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The novelty of the framing, the theoretical rigor, and the practical insight regarding AND queries outweigh the weaknesses in clarity and experimental scope. I recommend **acceptance** to NIPS 2017, with the caveat that the authors may be encouraged to provide more detailed explanations of the proofs and to consider expanding the experimental validation in a future version.
