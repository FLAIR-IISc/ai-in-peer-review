Summary
The paper studies nonadaptive, budgeted querying for semisupervised clustering/label recovery under a probabilistic prior on labels and possibly noisy oracle answers. The authors recast the problem as locally encodable source coding: each query/encoded bit depends on at most Δ source labels, which is a realistic constraint for human-in-the-loop settings. The main technical contributions are:
- Exact recovery, noiseless: With Δ-ary XOR (parity) queries, show achievability at m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) where α = 0.5(1 + (1 − 4p(1 − p))^Δ), strictly < n for a range of p even with Δ = 2. Provide a converse that improves on the classical n h(p) when locality is enforced.
- Approximate recovery, noiseless: Derive a locality-aware lower bound (Theorem 3) and argue that “same-cluster” (XOR) queries are fundamentally suboptimal for rate–distortion via Massey/Ancheta linear code limits. Propose pairwise AND queries with a simple decoder and analyze the Hamming error as a function of m via a Poisson left-degree calculation on a random bipartite graph.
- Approximate recovery, noisy answers, k classes: Generalize to a k-ary pairwise query that returns i if both endpoints are label i, else 0, with symmetric noise. A thresholding algorithm over a d-regular graph achieves (1 − δ)-accuracy with m = O(n log(k/δ)). A small crowdsourcing study suggests AND-type queries are easier for humans than “same-cluster” queries.

Relation to prior work is made to crowdsourced clustering, sparse-graph codes, smooth compression, and locally decodable/encodable coding; the paper positions its results as the first nontrivial limits for locally encodable source coding under nonadaptive querying.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The locally encodable source-coding lens is insightful and aligns the querying constraint with classical information-theoretic limits. This framing yields meaningful new converses beyond n h(p).
- Provable gains from switching query primitives: The AND query advantage for approximate recovery is well argued. For k = 2, the analysis clearly exploits the asymmetry (no false positives) and the neighborhood structure to show exponential decay in error with degree.
- Clean, scalable noisy algorithm: The d-regular graph construction with simple per-node thresholding is attractive, nonadaptive, and has tight, interpretable complexity m = Θ(n log(k/δ)).
- Practical angle: The crowdsourcing motivation is compelling; the observation that humans find AND-type questions easier than “same-cluster” is plausible and supported by a small study.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Clarity and correctness of statements/proofs:
  - Theorem 3 (approximate recovery lower bound under locality) is central but its proof is deferred, and the bound’s form (especially the term involving e^{Δ h′(·)}) is unusual. More intuition, a simplified corollary, and a sanity check against known rate–distortion limits would help. As written, it’s hard to assess tightness.
  - There is a pervasive typesetting ambiguity: “nd2” appears throughout but clearly means n d / 2 (number of edges in a d-regular graph). This caused confusion until the experiment section. Please fix globally.
  - Theorem 1’s expurgation/two-codeword confusion argument is standard but the typical set expurgation over GF(2) parity checks deserves a more self-contained sketch (e.g., explicitly bounding the union over typical pairs and quantifying the o(1) terms).
  - Theorem 2’s converse (locality-aware) uses a projection and Hamming-ball argument. The formula is plausible, but the derivation is terse; a figure or more detailed steps to justify the ball radius ((1 − ρ) r(p) Δ m / ρ) and the entropy term would improve confidence. Also quantify regimes where the converse beats n h(p) beyond the isolated p ≈ 0.035 for Δ=2.
- Scope of “XOR is suboptimal”:
  - The Massey/Ancheta linear rate–distortion gap supports the claim at p = 0.5; the conjectured general p bound (Conj. 1) should be clearly delimited as conjecture. It would strengthen the paper to provide a rigorous lower bound for XOR-only schemes even if looser than the conjecture, and to compare against a simple XOR-based approximate decoder (beyond random queries + ML asymptotics).
- Experimental evaluation:
  - The user study is small and the independence assumptions are violated (the same worker answers many queries). It nevertheless shows the AND question is easier for humans, but does not convincingly validate the m vs δ scaling.
  - Baselines: For “same-cluster” with noise, spectral clustering on a graph of XOR answers is a weak baseline for nonadaptive query design. Consider: (i) best-known nonadaptive schemes from [21,22], (ii) EM/Dawid–Skene-style worker models, and (iii) a matched-threshold method for XOR analogous to your AND threshold for fairness.
  - k > 2 experiments are absent; yet the main noisy result is for general k. Even a synthetic evaluation illustrating the m = O(n log(k/δ)) scaling with varying k would strengthen the empirical case.
- Related work coverage:
  - The AND query and thresholding over random graphs is reminiscent of sparse-graph source coding with LDGM/LDPC-style encoders, as well as nonadaptive group testing (though tests there are OR rather than AND). Please discuss connections/differences to nonadaptive group testing and to LDGM-based lossy compression (e.g., Montanari et al., also classic LDGM rate–distortion results), as they are close in spirit to “locally encodable” codes.
- Practicality of k-ary queries: The proposed k-ary pairwise query (“which class do both belong to, or 0 otherwise?”) may be unintuitive for workers when items differ; discuss UI design or a decomposition into binary subtasks.

Pros for acceptance
- New and relevant theoretical angle (local encodability) with nontrivial converses and achievable schemes.
- Clear message with actionable implication: prefer AND-style pairwise queries for approximate, nonadaptive crowdsourced labeling.
- Simple, scalable algorithm with strong query complexity.

Cons for acceptance
- Clarity issues (notation, deferred core proof), some technical gaps (rigor around XOR lower bounds), and limited experimental validation, especially for k > 2.
- Limited comparison to adjacent literatures (group testing, LDGM lossy compression) and to stronger baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix all instances of “nd2” to n d / 2 and tighten notation throughout.
- Bring a simplified, self-contained proof sketch of Theorem 3 into the main text with a corollary that highlights the dependence on Δ and m/n.
- Provide at least synthetic experiments for k > 2, and compare against stronger baselines for XOR queries under noise.
- Expand related work discussion to include nonadaptive group testing and LDGM-based lossy compression; clarify how your bounds differ from known LDGM locality limits.

Recommendation
Borderline accept (weak accept). The core ideas are interesting and potentially influential for both theory (locally encodable source coding) and practice (design of nonadaptive crowdsourcing queries). With improved clarity and stronger empirical comparisons, this could be a solid NeurIPS paper. My confidence is medium-high.
