Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonadaptive, budgeted query-based clustering/labeling through an information-theoretic lens. Given n items with labels in {0,…,k−1} drawn i.i.d. from a known prior p, the learner can issue a fixed set of queries, each a deterministic function of at most Δ labels, producing (mostly) binary answers. The goal is to exactly or approximately reconstruct labels with as few queries m as possible, in both noiseless and noisy settings. The key conceptual contribution is to cast this as locally encodable source coding: a compressed vector Y of length m where each bit depends on only Δ source symbols. This complements prior work on locally decodable codes and smooth compression, and bridges query-efficient clustering with information-theoretic limits.

Main results:
- Noiseless exact recovery (k=2): Using Δ-ary XOR queries (Δ=2 corresponds to “same-cluster” queries), the authors show achievability m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) with α = 1/2(1+(1−4p(1−p))^Δ), via a random constant-row-weight query matrix and a maximum-likelihood decoder; for some p, this yields m<n. They also prove a lower bound that beats the trivial nh(p) for certain p, by arguing via locality constraints and sphere-packing in the query-output space.
- Noiseless approximate recovery (k=2): They derive a locality-aware lower bound for Hamming distortion δ (proof in appendix). They argue that “same-cluster” (XOR) queries are provably suboptimal for approximate recovery (connecting to classical limitations of linear rate–distortion codes, e.g., Ancheta–Massey), and conjecture a concrete lower bound for XOR-based schemes. They then propose pairwise AND queries with a simple decoder (“predict 1 if any incident AND answer is 1”), and give an explicit expression for the error probability that beats the XOR lower bound over a range of (m,δ), especially for p near 1/2.
- Noisy approximate recovery (general k): With a k-ary AND-like query Q(X,X′)=i if X=X′=i, else 0, and answers flipped to a uniformly random incorrect symbol with probability q, they show that using a d-regular graph (m=nd/2 queries) and a per-node thresholding rule yields (1−δ)-accurate recovery with m=O(n log(k/δ)); analysis is via Chernoff bounds and dependency graphs. They further show the scheme works with known cluster sizes ni (rather than priors), using a random permutation in graph construction.
- Experiments: A small crowdsourcing study on 200 movies with 1000 pairwise queries shows markedly fewer errors for AND queries than “same-cluster” queries (105 vs. 234 wrong responses), and empirical performance close to the theoretical curves for the proposed algorithm. Additional synthetic results compare to spectral clustering on “same-cluster” answers.

This builds on query-based clustering/entity resolution (e.g., Ashtiani et al.; Mazumdar–Saha on noisy queries), crowdsourcing models (Karger–Oh–Shah), and coding-theoretic notions (locally decodable/compressible codes; rate–distortion; Massey’s joint source–channel coding).

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: introduces and leverages “locally encodable source coding” to derive lower bounds under locality constraints; insightful link between query design and information theory.
- Clear message: XOR/same-cluster queries are not the best primitive for approximate/noisy recovery; AND-type queries do better.
- Theoretical contributions: achievability below n for exact recovery at k=2 and certain p; nontrivial locality-aware lower bounds; O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive query complexity for noisy approximate recovery with a simple, scalable decoder.
- Practical relevance: AND queries appear easier for workers, empirically reducing noise; simple nonadaptive scheme is parallelizable.
- Some empirical validation, including a real (albeit small) crowdsourcing study.

Weaknesses
- Scope and rigor:
  - Several results are specialized to k=2; extensions to k>2 are primarily in the noisy setting and lack empirical validation.
  - The exact-recovery achievability relies on ML decoding over a random constant-weight XOR measurement matrix; computational feasibility and robustness are not discussed.
  - Theorem statements and proofs are terse; some arguments (expurgation in Theorem 1, lower bound in Theorem 2, Theorem 3 proof) need careful, self-contained presentation in the main text to assess rigor.
  - The “same-cluster” lower bound is a conjecture; comparisons hinge partly on it (though the paper is cautious).
- Clarity and notation:
  - m=nd/2 is repeatedly typeset as “nd2,” which is ambiguous (readable as n·d/2 vs. n·d^2). Please fix throughout.
  - Several typos and formatting issues (inline footnotes, missing equation numbers, occasional grammatical errors) hinder readability.
  - The error expression for the AND scheme (Theorem 4) could be simplified or accompanied by tight, interpretable bounds.
- Empirics:
  - Small-scale experiment with volunteers; noise independence is assumed but not checked; no k>2 experiments; limited baselines (e.g., no EM/Dawid–Skene or modern crowdsourcing algorithms under comparable budgets).
  - The comparison to spectral clustering on noisy “same-cluster” answers is not fully convincing without tuning and stronger baselines.

Quality
Overall technically sound and thoughtful. The achievability and concentration arguments for the noisy scheme are convincing; the exact-recovery XOR achievability is plausible. Lower bounds are interesting but would benefit from more detail and tighter constants. Some reliance on conjectures is acceptable if clearly labeled (as it is), but empirical/alternative evidence would strengthen the claim.

Clarity
The paper’s organization is reasonable, but clarity suffers from notation/typos and compressed proofs. Improving exposition (clear statement of models, explicit m=nd/2, intuition for thresholds, and proof sketches) would help. Figures are useful.

Originality
High. The locally encodable source coding perspective and AND-query advantage for approximate/noisy recovery appear novel in this community. The O(n log(k/δ)) result in the noisy nonadaptive setting is a notable advance over O(n log n) for same-cluster queries.

Significance
Moderate to high. The work can influence how practitioners design crowdsourcing queries (favoring AND-like functions), and provides a principled framework for query complexity under locality. The theoretical insights are likely to spur follow-up work on other query primitives, adaptive schemes, and tighter locality-aware bounds.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel, well-motivated theory linking query-based clustering and local source coding.
- Strong, simple algorithm with near-linear query complexity under noise.
- Clear negative result for XOR and positive result for AND, with both theory and empirical support.
- Relevant to NeurIPS audiences in crowdsourcing, clustering, and information theory.

Cons:
- Clarity/notation issues; some proofs relegated to appendix and are hard to assess.
- Experiments are small and lack strong baselines; no k>2 validation.
- Exact-recovery achievability uses ML, with no computational discussion.
- Some claims (XOR lower bound) are conjectural.

Questions and suggestions
- Please fix the m=nd/2 notation and define d once clearly; reconcile with experimental setup.
- Provide tighter, interpretable bounds for Theorem 4 (e.g., show δ ≈ p e^{−Θ(m/n)} with explicit constants).
- Include a brief computational discussion for exact-recovery decoding (or a polynomial-time alternative with slightly worse m).
- Add baselines (e.g., Dawid–Skene/EM, majority-vote with redundancy, recent coding-theoretic crowdsourcing methods) under the same budget; consider k>2 synthetic experiments.
- Discuss robustness to correlated worker errors and to prior misspecification.

Minor comments
- Check references for duplicates and completeness; ensure correct citations for Ancheta–Massey and related coding limits.
- Clean up footnotes and grammar for readability.

Overall, this is a creative and valuable contribution; with improved clarity and stronger empirical validation, it would be a solid fit for NeurIPS.
