Summary
This paper studies non-adaptive, budgeted crowdsourced clustering/labeling through the lens of “locally encodable source coding,” where each query/encoded bit depends on at most Δ labels. The key conceptual move is to treat query design as a constrained source coding problem and to exploit non-linear, low-arity functions of labels instead of standard “same-cluster” (XOR) queries. The paper delivers:

- Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2): A parity-based querying scheme with Δ-ary XOR queries that achieves m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) (< n for a range of priors), and a locality-aware lower bound that can beat the classical n h(p) bound.
- Noiseless, approximate recovery (k=2): A new lower bound under locality constraints. It argues that XOR queries are fundamentally limited for approximate recovery (invoking Massey’s linear rate–distortion limits) and proposes pairwise AND queries with a simple decoder, giving explicit δ(m) trade-offs that outperform XOR-based approaches.
- Noisy, approximate recovery (general k): A pairwise, k-ary “generalized AND” query (returns i if both labels are i, else 0) on a d-regular graph, with a threshold decoder. It achieves (1−δ)-accurate recovery with m = O(n log(k/δ)) non-adaptive queries under independent per-query noise. A small real crowdsourcing experiment (200 movies, 1000 pairwise queries) supports the theory and suggests AND-style questions incur fewer errors than “same-cluster” questions.

Originality and significance
- The locally encodable source coding view is fresh and compelling, and yields new, locality-aware lower bounds that improve on classical entropy limits in some regimes.
- The advocacy for AND (non-linear) queries over XOR “same-cluster” is a bold and well-argued shift that can materially change how practitioners design crowdsourcing tasks.
- The O(n log(k/δ)) guarantee with simple, non-adaptive, pairwise queries and a thresholding decoder under noise is clean and potentially impactful.
- The empirical observation that workers make fewer mistakes on AND questions is practically valuable.

Quality and technical soundness
- The theoretical development for exact recovery (Theorem 1) and locality-aware lower bound (Theorem 2) are plausible and align with coding-theoretic intuition (typical set, expurgation).
- The argument that XOR is suboptimal for approximate recovery relies on classical limitations of linear rate–distortion codes; however, the “same-cluster” lower bound (Conjecture 1) is only established at p=1/2. Claims that AND “beats the lower bound” should be phrased carefully where the bound is conjectural.
- The AND-based algorithm in noise (Theorem 5) is simple and the concentration-based analysis for per-node errors is standard and convincing. The sample complexity scaling is attractive.
- Important inconsistency: throughout Section 3.3 and the experiments, the paper conflates the number of queries m with nd^2. For a d-regular graph on n nodes, the number of queries/edges is m = nd/2, not nd^2. The algorithm and proofs count d queries per node, so the correct scaling is m = Θ(nd). This does not change the claimed O(n log(k/δ)) result (since d = Θ(log(k/δ))) but the formulas and figures should be fixed for correctness and clarity.
- Theorem 3 (locality-aware rate–distortion lower bound) is interesting but the bound expression is hard to parse and the proof is deferred; a more digestible statement and intuition would help.
- Computational aspects: exact-recovery decoding in Theorem 1 is ML and likely intractable; the paper should comment on computational vs information-theoretic optimality.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear but has several typos and notational slips (e.g., m=nd^2 vs nd/2; occasional missing parentheses; minor grammar). Figures are helpful. Please polish Section 3.3 and the experiment section to consistently use m = nd/2 and align thresholds/expectations accordingly.
- It would help to explicitly relate the AND scheme to known primitives in non-adaptive group testing (there is a close conceptual connection), and to discuss why your objective (label recovery vs sparse set identification) and noise model differ.

Relation to prior work
- The link to locally decodable source codes and smooth compression is apt. The paper would benefit from a brief comparison to non-adaptive group testing (AND-like tests, thresholding decoders, and O(k log n)–style bounds), clarifying similarities and differences.
- The discussion of XOR limitations is well referenced (Massey, Ancheta), but the conjectural status should be emphasized when drawing empirical comparisons.

Empirical evaluation
- The small crowdsourcing study is neat and aligns with the premise that AND questions are easier. However, it is limited in scope (one binary task, small n). Including at least one additional dataset or a robustness check (varying k, priors, worker variability) would strengthen the empirical case.
- The synthetic comparison to spectral clustering for same-cluster queries is reasonable, but please report full details (graph construction, noise levels, how δ is measured) in the main text or clearly refer to the appendix.

Pros
- Conceptually novel framing (locally encodable source coding) with new lower bounds.
- Bold, simple, and practical AND-query primitive with strong theoretical and empirical support.
- Tight sample complexity under noise with a very simple non-adaptive algorithm.
- Relevance to real crowdsourcing practice; suggests better question design.

Cons
- Notational and counting errors around the total number of queries; needs correction.
- Some claims about XOR suboptimality hinge on a conjectured lower bound.
- Limited experimental breadth; only one real dataset and small scale.
- Computational considerations for exact recovery not addressed.

Recommendation: Accept (clear accept if the m=nd/2 vs nd^2 issue and wording around conjectural bounds are fixed)
This paper introduces a genuinely new lens and a practically meaningful primitive (AND queries) that changes the conversation around crowdsourced clustering. Despite minor issues and modest experiments, the novelty and potential impact warrant acceptance. I encourage the authors to fix the query-count inconsistency, clarify the conjectural status of some XOR comparisons, and add a short discussion situating their method w.r.t. group testing. My enthusiasm stems from the bold, simple idea and strong theoretical underpinnings.
