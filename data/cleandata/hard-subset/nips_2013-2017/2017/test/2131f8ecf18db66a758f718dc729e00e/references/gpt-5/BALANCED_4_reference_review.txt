Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonadaptive, budgeted querying for learning labels/clusters via crowdsourcing. Each query is a deterministic function of a small number (typically two) of items’ labels, returning a simple, usually binary, response. The authors cast this as “locally encodable source coding”: compressed bits (query answers) each depend on at most Δ source bits (labels). This viewpoint yields information-theoretic lower bounds and algorithms.

Three regimes are considered:
- Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2): Using parity/XOR queries over Δ elements (for Δ=2 this is “same-cluster”), they give an existence result showing m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries suffice for some α that depends on p and Δ, yielding m < n for a range of priors. They also present a lower bound that can exceed the classical nh(p) bound by incorporating locality (Δ) constraints.
- Noiseless, approximate recovery (k=2): They derive a general lower bound under locality constraints (Theorem 3). Leveraging Massey/Ancheta results on linear rate–distortion, they argue XOR/same-cluster is inherently suboptimal for approximate recovery and propose pairwise AND queries with a simple decoder that outperform same-cluster in the tradeoff between m and Hamming distortion δ.
- Noisy, approximate recovery (k≥2): They propose a pairwise k-ary “AND-like” query returning i if both endpoints have label i, else 0, under a symmetric noise model. On a d-regular query graph with a thresholding decoder, they prove O(n log(k/δ)) queries suffice to recover a (1−δ) fraction of labels. They also show how to operate with only relative cluster sizes. A small crowdsourcing experiment suggests AND queries are easier for workers (fewer errors than same-cluster) and that empirical performance tracks the theory.

This work relates to: crowdsourced clustering with same-cluster queries and query complexity (e.g., Ashtiani et al., Mazumdar & Saha); budget/fidelity tradeoffs in crowdsourcing; locally decodable/smooth compression (Makhdoumi et al., Montanari–Mossel, Mazumdar et al.); sparse-graph coding ideas; and rate–distortion and joint source–channel coding (Massey). The “local encoding” constraint is a complementary angle to the better-studied local decoding literature.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: framing nonadaptive querying as locally encodable source coding is elegant and opens a door to principled lower bounds under locality.
- Clear insight: XOR/same-cluster is not the best primitive for approximate recovery; AND queries exploit asymmetry to create an expectation gap that simple thresholding can leverage, even with noise.
- Solid scaling law: O(n log(k/δ)) queries for noisy approximate recovery with a very simple algorithm is attractive and practically relevant.
- Some new lower bounds under locality constraints and a (provable) AND achievability analysis.
- Initial user study indicating AND queries may indeed be easier for crowd workers.

Weaknesses
- Algorithmic tractability in exact recovery: Theorem 1 relies on ML decoding over a random constant-weight parity design and typical-set expurgation; no efficient decoder is given. The result is existential and of limited algorithmic value.
- Several key technical pieces are deferred or sketched (e.g., Theorem 3’s proof), and some bounds depend on a conjecture (Massey/Ancheta-type lower bound specialized to same-cluster) for the strongest comparative statement.
- Fairness/completeness of empirical evaluation: the real-data study is small, with limited baselines. The same-cluster baseline uses spectral clustering on noisy edges, but the AND method benefits from a k-ary response and a tailored decoder; a more apples-to-apples comparison (e.g., maximum likelihood or EM on same-cluster with the same graph, or robust link-aggregation methods) would strengthen claims.
- Practical assumptions: independent per-query noise and known priors (or exact cluster sizes) are strong; although Theorem 6 relaxes priors, correlations in crowd errors are common.
- Clarity/organization: several typos and notation issues; some constants and parameter ranges are opaque; figures are doing heavy lifting for intuition.

Evaluation against review criteria
- Quality: The main noisy approximate recovery algorithm is sound and clean, with a plausible proof using concentration and dependency bounds. Lower bounds under locality are meaningful. However, the exact recovery result lacks an efficient decoder, and the approximate XOR suboptimality relies partly on conjectural or specialized results.
- Clarity: Generally readable but could be improved. Precise statements of all theorems (including constants and parameter regimes), a clearer exposition of α and its dependence on p, Δ, and tighter linkage between figures and theorems would help. Proof sketches for Theorem 3 in the main text would increase self-containment.
- Originality: High in framing and in highlighting the AND vs XOR contrast under locality and noise; the k-ary pairwise “AND” for k>2 and its analysis are novel to my knowledge.
- Significance: Moderate to high. The O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive scheme with simple decoding and the locality-aware bounds are likely to influence both the theory of query-efficient clustering and practical crowdsourcing design. The impact would be stronger with larger-scale experiments and stronger baselines.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel “locally encodable” perspective and corresponding bounds
- Simple, effective AND-based algorithm with strong scaling in the noisy, approximate regime
- Insightful critique of same-cluster/XOR for approximate recovery
- Initial empirical validation and user-centric rationale

Cons:
- Exact recovery result is existential and computationally impractical
- Reliance on conjectural/specialized lower bounds for strongest XOR comparisons
- Limited experiments and baselines; small-scale, with potential fairness issues
- Clarity and completeness (proofs, parameter regimes) need work

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide an efficient decoding algorithm (or hardness result) for the exact recovery scheme, or explicitly downgrade that result to a purely information-theoretic existence statement.
- Move a proof sketch of Theorem 3 into the main text and clarify constants/ranges for all theorems; tighten the presentation of α, δ̃, and dependencies on Δ.
- Expand empirical evaluation: larger datasets, multiple noise levels, and stronger baselines (e.g., EM/majority-based denoising on same-cluster edges, methods from crowdsourced ER). Report worker time/effort and cognitive load for AND vs same-cluster to substantiate practical claims.
- Discuss robustness to misspecified priors and correlated worker errors; consider simple calibration steps to estimate q and p from data.
- Clarify the fairness of comparing k-ary AND to binary XOR; consider binary reductions of the k-ary query and report sensitivity.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The ideas are compelling and potentially impactful, but the paper in its current form blends existential results with practical claims without sufficient algorithmic or empirical support for some parts. Strengthening clarity, proofs, and experiments would make this a strong submission. Confidence: medium.
