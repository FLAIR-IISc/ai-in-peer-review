## Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the problem of clustering with limited labeled data, framed as a querying problem to an oracle (e.g., crowd workers). The authors connect this problem to locally encodable source coding and derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required for exact and approximate recovery of cluster labels. A key contribution is the proposal of using AND-queries instead of the traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries, demonstrating improved performance, particularly in noisy settings. The work includes both theoretical analysis and a small-scale empirical validation using Amazon Mechanical Turk.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Connection:** The framing of the querying problem as locally encodable source coding is a novel and insightful connection, bringing tools from information theory to bear on a practical problem in crowdsourced clustering.
* **Information-Theoretic Rigor:** The paper provides rigorous information-theoretic lower bounds, establishing fundamental limits on query complexity.
* **AND-Query Proposal:** The identification of AND-queries as potentially superior to XOR-queries is a significant contribution, demonstrating that careful query design can significantly impact performance. The analysis explaining *why* AND queries perform better (reduced information requirement for a negative answer) is convincing.
* **Handling Noise:**  The extension to noisy oracle responses is a practical and important consideration, and the proposed algorithm appears well-suited to this scenario.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The small-scale experiment on Amazon Mechanical Turk provides some evidence supporting the theoretical claims, although limited in scope.
* **Clear Writing (mostly):** The introduction clearly motivates the problem, and the core ideas are presented reasonably well.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experimental validation is quite limited. While it's a positive sign that the authors conducted an experiment, the dataset size (100 movies x 2 clusters) is small and may not fully represent the performance in more complex scenarios. The comparison with spectral clustering on a synthetically generated dataset is welcome, but more comprehensive experiments with varied datasets and parameter settings are needed.
* **Complexity of Proofs:**  While the theoretical results are strong, the proofs appear dense and challenging to follow, even for experts in information theory. More detailed explanations and intermediate steps would improve accessibility.  The reliance on appendices for key proof details is understandable but hinders immediate understanding.
* **Assumptions & Practicality:**  The assumption of knowing the prior distribution *p* (or relative cluster sizes) is stated as "usually easy to estimate," but this is not always the case in real-world applications. The discussion of this assumption could be more nuanced.  Similarly, the assumption of *independent* query errors, while standard, may not always hold in practice (e.g., systematic biases of certain workers).
* **Related Work Discussion:** While related work is cited, the discussion feels somewhat scattered. A more focused discussion tying the work directly to existing crowdsourcing and active learning literature would be beneficial.  For example, the connection to active learning for clustering isn’t explicitly made.
* **Presentation:** Some notation is a bit heavy.  For instance, defining the distortion function *d(X,X̂)* seems unnecessary and clutters the explanation.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound and provides valuable theoretical insights.  The proofs are rigorous, but their complexity makes it difficult to fully assess their correctness without significant effort.
* **Clarity (7/10):** The paper is generally well-written, but some sections are dense and require careful reading. The notation could be streamlined, and more detailed explanations of the proofs would improve clarity.
* **Originality (9/10):** The connection to locally encodable source coding and the proposal of AND-queries are genuinely novel and represent a significant contribution to the field.
* **Significance (8/10):** The results are important for understanding the fundamental limits of crowdsourced clustering and designing efficient querying strategies.  The potential for reducing query costs in real-world applications is significant.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel theoretical framework connecting clustering to information theory.
* Strong information-theoretic lower bounds.
* Practical and insightful proposal of AND-queries.
*  Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in crowdsourcing.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited experimental validation.
* Proofs are dense and difficult to follow.
* Reliance on potentially unrealistic assumptions.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and significant contribution. While the experimental validation is limited and the proofs are complex, the theoretical insights and practical implications are compelling. I believe the paper is a good fit for NIPS and recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors should consider expanding the experimental evaluation and improving the clarity of the proofs in a final version. I’d suggest a minor revision.
