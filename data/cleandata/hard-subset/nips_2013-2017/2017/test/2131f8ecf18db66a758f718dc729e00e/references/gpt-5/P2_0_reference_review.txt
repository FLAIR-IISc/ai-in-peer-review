Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies non-adaptive, budgeted crowdsourced clustering/labeling through an information-theoretic lens. The authors formalize the task as “locally encodable source coding”: each query answer is a function of at most Δ labels (small arity), typically with binary output, and the decoder must reconstruct all or most labels. They contribute:

- Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2): Using random parity (XOR) queries of arity Δ (pairwise when Δ=2), ML decoding, and an expurgation argument, they show exact recovery with m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries for α = 0.5(1+(1−4p(1−p))^Δ), which is < n for a range of imbalanced priors. They also provide an information-theoretic lower bound strictly stronger than n h(p) in some regimes.

- Noiseless, approximate recovery: They derive a locality-aware rate–distortion lower bound for binary labels under Δ-local queries, and argue that XOR (same-cluster) queries are intrinsically sub-optimal for approximate recovery (invoking classical limits of linear codes as rate–distortion codes). They propose pairwise AND queries with a simple two-hop-neighborhood-based decoder and give an explicit performance bound that improves over what XOR-based schemes can achieve for p around 0.5.

- Noisy approximate recovery (k ≥ 2): They define a generalized AND-type pairwise query with k-ary output, a d-regular graph design, and a simple per-node threshold decoder. Under an independent symmetric noise model on query answers, they prove that O(n log(k/δ)) queries suffice to achieve (1−δ)-accurate recovery with high probability. They also show how to use only cluster size proportions instead of full priors.

- Experiments: A small human study (200 movies; 1000 pairwise queries) suggests AND queries yield lower error than same-cluster queries, and empirical error decays with d as predicted.

This work builds on and connects to: same-cluster query clustering (Ashtiani et al.), reliability in crowdsourcing (Karger–Oh–Shah, etc.), locally decodable/smooth compression (Makhdoumi et al., Montanari–Mossel), and rate–distortion limits/linear code limitations (Ancheta–Massey). The “locally encodable” viewpoint is a novel angle that complements the better-studied local decodability line.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The achievability for exact recovery via random Δ-sparse parity queries and ML decoding is standard and the analysis appears sound; the lower bound via covering of low-degree elements and Hamming balls is reasonable. The approximate-recovery lower bound with locality constraints is technically interesting, though its proof is deferred. The AND-based achievability analyses (both noiseless and noisy) rely on clean, transparent concentration arguments. One concern: there is a repeated and likely critical typo stating the number of pairwise queries on a d-regular graph as nd^2 instead of the correct nd/2; the experiments use nd/2. This should be corrected consistently, including in Theorem 5 and Algorithm 1.

- Claims vs. support: Claims that XOR is suboptimal for approximate recovery are well-motivated by known linear rate–distortion limits; however, one of the lower bounds for same-cluster queries is phrased as a conjecture (known at p=0.5). Claims comparing AND to XOR are sometimes based on this specialized bound; the paper should clearly separate proven statements from conjecture-based intuition. The exact-recovery scheme uses ML decoding over a random sparse linear code, which is computationally intractable in general; the paper focuses on information-theoretic limits but should state computational considerations explicitly.

- Experiments: The human study is small-scale (10 volunteers, 1000 queries) but helpful to show that AND questions can be cognitively easier. The empirical curves roughly track theory. Comparisons to stronger baselines (e.g., probabilistic models like Dawid–Skene, or inference using same-cluster queries with an optimized non-ML decoder) are limited.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-organized and readable, with helpful intuition. However, there are several typographical and notation issues:
  - Throughout Section 3.3 and Algorithm 1, correct m = |E| = nd/2, not nd^2.
  - Log bases and normalization are occasionally inconsistent; specify base-2 consistently or note conversions.
  - The definition of (1−δ)-good alternates between worst-case and average-case interpretations; clarify whether guarantees are probabilistic over X or uniform over labelings subject to priors/cluster sizes.
  - Some proofs are deferred; a brief proof sketch for Theorem 3 in the main text would aid understanding.

Originality:
- Framing non-adaptive crowd queries as locally encodable source coding and deriving locality-aware rate–distortion style limits is novel and timely. Observing and exploiting the advantage of AND over XOR in approximate/noisy settings is simple yet insightful. The k-ary generalization and the O(n log(k/δ)) query complexity under noise with a very simple decoder is a clean contribution.

Significance:
- On the theory side, the locality-constrained lower bounds and the demonstration that simple nonlinear queries can outperform XOR for approximate recovery are valuable. Practically, the requirement of k-ary outputs (for k>2) and knowledge of priors (or exact cluster sizes) may limit immediate deployment, but the algorithm’s simplicity and the human-study evidence are promising. The exact-recovery result improving over n in imbalanced priors is interesting but uses an intractable ML decoder, limiting applied impact.

Pros:
- Clear, principled information-theoretic framing with new locality-aware limits.
- Simple, effective AND-query schemes with provable O(n log(k/δ)) query complexity under noise.
- Insightful negative result on XOR for approximate recovery (grounded in linear code limitations).
- Small but relevant human study showing cognitive ease of AND queries.
- Extension to unknown priors via randomization and using only cluster sizes.

Cons:
- Critical typo on the total number of queries (nd^2 vs nd/2) in Section 3.3/Algorithm 1; needs correction.
- Some reliance on conjectured lower bounds for XOR in approximate recovery; clearly separate proven vs conjectural claims.
- Computational aspects of ML decoding (exact recovery) not addressed.
- Limited empirical evaluation and baselines; noise model may be simplistic for real crowds.
- Some clarity/notation issues (logs, definitions, worst-case vs average-case).

Recommendation:
Weak accept. The paper offers a fresh and well-argued theoretical perspective with concrete algorithmic takeaways and a small but illustrative user study. If the authors fix the nd^2 vs nd/2 error, clarify the scope of proven claims, and tighten presentation, this will be a solid contribution of interest to the NIPS community working on crowdsourcing, clustering with queries, and information-theoretic limits. Further empirical comparisons and a discussion of computational tractability would strengthen the paper.
