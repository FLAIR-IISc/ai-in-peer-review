

# Peer Review: "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper presents a novel perspective on clustering with crowdsourcing by framing the problem as locally encodable source coding, and proposes a new type of query ("AND queries") that outperforms traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries. The authors establish theoretical bounds for query complexity in exact and approximate recovery scenarios, both in noiseless and noisy settings, and validate their approach with a real crowdsourcing experiment.

**Strengths:**

1. **Original Theoretical Framework:** The paper's core contribution is the novel connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding, a perspective not previously explored in the literature. This provides fresh insights into the fundamental limits of query-based clustering. The authors correctly cite related work on locally decodable source codes [19, 16, 26] while establishing their unique contribution.

2. **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper provides rigorous lower and upper bounds for query complexity. The most significant theoretical contribution is showing that AND queries achieve substantially better performance than "same cluster" queries for approximate recovery (Theorem 4), resolving a key limitation identified by Massey [17]. The noise model for queries is well-justified and aligns with practical crowdsourcing considerations.

3. **Compelling Experimental Validation:** The real crowdsourcing experiment with 100 action and 100 romantic movies is well-designed and effectively demonstrates that AND queries yield significantly fewer errors (105 vs. 234 errors) than "same cluster" queries, validating the theoretical claims. The results in Figure 5 closely match theoretical predictions.

4. **Practical Impact:** The paper addresses a real-world problem in crowdsourced entity resolution with clear implications for reducing query costs in practical applications. The focus on non-adaptive queries is particularly relevant for real-time crowdsourcing systems.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** While the paper acknowledges existing work on clustering with crowdsourcing [8, 20, 21, 22], it doesn't directly compare its query complexity bounds with the best known results for similar settings. For example, the O(n log(1/δ)) bound for AND queries should be explicitly compared to the O(n log n) bound for "same cluster" queries under similar conditions.

2. **Lack of Computational Complexity Analysis:** The paper focuses on query complexity but doesn't address the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms (e.g., Algorithm 1). For practical implementation, it's important to know whether the decoding process is efficient, especially for large n.

3. **Insufficient Explanation of AND Query Advantage:** While the paper claims AND queries are easier for workers, it doesn't provide a clear psychological or cognitive explanation for why "Are both movies action movies?" is easier than "Do these two movies belong to the same cluster?" This would strengthen the practical justification.

4. **Limited Discussion of Algorithm Limitations:** The paper doesn't thoroughly explore scenarios where the AND query approach might fail. For instance, how does the method perform when the prior distribution p is highly skewed (e.g., p = 0.99 for one cluster)? The theoretical guarantees (Theorem 5) assume p is known, but how sensitive is the approach to prior estimation errors?

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes a significant advance in understanding query complexity for clustering with crowdsourcing. The paper's novel theoretical framework and strong experimental validation make it a compelling addition to the literature. The main weaknesses are the limited comparison with state-of-the-art methods and insufficient discussion of computational complexity and practical limitations.

The paper addresses a fundamental question in crowdsourced clustering with important theoretical and practical implications. The connection to locally encodable source coding is particularly insightful, and the AND query approach resolves a key limitation in existing methods. The real-world experiment provides strong empirical support for the theoretical claims, which is rare in theoretical papers.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field with strong theoretical results and compelling experimental validation. The minor weaknesses identified are not sufficient to outweigh the paper's substantial contributions. The work advances the state of the art in query-based clustering and provides both theoretical insights and practical guidance for real-world crowdsourcing applications.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Include a direct comparison of query complexity bounds with the best existing methods for similar settings.
2. Add a brief discussion of computational complexity for the proposed algorithms.
3. Provide a more detailed explanation of why AND queries are cognitively simpler for workers.
4. Expand the discussion of practical limitations and sensitivity to prior distribution estimation.

The paper is well-written and well-structured, with clear technical contributions that will be of interest to the NIPS community working on machine learning, information theory, and crowdsourcing. The theoretical results are rigorous, the experimental validation is convincing, and the practical implications are significant.
