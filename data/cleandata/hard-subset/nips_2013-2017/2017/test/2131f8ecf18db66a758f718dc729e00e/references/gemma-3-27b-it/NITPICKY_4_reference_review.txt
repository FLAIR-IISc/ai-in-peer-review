This paper explores the connection between crowdsourced clustering via pairwise queries and locally encodable source coding, aiming to derive information-theoretic lower bounds and propose efficient querying schemes. While the idea of framing the problem through this lens is intriguing and the theoretical results presented have potential value, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and inconsistencies that, cumulatively, detract from its overall quality.

The introduction is overly verbose and repetitive. The distinction between clustering and label learning feels contrived and is reiterated unnecessarily. The motivation for non-adaptive querying is weak – while parallelism is mentioned, the practical benefits are not convincingly argued.  The repeated emphasis on the desire for binary answers feels ad-hoc; why not explore the benefits of more informative query responses? The phrasing "almost always" used throughout is imprecise and weakens the rigor of the claims.

Section 2 establishes the problem setup, but lacks conciseness. The notation is generally acceptable, but the repeated reminders of assumptions (e.g., independent errors) disrupt the flow. The justification for disallowing query repetition is stated but feels insufficient. While the motivation for considering k-ary queries is briefly mentioned, the extension from binary to k-ary queries isn’t clearly explained, and is used without much further justification.

Section 3 contains the core contributions. Theorem 1’s proof relies on expurgation, but the discussion of its impact on performance is superficial. The claim of surpassing nh(p) even for small p (while mathematically possible) lacks a compelling practical demonstration. Theorem 2’s lower bound is presented without sufficient justification for its tightness, and the connection to existing lower bounds is weak. The discussion of ‘same cluster’ queries feels unnecessarily critical, and the claim that they are “highly inefficient” isn't fully substantiated beyond the Massey result. The derivation of the distortion bound in Theorem 4 lacks clarity. The assumed Poisson distribution for node degrees feels arbitrary without further justification, and the leap from degree distribution to error probability needs more explanation. The Algorithm 1 description is concise but lacks details about implementation considerations. The final Theorem 6 about relative cluster sizes is a positive addition, but feels tacked on and not fully integrated into the main narrative. 

The experimental section (Section 4) is arguably the weakest part of the paper. The setup is described adequately, but the justification for the chosen experimental parameters is lacking. The comparison to ‘same cluster’ queries relies on spectral clustering, which isn’t directly comparable to the proposed AND-query scheme. The interpretation of the results is overly optimistic, and the link between the theoretical bounds and the experimental observations is tenuous.  The presentation of results, especially Figures 3-5, is not optimal and lacks detailed captions. The claim that the error rate of AND queries is lower in the experiments is asserted, but the evidence presented isn't convincing – detailed error rate numbers, standard deviations, and statistical significance tests are missing.

Throughout the paper, the mathematical notation is inconsistent.  For example, the entropy H(p) is sometimes used with a base-2 logarithm, and other times without explicit specification.  Minor typographical errors are pervasive (e.g., "nd2 queries, for an integer d > 1" – this feels circular). The referencing is mostly adequate, but some key works on locally decodable codes are missing, and the citations themselves contain errors (e.g., missing page numbers).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The connection to locally encodable source coding provides a novel perspective on crowdsourced clustering.
*   The theoretical results offer potentially useful lower bounds on query complexity.
*   The experimental validation, while weak, provides some evidence of the algorithm's effectiveness.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper is overly verbose and lacks conciseness.
*   The mathematical derivations are often unclear and lack sufficient justification.
*   The experimental evaluation is inadequate and fails to convincingly demonstrate the algorithm's advantages.
*   The pervasive minor flaws and inconsistencies detract from the paper's overall quality.
*   The significance of the contributions is overstated given the theoretical nature of the results and the limitations of the experimental validation.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea behind this paper is potentially interesting, the execution is severely lacking. The sheer number of minor flaws – in clarity, rigor, and presentation – undermine the paper’s credibility.  The paper feels incomplete and prematurely submitted.  I recommend **rejection**. Substantial revisions addressing the issues outlined above would be necessary for reconsideration. The authors should focus on tightening the theoretical arguments, providing a more thorough experimental evaluation, and improving the overall clarity and presentation of the paper.
