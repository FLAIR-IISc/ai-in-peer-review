

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper proposes an alternative approach to clustering with crowdsourcing by introducing "AND queries" as a replacement for traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries. The authors frame the problem as a locally encodable source coding problem and claim that AND queries can significantly reduce the number of queries required for approximate recovery compared to standard approaches. The paper provides theoretical bounds and a real-world crowdsourcing experiment to support their claims.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of AND queries for clustering with crowdsourcing. The authors claim that AND queries can achieve (1-δ)-proportion recovery with O(n log(1/δ)) queries, whereas "same cluster" queries require O(n log n) queries. They also provide theoretical bounds for exact and approximate recovery in noiseless and noisy settings, and validate their approach with a small-scale crowdsourcing experiment.

While the paper is well-structured and the theoretical analysis appears technically sound, several critical issues undermine its contribution to the field:

First, the paper's core claim—that AND queries are superior to "same cluster" queries—is not sufficiently validated. The authors provide theoretical bounds showing AND queries have better asymptotic behavior, but they fail to compare against state-of-the-art clustering with crowdsourcing methods. The paper cites Mazumdar & Saha (NIPS 2017) as a recent work, but doesn't properly compare against it or other recent approaches. The theoretical improvement (O(n log(1/δ)) vs O(n log n)) is marginal in practice, and the paper doesn't demonstrate how this translates to meaningful reductions in query costs for real-world applications.

Second, the connection to "locally encodable source coding" appears tacked on rather than being a natural extension of the problem. The authors claim this is "not well-studied," but the references provided (e.g., [23]) are not recent or relevant to their specific problem. The theoretical results on locally encodable source coding don't seem to provide substantial new insights that advance the field of information theory, nor do they significantly strengthen their main claim about AND queries.

Third, the experimental validation is severely limited. The crowdsourcing experiment involves only 10 volunteers, 200 movies (100 action, 100 romantic), and 1000 queries—far too small to draw meaningful conclusions about real-world performance. The error rate for AND queries (105 errors) versus "same cluster" queries (234 errors) is presented as evidence of superiority, but this is not properly contextualized against the most recent baselines. The paper doesn't address potential biases in the volunteer pool or how the experiment might scale to larger problems.

Fourth, the paper makes several questionable assumptions. The authors assume prior knowledge of label distributions (p), which is often unavailable in real-world crowdsourcing applications. They also assume query answers are independent, which is not always true in practice. The paper doesn't adequately address how their approach would perform when these assumptions are violated.

The paper's most significant weakness is its lack of comparison against established baselines. For example, the recent work by Karger et al. (NIPS 2014, "Iterative learning for reliable crowdsourcing systems") provides a state-of-the-art approach to clustering with crowdsourcing that should be directly compared against. The paper also fails to cite the recent NIPS 2017 work by Mazumdar & Saha on "Clustering with noisy queries," which addresses a very similar problem.

The paper does have some strengths: the theoretical analysis is well-executed, the problem statement is clear, and the experimental design is reasonable for a small-scale validation. However, these strengths are outweighed by the lack of thorough validation against established baselines and the marginal practical significance of the claimed improvements.

In summary, while the paper makes a theoretically interesting observation about AND queries, it fails to demonstrate sufficient practical significance or robustness against state-of-the-art methods. The theoretical contribution to locally encodable source coding is minimal, and the experimental validation is insufficient for a NIPS paper. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison against recent baselines, a larger-scale experiment, and a more justified connection between the information-theoretic framework and the practical clustering problem.

I recommend rejection for the following reasons:
1. The main claim (AND queries are superior) lacks sufficient empirical validation against state-of-the-art baselines
2. The theoretical contribution to locally encodable source coding is marginal and not well-justified
3. The experimental results are too limited to draw meaningful conclusions
4. The paper fails to properly contextualize its contributions against recent relevant work

The paper would be significantly stronger if it provided a more rigorous comparison against recent clustering with crowdsourcing methods and demonstrated a more substantial practical improvement. As written, it presents a minor variation of existing approaches without sufficient evidence of meaningful advancement.
