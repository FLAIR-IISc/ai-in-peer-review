Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonadaptive, budgeted crowdsourced clustering/labeling through the lens of information theory, introducing the notion of locally encodable source coding: each query (compressed bit) depends on at most Δ input labels. The authors analyze both noiseless and noisy settings, for exact and approximate recovery. They argue that commonly used “same-cluster” (XOR-like) pairwise queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery and propose AND-type queries, showing improved query–accuracy trade-offs.

Main contributions:
- Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2): With pairwise XOR (same-cluster) queries extended to Δ-ary XOR, a random constant-weight sparse-linear encoder plus ML decoding achieves exact recovery with m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries, where α = 1/2 (1 + (1 − 4p(1−p))^Δ). A locality-aware lower bound exceeding n h(p) for some p is provided.
- Noiseless, approximate recovery (k=2): A general lower bound on achievable distortion under locality constraints is given (Theorem 3). The authors argue that linear/XOR queries are limited by a classical result due to Massey on rate–distortion with linear encoders, and conjecture a specific XOR lower bound. They introduce a simple AND-query scheme with a thresholding decoder and derive its distortion vs. number of queries m (Poisson-degree analysis).
- Noisy, approximate recovery (k≥2): They define a k-ary “AND” query Q(X,X′)=i if X=X′=i, and 0 otherwise, observed through a symmetric noise model. Using a d-regular graph of pairwise queries and a per-node counting/thresholding decoder, they prove that O(n log(k/δ)) queries suffice to recover a (1−δ) fraction of labels with high probability, with error per node decaying exponentially in d. They also show that the method can work with known cluster sizes rather than priors by using a random permutation in graph construction.
- Experiments: A small crowdsourcing experiment on 200 movies shows that AND queries yield fewer worker errors than same-cluster queries, and that error decreases with the number of queries, aligning qualitatively with the theory.

This work connects to: adaptive/nonadaptive clustering with same-cluster queries (Ashtiani et al., NIPS’16; Mazumdar & Saha, NIPS’17), budget–fidelity tradeoffs in labeling (Lahouti & Hassibi, NIPS’16), locally decodable/smooth compression (Makhdoumi et al.; Montanari & Mossel), sparse-graph source coding (Chandar; Mazumdar et al.), and crowdsourcing reliability (Karger–Oh–Shah). The AND query idea is reminiscent of principles in group testing but tailored to multi-class labels and pairwise tests.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The exact-recovery achievability (Theorem 1) is a standard sparse-linear encoding/ML-decoding argument with expurgation. The parity-zero probability bound for constant-weight rows is reasonable; the result improves over n for a nontrivial p-range even with Δ=2.
- The lower bound for exact recovery (Theorem 2) leverages that most nodes participate in few queries due to locality, leading to a packing argument via Hamming ball volumes; while somewhat loose, it captures the penalty of locality and is stronger than n h(p) in a regime.
- The locality-aware rate–distortion lower bound (Theorem 3) is stated without proof in the main text; its form is plausible but hard to evaluate and should be substantiated carefully in the appendix.
- The AND-based achievability for noiseless approximate recovery (Theorem 4) uses a Poisson left-degree approximation and a simple decoder; the derivation is plausible, though the exact expression is intricate and the dominant term intuition (errors from isolated 1’s with no 1-neighbors) could be highlighted.
- Noisy k-ary AND scheme (Theorem 5): The algorithm and Chernoff analysis yield exponential tails in d and the total m=Θ(n d). However, the text has notational inconsistencies: m is repeatedly written as “nd2” while in a d-regular graph the number of queries (edges) is nd/2; the proof line “m=nd2” appears to intend nd/2. Also, the threshold Ci and E(Nu,i) expressions seem off by constants: under a symmetric k-ary noise model, E(Nu,i) when Xu=i should be d [ p_i (1 − q) + q/(k−1) ] and dq/(k−1) otherwise; the formula shown includes terms like 1 − q k/(k−1) and an unexplained factor 1/2 in Ci. The high-level gap-based argument remains valid, but constants should be corrected for full rigor. The dependency/concentration claim “Z = EZ almost always” is too strong; a standard bounded-dependence concentration (e.g., Janson’s inequality) would show concentration around the mean with variance scaling; please clarify.
- Computational aspects: The exact-recovery scheme relies on ML decoding, which is exponential in n; no polynomial-time decoder is provided for that regime. The approximate/noisy schemes are linear-time.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable but marred by typos and notation issues that impede verification: “nd2” vs “nd/2”, inconsistent logarithm bases, occasional missing factors, and overloaded symbols. Some figures and proofs are deferred, reducing self-containment. The crowd experiment description is clear.
- Suggest clarifying: (i) the exact query budget m for d-regular graphs; (ii) the correct expectations/thresholds in Theorem 5; (iii) base of logarithms throughout; (iv) complexity of ML decoding vs. existence; and (v) the precise statement and proof outline of Theorem 3 in the main text.

Originality
- Framing nonadaptive crowdsourced queries as locally encodable source coding is original and insightful. The AND-query primitive for approximate recovery, and the O(n log(k/δ)) guarantee under noise with simple decoders, appear novel within the crowdsourcing/clustering literature. The exact-recovery sparse-linear scheme is related to known sparse-graph source codes but the localization to Δ-limited queries in clustering provides a new angle.

Significance
- The work advances understanding of fundamental limits under locality constraints and offers a practical, simple scheme (AND queries + counting decoder) with provable guarantees and some empirical validation. The observation that AND queries are easier for workers and can outperform XOR in approximate regimes is practically relevant. The main limitations are the reliance on ML for the exact-recovery result, the small-scale experiments, and some technical inaccuracies that should be corrected.

Pros
- Clear conceptual bridge between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding.
- New lower bounds incorporating locality; AND-query achievability that beats XOR in approximate recovery.
- Noisy multi-class scheme with O(n log(k/δ)) queries and simple decoding.
- Empirical evidence that AND queries yield fewer worker errors.

Cons
- Notational/technical inconsistencies (e.g., m=nd/2 vs “nd2”; thresholds/expectations in Theorem 5).
- Exact-recovery achievability uses ML decoding with no efficient alternative.
- Theorem 3 is opaque without a proof sketch; some bounds are conjectural (XOR linear encoder limit).
- Experiments are small and do not systematically compare against strong baselines for same-cluster queries under noise.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct and unify the noisy k-ary analysis (expected counts, thresholds, constants) and the query budget notation.
- Provide a brief proof sketch for Theorem 3 in the main text and clarify the scope of Conjecture 1.
- Discuss algorithmic complexity and any prospects for efficient decoders for the exact case (or state the result as existential).
- Expand empirical evaluation: larger datasets, comparison to robust same-cluster baselines (e.g., spectral methods with denoising), sensitivity to non-i.i.d. worker noise.
- Relate to group testing literature, which may offer additional insights on AND-like queries and decoding.

Recommendation
The paper contains novel and potentially impactful ideas with largely sound high-level arguments, but clarity and some technical details need attention. With the corrections and clarifications above, it would make a solid contribution.
