Summary
This paper studies non-adaptive crowdsourced clustering/label-recovery via pairwise (or small-arity) queries through an information-theoretic lens. It introduces “locally encodable source coding” (each query/encoded bit depends on at most Δ labels), derives lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery, and proposes AND-queries as a superior primitive to XOR (“same-cluster”) queries for approximate recovery. For k=2 and noiseless answers, Theorem 1 gives an achievability with m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) using Δ-wise XOR queries and ML decoding; Theorem 2 provides a converse stronger than nh(p) in a narrow regime. For approximate recovery, Theorem 3 states a lower bound under local-encodability; Theorem 4 analyzes a simple OR-of-AND decoder with random pairwise AND queries. In the noisy setting, the authors propose a k-ary AND query (returns i iff both labels equal i, else 0), a simple thresholding decoder on a d-regular graph, and claim (Theorem 5) that O(n log(k/δ)) queries suffice for (1−δ)-accurate recovery. A small crowdsourcing experiment on classifying 200 movies suggests AND queries yield fewer worker errors than same-cluster queries.

Strengths
- Poses crowdsourced clustering as locally encodable source coding; this connection is interesting and under-explored.
- Highlights an intuitive but underused primitive: AND vs XOR, and shows how asymmetry creates a useful expectation gap for simple thresholding in the noisy setting.
- The noisy approximate-recovery algorithm is very simple, non-adaptive, parallelizable, and its performance scaling O(n log(k/δ)) is plausible and potentially useful.
- Contains several bounds that could stimulate further work on locally encodable compression.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Major technical inconsistencies and gaps:
- Query count inconsistency (repeated and critical). In Sec. 3.3 the paper repeatedly states “we make nd^2 queries” with a d-regular graph and query all edges. A d-regular simple graph on n vertices has |E| = nd/2 edges, so the number of queries is nd/2, not nd^2. This error propagates to the statement of Theorem 5, Algorithm 1, the scaling arguments, and the experiments (where n=200, d=10, |E|=1000 indeed equals nd/2; yet the text claims nd^2=1000). This must be corrected throughout; as written, the theory/experiments are inconsistent.
- Theorem 1 achievability relies on ML decoding over parity constraints QX ≡ Y (mod 2) with Δ-wise XOR queries. The expurgation argument is only sketched; the “bad event” probability and the step replacing (sum over even i) by 1/2(1+(1−2r(p))^Δ) need justification. Moreover, ML decoding is exponential and no computationally efficient decoder is provided; the practicality for large n is unclear.
- Theorem 2 converse is insufficiently justified. The step “any query output for inputs in C must reside in a Hamming ball of radius (1−ρ) r(p) Δ m / ρ” is not derived. The choice of ρ and the averaging over elements with low degree requires a rigorous counting or Fano-type argument; as presented, the logic is opaque.
- Theorem 3 (approximate recovery lower bound) is central but its proof is relegated to the supplement and the bound is hard to parse: δ ≥ δ̃(m/n) + [h(p) − h(δ̃(m/n))]/[h′(δ̃(m/n))(1 + e^{Δ h′(δ̃(m/n))})]. The base of logs and dimensions are unclear; without an in-paper proof sketch it is difficult to assess correctness or tightness.
- Theorem 4 (AND queries, noiseless) uses multiple approximations: Poissonization of left degrees, occupancy (f(k,d)) for distinct two-hop neighbors, and then averages over d. There are conflicts between the use of a fixed degree d and Poisson(2m/n). The error event and the derived δ expression appear plausible but the derivation is very terse and not fully convincing without more careful probabilistic arguments (e.g., handling dependencies and repeated neighbors).
- Theorem 5 (noisy, k≥2). The expectation calculations for Nu,i are correct and the midpoint threshold is sensible. However, the bound Pi ≤ c k e^{−2 d^2} (constants suppressed) is too hand-wavy. The error exponent should depend explicitly on p_i via the mean gap p_i(1 − q k/(k−1)), and thus the required d, and hence the sample complexity, should depend on 1/min_i p_i. Claiming O(n log(k/δ)) independent of class imbalance seems optimistic; prior works in noisy clustering often scale with γ (largest/smallest cluster ratio). This dependency should be made explicit or carefully justified.
- Concentration step: “Using Corollary 4.3.5 in [3], it is evident that Z=EZ almost always.” This is not precise; one needs an explicit bounded-dependency concentration inequality (e.g., Janson’s inequality) with a full calculation of ∆* to get P(Z>(1+ε)EZ) bounds. As written, it is not rigorous.

Experimental issues:
- Very small-scale and local “crowdsourcing” (10 volunteers, 6 minutes) with no quality control, worker variability, or robust aggregation. The noise model assumed in theory (independent per-query flips with uniform error among k−1 wrong symbols) is unlikely to match reality.
- The claim that AND queries yield fewer errors than same-cluster queries is interesting, but the comparison is anecdotal and potentially biased (same subjects doing both tasks, no randomization of order, no measurement of per-worker reliability). No statistical tests provided.
- Baselines are weak; for same-cluster queries, the paper resorts to spectral clustering on a synthetic dataset rather than under the same noise process as the real task. No comparison to more advanced oracles/algorithms (e.g., iterative inference under Dawid–Skene; KOS/Karger–Oh–Shah; adaptive schemes) in the approximate regime.
- Figures referred in the main text are not shown here; quantitative conclusions are hard to verify.

Clarity and presentation:
- Numerous typos and formatting problems: “to to”; broken footnotes inserted into body; “st Conference on NIPS”; inconsistent notation (log vs log2; α definition and use as 1/2(1+(1−4p(1−p))^Δ) vs 1−2r(p) forms); δ̃(γ) defined without base; m = n(h(p)+o(1)) log 1/α mixing numerator/denominator; repeated and incomplete references (e.g., [9]/[10] duplicates; many entries missing years/pages/venues).
- Key proofs deferred; some claims rely on a conjecture (Massey) to argue limits of XOR queries. It is fine to use a conjecture, but then claims like “we beat the ‘same cluster’ lower bound” should be phrased more carefully and not as universal.

Originality and significance:
- The “locally encodable source coding” framing is novel and could be impactful. The demonstration that asymmetric AND queries can be better than XOR for approximate/noisy settings is a useful insight.
- However, due to the technical inconsistencies and lack of rigorous, self-contained proofs for several core results, and the limited empirical validation, it is hard to assess the true significance at this stage.

Pros
- Interesting, timely problem; novel perspective.
- Simple, scalable non-adaptive scheme with plausible O(n log(k/δ)) scaling.
- Insight about asymmetric queries lowering worker error.

Cons
- Critical inconsistency in query counts (nd^2 vs nd/2).
- Several proofs are sketchy/incomplete; key bounds lack rigor or hide dependencies on class imbalance.
- Experimental validation is minimal; baselines and statistical analysis are insufficient.
- Many presentation and referencing issues.

Recommendation: Reject
The paper has promising ideas, but the accumulation of technical and presentation flaws, especially the systemic nd^2 vs nd/2 error and the lack of rigorous proofs for central results, outweighs the contributions. I encourage the authors to:
- Fix the query-count inconsistency everywhere and re-derive all bounds accordingly.
- Provide rigorous, self-contained proofs (or detailed sketches) for Theorems 2–5, making dependencies on p (or min p_i) explicit.
- Discuss computational complexity and provide efficient decoders (beyond ML) where applicable.
- Substantially strengthen experiments: larger datasets, randomized controlled comparisons of query types, and stronger baselines.
- Thoroughly edit for clarity, notation, and complete references.
