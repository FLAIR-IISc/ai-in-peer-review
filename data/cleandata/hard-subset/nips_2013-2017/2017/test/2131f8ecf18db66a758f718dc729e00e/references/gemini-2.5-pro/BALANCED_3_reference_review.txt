### Summary

This paper investigates the problem of semi-supervised clustering where information is gathered via non-adaptive queries on small subsets of elements. The central and novel contribution is to frame this problem within the information-theoretic lens of locally encodable source coding, where each bit of the compressed representation (a query answer) depends on a limited number of source bits (the element labels). This framework allows the authors to derive new information-theoretic lower bounds on the required number of queries and to design and analyze novel querying schemes.

The authors study three primary settings: (1) noiseless queries for exact recovery, (2) noiseless queries for approximate recovery, and (3) noisy queries for approximate recovery. A key insight of the paper is that the commonly used 'same-cluster' query (equivalent to an XOR operation for binary labels) is suboptimal for approximate recovery. To address this, the authors propose and analyze a new type of query, the 'AND-query'. For the noisy, approximate recovery setting, they present an algorithm based on AND-queries that can recover a `(1-δ)` fraction of labels correctly using `O(n log(k/δ))` queries, which is a significant improvement over prior work. The theoretical contributions are supported by a small-scale real-world crowdsourcing experiment and synthetic simulations.

This work connects to the growing literature on active clustering and clustering with an oracle (e.g., [4, 21, 22]) but distinguishes itself by its deep information-theoretic perspective and its focus on non-adaptive, parallelizable queries. The connection to source coding, particularly the less-studied locally encodable codes, is a novel contribution to both the machine learning and information theory communities.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Powerful Theoretical Framework:** The formulation of query-based clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem is highly original and insightful. This perspective is not merely a recasting of the problem; it provides a principled way to derive fundamental limits (lower bounds) and inspires the design of new, more efficient query types.
2.  **Significant Algorithmic Contribution (AND-Queries):** The paper makes a compelling case, both theoretically and empirically, that standard 'same-cluster' (XOR) queries are not optimal for approximate recovery. The proposed AND-query is a simple, elegant, and practical alternative. The analysis culminating in Algorithm 1 for the noisy setting is a strong result, achieving `(1-δ)` recovery with `O(n log(k/δ))` queries, a substantial improvement over the `O(n log n)` complexity mentioned for prior work in this challenging setting.
3.  **Thorough and Rigorous Analysis:** The paper presents a comprehensive theoretical analysis covering three distinct and important settings. The results include both achievability bounds (upper bounds on query complexity) for the proposed schemes and fundamental limits (lower bounds), providing a well-rounded picture of the problem.
4.  **Practical Relevance and Validation:** The authors make a strong practical argument for AND-queries, suggesting they are cognitively easier for human annotators. This claim is substantiated by their crowdsourcing experiment, where error rates for AND-queries were less than half those for 'same-cluster' queries on the same pairs. This experimental finding, while based on a small sample, adds significant practical weight to the theoretical contributions.

### Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions

1.  **Clarity of Proofs and Technical Details:** While the results are strong, some of the proofs in the main text are presented as very high-level sketches, which makes it difficult to fully verify the technical soundness without resorting to the appendix. For instance, the proof of Theorem 5 relies on Chernoff bounds and a concentration inequality, but the specific application and the derivation of constants are omitted. For the final version, it would be beneficial to flesh out these key proof steps more explicitly in the main paper, perhaps by providing the precise inequalities used and defining the constants involved. This would improve the paper's self-containment and readability.
2.  **Strength of Assumptions:** The analysis heavily relies on the assumption that element labels are drawn i.i.d. from a known prior distribution `p`. This is a significant simplification of many real-world clustering scenarios where data exhibits more complex structure. While Theorem 6 laudably attempts to relax this by using known cluster sizes, the proposed mechanism (randomly permuting nodes) feels like a technique to make the problem amenable to the i.i.d. analysis rather than a fundamental handling of non-i.i.d. data. A more explicit discussion of the limitations imposed by the i.i.d. assumption and potential directions for extending the analysis to structured models (like the Stochastic Block Model) would strengthen the paper.
3.  **Scope of Experiments:** The crowdsourcing experiment is a valuable addition, but its small scale (200 items, 10 volunteers) means the conclusions should be treated as preliminary but promising evidence. More importantly, in the synthetic comparison between AND-queries and 'same-cluster' queries, the latter uses spectral clustering for decoding. It is not clear if this is the optimal or fairest baseline. A more direct comparison would involve using a decoder for the 'same-cluster' queries that is analogous to Algorithm 1, such as one based on belief propagation or even the (potentially intractable) maximum likelihood decoder analyzed in Section 3.1. Justifying the choice of spectral clustering as a representative baseline would be helpful.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel and powerful information-theoretic framework for a core machine learning problem.
*   **Pro:** The proposed AND-query is a simple, significant, and practical contribution that challenges standard practice and is shown to be superior in important settings.
*   **Pro:** The theoretical results are strong, particularly the `O(n log(1/δ))` algorithm for noisy, approximate recovery, which represents a clear advance in the state of the art.
*   **Pro:** The work is a complete research contribution, featuring rigorous theory, a new algorithm, and both real-world and synthetic experimental validation.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

*   **Con:** The i.i.d. assumption on labels may limit the direct applicability of the theoretical results to more structured real-world problems.
*   **Con:** Some proof sketches in the main body are too brief, requiring the reader to trust the derivations or dig into the appendix.
*   **Con:** The experimental validation, while positive, is conducted on a small scale.

### Decision

The strengths of this paper, particularly the novelty of the theoretical framework and the significance of the proposed AND-query scheme, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. The paper presents a complete and impactful piece of research that advances our understanding of query-based clustering. The weaknesses are primarily related to presentation clarity and the scope of assumptions/experiments, which can be addressed in a final version. I recommend accepting this paper.
