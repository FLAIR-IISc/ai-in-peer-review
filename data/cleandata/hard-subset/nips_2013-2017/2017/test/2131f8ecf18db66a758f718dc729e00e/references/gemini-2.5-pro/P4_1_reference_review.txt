### Summary

This paper investigates the problem of semi-supervised clustering, where the labels of `n` items are to be recovered by making non-adaptive queries to an oracle. The central contribution is a novel information-theoretic framing of this problem as "locally encodable source coding," where each query answer (a compressed bit) depends on a small, constant number of item labels (source bits). This perspective is used to derive new upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery of the labels.

The paper explores three main settings. First, for noiseless queries and exact recovery with two clusters (`k=2`), it shows that when the prior label distribution is known, "same cluster" (XOR) queries can recover all labels with fewer than `n` queries, and provides a new information-theoretic lower bound. Second, for noiseless queries and approximate recovery (recovering a `(1-δ)` fraction of labels), the paper argues that XOR queries are suboptimal. It proposes a new "AND" query (`Q(X, X') = X ∧ X'`) and demonstrates its superiority through theoretical analysis. Third, and most significantly, the paper addresses the noisy query setting for `k ≥ 2` clusters. It presents an algorithm based on a generalization of AND queries on a d-regular graph structure that achieves `(1-δ)`-approximate recovery with `O(n log(k/δ))` queries. This theoretical result is validated with a real crowdsourcing experiment.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Powerful Framework:** The formulation of query-based clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem is a significant conceptual contribution. It provides a principled and powerful lens through which to analyze the fundamental limits of this learning problem. This connection is novel and distinguishes the paper from prior work which has focused more on algorithmic or combinatorial approaches.

2.  **Introduction of AND Queries:** The paper makes a compelling case that the standard "same cluster" (XOR) query is not always optimal, particularly for approximate recovery. The proposal and analysis of AND queries is a key insight. The theoretical justification for its advantage in both noiseless and noisy settings is strong, and the empirical evidence from the crowdsourcing experiment (showing lower human error rates for AND queries) adds practical weight to this claim.

3.  **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper delivers several new and interesting theoretical results.
    *   For exact recovery (Sec 3.1), the demonstration that fewer than `n` queries suffice by exploiting label priors is a neat result, improving on the naive `n-1` query baseline. The accompanying lower bound (Theorem 2) is also a novel contribution.
    *   The algorithm for noisy, approximate recovery (Sec 3.3) and its analysis leading to an `O(n log(k/δ))` query complexity (Theorem 5) is the paper's strongest result. This is a substantial improvement over `O(n log n)` complexities often seen in related problems, and the algorithm itself is simple and practical.

4.  **Practical Relevance:** The focus on non-adaptive queries is well-motivated for parallelized crowdsourcing platforms. The consideration of noisy oracles is crucial for any real-world application. The paper successfully bridges theory and practice by including a real (albeit small-scale) experiment that validates the core ideas.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Computational Complexity of Decoding:** For the exact recovery setting (Theorem 1), the achievability proof relies on a Maximum Likelihood (ML) decoder. While this is standard for proving information-theoretic bounds, ML decoding is computationally intractable for the codes being considered. The paper does not discuss any polynomial-time decoding algorithm for this setting, which limits the practical applicability of that specific result.

2.  **Generalization of AND Query for k>2:** The proposed query for the `k>2` case (`Q(X, X') = i if X = X' = i, 0 otherwise`) is asymmetric and non-binary; it requires the oracle to return one of `k` values. This departs from the simple, binary query model emphasized in the introduction. The algorithm also treats the label '0' as a default case. The implications of this asymmetry and the shift from binary answers could be discussed more thoroughly. It would be beneficial to clarify why this specific query structure was chosen and if alternatives exist.

3.  **Experimental Scale:** The crowdsourcing experiment is a valuable addition, but its scale is limited (n=200, 10 volunteers). While the results support the paper's claims, particularly the lower error rate for AND queries, a larger and more diverse set of experiments would provide more conclusive evidence of the algorithm's performance and the practical advantages of AND queries across different domains.

### Detailed Comments

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation, problem setup, and contributions are laid out effectively. The theoretical arguments are presented logically.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. The core idea of using a locally encodable source coding framework is new in this context. The introduction and analysis of the AND query as a superior alternative to the standard 'same cluster' query is a novel and important contribution.
*   **Quality:** The technical quality of the paper is high. The theoretical analysis appears to be sound, using standard techniques from information theory and probability in a novel way. Claims are well-supported by proofs, which are detailed appropriately.
*   **Significance:** The results are significant for both the theoretical machine learning and the crowdsourcing communities. The paper advances our understanding of the fundamental limits of query-based learning and provides a practical, high-performing algorithm for a realistic (noisy, non-adaptive) setting. The insight that query design (AND vs. XOR) can dramatically impact performance is an important message for practitioners.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper with significant novel contributions in its theoretical framing, algorithmic design, and analysis. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor compared to the strengths and mostly represent avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws. The paper presents a complete piece of work that advances the state of the art and is likely to inspire follow-up research.
