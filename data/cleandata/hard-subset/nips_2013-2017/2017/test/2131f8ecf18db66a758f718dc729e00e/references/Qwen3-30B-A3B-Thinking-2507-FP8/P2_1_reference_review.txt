

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work
This paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for clustering problems by framing them as locally encodable source coding problems. The authors propose using AND-queries instead of traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries, demonstrating theoretically and empirically that AND-queries require significantly fewer queries for approximate recovery. The work connects clustering with information theory, establishing new lower bounds for locally encodable source coding and providing efficient query schemes. This builds upon recent work on clustering with queries (e.g., [4, 21]) while introducing a fundamentally new perspective that bridges information theory and crowdsourcing.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors establish novel information-theoretic lower bounds (Theorems 2, 3) and provide constructive upper bounds (Theorems 1, 4, 5) that are asymptotically tight in many regimes. The proofs are well-structured, though some technical details (particularly Theorem 3) are dense. The real-world experiment with crowdsourced movie labeling (Section 4) provides compelling empirical validation, showing that AND-queries produce 55% fewer errors than "same cluster" queries (105 vs. 234 errors). The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the focus on k=2 clusters and the need for prior knowledge of cluster sizes.

## Clarity
The paper is clearly written with logical organization and consistent notation. The problem statement (Section 2) is particularly well-defined, making it easy to understand the theoretical setup. The main contributions are concisely summarized in Section 2, and the results are presented with appropriate mathematical rigor. The figures (Figures 1-5) effectively illustrate the theoretical findings and experimental results. Some sections could benefit from slightly more intuitive explanations of the technical results (e.g., the derivation of the error probability in Theorem 4), but the paper remains accessible to experts in information theory and machine learning.

## Originality
This paper makes significant original contributions. The connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding is novel within the NIPS community, with the authors noting that "the notion of locally encodable source coding is not well-studied even within information theory community." The AND-query approach represents a substantial conceptual departure from prior work, with the authors demonstrating through Theorem 4 that it outperforms "same cluster" queries for approximate recovery. The paper also extends the theoretical framework to noisy query settings (Section 3.3), which is a meaningful contribution to the field of robust crowdsourcing.

## Significance
The work addresses a practical problem in crowdsourcing—minimizing the number of queries while maintaining accuracy—with significant theoretical and practical implications. The authors demonstrate that AND-queries can reduce the number of required queries from O(n log n) to O(n log(1/δ)) for (1-δ)-approximate recovery. The real-world validation with crowdsourced data provides strong evidence of the approach's practical utility. This work advances the state-of-the-art in clustering with queries and could influence future crowdsourcing systems. The connection to information theory also opens new theoretical avenues for studying query complexity.

## Strengths and Weaknesses
**Strengths:**
- Novel theoretical framework connecting clustering to locally encodable source coding
- Strong theoretical guarantees with matching lower and upper bounds
- Clear empirical validation with real crowdsourcing data
- Practical insights for crowdsourcing applications (AND queries are easier for workers to answer)
- Careful analysis of both exact and approximate recovery scenarios

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited focus on k > 2 clusters (though the paper mentions extensions)
- Theoretical results assume prior knowledge of cluster sizes, which may be challenging in practice
- The experimental validation is limited to a single dataset (IMDB movies)
- Some technical details in the proofs (e.g., Theorem 3) could be made more accessible

## Recommendation
This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of clustering with queries and establishes a novel theoretical connection to information theory. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and validated with real data. While the focus on k=2 clusters and the need for prior knowledge are limitations, these are acknowledged by the authors and do not undermine the fundamental novelty of the approach.

The paper aligns well with NIPS topics, particularly the intersection of machine learning, information theory, and practical applications in crowdsourcing. The theoretical insights advance the state of the art in query complexity for clustering, and the practical implications are significant for real-world crowdsourcing applications.

**Overall, I recommend acceptance.** This paper presents a novel and important contribution that will be of interest to both theoretical and applied researchers in machine learning and information theory. The quality of the theoretical analysis combined with the compelling empirical validation makes this a strong candidate for publication at NIPS.
