Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies non-adaptive, budgeted crowdsourcing for clustering/labeling n items into k classes via small-arity queries, with an information-theoretic perspective. It formalizes “locally encodable source coding”: each query (compressed bit) depends on at most Δ labels. Main contributions:
- Noiseless exact recovery (k=2): upper bound using parity/XOR queries on Δ items and ML decoding that achieves m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) (< n for a range of p) and a converse improving on the naive nh(p) bound for some p.
- Noiseless approximate recovery: a lower bound that accounts for locality Δ, a conjectured lower bound specialized to “same-cluster” (XOR) based on Massey’s limitations for linear rate-distortion codes, and a provably better scheme using AND queries with simple local decoding and an exact error expression.
- Noisy approximate recovery (k≥2): a pairwise “k-ary AND” query Q(X,X′)=i if X=X′=i, else 0; on a d-regular graph, a threshold decoder per item yields (1−δ)-accurate recovery with m=O(n log(k/δ)) queries under independent per-query noise. They also show how to replace priors by known cluster sizes. A small crowdsourcing experiment and a synthetic comparison complement the theory.

This builds on: crowdsourced clustering/query complexity and entity resolution (Ashtiani et al., Karger-Oh-Shah, Mazumdar-Saha), limitations of linear codes in rate-distortion (Ancheta-Massey), locally decodable/smooth compression (Makhdoumi et al., Montanari-Mossel), and sparse-graph coding (Chandar). The “AND vs XOR” insight and the locally encodable viewpoint appear novel.

Quality
- Theoretical contributions are mostly sound at a high level, with clear intuition: locality constraints degrade achievable distortion; AND queries create an expectation gap exploitable by simple thresholds; a sparse, regular query graph yields O(n log(k/δ)) scaling under noise.
- However, there are several issues that require clarification/correction:
  - In Sec. 3.3, the total number of queries on a d-regular graph should be m=nd/2, but the text and Algorithm 1 header repeatedly say “nd2/nd^2”. The final theorem states O(n log(k/δ)), which matches m=nd/2 with d=Θ(log(k/δ)). Please fix this inconsistency everywhere.
  - In the proof sketch of Theorem 5, the Chernoff tail is written as e^{-2 d^2}; the correct exponent should be e^{−Θ(d)} (linear in d), which still yields the claimed scaling but matters for constants.
  - The expurgation argument in Theorem 1 is under-explained: you “throw out” typical sequences to eliminate collisions. For correctness, argue that the fraction of typical sequences removed is o(1), hence the probability mass lost is o(1), so ML succeeds w.h.p. over the source. As written, “this will imply perfect recovery” is misleading without this probability argument.
  - Theorem 3 (Δ-local rate-distortion lower bound) is deferred; given its centrality, a sketch or key lemma would help assess rigor.
  - The XOR lower bound for approximate recovery is stated as a conjecture (except at p=0.5); it is appropriately labeled, but the empirical comparisons rely on it as a reference; please be explicit where the comparison is provable vs conjectural.

- Experimental validation is modest but sensible for a primarily theoretical paper. The small real-user study suggests AND queries are easier than same-cluster, and the synthetic comparison fills in the missing accuracy numbers. However:
  - The real experiment is very small (10 volunteers, 1000 pair queries), with potential confounds (no counterbalancing/order effects). Reporting confidence intervals and inter-worker variability would improve credibility.
  - In practice, repeated queries and advanced aggregation (e.g., Dawid-Skene, minimax entropy) are common; here you model aggregation via a single error rate q and forbid repetitions. That is fine theoretically, but a brief comparison/discussion would locate the contribution within the crowdsourcing literature more clearly.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable and well-motivated, but there are numerous typos/notation inconsistencies and footnote placement issues. Most importantly, fix m=nd/2 vs nd^2 throughout Sec. 3.3 and Figures/Algorithm captions. Define α cleanly in Theorem 1 (currently nested parentheses and powers are easy to misread).
- Some proofs (Theorems 1, 2, 4, 5) are only sketched; the appendix is referenced but not included here. Given space constraints, that’s acceptable, but please ensure constants and dependence on Δ, p, q are correct in the final.

Originality
- The locally encodable source coding framing and Δ-local rate-distortion lower bound appear new and relevant.
- The use of AND queries and the demonstration that XOR can be suboptimal for approximate recovery is a novel and useful insight for query design in crowdsourcing.
- The noisy O(n log(k/δ)) non-adaptive scheme with simple thresholds is a clean contribution.

Significance
- The work advances our understanding of how locality constraints change information-theoretic limits and algorithm design for query-based labeling/clustering.
- The AND-query design has practical implications: simpler cognitive load and better sample efficiency for approximate recovery.
- While empirical scope is limited, the theory is likely to influence subsequent work on active/query-efficient clustering and coding-theoretic perspectives on crowdsourcing.

Pros
- Clear theoretical advances (upper/lower bounds) under realistic locality constraints.
- Novel AND-query strategy with provable and practical advantages over “same-cluster” queries for approximate recovery.
- Simple, non-adaptive, scalable algorithm with near-optimal query complexity under noise.
- Useful bridge to information theory (locally encodable codes).

Cons
- Notational/typographical inconsistencies (most notably m=nd/2 vs nd^2) and a few proof-level slips (Chernoff exponent, expurgation explanation).
- Experimental validation is small-scale; no comparison to stronger crowdsourcing baselines that use repeated queries/aggregation.
- Some key results deferred; the Δ-local rate-distortion bound needs more visibility.

Questions for authors
- Please clarify the total number of queries in Sec. 3.3 and fix the Chernoff exponent. What constants are hidden in O(n log(k/δ))?
- Can you provide a short proof sketch for Theorem 3 in the main text?
- How sensitive is Algorithm 1 to mis-specified priors p? Empirically, how robust is the threshold when p is estimated?
- Could AND queries be extended to small hyperedge size Δ>2 for further gains?

Recommendation
Borderline accept (weak accept), contingent on fixing clarity/typos and tightening the proofs. The theoretical contributions and the AND-query insight are novel and of interest to the NeurIPS community, and the algorithm is simple and practical. My confidence is medium.
