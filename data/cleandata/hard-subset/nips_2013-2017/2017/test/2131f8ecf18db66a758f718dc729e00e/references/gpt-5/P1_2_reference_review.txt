Summary
The paper studies non-adaptive, pairwise query-based semisupervised clustering/label recovery through an information-theoretic lens. The key idea is to model the m query answers Y as a locally encodable source code of the label vector X, where each compressed bit depends on at most Δ labels. This framing yields new lower bounds and near-matching achievability results.

Main technical contributions:
- Exact recovery, noiseless (k=2): Using parity/XOR queries on Δ-tuples, the authors show sufficiency m ≈ n(h(p)+o(1))/log(1/α) with α=1/2(1+(1−4p(1−p))^Δ), which is < n for a nontrivial range of p even with Δ=2 (same-cluster queries). They also give a converse that improves on the naive n h(p) bound when Δ is constrained.
- Approximate recovery, noiseless (k=2): A locality-aware lower bound is derived via rate-distortion arguments. The paper argues that linear/XOR (“same-cluster”) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery, leveraging classical limits of linear rate-distortion codes (Massey/Ancheta; conjectured bound for general p, known at p=0.5). In contrast, the authors propose AND queries and analyze a simple decoder (label 1 if any incident query returns 1), deriving an explicit error expression under a random bipartite design; this strictly outperforms known/expected performance of XOR queries in several regimes.
- Approximate recovery with noisy oracle, k ≥ 2: They define a k-ary “AND-per-class” pairwise query Q(X,X′) that returns i if X=X′=i and 0 otherwise, under symmetric noise. On a d-regular graph, a simple per-node thresholding algorithm recovers a (1−δ) fraction with m=O(n log(k/δ)) queries. A dependency-graph concentration argument shows high-probability control of the total errors. They extend to the case where only relative cluster sizes are known. A small crowdsourcing experiment compares AND vs same-cluster queries, suggesting AND is easier (lower worker error) and that performance improves with more queries, aligning with theory.

Relation to prior work:
The work connects query-efficient clustering (e.g., Ashtiani et al., and subsequent noisy-query works by the authors) to locally encodable/source-channel coding, complementing literature on locally decodable codes and smooth compression (Makhdoumi et al., Montanari–Mossel, Mazumdar–Chandar–Wornell). The AND-vs-XOR observation for approximate recovery ties to classical limits of linear rate-distortion codes (Massey; Ancheta). The noisy-query algorithm competes with O(n log n) bounds known for certain noisy models.

Quality
- Theory: The paper contains a substantial set of bounds and algorithms. The achievability for AND queries and the noisy k-ary scheme are technically sound and use standard tools (Poissonization, inclusion–exclusion, Chernoff bounds, dependency graph concentration). The locality-aware converses are interesting; the rate-distortion lower bound (Thm 3) is deferred and nontrivial.
- A few places need tightening:
  - Theorem 1’s expurgation argument as written is confusing: the expurgation should be over the ensemble of matrices Q (show existence of a Q injective on the typical set) rather than “throwing out label vectors.” Please rephrase in terms of E[#collisions]<1 (or bound total probability mass of ambiguous typical sequences) to conclude Pr(error)→0 without “removing” source sequences.
  - The locality converse (Thm 2) is promising but terse; more detail on the step that bounds the image of C in a Hamming ball and the exact degree counting would help.
  - The lower bound against XOR for approximate recovery relies on a conjecture (except at p=0.5). The claims comparing AND vs XOR should be clearly labeled as empirical/heuristic where the converse is conjectural.
  - Notation: throughout the noisy section, “nd2 queries” seems to mean n d/2 (number of edges in a d-regular graph). The experiment (n=200, d=10, “nd2=1000”) confirms it’s nd/2; please fix the typography to avoid confusion. The threshold Ci expression also needs clearer typesetting and motivation (derivation from expectations).
- Experiments: The real-data test is small-scale but illustrative; the finding that AND queries have lower human error than same-cluster queries is valuable. However, algorithmic comparisons are limited: “same cluster” is paired with spectral clustering, which may not be the best decoder for noisy parity data; a more controlled comparison (e.g., MLE/LP relaxations, belief propagation on the same graph) with equal budgets would strengthen the empirical case. Results for k>2 on real or synthetic data would also help.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-structured, with a clear narrative (source-coding view → bounds → algorithms → experiments). However:
- Fix typographical issues (nd/2 vs nd2, inline fractions, missing parentheses).
- Define all variables precisely where used (e.g., Ci, constants in Chernoff bounds).
- State all assumptions (independent query noise across queries; non-repetition) up front in each theorem.
- When using “typical sets,” specify the probability-of-error criterion (average vs maximal) and how typicality is used.

Originality
- The locally encodable source coding viewpoint for query-based clustering and the resulting converses are novel and conceptually appealing.
- The explicit demonstration that AND queries can beat XOR for approximate recovery (both theoretically and empirically) is original and practically relevant.
- The noisy k-ary AND-per-class scheme with O(n log(k/δ)) queries and a very simple decoder is a nice contribution.

Significance
- The work advances understanding of the fundamental limits under locality constraints, which is relevant to crowdsourcing and other settings where queries must be simple.
- The AND-query insight has practical implications: fewer queries for approximate recovery and potentially lower annotator error.
- The paper is likely to stimulate follow-up on locally encodable source/channel coding and on optimal query design beyond XOR in interactive labeling/clustering.

Pros
- Fresh information-theoretic perspective (local encodability) with new bounds.
- Simple, effective AND-based algorithms with clean m=O(n log(k/δ)) guarantees under noise.
- Evidence that AND queries are cognitively easier for human workers.
- Bridges theory and (albeit small) experiments.

Cons
- Some proofs and notational choices need tightening; Theorem 1 expurgation and Thm 2 details.
- XOR lower bounds for approximate recovery rest on a conjecture (except at p=0.5).
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scale and baselines; no k>2 validation.
- Dependence on known priors; although mitigated in Thm 6, empirical verification of the prior-free variant would help.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and correct notation (especially m=nd/2).
- Strengthen Theorem 1’s argument and provide a clear average error bound.
- Add a tighter converse for noisy approximate recovery (Δ=2), if possible, to position O(n log(k/δ)) vis-à-vis optimality.
- Expand experiments: larger datasets, worker heterogeneity models, comparisons to stronger XOR decoders, and k>2 scenarios.
- Discuss connections to related literatures (e.g., group testing/pooling designs) and any limits when class 0 is not special.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a compelling theoretical framework and practical insights (AND queries) with promising algorithms. With improved clarity and somewhat stronger empirical comparisons, it would be a solid addition to the NeurIPS program.
