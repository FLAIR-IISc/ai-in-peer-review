This paper tackles the problem of semi-supervised clustering (or label recovery) for `n` items into `k` clusters using a minimal number of non-adaptive queries to an oracle, a common scenario in crowdsourcing. The central and most novel contribution is to establish a formal connection between this problem and the information-theoretic concept of locally encodable source coding, where each query (an "encoded bit") depends on a small number (`Δ`) of items (the "source bits").

Building on this framework, the paper makes several key contributions. First, for the noiseless, exact recovery case with two clusters (`k=2`), it provides new upper and lower bounds on the number of 'same cluster' (XOR) queries required, demonstrating that fewer than `n` queries are sufficient when the prior cluster distribution is non-uniform. Second, for the approximate recovery case, the paper argues that 'same cluster' queries are fundamentally suboptimal. It introduces a novel "AND query" and shows its superiority for this setting. Finally, and most significantly, the paper presents an algorithm for the general noisy, approximate recovery setting (`k ≥ 2`). This algorithm, based on a generalized AND query, achieves `(1-δ)`-approximate recovery with `O(n log(k/δ))` queries, a substantial improvement over the `O(n log n)` complexity of prior work for the noisy case. The theoretical results are supported by a real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance of the Framework:** The connection drawn between active clustering and locally encodable source coding is highly original and insightful. It provides a principled, information-theoretic lens through which to analyze query-based learning problems. This framework is not just a reframing; it enables the authors to derive new fundamental limits (lower bounds) and understand the limitations of existing query types. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could inspire future work in this area.

2.  **Introduction of AND Queries:** The paper makes a compelling case, both theoretically and empirically, that the standard 'same cluster' (XOR) query is not always the best choice, particularly for approximate recovery. The introduction of the AND query is a simple but powerful idea. The theoretical analysis (Theorem 4) and the experimental finding that workers make fewer errors on AND queries provide strong evidence for its utility. This challenges a common assumption in the literature and opens a new direction for designing better queries.

3.  **State-of-the-Art Algorithmic Result:** The main algorithmic contribution for noisy, approximate recovery (Algorithm 1 and Theorem 5) is a major strength. Improving the query complexity from `O(n log n)` to `O(n log(k/δ))` is a significant theoretical advance. This result is highly relevant as it addresses the most practical setting: allowing for both errors in the labels (approximate recovery) and errors from the oracle (noisy queries). The algorithm itself is simple, efficient, and well-analyzed.

4.  **Technical Quality and Clarity:** The paper is technically solid, employing standard but well-executed arguments from information theory, coding theory, and the probabilistic method. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, logically progressing from simpler theoretical settings to the more complex and practical algorithmic results.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a very strong paper, a few points could be clarified or strengthened to further improve its quality.

1.  **Constructive Aspects of Theoretical Schemes:** The proof of Theorem 1 relies on a random ensemble and an expurgation argument to show the *existence* of a good set of queries. This is a standard proof technique, but it would be beneficial to briefly discuss the practical implications. Is a randomly generated set of queries sufficient with high probability, or is a deterministic construction known or possible? A sentence or two on this would be helpful for practitioners.

2.  **Clarity on Approximations:** The proof of Theorem 4 (AND queries for approximate recovery) models the node degrees in the query graph using a Poisson distribution. This is a common and reasonable approximation for sparse random graphs, but it should be explicitly stated as such in the proof sketch to maintain full technical transparency.

3.  **Role of the Default Class in Algorithm 1:** Algorithm 1 assigns label 0 by default if no other label's count surpasses its threshold. The performance of this choice seems dependent on the prior probability `p_0` being relatively large. It would be helpful to add a short discussion on how this choice was made and whether the algorithm's performance is sensitive to it, especially in cases with a very small class 0.

4.  **Experimental Comparison:** The real-world crowdsourcing experiment provides excellent validation for Algorithm 1. However, the paper mentions a comparison against spectral clustering on a synthetic dataset (referencing Figures 6 and 7, which are not in the main text), but does not show it. Including a direct comparison to a baseline method (like spectral clustering on 'same cluster' queries) in the main paper, even if on the synthetic data, would more directly highlight the practical advantage of the proposed AND-query-based approach over a standard baseline in the noisy setting.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel and powerful theoretical framework connecting active clustering to locally encodable source coding.
*   Presents a significant algorithmic improvement for the noisy, approximate clustering problem, reducing query complexity from `O(n log n)` to `O(n log(1/δ))`.
*   Proposes the novel "AND query" and demonstrates its superiority over the standard 'same cluster' query, a key conceptual insight.
*   The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and supported by a real-world experiment.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The practical constructability of the scheme for exact recovery is not discussed.
*   The experimental section in the main paper lacks a direct comparison with a competing baseline method.

The arguments for acceptance are compelling and substantially outweigh the minor weaknesses, which can be addressed in a final version. This paper presents a significant advance in our understanding of query-based clustering, offering both deep theoretical insights and a new state-of-the-art algorithm for a practical problem setting.
