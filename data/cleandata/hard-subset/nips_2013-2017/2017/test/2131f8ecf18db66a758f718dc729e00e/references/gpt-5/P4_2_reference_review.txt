Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonadaptive, semisupervised clustering (label recovery) via crowdsourced queries. It recasts the problem as “locally encodable source coding,” where each query/encoded bit depends on at most Δ source labels. This lens yields information-theoretic lower bounds and query schemes for both exact recovery (noiseless oracle) and approximate recovery (noiseless and noisy oracles). A key insight is that pairwise AND queries (for k=2, “are both items labeled 1?”) can be strictly better than traditional “same-cluster” (XOR) queries for approximate recovery. For noisy oracles and k≥2, the authors propose a simple, nonadaptive scheme on a d-regular graph with a thresholding decoder that achieves (1−δ)-accuracy with O(n log(k/δ)) queries.

The work connects to: query-based clustering (e.g., Ashtiani et al., NIPS’16), crowdsourcing with noise (Karger-Oh-Shah; Vesdapunt et al.; Verroios & Garcia-Molina), and exact/approximate recovery in SBM (Abbe et al.; Hajek et al.). The locally encodable coding viewpoint extends ideas from locally decodable/smooth compression (Makhdoumi et al.; Montanari & Mossel; Mazumdar et al.) and leverages a classical limitation of linear codes for rate–distortion (Ancheta–Massey), to argue about the suboptimality of XOR queries. The empirical portion consists of a small-scale crowdsourcing study that suggests AND queries may be easier/less noisy for workers than same-cluster queries.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The locally encodable source coding framework is a clean, principled way to derive limits when each query only touches a constant number of labels. This appears underexplored and is timely.
- Theory: 
  - Noiseless exact recovery (k=2): Achievability with m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries using Δ-ary XOR measurements and ML decoding, beating n for a range of p and Δ=2; and a new lower bound that improves on the naïve n h(p) in some regimes.
  - Noiseless approximate: A new lower bound incorporating locality Δ; identification (via Massey’s result) that XOR/same-cluster queries are inherently limited for distortion-limited recovery.
  - Achievability with AND queries (k=2): Analysis of a simple decoder with an explicit error expression; shows favorable query–error tradeoffs and dominance over what’s achievable with XOR under the conjectured linear-code lower bound.
  - Noisy approximate (k≥2): A simple, scalable scheme on a d-regular graph with Chernoff-style analysis showing (1−δ)-accuracy with m=O(n log(k/δ)) under independent noise. Extension to known cluster sizes rather than full priors is also provided.
- Practical insight: AND queries can be easier for workers than same-cluster queries, supported by a small experiment.
- Scope: Covers exact vs. approximate recovery, noiseless vs. noisy, binary vs. multi-class labels.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Notational/typographical inconsistency that affects core results: Throughout Section 3.3, the total number of queries is stated as nd2 (which reads like n d^2), while a d-regular graph on n nodes has |E| = nd/2 queries. The numerical example (n=200, d=10 → 1000 queries) confirms the intended count is nd/2. This should be corrected everywhere (including Theorem 5 statement, algorithm heading, figures), as it impacts the scaling message. The final complexity claim O(n log(k/δ)) is consistent with m=Θ(n d) and d=Θ(log(k/δ)).
- Asymmetry of the multi-class query: The k-ary query returns i only when both endpoints have label i, otherwise 0. Since 0 is also a legitimate label, the method defaults to 0 when thresholds are not met. This asymmetry may bias errors toward label 0 and could be suboptimal when p0 is small. Discussion of how to symmetrize (e.g., rotating the “special” label or combining multiple query types) would strengthen the practical applicability.
- Exact recovery achievability (Theorem 1) is via ML over a typical set; it is not computationally efficient. This is fine as an information-theoretic achievability result, but should be stated explicitly.
- Lower bounds: 
  - Theorem 2 (exact recovery) sketch is intuitive but terse; the step that constrains outputs to a Hamming ball of radius ((1−ρ) r(p) Δ m)/ρ warrants more detail.
  - Theorem 3 (approximate recovery) is deferred; the bound form is unusual (involving h′ and e^{Δ h′}) and would benefit from intuition and sanity checks (limits in Δ→∞ recovers classical rate–distortion; Δ=1 behavior; smoothness in m/n).
  - The argument that XOR is suboptimal relies partly on a conjectured lower bound specialized to linear codes. The paper is careful to state it as a conjecture, but claims of dominance over “same-cluster” should be phrased accordingly (AND beats any XOR-based scheme if the conjecture holds), or complemented with unconditional bounds for restricted XOR schemes (e.g., random designs with ML or efficient decoders).
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scale and baselines. The crowdsourcing study with 10 volunteers and 200 items is illustrative but small; the “same-cluster” baseline via spectral clustering may not exploit best-known decoding for XOR-based measurements under noise. Reporting confidence intervals and including a stronger baseline (e.g., tailored ML/LP decoders for XOR queries) would increase credibility.

Quality
- Technical arguments are mostly sound at a high level, but key steps are in the supplement and the nd/2 vs nd^2 inconsistency needs correction. The reliance on a conjectured lower bound for XOR should be clearly separated from unconditional results.
- The noisy recovery algorithm and its analysis are clean and correct modulo the query-count typo.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable but contains several typos, missing bibliographic details, and notation issues. The nd^2/nd/2 inconsistency is the most serious. Some figures are referenced but not visible here; ensure they match statements. Provide intuition for Theorem 3 and a clearer proof sketch for Theorem 2.

Originality
- The locally encodable source coding perspective and derived bounds appear novel. Showing that AND queries can decisively outperform same-cluster/XOR for approximate recovery in both noiseless and noisy settings is an insightful contribution.

Significance
- The results provide actionable guidance for designing nonadaptive query schemes under crowdsourcing constraints, with provable near-optimal query complexity in the noisy approximate regime. The conceptual bridge to local encodability may stimulate further work on locality-constrained inference/compression.

Pros
- Novel theoretical framework (local encodability) with new lower bounds.
- Simple, practical AND-based scheme with strong guarantees: m=O(n log(k/δ)).
- Insightful contrast between AND and XOR queries; supported by a user study.
- Extensions to multi-class and unknown priors (known cluster sizes).

Cons
- Major notation error in query counts; needs correction.
- Some proofs deferred and some steps insufficiently detailed.
- Dependence on a conjectured lower bound for XOR to claim clear superiority.
- Experimental evaluation is small-scale and baselines limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix all instances of m=nd2 to m=nd/2; audit analysis for consistency.
- Provide more intuition and a short proof sketch for Theorem 3 and a clearer argument for Theorem 2.
- Clarify the role of label 0 in multi-class queries; discuss symmetric variants or multiple passes rotating the “special” label.
- Explicitly state that Theorem 1’s decoder is exponential-time; discuss any efficient alternatives or bounds for efficient decoders.
- Strengthen experiments: larger datasets, more workers, stronger XOR baselines; report confidence intervals.
- Where claims rely on the XOR linear-code conjecture, phrase them conditionally and, if possible, add unconditional comparisons for common XOR designs.

Overall assessment
A theoretically interesting and, with corrections, potentially impactful paper that advances our understanding of query design for crowdsourced clustering. The main results are promising, but the presentation requires significant polishing and clarification, especially on query counts and proof details.
