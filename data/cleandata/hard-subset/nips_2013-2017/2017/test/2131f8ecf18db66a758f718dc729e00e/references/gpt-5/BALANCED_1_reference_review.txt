Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonadaptive query-efficient label recovery (equivalently, clustering) using pairwise queries answered by a noisy oracle. It reframes the problem as locally encodable source coding: each query answer is a function of at most Δ labels. This viewpoint yields information-theoretic lower bounds and motivates query designs beyond the standard “same-cluster?” (XOR) queries. The paper’s main technical claims are:
- Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2): With Δ-ary XOR queries (including pairwise same-cluster), one can recover all labels with high probability using m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries (α depends on p and Δ), which can be strictly less than n for a range of priors p. A new lower bound strengthens the classical nh(p) bound for some parameters.
- Noiseless, approximate recovery (k=2): A locality-aware lower bound on distortion vs. number of queries is given. The paper argues, based on limitations of linear (XOR-based) rate–distortion codes, that same-cluster queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery, and proposes AND queries with a simple threshold decoder. A performance expression (via a Poisson left-degree model) shows the achievable distortion can beat the conjectured lower bound for XOR at p=1/2.
- Noisy, approximate recovery (general k): Using a pairwise k-ary “class-wise AND” query that outputs i if both endpoints are label i and 0 otherwise, and a d-regular query graph, Algorithm 1 labels nodes by thresholding the counts of response i around each node. It achieves (1–δ)-accuracy with m = O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive queries under an i.i.d. per-query symmetric noise model (no query repetition). A small crowdsourcing experiment (binary genres for 200 movies) suggests AND queries are easier for workers and supports the predicted trend of error vs. number of queries/thresholds.

The work connects clustering with side-information and crowdsourcing (e.g., Ashtiani et al. NeurIPS’16; Mazumdar & Saha NeurIPS’18; Vinayak & Hassibi triangles) to information-theoretic views of local constraints (locally decodable source coding, smooth compression). The emphasis on locally encodable source coding appears novel.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The “locally encodable source coding” lens is insightful and yields principled lower bounds that account for local query constraints.
- Query design insight: Clear case that XOR/same-cluster is not always the best primitive for approximate recovery; AND-like queries create an expectation gap that is easy to exploit algorithmically.
- Simple, scalable algorithm: Algorithm 1 is nonadaptive, linear-time, and achieves O(n log(k/δ)) queries for noisy approximate labeling; the analysis via Chernoff bounds and dependency graph is clean.
- Some empirical validation: Real (though small) crowdsourcing suggests AND queries incur fewer worker errors than same-cluster questions.

Weaknesses
- Practicality of exact recovery scheme: The XOR-based exact scheme relies on ML decoding over random parity constraints on the typical set; decoding is exponential and the paper does not provide a polynomial-time alternative with similar guarantees.
- Incomplete lower bound story for “same cluster” vs AND: The argument that same-cluster queries are inferior for approximate recovery relies partly on a conjecture (Massey’s) and does not provide unconditional lower bounds showing that same-cluster cannot achieve O(n log(k/δ)) in the noisy setting. It remains unclear whether more sophisticated decoders or graph/query designs for same-cluster can match the proposed AND scheme.
- Noise model and k-ary queries: The noisy model assumes independent symmetric noise over k answers and forbids repetition. While common in theory, it is stylized; the empirical study addresses only k=2 and small n. For k>2, the cognitive load of k-ary AND queries and their actual worker error profiles are not validated.
- Clarity and presentation: Several proofs (e.g., Theorem 3, Theorem 6 details) are deferred; notation and constants in Theorem 2 are terse; some typos and missing figure references reduce readability. The relation to existing bounds for nonadaptive noisy clustering (e.g., O(n log n) type results) is discussed but not compared apples-to-apples (exact vs approximate, different objectives).
- Limited experiments: Small dataset, limited baselines. The comparison to same-cluster uses spectral clustering on synthetic data; more robust comparisons (including algorithms tailored for noisy queries and different δ) would strengthen the empirical case.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: The theoretical components appear mostly sound: achievability analyses are standard (Chernoff, degree-Poissonization), and the exact-recovery XOR scheme uses well-known typicality/expurgation ideas. However, some key pieces are conjectural (limitations of XOR for approximate recovery) or deferred; tight optimality claims are not established.
- Clarity: The main ideas come through, but the manuscript would benefit from a tighter structure, clearer statements (explicit constants/ranges, base of logs), and a self-contained proof of Theorem 3 or at least an expanded sketch.
- Originality: High. The locally encodable source coding framing and the explicit use of AND in this context seem novel.
- Significance: Moderate. The O(n log(k/δ)) result for noisy, nonadaptive, approximate labeling with a simple decoder is attractive, but its broader impact is limited by the stylized noise/prior assumptions and lack of strong lower bounds ruling out same-cluster alternatives.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel theoretical perspective (local encodability) with new lower bounds.
- Simple, effective AND-based algorithm with clear analyses and O(n log(k/δ)) scaling.
- Potential practical insight: AND queries may be more reliable/easier for workers.

Cons for acceptance
- Dependence on conjecture for XOR limitations; no matching lower bounds in the noisy approximate setting.
- Exact-recovery scheme is not computationally practical.
- Empirical validation is limited (small scale; k=2 only; few baselines).
- Presentation issues and deferred proofs hinder accessibility.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper has interesting, potentially influential ideas, but it needs stronger, unconditional lower bounds for same-cluster queries in the approximate/noisy setting (or at least more thorough empirical evidence), a practical decoding scheme for the exact setting, clearer exposition, and more comprehensive experiments, especially for k>2.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen lower bounds: Provide unconditional bounds showing what same-cluster queries cannot achieve under your noise/δ regime, or explicitly narrow the gap by considering more powerful same-cluster decoders/query graphs.
- Practical decoding: For the XOR exact-recovery part, replace ML with a polynomial-time decoder (e.g., LDPC/LDGM-style constructions) and quantify the performance gap.
- Clarify Theorem 3: Include a proof sketch and intuition; clean up constants and the role of Δ in the bound.
- Broaden experiments: Evaluate k>2, larger n, real crowdsourcing with multiple worker populations; compare to strong baselines for noisy clustering with pairwise queries; explore sensitivity to mis-specified p and q, and to degree irregularities.
- Discuss worker design: For k-ary queries, propose concrete user interfaces and validate their error models; analyze trade-offs in cognitive load vs. error reduction.
- Improve presentation: Fix typos, define logs consistently, and ensure all figures and theorems are self-contained.
