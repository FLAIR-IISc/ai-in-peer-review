## NIPS 2017 Review: Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the problem of clustering/labeling with limited queries to an oracle (e.g., crowd workers). It frames this problem as a locally encodable source coding problem, establishing information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries needed for both exact and approximate recovery of labels.  A key contribution is demonstrating that “AND” queries can outperform traditional “same cluster” (XOR) queries, especially in the approximate recovery setting. The authors also consider the scenario of noisy oracle responses and propose a scheme leveraging AND queries, validating their theoretical results with a small-scale crowdsourcing experiment.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds upon a growing body of research exploring crowdsourcing for clustering and entity resolution (e.g., [4, 8, 20, 33]). It connects this area to fundamental concepts in information theory, specifically source coding and its variants like locally decodable and smooth coding [16, 19, 23].  The authors correctly position their contribution as extending the existing literature by providing new information-theoretic bounds and proposing a more efficient querying strategy than solely relying on XOR queries, drawing connections to Massey’s work on rate distortion [17].

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Framing:** The perspective of framing the querying problem as locally encodable source coding is insightful and allows the application of information-theoretic tools. This is a strong theoretical contribution.
* **Information-Theoretic Rigor:** The paper provides rigorous lower bounds for query complexity, which is a valuable contribution to understanding the fundamental limits of the problem.
* **AND Query Proposal:** The demonstration that AND queries can outperform XOR queries in approximate recovery is a significant finding.  The intuition behind this (requiring less information from the oracle for a “no” answer) is well explained.
* **Noisy Oracle Consideration:**  Addressing the practical scenario of noisy oracle responses and presenting a scheme that mitigates errors is commendable.
* **Empirical Validation:** The small-scale crowdsourcing experiment, while limited, provides some support for the theoretical findings.  The observation regarding majority voting increasing errors aligns with recent findings in the field [27].
* **Clear Mathematical Presentation:**  The paper includes rigorous mathematical derivations (although some are relegated to the appendix), which increase the confidence in the results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Experimental Scope:** The crowdsourcing experiment is relatively small and uses a specific dataset (movie genres). The generalizability of the findings to other datasets and query types isn't thoroughly investigated.
* **Complexity of the Noisy Case Analysis:**  While the theoretical result for the noisy case is significant, the assumptions (k-ary answers, independent errors) might be restrictive.  The derivation is complex, making it harder to assess the practicality of the proposed scheme.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper focuses heavily on theoretical aspects. Discussion of practical considerations for implementing and deploying the proposed schemes (e.g., choosing the appropriate graph structure for AND queries, managing query costs) is limited.
* **Connection to Existing Clustering Algorithms:** While the paper acknowledges the link to clustering, the connection to existing clustering algorithms (e.g., spectral clustering) beyond mentioning it in the noisy case experiment could be strengthened. A comparison of the proposed approach with established clustering techniques would be valuable.
* **Some Notation and Definitions Could Be Improved:** The introduction of several concepts (locally encodable/decodable coding, smooth compression) without fully explaining their nuances might be challenging for readers not deeply familiar with information theory.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of crowdsourced clustering. The framing of the problem as a locally encodable source coding problem is novel and insightful, leading to useful information-theoretic bounds and a practical improvement in query design (AND queries). While the experimental validation is limited, it provides some support for the theoretical findings. The complexity of the analysis, particularly for the noisy oracle case, and the lack of deeper discussion regarding practical implementation are minor weaknesses.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach.
* Provides concrete improvements to query strategies (AND queries).
* Offers valuable insights into the fundamental limits of crowdsourced clustering.
* Rigorous mathematical analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited experimental validation.
* Relatively high complexity and assumptions in the noisy oracle analysis.
* Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges.

**Rating:**

**Strong Accept (7/10)**

I believe this paper is a valuable contribution to NIPS and merits acceptance. The theoretical insights and proposed querying strategy are significant. Addressing the limitations regarding experimental scope and practical considerations would further strengthen the paper.
