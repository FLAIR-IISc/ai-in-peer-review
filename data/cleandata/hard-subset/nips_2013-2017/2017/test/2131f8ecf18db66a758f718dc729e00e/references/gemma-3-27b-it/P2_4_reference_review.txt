## Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the problem of clustering with limited pairwise queries to an oracle (e.g., crowdworkers), focusing on minimizing the number of queries needed for accurate label recovery. It frames this problem as a locally encodable source coding problem, establishing information-theoretic lower bounds and proposing a novel querying strategy based on "AND" queries, arguing these outperform traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries, especially in noisy settings.  The authors also present theoretical results for noisy oracles and validate their approach with a small-scale crowdsourcing experiment.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Framing:** The connection to locally encodable source coding is a significant and insightful perspective.  It allows the authors to leverage tools from information theory to provide rigorous bounds on query complexity.
* **Theoretical Rigor:**  The paper provides a substantial body of theoretical analysis, including lower bounds, algorithmic guarantees, and analysis of noisy oracle scenarios.  The proofs, while potentially dense, demonstrate a strong mathematical foundation.
* **AND Query Insight:** The identification of AND queries as potentially superior to XOR queries is a key contribution. The justification based on Massey's work and the subsequent algorithmic development are compelling.
* **Noisy Oracle Handling:** Addressing the realistic scenario of noisy oracles and proposing a scheme to mitigate errors is valuable and demonstrates practical consideration.
* **Experimental Validation:**  The small crowdsourcing experiment provides some initial support for the theoretical claims, although limited in scale.
* **Clear Connection to Related Work:** The authors meticulously position their work within the broader context of clustering, crowdsourcing, information theory, and source coding, citing relevant literature comprehensively.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity and Readability:** The paper is very dense and mathematically demanding. The numerous theorems and proofs, while rigorous, make it challenging for a broad audience to follow the core ideas without significant effort.  More intuitive explanations and high-level summaries could improve accessibility.
* **Experimental Scope:** The crowdsourcing experiment is limited in scale and scope. While it provides preliminary validation, it doesn't convincingly demonstrate the practical advantage of AND queries over XOR queries in a real-world scenario with larger datasets and more participants.  A more comprehensive experimental evaluation is needed. The choice of movies as data is somewhat arbitrary and doesn't demonstrate generalizability.
* **Practical Considerations:** While the theoretical results are strong, the practical implementation of the proposed algorithms, especially the d-regular graph construction and decoding step in the noisy case, could be complex and computationally expensive.  This is not adequately addressed.
* **Repetition and Redundancy:** Certain concepts and justifications are repeated multiple times throughout the paper, adding to its length and hindering readability.
* **Limited Discussion of Alternatives:** The paper primarily focuses on comparing AND and XOR queries.  A more thorough exploration of other query types or combinations of queries might reveal even better solutions.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and claims are generally well-supported. However, the density of the proofs and the limited experimental validation raise some concerns. It’s a complete piece of work in terms of theoretical development, but feels incomplete regarding practical implications.
* **Clarity:** The paper is often difficult to follow due to its mathematical complexity and dense writing style.  Significant improvements in clarity are needed to make the ideas accessible to a wider audience.
* **Originality:** The framing of the problem as locally encodable source coding and the proposal of AND queries are novel contributions. The paper builds upon existing work but offers a fresh perspective and demonstrates significant originality.
* **Significance:** The results are potentially important, particularly for scenarios where querying costs are high. The theoretical bounds and the exploration of alternative query types could have a lasting impact on the field. However, the practical significance is still unclear due to the limited experimental validation.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and novel framing of the problem.
*  Clear potential to advance the state of the art in query-efficient clustering.
*  Well-written and comprehensive literature review.
*  Identifies a potentially significant improvement in query efficiency (AND queries).

**Against Acceptance:**

*  High level of mathematical complexity and challenging readability.
* Limited experimental validation.
* Practical considerations of implementation are not fully addressed.
*  Potential for further exploration of alternative query strategies.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong borderline accept**. While the theoretical contributions are significant and the ideas are novel, the lack of a more compelling experimental evaluation and the challenges in readability hold it back from being a clear acceptance.  I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing the following:

1. **Improve Clarity:**  Provide more intuitive explanations and high-level summaries of the key results.
2. **Expand Experiments:**  Conduct a more comprehensive experimental evaluation on larger datasets and with more participants, directly comparing the performance of AND and XOR queries.
3. **Address Practicality:** Discuss the computational complexity and practical challenges of implementing the proposed algorithms.
4. **Consider Alternatives:**  Briefly explore other potential query strategies or combinations of queries.

If the authors can address these concerns, the paper has the potential to make a substantial contribution to the field.
