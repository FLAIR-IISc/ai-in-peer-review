### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of clustering, or more specifically label recovery, using a limited budget of non-adaptive queries to an oracle. The central and most novel contribution is the framing of this problem within the information-theoretic lens of "locally encodable source coding," where each query answer (an encoded bit) depends on a small, constant number of element labels (source bits). Building on this framework, the paper makes a compelling case that standard "same-cluster" queries (equivalent to XOR) are suboptimal for approximate recovery. It proposes a new query type, the "AND-query," and demonstrates its superiority, particularly in the challenging and practical setting of noisy oracle answers. The main algorithmic result is a non-adaptive scheme using these generalized AND-queries that can recover a (1-δ) fraction of labels correctly with O(n log(k/δ)) queries, even when the oracle is noisy. This theoretical result is supported by a small-scale real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by theorems and proof sketches that appear sound.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Sound Theoretical Framework:** The connection to locally encodable source coding is rigorous and provides a principled way to derive lower and upper bounds on the required number of queries. The application of concepts from coding theory (e.g., typical sets, expurgation, Chernoff bounds) is appropriate and well-executed.
2.  **Complete Analysis:** The paper systematically analyzes different settings: noiseless/exact, noiseless/approximate, and noisy/approximate recovery. This structured approach builds a comprehensive picture of the problem and highlights the specific regimes where the proposed AND-queries excel.
3.  **Strong Main Result:** The algorithm for noisy approximate recovery (Algorithm 1) and its analysis (Theorem 5) is the paper's strongest result. Achieving (1-δ) recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries in the presence of noise is a significant improvement over prior art, which often required O(n log n) queries.
4.  **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of a real crowdsourcing experiment, while small, serves as an important proof of concept that validates the theoretical model and the algorithm's performance. The observation that AND-queries elicited fewer errors from human workers than "same-cluster" queries provides strong practical justification for the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Proof Density:** The proof sketches in the main body are very dense and rely heavily on deep results from information and coding theory (e.g., Theorem 2 and 3). While this is understandable given page limits, it may make the core arguments less accessible to a broader machine learning audience. A bit more intuition in the main text could improve readability.
2.  **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on i.i.d. priors for the labels. While the authors justify this and show in Theorem 6 that knowing cluster sizes is sufficient, this assumption might not hold in all real-world clustering scenarios where labels can exhibit structural dependencies.

### Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and organized, but could be improved in a few areas.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Logical Structure:** The paper flows logically from the problem setup to the simplest case (noiseless, exact) and builds towards the main contribution (noisy, approximate). The introduction clearly states the contributions and provides necessary background.
2.  **Clear Motivation for AND-queries:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating why "same-cluster" (XOR) queries are limited for approximate recovery (citing Massey's work) and how the asymmetry of AND-queries can be exploited, especially in the noisy setting.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Terminology:** The use of "Semisupervised Clustering" in the title is potentially misleading. In machine learning, this term usually refers to having a small set of pre-labeled data points. The problem studied here is more accurately described as "active clustering," "clustering with an oracle," or "interactive clustering."
2.  **Experimental Details:** While the experiment is a valuable addition, more details on the comparison with spectral clustering would be beneficial. For instance, how were the noisy "same-cluster" query answers converted into an affinity matrix for the spectral method? Justifying the choice of spectral clustering as the baseline would also strengthen the comparison.

### Originality

The paper is highly original and makes several novel contributions.

1.  **New Theoretical Connection:** The primary novelty is the conceptual bridge between oracle-based clustering and locally encodable source coding. To my knowledge, this is a new perspective in the clustering literature and provides a powerful new toolkit for analysis.
2.  **Novel Query Type:** The proposal and analysis of AND-queries is a significant and original contribution. Most prior work in this area has focused exclusively on "same-cluster" queries. This paper challenges that default assumption and demonstrates that other simple, binary-answer queries can be substantially more efficient.
3.  **New Bounds and Algorithms:** The information-theoretic lower bounds and the near-optimal non-adaptive algorithms derived from this new perspective are novel contributions that advance the state of the art in the theory of active clustering.

The paper does a good job of situating its work within the context of related literature on crowdsourcing, active clustering, and information theory.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are significant for both researchers and potentially practitioners in crowdsourcing and data labeling.

1.  **Advancing the State of the Art:** The O(n log(k/δ)) query complexity for noisy approximate recovery is a strong theoretical advancement. It shows that it is possible to achieve high accuracy with a sub-linear (in n) number of queries per item, even with unreliable oracles.
2.  **Practical Implications:** The idea that the *type* of question asked to a crowd worker matters profoundly is a key insight. The finding that AND-queries are not only more query-efficient but also potentially less error-prone for human annotators has direct practical implications for designing better crowdsourcing systems for entity resolution and data labeling.
3.  **Opening New Research Directions:** The connection to local source coding opens up a new avenue for research. It invites further exploration of different query types, different coding schemes, and the fundamental limits of learning from local information.

### Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Highly novel and powerful theoretical framework connecting active clustering to locally encodable source coding.
*   Introduces a new, non-obvious query type (AND-query) and proves its superiority over the standard "same-cluster" query in important settings.
*   Presents a strong main result: an algorithm for noisy approximate recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) query complexity, advancing the state-of-the-art.
*   The work is comprehensive, including theoretical lower bounds, upper bounds (algorithms), and experimental validation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Clarity could be improved, particularly regarding the density of the theoretical arguments and the use of the term "semisupervised."
*   The real-world experiment is small-scale, providing a proof-of-concept rather than conclusive evidence of large-scale practical utility.

### Recommendation

The strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. The novelty of the approach and the significance of the results make it a standout contribution. It introduces a new and fruitful perspective on a well-studied problem and delivers strong theoretical and practical insights. I strongly recommend acceptance.
