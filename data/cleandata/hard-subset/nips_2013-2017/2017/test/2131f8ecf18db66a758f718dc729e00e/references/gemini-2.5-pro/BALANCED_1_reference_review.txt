### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of semi-supervised clustering, where the goal is to determine the correct labels for `n` items belonging to `k` clusters using a minimal number of non-adaptive queries to an oracle. The authors make a novel and significant contribution by framing this problem within the information-theoretic lens of "locally encodable source coding," where each query answer (a compressed bit) depends on a small, constant number (`∆`) of item labels (source bits). This perspective allows for the derivation of new performance bounds.

The paper presents several key results across different settings:
1.  **Noiseless, Exact Recovery:** For `k=2` clusters, the authors analyze "same-cluster" (XOR) queries. They provide an achievable query scheme that requires fewer than `n` queries for certain label priors, improving upon standard approaches. They also derive a new information-theoretic lower bound that is tighter than the classic entropy bound `nh(p)` in some regimes.
2.  **Noiseless, Approximate Recovery:** The paper investigates recovering labels up to a `δ` fraction of errors. It argues that "same-cluster" queries are suboptimal for this goal and proposes "AND" queries as a more efficient alternative. A new lower bound for any locally encodable scheme is presented, and an achievable scheme using AND queries is shown to outperform the theoretical limits of XOR-based methods.
3.  **Noisy, Approximate Recovery:** This is the paper's main algorithmic contribution. For the general `k`-cluster case with noisy query answers, the authors generalize the AND query and propose a simple, efficient recovery algorithm based on queries on a d-regular graph. They prove that this scheme achieves `(1-δ)`-good recovery with `O(n log(k/δ))` queries, a significant improvement over the `O(n log n)` complexity of prior art for noisy settings.

The theoretical results are supported by a real-world crowdsourcing experiment on a movie genre classification task, which validates the proposed algorithm and the practical benefits of AND queries over "same-cluster" queries.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel Conceptual Framework:** The connection drawn between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding is highly original and powerful. It provides a principled, information-theoretic foundation to study the fundamental limits of the problem. This fresh perspective is a significant contribution that could open up new research directions at the intersection of active learning, crowdsourcing, and coding theory.

2.  **Introduction of AND Queries:** The paper makes a compelling case against the ubiquitous "same-cluster" (XOR) query for approximate recovery. The introduction and analysis of AND queries is a key strength. The authors provide strong theoretical justification (building on ideas from rate-distortion theory) and empirical evidence (lower error rates from crowd workers) for the superiority of AND queries in certain settings. This challenges a default assumption in the field and offers a practical, better-performing alternative.

3.  **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper delivers technically sound and significant results. The achievability of exact recovery with fewer than `n` queries (Theorem 1) is interesting. More importantly, the query complexity of `O(n log(k/δ))` for noisy, approximate recovery (Theorem 5) is a state-of-the-art result. It demonstrates that for a constant error tolerance `δ`, the required number of queries per item does not grow with the total number of items `n`, which is a substantial improvement over existing `O(n log n)` bounds.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation is clear, and the progression from noiseless exact recovery to noisy approximate recovery helps build intuition. The information-theoretic viewpoint is introduced effectively in Section 2.

### Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions

While this is a strong paper, there are areas where it could be improved:

1.  **Clarity of Proof Sketches:** Some of the proof sketches in the main text are quite dense and could be more accessible. For instance, in the proof of Theorem 1, the use of an expurgation argument is mentioned, but the explanation is compressed. The sentence "if we take `epsilon` to be of the form `n^-β`..." seems to have a typo (`epsilon` is not defined in the surrounding text) and could be clarified to make the logic of the argument more transparent to the reader. Adding a bit more intuition would be helpful. Similarly, the proof of Theorem 3 is deferred entirely; a brief, high-level intuition in the main text about why local encodability leads to this specific bound would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Practicality of Algorithm 1:** The proposed algorithm for the noisy setting (Algorithm 1) relies on thresholds `Ci` that depend on the prior probabilities `p` and the noise rate `q`. The paper notes that `p` can be estimated. However, it is silent on the estimation of `q`. How is `q` obtained in practice? Furthermore, how robust is the algorithm's performance to errors in the estimation of `p` and `q`? A discussion on the sensitivity to these parameters, or a brief experimental evaluation of this robustness, would significantly enhance the practical value of the proposed method.

3.  **Scope of Experiments:** The experiment is a great "proof of concept" that validates the core ideas. However, its scope is somewhat limited.
    *   The graph construction for the queries (connecting neighbors on a permuted circle) is a specific method to generate a d-regular graph. It would be valuable to know if the algorithm's performance is sensitive to the graph structure. For example, does it perform similarly on an expander graph or other types of random d-regular graphs?
    *   In Figure 5, results for different prediction thresholds (T=2, 3, 4) are shown, while the theory points to T=2. The results for T=3 and T=4 are much worse. A sentence or two in the text explaining why T=2 is the theoretically correct choice and why the others fail would provide useful context for the reader.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly novel and insightful connection between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding.
*   Introduces AND queries as a superior alternative to standard "same-cluster" queries, a significant conceptual and practical contribution.
*   Delivers a state-of-the-art theoretical result for noisy, approximate clustering, reducing query complexity from `O(n log n)` to `O(n log(k/δ))`.
*   The work is technically solid and combines ideas from multiple fields in an elegant way.
*   The claims are supported by both theoretical proofs and a real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Some theoretical explanations in the main text are overly condensed and could be improved for clarity.
*   The practical implementation details of the main algorithm, particularly regarding the estimation of and sensitivity to model parameters (`p`, `q`), are not fully addressed.
*   The experimental validation, while positive, is based on a single dataset and a specific graph construction.

### Final Assessment

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant contributions in terms of its novel conceptual framework, its challenge to standard query types, and its strong theoretical results. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to clarifying some details and expanding the experimental discussion. These are minor issues that can be addressed in a final version. The paper advances the state of the art in semi-supervised clustering and is likely to inspire follow-up work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
