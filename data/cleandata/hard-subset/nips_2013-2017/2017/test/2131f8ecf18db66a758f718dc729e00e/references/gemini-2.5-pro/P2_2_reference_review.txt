### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of semi-supervised clustering, framed as learning a vector of `n` labels, each from a set of `k` possible values. The learning is performed by making non-adaptive queries to an oracle (e.g., a crowd worker). The central contributions are twofold: 1) It introduces a novel information-theoretic perspective by framing the problem as "locally encodable source coding," where each query (an encoded bit) can only depend on a small number (`Δ`) of input labels. 2) It challenges the ubiquitous "same-cluster" (or XOR) query, proposing that an "AND" query can be significantly more efficient, especially for approximate recovery and in noisy settings.

The paper analyzes three scenarios:
1.  **Noiseless, Exact Recovery:** It provides information-theoretic upper and lower bounds on the number of XOR queries needed for perfect label recovery, demonstrating that for certain label priors, fewer than `n` queries are sufficient.
2.  **Noiseless, Approximate Recovery:** It derives a new lower bound for any `Δ`-local query scheme and argues that XOR queries are suboptimal for this goal. It then proposes a pairwise AND query scheme and shows its superior performance in achieving a `(1-δ)`-good recovery (at most `δn` errors).
3.  **Noisy, Approximate Recovery:** This section presents the main algorithmic contribution. It develops a scheme using a generalized AND query for `k` clusters where oracle answers can be noisy. The proposed algorithm achieves `(1-δ)`-good recovery with `O(n log(k/δ))` queries, a significant improvement over prior work that required `O(n log n)`. The paper supports its claims with a small-scale, real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   **Strengths:** The theoretical analysis is its strongest aspect. The connection to locally encodable source coding is insightful and provides a principled way to derive lower bounds. The theorems presented appear sound, and the results are significant, particularly the `O(n log(k/δ))` query complexity for the noisy, approximate case. This result is impressive as it is sublinear in `n` in terms of total queries-per-item (`log(k/δ)`) and scales gracefully with the desired accuracy. The paper is a complete piece of work, containing theoretical lower bounds, constructive upper bounds (algorithms), and experimental validation.
*   **Weaknesses:** Some of the proof sketches in the main body are quite brief. For instance, the proof of Theorem 5 relies on Chernoff bounds with undefined constants and a final concentration step justified by a citation. While this is common due to space constraints, a slightly more detailed sketch would improve clarity and confidence in the result. The experimental validation, while a welcome addition, is on a very small scale (200 items, 10 volunteers). While it provides a good proof-of-concept for the claim that AND queries are less error-prone for humans, its small size limits its power as a definitive validation of the algorithm's performance scaling.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.
*   **Strengths:** The introduction and problem setup are very clear, effectively motivating the non-adaptive setting and the information-theoretic perspective. The distinction between XOR and AND queries and the rationale for preferring the latter are well-articulated.
*   **Weaknesses:** The transition between the different sections (3.1, 3.2, and 3.3) could be smoother. The focus shifts from binary labels and binary-output queries (3.1, 3.2) to k-ary labels and k-ary-output queries (3.3) somewhat abruptly. Explicitly stating this shift and the reasons for it would help the reader. The presentation of the main algorithm (Algorithm 1) and its analysis is dense, and a more intuitive walkthrough of why the thresholds `Ci` are chosen as they are would be beneficial.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original.
*   **Strengths:** The primary conceptual contribution—casting the active clustering problem as locally encodable source coding—is novel and powerful. To my knowledge, this connection has not been explored in the clustering or active learning literature. This reframing is not just a relabeling; it provides new tools for analysis and a new lens through which to view the problem. Secondly, the proposal to use AND queries instead of the standard "same-cluster" (XOR) queries is a simple but profound idea with significant practical and theoretical implications. The paper clearly delineates how its approach differs from prior work in active clustering and crowdsourcing.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant and likely to have an impact.
*   **Strengths:** The paper addresses a problem of practical importance in crowdsourcing and machine learning: minimizing query cost for clustering. The proposed `O(n log(k/δ))` algorithm for the noisy setting is a substantial improvement on the state of the art and makes large-scale clustering with noisy oracles much more feasible. The insight that the type of query matters fundamentally (AND vs. XOR) is a key takeaway that could inspire new research directions in active learning and query design beyond clustering. Researchers and practitioners working on crowdsourcing, active learning, and large-scale data labeling are likely to build upon these ideas.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions

*   The argument that AND queries are less error-prone for human workers because a "no" answer only requires identifying one item as not belonging to the target class is very persuasive and is a key practical justification for the proposed method. The experimental result (105 errors for AND vs. 234 for XOR) provides strong support for this claim.
*   The assumption that cluster priors `p` are known is common, but it is a limitation. The paper rightly points out this can be estimated via subsampling. Theorem 6, which shows the algorithm works with known cluster sizes (which is a slightly weaker assumption), is a good addition that strengthens the practical applicability.
*   In the experimental section, the comparison against spectral clustering on a synthetically generated dataset feels a bit indirect. Spectral clustering is a generic clustering algorithm, not one designed for the "noisy pairwise query" model. A more direct comparison would be to implement a baseline algorithm that uses noisy "same-cluster" queries (perhaps based on the cited work [21]) and compare its performance against Algorithm 1 on the same synthetic data.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper that makes significant theoretical and practical contributions to the field of semi-supervised clustering and active learning. Its core strengths are the novel and powerful information-theoretic framing and the insightful proposal and analysis of AND queries, which are shown to be superior to standard queries in the approximate and noisy settings. The theoretical results are strong, culminating in a highly efficient algorithm for noisy clustering. Despite minor weaknesses in the brevity of some proofs and the limited scale of the experiment, the originality and significance of the contributions are undeniable. The paper is well-written, tackles an important problem, and advances the state of the art. I strongly recommend acceptance.
