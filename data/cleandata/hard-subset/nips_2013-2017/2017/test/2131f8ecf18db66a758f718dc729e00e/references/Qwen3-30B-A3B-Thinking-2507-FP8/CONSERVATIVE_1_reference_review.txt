

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper proposes a novel information-theoretic framework for clustering by framing it as a locally encodable source coding problem and introduces AND-queries as a more efficient alternative to traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries. The authors claim that AND-queries can achieve better performance for approximate recovery with fewer queries, supported by theoretical bounds and a small-scale crowdsourcing experiment.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper builds on established work in clustering with pairwise queries (e.g., [4, 8, 21]) and information theory (e.g., source coding [7, 19]). While the authors frame clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem, this connection appears to be a rephrasing of known concepts rather than a fundamentally new theoretical development. The paper's main contribution is the proposal of AND-queries for approximate recovery, which the authors claim outperforms XOR ("same cluster") queries.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Rigor**: The paper provides non-trivial theoretical bounds (Theorems 1-5) for both noiseless and noisy settings, demonstrating that AND-queries can achieve better query complexity for approximate recovery compared to XOR queries. The proofs for Theorems 1, 4, and 5 are technically sound.

2. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper clearly defines the problem setup and distinguishes between exact and approximate recovery, which is important for understanding the practical implications.

3. **Real-World Validation**: The authors conducted a small-scale crowdsourcing experiment comparing AND-queries with "same cluster" queries, finding that AND-queries had fewer errors (105 vs. 234 errors in the experiment).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comprehensive Baseline Comparison**: The paper claims AND-queries are "substantially better" but fails to compare against state-of-the-art clustering algorithms that might be used in practice. For example, the authors mention spectral clustering for "same cluster" queries but don't compare the AND-query approach against standard clustering methods with similar query complexity.

2. **Overstated Novelty**: The paper claims to be the first to study locally encodable source coding, but this concept was mentioned in [23] (which the authors cite) under the name "robust compression." The paper's main contribution is the application to clustering, not the theoretical framework itself.

3. **Inadequate Experimental Validation**: The crowdsourcing experiment is limited to 100 action and 100 romantic movies with only 10 volunteers. This small-scale study lacks statistical significance and doesn't account for potential biases in the volunteer pool. The paper doesn't provide confidence intervals or statistical tests for the error rate comparisons.

4. **Questionable Practical Relevance**: The paper doesn't convincingly explain why AND-queries would be adopted in real-world applications over existing methods. The "easier to answer" claim for workers is not supported by sufficient evidence beyond the small experiment.

5. **Theoretical Claims Without Practical Guarantees**: The paper claims "O(n log(k/δ)) queries" are sufficient for (1-δ)-good recovery, but doesn't provide concrete constants or explain how these would scale in practice. The experimental results in Figure 5 show a gap between theoretical and empirical performance, raising questions about practical applicability.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theoretical understanding of query complexity in clustering.
- The AND-query approach is genuinely novel in the context of clustering with pairwise queries.
- The theoretical results are well-supported with rigorous proofs.
- The small-scale experiment provides initial empirical validation of the approach.

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- The practical significance of the work is unclear; the paper doesn't demonstrate how this would improve real-world clustering applications.
- The experimental validation is insufficiently rigorous and lacks statistical power.
- The paper overstates the novelty of the information-theoretic framework.
- The comparison against established methods is inadequate; the paper doesn't show how AND-queries compare to state-of-the-art clustering algorithms with similar query complexity.
- The theoretical guarantees don't translate clearly to practical performance as evidenced by the gap between theoretical and experimental results in Figure 5.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the understanding of query complexity in clustering, it falls short of making a compelling case for the practical significance of its approach. The main claim—that AND-queries substantially outperform "same cluster" queries—lacks sufficient experimental validation to support its adoption in real-world applications. The theoretical results are sound but don't address the most pressing question: why should practitioners use this approach instead of existing clustering methods?

The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison against state-of-the-art clustering algorithms, larger-scale experiments with proper statistical analysis, and a clearer explanation of the practical advantages of AND-queries over existing methods. Without these, the work remains primarily of theoretical interest with limited practical impact.

Given NIPS' emphasis on significant contributions that advance the state of the art, this paper does not meet the threshold for acceptance. The theoretical novelty is modest, and the practical significance is unconvincing. The authors should substantially strengthen their experimental validation and provide a more compelling case for why their approach would be adopted in practice before resubmission.
