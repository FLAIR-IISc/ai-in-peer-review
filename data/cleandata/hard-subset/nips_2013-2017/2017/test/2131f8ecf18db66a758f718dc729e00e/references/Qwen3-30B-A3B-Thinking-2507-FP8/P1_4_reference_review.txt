

# Peer Review: "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

## Summary

This paper addresses the problem of clustering elements with minimal query complexity, particularly in crowdsourcing contexts. The authors introduce a novel query type called "AND-queries" that outperforms traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery. They frame the problem as a locally encodable source coding problem in information theory, providing theoretical bounds and algorithms that leverage this perspective. The paper makes three main contributions: (1) noiseless exact recovery with pairwise queries, (2) noiseless approximate recovery with AND-queries, and (3) noisy approximate recovery with AND-queries as the main algorithmic contribution. The authors validate their theoretical results with a real crowdsourcing experiment on movie genre classification.

The paper connects to previous NIPS work on clustering with queries (e.g., [33, 30, 8, 20, 22, 21]) and information theory (locally decodable source coding [19, 16, 26, 6]). It differs significantly by proposing AND-queries rather than XOR queries and establishing their theoretical advantages for approximate recovery.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by introducing AND-queries and proving their superiority over traditional "same cluster" queries for approximate recovery. The authors establish new information-theoretic lower bounds for locally encodable source coding, which appears to be the first nontrivial result in this area.

2. **Strong Theoretical Results**: The proofs of Theorems 1-5 are technically sound and well-structured. The connection between clustering and source coding is insightful and provides a fresh perspective on an established problem. The paper's main result (Theorem 5) shows that AND-queries can recover (1-δ) proportion of labels with O(n log(k/δ)) queries, which is a significant improvement over the O(n log n) queries required by prior work.

3. **Practical Validation**: The real-world crowdsourcing experiment on movie genres provides strong empirical validation of the theoretical results. The authors show that AND-queries produce fewer errors (105 vs. 234) compared to "same cluster" queries, which directly supports their theoretical claims.

4. **Clear Organization**: The paper is well-organized with clear sections for each contribution. The figures effectively illustrate the theoretical results, particularly Figure 2 showing the performance advantage of AND-queries.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Theoretical Results**: While the paper mentions extensions to k > 2 clusters, most theoretical results focus on k=2. The main algorithmic contribution (Theorem 5) works for general k, but the experimental validation is limited to k=2. It would strengthen the paper to provide more intuition or results for k > 2.

2. **Supplementary Material Dependency**: Several key proofs and details are relegated to the supplementary material, which may make it difficult for readers to follow the main arguments without referring to additional content. For example, the proof of Theorem 3 is in the appendix.

3. **Practical Experiment Limitations**: The crowdsourcing experiment uses only 200 movies (100 action, 100 romantic) and 10 volunteers. While the results are promising, the small scale limits generalizability. It would be beneficial to see results on larger or more complex datasets.

4. **Unknown Prior Distribution**: The paper assumes knowledge of the prior distribution p (relative cluster sizes), which may not always be available in practice. While the authors mention using relative sizes as priors in the experiment, the paper doesn't address how to handle cases where p is unknown.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution with clear practical implications for crowdsourcing applications. The paper makes a significant advance in understanding query complexity for clustering by introducing AND-queries and establishing their theoretical advantages. The connection to locally encodable source coding is novel and well-executed.

The main weakness is the limited scope of the theoretical results (primarily k=2) and the relatively small-scale experiment. However, these limitations are minor compared to the paper's strong theoretical contributions and practical validation.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of query complexity in clustering, with a novel approach that has practical implications for crowdsourcing systems. The theoretical results are well-supported, the paper is well-written, and the experimental validation provides strong evidence for the practical relevance of the theoretical claims.

The authors have clearly established how their work differs from previous contributions (e.g., [21] requires O(n log n) queries while their approach achieves O(n log(1/δ))), and they have provided sufficient references to related work.

The significance of this work is high, as it addresses a fundamental problem in crowdsourcing (minimizing query cost) with a theoretically sound approach that outperforms existing methods. The results will likely influence future work on clustering with queries and has potential applications in real-world crowdsourcing systems.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include more discussion of the k > 2 case in the main results, not just in the supplementary material.

2. Provide a more detailed analysis of the unknown prior distribution case, as this is a common practical limitation.

3. Consider expanding the crowdsourcing experiment to a larger dataset or more diverse task to strengthen the empirical validation.

4. In the related work section, explicitly compare with [27] (mentioned in the paper) which also observes that majority voting can increase errors in crowdsourcing.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it combines theoretical contributions to information theory with practical applications in machine learning, aligning well with the conference's focus on theoretical advances with practical implications.
