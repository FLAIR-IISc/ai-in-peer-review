This paper addresses the problem of semi-supervised clustering, where the goal is to recover the ground-truth labels of `n` elements by making non-adaptive queries to an oracle. The central contributions are the novel framing of this problem as one of locally encodable source coding, the introduction of "AND" queries as an alternative to standard "same cluster" (XOR) queries, and the analysis of these queries in noiseless and noisy settings for both exact and approximate recovery.

The authors relate their work to the existing literature on query-based clustering and crowdsourcing (e.g., [4, 21, 33]). The primary distinction of this work is its information-theoretic perspective. By viewing the query process as a source coding problem where each compressed bit (query answer) depends on a limited number (`Δ`) of source bits (labels), the authors are able to derive new fundamental limits (lower bounds) and achievability schemes (upper bounds). The paper investigates three main scenarios: (1) noiseless queries for exact label recovery, (2) noiseless queries for approximate recovery, and (3) noisy queries for approximate recovery. For the latter two, the paper argues and demonstrates that the proposed AND queries are more efficient than traditional XOR queries. An algorithm for the noisy, approximate recovery setting is presented and validated with a real crowdsourcing experiment.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Theoretical Framework:** The connection drawn between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding is highly original. This framework provides a principled way to analyze the fundamental trade-offs between the number of queries and recovery accuracy, leading to new lower bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) that are not attainable through purely combinatorial or algorithmic perspectives. This is a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Novelty of Approach:** The proposal of using AND queries is a key strength. The paper provides compelling intuition for why AND queries might be preferable: they are asymmetric and can be easier for human annotators to answer correctly, and they break the symmetry that limits the effectiveness of linear (XOR) queries in noisy, approximate settings. This insight is valuable and opens up a new direction for designing queries in crowdsourcing systems.

3.  **Significant Results:** The paper presents a strong algorithmic result for the practical setting of noisy queries and approximate recovery. Theorem 5 shows that `O(n log(k/δ))` queries are sufficient to correctly label a `(1-δ)` fraction of elements. This query complexity is substantially better than the `O(n log n)` required by prior methods in the noisy setting and is highly relevant for budget-constrained applications.

4.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical claims appear to be sound, building upon established information-theoretic techniques such as random coding with expurgation, rate-distortion theory, and concentration bounds. The analysis provides a solid foundation for the claims made.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** While generally well-written, the paper's clarity suffers from deferring key proofs and derivations to the supplementary material (e.g., proofs for Theorems 3 and 6, and the detailed analysis for Theorem 5). The proof sketch for Theorem 4 relies on a Poisson degree distribution assumption for a graph with a fixed degree on one side, which is a common but not fully rigorous approximation; a more detailed justification would be beneficial. The presentation of Algorithm 1 requires knowledge of the prior `p` and the noise rate `q` to set the thresholds. While the dependence on `p` is addressed in Theorem 6, the algorithm's sensitivity to the estimation of `q` is not discussed, which is a crucial parameter in any real-world application.

2.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments provide a useful proof of concept but are somewhat limited. The crowdsourcing experiment involves a small number of items (`n=200`) and annotators (10 volunteers), which may not fully reflect the complexities of large-scale crowdsourcing. More importantly, while the paper claims that AND queries received far fewer erroneous answers than 'same cluster' queries on the real dataset (105 vs. 234), the performance comparison of the recovery algorithms is shown only for a synthetic dataset. A direct comparison on the real dataset would be more compelling, even if the high error rate for 'same cluster' queries leads to poor performance, as this would directly validate the central claim of the paper.

3.  **Scope and Generalizability:** The proposed AND query is asymmetric, defined as `Q(X, X') = i` if both labels are `i`, and 0 otherwise. This and the accompanying Algorithm 1 appear best suited for problems where one cluster (label '0') acts as a default or negative class, and the goal is to identify members of other specific classes. The paper could benefit from a discussion on how this approach would be adapted to scenarios with `k` equally important, symmetric clusters where no natural default class exists.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel and powerful theoretical framework (locally encodable source coding) to the problem of query-based clustering. It proposes a new type of query (AND query) with strong theoretical and intuitive justification, backed by a significant algorithmic result (`O(n log(k/δ))` queries for noisy, approximate recovery) that improves upon prior art. The core ideas are original and likely to inspire follow-up work.

*   **Con-acceptance:** The technical presentation could be clearer, with several key details and proofs deferred, making a full assessment of the technical contributions difficult from the main paper alone. The experimental validation is preliminary and does not fully compare the proposed method against the baseline on the collected real-world data. The practical applicability of the main algorithm depends on knowing the noise rate `q`, a sensitivity that is not analyzed.

Overall, the paper makes a strong and original contribution to the field. The introduction of the source coding perspective and the analysis of AND queries are significant. The weaknesses are primarily in the completeness of the presentation and the experimental section, which do not undermine the core conceptual and theoretical results.
