This paper explores semi-supervised clustering using a novel connection to locally encodable source coding, introducing AND-queries as a potentially more efficient alternative to standard ‘same cluster’ queries. While the core ideas are interesting and the attempt to frame the problem within an information-theoretic context is commendable, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws – in presentation, technical detail, and justification – that collectively detract from its overall impact. I find myself leaning towards rejection despite acknowledging the potential novelty of some aspects.

**Summary of Main Ideas:** The authors propose viewing crowdsourced clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem. They argue that AND-queries, instead of XOR (same cluster) queries, can achieve better performance, especially in noisy settings. They present theoretical bounds and some experimental validation on real datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Connection:** The framing of crowdsourced clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem is a novel perspective.
*   **AND-Query Proposal:**  The suggestion to explore AND-queries as an alternative to XOR queries is interesting and potentially valuable, particularly in scenarios with noisy oracle responses.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The authors attempt a formal theoretical analysis, providing lower bounds and establishing performance guarantees.
*   **Experimental Validation:**  The inclusion of experiments with real-world data (albeit referenced, not fully detailed within the paper) lends some credibility to their claims.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Clarity & Organization:** The introduction is overly verbose and lacks a clear, concise statement of the problem and contributions. Several sentences are awkwardly phrased and difficult to follow. The flow between sections feels disjointed. For example, the motivation for non-adaptive querying isn't strongly linked to the subsequent technical development.
*   **Technical Imprecision:** Numerous technical aspects require greater rigor. The definition of locally encodable source coding isn't entirely precise. The connection to smooth encoding is mentioned but not thoroughly elaborated. The justifications for assumptions (e.g., independence of query errors) are superficial.
*   **Insufficient Detail in Proofs:**  The proofs presented are often incomplete or lack sufficient justification. For example, Theorem 1’s proof relies on an “expurgation” argument without providing enough detail to assess its validity. The conditions for perfect recovery are vaguely stated ("vanishing fraction"). The crucial step regarding the 'bad event' and its probability is asserted without sufficient explanation.
*   **Overstated Claims & Lack of Critical Self-Assessment:** The authors claim a "strictly better" lower bound in Theorem 2 without demonstrating its advantage across a meaningful range of parameters.  The discussion of the Massey result seems dismissive; a more nuanced explanation of its limitations would be beneficial. The claim that majority voting *increases* errors (citing [27]) needs stronger justification; this contradicts standard wisdom.
*   **Experimental Setup Details are Insufficient:** The experiments are mentioned, and a link provided, but critical details regarding data preprocessing, parameter tuning, baseline comparisons, and statistical significance are missing.  Saying the experimental results “validate” the theoretical results is a stretch given the lack of detail. The mention of a d-regular graph construction is vague and should be elaborated.
*   **Formatting and Typographical Errors:** The paper contains numerous minor formatting issues, including inconsistent use of notation, missing punctuation, and typographical errors throughout. (e.g. ‘n−1’ instead of 'n-1').  The reference list is poorly formatted.
*   **Redundancy and Repetition:**  Certain ideas (e.g., the benefits of AND queries) are repeated multiple times throughout the paper without adding substantial new insight.
*   **Inconsistent Terminology:** The interchangeable use of “labeling” and “clustering” is confusing, despite the initial attempt to differentiate them.
*   **Missing Related Work:** There is no discussion of recent advances in active learning for clustering, which are highly relevant.
*   **Poor citation formatting**: Some references are missing information.

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Novel framing of the problem.
*   Interesting proposal of AND-queries.
*   Attempt at theoretical grounding.

**Con:**

*   Lack of clarity and rigor in technical presentation.
*   Insufficient experimental detail and validation.
*   Numerous minor flaws in writing and formatting.
*   Overstated claims and insufficient self-criticism.
*   Lack of thorough comparison with existing literature.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the core ideas presented in this paper have potential, the execution is significantly flawed. The lack of clarity, technical imprecision, and insufficient detail in both proofs and experiments render the contributions difficult to fully assess and appreciate. The numerous minor errors further detract from the paper's credibility. Although the connection to locally encodable source coding is interesting, the paper doesn’t fully capitalize on it, and the theoretical results, while present, lack the polish and depth expected for NIPS.  I recommend **rejection** at this time. The authors are encouraged to address the aforementioned weaknesses and resubmit to a future conference. A major revision addressing clarity, rigor, and experimental validation is essential.
