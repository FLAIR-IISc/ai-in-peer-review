**Summary:**
This paper addresses the problem of non-adaptive querying for semi-supervised clustering. The central contributions are twofold. First, it introduces a novel information-theoretic framework, viewing the problem as "locally encodable source coding," where each query answer (an encoded bit) depends on a small, constant number of element labels (source bits). This framing allows the authors to derive new upper and lower bounds on the required number of queries for exact recovery. Second, and more significantly, the paper argues that the standard "same cluster" (XOR-type) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery. It proposes an alternative, non-linear "AND query" and provides theoretical and experimental evidence for its superiority, particularly in noisy settings. The analysis spans three cases: noiseless exact recovery, noiseless approximate recovery, and noisy approximate recovery, culminating in an algorithm for the latter that achieves `(1-δ)` recovery with `O(n log(k/δ))` queries.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novel Theoretical Framework:** The connection drawn between non-adaptive querying and locally encodable source coding is insightful and original. This provides a principled, information-theoretic lens through which to analyze the problem, leading to new lower bounds that are not immediately obvious from a purely algorithmic perspective.
2.  **Introduction of AND Queries:** The paper makes a compelling case for moving beyond linear queries (like "same-cluster"). The proposal and analysis of AND queries, especially in the context of approximate and noisy recovery, is a significant contribution that could influence future work in crowdsourced clustering and active learning.
3.  **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper systematically addresses multiple facets of the problem, from exact to approximate recovery and from noiseless to noisy oracles. The main algorithmic result for the noisy, approximate case (Theorem 5) is particularly relevant for practical crowdsourcing applications.
4.  **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of a real-world crowdsourcing experiment, though small in scale, provides valuable validation for the theoretical claims, particularly the assertion that AND queries may be less error-prone for human workers than "same-cluster" queries.

**Weaknesses:**

**Clarity:** The paper suffers from a significant number of clarity issues, typos, and presentational flaws that detract from the quality of the work and make it difficult to verify.
1.  **Formatting:** The conference information ("st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)...") is incorrectly placed at the bottom of the first page. This suggests a lack of careful preparation.
2.  **Vague Justifications:** The dismissal of query repetition in Footnote 2 as "theoretically not interesting" is a subjective and overly strong claim. The trade-offs involved in repeated querying under a budget are a valid and interesting area of study.
3.  **Inscrutable Proof Sketches:** The proofs in the main body are exceedingly brief to the point of being cryptic.
    -   In the proof of Theorem 1, the variable `epsilon` (` `) appears without definition, rendering the argument for expurgation incomprehensible.
    -   The proofs of Theorems 1 and 2 make large logical leaps (e.g., "Therefore we must have...") that require the reader to fill in significant gaps in the information-theoretic arguments.
    -   The final expression for `δ` in Theorem 4 is presented without any intuition, making it hard to appreciate the result. The use of a Poisson approximation for the degree distribution should be more formally justified.
4.  **Ambiguous Algorithm:** Algorithm 1 is not well-defined.
    -   The threshold `dCie` contains an undefined variable `e`. This is likely a typo for `d*C_i`, but such a critical error undermines confidence in the result.
    -   The algorithm description should clarify if the noise probability `q` is assumed to be known, as it is used in defining the thresholds `C_i`.
    -   The `break` statement implies a preferential ordering for assigning labels. This should be explicitly discussed.
5.  **Confusing Experimental Description:** The text mentions results on a synthetic dataset in "Figure 6," but no such figure is present in the paper. The description of the graph construction for the real-world experiment (permuted circular list) is non-standard, and it is not clear if the theoretical analysis, which likely assumes a random graph, still holds.

**Quality:** The technical core of the paper appears sound, but several claims are not sufficiently supported.
1.  **Weak Comparative Analysis:** The claim that "same cluster" queries are poor for noisy recovery because of a lack of a gap in expectations is stated without a formal proof, weakening the argument for the superiority of AND queries. The experimental comparison uses spectral clustering as a decoder for "same cluster" queries; it's not established whether this is a fair or optimal choice for comparison against the specialized decoder for AND queries.
2.  **Overstated Experimental Claims:** The observation that workers made fewer errors on AND queries (105 vs. 234) is based on a very small experiment with 10 volunteers. While interesting, this is anecdotal evidence and should be presented with more caution rather than as a definitive finding.

**Originality:** The core ideas are original. However, the paper could do a better job of positioning its contribution. The notion of locally encodable source coding is admitted to have appeared in [23] (as robust compression), and a clearer delineation of what is new in this paper's information-theoretic results would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and promising theoretical framework connecting clustering queries to locally encodable codes.
*   The proposal of AND queries is a significant conceptual contribution, with strong theoretical results showing their advantage in the important noisy, approximate recovery setting.
*   The problem is well-motivated, and the results have clear implications for the design of practical crowdsourcing systems.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The manuscript is marred by numerous typos, formatting errors, and clarity issues, indicating a rushed preparation.
*   Key proof sketches in the main body are difficult to follow, and critical components of Algorithm 1 are ambiguously defined.
*   The experimental validation and comparisons to baseline methods are not as rigorous as they could be, with some unsupported claims and confusing presentation (e.g., the missing Figure 6).
*   The accumulation of many minor flaws significantly undermines the overall quality and trustworthiness of this otherwise interesting work.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper presents novel ideas of considerable potential significance, its current execution is not up to the standard expected for NIPS. The large number of minor flaws—ranging from typos in equations and formatting errors to unclear proof sketches and weak experimental comparisons—collectively constitute a major weakness. These issues prevent a confident assessment of the work's technical correctness and overall contribution. I therefore recommend **rejection**. However, I believe the core contributions are strong, and I would strongly encourage the authors to undertake a thorough revision and resubmit to a future conference. The paper has the potential to be excellent if the presentation and rigor are substantially improved.
