This paper investigates semi-supervised clustering via querying an oracle (e.g., crowd workers) with a focus on minimizing the number of queries. The authors frame this problem as a locally encodable source coding problem and derive information-theoretic lower bounds on query complexity.  A key contribution is demonstrating that AND queries can outperform “same cluster” (XOR) queries for approximate recovery, even with noisy oracle responses.  They present a querying scheme leveraging AND queries and validate it through experiments on real datasets. This work builds on a surge of recent interest in crowdsourcing for entity resolution and clustering (e.g., [33, 30, 8, 20]) and draws connections to information theory concepts like locally encodable and decodable codes ([23, 19]).

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Framing:**  The connection between querying for clustering and locally encodable source coding is a significant and insightful contribution. This allows the authors to leverage tools from information theory to derive fundamental limits.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper contains a strong theoretical foundation, with clear definitions, theorems, and proofs (though some are deferred to the supplementary material). The lower bounds established are valuable.
* **AND Query Insight:** Demonstrating that AND queries can be superior to XOR queries is a non-trivial and potentially impactful finding. This challenges a common assumption in the field and opens up new avenues for query design.
* **Noise Model & Algorithm:** The consideration of noisy oracle responses and the development of an algorithm robust to such noise are pragmatic and address a realistic scenario in crowdsourcing applications.
* **Experimental Validation:**  Including a real-world experiment with Amazon Mechanical Turk adds credibility to the theoretical results and provides empirical evidence supporting the proposed AND query approach.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of relevant literature, including connections to information theory, crowdsourcing, and clustering.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Organization:** While technically sound, the paper is dense and occasionally difficult to follow. The introduction is lengthy, and the flow between sections could be improved.  More intuitive explanations of key concepts (e.g., locally encodable source coding) would benefit a broader audience.
* **Theorem Presentation:** Several theorems are stated without sufficient context or motivation. It's often unclear *why* a particular theorem is important or how it relates to the overall objective.
* **Experimental Detail:** The experimental setup lacks some detail.  A more thorough description of the dataset, the design of the Mechanical Turk surveys, and the criteria used for evaluating the results would strengthen the empirical validation.  The choice of d=10 and justification for it is not completely clear.  The results presented in Figure 5 seem a bit superficial; a more detailed comparison of different querying strategies would be helpful.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of independent errors in the noisy query model, while standard, might not always hold in practice. The authors acknowledge this but could elaborate on potential scenarios where this assumption is violated and the implications for the algorithm's performance.
* **Practicality of AND Queries:** While theoretically superior, the practical benefit of AND queries needs more discussion. Are they significantly more difficult for crowd workers to understand or answer accurately? The paper briefly mentions this but doesn’t offer a detailed analysis.
* **Supplementary Material Reliance:** Deferring proofs to supplementary material is acceptable, but crucial derivations should be summarized or highlighted within the main paper to aid understanding.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in semi-supervised clustering with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* The insights regarding AND queries are potentially impactful and could lead to more efficient crowdsourcing strategies.
* The experimental validation provides support for the theoretical findings.
* The paper contributes to the intersection of information theory and crowdsourcing, opening up new research directions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The paper’s density and lack of clarity might make it inaccessible to some readers.
* The experimental validation could be more thorough and provide stronger evidence of the practical benefits of AND queries.
* The reliance on supplementary material for key proofs hinders a complete understanding of the technical contributions.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. The framing of the problem as a locally encodable source coding problem is clever, and the analysis of AND queries is particularly insightful. However, the paper's density and occasional lack of clarity detract from its overall impact.  I believe the paper has merit and could be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, *but* would benefit significantly from revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.

**Recommendation:**  **Weak Accept**.  I recommend acceptance contingent on addressing the clarity and presentation issues, providing more detailed experimental results, and ensuring that key derivations are adequately summarized within the main paper.  Specifically, the authors should consider:

1.  **Rewriting the introduction** to be more concise and focused.
2.  **Adding more intuitive explanations** of key concepts.
3.  **Providing more context and motivation** for the theorems.
4.  **Expanding the experimental section** with more details and analysis.
5.  **Discussing the practical implications** of using AND queries in more detail.
6. **Summarizing the key steps of the proofs** in the main text.
