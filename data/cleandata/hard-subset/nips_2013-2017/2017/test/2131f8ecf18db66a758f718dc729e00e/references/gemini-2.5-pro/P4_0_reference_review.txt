This paper addresses the problem of semi-supervised clustering, where the goal is to recover the ground-truth labels of `n` elements by making a minimum number of non-adaptive queries to an oracle. The central contribution is to frame this problem within the information-theoretic language of locally encodable source coding, where each query (an encoded bit) can only depend on a small, constant number (`∆`) of labels (source bits). This perspective allows the authors to derive new information-theoretic lower bounds and develop near-optimal querying schemes. The paper investigates three settings: (1) noiseless queries for exact label recovery, (2) noiseless queries for approximate recovery, and (3) noisy queries for approximate recovery. A key idea proposed is the use of "AND" queries as an alternative to the more standard "same cluster" (or XOR) queries, which are shown to be more efficient, particularly for approximate recovery in noisy settings. This work builds upon the literature of active clustering and crowdsourcing [4, 21, 33] but distinguishes itself by its novel information-theoretic formulation, drawing connections to concepts like locally decodable codes [19, 16] and smooth compression [23].

**Quality and Technical Soundness**

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs that leverage tools from information theory, random graph theory, and probability.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The derivation of upper and lower bounds for the noiseless, exact recovery case (Theorems 1 and 2) is sound. The use of a random coding argument with expurgation is a standard but well-executed technique to establish the achievability bound, which interestingly shows that fewer than `n` queries can suffice when priors are known.
    *   The analysis for the noisy, approximate recovery setting (Theorem 5) is the paper's main algorithmic contribution and appears solid. The proposed algorithm is simple and intuitive, exploiting the statistical gap in expected query responses. The use of Chernoff bounds to analyze the probability of error for a single element and a concentration inequality to extend this to the entire set is appropriate.
    *   The generalization from known priors `p` to known cluster sizes `n_i` (Theorem 6) by introducing a random permutation is a thoughtful addition that enhances the practical applicability of the algorithm.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The proof sketches in the main text are at times overly condensed. For instance, the dependency argument in the proof of Theorem 5, which uses a result from Alon and Spencer [3], is stated without a clear definition of the dependency graph or a rigorous justification for the bound on `∆*`. While space is limited, a more detailed explanation of this crucial step would improve confidence in the result.
    *   The justification for why 'same cluster' queries are suboptimal in the noisy setting could be stronger. The paper claims there is "no gap between the expected numbers," which appears to be true only if the prior label distribution is uniform. If priors are non-uniform, a gap does exist, and a more nuanced argument or experimental comparison would be needed to establish the superiority of AND queries in that regime.
    *   The proof of Theorem 4 relies on a Poisson approximation for the degree of a node in the random query graph. This is a standard and reasonable approximation but should be explicitly stated as such to maintain full technical precision.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the problem is formally defined, and the contributions are explicitly listed.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The introduction successfully situates the work within the broader context of clustering, crowdsourcing, and information theory.
    *   The information-theoretic view is explained clearly, making the novel connection accessible to readers who may not be experts in coding theory.
    *   Algorithm 1 is presented in a straightforward manner, making it easy to understand and implement.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   As noted above, some of the proof sketches are too brief, forcing the reader to either take claims on faith or consult the appendix. For example, the threshold `Ci` in Algorithm 1 appears without derivation, though its form suggests it is chosen to lie between the expected values under different true labels. A brief sentence explaining its origin would be helpful.
    *   The paper refers to several figures (e.g., Figures 1-7) that are crucial for understanding the performance comparisons between different query types and bounds. The review is conducted without access to these figures, which limits the assessment of the experimental results and the visualization of theoretical trade-offs.

**Originality**

The paper is highly original. While query-based clustering is an active area, the specific approach and several of the results are novel.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The primary novelty is the formulation of the problem as locally encodable source coding. This is a fresh and powerful perspective that generates new theoretical insights and connects two previously disparate fields.
    *   The proposal of the AND query is a simple but clever and non-obvious contribution. The insight that an asymmetric query can be more robust to noise and more efficient for approximate recovery than the symmetric 'same cluster' query is a significant conceptual advance.
    *   The information-theoretic lower bounds for locally constrained queries (Theorems 2 and 3) are new contributions to both the clustering and coding theory literature.

**Significance**

The results presented in this paper are significant for both researchers and practitioners.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The `O(n log(k/δ))` query complexity for noisy, approximate recovery is a substantial improvement over the `O(n log n)` complexity of prior work [21]. This is particularly important for large-scale crowdsourcing applications where minimizing queries is critical and some error is tolerable.
    *   The paper provides a strong theoretical argument for moving beyond the standard 'same cluster' query. The observation that AND queries may also be cognitively easier for human workers (as suggested by the pilot experiment) is a valuable practical insight that could influence the design of future crowdsourcing systems.
    *   The work opens up a new research direction by building a bridge between active learning/clustering and local coding theory, which could inspire future work in both areas.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The experimental validation, while a welcome addition to a primarily theoretical paper, is conducted on a small scale (10 volunteers, 200 items). While the results support the theory, a larger and more controlled study would be needed to make definitive claims about the practical performance of the algorithm and the human error rates on different query types.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Introduces a highly novel and insightful connection between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding.
2.  Provides significant theoretical improvements, most notably reducing the query complexity for noisy approximate recovery from `O(n log n)` to `O(n log(k/δ))`.
3.  Proposes the "AND query," a simple, practical, and effective alternative to the standard "same cluster" query.
4.  The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a problem of interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The experimental validation is preliminary and does not provide conclusive real-world evidence.
2.  Some theoretical arguments in the main body are condensed, and a key claim regarding the sub-optimality of 'same cluster' queries in the noisy case requires further clarification.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novelty and theoretical significance, far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It makes a substantial contribution to the understanding of query-based learning and is likely to have a lasting impact on the field.
