This paper investigates the problem of semi-supervised clustering through a novel lens – locally encodable source coding. The authors explore querying schemes, focusing on non-adaptive approaches, to minimize the number of queries needed to recover labels when using a crowdsourced oracle. They propose using AND queries as an alternative to traditional ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries, demonstrating both theoretical and empirical benefits, particularly in noisy environments. The work is well-motivated, connecting crowdsourcing to information theory and establishing bounds on query complexity.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Perspective:** Framing the problem as locally encodable source coding offers a powerful theoretical framework and opens up new avenues for analysis.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides solid theoretical foundations, including lower bounds and a clear analysis of the proposed AND-query scheme. The theorems and proofs appear carefully constructed.
* **Practical Relevance:** The focus on crowdsourcing and noisy oracle responses is highly relevant to real-world applications. The experimental validation using Amazon Mechanical Turk adds credibility.
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The problem setup is clearly defined, and the assumptions (e.g., independent query errors) are explicitly stated.
* **Comparison to Existing Work:** The paper effectively situates its contributions within the broader contexts of clustering, crowdsourcing, and information theory, referencing relevant literature.
* **AND Query Insight:** The demonstration that AND queries can outperform XOR queries, even with noisy responses, is a significant finding.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Proofs:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, the proofs are dense and potentially challenging for readers unfamiliar with information theory. More intuition or intermediate steps could aid understanding. The deferred proofs in the appendix are a significant barrier to full comprehension.
* **Experimental Setup Detail:** The experimental section lacks some details regarding the specific experimental setup. For example, the selection of action and romantic movies, the instructions given to the crowdworkers, and the specific parameters used for the algorithms could be more thoroughly described.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:**  The experiments primarily focus on validating the AND-query scheme.  Exploring the performance of the proposed algorithms with different datasets or query configurations would strengthen the empirical evaluation. A more direct comparison to other state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods for entity resolution would be valuable.
* **Discussion of Practical Challenges:** While the paper acknowledges the assumption of independent query errors, a deeper discussion of the limitations of this assumption in real-world scenarios and potential mitigation strategies would be beneficial.
* **Notation and Readability:** While generally clear, the paper occasionally uses dense notation that can hinder readability.  Simplifying some equations or providing more explanatory text would improve clarity.  The frequent referencing of sections and theorems can feel disruptive to the flow of reading.



**Originality:**

The combination of locally encodable source coding, the exploration of AND queries, and the analysis of noisy crowdsourcing environments is novel.  While the individual concepts (crowdsourcing, source coding) are well-established, the paper’s integration of these ideas and its resulting theoretical contributions are significant.  The comparison to locally decodable codes and smooth compression demonstrates an awareness of related work.

**Significance:**

The results have the potential to significantly impact the design of efficient and robust crowdsourcing algorithms for clustering and entity resolution. The theoretical bounds provide a fundamental understanding of the limits of query complexity, and the proposed AND-query scheme offers a practical improvement over existing methods.  The connection to locally encodable source coding could inspire further research in this area.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to semi-supervised clustering with crowdsourcing.
* The introduction of AND queries offers a practical improvement over traditional XOR queries, particularly in noisy environments.
* The experimental validation provides evidence of the algorithm’s effectiveness.
* The work addresses a relevant and important problem with potential real-world impact.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the proofs and the deferred presentation of some details may limit accessibility.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive and provide a more direct comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
* The assumptions made about query independence, while acknowledged, may not always hold in practice.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. While the proofs are dense and the experimental evaluation could be expanded, the novelty of the approach, the rigorous analysis, and the potential impact on crowdsourcing algorithms make it a valuable addition to the NIPS program.  I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors consider addressing the weaknesses outlined above in a revised version. Specifically, providing more intuitive explanations of the proofs and expanding the experimental evaluation would greatly enhance the paper’s impact.



**Rating:** Accept (with minor revisions)
