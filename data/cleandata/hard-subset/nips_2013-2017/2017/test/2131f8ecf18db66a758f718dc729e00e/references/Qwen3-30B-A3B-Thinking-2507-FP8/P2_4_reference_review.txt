

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

## Summary

This paper addresses the problem of minimizing query complexity in clustering/crowdsourcing applications by modeling it as a locally encodable source coding problem in information theory. The authors introduce AND queries as a superior alternative to traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery of labels. The paper provides theoretical bounds on query complexity for both noiseless and noisy settings, demonstrating that AND queries require significantly fewer queries than XOR queries for approximate recovery. The authors validate their theoretical results with a real crowdsourcing experiment where AND queries yielded fewer errors than "same cluster" queries.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors establish tight information-theoretic lower bounds for query complexity and provide upper bounds for various query types. The proofs are well-structured, with clear mathematical derivations that support their claims. The authors carefully distinguish between exact and approximate recovery scenarios, which is crucial for understanding the problem's nuances.

The paper's experimental validation adds significant strength to the theoretical claims. The real-world crowdsourcing experiment with 100 action and 100 romantic movies demonstrates that AND queries yield fewer errors (105 vs. 234) than "same cluster" queries, validating their theoretical superiority. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the assumption that prior probabilities are known, and provide a reasonable justification for this assumption (sub-sampling a small subset).

The paper's theoretical results are well-supported and the mathematical framework is sound. The authors correctly cite related work in information theory (locally decodable codes) and crowdsourcing, making clear distinctions between their contributions and prior work.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly establishes the problem context, motivation, and contributions. The technical sections flow logically from the simple k=2 case to the more general k>2 case. The authors use clear mathematical notation throughout, and the figures (particularly Figures 1-4) effectively illustrate the key theoretical results.

The paper is self-contained in its problem setup and theoretical framework, providing sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the results. The distinction between exact and approximate recovery is well-explained, and the authors carefully define key terms like "(1-δ)-good" querying schemes.

## Originality

This paper makes several significant original contributions:

1. The novel conceptual framework of "locally encodable source coding" for query-based clustering problems, which connects information theory to crowdsourcing applications.

2. The introduction and theoretical analysis of AND queries as a superior alternative to XOR ("same cluster") queries for approximate recovery. This is particularly noteworthy because the paper shows that XOR queries are fundamentally limited for approximate recovery (Theorem 3 and Conjecture 1), while AND queries can achieve substantially better performance.

3. The theoretical results showing that AND queries require O(n log(k/δ)) queries for (1-δ)-good recovery in noisy settings, compared to the O(n log n) complexity of previous methods.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work in clustering with queries (e.g., [4, 8, 21, 33]) by demonstrating the theoretical superiority of AND queries. The connection to information theory concepts (locally encodable source coding) is novel and well-executed.

## Significance

The problem addressed (minimizing query complexity in clustering/crowdsourcing) is highly significant for practical applications where query costs are high. The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution by showing that AND queries can reduce query complexity from O(n log n) to O(n log(1/δ)) for approximate recovery, which is a significant improvement.

The significance is further enhanced by the real-world validation, which demonstrates that AND queries are not only theoretically superior but also more practical for human workers (as evidenced by lower error rates in the crowdsourcing experiment). The paper advances the state of the art in query-based clustering by providing the first theoretical analysis showing that AND queries outperform XOR queries for approximate recovery.

The results are likely to be of interest to practitioners in crowdsourcing and to researchers in information theory, clustering, and machine learning. The framework could be extended to other query-based learning problems beyond clustering.

## Strengths

- Novel theoretical framework connecting query-based clustering to locally encodable source coding
- Strong theoretical results with tight bounds for both noiseless and noisy settings
- Clear demonstration that AND queries significantly outperform XOR ("same cluster") queries for approximate recovery
- Well-executed real-world validation experiment with human workers
- Comprehensive comparison with prior work and clear distinction of contributions
- Theoretical results that are practically relevant for crowdsourcing applications

## Weaknesses

- The assumption that the prior distribution p is known might be limiting in some applications, though the authors provide a reasonable justification for this assumption
- The paper focuses primarily on the k=2 case, with extensions to k>2 being somewhat secondary (though the authors do provide results for general k)
- Some of the theoretical results (like Theorem 3) are quite complex and might be difficult to follow for non-experts
- The real experiment is on a relatively small dataset (200 movies), though it's sufficient to validate the main claim

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of query-based clustering and crowdsourcing. The novel framework of locally encodable source coding, the introduction of AND queries, and the rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating their superiority over traditional approaches are significant advances. The real-world validation further strengthens the paper's claims.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem with practical implications. It advances the state of the art in query-based clustering by providing the first theoretical analysis showing that AND queries can significantly reduce query complexity for approximate recovery.

I recommend acceptance. The paper's theoretical contributions are substantial and the validation experiment provides compelling evidence for the practical relevance of the results. The framework and results are likely to inspire future work in query-based learning and crowdsourcing applications.

The authors have made a clear and significant contribution that advances our understanding of query complexity in clustering problems, and the work is well-suited for publication at NIPS.
