This paper tackles the problem of semisupervised clustering via querying an oracle (e.g., crowd workers) with binary answers, framing it within the context of locally encodable source coding. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries aren't necessarily optimal and proposing AND queries for improved performance, particularly in the presence of noisy oracle responses. The paper provides both theoretical analysis – including lower bounds and algorithmic schemes – and empirical validation using Amazon Mechanical Turk.

The paper is well-motivated, connecting the clustering problem to information-theoretic concepts like source coding, and particularly, locally encodable codes, a relatively unexplored area. The exploration of AND queries as an alternative to XOR queries is a novel and insightful idea. The theoretical results, particularly the lower bounds and algorithmic guarantees for approximate recovery with noisy queries, are significant. The experimental validation, albeit limited, provides encouraging evidence supporting the theoretical findings. The connection to real-world scenarios like crowdsourcing and entity resolution adds practical relevance.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The writing is dense and at times difficult to follow. The introduction, while establishing the context, feels overly lengthy and repetitive. Many sentences are complex and could benefit from simplification. While the paper meticulously cites related work, the connections to existing literature on active learning and query selection are surprisingly weak.  Existing active learning techniques often involve more sophisticated query strategies than simple pairwise comparisons. The authors acknowledge the connection to source/channel coding, but the treatment feels somewhat superficial; a deeper dive into relevant coding theory results could strengthen the analysis. 

The experimental section is relatively weak. The chosen dataset and experimental setup are not fully detailed, making it hard to assess the generalizability of the results.  Comparing the AND query performance solely against a baseline of *not* using the improved query strategy (spectral clustering on all queries) isn’t very compelling.  A comparison against existing active learning methods or other query strategies tailored for clustering would be much more informative. The paper states that repeated queries are disallowed "because it is theoretically not interesting," which feels like a somewhat arbitrary constraint. A discussion on the potential benefits of repetition, even if theoretically less compelling, would be valuable.

The theoretical analysis, while thorough, relies heavily on asymptotic results and relies on many inequalities that require further justification. The proof sketches lack detail, and the reader is often directed to the supplementary material. While acceptable to a degree, it hinders the immediate understanding of the core contributions. The scaling of the algorithm in terms of *k* (number of clusters) is also not clearly addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel framing of the clustering problem as a locally encodable source coding problem.
*   Insightful proposal of AND queries as an alternative to XOR queries.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees for approximate recovery, especially in the noisy oracle setting.
*   Empirical validation, albeit limited, supports the theoretical claims.
*   Relevance to real-world applications like crowdsourcing and entity resolution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Dense and difficult-to-follow writing style.
*   Limited experimental evaluation and weak baselines.
*   Missing connections to relevant literature in active learning and query selection.
*   Superficial treatment of the source/channel coding connection.
*   Lack of detailed proof sketches and reliance on supplementary material.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the area of semisupervised clustering. However, its impact is limited by the writing style, weak experimental evaluation, and lack of connections to related fields.  I believe the paper is currently **borderline reject**. 

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

*   **Rewrite for Clarity:**  Simplify the writing style and provide more intuitive explanations. Break down complex arguments into smaller, more manageable steps.
*   **Strengthen Experimental Evaluation:**  Conduct more comprehensive experiments with diverse datasets and compare against relevant baselines (e.g., active learning algorithms). Provide a detailed description of the experimental setup.
*   **Expand Related Work:**  Thoroughly discuss connections to existing work in active learning, query selection, and coding theory.
*   **Provide More Detailed Proofs:** Include more complete proof sketches in the main body of the paper.
*   **Address Scalability:**  Discuss the algorithm's performance as the number of clusters (*k*) increases.
*   **Reconsider the Repetition Constraint:** Justify the disallowance of repeated queries more convincingly or explore the potential benefits of repetition.
