

# Peer Review: "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel perspective on clustering and label learning problems by framing them as locally encodable source coding problems. The authors propose using AND queries instead of the conventional "same cluster" (XOR) queries, demonstrating that AND queries can significantly reduce the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery in noiseless and noisy settings. The paper makes several theoretical contributions: (1) new lower bounds for exact recovery with two clusters, (2) a demonstration that XOR queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery, and (3) a main algorithmic result for noisy approximate recovery with k clusters using AND queries. The theoretical results are validated through a real crowdsourcing experiment.

The work connects to previous NIPS work on clustering with queries (e.g., [4, 8, 21, 22, 30, 33]) but offers a fundamentally different theoretical framework by viewing the problem through the lens of locally encodable source coding, a concept not well-studied in information theory. The paper also relates to recent work on crowdsourcing for entity resolution [31, 34].

## Strengths

1. **Novel Perspective and Theoretical Contributions**: The paper's main contribution is the insightful connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding. This perspective enables new theoretical results that advance the understanding of query complexity for clustering problems. The authors establish the first nontrivial information-theoretic results for locally encodable source coding, which is a significant theoretical contribution.

2. **Strong Theoretical Results**: The paper provides rigorous lower and upper bounds for multiple settings (noiseless exact recovery, noiseless approximate recovery, and noisy approximate recovery). The AND query scheme for approximate recovery is particularly compelling, with the authors showing it substantially outperforms XOR ("same cluster") queries across a range of parameters.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The real crowdsourcing experiment with 200 movies is well-designed and effectively validates the theoretical results. The authors show that AND queries have a significantly lower error rate (10.5%) compared to "same cluster" queries (23.4%), directly supporting their theoretical claims.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-structured, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results to experimental validation. The figures effectively illustrate the key results, particularly Figure 2 showing the performance advantage of AND queries over XOR queries.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope for k ≥ 2**: While the paper mentions that results extend to k > 2, the theoretical development primarily focuses on the k=2 case. The extension to general k, while presented in Section 3.3, lacks sufficient explanation of how the AND query construction generalizes beyond binary labels.

2. **Computational Complexity**: The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms (beyond query complexity). For practical implementation, especially with large n, the computational cost of decoding or running the algorithms could be a significant limitation that deserves mention.

3. **Crowdsourcing Experiment Limitations**: The real-world experiment uses a small dataset (200 movies) and only 10 volunteers. While the results are promising, the experiment could benefit from a larger-scale validation with more diverse participants and a more complex dataset to better demonstrate the approach's scalability.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper could better differentiate itself from very recent work on clustering with queries, particularly [22] and [21], which also address approximate recovery with noisy queries. A more detailed comparison would strengthen the novelty claim.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental validation provides empirical support. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and limitations of their approach.

**Clarity**: The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The main contributions are clearly articulated, and the mathematical notation is consistent. The paper would benefit from slightly more intuitive explanations of the locally encodable source coding concept for readers outside information theory.

**Originality**: This paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by connecting clustering to locally encodable source coding and introducing AND queries as a superior alternative to XOR queries. The theoretical results on locally encodable source coding are new to the literature, and the practical implications for crowdsourcing are significant.

**Significance**: The results have clear practical implications for reducing costs in crowdsourcing applications. The paper advances the state of the art in query complexity for clustering problems, showing that AND queries can reduce the number of queries by orders of magnitude in certain regimes. The connection to source coding theory also opens up new research directions.

## Recommendation

This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of query complexity for clustering problems. The paper presents novel theoretical insights with practical implications for crowdsourcing applications. The real-world validation provides compelling evidence for the proposed approach.

While the paper has minor limitations in scope and experimental validation, these do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the theoretical contributions. The work is well-positioned to make a meaningful impact at NIPS, both for the theoretical community interested in source coding and for practitioners working on crowdsourcing applications.

**Recommendation**: Accept

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Expand the discussion of the k ≥ 2 case in the theoretical sections to better explain the generalization of the AND query construction.
2. Add a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms.
3. Include a more detailed comparison with the most recent work on clustering with queries (e.g., [22]).
4. Consider expanding the crowdsourcing experiment to a larger dataset or more participants to better demonstrate scalability.

This paper represents a significant theoretical advance with clear practical implications. The authors have made a compelling case for the superiority of AND queries over the conventional XOR ("same cluster") queries, and their theoretical results are supported by empirical validation. The work is well-suited for NIPS and should be accepted for presentation.
