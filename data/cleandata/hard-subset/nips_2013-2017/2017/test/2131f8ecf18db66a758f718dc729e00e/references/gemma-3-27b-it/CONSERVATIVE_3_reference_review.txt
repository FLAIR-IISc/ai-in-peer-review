This paper attempts to frame a crowdsourcing problem for clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem, deriving information-theoretic lower bounds and proposing an AND-query strategy to improve upon traditional ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries. While the problem setting is relevant and potentially interesting, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling novelty, a disconnect between theoretical results and practical impact, and insufficient empirical validation given the claims made.

The core idea of viewing the querying process as a source coding problem isn’t entirely new; the connection to information theory is acknowledged, and related work exists on budget-fidelity tradeoffs (e.g., Lahouti & Hassibi, NIPS 2015). The claim of framing it specifically as *locally* encodable source coding feels largely a reinterpretation, and the discussion of its limited prior study doesn’t justify a significant contribution. The proposed AND-query strategy is the most novel aspect, but its advantages, while theoretically demonstrated, remain largely unconvincing without significantly stronger empirical evidence.

**Strengths:**

*   **Well-defined problem:** The problem of minimizing queries in a crowdsourcing setting is clearly articulated.
*   **Information-theoretic grounding:** The attempt to provide lower bounds using information theory is commendable.
*   **AND-query proposal:** The exploration of AND queries as an alternative to XOR queries is a potentially valuable direction.
*   **Real-world experiment:** The inclusion of a crowdsourcing experiment, even if limited, shows an attempt to validate the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited novelty:** The core concepts – crowdsourcing, clustering, information theory – are well-established. The contribution feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.
*   **Theoretical focus, practical disconnect:** The paper heavily emphasizes theoretical analysis, but the practical implications of the results are not fully explored. The theoretical gains from using AND queries are not convincingly translated into significant advantages in the experimental setup.
*   **Insufficient empirical validation:** The crowdsourcing experiment is limited in scope and lacks a detailed comparison with established baselines (e.g., standard spectral clustering). The reported error rates are not significantly lower with AND queries, and the justification for the observed improvement (workers finding AND queries easier) feels anecdotal. The claim in the intro about majority voting increasing errors, while cited, appears weak given the limited experimental context.
*   **Overly complex notation & presentation:** The paper is dense and uses a significant amount of mathematical notation, which can make it difficult to follow for readers not already deeply familiar with information theory. The organization could be improved to better guide the reader through the key ideas.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of independent query errors is stated, but the justification for its use, particularly in light of the observed issues with majority voting, is weak. The assumption of known prior probabilities, while acknowledged, is a strong one in many real-world scenarios.

**Specific concerns:**

*   The proof of Theorem 1 feels incomplete and relies heavily on expurgation without sufficient justification.
*   The connection between the lower bounds (Theorem 3) and the practical performance of the AND-query scheme is not clearly established.
*   The “d-regular graph” construction for the experiment is a relatively simple approach and might not be representative of more complex data distributions.
*   The discussion of “smooth compression” feels tangential and doesn't significantly contribute to the paper’s main argument.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   The paper lacks substantial novelty and its contribution is incremental.
*   The theoretical results are not convincingly demonstrated to have practical significance.
*   The experimental validation is weak and does not provide strong support for the proposed approach.
*   The paper is dense and difficult to follow for a broad audience.

**For:**

*   The problem setting is relevant and well-defined.
*   The attempt to apply information-theoretic principles is valuable.
*   The AND-query proposal is a potentially interesting direction for future research.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper tackles a relevant problem and offers some interesting theoretical insights, the limited novelty, the disconnect between theory and practice, and the weak empirical validation do not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS. The paper feels more like a preliminary exploration of an idea than a complete and convincing scientific contribution. The authors need to significantly strengthen the experimental results, provide a more compelling demonstration of the practical benefits of their approach, and improve the clarity and accessibility of the presentation. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing clustering algorithms and a more rigorous analysis of the impact of various assumptions on the results.
