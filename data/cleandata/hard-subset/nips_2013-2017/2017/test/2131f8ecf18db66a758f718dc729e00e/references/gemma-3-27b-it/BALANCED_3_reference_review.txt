This paper explores the problem of semi-supervised clustering via querying an oracle (e.g., crowdworkers) to determine relationships between elements. The core contribution is framing this as a locally encodable source coding problem, deriving information-theoretic lower bounds on query complexity, and proposing a novel querying strategy using AND queries, which outperforms traditional “same cluster” (XOR) queries in certain scenarios, especially with noisy oracles. The authors also present experimental validation on real datasets collected from Amazon Mechanical Turk.

The paper is well-motivated, connecting a practical problem (clustering with limited labeled data) to a fundamental area of information theory. The exploration of locally encodable source coding as a lens for analyzing query complexity is a novel and promising direction. The theoretical results, particularly the lower bounds and the demonstration of the advantage of AND queries, are significant. The experimental validation, while relatively simple, provides some support for the theoretical findings. The authors also carefully consider the case of noisy oracles, which is crucial for real-world applications.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Framing the problem as locally encodable source coding and using this perspective to derive bounds is a genuinely new approach.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents information-theoretic lower bounds and establishes the advantages of AND queries through careful analysis.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem of efficient clustering with limited supervision is highly relevant in many applications.
*   **Consideration of Noise:**  Addressing the noisy oracle setting is a realistic and important aspect.
*   **Clear Presentation (mostly):**  The introduction clearly lays out the problem and motivation. The main contributions are well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Accessibility:** While the core ideas are interesting, the paper is often dense and assumes a strong background in information theory. Key concepts (locally encodable source coding, smooth compression) are mentioned but not thoroughly explained for a broader NIPS audience. The numerous theorem and equation references without sufficient context can make it difficult to follow the logic.
*   **Experimental Setup:** The experimental validation is limited in scope. While it’s good to see a real-world validation, the experimental setup described is relatively basic. The results are presented without detailed analysis and comparison to other existing crowdsourcing clustering approaches beyond a mention of spectral clustering. Further experiments with more complex datasets, different query strategies, and a more comprehensive performance evaluation are needed.  The discussion of the MTurk experiments is also somewhat brief.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** While the paper cites relevant work, the connections are sometimes superficial. For example, the link to the broader field of active learning for clustering could be strengthened. More discussion of how this work compares to other active learning techniques specifically designed for clustering would be beneficial.
*   **Complexity of Algorithm 1:**  Algorithm 1, while theoretically sound, seems complex to implement and might have practical limitations in terms of scalability and performance. The paper doesn’t delve into the computational cost of this algorithm.
*   **Repetitive Notation:** The paper makes heavy use of notation, and it is hard to keep track of the definitions and the context of use for each symbol. For example, the use of 'p' for both probability and a vector of probabilities.

**Specific Suggestions:**

*   **Expand the Introduction:** Provide a more accessible explanation of locally encodable source coding and its relevance to the querying problem.
*   **Provide More Intuition:**  For the key theoretical results, add more intuitive explanations alongside the formal proofs. Explain *why* AND queries perform better in plain language.
*   **Strengthen Experiments:**  Include comparisons to baseline algorithms (e.g., standard active learning for clustering), perform ablation studies to understand the impact of different parameters, and analyze the experimental results more thoroughly. Explore different types of datasets.
*   **Address Scalability:** Discuss the scalability of the proposed algorithms, particularly Algorithm 1.
*   **Clarify Notation:** Standardize notation and define all symbols clearly. Consider using more descriptive variable names.
*   **More Contextualized Related Work:**  Expand the discussion of related work, particularly in active learning and crowdsourcing for clustering.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to semi-supervised clustering.
*   The use of locally encodable source coding provides a new perspective on the problem.
*   The analysis of AND queries offers a practical improvement over traditional querying strategies.
*   The experimental validation, while limited, supports the theoretical findings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is dense and requires a strong background in information theory, potentially limiting its accessibility to a broader NIPS audience.
*   The experimental validation is limited in scope and lacks a thorough comparison to other existing methods.
*   The complexity of Algorithm 1 and its scalability are not adequately addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents an interesting and potentially significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach and the theoretical rigor are commendable. However, the clarity and accessibility of the paper, along with the limitations of the experimental validation, are concerns.  I rate this as a **Weak Reject**.

The authors should seriously consider revising the paper to improve its clarity, expand the experimental evaluation, and address the concerns raised above. If these revisions are made, the paper would be a stronger candidate for publication in NIPS.  Specifically, I suggest a more detailed explanation of the information-theoretic concepts for the broader NIPS audience, a more comprehensive experimental evaluation comparing against relevant baselines, and a discussion of the practical considerations and limitations of the proposed algorithms.
