Summary
This paper studies non-adaptive, budgeted crowdsourced clustering/label recovery through an information-theoretic lens, introducing “locally encodable source coding,” where each query/encoded bit depends on a constant number of labels. The authors (i) derive new lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery under locality constraints, (ii) show that pairwise AND queries can be strictly better than the ubiquitous “same-cluster” (XOR) queries for approximate recovery, and (iii) propose a simple, scalable algorithm for noisy queries that achieves O(n log(k/δ)) total queries to recover a (1−δ) fraction of labels for k classes, with a real crowdsourcing experiment supporting the model and the advantage of AND queries.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to: crowdsourced clustering and query complexity (e.g., Ashtiani et al.; Mazumdar & Saha), coding-theoretic approaches to crowdsourcing, and locally decodable/smooth compression. The “locally encodable source coding” angle appears genuinely new, complementing the more-studied local decodability and smoothness. The paper leverages Massey’s classical observations about linear rate–distortion limitations to explain why XOR/same-cluster is suboptimal for approximate recovery, and provides constructive schemes with AND queries that outperform what is achievable with random XOR queries. Compared to prior noisy-query clustering works needing O(n log n) queries, the O(n log(k/δ)) guarantee (for approximate recovery) is a notable improvement under the proposed oracle.

Main contributions and claims
- Noiseless exact recovery (k=2): Upper bound showing m < n queries suffice for a range of priors using ∆-local XOR queries and ML decoding; a refined lower bound that can beat the naive nH(p) limit due to locality constraints.
- Noiseless approximate recovery (k=2): A nontrivial lower bound under ∆-locality; a case against XOR via Massey’s linear-code limits; an AND-based querying/decoding scheme with explicit error characterization outperforming XOR-based schemes over a broad regime.
- Noisy approximate recovery (general k): A pairwise query that returns i if both items belong to class i and 0 otherwise; a simple thresholding algorithm on a d-regular graph achieving (1−δ) recovery with m=O(n log(k/δ)) queries. A concentration argument ensures high-probability control of errors.
- Empirics: A small-scale real crowdsourcing experiment (binary movie genres) where AND queries exhibit notably lower worker error rates than same-cluster queries, and an additional synthetic comparison to spectral clustering on same-cluster queries.

Strengths
- Novel conceptual framing: Casting nonadaptive query design as locally encodable source coding is fresh and appealing, opening a principled way to derive locality-aware bounds. This alone is a valuable contribution.
- Clear algorithmic takeaway: AND queries are a simple, implementable primitive that can significantly reduce query complexity for approximate recovery, especially under noise.
- Theoretical depth: Multiple new bounds (exact and approximate, noiseless and noisy), with plausible proof sketches and intuitive arguments (e.g., exploiting expectation gaps in noisy AND counts).
- Practical relevance: The nonadaptive, parallelizable design, tolerance to noise without query repetition, and the finding that AND queries are easier for workers make this impactful for real crowdsourcing systems.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Clarity/notation issues: Some expressions (e.g., the definition of α in Theorem 1) appear to have typos/ambiguity (is the term raised to ∆?). There are several minor typographical errors and dense arguments left to the appendix. A careful pass would help readability.
- Oracle model realism for k>2: The k-ary pairwise AND-like oracle (“returns i if both labels are i else 0”) may be less natural or harder for crowd workers than same-cluster or majority-style questions. The paper should discuss how such responses would be operationalized for multi-class tasks and whether simpler multi-choice interfaces suffice.
- Parameter knowledge and robustness: Algorithm 1 thresholds depend on p and q; the main text mentions using ni instead of p but still relies on q. How sensitive is performance to mis-specified q, and can q be estimated on the fly?
- Empirical evaluation is limited: The real experiment is small and binary; the synthetic comparison uses a specific baseline (spectral clustering) and a graph construction that favors the proposed scheme. Stronger empirical comparisons on standard datasets and more baselines would bolster the claims.
- Conjecture reliance: The negative result for same-cluster queries references Massey’s conjecture; while true at p=0.5, broader nonlinearity lower bounds are not fully established here. That said, the positive AND results do not hinge on this.

Pro-accept arguments
- Introduces a genuinely new lens (locally encodable source coding) with concrete lower bounds and constructive schemes.
- Simple, bold idea (AND queries) that challenges community defaults (XOR/same-cluster) and is supported by both theory and a real user study.
- Strong potential impact on query design for crowdsourcing and on theoretical work at the intersection of information theory and ML.
- Nonadaptive, scalable, and robust-to-noise methodology aligns well with practical constraints.

Pro-reject arguments
- Some theoretical statements lack full clarity in the main text; a few derivations depend on appendices or conjectures.
- The k-ary oracle could be seen as less realistic than same-cluster in multi-class settings; limited empirical validation for k>2.
- Experimental section is small in scope and may not convince all reviewers of practical superiority across tasks and noise models.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify all notation and constants (e.g., α, dependence on ∆), and provide more intuition around Theorem 3.
- Discuss practical instantiations of the k-ary pairwise query for multi-class tasks; consider alternative multi-class primitives and worker UI.
- Add robustness experiments for mis-specified priors and q; describe how to estimate q from a pilot subset.
- Strengthen empirical comparisons on public datasets with additional baselines (e.g., adaptive schemes, probabilistic label aggregation methods), and report wall-clock/cost benefits from nonadaptivity.

Overall evaluation
Originality: High
Quality: Good, with some clarity issues
Significance: Moderate to high
Clarity: Moderate; could be improved

Recommendation: Accept (weak accept to accept). The paper proposes a novel and promising direction with compelling theoretical and practical implications, and I am willing to overlook the relatively light empirical section and some presentation issues given the conceptual contribution.

Reviewer confidence: Medium-high.
