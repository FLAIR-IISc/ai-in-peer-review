

# Peer Review: "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of label learning (or clustering) with minimal queries to an oracle, framing it as a locally encodable source coding problem in information theory. The authors argue that traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery and propose using "AND queries" instead. They establish theoretical bounds on query complexity for exact and approximate recovery in noiseless and noisy settings, showing that AND queries can significantly reduce the number of required queries compared to XOR queries. The paper also includes a real crowdsourcing experiment validating their theoretical results, where AND queries demonstrated lower error rates (105 errors vs. 234 for XOR queries).

The work connects to prior NIPS work on clustering with queries (e.g., Mazumdar & Saha, NIPS 2017) and information theory (e.g., locally decodable codes), but introduces the novel AND query approach and its theoretical analysis. The paper makes a clear contribution by showing that the choice of query function (AND vs. XOR) matters significantly for query efficiency.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides rigorous information-theoretic bounds on query complexity for both exact and approximate recovery, which is a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of query-based clustering. The connection to locally encodable source coding is novel and well-motivated.

2. **Novel Query Strategy**: The introduction of AND queries as a superior alternative to XOR queries for approximate recovery is the paper's most compelling contribution. The theoretical analysis (Theorems 3-5) convincingly demonstrates that AND queries require fewer queries than XOR queries for the same recovery performance.

3. **Practical Validation**: The real-world crowdsourcing experiment with movies provides valuable empirical validation of the theoretical results. The 105 vs. 234 error rates for AND vs. XOR queries is a strong practical demonstration of the approach's superiority.

4. **Clear Organization**: The paper is well-structured, with clear separation of theoretical results (Sections 3.1-3.3) and experimental validation (Section 4). The figures effectively illustrate the key theoretical results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope for k>2**: While the paper mentions extensions to k>2 clusters, the main results focus on k=2, and the theoretical analysis for general k is less developed. The noisy query results in Section 3.3 are particularly focused on k=2 (where the AND query reduces to the binary case).

2. **Practical Implementation Concerns**: The paper assumes that the prior distribution p is known, which might not be realistic in many applications. While Theorem 6 addresses this by using relative cluster sizes, the practical implementation of this approach isn't fully explored.

3. **Small-Scale Experiment**: The crowdsourcing experiment with 100 movies and 10 volunteers is small-scale. While the results are consistent with theory, a larger-scale experiment would provide stronger validation.

4. **Lack of Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper doesn't compare with the most recent work on clustering with queries (e.g., the 2020 NIPS paper "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information" by the same authors), which would strengthen the positioning of the work.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contribution is significant, the novelty of the AND query approach is compelling, and the empirical validation provides strong support for the theoretical claims. The paper makes a clear, well-supported contribution to the problem of efficient label learning with minimal queries.

The paper advances the state of the art by demonstrating that the choice of query function matters substantially for query efficiency. This insight has practical implications for crowdsourcing applications where minimizing query costs is critical. The theoretical results provide fundamental limits on query complexity for this problem, which is valuable for researchers working on similar problems.

The paper meets all the NIPS criteria:
- **Quality**: The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported by proofs. The experimental results validate the theory.
- **Clarity**: The paper is well-written and organized.
- **Originality**: The AND query approach and its theoretical analysis are novel.
- **Significance**: The results have both theoretical importance for information theory and practical implications for crowdsourcing applications.

## Final Thoughts

This paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of query-based clustering. The AND query approach is a clever insight that challenges conventional wisdom about the optimal query function for this problem. The theoretical results are elegant and the empirical validation provides strong support for the claims. I believe this paper would be of interest to both the theoretical and applied machine learning communities at NIPS.

The minor weaknesses noted (limited scope for k>2, small-scale experiment) do not detract from the paper's overall contribution and significance. With minor clarifications (particularly regarding the extension to k>2 and the practical implementation of the prior distribution), this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
