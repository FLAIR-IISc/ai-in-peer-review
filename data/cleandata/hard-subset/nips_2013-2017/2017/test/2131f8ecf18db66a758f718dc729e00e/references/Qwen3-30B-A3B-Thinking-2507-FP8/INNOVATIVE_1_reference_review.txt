

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper presents a bold and novel approach to the fundamental problem of clustering with minimal queries, by introducing AND queries as a superior alternative to the standard "same cluster" (XOR) queries used in prior work. The authors establish a theoretical foundation by framing the problem as a locally encodable source coding problem, which enables them to derive tight information-theoretic bounds and develop a new class of efficient querying algorithms.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of AND queries, which the authors demonstrate can significantly reduce the number of queries required for approximate recovery from O(n log n) to O(n log(1/δ)) for (1-δ)-good recovery. This is a substantial theoretical improvement over existing approaches. The paper also establishes the first non-trivial information-theoretic results on locally encodable source coding, a novel perspective that connects clustering problems to information theory. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for noiseless and noisy settings, with a main algorithmic result (Theorem 5) showing that AND queries can achieve (1-δ)-good recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries for general k clusters.

The paper excels in originality, which is the reviewer's highest priority. The shift from XOR to AND queries represents a significant conceptual leap, and the authors provide compelling theoretical justification for why AND queries outperform standard approaches. The connection to locally encodable source coding is fresh and well-motivated, opening up a new research direction at the intersection of information theory and clustering.

The significance of this work is substantial. The problem of minimizing query complexity in clustering is important for practical applications like crowdsourcing, where each query incurs cost. The authors validate their theoretical results with a real crowdsourcing experiment, which shows that AND queries indeed require fewer errors (105 vs. 234) compared to "same cluster" queries. This practical validation strengthens the theoretical contributions.

The quality of the paper is high. The authors provide multiple theorems with complete proofs, establish tight lower bounds, and carefully analyze the performance trade-offs. The real experiment, while modest in scale (10 volunteers, 1000 queries), provides valuable empirical validation of the theoretical results.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The figures effectively illustrate the key results, and the connection between information theory and clustering is well-explained for readers familiar with either field. The paper's focus on the k=2 case for many results is understandable, but the authors do properly generalize to k>2 in their main algorithmic result (Theorem 5).

A few minor weaknesses: The claim that "the notion of locally encodable source coding is not well-studied" might be slightly overstated, as [23] mentions "robust compression" which is related. The real-world experiment could be strengthened with a larger scale or more detailed analysis of why AND queries are easier for crowd workers. The paper also doesn't fully address how their approach compares to recent work on triangle queries [31].

However, these are minor concerns given the paper's significant novelty. The authors have made a bold conceptual shift that promises to change how we think about query-based clustering. The theoretical results are strong, the practical validation is promising, and the connection to information theory opens up new research directions.

The paper is highly original, addresses an important problem with a novel solution, and provides strong theoretical and empirical validation. It represents a significant step forward in the field of query-based clustering and would be a valuable contribution to NIPS.

**Strengths:**
- Introduction of AND queries as a fundamentally superior approach to clustering queries
- Novel theoretical framework connecting clustering to locally encodable source coding
- Strong theoretical guarantees with tight lower and upper bounds
- Practical validation via real crowdsourcing experiment
- Clear demonstration of significant improvement over existing approaches

**Weaknesses:**
- Minor overstatement about the novelty of locally encodable source coding
- Limited scale of real-world experiment (could be larger)
- Slightly less detailed explanation of why AND queries are easier for workers

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper introduces a bold new direction in clustering with significant theoretical and practical implications. The novelty and conceptual shift are compelling, and the paper provides strong theoretical and empirical evidence to support its claims. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the paper's significant contribution to the field.
