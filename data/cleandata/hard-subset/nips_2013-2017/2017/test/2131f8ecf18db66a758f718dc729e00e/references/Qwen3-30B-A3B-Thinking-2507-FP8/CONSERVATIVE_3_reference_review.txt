

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper proposes a novel approach to semisupervised clustering by introducing "AND-queries" as an alternative to the standard "same cluster" (XOR) queries commonly used in crowdsourcing-based clustering. The authors frame the problem as a locally encodable source coding problem, derive theoretical bounds on query complexity, and present a real-world experiment comparing AND queries with standard "same cluster" queries.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a theoretically interesting connection between clustering with crowdsourcing and locally encodable source coding, which is a well-defined information theory concept. The theoretical results (Theorems 1-6) are generally sound and well-structured, with proper mathematical derivations. The authors provide clear distinctions between AND queries and traditional approaches, and the information-theoretic perspective offers a novel angle on query complexity.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the theoretical soundness, the paper has several significant shortcomings that undermine its contribution to the field:

1. **Lack of substantial empirical validation:** The real-world experiment is extremely limited (only 10 volunteers, 200 movies, 1000 queries), with no statistical analysis of the results. The claim that "AND queries are easier to answer for workers" is based on a single experiment with 105 errors vs. 234 for "same cluster" queries - this is insufficient to support such a strong claim about worker performance. The paper fails to provide statistical significance testing or replicate the experiment with more participants.

2. **Overstated practical significance:** The theoretical results show asymptotic improvements (e.g., O(n log(1/δ)) vs. O(n log n)), but the paper doesn't demonstrate how these translate to practical improvements. For instance, with n=200 movies in the experiment, the difference between O(n log(1/δ)) and O(n log n) queries is negligible, yet the paper presents this as a major advancement.

3. **Inadequate comparison with established methods:** The paper doesn't compare against state-of-the-art clustering algorithms that have been specifically designed for crowdsourcing applications (e.g., [21, 30, 33]). The claim that AND queries "perform substantially better" is not substantiated by comparison with existing methods in the literature.

4. **Questionable practical relevance:** The paper claims that AND queries are "easier to answer for workers" because "for a correct 'no' answer in an AND query, a worker just need to know one of the labels in the pair," but this is not supported by the experiment. In fact, the experiment showed that "same cluster" queries had 2.2x more errors than AND queries, but this difference could be due to the specific nature of the movie classification task rather than the query type itself.

5. **Theoretical claims not sufficiently justified:** The paper claims that "AND queries are substantially better" than "same cluster" queries for approximate recovery, but the evidence for this claim is limited to a single figure (Figure 2) that shows theoretical bounds rather than empirical performance. The paper doesn't demonstrate that the AND query approach actually achieves these theoretical bounds in practice.

**Significance concerns:** The paper's most significant contribution is the AND query approach, but this is presented as a major advancement without sufficient evidence. The paper fails to demonstrate that the theoretical improvements translate to meaningful practical benefits in real crowdsourcing applications. The information-theoretic results on locally encodable source coding are interesting but not particularly novel given prior work [23], and the connection to clustering is tenuous.

**Clarity issues:** While the paper is well-organized, the connection between the information theory concepts and the practical clustering problem is not sufficiently explained. The term "locally encodable source coding" is introduced without adequate context for readers unfamiliar with this niche concept in information theory.

**Conclusion:** While the paper presents theoretically interesting results, the claimed practical significance is not supported by sufficient evidence. The main contribution - the AND query approach - is presented as a major advancement without adequate experimental validation against established methods in the field. The theoretical results are sound but lack practical relevance, and the small-scale experiment does not provide convincing evidence for the claimed improvements.

I recommend rejection. The paper would benefit from substantially more rigorous experimental validation, including larger-scale crowdsourcing experiments with statistical significance testing, and a proper comparison against state-of-the-art clustering methods in the crowdsourcing literature. Without these, the paper makes a strong theoretical claim but fails to demonstrate its practical value. The authors have made a contribution to the theoretical understanding of query complexity, but the paper does not advance the state of the art in a way that would be significant for the NIPS community.
