

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper studies the problem of clustering elements using queries to an oracle, with a focus on minimizing the number of queries required. The authors make a novel connection between this problem and locally encodable source coding in information theory, and propose AND queries as a more efficient alternative to the commonly used "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery. The paper makes three main contributions: (1) establishing theoretical bounds for exact recovery with noiseless queries, (2) demonstrating the superiority of AND queries for approximate recovery, and (3) providing a practical algorithm for noisy queries with theoretical guarantees, validated through a real crowdsourcing experiment.

**Strengths:**

The paper's main strength is the novel insight that AND queries can significantly outperform "same cluster" queries for approximate recovery, which is both theoretically interesting and practically important. The authors provide rigorous information-theoretic lower bounds and upper bounds for the query complexity, showing that AND queries can achieve (1-δ)-good recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries, while "same cluster" queries require O(n log n) queries. The theoretical results are well-supported by detailed proofs and connections to information theory.

The real-world validation through a crowdsourcing experiment is particularly strong. The authors collected 1000 queries from 10 volunteers about movie genres, showing that AND queries (e.g., "Are both movies action movies?") had significantly fewer errors (105 errors) than "same cluster" queries (234 errors). This practical validation effectively bridges the gap between theoretical results and real-world applications.

The paper also makes important distinctions between noiseless and noisy settings, and between exact and approximate recovery, providing a comprehensive theoretical framework. The extension to k > 2 clusters and the demonstration that the algorithm works without knowing the prior distribution (only requiring cluster size ratios) are valuable additions.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper's main weakness is the limited scope of the experimental validation. The crowdsourcing experiment only uses a single dataset of 200 movies (100 action, 100 romantic), which is relatively small and may not generalize to other domains. The authors should expand their experimental evaluation to include more diverse datasets or larger-scale experiments to strengthen the practical relevance of their claims.

The paper also lacks discussion of how their nonadaptive approach compares to adaptive query strategies, which have been shown to be more efficient in some clustering settings. While the authors justify focusing on nonadaptive queries for parallelizability, they should acknowledge and briefly discuss the trade-offs with adaptive methods.

The theoretical analysis for Theorem 3 (the lower bound for approximate recovery) is quite involved and lacks intuitive explanation. The authors could improve clarity by providing more intuition for why AND queries outperform "same cluster" queries, beyond just citing Massey's result.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

1. Expand the experimental validation to include multiple datasets and potentially larger-scale crowdsourcing experiments to better demonstrate the practical utility of AND queries across different domains.

2. Include a brief comparison with adaptive query strategies, discussing when nonadaptive approaches might be preferable despite potentially higher query complexity.

3. Provide more intuitive explanations for the theoretical results, particularly for Theorem 3. A simple example illustrating why AND queries are more efficient than XOR queries for approximate recovery would help readers understand the core insight.

4. Consider adding a discussion of how the approach scales with very large n (e.g., n > 10^6), which is increasingly important for real-world applications.

5. Clarify the practical implementation details for AND queries in crowdsourcing platforms. While the paper notes that AND queries are easier for workers to answer, it would be helpful to provide more guidance on how to design such queries for different types of clustering problems.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of query-based clustering. The novel connection to locally encodable source coding and the introduction of AND queries as a more efficient alternative to standard "same cluster" queries are significant theoretical advances with clear practical implications. The paper's theoretical results are well-supported by proofs, and the real-world validation provides valuable empirical evidence.

The paper's main limitations are the limited scope of the experimental validation and the lack of comparison with adaptive query strategies. However, these are relatively minor issues that could be addressed with additional experiments and discussion. The work advances the state of the art in query-based clustering and would be of interest to the NIPS community.

**Decision:**

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution with practical relevance, provides rigorous analysis, and validates its claims with a real-world experiment. The strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses, and the work would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
