Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonadaptive crowdsourced clustering/labeling with pairwise queries, under both noiseless and noisy oracles, and frames the problem as locally encodable source coding: each query answer (compressed bit/symbol) depends on only a constant number Δ of labels. This perspective enables information-theoretic lower bounds and suggests query designs beyond the commonly used “same-cluster” (XOR) queries. The main results are:
- Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2): With sparse parity (XOR) queries of arity Δ, exact recovery with high probability is achievable using m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries, α = 1/2(1+(1−4p(1−p))^Δ), which can be < n when p is biased. A new lower bound improves over nh(p) for some p.
- Noiseless, approximate recovery: A lower bound for (1−δ)-accurate schemes under local encoding; and a demonstration (using Massey’s results on linear rate–distortion codes) that XOR queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery. A simple AND-based scheme with pairwise queries and a one-bit decoder achieves strictly better tradeoffs than XOR-based schemes.
- Noisy, approximate recovery, general k: With a pairwise k-ary “AND” query that outputs i if both endpoints are label i and 0 otherwise, and a simple per-node thresholding decoder on a d-regular graph, the paper proves (1−δ)-accurate recovery with m = Θ(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive queries under an i.i.d. symmetric noise model on query answers. A small real crowdsourcing experiment (movie genres) and a synthetic comparison support the claims and suggest AND queries are easier for workers than same-cluster queries.

This connects to prior NIPS and IT work on query complexity of clustering (Ashtiani et al.), crowdsourcing aggregation (Karger-Oh-Shah et al.), entity resolution, locally decodable/smooth compression (Makhdoumi et al., Montanari–Mossel), and classical source/joint source–channel coding (Cover–Thomas; Massey).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The high-level approaches and arguments are plausible and largely standard in flavor (typical set, random sparse query matrices, union bounds/expurgation, Chernoff bounds with bounded dependency graphs). The exact-recovery achievability (XOR with ML decoding) is information-theoretic (computationally intractable in general), which is acceptable for lower-bound matching. The noisy k-ary AND scheme/decoder is simple and the exponential-in-d error decay via Chernoff is sound; converting d = Θ(log(k/δ)) to m = Θ(n log(k/δ)) is reasonable for a d-regular graph.
- Gaps/ambiguities: Some central proofs are deferred (notably the approximate-recovery lower bound, Theorem 3), and the stated expression includes unusual terms (e.g., exp(Δ h′(·))) without intuition here. The “same-cluster lower bound” they “beat” in Fig. 2 depends on a conjecture (Massey-inspired); the paper is careful to label it as a conjecture, but claims about outperforming “lower bounds” should be qualified. The achievability for exact recovery relies on ML decoding and typical-set expurgation; decoding complexity is not discussed. The noisy k-ary AND noise model and thresholds Ci are reasonable, but some algebra and constants are left implicit.
- Experiments: The real-data study is small (200 movies, 10 volunteers) but illustrates the gap between AND vs same-cluster answers (fewer errors) and the dependence on d. The synthetic comparison to spectral clustering is mentioned; more detail would help. Overall, experiments are supportive but limited.

Clarity
- Writing/notation: The paper is readable and the key ideas come through, but there are several notational/typographical issues that hinder clarity. Most notably, the number of queries for a d-regular graph should be |E|=nd/2; the manuscript repeatedly writes “nd2,” which is confusing (the experiment section reveals they mean nd/2). Logs switch bases inconsistently while h(·) is in bits. Some equations have minor typesetting errors. Figures referenced are not in the excerpt.
- Organization: The structure (noiseless exact; noiseless approximate; noisy approximate) is logical. More intuition for Theorem 3, and an explicit statement of computational costs (ML vs linear-time thresholding) would improve clarity.

Originality
- The locally encodable source coding viewpoint for query design and lower bounds appears novel and insightful relative to prior work that focused on specific query types (same-cluster) and algorithmic heuristics. The identification that AND-type queries can strictly improve approximate recovery in nonadaptive settings, and the O(n log(k/δ)) guarantee under noise with a simple decoder, are new (to my knowledge) and nontrivial.
- The exact-recovery achievability with fewer than n pairwise queries for biased priors is a nice information-theoretic observation.

Significance
- The results are likely to influence both theory (limits under local encoding constraints; design of nonadaptive querying schemes) and practice (designing crowdsourcing tasks that are easier for workers and more statistically efficient). The O(n log(k/δ)) bound under noise with a very simple scheme is compelling. The AND-vs-XOR message is practical.
- Limitations: The nonadaptive focus is appropriate, but comparisons to adaptive baselines could contextualize gains. The experimental validation is small-scale; broader evaluation (e.g., MTurk, more classes, correlated errors) would strengthen impact.

Pros
- Conceptually novel and fruitful “locally encodable source coding” lens.
- New lower bounds and achievability results; exact recovery with < n queries (for biased p) using pairwise XOR; O(n log(k/δ)) under noise with simple decoder.
- Clear insight that AND queries can dominate XOR for approximate recovery; supported by theory and a small user study.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with explicit accuracy–query tradeoff.

Cons
- Clarity/notation issues (nd/2 vs nd^2; logs; typos) and some proof details deferred; Theorem 3 lacks intuition in the main text.
- Exact-recovery achievability uses ML decoding (computationally impractical); complexity not discussed.
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scale and scope; comparison to stronger baselines (beyond spectral clustering for same-cluster) is minimal.
- Claims about beating a “lower bound” rely partly on a conjecture; the text should more carefully qualify this.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please fix the query count notation (nd/2 for a d-regular graph) throughout, and unify log bases.
- Provide more intuition for Theorem 3 in the main text and, if possible, a simpler special case bound that highlights the locality penalty.
- Discuss decoding complexity: is there a practical decoder for the XOR-based exact-recovery scheme? Can peeling-like decoders be adapted?
- For the k-ary AND scheme, comment on UI/practicality: workers must choose i∈{0,…,k−1} or 0; how does this scale with k?
- Extend experiments: larger-scale crowdsourcing, comparison with EM/Dawid–Skene-style aggregation and adaptive query selection, robustness to correlated errors/priors.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Despite presentation issues, the paper offers a fresh and significant perspective with theoretically solid and practically relevant contributions. With improved clarity and expanded empirical validation, it would be a strong addition. Confidence: medium.
