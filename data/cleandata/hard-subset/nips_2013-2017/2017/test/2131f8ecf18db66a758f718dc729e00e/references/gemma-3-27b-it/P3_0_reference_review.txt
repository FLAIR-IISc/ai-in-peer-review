## NIPS 2017 Review: Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding

**Summary:**

This paper explores the problem of semisupervised clustering (or label learning) via querying an oracle (e.g., crowd workers) with a focus on minimizing the number of queries required for accurate recovery of labels. The authors cast this problem as a locally encodable source coding problem, deriving information-theoretic lower bounds and proposing a novel querying strategy using AND queries instead of traditional “same cluster” (XOR) queries. They demonstrate, both theoretically and experimentally, that AND queries can achieve better performance, especially in noisy oracle settings. The work extends to the case of k clusters and provides algorithms for approximate recovery with provable guarantees.

**Relationship to Prior Work:**

The paper builds on a growing body of work on crowdsourcing for entity resolution and clustering (references [33, 30, 8, 20]). It links this area to the well-established fields of information theory, particularly source coding [14] and, crucially, *locally encodable* source coding (a relatively unexplored area).  The connection to locally decodable codes [19, 16, 26, 6, 25, 5, 32] is also acknowledged. Importantly, the paper addresses limitations of existing approaches based on XOR queries, referencing relevant results from Massey [17] concerning linear codes.  The work builds upon and generalizes results related to query complexity in clustering [4, 21].

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Framing:** Casting the querying problem as locally encodable source coding provides a powerful theoretical framework and allows the application of tools from information theory. This is a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides information-theoretic lower bounds and algorithms with provable performance guarantees, which are impressive.  The derivation of these bounds and the algorithmic analysis appear sound.
* **AND Query Insight:** The demonstration that AND queries can outperform XOR queries for approximate recovery is a key finding, offering a practical improvement for real-world applications.
* **Handling Noisy Oracles:**  The analysis of the noisy oracle setting is well-motivated and provides valuable insights into designing robust querying strategies.
* **Experimental Validation:**  The inclusion of a real-world crowdsourcing experiment with Amazon Mechanical Turk provides empirical support for the theoretical claims.
* **Clear Structure & Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making it relatively easy to follow despite the technical depth.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Notation & Proofs:** While rigorous, the mathematical notation and proofs can be quite dense and challenging for readers unfamiliar with information theory. More intuitive explanations or intermediate steps would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While the theoretical results are promising, the practical scalability of the proposed algorithms for very large datasets (n >> 1000) is not fully addressed.  The computational cost of constructing and querying the d-regular graph needs further consideration.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the algorithm (particularly in the noisy setting) is likely sensitive to the choice of parameters (e.g., d, thresholds). A more thorough exploration of parameter sensitivity and guidelines for setting these parameters would strengthen the work.
* **Crowdsourcing Experiment Scope:** The crowdsourcing experiment is relatively small-scale (100 movies, 10 workers). While it serves as a proof of concept, a more extensive evaluation with diverse datasets and larger worker pools would be more convincing.
* **Repetition of ideas:** There is some repetition of ideas between sections, particularly in highlighting the advantage of AND queries.



**Detailed Comments:**

* **Section 2 (Problem Setup):**  The motivation for disallowing repeated queries is reasonable, but could be stated more concisely.
* **Section 3.1 (Noiseless, Exact Recovery):** The connection to standard clustering algorithms via pairwise comparisons is a nice observation.
* **Section 3.2 (Noiseless, Approximate Recovery):** The discussion of the limitations of XOR queries and the introduction of the AND query are the strongest parts of the paper.  The Massey result [17] is appropriately cited.
* **Section 3.3 (Noisy Queries):** The extension to k clusters and the analysis of noisy queries are significant contributions.  The Algorithm 1 description is clear, but the justification for its correctness could be further elaborated.
* **Section 4 (Experiments):** The experimental setup is clearly described. The observation that majority voting can *increase* errors is noteworthy and supports the need for more sophisticated querying strategies.
* **Overall:** The paper consistently uses the term "locally encodable source coding", and while this is a valid framing, it might benefit from cross-referencing or briefly defining it each time it appears, as it’s a relatively unfamiliar concept.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to semisupervised clustering.
* The AND query strategy is a significant practical improvement over existing methods.
* The work tackles a challenging problem with a rigorous methodology.
* The experimental validation provides evidence of the proposed approach's effectiveness.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The mathematical complexity might limit accessibility for some readers.
* The scalability of the algorithms for very large datasets is not fully addressed.
* The crowdsourcing experiment is relatively small-scale.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a **strong paper** with significant contributions to the field. The novel framing, theoretical rigor, and practical insights make it a valuable addition to the NIPS program. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions). I recommend that the authors consider clarifying the mathematical notation, providing more discussion on scalability, and expanding the experimental evaluation if possible.
