## NIPS 2017 Review: Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding

**Summary:**

This paper explores the problem of label learning (and equivalently, clustering) with limited, potentially noisy, pairwise queries to an oracle (e.g., crowd workers). It frames this problem as a locally encodable source coding problem, leveraging information-theoretic tools to derive lower bounds on query complexity and propose novel querying schemes.  The key contribution lies in demonstrating that "AND" queries can outperform traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery, even in the presence of noise. The authors provide theoretical results, alongside a validation experiment using Amazon Mechanical Turk.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon recent work in crowdsourced entity resolution (e.g., [33, 30, 8, 20]), adaptive querying for clustering ([33, 30, 8, 22, 21]), and information-theoretic approaches to crowdsourcing ([14]). It specifically connects the problem to locally encodable source coding, a relatively unexplored area within information theory [23]. The work also draws on related concepts like locally decodable codes [19, 16] and smooth compression [23].  The connection to Massey’s work on rate-distortion limitations [17] is particularly insightful.  The paper appropriately cites and positions itself within this body of work.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Framing:**  The formulation of the querying problem as locally encodable source coding provides a fresh perspective and allows for the application of powerful information-theoretic tools.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents rigorous theoretical analysis, deriving both lower bounds on query complexity and upper bounds achievable with the proposed AND-querying scheme. Theorems 2, 3, 4, and 5 are particularly strong contributions.
* **Counterintuitive Result:** The demonstration that AND queries can outperform XOR queries for approximate recovery is a significant and surprising finding.  The explanation connecting this to limitations of linear codes (XOR) is compelling.
* **Robustness to Noise:** The analysis of noisy oracle answers is a crucial addition, making the results more relevant to real-world crowdsourcing scenarios.
* **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of a crowdsourcing experiment, despite its limitations (discussed below), provides empirical support for the theoretical claims.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the practical motivation of minimizing query cost in crowdsourcing applications.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Setup Details:** The description of the crowdsourcing experiment is somewhat limited. More detail on the participant pool, the interface used for presenting queries, and the quality control measures employed would strengthen the findings. The claim that workers found AND queries "easier" is asserted without robust supporting data beyond the error rate comparison.
* **Complexity of Theorems:** While rigorous, some of the theorems and their proofs (especially in Section 3.2 and 3.3) are dense and challenging to follow.  More intuitive explanations or simplified versions of the results could improve accessibility.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments focus solely on two clusters. It would be beneficial to see results for larger values of k to assess the scalability of the proposed approach.
* **Assumption of Known Prior:** The assumption of knowing the prior distribution (or relative cluster sizes) is stated as "usually easy to estimate," but the paper doesn't fully address the challenges of accurate prior estimation in practical scenarios.
* **Repetition Prohibition Justification:** While the justification for prohibiting query repetition is given, its impact on real-world usability could be further discussed. Often, repeating queries *is* feasible and can improve reliability.
* **Figures:** Figure 1 is difficult to interpret without more context.  The axes labels and the meaning of the curves are not immediately clear.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by analysis and (to a lesser extent) experimental results. The work is complete within its defined scope. The authors are careful in presenting the limitations and assumptions. (Strong)
* **Clarity:**  The paper is generally well-written, but some sections (particularly those involving the theoretical derivations) are dense and could benefit from simplification.  (Good)
* **Originality:** The framing of the problem as locally encodable source coding and the discovery that AND queries can outperform XOR queries are novel contributions. The application of information-theoretic techniques to this area is also noteworthy. (Strong)
* **Significance:** The results have the potential to be significant for researchers and practitioners working on crowdsourcing, entity resolution, and clustering. Reducing query cost is a critical challenge in these areas, and the proposed approach offers a promising solution. (Good)

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Strong theoretical foundations and novel insights.
* Counterintuitive and potentially impactful result regarding AND queries.
* Well-positioned within the existing literature.
* Experimental validation, albeit limited.

**Con:**

* Complex theoretical derivations might limit accessibility.
* Limited experimental scope (k=2 only).
* Experimental details could be more comprehensive.
* Assumption of known prior could be a limitation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel approach to a relevant problem. The theoretical results are rigorous, the key finding regarding AND queries is intriguing, and the experimental validation provides some support for the claims.  While the paper has some minor weaknesses in terms of clarity and experimental scope, the strengths significantly outweigh the drawbacks.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept.** I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and is worthy of presentation at NIPS 2017. I recommend the authors address the minor weaknesses mentioned above, particularly improving the clarity of the theoretical sections and providing more details about the experimental setup.
