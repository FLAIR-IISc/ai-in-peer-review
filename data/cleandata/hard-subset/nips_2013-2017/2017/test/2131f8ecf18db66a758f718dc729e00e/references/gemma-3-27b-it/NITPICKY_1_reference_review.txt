This paper investigates semi-supervised clustering via querying an oracle, framing it within the locally encodable source coding paradigm. The authors explore the trade-offs between query complexity, oracle accuracy, and recovery guarantees, proposing an AND-query approach as an alternative to the standard “same cluster” (XOR) query. While the motivation and high-level ideas are interesting, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues, ranging from unclear notation and repetitive phrasing to questionable theoretical claims and a lack of rigorous experimental validation.

**Summary of Main Ideas:** The core idea is to recast the querying problem as a locally encodable source coding problem, allowing the application of information-theoretic bounds. The authors argue that AND queries can outperform XOR queries in approximate recovery scenarios, particularly when the oracle is noisy. They present theoretical results outlining the query complexity for both perfect and noisy oracles and demonstrate their approach on a small crowdsourcing experiment.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Framing:** The connection to locally encodable source coding is a potentially insightful perspective.
*   **AND Query Proposal:** The exploration of AND queries as a potentially superior alternative to XOR queries is a worthwhile investigation.
*   **Theoretical Analysis (attempted):** The authors attempt to provide information-theoretic bounds.

**Weaknesses (extensive):**

*   **Clarity and Notation:** The notation is inconsistent and often unclear. For example, the definition of 'p' changes contextually.  The use of X, X̂, and X̃ is not consistently applied, making the theoretical derivations hard to follow. The meaning of '∆' is used inconsistently - sometimes it is the number of labels in a query, and sometimes it relates to the locally decodable code context.
*   **Repetitive Phrasing:** The introduction is excessively repetitive, stating the same ideas multiple times in slightly different ways. Phrases like "queries with binary answers" and "same cluster queries" are repeated ad nauseam.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical results feel underdeveloped and lack sufficient justification. Theorem 2’s proof sketch is highly hand-wavy. The statement regarding Massey's result being a "limitation" of XOR queries feels unsupported, and the authors fail to adequately explain *why* AND queries circumvent this limitation beyond a superficial assertion. The connection between the locally encodable source coding framing and the actual querying scheme is not clearly established.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The crowdsourcing experiment is extremely limited in scope.  The experimental setup is described vaguely (e.g., how was the d-regular graph constructed?). The authors claim validation but present only limited comparative data, failing to demonstrate a statistically significant advantage of AND queries over XOR queries.  Simply observing lower error rates in one dataset does not constitute strong evidence.  Figure 5 is not adequately explained. The lack of baseline comparisons is a significant weakness. The claim regarding workers finding AND queries “easier” is anecdotal and requires proper user study methodology.
*   **Related Work:** While the authors cite a reasonable number of papers, the discussion lacks depth. The relevance of many cited papers to the specific contributions of this work is unclear. The connection to the broader literature on active learning is not explored.
*   **Minor Technical Errors:** The claim about needing O(n log n) queries for 2-cluster recovery with noisy oracles [21] is not generally true and relies on specific assumptions about cluster size ratios. The assertion that repeating a query is "not theoretically interesting" is incorrect, as it relates to error reduction.
* **Inconsistency in Assumptions:** The paper starts by assuming knowledge of the prior distribution *p* but then later considers the case where only cluster sizes are known, without fully reconciling the two scenarios.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:** The framing of the problem within locally encodable source coding is a novel idea that *could* lead to interesting results with further development. The exploration of AND queries is a worthwhile direction.

**Con:** The paper is plagued by clarity issues, lacks theoretical rigor, suffers from a weak experimental validation, and is overall insufficiently polished for NIPS. The numerous minor flaws detract significantly from the potential of the core ideas. The theoretical contributions, as presented, are not compelling. The experimental results, as presented, are not convincing. The claims made are often not adequately supported.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper touches upon interesting ideas, the numerous flaws in clarity, rigor, and presentation prevent it from meeting the standards of a NIPS publication. The authors need to significantly revise the paper, focusing on improving clarity, strengthening the theoretical analysis, providing a more rigorous experimental evaluation, and addressing the weaknesses outlined above. The current version feels like a preliminary investigation rather than a complete and polished research contribution. The paper would benefit from focusing on a more narrowly defined set of contributions (e.g., either the theoretical analysis or the experimental validation) and developing those aspects in more depth.
