## NIPS 2017 Review: Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of clustering (or label learning) with limited, potentially noisy, pairwise queries to an oracle (e.g., crowd workers). The authors frame this as a locally encodable source coding problem, drawing connections to information theory.  They establish theoretical lower bounds on the query complexity for both exact and approximate recovery of labels, and demonstrate that traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries are not optimal for approximate recovery. They propose using "AND" queries instead, offering improved performance guarantees, particularly in the presence of noise. A real-world crowdsourcing experiment with Amazon Mechanical Turk validates the proposed approach.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The work builds upon recent research in crowdsourced entity resolution and adaptive query strategies (e.g., [4, 8, 20, 21, 33]). It differentiates itself by leveraging the lens of locally encodable source coding, a relatively unexplored area within information theory [23], and connecting it directly to the querying problem. The paper also relates to and improves upon existing bounds from related fields like locally decodable codes [19, 16, 26] and smooth compression [23].  The comparison to Massey's work [17] regarding limitations of linear codes provides a strong theoretical foundation for proposing AND queries.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Framing:** The connection to locally encodable source coding offers a fresh perspective and allows for the application of information-theoretic tools to analyze the problem.
* **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper derives rigorous lower bounds on query complexity and establishes performance guarantees for the proposed AND query scheme, especially in the noisy oracle setting.
* **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to crowdsourcing applications where query cost is a significant concern.
* **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of a real-world experiment with Amazon Mechanical Turk provides supporting evidence for the effectiveness of the AND query approach.  The observation regarding majority voting failure in practice is insightful.
* **Clear Presentation (Generally):** The paper is generally well-structured, with a clear introduction outlining the problem and contributions.
* **Consideration of Noisy Oracles:** The explicit modeling and handling of noisy query answers is a crucial aspect that strengthens the applicability of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Proofs:**  Some of the proofs, particularly for Theorem 5, are complex and rely on significant theoretical machinery.  While rigorous, they may be difficult for readers not deeply versed in information theory to fully grasp.  More intuition could be provided alongside the formal proofs.
* **Limited Experimental Detail:** The description of the experimental setup is somewhat brief. More details on the specific tasks given to crowd workers, the control mechanisms employed, and the analysis of individual worker performance would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Algorithm 1 Complexity:** Algorithm 1, while theoretically sound, may not be the most practical to implement directly due to the need for a d-regular graph and associated calculations.  A discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithm would be beneficial.
* **Scope of Experiments:** The experiments primarily focus on a specific dataset (movies).  Testing on more diverse datasets would enhance the generalizability of the findings.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** The transition between sections and the motivation behind some technical choices (e.g., the specific choice of queries) could be clearer.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experiments. The analysis of the lower bounds and the proposed algorithm appear correct, and the treatment of noisy oracles is well-considered. It's a complete piece of work within the stated scope.
* **Clarity:**  Generally clear, but some sections (particularly proofs) are dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations.  The notation is generally consistent, but occasionally challenging.
* **Originality:**  The framing of the problem as locally encodable source coding and the proposal of AND queries as an alternative to XOR queries represent a novel contribution.  The related work section is thorough and demonstrates awareness of existing research.
* **Significance:** The results are significant, particularly for applications relying on crowdsourcing and active learning. The theoretical bounds provide valuable insights into the fundamental limits of querying, and the AND query scheme offers a practical improvement over existing approaches.



**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*  Provides a strong theoretical foundation for understanding query complexity in clustering with noisy oracles.
*  Offers a novel and potentially impactful querying strategy (AND queries) with demonstrated performance gains.
*  Connects a practical problem to a fundamental area of information theory.
*  Includes empirical validation.

**Con:**

*  The theoretical complexity of some proofs may limit accessibility.
*  The experimental evaluation could be more detailed and generalized.
*  The practical implementation complexity of Algorithm 1 is not fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field. The novel framing, rigorous theoretical analysis, and practical validation make it a valuable addition to the NIPS conference.  While some aspects could be improved with greater clarity and detail, the strengths of the work significantly outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions - specifically, requesting greater clarity in the proofs and more detailed explanation of the experimental setup).
