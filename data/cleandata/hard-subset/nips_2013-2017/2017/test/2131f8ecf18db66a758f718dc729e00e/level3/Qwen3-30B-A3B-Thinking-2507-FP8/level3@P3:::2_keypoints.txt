- Paper proposes a novel connection between semi-supervised clustering and locally encodable source coding and introduces AND-queries as an alternative to "same cluster" queries.  
- Theoretical and empirical results claim AND-queries can achieve better query complexity for approximate cluster recovery, especially with noisy oracles.  
- Strength: meaningful theoretical contribution linking two underexplored areas and providing algorithmic analysis.  
- Strength: empirical validation via crowdsourcing shows AND-queries outperform "same cluster" queries on a real dataset (105 vs 234 errors).  
- Weakness: restrictive query model forbids repeated identical queries without justification, which is unrealistic for crowdsourcing and error reduction.  
- Weakness: Theorem 1 conflicts with known O(n log n) exact-recovery results in the noiseless case and its proof is hard to follow.  
- Weakness: Theorems 3 and 4 are opaque—lower/upper bounds are mathematically complex and do not clearly show how m scales with n and δ or explain the gap between bounds.  
- Weakness: Inadequate comparisons—figures and experiments do not compare AND-queries to state-of-the-art "same cluster" algorithms, undermining claims of superiority.  
- Weakness: Algorithm 1 requires prior knowledge of cluster-size distribution p (or sizes) without discussion of estimation in practice; pseudocode is hard to follow.  
- Quality: theoretical contributions are interesting but technically problematic due to conflicts, unclear proofs, and restrictive assumptions.  
- Clarity: paper suffers from poor presentation and unintuitive explanation of scaling relationships; proofs for main theorems lack sufficient detail.  
- Originality: novel connection and query proposal, but originality is weakened by lack of proper baseline comparisons and restrictive model assumptions.  
- Significance: limited by the restrictive model, missing practical parameter estimation, and insufficient experimental baselines; real-world experiment is valuable but not conclusive.  
- Recommendation: reject—needs substantial revision to clarify theory, justify or relax model assumptions, and provide clear comparisons to prior state-of-the-art methods.
