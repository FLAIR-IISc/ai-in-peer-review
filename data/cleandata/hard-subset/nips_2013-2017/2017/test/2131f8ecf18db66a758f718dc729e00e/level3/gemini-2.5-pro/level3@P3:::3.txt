This paper tackles the problem of semi-supervised clustering where the goal is to recover ground-truth labels for `n` items using a minimum number of non-adaptive queries to an oracle (e.g., a crowdworker). The paper's primary contribution is the novel connection it establishes between this clustering problem and the information-theoretic concept of locally encodable source coding. This framework allows the authors to derive theoretical lower and upper bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery, considering both noiseless and noisy oracle responses. A key finding is that traditional "same-cluster" queries (equivalent to XOR) can be suboptimal, and the authors propose "AND" queries as a more efficient alternative in certain regimes, particularly for approximate recovery with noisy answers. The theoretical results are supported by simulations and a small-scale real crowdsourcing experiment.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The formulation of the query-based clustering problem as one of locally encodable source coding is highly original and insightful. This connection provides a principled, information-theoretic lens through which to analyze query complexity, moving beyond heuristic approaches. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could inspire further research at the intersection of machine learning, crowdsourcing, and information theory.

2.  **Theoretical Depth:** The paper presents a solid theoretical analysis, providing new lower bounds on the query complexity for exact and approximate recovery (Theorems 2 and 3) and corresponding upper bounds based on concrete query schemes (Theorems 1, 4, and 5). The analysis is rigorous and covers a range of important practical scenarios, including noisy oracles.

3.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem and walks the reader through the connection to source coding in an accessible manner. The core ideas are conveyed effectively, making a theoretically dense paper understandable to a broader machine learning audience.

4.  **Novelty of AND Queries:** The proposal and analysis of AND queries is a strong practical contribution. The authors provide a compelling theoretical argument, rooted in rate-distortion theory, for why these queries can outperform the more common same-cluster (XOR) queries, especially when only approximate recovery is needed. The experimental results, showing lower error rates for workers answering AND queries, further bolster this claim.

### Weaknesses and Questions for Authors

1.  **Motivation for AND Queries vs. Direct Label Queries:** My primary concern revolves around the practical motivation for using AND queries. A query like, "Are both movies 'action' movies?" seems to implicitly require the worker to know the label of each movie. If the worker can determine that both movies are 'action', they have already solved the labeling problem for those two items. In this scenario, why not ask a simpler direct query, such as "Is this movie an 'action' movie?" The paper's justification is that direct label queries require at least `n` queries for full recovery. However, the proposed AND query schemes also seem to require a number of queries linear in `n` (e.g., `O(n log(1/δ))`). A more detailed discussion clarifying the scenarios where AND queries are substantially more economical (in terms of cost, worker cognitive load, or information gained) than direct label queries would significantly strengthen the paper's motivation.

2.  **Complexity of Theoretical Bounds:** While the bounds in Theorems 1-4 are comprehensive, their mathematical expressions are quite complex, making it difficult to extract immediate intuition about the key trade-offs. For instance, the expression for `δ` in Theorem 4 is not very transparent. It would be beneficial for the reader if the authors could provide simplified asymptotic versions or corollaries of these theorems that highlight the essential dependencies on key parameters like the prior `p`, the error rate `δ`, and the query locality `Δ`, while preserving the core message of the results.

3.  **Scope of Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments in Section 4 provide a good proof-of-concept for the AND query algorithm in the noisy setting. However, the paper's theoretical contributions cover both AND and XOR queries in noiseless settings as well. The experimental validation would be more complete if it included a direct comparison of the proposed coding schemes for XOR and AND queries, rather than comparing the AND scheme to a generic spectral clustering baseline. This would allow for a more direct empirical test of the theoretical claims about the relative performance of these query types.

### Summary and Recommendation

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on clustering with crowdsourcing. The theoretical framework based on locally encodable source coding is elegant and powerful, and the analysis of AND queries presents a novel and potentially practical idea. The paper is technically sound and very clearly written.

While I have raised some questions about the practical motivation behind the proposed AND queries and suggested areas for improving the clarity of the bounds and the scope of the experiments, these are addressable points. The core theoretical contributions are strong enough to stand on their own. The paper advances our understanding of the fundamental limits of query-based clustering and provides new directions for designing efficient crowdsourcing protocols. Therefore, I recommend acceptance. I am confident that addressing the points above will further improve an already excellent paper.
