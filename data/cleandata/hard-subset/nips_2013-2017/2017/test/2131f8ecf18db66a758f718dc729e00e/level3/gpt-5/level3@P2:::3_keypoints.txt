- Paper studies non-adaptive, budgeted crowdsourced clustering through a novel locally encodable source coding lens, with Δ-local queries and goals of exact/approximate recovery in noiseless and noisy settings.
- Establishes an equivalence to locally encodable source coding and uses information-theoretic tools to derive lower bounds and near-optimal algorithms.
- Noiseless exact recovery (k=2): pairwise XOR queries give achievability m ≈ n(h(p)+o(1))/log(1/α) and a converse stronger than n h(p) in some regimes.
- Noiseless approximate recovery (k=2): derives a general Δ-local rate–distortion lower bound, shows XOR is fundamentally limited, and proposes pairwise AND with m = O(n log(1/δ)) for (1−δ)-accuracy.
- Noisy approximate recovery (general k): designs k-ary AND-like queries with a d-regular design and thresholding decoder achieving (1−δ)-accuracy with m = O(n log(k/δ)) under symmetric noise.
- Empirical validation: small real crowdsourcing (movie genres) and synthetic experiments show AND queries have lower worker error rates and algorithms match theoretical trends; no majority-voting on repeated queries.
- Places work within prior literature on same-cluster queries, coding-theoretic crowdsourcing, rate–distortion, and local decodability; locally encodable viewpoint is novel.
- Theoretical development is technically sound, largely self-contained, with clear assumptions (non-adaptivity, independent noise, no identical query repetition).
- Paper is well written and accessible, though main theorem statements are dense and could use simplified expressions and highlighted regimes.
- Originality: locally encodable formulation and systematic use of AND queries (and k-ary generalization) are novel; improved locality-aware converses are new.
- Significance: advances query-efficient clustering theory, shows XOR is suboptimal for approximate/noisy settings, and provides a simple near-optimal non-adaptive algorithm.
- Pros: principled information-theoretic framework, practical near-optimal algorithm, insight that AND outperforms XOR in key regimes, and supportive (if limited) empirical evidence.
- Main motivation concern: AND queries reference specific labels—need clearer justification for preferring AND over direct single-item label queries in practical settings and cost models.
- Experimental limitations: real-data comparisons to same-cluster (XOR) are limited and lack stronger, apples-to-apples XOR baselines and tuned decoders.
- Theorem presentation: statements are heavy; recommend providing simplified corollaries, dominant-term isolations, and short proof sketches to aid readability.
- Assumptions caveat: reliance on known priors or accurate estimates—robustness to prior misspecification and degree heterogeneity needs discussion.
- Suggested improvements: add explicit AND vs direct-label comparison, simplified corollaries for Theorems 1–4, expanded empirical XOR baselines with budget calibration, and discuss extensions (partial adaptivity, heterogeneity, correlated errors, triadic queries).
- Arguments for acceptance: novel solid theory, clear insights (local encodability; AND > XOR), simple near-optimal algorithm, and relevance to ML and information theory communities.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical validation and some practical assumptions (k-ary AND queries, known priors) may not always hold.
- Overall recommendation: Accept — substantive theoretical contribution with clear implications and manageable clarifications requested.
