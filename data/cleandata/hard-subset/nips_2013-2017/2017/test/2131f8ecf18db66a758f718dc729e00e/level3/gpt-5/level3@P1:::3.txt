Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies non-adaptive, budgeted crowdsourced clustering/labeling via low-arity queries and establishes a formal equivalence to locally encodable source coding. This viewpoint yields information-theoretic lower bounds and new algorithms. For k = 2, “same-cluster” queries correspond to XORs. The authors show that for exact recovery with noiseless answers and constant arity Δ, one can beat the trivial n queries by exploiting the prior p and using sparse random XOR (“same-cluster”) measurements, with query counts approaching n h(p)/log(1/α). They also prove converse bounds that improve on n h(p) in certain regimes.

For approximate recovery, the paper leverages rate–distortion insights and a lower bound that explicitly accounts for locality (Δ). It argues that XOR-based queries are provably suboptimal for approximate recovery, appealing to the Ancheta–Massey limitations of linear codes for rate–distortion. The key positive result proposes pairwise AND queries, showing that near-optimal trade-offs are achievable with simple local decoding.

With noise (symmetric error q), for general k ≥ 2 the paper proposes a pairwise generalized-AND query and a simple thresholding decoder on a d-regular graph. It proves that O(n log(k/δ)) queries suffice to correctly label a (1 − δ) fraction of items with high probability, under independent noise and known priors (or known cluster sizes via random permutation). Empirical results on a small crowdsourcing experiment (movie genres) and synthetic data corroborate the theoretical trends, and indicate that AND queries elicit fewer worker errors than “same-cluster” (XOR) queries.

The work is well-situated within crowdsourcing (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah; entity resolution) and query-based clustering (Ashtiani et al.). On the information-theoretic side, it connects to locally decodable/“smooth” compression and rate–distortion, and uses classical techniques (typicality, expurgation, Chernoff bounds).

Quality:
- Theory is careful and largely sound. The mapping to locally encodable source coding is elegant and enables nontrivial lower bounds (e.g., the (1 − ρ) term reflecting limited coverage due to Δ-locality). The AND-based achievability is backed by explicit error probability calculations under a Poissonized degree model and standard concentration.
- Assumptions are clearly stated: non-adaptive queries; independent label priors; independent query noise; no repeated queries; knowledge of priors or relative cluster sizes. The extension to known sizes (Theorem 6) mitigates the prior knowledge concern.
- The noisy-case guarantee O(n log(k/δ)) is compelling and matches practical non-adaptive needs.
- Proof sketches in the main text are helpful, though some expressions are dense (see clarity).

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written and accessible to a broad ML audience. The information-theoretic translation is explained clearly.
- Suggest simplifying and re-expressing the bounds in Theorems 1–4:
  - Provide intuitive corollaries that expose regimes (e.g., for Δ = 2 and p near 1/2, when is m < n?).
  - Replace long binomial expressions by clean approximations and state-leading terms, with precise constants deferred to the appendix.
  - For the approximate recovery lower bound (Theorem 3), give a short “rule-of-thumb” expression and plots versus Δ and m/n to illustrate the gap from the unconstrained rate–distortion curve.
- Unify notation (e.g., r(p) vs. 2p(1 − p)); explicitly name “XOR (same-cluster)” and “AND (conjunctive membership)” queries to avoid confusion.

Originality:
- The locally encodable source-coding perspective for crowdsourced clustering is novel and yields new lower bounds under Δ-locality.
- The identification and analysis of AND queries as strictly better than XOR for approximate recovery (both noiseless and noisy) is insightful and, to my knowledge, new in this context.
- The O(n log(k/δ)) non-adaptive, noise-robust scheme with simple decoding is a practical and original contribution.

Significance:
- Provides principled guidance on which query primitives to use in practice (AND vs XOR), and how many are needed as a function of δ, k, and noise q.
- The Δ-locality bounds are likely to influence subsequent work on query design and coding-theoretic approaches to crowdsourcing/ER.
- The experimental evidence, while small-scale, suggests that AND queries are easier for crowd workers, lending practical relevance to the theory.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Practicality of AND queries: An AND query can reveal the positive class label (e.g., “are both items class i?”), seemingly conflicting with the original motivation of not asking for direct labels. If revealing membership is acceptable, why not ask single-item labels? The paper notes that label queries need ≥ n queries for exact recovery, whereas pairwise functions can do better, and that under noise, AND allows error mitigation without repetition. Please make this trade-off explicit:
  - When is the per-query cognitive cost or error rate of AND substantially lower than label queries?
  - In settings with privacy or where revealing labels is disallowed, can AND be posed without leaking individual labels?
  - Provide a brief comparison against direct label queries (noiseless/noisy) in terms of query complexity and empirical worker error.
- Experiments: The main empirical evaluation uses only AND queries. Including “same-cluster” baselines with competitive decoders (e.g., spectral clustering from pairwise similarity matrices built from noisy answers) would strengthen the case. Also report sensitivity to:
  - Prior mis-specification and the “known sizes” variant.
  - Degree d and graph construction (beyond the circular permutation).
  - Unknown q and adaptive threshold selection (e.g., via validation edges).
- Presentation: Move some heavy algebra to the appendix; add plots summarizing the achievable/lower-bound regions for Δ = 2, k = 2 across p and δ.

Pros:
- Clear and impactful theoretical framework connecting query-based clustering to locally encodable coding.
- New lower bounds under Δ-locality and informative achievability results.
- Simple, scalable non-adaptive algorithm with O(n log(k/δ)) queries under noise.
- Empirical indication that AND queries reduce worker error.

Cons:
- Ambiguity about the realism and acceptability of AND queries vis-à-vis direct labeling and privacy.
- Limited empirical scope and baselines; reliance on a small crowdsourcing study.

Questions for authors:
- Please clarify the intended application settings where AND queries are feasible but direct labels are not, and discuss privacy/label-leakage considerations.
- Can you provide a simplified corollary or table summarizing Theorems 1–4 for Δ = 2 and typical p?
- How robust is Algorithm 1 to unknown q? Can thresholds be learned from data?
- Do your results extend to higher-arity queries (e.g., triangles) and to limited adaptivity?

Recommendation:
After considering the rebuttal and clarifications, I believe the contributions are solid and significant. Despite the noted concerns, the paper advances both the theory and practice of query design for clustering under noise, and the locally encodable viewpoint is likely to inspire further research. I recommend acceptance, with suggestions above for improving the camera-ready version.
