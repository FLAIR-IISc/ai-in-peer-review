- Frames query complexity of semi-supervised clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem, connecting clustering queries to information theory.  
- Derives upper and lower bounds on query complexity for exact and approximate label recovery under noiseless and noisy oracle models.  
- Introduces AND queries as a potentially more efficient alternative to traditional “same cluster” (XOR) queries, especially for approximate recovery.  
- Leverages and links to literature on crowdsourced clustering, entity resolution, and locally encodable/decodable codes.  
- Clear motivation: minimizing costly oracle queries (e.g., human annotators) for practical applications.  
- Strength: novel formal connection enabling use of information-theoretic tools.  
- Strength: rigorous analysis, with technically sound bounds for noisy oracles (notably Theorem 5).  
- Strength: practical implications supported by preliminary experiments showing promise for AND queries.  
- Strength: comprehensive treatment of multiple settings (noisy/noiseless, exact/approximate) and limitations of same-cluster queries.  
- Strength: generally well-written and organized presentation despite technical depth.  
- Weakness: very general assumptions limit tightness of bounds and applicability; domain-specific models (e.g., SBM) could yield sharper results.  
- Weakness: experimental validation is limited in scope; needs more datasets, noise regimes, and comparisons to other clustering methods.  
- Weakness: some complex proofs (e.g., Theorem 3) are deferred to supplements, reducing accessibility.  
- Weakness: crowdsourcing discussion feels somewhat disconnected from the core information-theoretic analysis.  
- Weakness: related-work section is overly extensive and could be streamlined to the most relevant prior work.  
- For acceptance: presents novel, technically sound link to information theory with useful bounds and promising AND query approach.  
- Against acceptance: generality reduces practical impact, experiments are limited, and proofs may be inaccessible to broader audience.  
- Overall: solid contribution deserving acceptance (score 7/10); recommends expanding experiments and exploring results under specific data assumptions.
