- Paper studies non-adaptive, small-arity (∆-ary) noisy query models for multiclass labeling/clustering and makes conceptual, theoretical, and empirical contributions.
- Conceptual: establishes an equivalence between crowdsourced same-cluster queries and locally encodable source coding, enabling information-theoretic lower bounds.
- Theory: proves lower bounds for exact/approximate recovery under locality, shows limitations of XOR queries, and introduces AND and k-ary AND query schemes.
- Algorithmic result: gives a simple non-adaptive noisy algorithm using pairwise k-ary AND queries with O(n log(k/δ)) queries to recover a (1−δ) fraction of labels with high probability.
- Empirics: small crowdsourcing experiment suggests AND queries may be easier for workers and produce fewer errors than same-cluster queries.
- Relation to prior work: connects to clustering-with-queries, crowdsourcing redundancy allocation, local/smooth source coding, and community recovery/query complexity literature.
- Strength: novel and potentially impactful locally encodable source coding viewpoint yielding clean lower bounds under locality.
- Strength: insightful finding that AND queries can outperform XOR for approximate recovery due to asymmetric evidence.
- Strength: simple, scalable non-adaptive algorithm with concentration-based correctness proof sketch.
- Strength: preliminary real crowd evidence supporting AND query reliability.
- Weakness 1: modeling choice disallowing repeated identical queries is a strong and potentially unrealistic restriction that may change optimal tradeoffs.
- Weakness 1 request: either justify no-repeats empirically or analyze the setting allowing repeats (e.g., t repeats, majority vote) and show effect on query complexity.
- Weakness 2: Theorem 1 (noiseless exact-recovery) proof via random sparse parity ensemble + expurgation is hard to follow and needs clearer, self-contained derivation.
- Weakness 2 request: reconcile Theorem 1 asymptotics/assumptions with prior related results (e.g., hypergraph/community recovery, adaptive vs non-adaptive).
- Weakness 3: The approximate-recovery lower bound (Theorem 3) and AND achievability (Theorem 4) are difficult to interpret and need simplified corollaries exposing scaling with n, m, δ, ∆, p.
- Weakness 3 request: provide explicit examples (e.g., ∆=2, m=c n) and approximations for f(k,d) and Poissonization terms to make converse/achievability gaps tangible.
- Weakness 4: empirical evaluation lacks algorithmic XOR/same-cluster baselines and controlled synthetic studies across p, q, δ to substantiate claims.
- Weakness 4 request: include state-of-the-art non-adaptive baselines (reference-element, spectral, ML) and visualize parameter ranges where AND dominates.
- Weakness 5: Algorithm 1 requires knowledge of p and q (or exact class sizes) and thresholds; paper lacks discussion on estimating/robustness to misspecification and adaptive thresholding.
- Weakness 5 request: provide sample complexity for parameter estimation, sensitivity plots, and guidance for practitioners.
- Weakness 6: several proofs and figures are terse or opaque; notation and presentation need tightening and clearer intuition (why XOR is bad, role of α/r(p), locality effects).
- Quality: promising theoretical direction but some proofs are hard to follow and several claims are unvalidated against algorithmic baselines.
- Originality: strong—locally encodable source coding lens and AND-vs-XOR insight appear novel.
- Significance: potentially significant if empirical validation and clearer theoretical takeaways (scaling laws, tightness) are provided.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel conceptual bridge, simple non-adaptive algorithm with good query complexity, practical insight on AND queries.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient empirical comparisons, hard-to-interpret main theorems, restrictive modeling choices with unclear robustness.
- Key questions for authors: provide simplified corollaries for Theorems 3–4; analyze up to t repeats; reconcile Theorem 1 with prior noiseless results; assess robustness to p/q misspecification; include XOR-based baseline comparisons.
- Recommendation: borderline reject—compelling ideas but needs clearer statements/proofs and stronger empirical comparisons.
