

This paper establishes a novel connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, proposing AND-queries as a more efficient alternative to traditional "same cluster" queries. The authors derive theoretical lower bounds and present an AND-query algorithm with real-world crowdsourcing validation, claiming significant performance improvements.

The paper's main strength lies in its insightful theoretical framework connecting clustering to information theory, which is well-motivated and represents a meaningful contribution to the field. The real-data evaluation provides practical validation of the theoretical claims, demonstrating the feasibility of the approach.

However, several critical issues undermine the paper's technical contribution. First, the restrictive model disallowing repeated identical queries lacks justification, and the authors should address the more practical case where query repetition is permitted. Second, Theorem 1 conflicts with prior work (Ahn-Lee-Suh) in the noiseless exact-recovery regime, and its proof is difficult to follow. Third, Theorems 3 and 4 suffer from unclear interpretation of the lower/upper bounds, particularly regarding how δ scales with n and the gap between bounds. Fourth, Figure 2 lacks comparison to the same-cluster-query algorithm, making the claimed superiority unsubstantiated. Fifth, Algorithm 1 requires prior knowledge of p or cluster sizes without discussing practical estimation methods, and its pseudocode is hard to follow. Finally, Figures 3-5 omit comparisons to state-of-the-art methods like same-cluster queries, failing to substantiate the claimed performance advantages.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing these issues: clarifying the justification for disallowing repeated queries, resolving conflicts with prior work, improving the interpretability of theoretical results, and providing comprehensive comparisons to relevant baselines. While the theoretical connection is promising, the current presentation and lack of proper comparisons prevent the claims from being properly validated.

Overall, the paper addresses an interesting problem with potentially valuable insights but falls short due to significant clarity and comparative issues. The theoretical contribution is promising but requires more rigorous treatment and validation to meet NIPS standards.
