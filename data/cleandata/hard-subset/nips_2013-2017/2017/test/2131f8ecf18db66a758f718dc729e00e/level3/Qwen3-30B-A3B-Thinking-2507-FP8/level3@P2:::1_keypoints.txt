- Establishes a novel connection between semi-supervised clustering and locally encodable source coding from information theory.  
- Studies query complexity for clustering using "same cluster" (XOR) and "AND" queries to an oracle.  
- Derives theoretical lower and upper bounds on queries for exact and approximate recovery, including noisy-oracle analysis.  
- Shows AND queries outperform "same cluster" queries for approximate recovery.  
- Provides rigorous, well-structured proofs and tight bounds in many cases.  
- Validates theoretical results with a real crowdsourcing experiment.  
- Relates to prior work on clustering with side information and locally decodable/source codes, but is distinct by linking to locally encodable coding.  
- Paper is well-written and organized, though initial source-coding sections may challenge non-experts.  
- Figures effectively illustrate theoretical bounds and experimental outcomes.  
- Suggests more intuition is needed on why AND queries are superior.  
- Highly original contribution with significant conceptual advance (AND queries + encoding connection).  
- Results are significant for crowdsourcing and entity resolution, giving fundamental limits on query complexity.  
- Strengths: novel theory-application link, rigorous analysis, practical validation, clear advantage of AND queries.  
- Weaknesses: reliance on marginal label distribution, primary focus on binary labels (limited k>2 treatment), no practical guidance for unknown label distributions.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical and experimental support, practical relevance, well-written.  
- Arguments against acceptance: assumptions limit scope (known distribution, binary focus), experimental validation is small-scale.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — technically sound, novel, and significant despite some scope limitations.
