The paper "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding" presents a novel approach to clustering with same-cluster queries to an oracle, aiming to recover the target clustering. The authors characterize the query complexity needed to perfectly or approximately recover the clustering, providing upper and lower bounds on the required queries, including analysis for noisy oracles.

The paper is well-motivated, with applications such as record deduplication and crowdsourcing. The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of the problem and its connections to information theory, particularly in the context of locally encodable source coding. The techniques used are novel to the machine learning community, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the underlying concepts.

The main contributions of the paper are threefold. Firstly, the authors provide a querying scheme that asks a non-adaptive pairwise 'same cluster' queries and recovers all labels with high probability for a range of prior probabilities. Secondly, they provide a new lower bound on the number of queries required to recover a fraction of the labels, which is strictly better than the naive bound. Thirdly, they propose a new querying strategy based on AND operation that performs substantially better than 'same cluster' queries.

The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise.

The originality of the paper lies in its novel approach to clustering with same-cluster queries, as well as its connections to information theory. The authors provide a unique perspective on the problem, and their results are significant contributions to the field.

The significance of the paper is high, as it addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The results have implications for various applications, including record deduplication and crowdsourcing. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, providing new insights and techniques for clustering with same-cluster queries.

One limitation of the paper is that it does not make any structural assumptions (e.g., hypothesis class) on the data, which limits the scope of the analysis. However, this is a common challenge in unsupervised learning problems.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results. The supplementary material is also well-organized and provides additional details on the proofs and experiments.

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, the paper meets all the criteria. The authors provide a clear summary of the main ideas, relate them to previous work, and discuss the strengths and weaknesses of the paper. The paper is technically sound, original, and significant.
