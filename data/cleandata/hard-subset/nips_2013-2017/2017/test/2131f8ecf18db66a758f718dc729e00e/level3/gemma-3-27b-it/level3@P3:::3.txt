This paper investigates clustering through crowdsourced similarity queries, establishing theoretical bounds for noisy and noiseless query responses. The core contribution lies in demonstrating the equivalence between this querying model and locally encodable source coding, allowing the authors to derive novel lower bounds on query complexity. The paper is exceptionally well-written and accessible, presenting a complex topic clearly to a broad machine learning audience. The connection to information theory is explained thoroughly and intuitively.

The work builds upon a growing body of research utilizing crowdsourcing for data labeling and clustering (e.g., [8, 20, 33]). It distinguishes itself by moving beyond empirical observations and providing rigorous theoretical guarantees.  Recent studies in entity resolution ([30, 33]) motivate the use of pairwise queries, but this paper goes further by analyzing the fundamental limits of such an approach and proposing an alternative – AND queries – with provably better performance in many scenarios. The connection to locally encodable source coding is a novel perspective, linking this problem to a well-studied area in information theory.  Related work in locally decodable source coding ([19, 16, 26]) is appropriately acknowledged.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper’s strength is its strong theoretical foundation. The proofs appear sound, and the derived bounds are insightful.
* **Clarity & Accessibility:**  The writing is clear, concise, and well-organized, making the paper easy to follow even for readers not deeply familiar with information theory.
* **Novelty:** The equivalence to locally encodable source coding offers a fresh perspective on crowdsourced clustering.  The introduction of AND queries as a potentially superior alternative is a significant contribution.
* **Information-Theoretic Connection:** The authors effectively leverage information-theoretic tools to analyze the problem and derive meaningful bounds.

**Weaknesses:**

* **AND Query Justification:** A key concern is the potential for AND queries to inadvertently reveal cluster labels. If the goal is simply to infer cluster membership, why not directly ask for labels, as noted by the authors themselves? This point needs further clarification.  The argument that asking for combinations is beneficial *because* it avoids direct labeling feels incomplete without a deeper discussion of the information revealed by each query type.
* **Bound Simplification:** The expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1–4 are somewhat complex and difficult to interpret intuitively. Simplifying these expressions – perhaps providing asymptotic approximations or focusing on specific parameter regimes – would significantly enhance their usability and impact. Preserving the essence of the bounds while making them more accessible is crucial.
* **Experimental Focus:** The experiments appear to exclusively utilize AND queries. While the authors report improved performance with AND queries in a crowdsourcing experiment, the validity of these conclusions is weakened without a comparative evaluation against 'same cluster' queries under the *same* experimental conditions.  If the theoretical justification for AND queries relies on specific assumptions about the query model or oracle behavior, those assumptions need to be explicitly stated and their impact on the experimental results discussed. The motivation behind *only* using AND queries needs stronger justification.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to crowdsourced clustering.
* The results offer valuable insights into the fundamental limits of this problem.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and accessible.
* The connection to locally encodable source coding is a significant contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The justification for AND queries requires further elaboration.
* The complexity of the derived bounds may limit their practical impact.
* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited and could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with alternative query types.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the concerns regarding the justification for AND queries and the complexity of the bounds, I believe this is a strong and valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical results are significant, the paper is exceptionally well-written, and the connection to information theory is insightful. The authors have addressed my concerns adequately in their response (as of the time of this review).  

**Recommendation:**  **Accept** with the suggestion that the authors consider simplifying the expressions for their bounds and providing a more thorough justification for the use of AND queries, potentially including a direct comparison with 'same cluster' queries in their experimental setup.
