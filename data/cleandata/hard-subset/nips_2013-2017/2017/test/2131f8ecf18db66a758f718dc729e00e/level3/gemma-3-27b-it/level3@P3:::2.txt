This paper explores the connection between semisupervised clustering via noisy pairwise queries and locally encodable source coding, deriving lower bounds and proposing an AND-query based algorithm with empirical validation. While the core idea of leveraging information theory for query design is promising, the paper suffers from several issues regarding clarity, justification of modeling choices, and comparison to existing work.

**Summary of Key Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:** The paper frames the problem of clustering with limited, potentially noisy, pairwise queries as a locally encodable source coding problem. This allows the authors to apply information-theoretic tools to establish lower bounds on query complexity. The central contribution lies in demonstrating the potential benefits of AND queries over traditional ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries, particularly in approximate recovery scenarios. This builds upon prior work in crowdsourced clustering (e.g., [4, 8, 20, 33]) and relates to the broader field of source coding and locally decodable/encodable codes [16, 19, 23].  The attempt to connect these areas is novel, but the execution is flawed in several aspects.

**Quality:** The technical soundness of the paper is questionable in parts. Theorems 1 and 3, in particular, are difficult to follow, and the justification for the model's constraints (e.g., disallowing repeated queries) is weak. While theoretical lower bounds are presented, their practical implications aren’t always clear, especially concerning the scaling of *m* with *n* and *δ*. The connection to locally encodable source coding, while conceptually interesting, doesn't seem to yield significantly stronger results than existing approaches directly applied to the clustering problem.  The experiments provide some validation, but are limited in scope (see Clarity and Significance).

**Clarity:** The paper is poorly written and organized.  Proofs are often referenced without sufficient context, making them difficult to understand. The notation is sometimes inconsistent.  Algorithm 1’s pseudocode is opaque without a detailed understanding of the accompanying proofs.  The meaning of parameters like δ in Theorem 3 is not immediately apparent.  The distinction between exact and approximate recovery is not always clearly articulated.  The flow between theoretical derivations and experimental results is disjointed.

**Originality:** The core idea of applying locally encodable source coding to this clustering problem *is* novel. However, the originality is diminished by the lack of clear advantages over existing techniques and the somewhat contrived restrictions placed on the model.  The AND-query suggestion, while potentially useful, isn't dramatically different from existing query strategies and lacks a compelling justification beyond the theoretical analysis. The prior work is cited, but the paper doesn’t sufficiently distinguish its contribution from that of existing approaches.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is limited.  The restrictive assumption of disallowing repeated queries weakens the practical relevance. The theoretical results, while mathematically interesting, don’t translate into substantial improvements in query complexity compared to existing methods. The experiments are insufficient to demonstrate a clear advantage of the proposed approach.  Without a more compelling demonstration of performance gains in realistic scenarios, the paper’s impact will be minimal.

**Specific Weaknesses (Addressing Points Directly):**

1. **No Repeated Queries:** The justification for prohibiting repeated identical queries is weak. The authors state it's related to error aggregation, but don't provide compelling evidence that this is a realistic constraint. An analysis of the case *with* repetition, or an independent annotator setting, is crucial.
2. **Theorem 1 Conflict:** The discrepancy with Ahn-Lee-Suh’s work needs to be addressed directly. A thorough comparison and explanation of the differences in assumptions and methodology is required. The proof of Theorem 1 is extremely hard to follow and lacks sufficient detail.
3. **Theorem 3 Interpretability:** The expression for δ in Theorem 3 is opaque. The authors need to clearly explain how *m* scales with *n* and *δ*. The practical benefit of relaxing *δ* needs to be demonstrated.
4. **Theorem 4 Interpretability:**  Similar to Theorem 3, the results of Theorem 4 are hard to interpret. The gap between its upper bound and Theorem 3’s lower bound isn't adequately explained.  Again, the proofs are difficult to follow.
5. **Figure 2 Comparison:** The lack of comparison to a same-cluster-query algorithm in Figure 2 is a major flaw. The claimed superiority of the AND-query method is unsupported without this baseline. The observed crossing behavior is unexplained.
6. **Algorithm 1 Parameter Estimation:** Requiring prior knowledge of *p* or cluster sizes is a significant limitation. The authors need to discuss practical methods for estimating these parameters and improve the pseudocode's readability.
7. **Figures 3-5 Comparison:** The absence of comparison to state-of-the-art methods (e.g., same-cluster queries, spectral clustering) in Figures 3-5 undermines the claim that AND queries outperform existing schemes.

**Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**For:**
* Novel connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding.
* Theoretical analysis of query complexity.
* Some empirical validation.

**Against:**
* Poor clarity and organization.
* Weak justification for modeling choices.
* Lack of comparison to relevant prior work.
* Limited practical significance.
* Difficult to follow proofs.
* Restrictive assumptions.



**Overall Assessment:**  This paper presents an interesting, but ultimately flawed, attempt to apply information-theoretic tools to the problem of semisupervised clustering. The lack of clarity, weak justification for modeling choices, and insufficient comparison to existing work significantly detract from its value.  I recommend **rejection** due to these significant weaknesses. The authors need to substantially revise the paper, addressing the points outlined above, before it can be considered for publication.
