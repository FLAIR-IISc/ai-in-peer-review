This paper presents a novel approach to clustering via crowdsourced similarity queries. The authors provide theoretical bounds for both noiseless and noisy query answers, as well as empirical simulations to support their claims. The main contribution of the paper is theoretical, but it has significant implications for practical applications in crowdsourced clustering.

The paper is well-written and accessible to a general machine learning audience. The authors provide a clear explanation of the information-theoretic connection between clustering and source coding, which is a key insight of the paper. The notation is also well-defined and easy to follow.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a unifying framework for understanding the limitations of different querying strategies. The authors show that 'same cluster' queries, which are commonly used in crowdsourced clustering, are not optimal for approximate recovery. Instead, they propose using AND queries, which can achieve better performance in many situations.

The experimental results presented in the paper are also convincing. The authors demonstrate that their algorithm can achieve good performance on real-world datasets, even in the presence of noise. The comparison with 'same cluster' queries is particularly useful, as it highlights the advantages of the proposed approach.

However, there are some concerns with the paper. One issue is that the AND queries may reveal the cluster label, which could conflict with the motivation for using crowdsourcing in the first place. If the labels are revealed, why not ask for them directly? The authors should address this concern and provide a clear justification for using AND queries.

Another issue is that the expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1-4 are quite complex and could be simplified while preserving their essence. The authors should consider simplifying these expressions to make them more accessible to a broader audience.

Additionally, the experiments appear to use only AND queries, which raises concerns about the validity of the results. The authors should clarify the motivation for using only AND queries and provide additional experiments to demonstrate the robustness of their approach.

Overall, I am satisfied with the authors' responses and recommend acceptance of the paper. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of crowdsourced clustering and provides a new perspective on the problem. With some minor revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, the paper has the potential to be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
