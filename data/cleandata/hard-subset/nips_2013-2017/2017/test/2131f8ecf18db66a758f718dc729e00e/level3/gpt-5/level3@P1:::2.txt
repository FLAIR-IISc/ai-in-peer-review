Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies nonadaptive semisupervised clustering (multiclass labeling) from small-set queries, with possibly noisy answers. The core conceptual contribution is an equivalence between this querying model and locally encodable source coding: each query answer is an encoded bit that depends on at most Δ source symbols (labels). Leveraging this lens, the authors derive information-theoretic lower bounds and propose algorithms. They argue that widely used “same cluster?” (XOR-type) pairwise queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery and propose AND queries instead. For k = 2, they give: (i) noiseless exact-recovery achievability with m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries (Δ-ary XOR), and an accompanying necessary bound sharper than n h(p) for some p; (ii) noiseless approximate-recovery lower bounds (rate-distortion with locality) and an AND-based achievability bound; and (iii) noisy approximate recovery for general k via a simple thresholding algorithm on a d-regular graph, achieving m = O(n log(k/δ)) to get (1 − δ) correct labels, with a small real-data demonstration. The work connects to several strands: crowdsourcing for entity resolution with same-cluster queries (e.g., Ashtiani et al., NIPS’16; Vesdapunt et al., PVLDB’14), rate-distortion and source coding (Cover–Thomas), locally decodable/smooth compression (Makhdoumi et al.; Montanari–Mossel), and limitations of linear codes for lossy compression (Ancheta–Massey).

Strengths:
- Conceptual unification: casting query design as locally encodable source coding is elegant and potentially influential. It brings fresh lower-bounding tools to query complexity for clustering.
- Identification of inherent limitations of XOR (same-cluster) queries for approximate recovery, and constructive AND-based alternatives with clean, threshold-based decoding.
- Nonadaptive schemes with near-linear queries and explicit scaling m = O(n log(k/δ)) under noise are simple and potentially practical.
- Some new lower bounds that improve over naïve entropy-based ones by incorporating locality constraints.
- The crowdsourcing experiment, while small, provides preliminary evidence that AND questions are easier for workers and can exhibit lower empirical noise.

Weaknesses and questions:
1) Non-repetition assumption. Disallowing repeated identical queries is a restrictive modeling choice. In practice, repetition and aggregation (possibly with better-than-majority aggregation) are standard in crowdsourcing; theory can treat repeated queries as a resource trade-off between m and error. Please justify more fully, analyze the no-restriction setting, and clarify whether your lower bounds/algorithms change when repeated queries are allowed. If the main reason is independence of answers, cite empirical evidence beyond the brief references, or quantify when repetition helps/hurts under your noise model.

2) Noiseless exact recovery and Theorem 1. The claimed sublinear-in-n factor (< n) with Δ = 2 for some p appears to conflict with, or at least be in tension with, prior nonadaptive pairwise-query results (e.g., random same-cluster queries requiring Θ(n log n) for exact recovery; Ahn–Lee–Suh on hypergraph/community recovery). The proof via expurgation over typical sets assumes i.i.d. labels with known p and allows nonrandom measurement design; many prior results consider adversarial instances or worst-case labelings. Please reconcile these models explicitly, state the exact regime and assumptions under which your improvement holds, and provide an intuitive explanation for why locality and prior knowledge enable m < n with Δ = 2. As written, the proof is hard to follow; a simplified overview with rank/ambiguity arguments and a clear comparison to [2,4,ALS16] would help.

3) Interpretability of Theorem 3 (noiseless approximate lower bound). The bound for δ as a function of m/n and Δ is opaque. It is difficult to read off how δ diminishes with m, or how the penalty due to locality compares to standard rate-distortion δ̃(γ) = h−1(h(p) − γ). Provide clean corollaries for common regimes (e.g., Δ = 2, p = 1/2), asymptotic expansions for small δ, and plots of achievable δ vs. m/n alongside your lower bound to visualize the gap.

4) Theorem 4 (AND achievability) has similar interpretability issues. The expression for δ involves Poissonization and combinatorial terms f(k,d) that obscure scaling. Provide streamlined bounds (e.g., δ ≤ p e−Θ(m/n) with explicit constants) and numeric guidance (how many queries for δ = 0.1 at p = 0.5?). Clarify the gap to Theorem 3’s lower bound.

5) Evidence against same-cluster queries. Figure 2 compares AND achievability to a lower bound for XOR, not to the best known XOR-based algorithms under the same budget. Without an actual XOR algorithmic curve (e.g., ML/LP decoders on the same random graph, or the best-known nonadaptive design), the claim of AND superiority is not empirically supported. Include budget-matched comparisons (and explain any crossing behavior) or temper the claim.

6) Algorithm 1 depends on p (or cluster sizes via Theorem 6). Practical estimation is not discussed in the main text. Please describe how to estimate p (or ni) from a small pilot or from the observed query answers themselves, and analyze sensitivity to misspecification. The pseudocode is terse; add a short derivation of thresholds Ci, a sketch proof showing the expectation gap that drives the Chernoff bound, and runtime/degree choices.

7) Experiments are limited and omit strong baselines. The movie experiment is small and uses volunteers; Figures 3–5 do not compare to state-of-the-art same-cluster-query methods (e.g., spectral/SDP-based recovery or ML decoding on XOR queries) under the same m and graph. Please add: (i) budget-matched comparisons to “same cluster” on the same graph with tuned decoders; (ii) larger synthetic studies across p, q, k, d; (iii) ablations on degree d and noise; (iv) error bars over multiple trials. If the same-cluster comparison appears only in the supplement, bring it into the main paper and discuss its implications.

Quality:
- The technical ideas are promising, but several proofs are hard to parse and key statements are difficult to interpret quantitatively. Clarifying statements, adding corollaries/plots, and reconciling with prior regimes would improve soundness and readability.

Clarity:
- The manuscript would benefit from a sharper problem statement (precise model and differences vs. prior work), a running comparison table of models/assumptions, intuitive proof sketches before technical appendices, and clearer figures with algorithmic baselines.

Originality:
- The locally encodable source-coding view and the AND-query advocacy for approximate recovery appear novel in this context. The new lower bounds under locality are also original.

Significance:
- If strengthened with clearer proofs and fair comparisons, the work could influence how we design queries for crowdsourced clustering, and spur further development of locally encodable coding theory.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant unifying perspective; new lower bounds under locality
- Simple, nonadaptive AND-based algorithm with favorable scaling
- Insightful critique of XOR queries; preliminary empirical evidence

Cons for acceptance:
- Model restrictions (no repetition), unclear reconciliation with prior results
- Hard-to-interpret bounds and missing algorithmic comparisons
- Limited experiments; dependence on priors not operationalized

Suggestions to improve:
- Justify or relax the non-repetition assumption; analyze repetition as a resource
- Reconcile Theorem 1 with prior literature and simplify its exposition
- Provide clean corollaries and plots for Theorems 3–4; quantify the gap
- Add budget-matched XOR baselines with strong decoders
- Detail prior estimation and robustness for Algorithm 1; improve pseudocode
- Expand experiments with larger synthetic and real datasets, multiple workers, and error models beyond i.i.d. BSC

Overall assessment:
A promising and original paper with strong ideas but important clarity and evaluation gaps. With substantial revisions to theory presentation and experimental comparisons, it could make a valuable contribution.
