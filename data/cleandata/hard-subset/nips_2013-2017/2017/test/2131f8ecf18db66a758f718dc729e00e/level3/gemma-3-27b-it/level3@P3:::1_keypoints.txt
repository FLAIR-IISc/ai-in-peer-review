- Studies clustering with limited binary-answer queries to an oracle, motivated by crowdsourcing and record deduplication.
- Establishes a novel connection between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding using information-theoretic tools.
- Derives upper and lower bounds on query complexity for exact and approximate clustering recovery.
- Proposes AND queries as potentially more efficient than standard 'same cluster' (XOR) queries, especially under noise.
- Explicitly models and analyzes noisy oracles, increasing practical applicability.
- Positions the work within recent query-based clustering and entity resolution literature and critiques prior XOR-based approaches.
- Novel framing of clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem.
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with provable bounds supporting the claims.
- Strong practical motivation tied to real-world applications like crowdsourcing and deduplication.
- Initial experimental results support the AND-query advantage but are limited in scale and diversity.
- Analysis lacks structural assumptions (e.g., hypothesis classes), limiting applicability to specific clustering scenarios.
- Results depend heavily on knowledge of label marginals and noise levels, which are hard to estimate in practice.
- Some proofs, particularly lower bounds, are complex and could be clarified or simplified.
- Limited exploration of alternative query functions beyond XOR and AND.
- Paper is technically sound, well-organized, and generally clearly written, though some mathematical sections need more intuition.
- Original combination of information-theoretic tools with crowdsourcing/entity resolution problems.
- Significant contribution by providing fundamental limits on query complexity and practical insights for query design.
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a relevant problem, offers novel theory, and proposes a practical AND-query improvement.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited scope due to missing structural assumptions, reliance on prior estimation, and modest experimental validation.
- Overall recommendation: a strong, worthy paper recommended for acceptance with suggestions for future work on structural assumptions, prior estimation, and broader experiments.
