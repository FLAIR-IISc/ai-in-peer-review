Summary. The paper connects non-adaptive, small-set (Δ-ary) query-based semisupervised clustering to locally encodable source coding, derives information-theoretic lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery, and proposes AND-queries with an analysis and a small real-data evaluation. This line builds on and contrasts with prior NIPS work on same-cluster queries (e.g., Ashtiani et al.) and on crowdsourcing reliability (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah; Zhou et al.), and relates to locally decodable/smooth compression.

Strengths.
- Conceptual bridge to locally encodable source coding is interesting and potentially influential.
- New lower bounds beyond the classic nH(p) in certain regimes; clear message that XOR/same-cluster queries have inherent rate–distortion limits.
- AND-query scheme with O(n log(1/δ)) scaling for approximate recovery is appealing and practically motivated; simple decoder.
- Initial real-data study suggests reduced error rates for AND vs. same-cluster responses.

Weaknesses and required clarifications.
Quality.
1) Model restriction: Disallowing repeated identical queries is strong. Please justify with empirical evidence beyond anecdotal observations, or analyze (i) no restriction with repeated queries and independent annotators (Dawid–Skene/KOS-type aggregation), and (ii) how majority-vote or more advanced aggregation would interact with your bounds; otherwise the practical value is unclear.
2) Exact recovery conflict: Theorem 1’s query complexity appears inconsistent with Ahn–Lee–Suh in the noiseless exact-recovery regime. Precisely reconcile assumptions (graph/hypergraph model, adaptivity, typical-set expurgation, Δ, sparsity) and provide a clearer, step-by-step proof; currently the expurgation/counting argument is difficult to follow.
3) Theorem 3: The δ lower-bound expression is hard to interpret. Please give explicit scaling laws m as a function of n and target δ (closed-form asymptotics, monotonicity), plots over p and Δ, and a plain-language explanation of the eΔh′ term.
4) Theorem 4: Same interpretability issues; quantify and plot the gap to Theorem 3’s lower bound, and add proof sketches conveying key probabilistic steps (two-hop neighborhood, Poissonization).
5) Experimental baselines: Figure 2 compares to a same-cluster lower bound but omits achievable algorithms; include state-of-the-art same-cluster baselines (e.g., ML/SDP/spectral with non-adaptive designs) to substantiate superiority and explain any crossing behavior.
6) Algorithm 1: Assumes known p or cluster sizes (Theorem 6). Discuss practical estimation (sample complexity, plug-in estimators), robustness to misspecification, and provide pseudocode that is understandable without the appendix; report computational complexity.
7) Figures 3–5: Add comparisons against strong same-cluster schemes on synthetic and real data with identical query budgets; otherwise claims that AND outperforms are not supported.

Originality and significance. The equivalence and some bounds are novel and relevant; with clearer proofs and stronger comparisons, the contribution could be significant. As written, lack of reconciliation with prior exact-recovery results and missing baselines limit confidence.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel theoretical lens and bounds
- Simple, practical AND-query primitive with O(n log(1/δ)) scaling

Cons for acceptance
- Restrictive model, unclear practicality without repeated-query analysis
- Proofs and statements hard to parse; conflict with prior work unresolved
- Empirical evaluation lacks state-of-the-art baselines and parameter estimation discussion

Overall: promising but needs substantial clarification and stronger comparisons.
