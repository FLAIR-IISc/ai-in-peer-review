Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies non-adaptive small-set queries for multiclass labeling/clustering under a probabilistic prior, connecting the problem to locally encodable source coding. It develops information-theoretic lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery when each query touches at most Δ items, and argues that pairwise AND queries can outperform pairwise same-cluster (XOR) queries for approximate recovery. For noisy oracles, the paper proposes a simple, non-adaptive algorithm (Algorithm 1) using pairwise AND-style queries on a d-regular graph and proves it achieves O(n log(k/δ)) queries to recover a (1−δ) fraction of labels. A small crowdsourcing experiment is provided.

This line of work relates to crowdsourced clustering/entity resolution with same-cluster queries (e.g., Ashtiani et al. NIPS’16; Mazumdar–Saha NIPS’17), hypergraph/community recovery (Ahn–Lee–Suh Allerton’16), rate–distortion and local (en/de)codability in information theory (Massey; Montanari–Mossel; Makhdoumi et al.). The locally encodable source-coding viewpoint is a novel and valuable lens.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: the equivalence to locally encodable source coding yields clean lower bounds and clarifies the limits of small-set queries.
- Identification of intrinsic limitations of XOR/same-cluster queries for approximate recovery; AND queries are a simple alternative with provable and empirical benefits.
- Non-adaptive, parallelizable algorithm with a transparent decision rule and O(n log(k/δ)) query complexity under noise.
- Initial real-data experiment and crowd study suggesting AND queries are easier for annotators.

Weaknesses and requested clarifications
1) Model choice: disallowing repeated identical queries
- Disallowing repetitions is restrictive and may hurt performance in practice when annotator noise is i.i.d. or conditionally independent across workers. Please justify this choice more thoroughly, or analyze:
  - the no-restriction model (allowing repeats) to quantify gains from repeated queries and majority/EM aggregation under a standard Dawid–Skene or KOS model; and/or
  - an “independent annotators” setting with bounded cost per item, contrasting optimal allocations across query types.
- The paper cites cases where majority can hurt, but that pertains to aggregation quality, not the value of repetition in principle. Please clarify when repetition is provably unhelpful and provide citations or counterexamples where it helps.

2) Theorem 1 vs prior work; proof clarity
- There appears to be a discrepancy with Ahn–Lee–Suh (and related connectivity-style arguments) in the noiseless, non-adaptive, pairwise regime. Your formula yields m≈n for p=1/2 (and m<n for imbalanced priors) with ML decoding over a random XOR-design, whereas graph-connectivity arguments for same-cluster queries suggest Θ(n log n) edges for exact recovery in worst-case/agnostic settings.
- Please reconcile: Is your guarantee average-case over the i.i.d. prior (with expurgation of a vanishing fraction of sequences) vs worst-case labels? Is your decoder truly using only same-cluster (XOR) answers? What role does knowing p play?
- The proof is hard to follow. A self-contained outline with explicit assumptions, a clean typical-set/expurgation argument, and a discussion of computational tractability of ML would help.

3) Theorem 3 interpretability
- The δ lower bound δ ≥ δ̃(m/n) + [h(p)−h(δ̃(m/n))]/[h′(δ̃(m/n))(1+e^{Δ h′(⋅)})] is opaque. Please provide:
  - Simplified corollaries in common regimes (e.g., Δ=2, p=1/2; m=θ(n); small δ).
  - Plots of δ vs m/n for several Δ and p, and explicit statements on how δ shrinks as Δ or m increase.
  - A back-of-the-envelope interpretation bridging standard rate–distortion with the locality penalty.

4) Theorem 4 upper bound; gap to Theorem 3 and proof readability
- The expression for δ under AND queries is difficult to parse. Provide simplified asymptotics (e.g., δ ≈ p e^{−2m/n} plus lower-order terms) and uniform bounds.
- Quantify the gap between Thm 3’s lower bound and Thm 4’s upper bound across regimes to show how close the scheme is to optimal. A figure overlaying both would help.
- The proof uses Poissonization and inclusion–exclusion; a clearer sketch and an appendix with step-by-step bounds (and approximations used) would improve readability.

5) Figure 2 comparisons
- The figure contrasts your AND-achievability with the same-cluster lower bound only. This does not substantiate superiority. Please add:
  - An explicit same-cluster algorithmic curve (e.g., ML over XOR responses on the same graph design, or best-known practical decoders).
  - Explain any crossing behavior and ensure axes/legends make the comparison unambiguous.

6) Algorithm 1’s parameter knowledge and pseudocode clarity
- The algorithm (and Theorem 6) require p or {n_i}. Discuss how to estimate these in practice, with guarantees (e.g., small pilot labeling O(log n), method-of-moments/EM from counts N_{u,i}, robustness to mis-specification).
- The thresholds C_i need intuition and sensitivity analysis. Provide ablations showing performance vs threshold misspecification.
- The pseudocode is terse; define all variables, give time/memory complexity, and summarize the proof idea to make it reproducible without deep appendix diving.

7) Experimental comparisons (Figs 3–5)
- There is no comparison against state-of-the-art same-cluster schemes (e.g., spectral clustering on a query graph; maximum-likelihood under XOR; adaptive baselines). Claims that AND-queries outperform existing schemes are thus unsubstantiated.
- Please add strong baselines, vary q, p, δ systematically, and report confidence intervals. On the real dataset, include same-cluster results and other multi-item query primitives (e.g., triangles) if feasible.

Additional comments
- Clarify with evidence whether repeating the same question is unhelpful in practice under reasonable worker models; otherwise soften the claim.
- Expand and make explicit the comparison to same-cluster methods throughout the paper, including in statements of theorems and in experiments.

Quality: Technically interesting with several nontrivial bounds and a simple algorithm, but key proofs are hard to follow, interpretability is limited, and experimental validation lacks strong baselines and parameter-estimation discussion.

Clarity: The paper is dense. Strengthen theorem statements, add intuition and simplified corollaries, and improve figures and pseudocode.

Originality: High. The locally encodable source-coding view and AND-query advocacy are novel.

Significance: Potentially high, but currently undercut by model restrictions, lack of baselines, and unclear gaps to lower bounds.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and timely theoretical perspective with new bounds.
- Clear identification of limitations of XOR queries and a simple alternative with theory and some empirical support.
- Non-adaptive, parallel scheme with favorable scaling.

Arguments against acceptance
- Confusion around exact-recovery query complexity vs prior work; proofs and assumptions need tightening.
- Key bounds (Thm 3–4) are difficult to interpret; gap to lower bounds unclear.
- Experiments do not compare to state-of-the-art same-cluster baselines; practical issues (parameter estimation, repetition) are not addressed.

Overall: Promising paper with substantial contributions but needs significant revisions to clarify assumptions, reconcile with prior work, sharpen interpretation, and strengthen empirical comparisons.
