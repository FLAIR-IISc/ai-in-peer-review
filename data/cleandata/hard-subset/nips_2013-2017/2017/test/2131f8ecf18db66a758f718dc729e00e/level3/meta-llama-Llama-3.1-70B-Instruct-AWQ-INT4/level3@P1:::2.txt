This paper proposes a novel approach to semisupervised clustering by casting it as a locally encodable source coding problem. The authors provide lower bounds on the number of queries required to recover the correct labeling and propose a pairwise AND querying scheme that provably performs better in many situations. The paper also presents a real crowdsourcing experiment to validate the algorithm.

The main ideas of the paper are related to previous work on semisupervised clustering, crowdsourcing, and information theory. The authors build upon recent approaches to clustering via crowdsourcing, where pairwise queries are made to an oracle to recover the labeling of elements. The paper also draws connections to information theory, specifically locally encodable source coding, to establish lower bounds on the number of queries required.

The strengths of the paper include its novel approach to semisupervised clustering, its theoretical contributions, and its experimental validation. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem and establish new lower bounds on the number of queries required. The proposed AND querying scheme is also shown to be effective in practice.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The presentation and clarity of the paper could be improved. Some of the theorems and proofs are difficult to follow, and the authors could benefit from providing more intuitive explanations and examples. Additionally, the paper could benefit from more comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, particularly in the experimental evaluation.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel approach to semisupervised clustering and establishes new lower bounds on the number of queries required. The proposed AND querying scheme is also a new contribution. However, the paper builds upon previous work in crowdsourcing and information theory, and the authors could benefit from more clearly highlighting the novelty of their contributions.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in semisupervised clustering and provides a novel solution. The results have implications for crowdsourcing and information theory, and the paper could be of interest to researchers in these fields. However, the paper could benefit from more discussion of the practical implications of the results and how they could be applied in real-world scenarios.

Overall, the paper is a good scientific contribution, but it could benefit from improvements in presentation, clarity, and comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
