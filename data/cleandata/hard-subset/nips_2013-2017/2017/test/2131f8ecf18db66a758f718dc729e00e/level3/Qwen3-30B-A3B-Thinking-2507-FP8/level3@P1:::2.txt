

This paper explores the connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, proposing an AND-query approach to improve upon traditional "same cluster" queries for non-adaptive clustering with noisy oracle responses. The authors establish theoretical lower bounds and present an algorithm (Algorithm 1) that achieves (1-δ)-good recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries for k clusters. They validate their approach with a real crowdsourcing experiment on movie classification.

The paper's main contribution lies in the theoretical connection between clustering and source coding, which is novel and well-motivated. The AND-query approach represents a meaningful departure from the "same cluster" query paradigm, with the authors providing compelling theoretical justification for why AND queries outperform XOR-based queries in certain regimes. The real-world experiment on movie classification provides valuable empirical validation of the theoretical claims, demonstrating that AND queries yield significantly fewer errors (105 vs. 234) than "same cluster" queries in the same crowdsourcing setup.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical depth, particularly in establishing the connection to source coding and deriving information-theoretic lower bounds. The authors correctly identify that "same cluster" queries (XOR) are fundamentally limited for approximate recovery, and their AND-query approach provides a more efficient alternative. The theoretical results for noisy queries (Theorem 5) are particularly significant for practical applications.

However, several critical issues undermine the paper's contribution. The most serious concern is the restrictive model assumption that prevents repeating identical queries. The authors claim this is justified by "crowd workers can compute simple functions" and "it's desirable to keep the number of elements involved in a query as small as possible," but this fails to address the practical reality that repeated queries are a standard technique for error reduction in crowdsourcing. The authors should either provide evidence supporting their assumption (e.g., from prior crowdsourcing studies) or analyze the case where query repetition is allowed, which would be more relevant to practice.

The paper contains theoretical inconsistencies that weaken its credibility. Theorem 1 claims a query complexity of n(h(p)+o(1))/log(1/α) for exact recovery with pairwise queries, but this conflicts with prior work (Ahn-Lee-Suh, 2016) that establishes Θ(n log n) query complexity for exact recovery with pairwise queries. The proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow and fails to resolve this discrepancy, raising questions about the paper's technical soundness.

The interpretability of key theoretical results is poor. Theorem 3's lower bound on δ for (1-δ)-good recovery (δ ≥ δ̃(m/n) + [h(p)-h(δ̃(m/n))]/h'(δ̃(m/n))(1+e^{Δh'(δ̃(m/n))})) is mathematically complex and lacks intuitive explanation. Theorem 4's upper bound for AND queries suffers from the same interpretability issues, and the gap between the two bounds is not clearly established. Figure 2, which compares the AND-query scheme to the "same cluster" lower bound, fails to include the performance of actual "same cluster" query algorithms, making the claimed superiority of AND queries unsubstantiated.

The practical implementation of Algorithm 1 requires knowledge of the prior distribution p or cluster sizes, which is rarely known in real applications. While Theorem 6 claims robustness to estimation of cluster sizes, the paper does not address how these parameters would be practically estimated from data, nor does it provide a clear method for doing so. The pseudocode for Algorithm 1 is also difficult to follow without extensive supporting explanations.

The experimental validation (Figures 3-5) fails to compare against state-of-the-art methods, particularly the "same cluster" query approach. Without such comparisons, the claim that AND queries "perform substantially better" is not empirically supported. The paper shows AND queries have fewer errors in their crowdsourcing experiment (105 vs. 234), but this doesn't demonstrate that AND queries outperform the best possible implementation of "same cluster" queries (which might use error-correcting techniques).

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically interesting connection between clustering and source coding, with some novel insights about query types. However, the restrictive model assumption, theoretical inconsistencies, poor interpretability of key results, and lack of meaningful comparisons to existing methods significantly undermine the contribution. The practical relevance is weakened by the unaddressed issue of estimating cluster sizes and the failure to consider query repetition.

Strengths: Novel theoretical connection, interesting AND-query approach, real-world validation.
Weaknesses: Restrictive model assumption, theoretical inconsistencies, poor interpretability, lack of comparisons to existing methods, insufficient practical implementation details.

For acceptance, the paper would require significant revision to address the model assumption, clarify theoretical results, provide meaningful comparisons to existing methods, and better explain practical implementation details. As it stands, the theoretical contribution is partially undermined by the restrictive model and inconsistent results, while the practical contribution is limited by the lack of comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
