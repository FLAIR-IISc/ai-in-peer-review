

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper connects semisupervised clustering with locally encodable source coding, establishing theoretical bounds and proposing a new AND-query approach for clustering with noisy, non-adaptive queries. The authors relate the problem of learning cluster labels to information theory's locally encodable source coding, showing that pairwise "same cluster" queries (XOR) have fundamental limitations, while AND queries can achieve better query complexity for approximate recovery. This work builds on recent NIPS contributions in clustering with side information (e.g., Ashtiani et al. 2016, Mazumdar & Saha 2017) and extends the theoretical analysis of query-based clustering.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a novel theoretical connection between clustering and source coding, providing interesting lower bounds and a practical AND-query algorithm. The real crowdsourcing experiment validates the theoretical claims, demonstrating that AND queries yield lower error rates than "same cluster" queries (105 vs. 234 errors in the experiment). The paper's theoretical contributions to locally encodable source coding (a relatively unexplored area) represent a significant advance in information theory.

**Weaknesses:** Several critical issues undermine the paper's contribution:

1. **Theoretical Conflicts:** Theorem 1's claimed query complexity for exact recovery conflicts with prior work (Ahn-Lee-Suh 2016), which showed O(n log n) queries are necessary for pairwise queries. The proof's reliance on expurgation with "typical sets" is unclear and contradicts established results.

2. **Interpretability Issues:** Theorem 3's lower bound for approximate recovery (δ ≥ ...) is mathematically complex without clear scaling behavior (how m scales with n and δ). Theorem 4's upper bound for AND queries suffers from the same interpretability problems, making it difficult to assess the gap between the bounds.

3. **Experimental Shortcomings:** Figures 3-5 fail to compare AND queries with state-of-the-art methods (e.g., "same cluster" query algorithms), making the claimed superiority unsubstantiated. Figure 2 only compares to the lower bound of "same cluster" queries, not to actual algorithms.

4. **Practical Assumptions:** Algorithm 1 requires precise knowledge of cluster sizes (p) or prior probabilities, which is unrealistic in practice. The paper doesn't address how to estimate these parameters from data, nor does it analyze the sensitivity to parameter estimation errors.

5. **Restrictive Model:** The paper's assumption that repeated queries are disallowed lacks justification. In crowdsourcing, repeating the same question multiple times with different workers is common practice to reduce errors, and the authors don't analyze this more practical setting.

**Quality:** The paper contains interesting theoretical insights but suffers from technical inconsistencies and unclear exposition. The theoretical results are not properly contextualized within existing work, and the claims about query complexity require stronger justification.

**Clarity:** The paper's presentation is often confusing, particularly regarding the relationship between the theoretical results and the proposed algorithms. The pseudocode for Algorithm 1 is hard to follow without extensive background, and key assumptions (like the disallowed repeated queries) are not adequately defended.

**Originality:** The connection to locally encodable source coding is novel, and the AND-query approach represents a creative departure from standard "same cluster" queries. However, the practical significance is diminished by the unresolved theoretical and experimental issues.

**Significance:** While the theoretical framework is promising, the paper's significance is limited by the lack of proper comparison to existing methods and the restrictive model assumptions. The real-world validation is valuable but insufficient to overcome the theoretical shortcomings.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:** 
- Novel theoretical connection between clustering and source coding
- Interesting AND-query approach with lower error rates in real crowdsourcing
- Validated with real-world experiment

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Theoretical results conflict with established work
- Critical interpretability issues in key theorems
- Experimental results lack comparison to state-of-the-art methods
- Unjustified restrictive assumption about query repetition
- Algorithm requires impractical prior knowledge

This paper has promising theoretical insights but requires significant revision to address the technical inconsistencies, clarify theoretical results, and properly compare with existing methods. The authors should justify their restrictive query repetition assumption, resolve the conflict with prior work, and include comparisons to actual algorithms (not just lower bounds) in their experiments. Without these improvements, the paper's contribution is significantly diminished. I recommend rejection due to unresolved theoretical inconsistencies and insufficient experimental validation.
