

This paper establishes a novel theoretical connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, providing fundamental information-theoretic insights into query complexity for clustering problems. The authors model the clustering task as a query-based learning problem where an oracle provides answers to queries about whether elements belong to the same cluster (or more generally, about functions of labels), with the goal of minimizing the number of queries required to recover the clustering. The key insight is that "same cluster" (XOR) queries have inherent limitations, and the authors propose AND queries as a superior alternative that requires significantly fewer queries for approximate recovery.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes the equivalence between clustering and locally encodable source coding, allowing the application of information-theoretic tools to derive novel lower bounds on query complexity. For exact recovery with noiseless queries, the authors provide a querying scheme that uses fewer than n queries for certain label distributions, and establish a tighter lower bound than the naive entropy bound. For approximate recovery, they prove that "same cluster" queries are fundamentally inefficient, and demonstrate that AND queries achieve substantially better query-performance trade-offs. The paper also provides a comprehensive analysis for noisy queries, showing that with AND queries, (1-δ)-approximate recovery can be achieved with O(n log(k/δ)) queries, which is near-optimal.

The theoretical results are well-supported by rigorous proofs, including a clever application of rate-distortion theory and Chernoff bounds. The authors also provide empirical validation through a real crowdsourcing experiment on movie classification, where AND queries yielded significantly fewer errors (105 vs. 234) than "same cluster" queries, validating their theoretical claims. The experimental setup is well-designed and properly controls for factors like worker fatigue and response time.

The paper excels in originality: the connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding is novel within machine learning, though it draws on information theory concepts (citing [23]). The authors' observation that AND queries outperform XOR-based "same cluster" queries for approximate recovery is particularly insightful and addresses a practical limitation in crowdsourcing applications. The work builds on recent NIPS papers in clustering with queries [4, 21, 22] while making a distinct theoretical contribution.

The significance of this work is clear for applications like entity resolution and crowdsourcing, where query costs are high. The paper provides fundamental limits for query-based clustering, which can guide the design of more efficient algorithms. The proposed AND query method offers a practical improvement that could reduce costs in real-world applications.

The paper is well-written with clear organization, though the information-theoretic background might be challenging for some machine learning readers. The key contributions are well-motivated and the technical results are presented with sufficient detail. The authors properly contextualize their work within existing literature, including recent NIPS papers on clustering with queries.

Potential weaknesses include the focus on binary labels (k=2) in many theoretical results, though the paper does extend to general k. The analysis assumes a fixed Δ (number of elements per query), which might not capture all practical scenarios, though the authors justify this as a natural constraint for crowdsourcing applications. The theoretical results depend on knowing the prior distribution of labels, which might not always be available, though the authors provide a practical solution using cluster size ratios.

The paper's strength lies in its elegant theoretical framework and its successful bridging of information theory and clustering. The real-world validation of the AND query approach demonstrates the practical relevance of the theoretical insights. This work represents a valuable first step toward understanding the information-theoretic limits of query-based clustering and provides a practical alternative to existing methods.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of query complexity in clustering. The paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and offers both theoretical insights and practical improvements. The connection to source coding is novel and insightful, and the AND query approach represents a significant improvement over existing methods. The empirical validation further strengthens the claims. This paper makes a clear contribution to the field and would be of interest to researchers working on clustering, crowdsourcing, and information theory.
