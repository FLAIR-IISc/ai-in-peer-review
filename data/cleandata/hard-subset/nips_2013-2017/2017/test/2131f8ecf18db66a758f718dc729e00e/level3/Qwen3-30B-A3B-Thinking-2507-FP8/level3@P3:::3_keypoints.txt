- Novel connection established between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding in information theory.
- Clustering via crowdsourced similarity queries is cast as a locally encodable source coding problem where each query depends on a constant number of labels.
- Derives tight information-theoretic bounds on query complexity for various scenarios.
- Shows pairwise "same cluster" (XOR) queries have fundamental limitations for approximate recovery.
- Proposes AND queries as a more efficient alternative and proves they need significantly fewer queries (Theorems 3–4).
- Equivalence to locally encodable source coding yields new information-theoretic results for an under-studied problem class.
- Develops practical algorithms for noisy query settings with theoretical guarantees (Theorem 5).
- Validates theoretical predictions with simulations and a real crowdsourcing experiment (movie classification).
- Paper is well-written, accessible, and clearly explains the information-theoretic connections.
- Strong empirical support and proper contextualization relative to prior crowdsourced clustering work.
- Weakness: AND queries may reveal labels — the paper should clarify privacy/label-revelation concerns and why not ask labels directly.
- Weakness: Expressions for bounds in Theorems 1–4 are somewhat complex and could be simplified for readability.
- Weakness: Experiments appear to use only AND queries; the paper should explicitly justify this experimental choice.
- Recommendation: Accept — significant theoretical contribution with practical implications and empirical validation.
