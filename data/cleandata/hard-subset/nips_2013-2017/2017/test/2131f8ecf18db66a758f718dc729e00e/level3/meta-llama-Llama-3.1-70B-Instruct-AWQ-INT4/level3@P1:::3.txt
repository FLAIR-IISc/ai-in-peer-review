This paper proposes a novel approach to semisupervised clustering by leveraging the concept of locally encodable source coding. The authors establish an equivalence between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, which enables the derivation of information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required for clustering. The paper provides several key contributions, including a querying scheme that achieves near-optimal performance for exact recovery, a new lower bound on the number of queries required for approximate recovery, and an algorithm that performs well in the presence of noisy queries.

The paper is well-written and accessible to a general machine learning audience. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the information-theoretic connection and deriving the bounds in a clear and concise manner. The experimental results are also well-presented and provide a good validation of the theoretical results.

One potential concern is that the use of AND queries may reveal the cluster label, which could conflict with the motivation for using crowdsourcing. However, the authors address this concern by noting that asking for labels directly would require at least n queries, whereas their approach can achieve good performance with fewer queries.

The reviewer requests that the authors simplify the expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1-4 while preserving their essence. This would make the paper even more accessible to a broader audience.

The experiments appear to use only AND queries, which raises some validity concerns. However, the authors' responses to these concerns are satisfactory, and the reviewer recommends acceptance of the paper.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The paper is also well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise.

The originality of the paper is high, as it proposes a novel approach to semisupervised clustering and establishes a new connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding. The significance of the paper is also high, as it provides new insights into the fundamental limits of clustering and proposes a practical algorithm for crowdsourced clustering.

Overall, the reviewer recommends acceptance of the paper, citing its technical soundness, originality, and significance.
