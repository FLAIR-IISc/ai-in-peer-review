Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies query-based clustering/labeling where an oracle answers simple functions of a small number (Δ) of items’ labels. The central questions are: (i) how many nonadaptive queries suffice to exactly or approximately recover the ground-truth labels, and (ii) what are the intrinsic information-theoretic limits, especially under noisy answers? The authors establish an equivalence between this semisupervised clustering model and locally encodable source coding, enabling lower bounds and algorithms that are new to the ML community.

For k = 2, they analyze pairwise “same-cluster” (XOR) queries and show that, leveraging the prior p of labels, one can exactly recover with strictly fewer than n queries in certain ranges of p, and provide a stronger lower bound than the classic nH(p) for some regimes. For approximate recovery, they prove general lower bounds under locality constraints and show that XOR-based schemes are fundamentally limited (via classical results on linear rate–distortion codes), while a different primitive—pairwise AND queries—achieves substantially better query–accuracy tradeoffs. For noisy oracles, they design a simple nonadaptive algorithm based on pairwise AND-like queries generalized to k classes, achieving (1−δ)-accurate recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries, and they validate the approach in a small crowdsourcing experiment.

The work connects to: Ashtiani et al. (NIPS’16) on clustering with same-cluster queries; crowd labeling/coding-theoretic models (Karger–Oh–Shah; Vempaty et al.); hyperedge/triangle queries (Vinayak & Hassibi); and information-theoretic notions of locally decodable/compressible codes and smooth compression. The locally encodable source coding viewpoint and the explicit use of Massey/Ancheta bounds to critique XOR queries are particularly novel in this community.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: clean equivalence to locally encodable source coding; imports powerful information-theoretic tools to ML/crowdsourcing clustering.
- Technical contributions: new lower bounds under locality and for exact/approximate recovery; identification of inherent limitations of XOR “same-cluster” queries; constructive upper bounds and simple algorithms.
- Practical insight: AND queries can be easier and more reliable for workers than “same-cluster” questions; empirical study supports lower error rates and the predicted O(n log(k/δ)) scaling.
- Clear motivation: record linkage/entity resolution and crowdsourcing settings where parallel, nonadaptive, small-arity simple queries are desirable.

Weaknesses
- Modeling scope: analysis assumes i.i.d. labels with known (or estimated) marginals and independent query noise; no structural/hypothesis-class assumptions or geometric/similarity information are used, limiting applicability to many ML clustering regimes.
- Query model deviations: for k > 2, the main noisy result uses k-ary answers, diverging from the paper’s emphasis on binary answers; discussion of trade-offs here is brief.
- Limited adaptivity: only nonadaptive designs are considered; no comparison to adaptive strategies that could significantly reduce queries.
- Tightness and coverage: lower bounds are not tight in all regimes; comparisons with other primitives (e.g., triangle/hyperedge queries, OR/majority functions) are limited.
- Empirical validation: small-scale, single-domain experiment; worker error independence and “no repetition” assumptions may not hold in real platforms where the same worker answers many queries.

Quality
The theoretical results are soundly developed, with clear reductions to coding problems, careful use of typicality/expurgation for exact recovery, and Chernoff-type analyses for the noisy approximate algorithm. The AND-based decoder is simple and computationally efficient; the XOR-based exact-recovery decoder reduces to solving linear systems modulo 2 and is practical. Proof sketches are provided; detailed proofs are deferred to the appendix. Experiments are limited but aligned with the theory and include a direct comparison showing higher error for same-cluster queries.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable. The separation between noiseless/noisy and exact/approximate regimes is clear, and the role of Δ-locality is explicit. Some presentation could be streamlined (e.g., more intuition around the rate–distortion lower bound with locality; clearer statements about the regimes where m < n is achievable). A short “practical guidance” paragraph summarizing how to set d and thresholds as a function of p, q, δ, k would help practitioners.

Originality
High. The locally encodable source coding formulation and its consequences for query complexity in clustering appear novel in ML. Demonstrating that AND queries beat XOR in approximate recovery, with both information-theoretic justification and an implementable scheme, is a fresh and compelling insight.

Significance
Moderate to high. The paper provides a first-principles baseline for the information-theoretic limits of query-based clustering without structural assumptions, and it opens a new line connecting local encodability to interactive labeling. While the assumptions limit direct applicability to geometric clustering, the results should influence the design of efficient crowdsourcing protocols and stimulate further work on tighter bounds, richer noise models, and adaptive strategies.

Constructive suggestions
- Explore adaptive variants and whether AND queries retain their advantage; quantify adaptive gains vs nonadaptive.
- Extend to more realistic worker models (heterogeneous reliabilities, correlated errors, abstentions) and show robustness of thresholds.
- Compare systematically against other small-arity primitives (OR, majority-of-Δ, triangle queries) under identical noise budgets.
- Clarify the practical consequences of moving from binary to k-ary answers for k > 2; can binary reductions approximate the k-ary scheme with similar guarantees?
- Discuss estimation of p in practice and sensitivity to misspecification; experiments with unknown p would strengthen the empirical section.
- Where possible, tighten lower bounds and delineate regimes of optimality.

Arguments for acceptance
- New conceptual bridge and techniques; rigorous bounds; simple algorithm with compelling insight (AND vs XOR).
- Addresses a timely topic in crowdsourcing/entity resolution; provides clean, parallelizable protocols with noise robustness.

Arguments against acceptance
- Modeling assumptions (i.i.d. labels, independent noise, nonadaptive only) limit generality.
- Empirical evaluation is small-scale; limited comparison to alternative query types and adaptive baselines.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is Algorithm 1 to misspecified p and q? Can thresholds be set adaptively from data?
- Can you provide bounds for binary-output schemes for k > 2 that approach the k-ary O(n log(k/δ)) rate?
- Do your lower bounds extend to worker models with adversarial or class-dependent noise?
- How do results change if limited repetitions are allowed with diverse workers (not identical queries to the same worker)?

Overall assessment
A thoughtful, well-executed first step toward understanding information-theoretic limits and lower bounds for query-based clustering. Despite scope limitations, the paper offers clear contributions and practical insights likely to influence subsequent work. I lean toward acceptance.
