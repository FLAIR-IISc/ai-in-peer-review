This paper explores the connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, providing theoretical results on the query complexity of clustering with noisy oracle answers. The authors establish lower bounds and propose an algorithm utilizing AND-queries, demonstrating improved performance compared to traditional ‘same cluster’ queries, validated with a crowdsourcing experiment. While the core ideas are interesting and potentially impactful, the presentation suffers from clarity issues, lacks sufficient comparison to related work, and contains potentially conflicting claims.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The paper draws a parallel between learning cluster labels via queries to an oracle and the information-theoretic problem of locally encodable source coding. This connection allows the authors to derive lower bounds on the number of queries needed for accurate clustering, particularly when the oracle responses are noisy. They argue that traditional ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries are suboptimal and introduce AND-queries as a more efficient alternative. The work includes theoretical analysis and a small-scale crowdsourcing experiment to support these findings.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Connection:** The linking of semisupervised clustering with locally encodable source coding is a novel and potentially insightful perspective. This connection provides a powerful framework for analyzing query complexity using tools from information theory.
* **Theoretical Contributions:** The paper presents lower bounds on query complexity, providing a fundamental limit on achievable performance. The proposed AND-query scheme offers a practical alternative to existing methods.
* **Real-World Validation:** The inclusion of a crowdsourcing experiment, even with limited scale, lends some support to the theoretical claims and demonstrates the potential applicability of the approach.
* **Exploration of Noisy Oracle:** Considering the scenario of noisy oracle answers is realistic and enhances the practical relevance of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from a lack of clarity, particularly in the presentation of the theoretical results. The notations are dense, and the proofs are often difficult to follow without substantial background knowledge.  The extensive use of theorems without sufficient intuitive explanation hinders understanding.
* **Comparison to Related Work:** The comparison to existing work is insufficient. Specifically, Figure 2 only compares the proposed lower bound to *itself*, not to the performance of a standard ‘same cluster’ query algorithm. This makes it difficult to assess the practical benefit of the proposed approach. The claim of outperforming existing methods lacks concrete evidence. Theorem 1’s claim seems to conflict with recent work by Ahn, Lee, and Suh, but this discrepancy isn't addressed.
* **Model Restriction and Justification:** The authors’ restriction to non-repeated queries feels arbitrary and potentially detrimental. The justification provided – avoiding theoretical interest in the repeated-query case – is weak. Analyzing the performance under relaxed conditions (allowing repetitions or considering an independent annotator setting) would strengthen the work.
* **Interpretability of Lower Bounds:** Theorems 3 and 4 present complex expressions for the lower bound on error probability (δ). It's not immediately clear how these bounds scale with the number of queries (m) and the dataset size (n), or how a relaxation in δ translates to improved performance.
* **Prior Knowledge Assumption:** Algorithm 1 relies on prior knowledge of the cluster size distribution (p or ni). This is a significant limitation, as accurate estimation of these parameters is often challenging in practice. The paper doesn’t discuss strategies for estimating these priors or the impact of inaccurate estimates.
* **Experimental Setup:** The crowdsourcing experiment is limited in scale. Comparing the AND-query approach to established methods (e.g., spectral clustering with ‘same cluster’ queries) would provide a stronger validation of the proposed algorithm. The lack of baseline comparison weakens the conclusions drawn from the experiment.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel connection between clustering and source coding, potentially opening new avenues for research.
* The theoretical results provide valuable insights into the fundamental limits of query complexity in semisupervised clustering.
* The real-world experiment, while limited, provides some empirical support for the proposed approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of clarity and the difficult-to-follow proofs significantly hinder understanding.
* The insufficient comparison to related work weakens the claims of improved performance.
* The restrictive model assumption (no repeated queries) and reliance on prior knowledge limit the practical applicability of the work.
* The interpretability of the theoretical bounds is poor, making it difficult to assess their practical significance.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting idea with some solid theoretical contributions. However, the significant clarity issues, lack of comprehensive comparison to existing work, and restrictive model assumptions make it difficult to recommend acceptance in its current form. I believe a major revision, addressing the weaknesses outlined above, is necessary before this work could be considered suitable for NIPS. Specifically, the authors need to: 1) clarify the presentation and proofs; 2) provide a thorough comparison to relevant baselines; 3) justify or relax the no-repetition constraint; and 4) address the practical challenges of prior knowledge estimation.



**Confidence:** 3/5 (Potentially good work, but requires significant revision)
