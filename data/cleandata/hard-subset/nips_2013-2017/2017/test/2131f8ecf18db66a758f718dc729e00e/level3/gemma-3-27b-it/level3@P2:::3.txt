This paper investigates the connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, providing theoretical bounds on the number of queries required to accurately cluster data points using a crowdsourced oracle that may provide noisy answers. The authors establish a compelling link between this problem and information theory, deriving lower bounds and proposing an AND-query strategy that demonstrably outperforms traditional “same cluster” (XOR) queries, particularly in the approximate recovery setting. The work is well-written and presented, and the theoretical contributions appear sound.

The problem of clustering with limited, potentially noisy, pairwise comparisons is a relevant and increasingly important one, particularly with the rise of crowdsourcing platforms. Previous NIPS work has explored clustering using various approaches, including spectral clustering (Ng et al., 2002) and techniques leveraging side information (Hajek et al., 2016). More recent NIPS publications have focused specifically on the challenges of querying oracles for similarity judgments (Ashtiani et al., 2017; Mazumdar et al., 2017), setting the stage for the current paper’s theoretical investigation. The connection drawn to locally encodable source coding is a novel and insightful framing of the problem.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The equivalence to locally encodable source coding provides a fresh perspective on the clustering problem and allows the authors to leverage tools from information theory.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a series of theorems with clear proofs (or references to proofs in supplementary material) establishing bounds on query complexity.
*   **Practical Implications:** The proposed AND-query strategy is a concrete and potentially valuable contribution to the design of efficient crowdsourcing schemes.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, even for readers not deeply familiar with information theory. The motivation for the work and the significance of the results are clearly articulated.
*   **Empirical Validation:** While primarily theoretical, the experiments on real crowdsourced datasets provide valuable support for the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **AND Query Motivation:** A potential point of confusion arises regarding the motivation behind the AND-query strategy. If the goal is to infer cluster membership from a crowd, revealing partial label information (as the AND query does) seems counterintuitive. Why not directly ask for the label? The paper touches on this in the introduction (cost of queries, simplicity of answers), but a more in-depth discussion of the trade-offs would be beneficial.
*   **Bound Complexity:** The expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1-4 are somewhat complex and difficult to parse quickly. While mathematically precise, simplifying these expressions (perhaps focusing on asymptotic behavior or illustrative examples) could improve readability.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The experiments appear to focus almost exclusively on the AND-query strategy, without a direct comparison to a baseline using "same cluster" queries in the same experimental setup. This raises questions about the relative performance improvement in a real-world scenario.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a relevant problem in machine learning.
*   The connection to locally encodable source coding is a significant contribution, opening up new avenues for research.
*   The AND-query strategy is a promising practical improvement over existing methods.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The motivation for the AND-query strategy could be more clearly articulated.
*   The complexity of the bounds may hinder their practical application.
*   The experimental validation could be strengthened with a more comprehensive comparison to baseline methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution to the field of clustering and crowdsourcing. The authors have successfully established a novel connection between two seemingly disparate areas, and the proposed AND-query strategy offers a promising avenue for improving the efficiency of crowdsourced clustering. While the concerns regarding the AND query motivation and the complexity of the bounds are valid, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. 

I recommend acceptance with the following minor suggestions:

1.  **Expand the discussion on the motivation for AND queries.** Clearly articulate the trade-offs between directly querying for labels versus using AND queries.
2.  **Simplify the expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1-4** where possible, focusing on conveying the essential scaling behavior.
3.  **Include a direct comparison of the AND-query strategy with the "same cluster" query strategy in the experimental setup.** This would strengthen the empirical validation and provide a more compelling demonstration of the proposed approach’s benefits.
