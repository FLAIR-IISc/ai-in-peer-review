## Review of “Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding”

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering using a semi-supervised approach where an oracle provides binary answers to pairwise queries about cluster membership. The authors establish a connection between this problem and locally encodable source coding, leveraging information-theoretic tools to derive upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required for perfect or approximate recovery of the clustering, even in the presence of noisy oracles.

The work builds on recent interest in query-based clustering, particularly in areas like crowdsourcing and record deduplication (citing relevant work by Ashtiani et al., Firmani et al., and others). It positions itself within a growing body of research exploring the trade-offs between query cost and clustering accuracy (e.g., Karger et al., Lahouti & Hassibi). The novel contribution lies in framing the problem through the lens of locally encodable source coding, a relatively unexplored area within the information theory community (citing Massey and related works). This perspective allows the authors to apply information-theoretic analyses to establish new bounds.

**Strengths:** The paper is technically sound and presents a clear, well-motivated problem. The connection to locally encodable source coding is insightful and provides a novel theoretical framework. The derivation of both upper and lower bounds is a significant achievement, and the analysis extends to the challenging scenario of noisy oracles. The authors demonstrate the potential benefits of using AND queries instead of standard “same cluster” queries. The inclusion of a real crowdsourcing experiment, though limited, adds practical validation to the theoretical findings. 

**Weaknesses:** A key limitation, as acknowledged by the authors, is the lack of structural assumptions on the underlying clustering. This restricts the scope of the analysis and may not fully capture the characteristics of real-world datasets. While the paper presents theoretical bounds, the practical implications and scalability of the proposed algorithms for large-scale clustering are not thoroughly investigated. The dependence of the bounds on the marginal distribution of labels and noise level could be challenging to estimate in practice. The experimental validation is limited; a more extensive evaluation with varied datasets and parameter settings would strengthen the results. 

**Overall:** This paper represents a valuable first step towards a deeper understanding of the information-theoretic limits of query-based clustering. While the lack of structural assumptions and limited experimental validation temper its immediate practical impact, the theoretical insights and novel approach are significant.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
* Novel application of information-theoretic tools to a relevant clustering problem.
* Derivation of meaningful upper and lower bounds on query complexity.
* Insightful analysis of the limitations of “same cluster” queries and the advantages of AND queries.
* Theoretical results are supported by a preliminary experimental evaluation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
* Limited scope due to the lack of structural assumptions.
* Limited experimental validation and discussion of scalability.
* Dependence on accurate estimation of prior distributions and noise levels.



I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more thorough discussion of the limitations and potential extensions of the work, as well as a more comprehensive experimental evaluation.
