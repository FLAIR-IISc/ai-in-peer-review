

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding" (NIPS 2017)

This paper establishes a novel connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding in information theory. The authors address the problem of clustering via crowdsourced similarity queries, where the goal is to minimize the number of queries needed to recover cluster labels while accounting for noisy responses. The key insight is that the clustering problem can be viewed as a locally encodable source coding problem, where each compressed bit (query answer) depends on only a constant number of input bits (element labels). This perspective enables the authors to derive tight information-theoretic bounds on query complexity for various scenarios.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it demonstrates that pairwise "same cluster" queries (equivalent to XOR operations) have fundamental limitations for approximate recovery, and proposes AND queries as a more efficient alternative. The authors prove that AND queries require significantly fewer queries to achieve the same level of accuracy (Theorems 3-4). Second, they establish the equivalence to locally encodable source coding, providing new information-theoretic results for this under-studied problem class. Third, they develop practical algorithms for noisy query scenarios with theoretical guarantees (Theorem 5), validated through a real crowdsourcing experiment.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and accessible to a general ML audience. The information-theoretic connections are explained clearly, making complex concepts approachable. The theoretical results are supported by empirical simulations, including a real-world crowdsourcing experiment on movie classification that validates the theoretical predictions. The paper also properly contextualizes its contributions relative to prior work on crowdsourced clustering (e.g., [4, 8, 21, 22, 33]).

**Strengths:**
- Novel theoretical connection between clustering and locally encodable source coding
- Rigorous information-theoretic analysis with tight bounds for multiple scenarios
- Practical AND query approach that outperforms conventional "same cluster" queries
- Clear explanation of theoretical concepts for general ML audience
- Well-designed real-world experiment validating theoretical results
- Strong empirical support for the proposed approach

**Weaknesses:**
- The motivation for AND queries needs further clarification regarding the concern that they might reveal cluster labels (a valid concern, as the reviewer notes). If labels are revealed, why not ask for labels directly? The paper should explicitly address this tension between query design and privacy/label revelation.
- The expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1-4 are somewhat complex. Simplifying these while preserving their essence would enhance readability and accessibility for the broader ML community.
- The experiments appear to use only AND queries, raising validity concerns unless the motivation for this choice is clarified. The paper should explicitly address why AND queries are preferred over "same cluster" queries in the experimental setup.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution with practical implications for crowdsourcing applications. The theoretical results are sound and well-supported by empirical validation. The concerns raised are valid but not fundamental to the paper's contribution; they can be addressed in a revision. The authors have already responded to these concerns in their rebuttal, showing they understand the issues and can provide clarifications.

The paper advances the state of the art in several ways: it provides the first information-theoretic analysis of clustering with crowdsourced queries, establishes a novel connection to source coding, and proposes a more efficient query type (AND queries) that outperforms conventional approaches. For practitioners, the paper offers a practical framework for designing efficient crowdsourcing schemes that minimize query costs while maintaining accuracy.

The significance of this work is evident in its potential to reduce the cost and time of crowdsourced clustering applications. The theoretical results provide fundamental limits that guide future work, while the practical algorithms can be immediately applied to real-world crowdsourcing problems. The paper's clarity and accessibility ensure it will be widely understood and appreciated by the NIPS community.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution with strong empirical validation. The concerns raised are valid but addressable, and the paper's overall contribution is substantial and novel. I am confident this work will be valuable to both theoretical and applied researchers in machine learning and information retrieval.
