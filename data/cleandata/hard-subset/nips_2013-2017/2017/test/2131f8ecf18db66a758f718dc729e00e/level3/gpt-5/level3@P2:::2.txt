Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies non-adaptive, small-arity (∆-ary) query models for multiclass labeling/clustering with possibly noisy oracle answers. It makes three main contributions:
- Conceptual: It establishes an equivalence between a popular crowdsourced clustering model (e.g., same-cluster queries) and locally encodable source coding, where each compressed bit depends on at most ∆ source bits. This viewpoint enables information-theoretic lower bounds and connects to locally decodable/smooth compression literatures.
- Theory: It derives lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery under locality constraints; shows limitations of pairwise same-cluster (XOR) queries via Massey/Ancheta-type arguments; and proposes pairwise AND queries that can outperform XOR for approximate recovery. For noisy queries and k classes, it presents a non-adaptive algorithm using pairwise “k-ary AND” queries with O(n log(k/δ)) queries to recover a (1−δ) fraction of labels, with concentration arguments ensuring high-probability guarantees.
- Empirics: A small crowdsourcing experiment suggests that AND queries can be easier for workers (fewer errors) than same-cluster queries.

The work relates to clustering with queries (e.g., Ashtiani et al. NIPS’16; Mazumdar–Saha NIPS’17), crowdsourcing/redundancy allocation (Karger–Oh–Shah NIPS’11/OR’14), and local/smooth source coding (Makhdoumi et al. ICC’15; Montanari–Mossel ISIT’08; Mazumdar et al. ISIT’15). It also touches community recovery and query complexity in graphs/hypergraphs (e.g., Ahn–Lee–Suh Allerton’16).

Strengths
- Novel and potentially impactful connection to locally encodable source coding that yields clean information-theoretic lower bounds under locality constraints.
- Insight that AND queries can be fundamentally better than XOR for approximate recovery, with a clear intuition (asymmetric evidence and expectation gap).
- A simple, scalable, non-adaptive algorithm achieving O(n log(k/δ)) queries in the noisy setting, with a proof sketch and concentration-based correctness.
- Preliminary empirical evidence (including a real crowd study) that AND queries can be answered more reliably than same-cluster queries.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
1) Modeling choice (no repeated identical queries)
- The paper disallows repeated queries to reduce noise, which is a strong restriction that can materially change optimal tradeoffs. While some rationale is given, the paper should either (i) justify this choice with empirical evidence (beyond anecdotal references), or (ii) analyze the more common setting with independent annotators and allowed repetition (e.g., majority vote or better aggregation), and compare query complexity. Even a brief extension showing how O(n log(k/δ)) changes when up to t repeats are allowed would help.

2) Noiseless exact-recovery regime and Theorem 1
- The stated sufficiency m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) with locality constraints is intriguing, but the proof (random sparse parity ensemble + expurgation) is hard to follow and key steps (typicality, bad-event probability, and expurgation overhead) would benefit from a clearer, self-contained derivation.
- There appears to be a discrepancy with prior results in related query models (e.g., Ahn–Lee–Suh on hypergraph/community recovery; also known O(n) strategies with structured pairings). Please reconcile the asymptotics and assumptions (pairwise v. hyperedge queries, adaptivity, prior known/unknown, exact v. high-probability recovery).

3) Approximate recovery lower bound (Theorem 3) and AND achievability (Theorem 4)
- Both statements are difficult to interpret. Provide simplified corollaries that expose scaling with n, m, δ, ∆, and p. For example: for ∆=2 and m=c n, what explicit δ(c,p) does the lower bound impose, and what δ(c,p) does the AND scheme achieve? Plotting these closed forms would make the converse/achievability gap tangible.
- Theorem 3’s expression depends on h′(·) and ∆ in a way that obscures intuition. A reader needs a short “rule of thumb” and tight regimes (small/large δ, balanced/unbalanced p).
- Theorem 4’s bound involves Poissonization and inclusion–exclusion terms f(k,d), which makes the final expression opaque. Provide approximations (e.g., first-order in d, or tail bounds producing δ ≤ p e−Θ(m/n) for regimes of interest).

4) Empirical evaluation and comparisons
- Figure 2 compares AND achievability only to a lower bound for same-cluster (XOR) queries; without a concrete algorithmic baseline, the superiority claim is unsupported. Please include comparisons to state-of-the-art same-cluster methods under non-adaptive budgets (e.g., simple reference-element schemes, spectral methods on signed graphs, or the best-known non-adaptive constructions).
- Figures 3–5 similarly lack strong baselines. The small-scale real experiment is appreciated, but a synthetic study with controlled p, q, δ and matched budgets comparing AND vs XOR (with ML or spectral decoders) is needed to substantiate the claims across regimes.
- The crossing behavior and parameter ranges where AND dominates should be explained and visualized (as functions of p, q, and δ).

5) Practicality and parameter knowledge
- Algorithm 1 requires p (or exact class sizes per Theorem 6) and q (via thresholds). Please discuss practical estimation (sample complexity, bias), robustness to misspecification, and how to set thresholds adaptively. Provide sensitivity plots for mis-specified p and q, and guidance for practitioners.
- The pseudocode (and C_i thresholds) is difficult to parse without the proofs. Add a derivation, plain-language intuition, and a step explaining how to compute thresholds from estimated parameters.

6) Clarity and presentation
- Several proofs (Theorems 1, 3, 4) are terse. Adding intuition (why XOR is bad for distortion; where α and r(p) come from; how locality tightens the rate-distortion curve) would help.
- Figure 2 should include algorithmic curves for XOR; Figures 3–5 should clearly label axes, budgets, and parameters, and report confidence intervals.
- Please tighten notation (e.g., consistent use of r(p)=2p(1−p), α, and the role of ∆), and unify the binary vs k-ary sections.

Quality
- The theoretical direction is promising, but currently some proofs are hard to follow and several claims are not validated against algorithmic baselines. The modeling restriction and lack of clarity around key theorems weaken the case.

Clarity
- Mixed. High-level ideas are clear; technical sections and figures need clearer exposition and actionable corollaries.

Originality
- Strong. The locally encodable source coding lens and the AND-vs-XOR insight for approximate recovery appear novel and interesting.

Significance
- Potentially significant for query-efficient clustering and crowdsourcing, but impact depends on stronger empirical validation and clearer theoretical takeaways (scaling laws, tightness).

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel conceptual bridge enabling new lower bounds.
- Simple, non-adaptive algorithm with favorable query complexity.
- Practical insight that AND queries can be easier for workers.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient empirical comparisons to existing same-cluster methods.
- Hard-to-interpret main theorems; unclear gaps between bounds and achievability.
- Restrictive model choices insufficiently justified; unclear robustness to parameter estimation.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide simplified corollaries for Theorems 3–4 (e.g., explicit δ(c,p,∆) for m=c n)?
- How does allowing up to t repeated queries per edge change your guarantees?
- Please reconcile Theorem 1 with prior results in related noiseless regimes; specify assumptions precisely.
- How robust is Algorithm 1 to mis-specified p and q? Can thresholds be set without q?
- Can you include direct comparisons to strong XOR-based baselines under matched budgets?

Recommendation: Borderline reject. The paper has compelling ideas and promising theory, but needs clearer statements/proofs and stronger empirical comparisons to substantiate its main claims.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
