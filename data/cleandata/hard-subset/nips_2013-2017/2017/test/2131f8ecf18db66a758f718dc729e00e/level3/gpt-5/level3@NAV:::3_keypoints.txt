- Studies semisupervised clustering via crowdsourced similarity queries using an information-theoretic framework.  
- Establishes equivalence between nonadaptive querying for multiclass labeling and locally encodable source coding, providing lower and upper bounds for noiseless and noisy answers.  
- Key insight: pairwise AND queries outperform XOR/same-cluster queries for approximate recovery, enabling O(n log(1/δ)) queries to recover a (1−δ) fraction of labels even with noise.  
- Connects to and extends literature on same-cluster queries, noisy crowdsourcing, and stochastic block models, bringing in rate-distortion and locally decodable/encodable code tools.  
- Technically sound and carefully argued; improved information-theoretic lower bounds and constructive AND-based algorithms with concentration analyses are convincing.  
- Assumptions (nonadaptive design, independent BSC noise, known/estimated priors) are clearly stated; simulations and a small crowd experiment support the theory.  
- Writing is accessible overall, but Theorems 1–4 are hard to parse; recommend simplified corollaries (e.g., k=2, balanced priors, small δ) and short intuition paragraphs.  
- Novelty: the locally encodable source coding perspective and the AND-query strategy are original and insightful.  
- Significance: results likely to influence choice of query primitives in crowdsourced clustering; the AND vs XOR comparison is actionable.  
- Concern: AND-query semantics can reveal absolute cluster membership, potentially undermining the motivation to avoid direct labels—need an explicit cost/privacy model.  
- Concern: experiments use only AND queries; must include fair baselines using same-cluster and label queries under the same query budget and discuss label leakage.  
- Suggestion: simplify bound expressions and add intuitive explanations to improve readability.  
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution with practical implications; reviewer recommends acceptance.  
- Pros: novel theory, clear information-theoretic connection, improved bounds, simple algorithms, supportive experiments.  
- Cons: ambiguous crowdsourcing motivation for AND, complex theorem statements, limited empirical baselines.
