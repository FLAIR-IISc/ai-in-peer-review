## Review of “Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding”

This paper presents a novel connection between semisupervised clustering via crowdsourced similarity queries and locally encodable source coding, deriving theoretical bounds for query complexity in both noisy and noiseless scenarios. The core idea of framing the clustering problem as a source coding problem is interesting and allows the authors to leverage information-theoretic tools to obtain meaningful results. The paper is exceptionally well-written and accessible, even for those not deeply familiar with the nuances of source coding. The explanation of the information-theoretic connections is particularly clear, making the theoretical contributions understandable. 

The main contribution lies in the theoretical analysis, which is supported by empirical simulations. Specifically, the authors demonstrate the limitations of pairwise ‘same cluster’ queries and propose utilizing AND queries, which provably outperform the former in many cases. The theorems are well-stated and the proofs, while not included in full due to space constraints, appear logically sound based on the provided sketches. 

However, a significant concern arises from the exclusive use of AND queries in the experiments. The motivation for focusing solely on AND queries isn’t fully justified – if these queries demonstrably yield better performance, why were ‘same cluster’ queries not also thoroughly evaluated empirically? It feels as though the experimental validation is primarily designed to confirm the theoretical advantages of AND queries, rather than a comprehensive comparison of different query types. This raises questions about the practical implications of the findings. Furthermore, the paper states that asking for labels directly is not a good choice, but doesn't fully explain why, given the performance gains of AND queries. A clearer distinction between the benefits of querying functions of labels versus querying labels directly would strengthen the argument.

While the bounds presented in Theorems 1-4 are valuable, they could benefit from simplification to enhance their readability and impact. Preserving the essence of the results while reducing the complexity of the expressions would make them more accessible to a broader audience.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation with clear connections to established information-theoretic principles.
*   Novel framing of the clustering problem as a locally encodable source coding problem.
*   Well-written and accessible to a general ML audience.
*   Clear identification of limitations of existing approaches and a promising alternative (AND queries).
*   Authors have responded thoroughly to concerns.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Experimental evaluation is focused solely on AND queries, potentially biasing the results.
*   The motivation for exclusive use of AND queries needs further clarification.
*   The bounds presented in the theorems could be simplified for better readability.



**Overall Recommendation:**

Despite the concerns regarding the experimental validation, the theoretical contributions of this paper are substantial and the presentation is excellent. The authors have addressed reviewer comments effectively. Therefore, I **recommend acceptance** with the suggestion that the authors consider clarifying the rationale behind the exclusive focus on AND queries and simplifying the expressions in Theorems 1-4 where possible.
