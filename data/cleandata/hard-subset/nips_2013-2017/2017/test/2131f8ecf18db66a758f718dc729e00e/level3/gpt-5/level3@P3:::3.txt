Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies semisupervised clustering via crowd-sourced similarity (and related) queries, with a focus on non-adaptive query design and both noiseless and noisy answer models. The central conceptual contribution is an information-theoretic formulation: clustering with bounded-arity queries corresponds to locally encodable source coding, in contrast to classical source coding (global encoders) and the more-studied locally decodable/smooth compression settings. Leveraging this lens, the paper establishes lower bounds (exact and approximate recovery), proposes improved achievability via AND-type queries (as opposed to standard “same-cluster”/XOR queries), and gives an algorithm for the noisy setting that recovers a (1−δ) fraction of labels with O(n log(k/δ)) non-adaptive pairwise queries. The paper connects to and extends lines of work on query complexity for clustering and entity resolution (e.g., “same-cluster” queries and their O(n log n) behavior), on budgeted crowdsourcing, and on information-theoretic limits of locally constrained compression (Massey; Ancheta–Massey; smooth compression; locally decodable source coding).

Quality
- Theory: The lower bounds and achievability results are technically solid and novel. The information-theoretic mapping is explained cleanly and used effectively to derive tight(er) bounds than naive entropy limits in some regimes. The argument that XOR (“same-cluster”) is suboptimal for approximate recovery, and that AND unlocks better query–error tradeoffs, is convincing and well supported by both analysis and simulations.
- Algorithms: The noisy-case algorithm is simple, scalable (pairwise, degree-d graph), and its guarantee m = O(n log(k/δ)) is crisp and easy to interpret.
- Experiments: The real crowdsourcing experiment is helpful, showing lower empirical error for AND than XOR questions on the same pairs. However, the empirical section is limited in scope and mainly probes the AND primitive; further baselines and datasets would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is well written and accessible to a broad ML audience. The information-theoretic connection is particularly clear. A few of the theorem statements (esp. Theorems 1–4) present long expressions; see “Requests” below for streamlined, practitioner-friendly corollaries.

Originality
The locally encodable source coding viewpoint for clustering with bounded-arity queries is, to my knowledge, new and insightful. The lower bounds under locality constraints, the AND-query achievability for approximate recovery, and the noisy-case O(n log(k/δ)) scheme collectively represent a significant theoretical advance over prior XOR/same-cluster-centric approaches.

Significance
Understanding optimal query types and rates is central for crowd-powered clustering/entity resolution. The bounds clarify when sub-n queries are possible (exact, noiseless) and when AND-like primitives dramatically outperform same-cluster for approximate recovery. The noisy-case O(n log(k/δ)) result is a strong, usable guideline. I expect these ideas to influence both theory and practical system design.

Main concerns and questions
- Query semantics and the crowdsourcing motivation (AND may reveal labels): The proposed AND primitive (for k=2: “are both items class 1?”; for general k: return i if both items are i, else 0) can reveal the actual label when the answer is nonzero. This seems at odds with one of the motivations for similarity queries—avoiding direct labeling when labels are hard/expensive or undefined (unsupervised clustering). If labels are explicitly identified in the query, why not ask for single-item labels directly? Please:
  1) Clarify the target application: is it labeling (semisupervised classification) rather than unlabeled clustering? Your movie experiment (“both action?”) suggests known semantic classes.
  2) Compare theoretically and empirically to the baseline of single-item label queries (with/without noise). In noiseless exact recovery, your sub-n result (for skewed priors) is a compelling advantage over n label queries—this deserves highlighting. In noisy approximate regimes, when is AND preferable to labeling a randomly chosen subset of items?
  3) Discuss worker cognitive load and bias: AND questions may be easier than “same cluster?” because only one label needs to be known to answer “no,” which your data supports. This strengthens your case—please make the practical implication explicit.
- Experiments mainly use AND queries: The empirical validation focuses on AND; XOR results (with a standard spectral baseline) are reported only synthetically. This is acceptable given the theoretical thrust, but please clarify that conclusions about empirical superiority are limited to AND vs XOR, not versus broader active/semi-supervised baselines.

Requests: simplify bound expressions (while preserving essence)
It would greatly aid readers to include short corollaries/approximations alongside the precise theorems.

- Theorem 1 (noiseless, exact, upper bound):
  Exact: m ≈ n h(p) / log2(2/(1 + (1−2p)^{2Δ})).
  Δ=2 corollary: m ≈ n h(p) / log2(2/(1 + (1−2p)^4)).
  Intuition: The gain over n depends on the class imbalance b = |1−2p|; larger b and larger Δ increase log2(2/(1+b^{2Δ})) and reduce the required m. For p near 1/2, m approaches n h(p)/( (b^{2Δ})/(2 ln 2) ), so the gain vanishes smoothly.

- Theorem 2 (noiseless, exact, lower bound):
  Present as m ≥ n h(p) · max{1, max_ρ (1−ρ)/h(((1−ρ) Δ r(p))/ρ)}, with r(p)=2p(1−p).
  Rule of thumb: For Δ=2 and small p, the second factor can exceed 1 by a noticeable constant, sharpening the naive nh(p) converse.

- Theorem 3 (noiseless, approximate, converse under locality):
  Keep the exact expression, but add: For fixed Δ, the rate–distortion optimum δ̃(m/n) suffers an additive penalty that decays exponentially in Δ: δ ≥ δ̃(m/n) + Θ(exp(−Δ·h′(δ̃))). This conveys that locality imposes a nonzero, but rapidly vanishing (in Δ), gap.

- Theorem 4 (noiseless, approximate, AND achievability):
  Alongside the exact series, add the Poissonized corollary (ignoring collisions):
  Let λ = 2m/n. Then δ ≲ p·exp(−p λ). Thus to reach error δ, m ≳ (n/(2p))·log(p/δ).
  This back-of-the-envelope bound tracks your first term pe^(−2m/n) and is very useful for planning m.

- Theorem 5 (noisy, approximate, k classes):
  State the guarantee as: choose d = Θ(log(k/δ)) with a constant depending only on q and min_i p_i, then m = n d/2 = Θ(n log(k/δ)) queries suffice. If space permits, give the constant via an explicit Chernoff gap.

Pros
- Clear, original information-theoretic framing (locally encodable source coding).
- New lower bounds under locality; clean insights into XOR’s suboptimality.
- Simple, effective AND-based achievability; O(n log(k/δ)) noisy-case algorithm.
- Well written; proofs and intuition are clearly communicated.
- Empirical indication that AND queries are easier for workers than XOR.

Cons
- AND queries can reveal labels; application scope and comparison to label queries need clarification.
- Experimental section is limited; mostly AND vs XOR; broader baselines would help.
- Some theorem statements are heavy; practitioner-friendly corollaries would improve readability.

Overall assessment and recommendation
I am satisfied with the authors’ responses and clarifications and recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical implications for the design of crowdsourced clustering/labeling systems. Addressing the AND-query semantics and adding simplified corollaries to the main theorems will further strengthen the final version.
