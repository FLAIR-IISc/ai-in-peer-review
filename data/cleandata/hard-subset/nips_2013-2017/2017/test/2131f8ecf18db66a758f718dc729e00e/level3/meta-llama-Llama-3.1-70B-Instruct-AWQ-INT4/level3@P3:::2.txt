This paper presents a novel approach to semisupervised clustering using AND-queries and locally encodable source coding. The authors provide lower bounds and an algorithm for reconstructing cluster labels from noisy, non-adaptive small-set queries. The paper also includes real-data evaluation, which is a significant contribution to the field.

The main ideas of the paper can be summarized as follows. The authors consider a model of semisupervised clustering where the task is to perform multiclass labeling of unlabeled elements. They show that this problem is equivalent to locally encodable source coding, a data compression problem where each compressed bit depends only on a constant number of input bits. The authors provide lower bounds on the number of queries required to recover the correct labelings and propose pairwise AND-queries, which provably perform better in many situations.

The strengths of the paper include the novel approach to semisupervised clustering using AND-queries and locally encodable source coding. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem, including lower bounds and an algorithm for reconstructing cluster labels. The real-data evaluation is also a significant contribution to the field.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. The presentation, clarity, and comparison with existing methods could be improved. Specifically, the authors assume that repeated identical queries are not allowed, which may hurt performance. The authors should justify this choice or analyze the no-restriction case and/or independent annotator setting.

Additionally, the proofs of Theorems 1 and 3 are hard to follow, and the lower-bound expressions for δ are difficult to interpret. The gap between the upper bound and the lower bound is unclear, and the proofs are difficult to follow. Furthermore, Figure 2 lacks a comparison to the same-cluster-query algorithm, which makes it difficult to evaluate the superiority of the proposed method.

The algorithm proposed in the paper requires prior knowledge of p or cluster sizes, which may not be practical in real-world scenarios. The pseudocode is also hard to follow without complex proofs. Moreover, Figures 3-5 do not compare to state-of-the-art methods, which makes it difficult to evaluate the performance of the proposed algorithm.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to semisupervised clustering using AND-queries and locally encodable source coding. However, the authors could have done a better job in comparing their approach to existing methods and highlighting the advantages and disadvantages of their approach.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The results have the potential to impact the field of semisupervised clustering and crowdsourcing. However, the authors could have done a better job in discussing the implications of their results and the potential applications of their approach.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors have made a significant contribution to the field. However, there are some areas that need improvement, including the presentation, clarity, and comparison with existing methods.
