- Problem: semi-supervised clustering via non-adaptive, local (Δ-item) queries to recover n items into k clusters.  
- Main contribution: formal equivalence between this querying problem and locally encodable source coding.  
- Results: new information-theoretic lower bounds on query complexity for exact and approximate recovery (noiseless and noisy settings).  
- Key insight: standard "same-cluster" (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery.  
- Proposal: AND queries, shown theoretically and experimentally to outperform XOR, especially under noise.  
- Motivation: applications in crowdsourcing and entity resolution where minimizing query budget matters.  
- Strength — originality: novel framing brings powerful information-theoretic tools to the clustering query problem.  
- Strength — technical quality: solid proofs, tight upper/lower bounds, and a simple algorithm with performance guarantees for noisy approximate recovery.  
- Strength — clarity: well-written exposition, clear theorems, and small-scale experiments validating claims.  
- Weakness — modeling: assuming labels are i.i.d. from a known prior p ignores geometric/structural data properties common in real clustering tasks.  
- Weakness — practicality: algorithms/bounds depend on knowing p or cluster sizes; sensitivity to estimation errors is not deeply explored.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel framework, fundamental bounds, practical AND-query design, technically sound and clearly presented.  
- Argument against acceptance: strong i.i.d. prior assumption may limit broader impact of the specific bounds.  
- Conclusion: overall a strong, novel contribution that merits acceptance despite modeling limitations.
