This paper investigates the query complexity of semisupervised clustering, framing it as a locally encodable source coding problem. The authors establish connections between this clustering problem, where queries about label similarity are posed to an oracle, and fundamental concepts in information theory. They derive upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery of labels, considering both noiseless and noisy oracle responses. A key contribution is the introduction of AND queries as a potentially more efficient alternative to traditional ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries, particularly in the approximate recovery setting.

The paper builds upon a growing body of work on crowdsourced clustering and entity resolution (e.g., [4, 8, 20, 33]), and connects it to the well-established field of source coding. The authors correctly identify the relevance of locally encodable source coding [23] and relate their work to concepts like locally decodable codes [19, 16]. The motivation for studying this problem is clear: minimizing the number of queries to a potentially expensive oracle (e.g., human annotators) is crucial for practical applications. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Connection:** The paper’s most significant strength lies in establishing a formal link between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding. This allows the authors to leverage information-theoretic tools, offering a fresh perspective on the problem.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The derived upper and lower bounds, particularly in the noisy oracle setting (Theorem 5), are technically sound and contribute to a deeper understanding of the query complexity.
* **Practical Implications:** The proposed AND query strategy is a promising direction for improving the efficiency of clustering algorithms in real-world scenarios, as demonstrated by preliminary experimental results.
* **Comprehensive Coverage:** The paper considers various aspects of the problem, including noiseless/noisy oracles, exact/approximate recovery, and explores limitations of ‘same cluster’ queries.
* **Clear Presentation:**  Despite the technical depth, the paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the problem setup and contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Assumptions:** The paper operates under fairly general assumptions, without imposing specific structural constraints on the underlying data or label distributions. This generality limits the tightness of the bounds and potentially the applicability of the results in more specific settings. For instance, assuming a stochastic block model or a specific hypothesis class could lead to sharper bounds.
* **Experimental Validation:** While the authors include a real-world experiment, it is relatively limited in scope and primarily serves as a proof of concept. More extensive experiments on diverse datasets and with varying noise levels would strengthen the empirical evidence supporting the proposed AND query strategy.  The comparison to other existing clustering algorithms beyond spectral clustering would be valuable.
* **Complexity of Proofs:** Some of the proofs, particularly for Theorem 3, are relegated to the supplementary material. While this is acceptable, it hinders accessibility for readers less familiar with the underlying mathematical concepts.
* **Crowdsourcing Context:** The discussion on crowdsourcing and its limitations (e.g., majority voting issues) feels somewhat disconnected from the core information-theoretic analysis. A tighter integration of these aspects would be beneficial.
* **Related Work:** The extensive list of references could be streamlined to focus on the most directly relevant prior work.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound connection between semisupervised clustering and information theory.
* The derived bounds provide valuable insights into the fundamental limits of query-based clustering.
* The proposed AND query strategy shows promise for improving practical performance.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The generality of the assumptions limits the practical applicability and tightness of the results.
* The experimental validation is somewhat limited.
* The proofs are complex and may be inaccessible to a broader audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper represents a solid contribution to the understanding of query complexity in semisupervised clustering. The novel connection to locally encodable source coding is a significant achievement, and the derived bounds and proposed AND query strategy are valuable additions to the field. While the limitations regarding assumptions and experimental validation warrant consideration, the overall quality of the work and its potential impact justify acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper with a score of **7/10**. I would encourage the authors to consider expanding the experimental evaluation and exploring the implications of more specific data assumptions in future work.
