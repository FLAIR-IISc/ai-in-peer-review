- Central contribution: Establishes an equivalence between semi-supervised query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding, enabling new information-theoretic analysis.  
- Main results: Derives novel lower and upper bounds on query complexity for exact and approximate label recovery under noiseless and noisy oracles.  
- Key insight: Shows "same-cluster" (XOR) queries can be suboptimal for approximate recovery and proposes "AND" queries as a superior alternative in certain regimes.  
- Theoretical support: Provides rigorous proofs and thorough analysis covering multiple recovery and noise settings.  
- Empirical validation: Real-world crowdsourcing experiment supports theoretical claims and suggests AND queries may be cognitively easier and less noisy for humans.  
- Originality & significance: The information-theoretic framing is novel, significant, and likely to inspire further work.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-written, organized, and makes complex concepts accessible to a broad ML audience.  
- Practical-motivation concern: Unclear why AND queries are preferable to direct label queries in realistic settings; stronger practical justification is needed.  
- Presentation concern: Theorems’ bounds are complex; simplified/asymptotic forms or corollaries would aid intuition about parameter dependencies.  
- Experimental concern: Comparison to same-cluster baselines on the same real dataset is limited; a stronger, direct empirical baseline (e.g., spectral clustering with noisy same-cluster queries) would strengthen claims.  
- Overall: Strong, novel theoretical contribution with clear writing and supporting experiment, but needs better practical motivation for the AND query model and clearer presentation of bounds.
