This paper studies the problem of semi-supervised clustering, where the goal is to recover a ground-truth labeling of `n` items into `k` clusters by making non-adaptive queries to an oracle. The key constraint is that each query is "local," meaning it can only involve a fixed, constant number of items (Δ). The central and most novel contribution of the paper is to establish a formal equivalence between this querying problem and the information-theoretic problem of locally encodable source coding. Leveraging this connection, the authors derive new information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery of the labels. A significant finding is that standard "same-cluster" (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery. The paper proposes an alternative, "AND" queries, and provides theoretical and experimental evidence that they perform substantially better, especially in the presence of noise. The work is well-motivated by applications in crowdsourcing and entity resolution where minimizing the query budget is paramount.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength lies in its novel approach. Framing the query-based clustering problem in the language of locally encodable source coding is a highly original contribution that brings powerful information-theoretic tools to bear on a problem of interest to the machine learning community. This new perspective allows the authors to derive fundamental limits on query complexity, which is a significant step toward a deeper understanding of this problem space. The finding that AND queries can outperform the more conventional XOR ("same-cluster") queries is an important and actionable insight for practitioners designing crowdsourcing systems.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically solid. The theoretical results, including the upper and lower bounds for different recovery settings (noiseless/noisy, exact/approximate), are well-supported by detailed proofs that employ standard, yet powerful, information-theoretic and probabilistic arguments (e.g., typical set analysis, expurgation, Chernoff bounds). The proposed algorithm for approximate recovery in the noisy setting is simple, intuitive, and comes with strong performance guarantees.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors do a commendable job of explaining the information-theoretic connection, defining the problem setup, and motivating the introduction of AND queries. The main results are stated clearly as theorems, and the experimental section provides a good, albeit small-scale, validation of the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limiting Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on the assumption that the labels are drawn i.i.d. from a known prior distribution `p`. This is a strong assumption that may not hold in many real-world clustering scenarios where data often exhibits significant structure (e.g., clusters of varying density, non-independent features). The performance bounds derived are heavily dependent on this prior, and the model does not account for any underlying geometric or structural properties of the data points themselves. This limits the scope of the analysis and its direct applicability to problems where such structural information is key.

2.  **Practicality of Prior Knowledge:** The algorithms and bounds depend on knowing the prior `p` (or the relative cluster sizes). While the authors suggest this can be estimated, the quality of this estimation could significantly affect performance, and this aspect is not deeply explored. Theorem 6 partially addresses this by showing the algorithm works with known cluster sizes and random permutations, but the sensitivity to estimation errors in these sizes remains an open question.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   Highly original connection between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding.
    *   Provides new, fundamental lower bounds on the query complexity for this problem.
    *   Introduces AND queries as a provably superior alternative to standard same-cluster queries, a significant practical and theoretical insight.
    *   The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses both noiseless and noisy query models.
    *   It represents a valuable first step toward a rigorous information-theoretic understanding of query-based learning.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    *   The i.i.d. label assumption is a major simplification that sidesteps the structural aspects inherent in most clustering problems, potentially limiting the broader impact of the specific bounds derived.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a novel and significant contribution. The weaknesses identified, primarily the strong modeling assumptions, do not undermine the core contributions but rather point to interesting directions for future research. The paper introduces a new and powerful analytical framework, provides important theoretical insights, and offers practical guidance for query design in crowdsourcing. It is a valuable addition to the literature and should be accepted.
