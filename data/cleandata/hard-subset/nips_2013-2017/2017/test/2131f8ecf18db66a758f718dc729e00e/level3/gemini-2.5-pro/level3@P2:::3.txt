This paper addresses the problem of semi-supervised clustering where the goal is to learn the true labels of `n` elements by making non-adaptive queries to an oracle. The central and novel contribution is the establishment of an equivalence between this problem and the information-theoretic concept of locally encodable source coding. This framework allows the authors to derive new lower and upper bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery of labels, under both noiseless and noisy oracle models. A key finding is that traditional "same-cluster" (XOR) queries can be suboptimal for approximate recovery. The authors propose using "AND" queries instead and demonstrate theoretically and empirically that this new query type can achieve better performance with fewer queries in certain regimes. This work builds upon recent literature in clustering with crowdsourced queries and entity resolution but provides a fresh and powerful theoretical perspective.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality & Significance:** The connection drawn between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding is highly original and significant. It provides a new principled framework for analyzing the fundamental limits of this problem. This information-theoretic viewpoint is a valuable contribution that could inspire new research directions.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The authors provide rigorous proofs for their theoretical claims, deriving novel bounds on query complexity. The proposed "AND" query scheme is well-motivated from a theoretical standpoint, and its superiority over "same-cluster" queries in the approximate recovery setting is a strong result. The analysis is thorough, covering exact and approximate recovery as well as noisy and noiseless settings.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It successfully makes a deep information-theoretic concept accessible to a broader machine learning audience. The problem setup, motivations, and the main results are presented with admirable clarity. The connection to source coding is explained intuitively before the formal results are presented.
*   **Empirical Validation:** While the paper's core contributions are theoretical, the authors validate their proposed algorithm with a real-world crowdsourcing experiment. The experiment not only supports the theoretical findings but also provides evidence for a practical claim: that AND queries may be cognitively easier for human workers to answer correctly than same-cluster queries, leading to lower noise rates.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

My main concern revolves around the practical motivation for using AND queries.
1.  **Motivation for AND queries:** An AND query, such as "Are both movies 'action' movies?", seems to require the worker to know the absolute label of at least one movie to answer "no" and both to answer "yes". If a worker possesses this level of knowledge, it is not clear why one wouldn't simply ask for the label of each item directly ("Is this movie an 'action' movie?"). The paper argues that label queries require at least `n` queries for recovery, whereas their method requires fewer. However, this argument feels incomplete. Could the authors please elaborate on the practical scenarios where an AND query is feasible and preferable, but a direct label query is not? A stronger justification for this query model is needed to fully appreciate its practical significance.

2.  **Clarity of Theoretical Bounds:** The expressions for the bounds presented in Theorems 1-4 are quite complex. While they are precise, their current form makes it difficult for the reader to quickly grasp the key takeaways and dependencies on the problem parameters (e.g., `p`, `δ`, `m/n`). The paper's impact could be increased if the authors provided simplified or asymptotic versions of these bounds, perhaps in a corollary or a discussion section, to highlight the essential trade-offs more intuitively.

3.  **Experimental Comparison:** The experimental section provides good validation for the proposed algorithm using AND queries. However, the comparison to "same-cluster" queries could be more direct. The paper notes that the empirical error rate for same-cluster queries was much higher on the real dataset, and then resorts to synthetic data for a performance comparison (Figure 6). To make a more compelling case, it would be beneficial to include results from a strong baseline using same-cluster queries (e.g., spectral clustering) on the same noisy, real-world movie dataset, even if the noise level is higher.

**Summary of Pro/Con Arguments:**

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel and powerful information-theoretic framework for analyzing query-based clustering, leading to new theoretical bounds and the insightful proposal of AND queries. The work is technically strong, clearly written, and supported by a real-world experiment.
*   **Con:** The practical motivation for the proposed AND query model over direct label queries is not sufficiently justified. The presentation of the theoretical bounds could be made more accessible.

Despite the concerns raised, the theoretical contributions of this paper are substantial and novel. The connection to locally encodable source coding is a genuine insight that advances our understanding of this problem space. If the authors can strengthen the motivation for their query model, this would be a very strong paper.
