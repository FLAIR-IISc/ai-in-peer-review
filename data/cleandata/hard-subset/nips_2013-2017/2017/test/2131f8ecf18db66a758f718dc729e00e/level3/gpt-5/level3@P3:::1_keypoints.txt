- Paper studies nonadaptive Δ-ary query-based clustering/labeling and asks how many queries suffice for exact/approximate recovery, including noisy answers.
- Establishes an equivalence between this clustering model and locally encodable source coding to derive lower bounds and algorithms.
- For k=2, analyzes pairwise XOR (“same-cluster”) queries, showing exact recovery with < n queries in some p regimes and stronger lower bounds than nH(p) in others.
- Proves general lower bounds under locality for approximate recovery and shows XOR schemes are fundamentally limited by linear rate–distortion results.
- Shows pairwise AND queries achieve substantially better query–accuracy tradeoffs for approximate recovery.
- Designs a simple nonadaptive algorithm using AND-like queries generalized to k classes that achieves (1−δ)-accurate recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries under noise.
- Validates the AND-based approach with a small crowdsourcing experiment showing lower error and predicted scaling.
- Novelty: the locally encodable source coding viewpoint and application of Massey/Ancheta bounds to critique XOR queries are new to the ML community.
- Strength: clear conceptual novelty connecting coding theory to crowdsourced clustering.
- Strength: technical contributions include new locality-based lower bounds and constructive, simple algorithms.
- Strength: practical insight that AND queries may be easier and more reliable for workers, supported empirically.
- Strength: well-motivated by record linkage/entity resolution and crowdsourcing use cases favoring nonadaptive small-arity queries.
- Weakness: assumes i.i.d. labels with known/estimated marginals and independent noise, limiting applicability to structured clustering.
- Weakness: for k>2 the noisy result requires k-ary answers, diverging from the binary-answer emphasis and with limited discussion.
- Weakness: only nonadaptive designs are studied; no comparison to adaptive strategies that might reduce queries.
- Weakness: lower bounds are not tight in all regimes and comparisons to other primitives (OR, majority, triangle) are limited.
- Weakness: empirical validation is small-scale and assumes worker error independence and no repetition, which may not hold in practice.
- Quality: theoretical results are sound with clear coding reductions and standard concentration/typicality analyses; algorithms are practical.
- Clarity: paper is well-organized; some presentations (rate–distortion intuition, regimes with m<n) could be streamlined and practical guidance added.
- Originality: high — the formulation and conclusions appear novel for ML and crowdsourcing.
- Significance: moderate-to-high — provides baseline information-theoretic limits and practical protocols, though assumptions limit direct applicability.
- Suggestion: explore adaptive variants and whether AND retains advantages, quantifying adaptive gains.
- Suggestion: extend to realistic worker models (heterogeneous reliability, correlated errors, abstentions) and test robustness.
- Suggestion: systematically compare against other small-arity primitives under identical noise budgets.
- Suggestion: clarify consequences of binary vs k-ary answers and whether binary reductions can match k-ary rates.
- Suggestion: study estimation and sensitivity of p in practice and include experiments with unknown p.
- Suggestion: tighten lower bounds and delineate regimes of optimality where possible.
- Arguments for acceptance: introduces a new conceptual bridge, rigorous bounds, and a simple, insightful algorithm (AND vs XOR) with noise robustness.
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive modeling assumptions and limited empirical evaluation against alternatives and adaptive baselines.
- Questions: sensitivity of Algorithm 1 to misspecified p and q and whether thresholds can be set adaptively.
- Questions: can binary-output schemes for k>2 approach the k-ary O(n log(k/δ)) rate?
- Questions: do lower bounds extend to adversarial or class-dependent worker noise models?
- Questions: how do results change with limited repetitions across diverse workers (not identical query repetition)?
