This paper proposes a novel framework for semisupervised clustering using locally encodable source coding, which is equivalent to asking simple queries to an oracle. The authors provide new information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required to recover the correct labeling, both exactly and approximately. They also propose a new querying strategy based on AND operations that performs better than traditional 'same cluster' queries. The paper further extends the results to the case of noisy oracles and provides a real crowdsourcing experiment to validate the theoretical results.

The main contributions of the paper are:

1. A new framework for semisupervised clustering using locally encodable source coding.
2. Information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required to recover the correct labeling, both exactly and approximately.
3. A new querying strategy based on AND operations that performs better than traditional 'same cluster' queries.
4. Extension of the results to the case of noisy oracles.
5. A real crowdsourcing experiment to validate the theoretical results.

The strengths of the paper are:

1. The authors provide a novel and well-motivated framework for semisupervised clustering.
2. The paper provides new information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required to recover the correct labeling.
3. The proposed querying strategy based on AND operations is shown to perform better than traditional 'same cluster' queries.
4. The extension of the results to the case of noisy oracles is a significant contribution.
5. The real crowdsourcing experiment provides a practical validation of the theoretical results.

The weaknesses of the paper are:

1. The analysis is limited in scope, as no structural assumptions are made about the hypothesis class.
2. The bounds mainly rely on the marginal distribution of labels and the noise level, which may not be sufficient in all cases.
3. The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison with existing methods.

Overall, the paper provides a useful first step toward understanding information-theoretic limits and lower bounds for query-based clustering. The proposed framework and querying strategy are novel and well-motivated, and the extension to the case of noisy oracles is a significant contribution.
