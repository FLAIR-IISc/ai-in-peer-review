Summary
The paper studies non-adaptive, budgeted crowdsourced clustering/labeling through the lens of locally encodable source coding. Each query is a simple function of a small number (Δ) of items’ unknown labels, and the goal is to recover all (exact) or most (approximate) labels with as few queries as possible, in noiseless and noisy settings. The authors establish an equivalence to locally encodable source coding and use information-theoretic tools to derive lower bounds and to design algorithms that meet these bounds up to constants.

Key technical contributions include:
- Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2). With pairwise XOR (“same cluster”) queries, the paper gives an achievability bound m ≈ n(h(p)+o(1))/log(1/α), α = 1/2·(1 + (1−4p(1−p))^Δ), showing < n queries suffice for a nontrivial range of priors p, and provides a converse strictly stronger than the naive n h(p) bound in some regimes.
- Noiseless, approximate recovery (k=2). A general lower bound for any Δ-local scheme is derived via rate–distortion and locality constraints. The paper argues that XOR queries are fundamentally limited (via classical Massey/Ancheta insights on linear codes for lossy compression), and proposes pairwise AND queries with a simple decoder that provably outperforms XOR for a broad regime, achieving (1−δ)-accurate recovery with m = O(n log(1/δ)) for k=2.
- Noisy, approximate recovery (general k). Using pairwise AND-like k-ary queries Q(X,X′)=i iff X=X′=i and a d-regular design, the authors give a thresholding decoder (Algorithm 1) with guarantees of (1−δ)-accuracy using m = O(n log(k/δ)) queries when answers pass through a symmetric noise model. A concentration argument ensures high-probability control of the total number of errors.
- Empirical validation. A small real crowdsourcing study (movie genres) and synthetic experiments corroborate that AND-style queries are easier for workers (lower error rates) and that the proposed algorithm matches theoretical trends; majority voting on repeated identical queries is not assumed/used.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to: “same cluster” querying for clustering/entity resolution (e.g., Ashtiani et al., Mazumdar–Saha), coding-theoretic views of crowdsourcing (Vempaty–Varshney; Lahouti–Hassibi), rate–distortion and source/channel coding (Cover–Thomas; Massey), and local decodability/smooth compression (Makhdoumi et al.; Montanari–Mossel; Mazumdar–Chandar–Wornell). The proposed locally encodable viewpoint appears novel and yields sharper, locality-aware bounds and new algorithmic choices (AND vs XOR).

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality: The theoretical development is technically sound and largely self-contained, with appropriate use of information-theoretic tools. The proofs are careful (details deferred to appendix where appropriate). Assumptions (non-adaptivity, independence of query noise, no repetition of identical queries) are clearly stated. The empirical section is modest in scale but consistent with the theory.
- Clarity: The paper is well written and accessible to a broad ML audience. The information-theoretic connection is explained clearly and helps unify results. That said, the statements of Theorems 1–4 are quite dense. It would help to simplify the expressions (e.g., isolate the dominant terms, explicitly state regimes where bounds beat n or n h(p), and provide brief intuition highlights after each theorem).
- Originality: The locally encodable source coding formulation for semisupervised clustering is original, as is the systematic use of AND queries (and k-ary generalization) showing superiority to XOR for approximate/noisy recovery. The improved converse bounds that incorporate locality constraints are also novel.
- Significance: Results advance the theoretical understanding of query-efficient clustering, indicating that “same cluster” (XOR) is suboptimal for approximate/noisy settings and motivating different primitives for practice. The O(n log(k/δ)) non-adaptive guarantee with a simple decoder is likely to influence future algorithm design. The coding-theoretic perspective should be of broad interest at NIPS.

Pros
- Clear, principled information-theoretic framework with nontrivial new converses under locality.
- Practical algorithm with near-optimal query complexity and simple decoding.
- Insightful finding that AND-type queries can be substantially better than XOR for approximate/noisy recovery.
- Empirical evidence (albeit limited) that AND queries are easier for workers and align with theory.
- Good coverage of related work and positioning within both ML and information theory.

Cons / points to clarify
- Crowdsourcing motivation for AND queries: AND explicitly references a particular label (e.g., “Are both action?”). If workers can identify labels, why not ask single-item labels directly? The paper argues that label queries need n queries for exact recovery while combining labels can beat n, but more discussion is needed: when does the platform allow label-specific questions, and when are we constrained to symmetric “same cluster” questions? Clarify the application scenarios and cost models that favor AND over direct labels, especially under noise and for approximate recovery targets.
- Experimental scope: Real-data experiments focus on AND; the “same cluster” comparison is small and uses a spectral baseline. While consistent with the theory, a more apples-to-apples comparison (e.g., ML decoder or stronger baselines for XOR in the approximate regime) would strengthen the case.
- Theorem statements: Expressions in Theorems 1–4 are heavy. Providing simplified corollaries (e.g., explicit constant-factor approximations, threshold regimes in p, Δ, q) and short proof sketches would aid readability.
- Assumptions: Results rely on known priors or accurate estimates thereof (though Theorem 6 relaxes this with known cluster sizes). A brief discussion of robustness to prior misspecification and degree heterogeneity would be useful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a short section explicitly comparing AND vs direct label queries under noiseless/noisy and exact/approximate recovery, including query complexity and error tradeoffs, to fully address the “why not ask labels?” question.
- Provide simplified corollaries for Theorems 1–4 with clean constants and intuitive regimes; move technical expressions to appendix.
- Expand the empirical comparison to include stronger XOR baselines for approximate recovery (e.g., tuned thresholding/likelihood decoders) and report calibration to match query budgets.
- Discuss extensions: partial adaptivity, heterogeneous degrees, correlated worker errors, and triadic queries vs AND.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and solid theory with clear insights (local encodability; AND > XOR for approximate/noisy).
- Simple, near-optimal non-adaptive algorithm with broad applicability.
- Well written and relevant to NIPS audiences in ML theory, crowdsourcing, and coding/information theory.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical evaluation and narrower practical validation of the AND vs XOR story.
- Some modeling assumptions (k-ary AND queries, known priors) may not always hold in practice.

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper makes a substantive theoretical contribution with clear implications for crowdsourced clustering, and the authors’ clarifications satisfactorily address concerns about motivation and evaluation.
