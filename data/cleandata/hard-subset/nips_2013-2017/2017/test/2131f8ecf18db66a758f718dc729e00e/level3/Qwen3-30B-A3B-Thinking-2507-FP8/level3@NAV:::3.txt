

This paper establishes a novel connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, providing theoretical bounds for clustering via crowdsourced similarity queries. The authors demonstrate that AND queries (which ask whether two elements share a specific label) outperform traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries in terms of query complexity for both noiseless and noisy settings. The information-theoretic framework is elegantly developed, with clear explanations of how the clustering problem maps to source coding constraints.

The paper excels in clarity and accessibility, making sophisticated information-theoretic concepts approachable for a general ML audience. The theoretical contributions are well-supported by empirical simulations, including a real crowdsourcing experiment validating the AND query approach. The connection to locally encodable source coding represents a significant conceptual advance, as the authors establish fundamental limits that were previously unexplored in this context.

Regarding strengths, the paper provides tight theoretical bounds (Theorems 1-4) that are both mathematically rigorous and practically meaningful. The experimental validation on a real crowdsourcing task demonstrates the practical relevance of the theoretical findings, showing that AND queries yield lower error rates than traditional "same cluster" queries.

However, two concerns require attention. First, the paper should address whether AND queries inadvertently reveal cluster labels, which contradicts the fundamental motivation for crowdsourcing (since if labels could be directly revealed, direct labeling would be preferable). Second, the expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1-4 could be simplified while preserving their essence, as the current formulations are somewhat cumbersome.

The experiments primarily use AND queries without sufficient justification for why "same cluster" queries were abandoned, raising questions about the validity of the comparison. While the authors' responses to these concerns appear satisfactory, these points should be clarified in the final version.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution with clear significance for crowdsourcing applications. The paper advances the state of the art in query-efficient clustering by establishing new theoretical limits and proposing a more efficient querying strategy. The work is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
