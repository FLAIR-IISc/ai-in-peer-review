- Paper: Presents an information-theoretic study of query-based clustering, linking query complexity to perfect/approximate recovery.  
- Paper: Establishes a novel equivalence between semi-supervised clustering and locally encodable source coding.  
- Paper: Provides theoretical upper and lower bounds on query complexity across several scenarios, including noisy oracles.  
- Paper: Introduces AND queries as a more efficient alternative to "same cluster" queries and analyzes them in noiseless and noisy settings.  
- Paper: Validates theoretical results with experiments, including a real-world crowdsourcing dataset.

- Strength: Novelty — bridges machine learning and information theory via a fresh equivalence likely to inspire follow-up work.  
- Strength: Theoretical rigor — provides detailed bounds and rigorous proofs, with compelling analysis of AND queries.  
- Strength: Practical relevance — real-world crowdsourcing experiment aligns with theoretical predictions.  
- Strength: Clarity in contributions — clearly lists new concepts, bounds, and an efficient noisy-query algorithm.

- Weakness: Limited structural assumptions — broad applicability but less practical leverage where structural priors exist.  
- Weakness: Experimental scope — experiments are limited in scale/diversity (small crowdsourcing dataset, limited synthetic settings).  
- Weakness: Comparison with prior work — lacks explicit empirical comparison on query efficiency and accuracy against existing methods.  
- Weakness: Presentation clarity — dense mathematical exposition may be hard for readers without an information-theory background.

- For acceptance: Novel, theoretically sound framework that advances both ML and information theory.  
- For acceptance: AND queries and analysis address limitations of existing query models.  
- For acceptance: Real-world validation supports practical relevance.

- Against acceptance: Experimental evaluation is not comprehensive enough to demonstrate robustness across diverse settings.  
- Against acceptance: Absence of structural assumptions may limit practical applicability in many real-world tasks.

- Recommendation: Recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and expand experimental evaluation.
