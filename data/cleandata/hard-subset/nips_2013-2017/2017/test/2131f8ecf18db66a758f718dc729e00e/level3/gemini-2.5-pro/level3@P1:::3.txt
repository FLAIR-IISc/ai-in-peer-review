This paper addresses the problem of semi-supervised clustering, where the goal is to recover the true labels of `n` items belonging to `k` clusters by making a minimum number of non-adaptive queries to an oracle. The central and novel contribution of the work is to establish an equivalence between this clustering problem and the information-theoretic problem of locally encodable source coding, where each compressed bit depends on only a small, constant number of input bits. This connection allows the authors to derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery of labels. The paper analyzes the performance of standard 'same-cluster' (XOR) queries and proposes a new type of query, the AND query, which is shown to be provably more efficient for approximate recovery, particularly in the presence of noise. The theoretical results are supported by a real-world crowdsourcing experiment.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of the paper is the novel connection it establishes between active clustering and locally encodable source coding. This is a significant conceptual contribution that provides a powerful new framework for analyzing the fundamental limits of query-based clustering. This perspective is not well-explored in the machine learning literature and could inspire new approaches to related problems in active learning and crowdsourcing.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper presents a solid and complete theoretical analysis. It derives new lower bounds for exact recovery that improve upon the standard entropy bound in certain regimes (Theorem 2) and provides a novel lower bound for approximate recovery (Theorem 3). The analysis of AND queries, culminating in an algorithm (Algorithm 1) with strong theoretical guarantees for the noisy, approximate recovery setting (Theorem 5), is a key contribution. The work is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by the proofs.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the information-theoretic viewpoint and motivating its application to the clustering problem, making the paper accessible to a broader NIPS audience who may not be experts in information theory. The problem setup and contributions are clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Motivation for AND Queries in a Crowdsourcing Context:** A central concern revolves around the practical motivation for the proposed AND queries. To answer a query like "Are both movies 'action' movies?", a crowd worker must know that the label of interest is 'action'. This seems to undermine one of the main motivations for using pairwise similarity queries in the first place, which is to elicit information when workers can compare items but may not know or agree upon explicit labels. If the worker can verify that an item has the label 'action', why not simply ask for the label of each item directly? The paper argues that label-queries are inefficient, but the proposed AND query appears to implicitly require the same level of knowledge. This makes the experimental setup feel somewhat artificial. Could the authors clarify the real-world scenarios where such queries are more practical than direct label queries?

2.  **Generality of Experimental Validation:** The experiment focuses exclusively on the proposed AND query. Given the concern raised above, this limits the validation of the paper's claims. The comparison against 'same cluster' queries is mentioned but seems to have been relegated to a synthetic dataset due to high error rates. While the high error rate for 'same cluster' queries is itself an interesting finding, it would strengthen the paper to see a more direct comparison of the algorithms on the same real-world data, even if the error model needs adjustment. The current experiment validates that Algorithm 1 works for the specific type of AND query posed, but it does not convincingly demonstrate its superiority over alternatives in a practical setting.

3.  **Complexity of Bound Expressions:** While technically sound, the expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1-4 are quite complex and difficult to parse intuitively. For instance, the expression for `δ` in Theorem 4 is unwieldy. To improve readability and impact, it would be beneficial if the authors could provide simplified asymptotic expressions or insightful interpretations of these bounds in the main body of the paper. This would help the reader to better appreciate the core trade-offs between the number of queries (`m`), the prior (`p`), and the error (`δ`).

**Summary of Review:**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a strong and original theoretical contribution. The framing of active clustering as a locally encodable source coding problem is elegant and opens up new avenues for research. The analysis of AND queries and their superiority in noisy settings is a significant result. The main weakness lies in the practical motivation of the AND query, which seems to conflict with the typical assumptions of crowdsourced clustering. If the authors can provide a convincing clarification on this point, it would substantially strengthen the paper. Despite this concern, the theoretical novelty and rigor of the work make it a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
