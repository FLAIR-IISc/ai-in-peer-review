This paper investigates the query complexity of non-adaptive, semi-supervised clustering. The problem setting involves recovering a ground-truth clustering by making a batch of queries to an oracle, which provides binary (and possibly noisy) answers about the relationships between small sets of points. The central and most original contribution is to frame this problem as one of locally encodable source coding, a concept from information theory. This novel perspective allows the authors to derive information-theoretic upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed for both perfect and approximate cluster recovery. This work connects to a growing body of literature on query-based clustering and crowdsourcing (e.g., Ashtiani et al., NIPS 2016) but distinguishes itself by focusing on fundamental information-theoretic limits rather than specific algorithmic objectives.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The connection drawn between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding is highly novel and represents a significant conceptual contribution. It brings a new set of powerful analytical tools to a problem of interest in machine learning, providing a deeper understanding of its fundamental limits. The proposal and analysis of "AND queries" as a provably more efficient alternative to standard "same-cluster" (XOR) queries for approximate recovery is another major strength and a key practical insight.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis that is technically sound. The derivation of distinct bounds for exact and approximate recovery, as well as the inclusion of a noise model, makes the work comprehensive. The authors are also transparent about the limitations of their analysis.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly motivated by practical applications like entity resolution. The small-scale crowdsourcing experiment, while not extensive, serves as a valuable proof-of-concept, validating the theoretical claim that AND queries can be more robust for human annotators.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Scope of Analysis:** The primary limitation is that the analysis makes no structural assumptions about the data (e.g., geometric separability of clusters). The resulting bounds depend mainly on the marginal label distribution and noise level. While this provides a clean information-theoretic result, it may not fully capture many practical clustering scenarios where the underlying data structure is key. This could be positioned as a clear direction for future work.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper that introduces a novel and fruitful perspective on query-based clustering. The strengths in originality and technical depth significantly outweigh the limitations in scope.
