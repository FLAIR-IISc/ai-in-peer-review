

**Review**

This paper establishes a novel connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding in information theory. The authors study the query complexity of clustering problems where an algorithm can ask "same cluster" (XOR) or "AND" queries to an oracle, with the goal of recovering the target clustering with minimal queries. They provide theoretical bounds on query complexity for both exact and approximate recovery, including analysis for noisy oracles, and validate their approach with a real crowdsourcing experiment.

The paper relates to previous work on clustering with side information (e.g., Ashtiani et al., NIPS 2016; Mazumdar & Saha, NIPS 2017) and locally decodable source codes (e.g., Makhdoumi et al., ISIT 2015), but makes a novel connection to locally encodable source coding, which is less studied than locally decodable codes. The authors show that "same cluster" queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery and propose AND queries as a superior alternative.

**Quality**: The paper provides technically sound theoretical results with well-supported claims. The authors derive new information-theoretic lower bounds and provide upper bounds that match the lower bounds in many cases. The proofs are rigorous and well-structured. The theoretical analysis is complemented by a real-world crowdsourcing experiment that validates the theoretical results. The paper carefully considers both strengths and weaknesses of the proposed methods.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and organized. The problem setup is clearly defined, and the theoretical results are presented with sufficient detail. The connection to information theory is well-explained, though some readers unfamiliar with source coding might find the initial sections challenging. The figures illustrating the theoretical bounds and experimental results are clear and helpful. The authors could improve clarity by providing more intuition for why AND queries outperform "same cluster" queries.

**Originality**: This is a highly original contribution. While there has been work on clustering with side information, the connection to locally encodable source coding is novel. The paper also introduces AND queries as a superior alternative to "same cluster" queries, which is a significant conceptual advance. The authors thoroughly review related work in NIPS proceedings and previous conferences, demonstrating that their work is distinct from prior contributions.

**Significance**: The results have clear significance for practical applications in crowdsourcing and entity resolution. The theoretical bounds provide fundamental limits on query complexity, and the proposed AND queries lead to substantially better performance in practice. The paper advances the state of the art by providing the first tight information-theoretic analysis of query complexity for clustering with side information.

**Strengths**: 
- Novel connection between clustering and information theory (locally encodable source coding)
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with tight bounds
- Practical validation via real crowdsourcing experiment
- Clear demonstration that AND queries outperform "same cluster" queries
- Well-motivated problem with important applications

**Weaknesses**: 
- The theoretical results mainly depend on the marginal distribution of labels, which may not capture all structural properties of real clustering problems
- The focus on binary labels (k=2) for the main theoretical results, with extension to k>2 being somewhat superficial
- The paper doesn't provide guidance for handling unknown label distributions in practice

**Arguments for acceptance**: 
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution with novel connections between information theory and clustering
- The results are well-supported by both theory and experiments
- The work has clear practical implications for crowdsourcing applications
- The paper is well-written and addresses the relevant literature thoroughly

**Arguments against acceptance**: 
- The theoretical results are somewhat limited to specific assumptions (e.g., known label distribution, binary labels)
- The practical validation is limited to a small-scale experiment

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the theoretical understanding of query-based clustering. While the results have some limitations in scope, they provide important new insights and tools for future work. The connection to information theory is particularly valuable, as it opens up new avenues for applying information-theoretic techniques to clustering problems. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a problem of clear significance to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
