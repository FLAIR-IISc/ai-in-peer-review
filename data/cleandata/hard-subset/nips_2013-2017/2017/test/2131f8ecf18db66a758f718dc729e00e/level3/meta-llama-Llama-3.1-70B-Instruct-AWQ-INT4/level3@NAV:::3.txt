The paper "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding" presents a novel approach to semisupervised clustering by leveraging the concept of locally encodable source coding from information theory. The authors tackle the problem of clustering via crowdsourced similarity queries and provide bounds for both noisy and noiseless answers.

The main contribution of this paper is theoretical, supported by empirical simulations. The authors show that a recently popular model of semisupervised clustering is equivalent to locally encodable source coding, which leads to new information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required to recover the correct labeling. They also propose pairwise AND queries, which provably perform better than traditional 'same cluster' queries in many situations.

The paper is well-written and accessible to a general machine learning audience. The information-theoretic connection for deriving bounds is explained clearly, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of their results.

One concern is that AND queries may reveal the cluster label, conflicting with the crowdsourcing motivation. However, the authors argue that this is not a significant issue, as the queries can be designed to be simple and binary, reducing the risk of revealing sensitive information.

The reviewer suggests simplifying the expressions for bounds in Theorems 1-4 while preserving their essence. Additionally, the experiments appear to use only AND queries, raising validity concerns unless the AND-query motivation/implications are clarified.

Despite these minor concerns, the reviewer is satisfied with the authors' responses and recommends acceptance. The paper makes significant contributions to the field of semisupervised clustering and provides new insights into the limitations of traditional 'same cluster' queries.
