

**Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"**

This paper establishes a novel connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding in information theory. The authors consider a crowdsourcing-based clustering model where the goal is to label unlabeled elements by asking queries to an oracle, with each query involving at most Δ elements. The main contribution is showing that this problem is equivalent to locally encodable source coding, enabling information-theoretic lower bounds on query complexity. They introduce AND queries as a more efficient alternative to traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries, demonstrating theoretically and empirically that AND queries can recover labels with fewer queries and lower error rates.

The paper builds upon recent work in crowdsourcing for entity resolution (e.g., [8, 20, 21, 33]) and clustering with side information (e.g., [4, 22]), extending the "same cluster" query framework by introducing AND queries. The information-theoretic approach to analyzing query complexity is novel, as it connects to the under-studied problem of locally encodable source coding.

**Strengths:**
- **Quality:** The theoretical results are rigorously proved with matching upper and lower bounds. The authors derive meaningful bounds for exact and approximate recovery in both noiseless and noisy settings (Theorems 1-5), and the empirical validation in Section 4 provides strong support for their theoretical claims.
- **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and accessible to a general machine learning audience. The information-theoretic connections are explained clearly, and the technical content is logically organized with well-motivated theorems.
- **Originality:** The connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is novel. The introduction of AND queries as a more efficient alternative to "same cluster" queries represents a significant conceptual advance, as these queries can reveal more information per query while being easier for crowd workers to answer (validated in their experiment).
- **Significance:** The results have clear practical implications for designing efficient crowdsourcing schemes for clustering tasks. The paper demonstrates that AND queries can substantially reduce query complexity for approximate recovery, which is directly relevant to budget-constrained crowdsourcing applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- **AND Query Motivation:** The main concern is that AND queries may reveal cluster labels directly (e.g., an AND query returning 1 implies both elements are in the "1" cluster), which conflicts with the motivation for crowdsourcing. If the goal is to avoid asking for labels directly, the authors should clarify why AND queries are preferable to direct label queries (which would require at least n queries for exact recovery). This needs stronger justification.
- **Experimental Validation:** The experiments in Section 4 appear to use only AND queries without comparing to "same cluster" queries in the same setting. The authors should provide a more comprehensive comparison to address whether AND queries are truly better than other approaches in realistic crowdsourcing scenarios.
- **Technical Presentation:** While the theorems are well-proven, expressions for bounds in Theorems 1-4 are somewhat complex. The authors could simplify these expressions while preserving their essence to enhance accessibility.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Novel theoretical connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding
- Introduction of AND queries as a more efficient alternative to "same cluster" queries
- Strong theoretical guarantees with matching upper and lower bounds
- Empirical validation on real crowdsourcing data
- Well-written and accessible to NIPS audience

*Con:*
- The motivation for AND queries versus direct label queries needs clearer justification
- Experimental validation could be more comprehensive with direct comparisons
- Some technical expressions could be simplified for better readability

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the problem of efficient clustering via crowdsourcing, with clear practical implications. While the motivation for AND queries requires clarification, the theoretical results are strong, the writing is excellent, and the paper is highly relevant to NIPS. The authors have already addressed some concerns in their responses, and the paper is in a strong position for acceptance. The connection to information theory is particularly compelling, and the AND query approach represents a meaningful advance over existing methods for approximate recovery. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the AND query motivation and simplify technical expressions.
