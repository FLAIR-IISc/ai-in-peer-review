- Studies query-efficient clustering/labeling with simple oracle queries and connects the problem to locally encodable source coding.  
- Treats both perfect and noisy oracles and characterizes query complexity for exact and approximate recovery.  
- Contribution (i): improved lower bounds on queries via information-theoretic arguments tailored to locality constraints.  
- Contribution (ii): achievability for exact recovery using parity (XOR) queries below n in certain regimes.  
- Contribution (iii): formalizes limitations of same-cluster/XOR queries for approximate recovery using linear rate–distortion results.  
- Contribution (iv): introduces pairwise AND queries with decoding achieving O(n log(1/δ)) queries to recover a (1−δ) fraction of labels, extended to k-ary outputs and noisy responses.  
- Positions work relative to prior NIPS/IT literature and to locally decodable/smooth compression, emphasizing the complementary “locally encodable” viewpoint.  
- Technical core appears sound, using typical-set/expurgation, Chernoff bounds on regular graphs, and rate–distortion arguments; AND-vs-XOR separation supported theoretically and with small-scale experiments.  
- Some steps need more substantiation: tightness of approximate-recovery lower bounds and the Massey-based conjecture specialized to XOR lack nonasymptotic or stronger evidence.  
- Clarity: generally well-organized but notation is heavy and several proofs are deferred; recommend a “toolkit” with intuition, explicit constants, and clearer comparison to known O(n log n) random XOR results.  
- Originality: high — the locally encodable coding lens and AND-query advantage in noisy/approximate recovery are novel.  
- Significance: advances information-theoretic limits and suggests practical query designs, but scope limited by iid label/noise assumptions, nonadaptive querying, and modest empirical validation.  
- Pros: novel theoretical framework, clear AND vs XOR separation, handles noisy oracles with simple scalable decoding, relevant to crowdsourcing and deduplication.  
- Cons: assumes iid labels and independent noise, focuses on nonadaptive queries, experiments are limited and lack broader baselines, and some lower bounds rely on conjectural linear-code limitations.  
- Overall: solid, insightful first step on oracle-based clustering; reviewer leans toward acceptance.
