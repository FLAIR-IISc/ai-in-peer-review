This paper studies query-efficient clustering/labeling with access to simple oracle queries and draws a precise connection to locally encodable source coding. The authors consider both perfect and noisy oracles and characterize the query complexity for exact and approximate recovery. Key contributions include: (i) improved lower bounds on the number of queries via information-theoretic arguments tailored to locality constraints; (ii) achievability for exact recovery using parity (XOR) queries below n in certain regimes; (iii) limitations of “same-cluster” (XOR) queries for approximate recovery, formalized via classical results on linear rate–distortion codes; and (iv) a new class of pairwise AND queries with decoding schemes that achieve O(n log(1/δ)) queries for recovering a (1−δ) fraction of labels, extended to k-ary outputs and noisy responses. The work relates to prior NIPS/IT literature on query-based clustering and crowdsourcing (e.g., same-cluster queries, budgeted elicitation) and to locally decodable/smooth compression (Makhdoumi et al.; Montanari–Mossel), but focuses on the complementary “locally encodable” viewpoint.

Quality: The technical core appears sound and careful, with proofs leveraging typical-set/expurgation, Chernoff bounds on regular graphs, and rate–distortion arguments. The AND-vs-XOR separation is well-motivated and supported theoretically and empirically (small-scale). Some steps (e.g., tightness of approximate-recovery lower bounds, the Massey-based conjecture specialized to XOR) could be further substantiated or bounded nonasymptotically.

Clarity: Generally well-organized, though notation is heavy and several proofs are deferred. A brief “toolkit” section with intuition for the main inequalities, explicit constants in thresholds, and a clearer comparison to known O(n log n) results for random XOR queries would aid readability.

Originality: High. The locally encodable coding lens and the concrete advantage of AND queries in noisy, approximate recovery are novel and likely unfamiliar to much of the ML community.

Significance: The work advances our understanding of information-theoretic limits for query-based clustering and suggests practically actionable query designs (AND) for crowdsourcing and entity resolution. Scope is limited by strong independence assumptions and nonadaptive querying; empirical validation is modest.

Pros:
- Novel theoretical framework and bounds under locality constraints
- Clear separation showing AND queries outperform XOR for approximation
- Handles noisy oracles; simple, scalable decoding
- Relevance to crowdsourcing and deduplication

Cons:
- Assumes iid labels and independent noise; no structural/hypothesis assumptions
- Nonadaptive focus; no adaptivity–complexity tradeoff
- Experimental evaluation is small and lacks comparisons beyond basic baselines
- Some lower bounds depend on conjectural linear-code limitations

Overall: A solid, insightful first step on information-theoretic limits for oracle-based clustering; I lean toward acceptance.
