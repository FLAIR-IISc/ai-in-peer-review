## Review of “Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding”

This paper investigates the query complexity of semisupervised clustering, framing it as a locally encodable source coding problem. The authors establish connections between this clustering task and information theory, deriving upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required for accurate label recovery, both in noiseless and noisy oracle settings. They further demonstrate that AND queries can outperform traditional “same cluster” (XOR) queries for approximate recovery, and validate their findings with experiments on real datasets.

The paper builds upon a recent trend in clustering via querying oracles, a paradigm gaining traction in areas like entity resolution and crowdsourcing (e.g., [4, 8, 20, 33]).  The central idea of reducing this problem to locally encodable source coding is a novel and potentially impactful framing, as it allows the application of well-established information-theoretic tools. Prior work in related areas like locally decodable codes [19, 16, 26] and smooth compression [23] is acknowledged, though the authors correctly point out the relative scarcity of results specifically for locally *encodable* codes.  This connection to information theory provides a strong foundation for their theoretical contributions.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The reduction of the semisupervised clustering problem to locally encodable source coding is a key innovation. This perspective offers a fresh approach to analyzing the query complexity of clustering.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a mathematically sound analysis, deriving both upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required. The bounds are presented for both perfect and approximate recovery, as well as for noisy oracles.
* **Practical Relevance:** The motivation stemming from crowdsourcing and entity resolution is compelling. The validation through experiments on real datasets, albeit limited in scope, strengthens the practical relevance of the findings.
* **Insightful Query Design:** The proposal of AND queries as a potentially superior alternative to XOR (“same cluster”) queries is a valuable contribution. The authors provide theoretical justification and experimental support for this claim.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-organized, and the authors strive to explain the concepts in a clear and accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Assumptions:** The analysis relies on relatively few assumptions, which while simplifying the theoretical development, limits the applicability of the results. Notably, there’s an absence of structural assumptions on the underlying data or the hypothesis class being considered.  This means the bounds may be loose for scenarios where specific data properties are known.
* **Experimental Validation:** While the real-world experiment provides some validation, it is relatively small in scale. More extensive experimental evaluation, including comparisons to existing clustering algorithms under similar oracle constraints, would further strengthen the results.
* **Complexity of Proofs:** Some of the proofs, particularly those related to the lower bounds, appear quite involved. A more streamlined presentation or a more detailed breakdown of the key steps would be beneficial for readers.
* **Discussion of Related Work:** The paper mentions relevant prior work, but a more thorough discussion of how the current work builds upon or diverges from those contributions would be valuable. For example, relating the results to the stochastic block model or other related graph clustering problems might be insightful.
* **Repetition of Information:** Some sections, such as the introduction, contain a degree of repetition, particularly when outlining the problem and related work.


**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles an important and timely problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* The connection to locally encodable source coding is a significant contribution that opens up new avenues for research.
* The theoretical results are well-supported and provide valuable insights into the fundamental limits of query-based clustering.
* The proposal and validation of AND queries offer a practical improvement over existing methods.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The limited scope of assumptions and the lack of strong structural results may limit the practical impact of the paper.
* The experimental validation is relatively limited and could be strengthened with more extensive evaluations.
* The complexity of the proofs may hinder accessibility for some readers.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of semisupervised clustering. The novel framing of the problem as a locally encodable source coding task, combined with the rigorous theoretical analysis and the insightful proposal of AND queries, make this work deserving of acceptance. While the limitations regarding assumptions and experimental validation should be acknowledged, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity of proofs and potentially expand the discussion of related work.
