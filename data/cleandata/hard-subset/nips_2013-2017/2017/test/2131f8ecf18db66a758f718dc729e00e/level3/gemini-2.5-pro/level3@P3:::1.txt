This paper studies the problem of semi-supervised clustering where the goal is to recover a ground-truth partition of `n` items into `k` clusters. The learning algorithm operates by making a set of non-adaptive queries to an oracle. Each query involves a small, constant number of items (`Δ`), and the oracle provides a binary (or `k`-ary) answer, which may be noisy. The primary goal is to characterize the query complexity—the minimum number of queries required—for both perfect and approximate recovery of the true clustering.

The central and most novel contribution of the paper is to establish a formal equivalence between this query-based clustering problem and the information-theoretic problem of locally encodable source coding. In this framing, the labels of the items form a source vector, and the query answers are the compressed representation. The "local" constraint comes from the fact that each query (a compressed bit) depends on only a few source elements (item labels). This fresh perspective allows the authors to leverage powerful tools from information theory to derive fundamental lower bounds on the query complexity.

This work builds upon a growing literature in clustering with queries, particularly in the context of crowdsourcing and entity resolution (e.g., Ashtiani et al., NIPS 2016; Mazumdar and Saha, NIPS 2017). However, previous works often focused on adaptive querying schemes or specific objectives like k-means, whereas this paper provides a non-adaptive, information-theoretic analysis of the recovery problem itself. The key results include: (1) For noiseless, exact recovery with two clusters, the authors provide upper and lower bounds that show, perhaps surprisingly, that fewer than `n` "same-cluster" queries can suffice when the prior distribution of cluster sizes is known and imbalanced. (2) For approximate recovery, they derive a new lower bound and compellingly argue that standard "same-cluster" (XOR) queries are suboptimal. They propose "AND" queries as an alternative and demonstrate their superior performance. (3) For the noisy oracle setting, they present an efficient algorithm based on AND-type queries that achieves `(1-δ)`-approximate recovery with `O(n log(k/δ))` queries. A small crowdsourcing experiment provides empirical validation for their claims.

### Strengths

**Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength lies in its originality. The connection drawn between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding is a genuinely novel and insightful contribution to the machine learning community. This new viewpoint allows the authors to derive fundamental, algorithm-independent lower bounds on query complexity, which is a significant achievement. These results advance our theoretical understanding of the limits of learning from simple, local queries. The finding that AND queries can be provably more efficient than the commonly used same-cluster (XOR) queries is a practically important insight that could directly influence the design of crowdsourcing tasks for problems like entity resolution.

**Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the theoretical claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs. The application of tools from rate-distortion theory and coding theory to derive the bounds is non-trivial and executed skillfully. The work is comprehensive, addressing both noiseless and noisy oracles, as well as exact and approximate recovery criteria. This provides a fairly complete picture of the information-theoretic landscape of the problem.

**Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is well-written and the problem is clearly motivated with relevant applications in crowdsourcing and data deduplication. The authors do a good job of explaining the information-theoretic analogy, making a potentially esoteric topic accessible. The inclusion of an experiment on a real crowdsourced dataset, though small-scale, effectively grounds the theoretical claims and validates the proposed model and algorithm.

### Weaknesses

**Limited Scope of Assumptions:** The main limitation of the analysis is its reliance on the assumption that item labels are drawn i.i.d. from a known prior distribution `p`. This effectively models the problem as one of pure label recovery, ignoring any underlying feature space or structural properties of the data (e.g., geometric separation between clusters) that are central to much of the clustering literature. Consequently, the derived bounds depend primarily on the entropy of the label distribution and the noise level, rather than on properties of the data instances themselves. While this provides a clean information-theoretic result, it limits the applicability of the findings to scenarios where such structure is irrelevant.

**Practicality of Decoding:** The paper focuses more on deriving bounds on the number of queries (the encoding part) than on the design of efficient recovery algorithms (the decoding part). For the exact recovery case, the achievability result relies on a Maximum Likelihood decoder, which is generally computationally intractable. For the approximate recovery with AND queries, a simple and efficient thresholding-based algorithm is provided, but it is not clear if this algorithm is optimal or how it relates to the information-theoretic lower bounds. A deeper discussion of the trade-offs between query complexity and decoding complexity would have strengthened the paper.

**Clarity of Some Results:** While generally clear, some theoretical results are presented in a form that is difficult to parse. For example, the exact expression for `δ` in Theorem 4 is quite complex, and a more intuitive asymptotic analysis or a simplified bound would have been more illuminating for the reader.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **High Originality:** Establishes a novel and powerful connection between query-based clustering and information theory.
2.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** Provides fundamental lower bounds on query complexity, advancing the state of the art in the theoretical understanding of this problem.
3.  **Actionable Insights:** The introduction and analysis of AND queries as a superior alternative to same-cluster queries is a valuable and practical result for designing crowdsourcing systems.
4.  **Solid Technical Execution:** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs and a comprehensive analysis.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  **Restricted Model:** The i.i.d. label assumption ignores data structure, limiting the scope and applicability of the results compared to mainstream clustering analysis.
2.  **Focus on Intractable Decoders:** The achievability results for exact recovery rely on non-constructive arguments or computationally intractable decoders.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novelty and theoretical significance, far outweigh its weaknesses. It introduces a new and fruitful way of thinking about a relevant problem in machine learning and provides important foundational results. This is a valuable contribution that is likely to inspire follow-up work on both the theoretical and practical fronts.
