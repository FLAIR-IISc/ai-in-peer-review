Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies non-adaptive, query-based clustering/labeling with a noisy oracle that answers simple functions of small subsets of items’ labels. It makes a clean connection to locally encodable source coding: each query answer is a compressed bit that depends on at most Δ labels. For k=2, “same-cluster” queries are XORs; the paper shows limitations of XOR for approximate recovery and proposes pairwise AND queries that can strictly outperform XOR in both noiseless and noisy regimes.

Main technical results:
- Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2): An upper bound using Δ-ary XOR queries and ML decoding achieves m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries with α = 1/2(1+(1−4p(1−p))^Δ), strictly less than n for a nontrivial range of p even for Δ=2. A lower bound improves on the naive n h(p) bound for some p.
- Noiseless, approximate recovery (k=2): A locality-aware lower bound strengthens rate–distortion limits when each query involves at most Δ labels. The paper argues that XOR-based schemes are fundamentally limited for approximation (invoking classical results on linear rate–distortion codes). It then analyzes a simple non-adaptive AND-query scheme and provides explicit error–query tradeoffs that can beat any XOR-only scheme over a range of regimes.
- Noisy, approximate recovery (general k): With a k-ary “multi-class AND” query (returns i if both endpoints have label i, else 0), a simple thresholding algorithm on a d-regular graph gives (1−δ)-accurate recovery with m = O(n log(k/δ)) queries under independent per-query noise (correct w.p. 1−q; each wrong option equally likely). The analysis relies on Chernoff bounds and bounded-dependence concentration. The method also works with known cluster sizes instead of priors.

Relation to prior work includes: same-cluster queries and interactive clustering [Ashtiani et al., NIPS’16], crowdsourcing reliability and task allocation [Karger–Oh–Shah], query complexity for noisy clustering [Mazumdar–Saha], and spectral clustering baselines. The information-theoretic framing builds on rate–distortion, locally decodable/smooth compression [Makhdoumi et al.; Montanari–Mossel], and a classical observation on linear codes’ suboptimality for lossy compression [Massey/Ancheta].

Strengths
- Novel and timely perspective: The equivalence to locally encodable source coding is elegant and informative, yielding new lower bounds that explicitly account for locality (Δ).
- Concrete positive results: Simple non-adaptive schemes (AND queries) with explicit, near-optimal query complexity for approximate recovery; clean dependence on n, k, δ, and noise q.
- Clear separation between XOR and AND: Theoretical and empirical evidence that AND queries can be superior for approximation, aligning with human-cognition arguments in crowdsourcing.
- Scope beyond theory: A small but thoughtful crowdsourcing experiment supports modeling choices and practical feasibility.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Assumptions on label generation and noise: i.i.d. labels with known priors (or exact cluster sizes) and independent query noise may be restrictive for many clustering settings with structure (graphs/metrics) or correlated labels.
- Query model practicality for k>2: The k-ary “multi-class AND” oracle is natural theoretically but may be less standard in practice than same-cluster or one-vs-rest label queries; empirical validation for k>2 is limited.
- Non-adaptivity only: While well-motivated for parallelization, the paper does not explore how adaptivity could further reduce query complexity or whether lower bounds extend to adaptive settings.
- Some proofs deferred; dependence on approximations: The AND-query analysis uses Poissonization and bounded dependency arguments; more explicit constants and finite-sample guidance would help practitioners.
- Experimental evaluation is small-scale and task difficulty may differ across query types (fairness of AND vs XOR comparisons could be better controlled).

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Generally strong. The main theorems appear sound and use appropriate information-theoretic and probabilistic tools. Clear articulation of when bounds beat classical n h(p) and when AND helps. Some claims (e.g., linear-code conjecture for all p) are positioned as conjectures; good.
- Clarity: Mostly clear and well-organized. Exposition of the information-theoretic mapping is helpful. A few notation inconsistencies (H vs h), minor typos, and places where constants could be summarized would improve readability.
- Originality: High. The locally encodable viewpoint, the lower bounds under locality, and the AND-vs-XOR contrast for approximate recovery are novel to the NIPS community.
- Significance: Good. Establishes fundamental limits for a crowd-query clustering model under realistic constraints and provides simple, implementable schemes that can inform practice. Scope is limited by assumptions, but this is a solid first step.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Adaptivity: Can your lower bounds be extended to adaptive queries? Is there an adaptive AND scheme that further reduces queries or improves constants?
- Practical k-ary queries: For k>2, how would you implement the multi-class AND in a crowd UI? One k-ary prompt vs k binary prompts changes cost and noise; discuss this trade-off.
- Robustness: How sensitive are the thresholds to misspecified priors/cluster-size estimates? Can you provide bounds under model mismatch?
- Correlated noise: Do your results extend if worker errors are correlated or if noise depends on item difficulty?
- Comparisons: For the noisy case (k=2), can you compare empirically against state-of-the-art majority-vote and inference methods (e.g., Dawid–Skene variants) under the same query budget?
- Empirical fairness: Please ensure within-worker randomization and matched difficulty for AND vs XOR so error-rate differences are not due to task framing.

Minor comments
- Clean up repeated/partial references and formatting; define α on first use; standardize H(p) vs h(p).
- Clarify the exact regime where the Theorem 1 upper bound is < n for Δ=2.
- Explicit constants in Theorem 5 (d vs δ, k, q, and minimal d for a target δ) would aid practitioners.

Arguments for acceptance
- New conceptual bridge to locally encodable source coding with concrete lower/upper bounds.
- Simple, near-optimal non-adaptive algorithms with provable advantages of AND queries.
- Both theory and (preliminary) practical validation; of interest to NIPS communities in clustering, crowdsourcing, and information theory.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited model realism (i.i.d. labels; independent noise; non-adaptive only) and modest empirical scope.
- Practicality of k-ary query primitive for k>2 may be debatable without stronger user studies.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The conceptual novelty and solid technical results outweigh limitations; the work should stimulate follow-up research on adaptive schemes, more realistic noise/structure, and practical query design.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
