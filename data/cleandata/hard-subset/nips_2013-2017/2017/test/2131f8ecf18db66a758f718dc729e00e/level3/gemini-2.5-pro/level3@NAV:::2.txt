This paper studies the problem of reconstructing cluster labels from a limited budget of noisy, non-adaptive queries involving a small, constant number of elements. The authors make a novel connection between this semi-supervised clustering problem and the information-theoretic concept of locally encodable source coding. This framework is used to derive lower bounds on the required number of queries for both exact and approximate recovery. The main algorithmic contribution is a new query type, the "AND-query," which is proposed as a more efficient alternative to the standard "same-cluster" (or XOR) query. The paper presents an algorithm based on these queries and provides an evaluation on real-world crowdsourced data.

The paper's primary strength is its originality in framing the clustering query problem through the lens of source coding, which offers a new perspective for deriving theoretical limits. The focus on non-adaptive queries is significant, as these are highly parallelizable and practical for real-world systems like crowdsourcing. The introduction of AND-queries is a novel idea that could potentially advance the state of the art.

However, the paper has several major weaknesses in its current form, primarily concerning technical quality, clarity, and the validation of its claims.

**Quality and Clarity:**
The theoretical results are not well-supported or clearly presented. Theorem 1 appears to conflict with prior work by Ahn–Lee–Suh on query complexity in the noiseless, exact-recovery setting, and its proof is difficult to follow. Theorems 3 and 4, which present lower and upper bounds, suffer from interpretability issues; the expressions are complex, and it is not clear how the number of queries scales or how the bounds relate to each other. Furthermore, the model's restriction disallowing repeated identical queries is not well-justified and may be practically limiting.

**Experimental Validation:**
The empirical evaluation is insufficient to support the paper's central claims. In Figure 2, the proposed AND-query algorithm is only compared against a lower bound for same-cluster queries, not an actual implementation, making claims of superiority unsubstantiated. Similarly, Figures 3-5 lack comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, failing to demonstrate that AND-queries outperform existing schemes in practice. Algorithm 1's practicality is also questionable, as it requires prior knowledge of cluster sizes, and the process for estimating these is not discussed.

In summary, while the paper introduces an interesting and original direction, the core technical contributions are undermined by unclear proofs, potential inconsistencies with prior work, and a lack of rigorous empirical comparison. The claims of superiority for the proposed AND-query method are not convincingly demonstrated.
