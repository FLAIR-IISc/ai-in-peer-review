- Novel connection established between semi-supervised clustering and locally encodable source coding.
- Proposal of an AND-query approach as an alternative to traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries.
- Theoretical lower bounds derived and justification that AND queries can outperform XOR in certain regimes.
- Algorithm 1 claims (1-δ)-good recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries for k clusters.
- Theoretical results for noisy queries (Theorem 5) are highlighted as practically significant.
- Empirical validation via a crowdsourcing experiment on movie classification showing fewer errors for AND queries (105 vs. 234).
- Major concern: restrictive model assumption forbidding repeated identical queries, which contradicts common crowdsourcing error-reduction practices.
- Authors fail to justify the no-repetition assumption with evidence or to analyze the more practical case allowing query repetition.
- Serious theoretical inconsistency: Theorem 1’s query complexity conflicts with prior work (Ahn-Lee-Suh 2016) that reports Θ(n log n) for pairwise exact recovery.
- Proof of Theorem 1 is hard to follow and does not resolve the discrepancy with prior results, raising technical-soundness doubts.
- Key theoretical results (Theorems 3 and 4) are mathematically complex and lack intuitive explanation or clear interpretability.
- Gap between the lower and upper bounds is not clearly established or explained.
- Figure 2 comparison is incomplete: it omits performance of actual "same cluster" query algorithms, undermining the superiority claims for AND queries.
- Algorithm 1’s practical implementation depends on knowing the prior distribution p or cluster sizes, which is rarely available in practice.
- Theorem 6’s robustness claim lacks a practical method for estimating cluster sizes from data.
- Pseudocode for Algorithm 1 is difficult to follow and under-explained.
- Experimental evaluation fails to compare against state-of-the-art methods or best implementations of "same cluster" queries with error correction.
- Overall assessment: interesting theoretical idea and promising empirical hint, but undermined by restrictive assumptions, theoretical inconsistencies, poor interpretability, and insufficient experimental comparisons; major revisions needed for acceptance.
