## Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper investigates the problem of learning cluster labels from noisy, non-adaptive queries to an oracle, framing it as a locally encodable source coding problem. The authors establish lower bounds on the number of required queries and propose an algorithm employing AND-queries that purportedly outperforms traditional ‘same cluster’ queries, validating their approach through a real-world crowdsourcing experiment. While the paper tackles an interesting problem with connections to information theory and crowdsourcing, its presentation suffers from clarity issues, lacks sufficient comparative analysis, and contains potential inconsistencies with existing work.

The central idea—equating semisupervised clustering with locally encodable source coding—is novel and provides a valuable theoretical framework for analyzing query complexity.  The derivation of lower bounds and the proposition of AND-queries as an alternative to XOR (same cluster) queries represent a significant attempt to push the boundaries of efficient clustering with limited, noisy information. Related work is acknowledged, referencing key papers in crowdsourcing, entity resolution, and information theory (e.g., [4, 12, 14, 17, 23]). However, the connection to existing approaches, particularly in the context of adaptive querying algorithms [8, 20, 21, 22], could be further elaborated.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  The connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is a compelling and relatively unexplored avenue.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper attempts to provide rigorous theoretical analysis, including lower bounds on query complexity.
* **Practical Validation:**  The inclusion of a crowdsourcing experiment provides some empirical support for the proposed AND-query approach.
* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The problem setup is well-defined, and the motivation for minimizing query complexity in crowdsourcing scenarios is clear.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is dense and suffers from a lack of clarity in presentation. Theorems and proofs are often difficult to follow, hindering a full understanding of the results. The notation is also sometimes inconsistent.
* **Justification of Model Assumptions:** The restriction to non-adaptive queries and the prohibition of repeated identical queries is a significant assumption that lacks sufficient justification. The authors should either provide a stronger rationale or analyze the implications of relaxing this constraint. Considering an independent annotator setting would also strengthen the work.
* **Inconsistency with Prior Work:** Theorem 1’s claim regarding query complexity in the noiseless, exact-recovery scenario appears to contradict findings in related work, particularly by Ahn, Lee, and Suh [2]. A thorough reconciliation or explanation of this discrepancy is crucial.
* **Limited Comparative Analysis:**  Crucially, the paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to existing clustering algorithms, such as those based on ‘same cluster’ queries, both theoretically and experimentally.  Figure 2, for instance, only compares against the *lower bound* of the ‘same cluster’ query method, not the algorithm itself.  Demonstrating a clear advantage over established methods is vital.
* **Interpretability of Results:** The lower bound expressions in Theorems 3 and 4 are complex and difficult to interpret. It’s unclear how the number of queries (m) scales with the dataset size (n) and the desired error tolerance (δ).  A more intuitive understanding of these scaling relationships is needed.
* **Practical Considerations:**  The reliance on prior knowledge of the label distribution (p) or cluster sizes is a limitation. The paper does not address the practical challenges of estimating these parameters accurately. The pseudocode for Algorithm 1 is also quite dense and requires deep understanding of the proofs to follow effectively.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful theoretical framework for understanding query complexity in semisupervised clustering.
* The initial empirical results provide some support for the proposed AND-query approach.
* The problem addressed is relevant to growing fields like crowdsourcing and entity resolution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper suffers from significant clarity issues and lacks a thorough comparative analysis.
* The theoretical claims require further scrutiny and reconciliation with existing work.
* The strong assumptions regarding query structure and the lack of discussion of practical estimation challenges raise concerns about the applicability of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **Weak Reject**. While the core idea is intriguing and the authors demonstrate a significant theoretical effort, the lack of clarity, limited comparative analysis, and potential inconsistencies with prior work prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. Significant revisions addressing the concerns outlined above, including a more thorough experimental evaluation and improved presentation, would be necessary for reconsideration. The authors should focus on clarifying the theoretical contributions, providing a more convincing comparison to existing methods, and addressing the limitations of their assumptions.
