

This paper studies clustering with same-cluster queries to an oracle, establishing information-theoretic bounds on query complexity for exact and approximate recovery. The authors make a novel connection between query-based clustering and locally encodable source coding in information theory, which they use to derive tight upper and lower bounds. They demonstrate that "same-cluster" (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery and propose AND queries that require significantly fewer queries to achieve comparable accuracy.

The work builds on recent advances in query-based clustering [4, 21, 33] while introducing information-theoretic tools previously unused in the ML community. The motivation is well-justified with applications in crowdsourcing and record deduplication, and the authors validate their theoretical findings with a real-world crowdsourcing experiment showing AND queries yield lower error rates than same-cluster queries.

Strengths include the elegant theoretical framework, rigorous proofs establishing tight bounds, and practical validation through crowdsourcing experiments. The paper makes a clear step toward understanding information-theoretic limits of query-based clustering. The AND query approach represents a meaningful methodological advancement over existing techniques.

Weaknesses include the lack of structural assumptions (e.g., hypothesis class), limiting the analysis to marginal label distributions and noise levels. While the paper acknowledges this limitation, it means the results may not generalize to more complex clustering scenarios. The focus on pairwise queries (Δ=2) also restricts the generality of the findings.

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written with good organization, and makes a significant theoretical contribution. The connection to locally encodable source coding is novel, and the results advance the state of the art in query complexity for clustering.

The significance lies in providing fundamental insights into query-based clustering's information-theoretic limits, which will inform future work in active learning and crowdsourcing. While the scope is somewhat limited by the lack of structural assumptions, the paper's clean theoretical framework and practical validation make it a valuable contribution to the field. This work provides essential theoretical foundations for understanding when and how query-based clustering can be efficiently implemented.
