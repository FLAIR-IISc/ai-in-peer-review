Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonadaptive query-based clustering/label recovery with an oracle that answers simple functions of at most Δ elements’ labels. The core idea is to recast this semisupervised clustering scenario as “locally encodable source coding,” where each compressed bit depends on a constant number of source bits. This yields information-theoretic lower bounds and simple near-optimal querying/decoding schemes.

Contributions span three regimes:
- Noiseless, exact recovery (k=2): Using Δ-ary XOR queries (pairwise “same-cluster” when Δ=2) and a typical-set/expurgation ML argument, the authors show achievability with m ≈ n h(p) / log(1/α) queries, α = 1/2(1 + (1 − 4p(1 − p))^Δ), strictly below n for a nontrivial range of p even with Δ=2; they also give a locality-aware lower bound improving on n h(p) for small p.
- Noiseless, approximate recovery (k=2): A new lower bound combines rate–distortion with Δ-local constraints (Theorem 3). Crucially, they show that XOR/same-cluster queries are fundamentally limited for approximate recovery (invoking classical results on linear codes’ suboptimality for rate–distortion) and propose AND queries with a simple decoding that provably outperform same-cluster queries over a range of m,δ. Theoretical performance is derived via a Poisson degree model on random pairwise designs.
- Noisy, approximate recovery (k≥2): With a k-ary “AND-like” pairwise query Q(X,X′)=i if both labels equal i, otherwise 0, under a BSC-like oracle noise model, they present a thresholding algorithm on a d-regular query graph that achieves (1−δ)-accurate labeling with m=O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive queries; the proof uses Chernoff bounds and a dependency-graph concentration argument. They also show robustness to using empirical priors via random permutation.

The work connects to prior crowdsourcing/ER papers at NIPS and beyond (e.g., Ashtiani et al. on same-cluster queries; Vinayak & Hassibi on triangle queries; Karger/Oh/Shah on crowdsourcing) as well as coding-theoretic notions of local decodability and smooth compression (Makhdoumi et al., Montanari–Mossel). The novelty is the “local encodability” lens and the resulting limits/algorithms, plus the insight that AND-type queries beat same-cluster for approximate recovery.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Casting query-based clustering as locally encodable source coding is elegant and unlocks clean lower bounds that respect locality constraints often ignored in classical source coding.
- New limits and simple schemes: Tight-ish achievability for k=2 in the noiseless exact case with m<n for nontrivial p; Δ-aware converse improving on n h(p) in some regimes; and an O(n log(k/δ)) nonadaptive scheme in the noisy approximate regime.
- Actionable design insight: Demonstrates that XOR/same-cluster queries are not optimal for approximate recovery; AND queries yield larger “expectation gaps” enabling simple thresholding.
- Practical considerations: The nonadaptive design fits crowdsourcing parallelism. The small-scale human study shows AND answers are easier for workers (fewer mistakes than same-cluster), aligning with the theory.
- Broader impact: Opens a line of inquiry on locality-constrained compression for interactive labeling and crowdsourcing.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope and realism of queries: The best noisy result uses k-ary answers; many platforms prefer binary questions. For k>2, can binary composite queries (e.g., multiple ANDs per pair) match the O(n log(k/δ)) rate up to constants?
- Dependence on priors and noise: Algorithm 1 requires priors p and noise q (or accurate surrogates). How sensitive is performance to misspecification? Can thresholds be learned from a small calibration set?
- Limited multi-class development: The k>2 analysis is mostly for the specific AND-like query; exact/approximate lower bounds beyond k=2 are less developed, and comparison to multi-class baselines is light.
- Nonadaptivity only: While nonadaptivity is motivated by parallelism, adaptive strategies can reduce queries. A discussion or lower bounds that separate adaptive vs nonadaptive would strengthen the story.
- Empirics: Experiments are small-scale and do not compare end-to-end to strong entity resolution systems; synthetic comparisons use spectral clustering for same-cluster but no robust/noisy matching baselines.
- Tightness and generality of bounds: Theorem 3 is technically involved and its numerical tightness is unclear; the XOR lower bound for approximate recovery partly leans on a conjecture outside p=0.5.

Quality
The theoretical results appear sound and well-motivated, with correct use of typicality, expurgation, Chernoff bounds, and dependency graphs. Some proofs are deferred; providing more intuition (e.g., where α<1 yields m<n) and tighter constants in Theorem 5 would aid reproducibility.

Clarity
Generally well-written, though dense. Suggestions: clarify the ranges of p,Δ where m<n in Theorem 1 with a table/plot; give an intuitive explanation of Theorem 3; spell out computational complexity of decoding; and discuss threshold selection robustness.

Originality
High. The locally encodable source coding perspective and the AND-query advantage for approximate recovery are new to the ML/crowdsourcing literature at NIPS.

Significance
Moderate to high. The results inform the design of query primitives and budgets in crowdsourcing and label-efficient clustering, and contribute new IT limits under locality constraints. Practical impact will hinge on adoption of nonstandard queries and availability of priors.

Reasons to accept
- Novel and elegant theoretical framework yielding meaningful, locality-aware lower bounds.
- Clear positive result: simple, nonadaptive O(n log(k/δ)) scheme under noise; AND queries outperform same-cluster for approximate recovery.
- Bridges ML crowdsourcing with information theory; likely to inspire follow-up work.

Reasons to reject
- Empirical validation is limited; k-ary query assumption may be restrictive in practice.
- Multi-class theory beyond the specific AND-like query is thin; tightness of approximate-recovery bounds (esp. Theorem 3, XOR conjecture) could be clearer.
- Nonadaptive focus without adaptive baselines or separations.

Suggestions for improvement
- Explore binary-only constructions for k>2 achieving near O(n log(k/δ)).
- Provide sensitivity analyses for prior/noise misspecification and an adaptive thresholding variant.
- Expand empirical evaluation to larger datasets and compare against strong ER/crowdsourcing baselines under the same budget.
- Clarify the status and scope of the XOR/same-cluster lower bound beyond p=0.5, and quantify gaps between your achievability and converses numerically.
