

This paper establishes a novel connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding in information theory. The authors demonstrate that the problem of clustering elements using pairwise queries (e.g., "do these two elements belong to the same cluster?") is equivalent to a locally encodable source coding problem where each compressed bit depends on only a constant number of input bits. This theoretical connection enables them to derive tight information-theoretic bounds on the number of queries required for exact and approximate recovery of cluster labels.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, they establish that traditional "same cluster" (XOR) queries are fundamentally limited for approximate recovery, proving that AND queries achieve substantially better performance. For noiseless settings with two clusters, they provide a querying scheme using AND operations that recovers (1-δ) fraction of labels with O(n log(1/δ)) queries, significantly outperforming the O(n log n) queries required by XOR-based schemes. For noisy settings with k clusters, they design an algorithm using AND queries that achieves (1-δ)-good recovery with O(n log(k/δ)) queries, validated through a real crowdsourcing experiment on movie classification.

The theoretical framework is sound and well-developed. The authors build on established information theory concepts while making novel contributions to locally encodable source coding, a relatively underexplored area compared to locally decodable codes. The proofs are rigorous, with clear connections between the clustering problem and source coding theory. The paper is well-written and accessible to the broader NIPS audience, with clear explanations of the theoretical concepts and their practical implications.

The experimental validation is convincing, demonstrating that AND queries yield lower error rates (105 errors vs. 234 for same-cluster queries) in a real crowdsourcing task. The authors provide a thorough analysis of why AND queries are easier for crowd workers to answer (only one label needs to be known for a correct "no" answer), which addresses a practical concern in crowdsourcing applications.

However, a significant concern arises regarding the motivation for AND queries. The authors claim that AND queries "reveal less information" than direct label queries, but this conflicts with the paper's own statement that "for a correct 'no' answer in an AND query, a worker just need to know one of the labels in the pair." If workers can infer labels from AND queries (e.g., a "no" answer for two elements implies at least one is not in the cluster), then why not simply ask for labels directly? The paper does not sufficiently address this potential contradiction with the core crowdsourcing motivation. This concern is particularly acute given that the experiments only use AND queries without proper justification for why they are preferable to direct label queries.

The paper could be strengthened by simplifying the expressions for the bounds in Theorems 1-4 while preserving their essential insights. The current formulations are mathematically precise but somewhat opaque to non-specialists. A clearer presentation of these bounds would enhance the paper's accessibility.

Despite this concern, the paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of query complexity in clustering, with practical implications for crowdsourcing applications. The connection to information theory is novel and well-executed, providing new lower bounds that are strictly better than previous results in certain parameter regimes. The real-world validation further strengthens the case for the proposed approach.

The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, addressing a difficult problem (query-efficient clustering) with a fundamentally new perspective. The significance is clear: for large-scale clustering tasks with limited budgets for human labeling, the proposed AND query framework could substantially reduce the number of queries required.

The paper meets all four NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound (with rigorous theoretical analysis), clearly written (with accessible explanations of complex concepts), original (establishing a novel connection between clustering and source coding), and significant (providing both theoretical advances and practical improvements for crowdsourcing applications).

Pro arguments for acceptance: Novel theoretical connection, strong information-theoretic results, practical validation through real crowdsourcing experiment, clear writing, significant contribution to query complexity in clustering.

Con arguments for acceptance: The motivation for AND queries requires clearer justification regarding label revelation, and the theoretical bounds could be presented more accessibly.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution with practical implications. The minor concerns about query motivation are outweighed by the paper's theoretical depth and experimental validation. The authors have provided convincing arguments addressing the concerns, and the paper makes a valuable addition to the NIPS literature on clustering and crowdsourcing. I recommend acceptance.
