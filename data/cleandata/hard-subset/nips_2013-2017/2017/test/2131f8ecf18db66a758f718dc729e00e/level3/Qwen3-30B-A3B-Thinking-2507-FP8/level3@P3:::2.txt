

# Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding" (NIPS 2017)

This paper establishes a connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, proposing AND-queries as an alternative to the commonly used "same cluster" queries. The authors demonstrate theoretically and empirically that AND-queries can achieve better query complexity for approximate recovery of cluster labels, particularly when dealing with noisy oracle responses. The work builds on prior clustering research (e.g., [33, 8, 21]) and information theory concepts of locally encodable source coding [23, 19], making a novel connection between these fields.

## Strengths

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by establishing a connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding, a relatively underexplored area. The authors provide meaningful theoretical bounds and an algorithmic approach that is validated through real-world crowdsourcing experiments. The experimental section (Section 4) effectively demonstrates the practical advantages of AND-queries over "same cluster" queries, with 105 errors versus 234 errors in the real dataset, supporting their theoretical claims.

## Weaknesses

The paper suffers from significant clarity and presentation issues that undermine its contributions:

1. **Restrictive query model**: The authors disallow repeated identical queries without justification. This is a restrictive assumption that may not reflect practical crowdsourcing scenarios where repeating queries to reduce error rates is common. The authors should either justify this choice or analyze the no-restriction case. As noted by [10], independent error models typically assume the option to repeat queries for error reduction.

2. **Theorem 1 conflict**: Theorem 1 claims an upper bound of n(h(p)+o(1))log₂(1/α) queries for exact recovery with pairwise "same cluster" queries, but this conflicts with the known O(n log n) query complexity for exact recovery in the noiseless case [21]. The proof is hard to follow, and the discrepancy needs clarification.

3. **Theorem 3 and 4 interpretability**: The lower bound in Theorem 3 (δ ≥ δ̃(m/n) + [h(p)-h(δ̃(m/n))]/h'(δ̃(m/n))(1+e^(Δh'(δ̃(m/n))))) is mathematically opaque and doesn't clearly show how m scales with n and δ. Similarly, Theorem 4's upper bound for AND-queries has unclear scaling with δ, and the gap between the upper and lower bounds is not explained.

4. **Inadequate comparisons**: Figure 2 only compares the proposed AND-query method to its own lower bound, not to the same-cluster-query algorithm. The claimed superiority of AND-queries is thus unsubstantiated. The paper should explicitly compare to the state-of-the-art "same cluster" query algorithms, as done in [21, 8].

5. **Algorithm 1's requirements**: Algorithm 1 requires prior knowledge of p (the cluster size distribution) or cluster sizes, which is not discussed in practice. Theoretical results assume perfect knowledge of p, but the paper doesn't address how to estimate these parameters in real applications. The pseudocode is also hard to follow without complex proofs.

6. **Missing comparisons in experiments**: Figures 3-5 do not compare to state-of-the-art methods like those in [21, 8, 22] that use "same cluster" queries, making the claim that AND-queries "outperform existing schemes" unsubstantiated. The paper should include these comparisons to strengthen its claims.

## Quality

The theoretical contributions are interesting but technically problematic. The conflict in Theorem 1 with prior work, the lack of clear interpretation for key results (Theorems 3 and 4), and the restrictive query model significantly weaken the technical quality. The experimental validation is good but insufficient to support the main claims without proper comparisons to baseline methods.

## Clarity

The paper suffers from poor clarity, particularly in the theoretical results. The proofs for Theorems 3 and 4 are described as "quite involved" but lack sufficient detail. The scaling relationships between m, n, and δ in the bounds are not intuitive, and the paper fails to explain how the bounds translate to practical query complexity.

## Originality

The connection between semisupervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is novel. The proposal of AND-queries as a superior alternative to "same cluster" queries is also novel. However, the paper's originality is somewhat diminished by the lack of clear comparison to the state-of-the-art and the restrictive assumptions.

## Significance

The significance is limited by the restrictive model and inadequate comparisons. While the real-world experiment is valuable, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that AND-queries would be adopted in practice over existing methods without addressing the parameter estimation issue and providing clear comparisons to state-of-the-art algorithms.

## Recommendation

This paper presents an interesting theoretical connection between clustering and source coding and provides some empirical validation of the AND-query approach. However, the significant issues with presentation, clarity, and comparison to prior work substantially undermine its contribution. The paper would benefit from addressing the key weaknesses outlined above, particularly by providing clear comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, clarifying the scaling relationships in the bounds, and justifying the restrictive query model.

**Pro Acceptance**: Novel connection between clustering and source coding, real-world validation of AND-queries.

**Con Acceptance**: Major clarity and presentation issues, inadequate comparisons to prior work, restrictive model without justification, key theoretical results difficult to interpret.

I recommend rejection due to the fundamental issues with clarity, presentation, and comparison to prior work that prevent the paper from making a convincing contribution to the field. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to address these concerns.
