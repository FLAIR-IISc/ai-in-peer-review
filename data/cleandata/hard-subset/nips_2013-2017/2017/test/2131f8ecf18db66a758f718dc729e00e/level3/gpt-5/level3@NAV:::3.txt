This paper studies semisupervised clustering via crowdsourced similarity queries through an elegant information-theoretic lens. The authors show an equivalence between nonadaptive querying for multiclass labeling and locally encodable source coding, yielding lower and upper bounds on query complexity under noiseless and noisy answers. A key insight is that pairwise AND queries dominate XOR/same-cluster queries for approximate recovery, enabling O(n log(1/δ)) queries for recovering a (1−δ) fraction of labels even with noise. The work connects to and extends NIPS literature on clustering with queries (e.g., same-cluster queries), noisy crowdsourcing (e.g., Karger-Oh-Shah), and learning under stochastic block models, while importing tools from rate-distortion and bounds on locally decodable/encodable codes (e.g., Ancheta–Massey).

Quality: The paper is technically sound and carefully argued. The information-theoretic lower bounds that improve on the naive nH(p) in certain regimes and the constructive AND-based algorithms with concentration-based analyses are convincing. Assumptions (nonadaptive design, independent BSC noise, known priors or estimated sizes) are clearly stated; simulations and a small crowd experiment support the theory.

Clarity: The writing is accessible to a general ML audience, with clear problem setup and motivation. However, the expressions in Theorems 1–4 are hard to parse on first read; simplified statements (e.g., special-case corollaries for k=2, balanced priors, small δ, low ∆) and a short intuition paragraph after each bound would substantially improve readability.

Originality: The locally encodable source coding perspective for clustering with limited-arity queries is novel, and the use of AND queries to overcome linear/XOR limitations in rate–distortion is insightful. The bounds that are strictly better than nH(p) in certain ranges and the O(n log(k/δ)) scaling under noise are new and interesting.

Significance: Results are likely to influence how query primitives are chosen in crowdsourced clustering and related interactive learning tasks. The AND vs XOR comparison is particularly actionable.

Concerns and suggestions:
- AND-query semantics may reveal absolute cluster membership (e.g., “is both i?”), which can undermine the motivation for not asking labels directly. Please articulate the cost/privacy model: when are AND queries feasible but direct labels not? What are the budget/error trade-offs relative to label queries?
- Experiments appear to use only AND queries; ensure fair baselines under the same query budget with same-cluster queries and label queries, clarifying the implications of potential label leakage.
- Simplify the bound expressions as requested above.

Overall: Strong theoretical contribution with clear practical implications. Authors’ responses addressed my main concerns; I recommend acceptance.

Pros: novel theory; clear info-theoretic connection; improved bounds; simple algorithms; supportive experiments.
Cons: potential ambiguity in crowdsourcing motivation for AND; complex theorem statements; limited empirical baselines.
