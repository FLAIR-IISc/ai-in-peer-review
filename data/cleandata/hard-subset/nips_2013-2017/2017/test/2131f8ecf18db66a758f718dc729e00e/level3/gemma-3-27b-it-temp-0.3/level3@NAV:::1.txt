## Review of "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering using a semi-supervised approach where an oracle provides binary answers to pairwise queries about cluster membership. The authors establish a connection between this problem and locally encodable source coding, leveraging information-theoretic tools to derive upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required for perfect or approximate recovery of the clustering. A key contribution is the introduction of AND queries as a potentially more efficient alternative to standard ‘same cluster’ (XOR) queries, particularly in noisy settings.

The work builds upon recent interest in query-based clustering, drawing parallels to entity resolution and crowdsourcing applications (e.g., [4, 33, 30]). The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of information theory, specifically highlighting the relevance of locally encodable and decodable source codes, areas less explored within the ML community. The motivation is strong, as minimizing query costs is crucial in practical scenarios like crowdsourcing. 

The paper’s strengths lie in its novel application of information-theoretic techniques to a clustering problem, resulting in rigorous bounds on query complexity. The analysis of noisy oracles is particularly valuable, and the proposed AND query strategy offers a promising direction for improving efficiency. The experimental validation, though limited, provides some support for the theoretical findings. 

However, the paper's limitation of making no structural assumptions about the hypothesis class restricts the scope of the analysis. While understandable for a foundational study, it means the bounds are relatively general and may not be tight for specific clustering scenarios. The dependence of the bounds on the marginal distribution of labels and noise level is also a constraint. Furthermore, while the connection to locally encodable source coding is insightful, the paper doesn’t fully explore the implications of this connection beyond deriving query complexity bounds.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information theory to query-based clustering.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis with well-defined upper and lower bounds.
*   Introduction of a potentially more efficient querying strategy (AND queries).
*   Strong motivation and relevance to practical applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope due to lack of structural assumptions.
*   Bounds are general and may not be tight for specific scenarios.
*   Limited experimental evaluation.



**Overall:** This is a valuable first step toward understanding the fundamental limits of query-based clustering. While the lack of structural assumptions limits the scope, the theoretical contributions and the introduction of AND queries are significant. I recommend acceptance, with encouragement for future work to explore more specific clustering scenarios and further investigate the connection to locally encodable source coding.
