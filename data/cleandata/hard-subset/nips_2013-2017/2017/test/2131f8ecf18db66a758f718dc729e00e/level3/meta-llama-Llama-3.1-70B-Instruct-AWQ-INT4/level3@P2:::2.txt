This paper studies the problem of reconstructing cluster labels from noisy, non-adaptive small-set queries. The authors provide lower bounds on the number of queries required for exact and approximate recovery, as well as an AND-queries algorithm that is evaluated on real data. The paper also explores the connection between this problem and locally encodable source coding.

The main ideas of the paper are related to previous work on clustering and crowdsourcing. The authors' approach to viewing the querying problem as a locally encodable source coding problem is novel and provides a new perspective on the problem. The use of AND-queries as a primitive for querying is also an interesting contribution.

The strengths of the paper include its theoretical contributions, particularly the lower bounds on the number of queries required for exact and approximate recovery. The authors also provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their results, making it easy to follow their arguments.

However, there are several weaknesses and areas for improvement. One major issue is the restrictive model of disallowing repeated identical queries, which may hurt performance. The authors should justify this choice or analyze the no-restriction case and/or independent annotator setting. Additionally, Theorem 1 conflicts with prior work (Ahn–Lee–Suh) in the noiseless exact-recovery regime, and its proof is hard to follow.

The presentation and clarity of the paper could also be improved. Theorem 3's lower-bound expression for δ is hard to interpret, and it's not clear how m scales with n and δ or how relaxation in δ improves performance. Theorem 4 suffers from the same interpretability issues as Theorem 3, and the gap between its upper bound and Theorem 3’s lower bound is unclear.

Furthermore, the paper lacks comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, particularly the same-cluster-query algorithm. Figure 2 only compares to the lower bound of the same-cluster-query algorithm, and the claimed superiority of the proposed method is unsupported. Any crossing behavior is also unexplained.

Algorithm 1 requires prior knowledge of p or cluster sizes (per Theorem 6), but practical estimation of these parameters is not discussed. The pseudocode is also hard to follow without complex proofs.

The experimental evaluation of the paper is limited, and the results do not compare to state-of-the-art methods. Figures 3–5 do not compare to existing schemes, so claims that AND-queries outperform existing schemes are not substantiated.

In terms of originality, the paper provides a new perspective on the problem by viewing it as a locally encodable source coding problem. The use of AND-queries as a primitive for querying is also an interesting contribution. However, the paper builds on existing work in clustering and crowdsourcing, and the authors could have done a better job of highlighting the connections to previous work.

In terms of significance, the paper provides important theoretical contributions to the field of clustering and crowdsourcing. The lower bounds on the number of queries required for exact and approximate recovery are particularly significant, as they provide a fundamental limit on the performance of any algorithm.

However, the practical impact of the paper is limited by the restrictive model and the lack of comparisons to state-of-the-art methods. The experimental evaluation is also limited, and the results do not provide a clear understanding of the performance of the algorithm in practice.

Overall, the paper provides important theoretical contributions to the field of clustering and crowdsourcing. However, the presentation and clarity of the paper could be improved, and the authors should address the weaknesses and areas for improvement mentioned above.
