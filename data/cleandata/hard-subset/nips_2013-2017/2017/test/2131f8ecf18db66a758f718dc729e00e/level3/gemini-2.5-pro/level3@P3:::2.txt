This paper studies the problem of semi-supervised clustering, where the goal is to reconstruct the ground-truth labels of `n` items into `k` clusters using a minimum number of non-adaptive queries. The central contribution is framing this problem as one of locally encodable source coding, where each query (an encoded bit) depends on a small, constant number (`Δ`) of items (source bits). This perspective is used to derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the required number of queries for both exact and approximate recovery. The authors introduce a new type of query, the "AND-query," arguing it is superior to the standard "same-cluster" (XOR) query, particularly for approximate recovery in noisy settings. They present an algorithm based on AND-queries and evaluate it on real and synthetic data. The work relates to a growing body of literature on clustering with queries and crowdsourcing, but brings a novel information-theoretic viewpoint.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality:** The connection drawn between non-adaptive clustering queries and locally encodable source coding is novel and provides a fresh theoretical perspective on the problem. This framing has the potential to yield new insights and tools for analysis.
*   **Novel Approach:** The introduction of AND-queries as an alternative to the ubiquitous same-cluster query is a key contribution. The theoretical motivation for why AND-queries might be more efficient for approximate recovery is interesting and significant.
*   **Relevance:** The focus on non-adaptive, parallelizable queries with noisy answers is highly relevant for practical large-scale systems like crowdsourcing, where minimizing latency and handling unreliable workers are critical challenges.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper suffers from significant issues related to clarity, theoretical soundness, and experimental evaluation that undermine its contributions.

*   **Restrictive Modeling:** The model explicitly disallows repeating the same query, arguing that repeated queries can be aggregated into a single, less noisy query. This is a restrictive and poorly justified assumption. In practice, querying multiple independent annotators for the same item pair is a standard technique for noise reduction. The current model sidesteps this important aspect of the problem. An analysis of the unrestricted case or a more realistic model with independent annotators would be much more compelling. It is unclear if the core results would hold in such a setting.
*   **Clarity and Soundness of Theoretical Results:**
    1.  **Theorem 1:** The query complexity for noiseless exact recovery, `m = n(h(p)+o(1))/log(1/α)`, appears to conflict with the `Ω(nk)` lower bound from prior work (Ahn et al., NIPS 2016) for `k=2` clusters. While the models may differ, this potential discrepancy is not discussed or reconciled. Furthermore, the proof is highly condensed and difficult to follow, making it hard to verify the result or understand its implications.
    2.  **Theorems 3 & 4:** The expressions for the lower bound (Thm 3) and the upper bound performance of AND-queries (Thm 4) are extremely complex and hard to interpret. It is not clear how the number of queries `m` scales with `n` and the error tolerance `δ`, which is the primary quantity of interest. The paper fails to provide simplified asymptotic analysis or intuition, making it difficult to assess the tightness of the bounds or the practical improvement offered by relaxing `δ`. The proofs are similarly opaque.
*   **Insufficient Experimental Comparisons:** The empirical evidence provided is not sufficient to support the paper's central claims.
    1.  **Figure 2:** This figure claims to show the superiority of AND-queries. However, it compares the performance of the proposed AND-query algorithm to a theoretical *lower bound* for same-cluster queries, not to an actual, well-known algorithm (e.g., spectral clustering on the query graph). This is not a fair or valid comparison. Any claims of superiority are therefore unsubstantiated. The crossing behavior of the curves is also left unexplained.
    2.  **Figures 3-5:** These figures evaluate the proposed algorithm (Algorithm 1) in isolation. They lack any comparison to baseline or state-of-the-art methods that use same-cluster queries. To demonstrate the value of AND-queries, a direct comparison against a strong competitor on the same dataset is essential. Without it, the results are uninterpretable in the context of existing work.
*   **Algorithm Practicality and Clarity:**
    1.  **Algorithm 1:** The algorithm requires prior knowledge of the label distribution `p` or exact cluster sizes (per Theorem 6). However, the paper offers no discussion on how these parameters would be estimated in a practical setting beyond a brief, hand-wavy suggestion.
    2.  The pseudocode for Algorithm 1 is difficult to follow. The logic, particularly the choice of thresholds `Ci`, is not intuitive and can only be understood by working through the complex proofs, which hinders reproducibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The novel connection to locally encodable source coding is theoretically interesting.
*   The introduction and motivation of AND-queries as an alternative to same-cluster queries is a potentially significant idea.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The claims of superiority for AND-queries are not supported by fair and rigorous experimental comparisons.
*   Key theoretical results are presented in an uninterpretable form and have questionable soundness due to a potential conflict with prior work and hard-to-follow proofs.
*   The model's restriction against repeated queries is unrealistic and limits the applicability of the results.
*   The paper suffers from major clarity issues in its theoretical exposition and algorithmic presentation, making the work difficult to verify and build upon.

**Conclusion:**

While the paper introduces novel and potentially valuable ideas, the execution is critically flawed. The lack of clarity in the theoretical results, questionable modeling assumptions, and fundamentally weak experimental comparisons prevent the paper from making a convincing scientific contribution. The claims of superiority for the proposed method are not substantiated. Therefore, I recommend rejection in its current form. The authors should focus on clarifying their theoretical results, justifying their model, and performing rigorous, fair comparisons against established baselines.
