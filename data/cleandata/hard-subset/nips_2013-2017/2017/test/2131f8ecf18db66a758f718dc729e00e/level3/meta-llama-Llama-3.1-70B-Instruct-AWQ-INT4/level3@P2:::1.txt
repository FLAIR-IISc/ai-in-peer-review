The paper "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding" presents a novel approach to clustering by framing it as a locally encodable source coding problem. The authors consider a model where an oracle provides binary answers to simple queries about pairs of elements, and the goal is to recover the correct clustering while minimizing the number of queries. They establish upper and lower bounds on the number of queries required for perfect and approximate recovery, including cases with noisy oracles.

The paper is well-motivated by applications in record deduplication and crowdsourcing, and the authors' use of information-theoretic tools is novel to the machine learning community. The analysis is thorough, and the results provide a useful first step toward understanding the information-theoretic limits and lower bounds for query-based clustering.

One of the strengths of the paper is its clarity and organization. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the problem setup, their approach, and the results. The paper is well-written, and the notation is clear and consistent.

The technical contributions of the paper are significant. The authors establish a new lower bound on the number of queries required for perfect recovery, which is strictly better than the naive bound of nH(p) for some prior distributions. They also propose a new querying scheme based on AND operations, which performs substantially better than the traditional "same cluster" queries for approximate recovery.

The experimental results presented in the paper are also noteworthy. The authors validate their theoretical results using a real crowdsourcing experiment, which demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach in practice.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The analysis is limited in scope, as no structural assumptions are made about the hypothesis class. The bounds mainly rely on the marginal distribution of labels and the noise level, which may not be sufficient to capture the complexity of real-world clustering problems.

Additionally, the paper could benefit from more discussion on the implications of the results. For example, how do the bounds change when the number of clusters is large? What are the implications of the results for other clustering algorithms, such as spectral clustering or k-means?

Overall, the paper is a significant contribution to the field of clustering and information theory. The authors' novel approach and thorough analysis provide a useful foundation for future research in this area.
