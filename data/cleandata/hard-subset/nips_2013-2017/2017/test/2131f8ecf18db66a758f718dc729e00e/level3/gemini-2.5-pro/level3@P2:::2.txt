This paper explores the problem of semi-supervised clustering, where the goal is to recover the ground-truth labels of `n` elements into `k` clusters by making a minimum number of non-adaptive queries. The central and novel contribution is the framing of this problem as one of locally encodable source coding, where each query (an encoded bit) depends on a small, constant number of elements (source bits). This information-theoretic perspective is used to derive lower bounds on the query complexity for both exact and approximate recovery. The authors also introduce "AND-queries" as an alternative to the more common "same-cluster" (or XOR) queries, arguing they are more efficient for approximate recovery, especially in noisy settings. The paper presents algorithms based on AND-queries and validates them with theoretical analysis and experiments on both synthetic and real crowdsourced data.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The connection drawn between non-adaptive clustering queries and locally encodable source coding is a primary strength. This is a novel and insightful perspective that provides a new theoretical framework for analyzing query-based clustering and could inspire further research at the intersection of information theory and machine learning.
*   **Novel Approach:** The proposal of using AND-queries instead of the standard same-cluster queries is an interesting contribution. The intuition that AND-queries might be easier for human annotators to answer correctly and more robust to noise is compelling and practically relevant.
*   **Theoretical Depth:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical treatment, developing new information-theoretic lower bounds for exact and approximate recovery under local encoding constraints (Theorems 2 and 3). It also provides achievability results (upper bounds) for its proposed schemes (Theorems 1, 4, and 5).

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

Despite its strong conceptual contributions, the paper suffers from significant issues in clarity, technical soundness, and experimental validation that undermine its main claims.

**1. Clarity and Interpretation of Theoretical Results:**
The main theoretical results are presented in a form that is very difficult to parse and interpret.
*   **(Clarity/Quality) Theorem 3 & 4:** The expressions for the lower bound on distortion `δ` in Theorem 3 and the achievability result in Theorem 4 are highly complex. It is not clear from these expressions how the number of queries `m` scales with `n` and `δ`, which is the crucial takeaway for a practitioner or researcher. The authors should provide simplified asymptotic analyses or plots to illustrate these trade-offs and clarify the gap between the upper and lower bounds. The proofs are also quite dense and hard to follow, which hinders verification.
*   **(Clarity) Algorithm 1:** The pseudocode for Algorithm 1 is difficult to understand without a deep dive into the proof. Furthermore, the algorithm requires prior knowledge of the cluster distribution `p` (or cluster sizes `n_i`). While the paper briefly mentions that these can be estimated, it does not discuss the query cost of this estimation step or how estimation errors might affect the algorithm's performance. This is a critical practical detail that should be addressed.

**2. Technical Soundness and Comparison to Prior Work:**
There are concerns about the correctness and context of some theoretical claims.
*   **(Quality) Conflict with Prior Work:** Theorem 1 provides an upper bound on the query complexity for exact recovery. However, this result seems to conflict with the O(n log n) requirement for random graphs established in prior work (e.g., [2] Ahn, Lee, Suh, 2016), which is necessary to ensure the graph is connected. The authors state that their scheme requires `m < n` queries for a range of priors `p`. This discrepancy needs to be explicitly addressed and resolved. The proof relies on an expurgation argument on a random ensemble, but it's not clear how this translates to a constructive, valid querying scheme.

**3. Insufficient Experimental Validation:**
The experiments do not provide sufficient evidence to support the central claim that AND-queries outperform same-cluster queries.
*   **(Quality/Significance) Lack of Baselines:** Figures 3, 4, and 5 show the performance of the proposed algorithm but do not compare it against any state-of-the-art baselines using same-cluster queries (e.g., spectral clustering). Without these comparisons, it is impossible to conclude that the proposed method offers a significant improvement.
*   **(Quality) Unfair Comparison in Figure 2:** Figure 2 compares the performance of the proposed AND-query *algorithm* to a *lower bound* for same-cluster queries. This is not a fair or meaningful comparison. To demonstrate superiority, the authors must compare their algorithm's performance against the performance of a competitive *algorithm* for same-cluster queries.
*   The paper mentions that for a real-world experiment, same-cluster queries resulted in more errors (234 vs. 105). While interesting, this result is then used to justify running the main comparison on synthetic data (Figure 6, which is referenced but not shown). The core experimental results in the main paper must include this crucial comparison.

**4. Modeling Assumptions:**
The problem setup relies on a key assumption that is not well-justified.
*   **(Quality/Originality) Disallowing Repeated Queries:** The model explicitly disallows asking the same query multiple times, dismissing it as "theoretically not interesting." However, in practical crowdsourcing, obtaining multiple opinions on the same item is a standard technique for noise reduction. This modeling choice needs a stronger justification. Is there evidence that repeating queries is unhelpful in practice? How would the analysis change if one considers a model with multiple independent annotators, which is a natural form of repetition? The claimed benefits of the AND-query scheme might be tied to this restrictive assumption.

In summary, the paper introduces a promising and original theoretical connection, but its execution falls short. The claims of superiority for the AND-query scheme are not substantiated by fair, direct comparisons to existing methods. The theoretical results are difficult to interpret, and a potential conflict with prior work raises concerns about technical soundness. I would encourage the authors to significantly revise the paper to address these points, as the core ideas are valuable to the community.
