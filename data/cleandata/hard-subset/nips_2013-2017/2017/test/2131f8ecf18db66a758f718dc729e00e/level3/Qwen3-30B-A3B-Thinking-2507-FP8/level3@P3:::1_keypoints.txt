- Novel connection established between semisupervised clustering with binary queries and locally encodable source coding in information theory.
- Problem model: algorithm issues binary queries on ≤Δ elements to an oracle to recover clustering with minimal queries; shown equivalent to locally encodable source coding.
- Contribution 1: information-theoretic lower bounds for exact recovery in the noiseless case, showing query complexity can be less than n for certain label distributions.
- Contribution 2: shows "same cluster" (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery and proposes AND queries that substantially outperform them.
- Contribution 3: provides a noisy approximate-recovery algorithm for k clusters achieving O(n log k / δ) queries for (1−δ)-good recovery.
- Empirical validation: crowdsourcing experiment on movie classification supports theory and shows AND queries produce fewer errors (105 vs. 234).
- Motivation and relevance: well-motivated by practical problems like record deduplication and crowdsourcing where query cost is significant.
- Novelty: information-theoretic framing via locally encodable source coding is novel for the ML community and shifts query-design perspective.
- Technical quality: rigorous theoretical analysis with meaningful bounds and insightful connection to Massey’s work on linear codes.
- Clarity and organization: generally clear and logically organized, though the info-theory connection may require background for ML readers.
- Limitations: focus on settings without structural clustering assumptions; results depend heavily on label marginals and noise level.
- Significance: provides fundamental limits and practical implications, laying groundwork for future query-design research.
- Recommendation: reviewer recommends acceptance as an important and well-executed contribution.
