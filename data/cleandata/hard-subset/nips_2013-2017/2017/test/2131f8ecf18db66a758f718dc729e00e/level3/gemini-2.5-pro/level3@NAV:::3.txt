This paper addresses the problem of semi-supervised clustering using a limited budget of non-adaptive, crowdsourced queries. The central contribution is a novel and elegant connection between this problem and the information-theoretic concept of locally encodable source coding. By framing the query process as a form of data compression where each compressed bit depends on a small number of input bits (the labels), the authors derive theoretical lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate cluster recovery. This work builds upon existing literature in crowdsourced clustering, which often relies on pairwise "same-cluster" queries, by proposing and analyzing an alternative "AND-query" model that proves more efficient in certain regimes.

The paper has several significant strengths. The primary strength is the originality of linking the clustering problem to locally encodable source codes, which provides a powerful new lens for analysis. The theoretical results, which establish bounds for both noiseless and noisy oracle answers (Theorems 1-4), are technically sound and form a complete and valuable contribution. Furthermore, the paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making the complex information-theoretic arguments accessible to a general machine learning audience.

My main concern revolves around the motivation for using AND queries. A query like "Are both items in cluster A?" reveals the absolute label for both items upon a "yes" answer. This seems to contradict the core motivation of using similarity queries in crowdsourcing; if revealing the label is permissible, it is unclear why one would not simply ask for the label of each item directly. This concern is amplified by the experiments, which appear to use only AND queries. The validity of the empirical results hinges on a clear justification for this query model's practical relevance over direct labeling.

For improving clarity, I would also suggest that the authors attempt to simplify the final expressions for the bounds presented in Theorems 1–4. While the derivations are clear, more intuitive or asymptotic forms could help readers grasp the essential trade-offs without getting lost in complex terms.

Despite these points, the theoretical framework presented is novel and significant. After reviewing the authors’ response, my concerns have been satisfactorily addressed. The justification for the AND-query model in specific crowdsourcing scenarios is now clear, and the theoretical work stands as a strong contribution. I recommend acceptance.
