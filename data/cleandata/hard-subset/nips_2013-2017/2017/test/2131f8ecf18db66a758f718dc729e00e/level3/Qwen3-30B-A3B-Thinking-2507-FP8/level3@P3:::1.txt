

# Review: "Semisupervised Clustering, AND-Queries and Locally Encodable Source Coding"

This paper makes an interesting connection between semisupervised clustering with query-based learning and locally encodable source coding in information theory. The authors study a model where an algorithm can ask binary queries about element relationships (with at most ∆ elements per query) to an oracle, aiming to recover the clustering of n elements with minimal queries. They demonstrate that this problem is equivalent to locally encodable source coding, where each compressed bit depends on only a constant number of input bits.

The paper's main contributions include: (1) information-theoretic lower bounds for exact recovery in the noiseless case, showing that query complexity can be less than n for certain label distributions; (2) a demonstration that "same cluster" (XOR) queries are suboptimal for approximate recovery, with the authors proposing AND queries that substantially outperform them; and (3) a novel algorithm for noisy approximate recovery with k clusters that achieves O(n log k/δ) queries for (1-δ)-good recovery. The authors validate their theoretical results with a real crowdsourcing experiment on movie classification.

This work is well-motivated by practical applications in record deduplication and crowdsourcing, where query costs are significant. The connection to information theory is novel to the ML community, as locally encodable source coding is underexplored compared to locally decodable codes. The paper's technical approach is rigorous, with careful theoretical analysis supported by empirical validation.

The paper excels in quality with sound theoretical results. The information-theoretic techniques are well-applied and provide meaningful bounds. The experimental validation on real crowdsourcing data effectively supports their claims about AND queries being easier for workers than "same cluster" queries (105 vs. 234 errors in their experiment). The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, including the absence of structural assumptions about the clustering.

The paper is generally clear, though the connection to information theory might require some background for ML readers. The organization is logical, moving from problem formulation to theoretical results to experimental validation. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce their results.

The work is highly original in its approach. While query-based clustering has been studied (e.g., [4, 8, 21]), the authors' information-theoretic framing using locally encodable source coding is novel to ML. The AND query proposal represents a significant conceptual shift from the prevailing XOR-based approaches. The paper properly situates itself within related work, citing recent NIPS papers on clustering with queries ([4, 21, 22]).

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides fundamental information-theoretic limits for query-based clustering, which is a crucial problem in practical applications like crowdsourcing. The AND query approach has clear practical advantages, as evidenced by the lower error rates in their crowdsourcing experiment. The paper establishes a theoretical foundation for future work in this area, with the authors noting that their results "provide a useful first step toward understanding information-theoretic limits."

A few limitations are worth noting. The paper primarily focuses on the case where no structural assumptions are made about the clustering (beyond label distributions), which limits the scope of their analysis. The results depend heavily on the marginal distribution of labels and noise level, which might not capture all real-world clustering scenarios. While the authors acknowledge this, future work could explore how their bounds change under structural assumptions.

The paper's technical depth is impressive, with the authors establishing new information-theoretic results on locally encodable source coding. The connection to Massey's work on linear codes [17] is particularly insightful for understanding the limitations of XOR queries.

In summary, this paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with practical implications. It successfully bridges information theory and clustering with queries, providing valuable insights into query complexity limits. The results are well-supported by both theory and experiment, and the proposed AND query method has clear advantages for crowdsourcing applications.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it's technically sound with rigorous analysis, well-written with clear organization, highly original in its approach, and significant for both theoretical and practical reasons. The authors' careful evaluation of their work's limitations demonstrates scholarly integrity.

I recommend acceptance as it represents an important step toward understanding the fundamental limits of query-based clustering, with clear implications for practical applications. The work opens promising avenues for future research on query design in semisupervised learning.
