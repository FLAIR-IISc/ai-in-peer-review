- Paper studies non-adaptive, query-based clustering with a noisy oracle and connects queries to locally encodable source coding (each query depends on ≤Δ labels).  
- For k=2, shows same-cluster XOR queries are limited for approximate recovery and proposes pairwise AND queries that can outperform XOR in noiseless and noisy settings.  
- Noiseless exact recovery (k=2): Δ-ary XOR + ML decoding yields m ≈ n h(p)/log(1/α) queries, improving over n for some p even at Δ=2; a nontrivial lower bound also given.  
- Noiseless approximate recovery (k=2): Presents a locality-aware lower bound strengthening rate–distortion limits and argues XOR schemes are fundamentally limited for approximation.  
- Noiseless approximate recovery (k=2): Proposes a simple non-adaptive AND-query scheme with explicit error–query tradeoffs that beat XOR-only schemes in several regimes.  
- Noisy approximate recovery (general k): Introduces a k-ary “multi-class AND” query and a thresholding algorithm on d-regular graphs achieving (1−δ)-accuracy with m = O(n log(k/δ)) queries under independent per-query noise.  
- Noisy analysis uses Chernoff bounds and bounded-dependence concentration and extends to known cluster sizes.  
- Relates to prior work on same-cluster queries, crowdsourcing reliability, noisy clustering, and rate–distortion and locally decodable compression; cites classical results on linear codes’ suboptimality for lossy compression.  
- Strength: Novel equivalence to locally encodable source coding yielding new locality-dependent lower bounds.  
- Strength: Concrete, simple non-adaptive schemes (AND queries) with explicit, near-optimal query complexity and clear dependence on n, k, δ, q.  
- Strength: Theoretical and empirical separation between XOR and AND, supporting practical intuition from crowdsourcing.  
- Strength: Includes a small crowdsourcing experiment supporting modeling choices and feasibility.  
- Weakness: Assumes i.i.d. labels with known priors or exact cluster sizes and independent query noise, which may be restrictive for structured/correlated settings.  
- Weakness: k-ary multi-class AND oracle may be less practical than standard query primitives; limited empirical validation for k>2.  
- Weakness: Focuses only on non-adaptive schemes; does not explore potential gains or bounds for adaptive queries.  
- Weakness: Some proofs deferred and analyses use approximations (Poissonization, bounded dependence); explicit constants and finite-sample guidance are limited.  
- Weakness: Small-scale experiments and potential imbalance in task difficulty between AND vs XOR queries; fairness of comparisons could be improved.  
- Quality: Main theorems appear sound and use appropriate tools; some conjectures are clearly labeled.  
- Clarity: Mostly clear and organized, but minor notation inconsistencies (H vs h), typos, and places lacking summarized constants.  
- Originality: High — locally encodable viewpoint and AND-vs-XOR contrast are novel for the community.  
- Significance: Good — establishes limits and simple schemes of practical interest though scope limited by assumptions.  
- Questions for authors: Can bounds extend to adaptive queries; how to implement k-ary AND in crowd UIs and compare cost/noise tradeoffs; robustness to misspecified priors and correlated noise?  
- Suggestions: Compare empirically to majority-vote/Dawid–Skene under same budget; ensure within-worker randomization and matched difficulty for fair AND vs XOR tests.  
- Minor: Clean up references/formatting, define α on first use, standardize H/h, and state regimes/constants (e.g., when Theorem 1 < n for Δ=2, explicit d thresholds in Theorem 5).  
- Arguments for acceptance: New conceptual bridge, solid lower/upper bounds, and simple near-optimal algorithms with preliminary practical validation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Limited realism (i.i.d. labels, independent noise, non-adaptive), modest empirical scope, and practicality concerns for k>2 queries.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept; novelty and technical results outweigh limitations.
