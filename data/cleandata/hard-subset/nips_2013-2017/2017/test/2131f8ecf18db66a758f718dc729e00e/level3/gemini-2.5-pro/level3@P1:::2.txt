This paper explores the problem of semi-supervised clustering, where the goal is to recover `n` labels from a set of `k` clusters by making non-adaptive queries to an oracle. The central contribution is framing this problem as one of locally encodable source coding, a concept from information theory where each compressed bit depends on only a small number (∆) of input bits. This connection allows the authors to derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required for both exact and approximate recovery of the labels. A key finding is the potential limitation of standard "same-cluster" (XOR) queries. The authors propose an alternative, "AND queries," and present an algorithm that they claim outperforms same-cluster queries, particularly in the noisy, approximate recovery setting. The paper includes theoretical analysis for various scenarios (noiseless/noisy, exact/approximate) and provides an experimental evaluation on a real crowdsourced dataset to validate the proposed method.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The connection established between semi-supervised clustering and locally encodable source coding is novel and provides a powerful theoretical lens through which to analyze the problem. This information-theoretic framing is a significant conceptual contribution that could inspire new approaches to active clustering and crowdsourcing.

2.  **Novel Query Type:** The introduction of pairwise AND queries as an alternative to the more commonly studied same-cluster queries is an interesting and non-obvious idea. The theoretical motivation and empirical results suggesting their superiority in noisy settings are compelling and could be impactful for practitioners designing crowdsourcing systems.

3.  **Comprehensive Theoretical Scope:** The paper attempts a broad analysis, covering noiseless and noisy oracles, as well as exact and approximate recovery goals. Deriving new lower and upper bounds across these different settings demonstrates a thorough investigation of the problem space.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection):**

1.  **Restrictive Modeling Assumptions:** The paper's model disallows repeating the same query to reduce noise. The justification provided—that repeating a query O(log n) times can nearly eliminate error—is a theoretical argument that may not align with practical crowdsourcing scenarios. In reality, queries are often sent to multiple independent workers, not repeated to the same oracle. The assumption that different queries result in independent errors but the same query does not is a strong one. The paper would be strengthened by either providing a more compelling justification for this model or analyzing the more standard setting where queries can be repeated to different annotators.

2.  **Clarity and Correctness of Theoretical Results:** Several key theoretical results are difficult to interpret or appear to conflict with existing work.
    *   Theorem 1, on noiseless exact recovery, claims a query complexity that appears to be at odds with prior results (e.g., Ahn, Lee, Suh, 2016) for specific parameter regimes. The proof sketch provided is difficult to follow, making it hard to verify the result or understand the source of the discrepancy.
    *   The lower bound for approximate recovery in Theorem 3 is presented as a complex expression for the error `δ`. This makes it difficult to understand the fundamental trade-offs, such as how the number of queries `m` scales with `n` and `δ`. A more interpretable form of the bound would significantly improve its utility.
    *   Similarly, the performance guarantee for AND queries in Theorem 4 is hard to parse, and the gap between this upper bound and the lower bound of Theorem 3 is not analyzed or discussed, leaving the tightness of the results unclear.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Comparison:** The claims of superiority for AND queries are not adequately substantiated by the experiments.
    *   Figure 2 compares the performance of the AND query algorithm to a *lower bound* for same-cluster queries, not to the performance of a competitive same-cluster query algorithm. This is not a fair comparison and does not prove that the proposed method is better in practice. The crossing behavior in the plot is also left unexplained.
    *   The experiments in Figures 3-5 and the real-data evaluation in Section 4 lack a baseline comparison against state-of-the-art methods using same-cluster queries (e.g., spectral methods on the query graph). Without such a baseline, the practical significance of the proposed algorithm is difficult to assess. While the paper notes that same-cluster queries had a higher error rate in their experiment, a full algorithmic performance comparison on the noisy data is missing.

4.  **Practicality and Clarity of the Proposed Algorithm:** Algorithm 1 requires prior knowledge of the cluster proportions `p` (or the exact sizes `n_i` per Theorem 6). While the paper suggests this can be estimated via subsampling, this is a non-trivial step that is not discussed in detail. Furthermore, the pseudocode for Algorithm 1 is hard to follow without digging into the complex proofs, limiting its accessibility and reproducibility.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper introduces a valuable and original perspective by connecting active clustering to locally encodable source coding. The proposal of AND queries is a creative contribution with potential practical significance. However, the paper is currently hampered by significant issues with clarity in its theoretical results, a lack of convincing experimental comparisons to established baselines, and modeling assumptions that may not reflect practical scenarios. The claims of superiority for the proposed method are not yet fully supported by the evidence presented.

While the core ideas are promising, the paper requires substantial revision to improve the clarity of its theoretical contributions, strengthen its experimental evaluation, and better justify its modeling choices. For these reasons, I lean towards rejecting the paper in its current form, but I would be open to reconsidering a revised version that addresses these major points.
