This paper studies the problem of clustering with limited, binary-answer queries to an oracle, motivated by applications like crowdsourcing and record deduplication. The authors establish a connection between this problem and locally encodable source coding, leveraging information-theoretic tools to derive upper and lower bounds on the query complexity required for exact and approximate recovery of the clustering. A key contribution is the proposal of AND queries as potentially more efficient than standard 'same cluster' queries, particularly in noisy scenarios.

The work builds upon a recent surge of interest in query-based clustering (e.g., [4, 8, 20, 33]), especially within the entity resolution community [30, 33]. The authors correctly position their work within this context, highlighting the cost of queries and the need to minimize them.  The connection to source coding, specifically locally encodable codes [19, 23], is a novel perspective, and they appropriately acknowledge that this area is relatively underexplored even within information theory.  The consideration of noisy oracles is also valuable, drawing on related work in crowdsourcing [10, 12, 13, 14, 15, 27, 28, 34].  The comparison to and critique of 'same cluster' (XOR) queries based on Massey’s work [17] is insightful.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The framing of the clustering problem as a locally encodable source coding problem is a novel and promising approach.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, including both upper and lower bounds on query complexity.
* **Practical Motivation:** The problem is well-motivated by real-world applications like crowdsourcing and record deduplication.
* **AND Query Proposal:** The proposal of AND queries as a potential improvement over 'same cluster' queries is a significant contribution, especially in noisy settings, and is supported by both theoretical analysis and initial experimental results.
* **Handling Noise:** The analysis explicitly considers noisy oracles, making the results more applicable to real-world scenarios.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the problem setup, proposed techniques, and results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope:** The authors explicitly acknowledge that the analysis lacks structural assumptions (e.g., a hypothesis class) which limits its scope. This makes the results somewhat general, but also less directly applicable to specific clustering scenarios with known properties.
* **Dependence on Marginal Distribution:** The bounds heavily rely on the marginal distribution of labels (prior probabilities) and the noise level. Estimating these accurately in practice can be challenging.
* **Experimental Validation:** While the included experiment is a good initial validation, it's relatively small-scale and focuses on a specific dataset. More extensive experimentation with diverse datasets and crowd worker populations would strengthen the claims.
* **Complexity of Proofs:** Some of the proofs, particularly those related to the lower bounds, are quite involved and could benefit from further clarification and simplification.
* **Limited Discussion of Alternative Queries:** While AND queries are proposed, a more thorough exploration of other potential query functions beyond XOR and AND might be beneficial.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis.  It presents a complete piece of work in terms of establishing a theoretical framework and providing initial validation. The authors are careful to state the limitations of their analysis.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and generally clearly written. However, some sections, particularly those with the more complex mathematical derivations, could benefit from improved readability.  More intuitive explanations alongside the formal proofs would be helpful.

**Originality:** The approach of connecting clustering to locally encodable source coding is novel. The combination of information-theoretic tools with the crowdsourcing/entity resolution problem is also a unique contribution. The authors adequately reference related work.

**Significance:** The results are important as they provide fundamental limits on the query complexity of clustering and offer insights into the design of more efficient querying strategies. The AND query proposal has the potential to improve the performance of practical clustering systems. This work advances the state of the art by establishing a theoretical foundation for understanding the trade-offs between query cost and clustering accuracy.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a challenging and relevant problem.
* It provides novel theoretical insights and establishes a connection to a related field (information theory).
* The AND query proposal is a potentially significant practical contribution.
* The work is technically sound and well-motivated.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The limited scope due to the lack of structural assumptions.
* The dependence on accurately estimating the prior distribution.
* The relatively limited experimental validation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of query-based clustering. While the limitations are acknowledged and reasonable, the novel approach, rigorous analysis, and potential for practical impact make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.  Future work could focus on exploring structural assumptions to tighten the bounds, developing more robust methods for estimating prior probabilities, and conducting more extensive experimental validation.
