This paper proposes a novel paradigm for modeling visual attention based on the principle that there are foundational laws that drive the emergence of visual attention. The authors devise variational laws of eye movement that rely on a generalized view of the Least Action Principle in physics. The model combines three fundamental principles: boundedness of the trajectory, curiosity-driven principle, and brightness invariance. The curiosity-driven principle is based on the idea that visual attention is attracted to regions with many details, while the brightness invariance principle is motivated by the need to achieve fixation and motion tracking.

The model is mathematically formulated using a Lagrangian, which includes terms for kinetic energy, potential energy, and a brightness invariance term. The Euler-Lagrange equations are derived to describe the visual attention scanpath. The model is tested on several datasets, including MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU, and the results are compared with other popular saliency models.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the model and its components. The authors provide a thorough discussion of the related work and the limitations of existing models. The experimental results are presented in a clear and concise manner, and the comparison with other models is useful in evaluating the performance of the proposed model.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to capture the dynamics of visual attention in a unified framework. The model is able to simulate scanpaths that are similar to those observed in human subjects, and the results on dynamic scenes are particularly promising. The use of a variational approach to model visual attention is also a novel contribution of the paper.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The model relies on several ad-hoc parameters, such as the weights for the curiosity and brightness invariance terms, which are estimated using simulated annealing. While the authors provide some justification for these parameters, it is not clear how they relate to the underlying biology of visual attention. Additionally, the model does not provide a clear explanation for how visual attention is modulated by top-down influences, such as task-dependent strategies.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of visual attention modeling. The use of a variational approach to model visual attention is novel, and the combination of curiosity-driven and brightness invariance principles is a unique aspect of the model. However, the paper builds on existing work in the field, and the authors acknowledge the influence of previous models on their work.

In terms of significance, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact on the field of visual attention modeling. The model's ability to capture the dynamics of visual attention in a unified framework makes it a useful tool for understanding how humans process visual information. Additionally, the model's performance on dynamic scenes suggests that it could be used in applications such as video saliency detection.

Overall, the paper is well-written and makes a significant contribution to the field of visual attention modeling. While there are some limitations to the paper, the authors provide a clear explanation of the model and its components, and the experimental results are presented in a clear and concise manner.
