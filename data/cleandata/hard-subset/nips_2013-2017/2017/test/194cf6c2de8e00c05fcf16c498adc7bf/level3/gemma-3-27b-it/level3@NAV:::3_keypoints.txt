Proposes EYMOL, a novel model that directly generates gaze trajectories instead of saliency maps.  
Frames eye movements using a Least Action Principle with a Lagrangian comprising kinetic/potential energies and a brightness invariance term.  
Diverges from prevalent saliency-map and fixation-selection optimization approaches, offering a physics-inspired perspective.  
Mathematical formulation is intriguing and original, though some derivations (esp. appendix) were hard for the reviewer to fully evaluate.  
Brightness invariance constraint is an interesting addition intended to better capture fixation behavior.  
Evaluated thoroughly on static and dynamic scenes against established models using metrics like AUC-Judd and NSS.  
Results are competitive and lend credibility but do not overwhelmingly outperform existing methods across all metrics.  
Uses simulated annealing for parameter estimation and explores approximations for computational efficiency.  
Technically sound within the reviewer’s understanding; experimental setup and claims are well supported.  
Clarity is generally good, but the mathematical sections require careful attention and may be dense for some readers.  
Originality is a strong point; the Least Action Principle framing is a significant contribution.  
Significance to the broader NIPS community is debatable; appears more directly relevant to vision science audiences.  
Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical framework, competitive empirical results, potential to inspire further research.  
Arguments against acceptance: limited NIPS relevance, mathematical complexity could hinder adoption, and improvements are not dramatic.  
Overall assessment: valuable contribution to computational visual attention and perception sciences, but fit for NIPS is uncertain; reviewer defers to experts on theoretical aspects.
