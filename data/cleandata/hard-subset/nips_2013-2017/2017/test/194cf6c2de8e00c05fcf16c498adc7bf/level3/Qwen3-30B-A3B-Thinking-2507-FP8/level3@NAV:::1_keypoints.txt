- Novel theoretical framework: applies variational principles/Least Action Principle to model scanpath dynamics and visual attention.
- Model formulation: introduces "affordances" (curiosity for detail and brightness invariance) as forces, builds a Lagrangian and derives Euler–Lagrange equations for eye movements.
- Emergent saliency: generates saliency maps as a byproduct of simulated scanpaths, proposing an alternative to centralized saliency models.
- Novelty relative to prior work: distinct from Itti’s feature-integration model and modern deep-learning saliency approaches.
- Weak motivation for affordances: key driving concepts appear ad hoc with insufficient biological justification.
- Missing connection to prior optimal-control work: paper should link its Least Action derivation to Wolpert et al.’s energy-minimization eye-movement literature.
- Mixed empirical results: reaches top scores on some MIT300/CAT2000 metrics but overall underperforms state-of-the-art deep models.
- Lack of scanpath analysis: no dynamic visualizations or detailed scanpath statistics, only static saliency metrics reported.
- Limited biological grounding: weak evidence to support claims of modeling biologically plausible attention dynamics.
- Strengths: elegant, unified theoretical approach and the interesting idea that saliency can emerge from scanpath dynamics.
- Key weaknesses: insufficient affordance justification, absence of scanpath visualization, and performance shortfalls versus leading methods.
- Recommended improvements: strengthen literature connections, provide scanpath visualizations and analyses, and empirically/biologically justify the proposed affordances.
