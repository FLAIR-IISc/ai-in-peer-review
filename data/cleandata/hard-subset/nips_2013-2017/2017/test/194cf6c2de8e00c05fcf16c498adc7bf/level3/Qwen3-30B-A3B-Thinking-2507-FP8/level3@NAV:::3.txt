

This paper introduces EYMOL, a novel framework for modeling eye movements by directly deriving gaze trajectories from a generalized Least Action Principle, contrasting with prevailing saliency-map-based approaches that optimize fixation selection. The authors correctly contextualize their work within the vision science literature, acknowledging the dominance of feature-integration theories (e.g., Itti & Koch) and recent ML-based saliency models. EYMOL’s core innovation lies in framing attention as a physical system: the Lagrangian incorporates "curiosity" (gradient-driven detail attraction), "brightness invariance" (for fixation/fixation tracking), and boundary constraints, yielding Euler-Lagrange equations that govern scanpath dynamics. The model computes saliency maps *as a byproduct* of trajectory simulation, evaluated on MIT1003, CAT2000, and SFU datasets against five state-of-the-art baselines.

**Strengths:**  
*Originality* is compelling—the variational physics-inspired approach offers a fresh theoretical lens distinct from optimization-centric models. *Quality* is strong: experiments demonstrate favorable (though not dominant) results across metrics (e.g., top scores in half the metrics on MIT300), with thorough parameter estimation via simulated annealing. The *clarity* of the main text is excellent; mathematical depth is appropriately delegated to the appendix, though the physics-heavy derivation slightly challenges accessibility. The authors transparently acknowledge limitations (e.g., approximation in brightness invariance for static scenes).

**Weaknesses:**  
The *significance* for NIPS is somewhat limited. While the theory holds merit for vision science (VSS community), it doesn’t advance core ML/NIPS challenges like scalability, generalization, or novel algorithmic design. The paper’s physics orientation may resonate more with cognitive scientists than ML researchers, and the results, while valid, don’t represent a breakthrough for computer vision applications. The appendix’s complexity (despite the authors’ careful derivation) may hinder reproducibility for non-physics audiences.

**Verdict:**  
This is a technically sound, well-motivated contribution to visual attention theory with clear value for perception sciences. It offers a novel perspective that could inspire future empirical work, though its immediate impact on NIPS-relevant ML research is modest. The paper is a solid fit for vision science venues but warrants careful consideration for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with the caveat that its significance for the NIPS audience is secondary to its theoretical contribution for vision science. The work merits attention from the broader perception community.
