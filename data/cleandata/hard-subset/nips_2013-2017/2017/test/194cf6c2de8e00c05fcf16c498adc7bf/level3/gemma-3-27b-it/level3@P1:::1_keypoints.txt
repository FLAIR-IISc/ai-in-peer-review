- Proposes a novel computational model framing scanpath generation as finding the stationary point of a generalized action integral (Lagrangian-based).
- Incorporates curiosity (driven by image detail) and brightness invariance as “affordances” into the Lagrangian to derive differential equations for attention.
- Numerical solution of the differential equations produces scanpaths and yields saliency maps as a byproduct.
- Strength: original, principled optimization framing and elegant mathematical formulation inspired by the Least Action Principle.
- Strength: comprehensive experimental evaluation on standard datasets (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, SFU) with clear parameter estimation via simulated annealing.
- Strength: potential for a more interpretable alternative to black-box deep learning saliency models.
- Weakness: the curiosity and brightness-invariance affordances feel ad hoc and lack strong justification.
- Weakness: limited biological and neurophysiological grounding; insufficient connection to motor-control and eye-movement literature (e.g., Wolpert/Haruno).
- Weakness: empirical performance is competitive but generally lags behind state-of-the-art deep learning approaches.
- Weakness: paper lacks illustrative visualizations of generated scanpaths and insufficient quantitative/visual support for the claimed object-tracking capability.
- Weakness: limited discussion linking the model to existing theories of attention and developmental evidence (newborn attention).
- Recommendation: reject but encourage revision and resubmission after strengthening theoretical/motivational grounding, adding scanpath visualizations, and deeper analysis of dynamic behavior.
