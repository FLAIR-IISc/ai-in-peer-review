## Review of "Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes"

This paper proposes a novel model of visual attention, termed EYMOL, grounded in a Least Action Principle and incorporating concepts of “curiosity” and brightness invariance to drive scanpath generation. The approach is interesting in its attempt to unify attention mechanisms through a physics-inspired framework, moving beyond purely data-driven saliency models. The authors connect their work to established theories of eye movements and attempt to provide a more principled, dynamic model of attention. 

The core idea of formulating scanpath generation as a variational problem is a potentially fruitful direction. The inclusion of “affordances” like curiosity and brightness invariance, intended to capture fundamental principles of visual exploration, is a unique aspect. The authors demonstrate the model’s application to both static images and dynamic scenes, comparing its performance to existing saliency detection methods. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The introduced “affordances” feel somewhat *ad hoc*. While intuitively plausible, their mathematical formulation and justification lack sufficient motivation. The connection to existing work on the Least Action Principle in the context of motor control and perception (e.g., the work of the Wolpert lab) is superficial and needs to be significantly strengthened. The biological motivation, while acknowledged as limited, could be improved with a more detailed discussion of how these principles align with known neural mechanisms.

Empirically, the model’s performance is mixed and generally lags behind current deep learning-based saliency models. More critically, the paper lacks a thorough analysis of the *dynamics* of the generated scanpaths. While overall statistics are reported, there’s a noticeable absence of visualizations or quantitative analyses demonstrating the claimed dynamic properties – for example, how the model captures smooth pursuit or exploratory behaviors. The lack of figures characterizing the scanpaths themselves is a significant omission.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to modeling scanpaths using a variational framework.
*   Interesting attempt to ground attention in fundamental principles.
*   Demonstration of applicability to both static and dynamic scenes.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Weak motivation for the chosen “affordances”.
*   Insufficient connection to relevant literature on the Least Action Principle and motor control.
*   Limited empirical performance compared to benchmarks.
*   Lack of detailed analysis of scanpath dynamics.



**Overall:** This is an enjoyable paper with interesting ideas, but it requires substantial revision. Strengthening the theoretical motivation, grounding the model more firmly in biological principles, and providing a more comprehensive empirical evaluation – particularly focusing on the dynamic characteristics of the generated scanpaths – are crucial for improving its contribution and justifying acceptance. I recommend rejection in its current form, but encourage resubmission after addressing these concerns.
