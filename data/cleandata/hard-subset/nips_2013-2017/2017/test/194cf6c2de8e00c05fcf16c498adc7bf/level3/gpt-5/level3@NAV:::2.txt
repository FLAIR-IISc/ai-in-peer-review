This paper proposes an energy-based, variational model of visual attention that predicts scanpaths in static and dynamic scenes by treating gaze as a particle following Euler–Lagrange dynamics under a generalized least-action principle. The Lagrangian combines three terms: (i) a harmonic-oscillator boundary potential to confine trajectories to the retinal/image extent; (ii) a curiosity-driven term alternating between local and peripheral spatial gradients (via cos^2/sin^2 modulation) to bias toward detailed regions across scales; and (iii) a brightness-invariance term (à la optical flow brightness constancy) to induce fixation and motion tracking. From this, the authors derive differential equations that map initial position/velocity to full trajectories and obtain saliency maps as visitation counts. Quantitative results on MIT1003/MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video indicate performance comparable to contemporary saliency approaches, including deep models.

Quality: The formulation is principled and technically interesting; deriving explicit equations of motion and analyzing stability (e.g., constraints on λ) is valuable. However, the exposition is hard to follow, with undefined symbols, inconsistent notation, and probable typos (e.g., kinetic energy missing the 1/2 factor). The use of Iverson brackets introduces nondifferentiabilities at borders; a smooth barrier may be preferable. The evaluation is sensible but would benefit from ablations on initial conditions, center bias, blur post-processing, and ω sensitivity, as these can materially affect AUC/NSS.

Clarity: The paper requires substantial proofreading and reorganization. All abbreviations should be expanded at first use, and every variable/function in the equations (especially Section 3 and the Appendix) must be explicitly defined. Move the two-page appendix to the supplement and use the space to clarify the main text, provide algorithmic details (ODE solver, step size, parameter domains, SA schedule), and report parameter values.

Originality: Framing bottom-up attention as a least-action dynamical system that unifies multi-scale “curiosity” with brightness invariance is novel relative to saliency-map pipelines and data-driven CNNs. It connects classical variational vision (optical flow) with scanpath generation.

Significance: A formal, interpretable model that yields competitive saliency without heavy learning is valuable for both vision and cognitive science, and could inform active vision/control. The link to psychological models of optimal eye movements (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler) should be discussed, including how this framework could incorporate task priors or decision-theoretic objectives.

Pros:
- Elegant, unified variational formulation with interpretable components.
- Predicts scanpaths and saliency; competitive on static and dynamic benchmarks.
- Potentially impactful for theory-building in attention.

Cons:
- Poor clarity/notation; missing definitions and likely typos.
- Evaluation lacks ablations on biases and hyperparameters.
- Limited connection to broader literature (optimal search, dynamic neural fields, RL-based gaze control).

Recommendation: Weak accept if clarity and evaluation issues are addressed; otherwise borderline.
