- Proposes a bottom-up variational/least-action dynamical model of overt visual attention where scanpaths are stationary points of a Lagrangian-derived ODE.
- Lagrangian combines a boundary potential, a curiosity potential (mixture of local and peripheral gradients with cos²/sin² alternation), and a brightness-invariance (optical flow) term.
- Euler–Lagrange yields a second-order velocity-dependent ODE; scanpaths generated by integrating from near-center initial conditions and saliency maps from visit counts.
- Parameters tuned by simulated annealing on CAT2000; evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU videos against classical and some learning-based baselines.
- Novelty: least-action formulation applied to attention/scanpath modeling is original within saliency literature, though related to optimal control and active inference.
- Strength: interpretable, unified dynamical formulation that captures saccade-like exploration and fixation/pursuit within one model.
- Strength: clear mathematical derivations with solvable ODEs; model is simple and fast to simulate.
- Strength: single framework applied to both static images and videos with reasonable performance for a non-deep, bottom-up approach.
- Strength: reports on standard datasets/metrics and explores exact vs approximate brightness-invariance.
- Weakness: several design choices are ad hoc (cos²/sin² alternation, ω, gradient mixture, boundary potential) with no principled justification or ablation evidence.
- Weakness: limited biological plausibility—no oculomotor constraints, inhibition of return, motor noise, latencies, or validation against scanpath statistics.
- Weakness: empirical evaluation focuses on static saliency metrics; core claim about dynamic scanpath behavior is not visualized or quantitatively analyzed in the main paper.
- Weakness: omits comparisons to top contemporary deep saliency models; performance generally below SOTA deep methods.
- Weakness: center bias and blur are injected/tuned which may inflate scores and raise fairness/cross-dataset generalization concerns.
- Technical issues: nonstandard kinetic energy expression, confusing well-posedness condition on λ, and nondifferentiable boundary forces due to indicator functions.
- Quality: derivations largely correct and ODE implementable, but some formulas and the Iverson bracket use need correction; tracking claims need quantitative pursuit metrics.
- Clarity: paper is generally organized with helpful appendix but needs consistent notation, corrected kinetic term, clarified hyperparameters, and main-paper scanpath figures/energy plots.
- Specific suggestions: position relative to optimal control, active inference, and oculomotor/scanpath models; run ablations for each term and alternation; evaluate scanpaths with MultiMatch/ScanMatch and saccade/fixation stats.
- Specific suggestions: use shuffled AUC/IG/sAUC to control center bias, add strong deep baselines, report runtime, and smooth boundary forces; consider learning ω and weights or using a contrast sensitivity function.
- Pros for acceptance: elegant, interpretable variational formulation; fast and simple generative scanpath baseline; cross-domain applicability to images and videos.
- Cons for acceptance: heuristic design choices, limited biological grounding, missing key baselines, sub-SOTA performance, and absence of empirical scanpath validation; some technical ambiguities.
- Overall recommendation: borderline reject—interesting ideas but requires stronger motivation, connections to prior work, and substantially more empirical evidence on scanpath dynamics.
- Reviewer confidence: high regarding empirical/methodological gaps; medium regarding completeness of related-work coverage in oculomotor modeling.
