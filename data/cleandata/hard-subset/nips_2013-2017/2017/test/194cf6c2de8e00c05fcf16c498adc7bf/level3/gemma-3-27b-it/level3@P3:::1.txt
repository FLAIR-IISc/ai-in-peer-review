This paper proposes a novel model of visual attention grounded in a Least Action Principle, aiming to explain scanpath dynamics through concepts of "curiosity" and brightness invariance. While the core idea is intriguing and attempts a more principled, dynamic approach than many current saliency models, the work suffers from a lack of strong motivation for its specific choices, limited biological grounding, and mixed empirical results.

The paper correctly identifies the limitations of existing saliency models which largely focus on static saliency map generation followed by rule-based scanpath simulation (citing Itti et al., Koch & Ullman, and recent deep learning approaches). The authors position their work as a departure, attempting to model scanpaths *directly* from underlying principles. This is a valuable goal, as current approaches often feel ad-hoc in their scanpath generation. The introduction effectively frames the problem, acknowledging the interplay between bottom-up and top-down attention and highlighting the surprisingly consistent early fixations observed even in novel stimuli.

However, the core "principles of visual attention" – boundedness, curiosity, and brightness invariance – feel somewhat arbitrarily chosen. While individually plausible, their combination and mathematical formulation lack a compelling justification. The "curiosity" term, specifically, feels ad-hoc, relying on an alternation between local and peripheral information without a clear rationale. The brightness invariance constraint is a stronger choice, connecting to established concepts in vision (optical flow estimation) but still feels isolated without broader integration into a cognitive framework. Connecting these affordances more directly to established theories of attention, or providing a more compelling argument for their selection, would significantly strengthen the paper.

The application of the Least Action Principle is an interesting attempt to provide a unifying mathematical framework. However, the paper lacks a sufficient connection to the extensive literature on optimal control and eye movements, particularly the work of the Wolpert lab and others who have formalized eye movements as minimizing uncertainty or maximizing information gain. A discussion of how this work relates to existing formulations of motor control and predictive coding would be incredibly beneficial. The derivation itself, while mathematically sound, lacks clarity in relating the “potential” and “kinetic” energies to actual neural processes.

The experimental evaluation presents mixed results. While the model achieves competitive performance on standard datasets (MIT300, CAT2000, SFU), it generally *underperforms* compared to established deep learning-based saliency models. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't delve deeply into the reasons. A more thorough ablation study, examining the contribution of each term in the Lagrangian, could reveal the strengths and weaknesses of the model. Critically, the paper almost entirely focuses on *static* saliency metrics (AUC, NSS) and fails to provide any visual examples or quantitative analysis of the *generated scanpaths* themselves. Given the paper's focus on dynamics, this is a major omission. Showing example scanpaths, comparing them to human fixations visually, and analyzing scanpath statistics (e.g., saccade length, fixation duration, scanpath smoothness) would dramatically strengthen the paper. The simplified version of the brightness invariance term (V1) is justified, but the lack of a detailed comparison of the generated scanpaths between V1 and V2 is a missed opportunity.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, though the mathematical notation could be improved for readability. The organization is logical, and the writing is clear. However, the lack of detailed explanations for key choices and the limited biological grounding detract from the overall impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel attempt to model scanpaths directly from principled dynamics.
*   Interesting application of the Least Action Principle to visual attention.
*   Competitive performance on standard saliency datasets.
*   Potential for a more unified theory of attention and eye movements.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Ad-hoc nature of the chosen “affordances” (curiosity, brightness invariance).
*   Insufficient connection to existing literature on optimal control and eye movements.
*   Limited biological grounding.
*   Mixed empirical results and lack of scanpath-level analysis.
*   Weak motivation for specific mathematical formulations.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an enjoyable and thought-provoking attempt to model visual attention. However, it falls short of being a strong scientific contribution due to its lack of strong motivation, limited biological grounding, and insufficient empirical demonstration of its claimed dynamical properties. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit, focusing on strengthening the theoretical justification, providing detailed scanpath analysis, and connecting the work more directly to existing literature in both neuroscience and control theory. The potential of the idea is there, but it requires significantly more development.
