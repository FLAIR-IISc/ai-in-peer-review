- Proposes an energy-based variational model of visual attention that generates scanpaths by treating gaze as a particle following Euler–Lagrange dynamics from a least-action principle.  
- Lagrangian combines three terms: boundary potential (harmonic oscillator), curiosity-driven multi-scale gradient modulation (cos^2/sin^2), and brightness-invariance for fixation/motion tracking.  
- Derives differential equations mapping initial position/velocity to trajectories and produces saliency as visitation counts.  
- Quantitative results on MIT1003/MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video are competitive with contemporary saliency approaches, including deep models.  
- Formulation is principled and technically interesting; stability analysis and constraints on λ are valuable.  
- Exposition is hard to follow: undefined symbols, inconsistent notation, likely typos (e.g., missing 1/2 in kinetic energy).  
- Use of Iverson brackets creates nondifferentiabilities at borders; recommend using a smooth barrier instead.  
- Evaluation sensible but missing important ablations: initial conditions, center bias, blur post-processing, and ω sensitivity (can affect AUC/NSS).  
- Paper needs substantial proofreading and reorganization; expand abbreviations and define every variable/function, especially in Section 3 and Appendix.  
- Move two-page appendix to the supplement and use main text space to clarify algorithmic details (ODE solver, step size, parameter domains, SA schedule) and report parameter values.  
- Original framing is novel, linking variational optical-flow ideas with scanpath generation and multi-scale curiosity.  
- Potentially significant for vision and cognitive science as an interpretable, non‑learning competitive saliency model and for active vision/control applications.  
- Should discuss connections to psychological models of optimal eye movements and how task priors or decision-theoretic objectives could be incorporated.  
- Pros: elegant unified formulation, predicts both scanpaths and saliency, and is competitive without heavy learning.  
- Cons: poor clarity/notation, missing definitions and typos, limited evaluation ablations, and weak connections to broader literature (optimal search, dynamic neural fields, RL-based gaze control).  
- Recommendation: Weak accept if clarity and evaluation issues are addressed; otherwise borderline.
