- Proposes EYMOL, a novel visual-attention model that uses a generalized Least Action Principle to directly model scanpaths as action-minimizing trajectories.  
- The model's potential energy encodes image detail and peripheral features, while kinetic energy includes a brightness invariance term important for fixation.  
- Derives Euler–Lagrange differential equations governing attention and uses simulated annealing for parameter estimation.  
- Evaluated on image and video datasets (MIT300, CAT2000, SFU) and shows competitive or superior saliency detection performance.  
- Correctly situates the work within prior vision and cognitive science literature, contrasting with saliency-map–based and CNN approaches.  
- Novelty: applying a variational (Least Action) principle to visual attention offers a fundamentally different perspective.  
- Theoretical foundation: solid, mathematically rigorous derivation provides a clear mechanistic interpretation.  
- Direct scanpath modeling avoids intermediate saliency-map steps and may capture richer attention dynamics.  
- Presentation: generally well-written and clearly articulated despite dense mathematics.  
- Mathematical accessibility: heavy use of variational calculus may limit readership; more intuitive explanations are needed.  
- Parameter sensitivity: reliance on simulated annealing raises concerns about initialization, tuning, and robustness; sensitivity analysis is lacking.  
- Biological plausibility: connections to neural mechanisms are underdeveloped and require further discussion.  
- Relevance to NIPS: contribution may be more aligned with vision science than core NIPS priorities, possibly seen as incremental.  
- Limited ablation studies: insufficient analysis of individual model components (e.g., brightness invariance) to assess their contributions.  
- Recommendation: overall positive—novel, well-grounded, competitive results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve accessibility and biological discussion.
