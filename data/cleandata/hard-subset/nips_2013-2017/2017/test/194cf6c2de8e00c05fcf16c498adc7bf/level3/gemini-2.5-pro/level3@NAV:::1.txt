This paper proposes a novel bottom-up model of visual attention that generates scanpaths for both static and dynamic scenes. The core idea is to model the trajectory of the focus of attention as a dynamical system derived from a generalized Least Action Principle. This is a departure from classic feature-integration models (e.g., Itti et al.) and modern deep learning approaches that typically predict a static saliency map. The authors formulate a Lagrangian containing terms they describe as "affordances," namely a "curiosity" principle that attracts attention to high-gradient regions and a "brightness invariance" principle to encourage fixation and tracking. By finding the stationary point of the action, they derive the Euler-Lagrange equations of motion that govern the scanpath. Saliency maps are then generated as a byproduct by accumulating fixation locations over time.

The primary strength of this work is its originality and elegance. Framing scanpath generation as a variational problem is a principled and intriguing approach that seeks to uncover the fundamental laws driving eye movements, rather than relying on heuristics or black-box deep models. The attempt to unify saccadic exploration and object tracking within a single dynamical framework is ambitious and commendable.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that limit its contribution. First, the foundational "affordances" of curiosity and brightness invariance appear somewhat ad hoc. The paper would be much stronger if it provided a more rigorous motivation—either from cognitive science or information theory—for why these specific principles, in their proposed mathematical forms, are the key drivers of attention.

Second, the paper fails to connect its theoretical framework to the extensive existing literature on optimal control models of eye movements. The application of least-action or minimum-cost principles to motor control, including saccades, has been a major research theme for decades (e.g., work from the labs of Daniel Wolpert or Eero Simoncelli). The lack of engagement with this prior art is a critical omission.

Finally, the empirical evaluation does not sufficiently support the paper's central claims about modeling dynamics. While the model generates scanpaths, the evaluation is almost entirely based on static saliency map metrics (AUC, NSS, etc.), where its performance is mixed and generally lower than benchmark deep models. The paper crucially lacks figures or quantitative analyses characterizing the *dynamics* of the generated scanpaths (e.g., saccade amplitude/direction distributions, comparison to human scanpath statistics), which would be the most direct way to validate its primary contribution.

In summary, this is an enjoyable paper with interesting ideas. However, it requires stronger motivation for its core principles, better grounding in related literature, and a more direct empirical demonstration of its claimed dynamic modeling capabilities.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant variational framework for generating attentional scanpaths.
*   Addresses the important goal of modeling attention dynamics, moving beyond static saliency prediction.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The core principles ("affordances") are insufficiently motivated and appear ad hoc.
*   Fails to cite or discuss highly relevant prior work on optimal control models of eye movements.
*   The empirical evaluation does not validate the model's primary contribution—scanpath dynamics.
*   Performance on standard saliency benchmarks is not competitive.
