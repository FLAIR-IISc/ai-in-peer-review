This paper introduces EYMOL, a novel, physics-inspired model for generating visual attention scanpaths in both static and dynamic scenes. The central idea is to frame the generation of an eye-movement trajectory as a variational problem, governed by a generalized Least Action Principle. This stands in contrast to the dominant paradigm in computational attention, which typically involves first computing a spatial saliency map (e.g., based on feature integration theory) and then applying a winner-take-all selection mechanism, often supplemented with heuristics like inhibition-of-return, to generate fixations. EYMOL instead models the dynamics of the scanpath directly. The authors construct a Lagrangian function composed of a standard kinetic energy term and a generalized potential energy term. This potential energy cleverly combines three principles: a boundary constraint (a harmonic oscillator), a "curiosity" term that attracts attention to regions with high detail (image gradients), and a brightness invariance constraint (similar to the optical flow constraint) that encourages fixation and object tracking. The scanpath is then derived as the trajectory that finds the stationary point of the action integral, leading to a set of Euler-Lagrange differential equations that govern the motion of the focus of attention.

**Quality**

The paper presents a technically solid piece of work. The theoretical framework is laid out, the governing equations are derived, and an algorithm for parameter estimation is described. The model is then validated empirically on several standard eye-tracking datasets for both images (MIT300, CAT2000) and video (SFU). The method of generating saliency maps as a byproduct (by aggregating visitations from simulated trajectories) is sound, and the quantitative comparisons against several representative models from the literature show that EYMOL is competitive, achieving strong performance on several metrics.

One area where I cannot fully assess the technical soundness is in the detailed mathematical derivations presented in the appendix. My background in analytical mechanics is not sufficient to rigorously verify the steps leading from the Lagrangian to the final system of differential equations. However, the high-level formulation appears coherent. Assuming the derivations are correct, the work is a complete and well-supported contribution.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating their approach and situating it within the existing literature. The three core principles driving the model are explained intuitively before their mathematical formulation is introduced. Despite the complexity of the underlying mathematics, the main conceptual contribution—modeling scanpath dynamics directly via a variational principle—is communicated very effectively. The experimental setup and results are also presented clearly.

**Originality**

The main strength of this paper is its originality. The application of the Least Action Principle to model the dynamics of visual attention is a fundamentally novel approach. It provides an elegant and unifying framework that can account for both saccadic movements (driven by curiosity) and smooth pursuit/fixation (driven by brightness invariance) within a single set of differential equations. This is a significant departure from the multi-stage, heuristic-driven models that have dominated the field. The work effectively bridges concepts from classical mechanics and computational vision to propose a new foundational theory for a low-level cognitive process.

**Significance**

The work is highly significant for the computational vision and vision science communities. It offers a new, principled theory of eye-movement generation that could inspire considerable follow-up research, both in terms of computational modeling and in designing new empirical studies to test its predictions. The idea of deriving attentional laws from a simple functional could have a lasting impact.

However, the significance for the core NIPS audience is less certain. While principled, theory-driven models are valuable, the immediate connection to mainstream machine learning and neural information processing is not explicitly drawn. It is not clear if or how these ideas could be integrated with or inspire new developments in, for example, attention mechanisms for deep learning. The paper feels more aligned with a specialized venue like VSS or a computational neuroscience journal. That said, its theoretical novelty and elegance make it a thought-provoking contribution that could be of interest to a subset of the NIPS community.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly original and conceptually elegant theoretical framework for modeling visual attention.
*   The variational approach provides a unifying account of different eye-movement behaviors (saccades, tracking) that are often modeled with separate mechanisms.
*   The model is validated on standard benchmarks for both images and video, demonstrating competitive performance against established methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The primary weakness is a potential mismatch in audience. The paper's significance and impact may be greater in the vision science or computational neuroscience communities than in the general machine learning community represented at NIPS.
*   The technical details of the physics-based derivations are complex and may be difficult for many reviewers to fully verify.
