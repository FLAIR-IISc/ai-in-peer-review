Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a variational, energy-based model of bottom-up visual attention that directly generates scanpaths via Euler–Lagrange equations derived from a generalized least-action principle. The Lagrangian consists of: (i) a harmonic-oscillator “retinal” potential to keep the focus of attention bounded within the image; (ii) a curiosity-driven term that alternates local and peripheral detail via gradients of brightness and a blurred version of the image (cos2/sin2 modulation); and (iii) a brightness-invariance term encouraging bt + ∇b · ẋ ≈ 0, akin to the optical-flow brightness constancy constraint, to support fixation and motion tracking. From this Lagrangian, the authors derive a cost and explicit second-order ODEs predicting eye position from initial position and velocity. They compute saliency maps as visit counts aggregated over many model trajectories. Parameters are set by simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000-TRAIN to optimize NSS. Quantitative results on static (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and dynamic (SFU) eye-tracking datasets show performance comparable to several baselines, including classical models and a recent deep method, despite the model’s simplicity.

The idea of bottom-up saliency as a global computation over feature maps is well established (e.g., Koch & Ullman; Itti et al.), as are information-theoretic and surprise-based accounts (Bruce & Tsotsos; Itti & Baldi). There is also prior work on scanpath simulation with heuristics (winner-take-all, inhibition of return) and on biologically inspired saccade models (e.g., Le Meur & Liu). The novelty here is a formal, unified variational perspective that ties curiosity and brightness invariance into an explicit dynamical system, bridging analytic mechanics with eye movement generation.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The variational construction and derivation of Euler–Lagrange equations are conceptually sound and technically nontrivial. The inclusion of the brightness-constancy term is well-motivated and connects to optical flow. The alternation between local and peripheral gradients is an interesting device to avoid local traps.
- Empirical support: Static and dynamic experiments report competitive AUC/NSS against representative baselines. However, the evaluation emphasizes saliency maps rather than scanpaths (the model’s primary output). No scanpath metrics (e.g., MultiMatch, string-edit distance) or temporal analyses are reported. Performance could be confounded by center bias and post-hoc blur tuning; including sAUC/IG would help control for these.
- Reproducibility: Important details are missing. It is not clear how m, k, λ, ηb, ηp, ω, and the Gaussian blur/center-bias parameters are chosen or constrained beyond annealing; initial-condition distributions, integration method and step size, and parameter domain D are under-specified. The kinetic term K = (1/m)||ẋ||2 (without 1/2) deviates from the standard definition; a justification or note on absorbed constants would help. The boundary potential uses Iverson brackets, making forces piecewise and potentially non-smooth at borders; numerical implications are not discussed. Provide code and exact parameter values for each dataset.
- Statistical care: Parameters are tuned on CAT2000-TRAIN; blur and center bias are optimized via grid search. Please ensure no leakage into test sets and report results with and without center-bias tuning. Report variance across initializations and parameter sensitivity/ablation (remove each Lagrangian term in turn).

Clarity
The paper is difficult to follow and needs substantial proofreading and exposition improvements. Abbreviations and notation are introduced without definitions in places; variables/functions (especially in Section 2/3 and equations) must be explicitly defined when first used. Examples:
- Define all symbols in the Lagrangian and equations (x, ẋ, ẍ, b, p, bx, px, ḃ, bxt, etc.), their dimensions and units, and the roles of m, k, λ, ηb, ηp, ω.
- Clarify the construction of p(t, x) and the center-bias Gaussian; specify kernel sizes, σ, and normalization.
- Explain the cos2/sin2 alternation and how ω relates to frame rate/visual timescales.
- State integration details (SciPy solver name, tolerances, time step, total time) and initial condition distributions for position/velocity.
- Fix typos and grammar (“the the”, “psedo-code”, inconsistent equation numbering, footnote markers), and ensure all references are complete and consistently formatted.
Consider moving the two-page appendix to the supplementary and use the freed space to define variables and present the model more clearly in the main text. Start with the static-image simplification (the tight upper bound used for brightness invariance) and then present the full dynamic case.

Originality
The variational least-action framing of eye-movement generation is novel for saliency and provides a principled alternative to heuristic scanpath simulators. While variational/optimal-control ideas have appeared in vision and oculomotor control, the specific Lagrangian combining curiosity and brightness invariance, and the resulting explicit dynamical system for scanpaths, is new and interesting. The paper should more clearly position itself relative to optimal eye-movement theories in psychology and vision science, particularly Najemnik & Geisler (2005, 2008) on optimal visual search and information gain, and discuss how the proposed laws might integrate with or approximate optimal policies.

Significance
Conceptually strong: a formal bridge between physics-inspired principles and eye-movement dynamics that could be valuable to both computer vision and cognitive science. Practically, reported performance is competitive but not clearly state-of-the-art versus modern deep saliency models; the main value lies in interpretability and the principled dynamical formulation. Additional analyses could increase impact:
- Compare model scanpaths to human scanpaths temporally.
- Ablations for each Lagrangian term and sensitivity to ω, λ, ηb, ηp.
- Evaluate on more dynamic datasets (e.g., DIEM, DHF1K) and include center-bias-controlled metrics (sAUC, IG).
- Analyze the learned parameter regime and theoretical stability (e.g., the λ < m/4 condition) on real data.

Pros
- Elegant, principled variational formulation; explicit ODEs for scanpaths from initial states.
- Unifies saccades, fixation, and motion tracking via brightness invariance.
- Competitive saliency performance on static and dynamic datasets with a simple, interpretable model.
- Potentially broad relevance to cognitive science and computational neuroscience.

Cons
- Clarity and organization issues impede understanding; missing definitions and notation.
- Limited evaluation of the core claim (scanpaths); heavy reliance on saliency map metrics.
- Potential center-bias/blur tuning confounds; incomplete reporting of training/test hygiene.
- Reproducibility gaps (parameters, solver details, code).
- Limited connection to optimal eye-movement literature (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler) and modern deep baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Thorough proofreading; define all variables/functions and abbreviations on first use; reorganize to present the simplified static case first; move derivations to supplementary.
- Add scanpath-level evaluations and ablations; include sAUC/IG metrics; report with/without center bias.
- Discuss relation to optimal search/active-sensing models (Najemnik & Geisler 2005/2008) and possibilities for integrating top-down/task signals into the Lagrangian.
- Justify modeling choices (K scaling, boundary potential smoothness, ω selection) and provide stability analysis and parameter sensitivity.
- Release code and exact parameter settings.

Recommendation: Weak reject (borderline). The core idea is original and promising, and the empirical results are encouraging, but the paper’s current clarity, missing definitions, and limited evaluation of the central scanpath claim make it difficult to fully assess and reproduce. With substantial rewriting, additional analyses (especially scanpath comparisons and ablations), and stronger positioning within the broader attention/optimality literature, this could become a valuable contribution. Reviewer confidence: medium.
