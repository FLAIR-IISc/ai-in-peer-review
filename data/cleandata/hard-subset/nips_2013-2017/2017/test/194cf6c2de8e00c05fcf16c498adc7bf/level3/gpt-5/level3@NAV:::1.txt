This paper proposes a continuous-time, bottom-up model of visual attention (EYMOL) in which scanpaths arise as trajectories minimizing a generalized action. The Lagrangian combines kinetic energy with a “potential” comprising: (i) a boundary term confining the gaze to the retinal field, (ii) a curiosity term that alternates between local and peripheral gradient energy, and (iii) a brightness-invariance term derived from the optical-flow constraint. Saliency maps are produced as visit histograms over multiple simulated trajectories. Experiments on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU report mixed performance relative to established baselines.

Relation to prior work: The model contrasts with winner-take-all saliency pipelines (Itti-Koch-Ullman), information-theoretic approaches (Bruce & Tsotsos; Itti & Baldi), and modern deep benchmarks, and aligns conceptually with optimality principles in motor control. However, the manuscript would benefit from explicit connections to the eye-movement and motor control literature on least-action/optimal control (e.g., Harris & Wolpert on signal-dependent noise and minimum-variance saccades, Todorov & Jordan on optimal feedback control), as well as smooth pursuit control, to situate the proposed variational laws within existing theories.

Strengths
- A clear, elegant dynamical-systems formulation that unifies saccades, fixation, and tracking within one variational framework.
- Scanpaths are primary and saliency maps emerge as a byproduct—conceptually appealing and different from map-first pipelines.
- Simple ODEs with real-time integrability are attractive for simulation and potential robotics applications.

Weaknesses
- The “affordances” (curiosity via gradient energy with cosine/sine switching; brightness invariance) feel ad hoc and insufficiently motivated biologically or information-theoretically. The externally injected center bias further weakens the bottom-up claim.
- The Least Action derivation lacks linkage to established eye-movement optimality accounts (Wolpert lab; minimum-jerk/variance; stochastic control), and the velocity-dependent “potential” would be better framed via constrained optimization or Lagrange multipliers.
- Empirical evidence is limited: performance is mixed and generally below current deep saliency models; no scanpath-level analyses (e.g., MultiMatch, saccade amplitude/angle distributions, TTFF, temporal NSS) or qualitative trajectory figures in the main paper.
- Parameter tuning (simulated annealing) is weakly justified, with potential overfitting to NSS and sensitivity to initial conditions.

Quality: Mathematically sound but missing key theoretical links and thorough empirical validation. Clarity: Generally well written, but the paper needs main-text figures of trajectories and an expanded related-work section. Originality: Novel synthesis of least-action with saliency, though conceptually close to optimal control traditions. Significance: Promising as a generative scanpath model, but current results do not advance state of the art.

Pros for acceptance
- Fresh perspective; unifying dynamical account of attention
- Potentially useful for generative modeling and robotics

Cons against acceptance
- Ad hoc affordances; limited biological grounding
- Mixed performance vs deep baselines
- Insufficient scanpath/temporal analyses and missing figures
- Weak connections to established optimal control literature and eye-movement theory
