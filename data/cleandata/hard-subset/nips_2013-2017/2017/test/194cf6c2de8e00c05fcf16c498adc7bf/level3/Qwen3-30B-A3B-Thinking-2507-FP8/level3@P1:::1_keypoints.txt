- Proposes EYMOL, a novel computational model of visual attention grounded in variational principles (Least Action) using Euler–Lagrange-derived scanpath dynamics.  
- Models three affordances: boundedness (harmonic oscillator), curiosity-driven attention to high-gradient regions, and brightness invariance for fixation/tracking.  
- Generates saliency maps as a byproduct of simulated scanpaths, avoiding explicit saliency-map construction.  
- Represents a distinct, dynamics-focused paradigm compared to neural network–based saliency models.  
- Mathematical formulation and derivation of Euler–Lagrange equations are technically sound and elegant.  
- Real-time scanpath generation is a practical advantage over iterative deep-learning approaches.  
- Experimental validation on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU shows competitive performance on some metrics (e.g., NSS on MIT300).  
- Parameter estimation via simulated annealing is well-justified; brightness-invariance approximation is numerically effective.  
- Core weakness: affordances (curiosity and brightness invariance) lack cognitive/behavioral grounding and empirical justification.  
- Curiosity modulation (oscillatory cos²/sin² term) appears arbitrary and unsupported by visual neuroscience evidence.  
- Paper neglects established links to optimal control and sensorimotor theories (e.g., Wolpert, Todorov), weakening the Least Action claim.  
- Performance is mixed: underperforms leading deep models on most metrics and weaker on CAT2000TEST.  
- Major omission: no dynamic scanpath visualizations to assess temporal attention dynamics despite focus on trajectories.  
- Biological motivation is superficial and does not demonstrate alignment with known eye-movement properties (saccade distributions, inhibition-of-return).  
- Novelty: conceptually original application of variational principles but not sufficiently differentiated or impactful relative to prior work.  
- Recommendation: borderline rejection; paper needs stronger theoretical grounding, empirical motivation for affordances, dynamic visualizations, and broader comparisons with recent deep models.
