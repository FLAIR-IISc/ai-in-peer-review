- Novel variational/physics-inspired approach using the Least Action principle to derive differential equations predicting eye movements in static and dynamic scenes.
- Model built from three principles: harmonic-oscillator eye-movement constraints, curiosity-driven multi-scale brightness-change, and brightness-invariance.
- Saliency maps are produced as a byproduct of simulated attentional scanpaths rather than being constructed as primary outputs.
- Formal mathematical foundation with Euler–Lagrange derivation, offering a principled unification of static and dynamic attention.
- Strong quantitative performance on static (MIT1003, CAT2000) and dynamic (SFU) datasets, matching state-of-the-art in half of the metrics.
- Originality: integrating variational physics into visual attention modeling is a distinctive conceptual advance.
- Potential significance: provides a principled framework that could bridge computational neuroscience and computer vision and generate realistic scanpaths from first principles.
- Major clarity issues: dense, poorly organized writing with many undefined variables and insufficient explanations (especially Section 3 and the equations).
- Essential technical details are relegated to a two-page appendix that should be moved to supplementary material and integrated into the main text.
- Lacks connection to psychological literature on optimal eye movements (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2008), weakening cognitive validity.
- Parameter estimation is under-described; simulated annealing and chosen parameter ranges are insufficiently justified.
- Needs more thorough analysis of model limitations and clearer explanation of how parameters were optimized.
- Recommendation: minor revision with specific changes—move appendix, explicitly define all variables/functions, expand parameter-estimation discussion, connect to psychological theories, and improve organization (Sections 2.2 and 3).
