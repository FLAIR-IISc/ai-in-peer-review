

This paper proposes a novel variational framework for modeling visual attention scanpaths, grounded in a generalized Least Action Principle from physics. The authors derive differential equations for eye movements by combining three key principles: (1) harmonic oscillator constraints for bounded retina motion, (2) curiosity-driven attention to regions with high gradient magnitude (via a blend of local and peripheral features), and (3) brightness invariance for fixation and tracking. The model, termed EYMOL, produces saliency maps as a byproduct of scanpath simulation, contrasting with most prior work that directly optimizes saliency maps. The approach extends the physics-inspired modeling of attention seen in earlier NIPS work (e.g., [29] on variational learning) but applies it to the *dynamics* of eye movements rather than static saliency.

**Strengths:** The core theoretical contribution is original and compelling. The unification of curiosity-driven exploration and brightness invariance into a single variational framework offers a fresh perspective on bottom-up attention, distinct from feature-integration models [9, 10] or learning-based approaches [1, 18]. The derivation of Euler-Lagrange equations (Appendix) is technically sound, and the model’s ability to generate realistic scanpaths *and* saliency maps from the same equations is elegant. Experimental results on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video datasets are competitive with state-of-the-art methods (e.g., achieving top scores in half the metrics on CAT2000), demonstrating practical utility. The model’s real-time capability for dynamic scenes (video tracking) is a notable strength absent in many prior works.

**Weaknesses:** The paper suffers from severe clarity issues that undermine its scientific contribution. Critical variables (e.g., *b(t,x)*, *ω*, *λ*) and parameters are introduced without explicit definitions in the main text, forcing readers to parse dense equations in Section 2.3 and the Appendix. For instance, *ω* (frequency in Eq. 4) is never explained, and *ηb/ηp* (Section 2.3) are used without justification. The Appendix, which contains 2 pages of technical derivation, should be moved to supplementary material to free space for clarifying definitions in the main text. Section 3’s experimental descriptions lack precision: "a Gaussian blob" (3.1) and "grid search" (3.2) are vague, making reproducibility difficult. The claim that "the approximation [in Eq. 6] is very good" (3.3) lacks quantitative justification beyond Table 2’s statistical equivalence. Crucially, the paper misses a key opportunity to connect to psychological literature on *optimal* eye movements (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2008), which could have strengthened the significance argument by showing how EYMOL aligns with empirical findings on efficient visual search.

**Quality & Significance:** The technical quality is high in theory but compromised by poor exposition. The experiments validate the model’s efficacy but do not *advance* the state of the art demonstrably—performance is comparable, not superior, to SOTA. Significance is limited because the model’s novelty (physics analogy) doesn’t translate to substantial improvements in saliency prediction or mechanistic insight beyond existing scanpath models [6, 24]. The focus on "laws" of attention is intriguing but overreaches given the lack of biological validation (e.g., no comparison of generated scanpaths to human fixation sequences beyond saliency maps).

**Recommendation:** **Reject**. The paper’s core idea is promising and original, but the writing flaws are severe enough to prevent meaningful assessment of its contribution. Without clear definitions, reproducibility is impossible, and the lack of connection to psychological literature weakens the significance. A revised version addressing these issues—moving the Appendix, defining all variables in the main text, adding psychological context, and clarifying experimental details—would be suitable for NIPS. As submitted, the clarity deficits outweigh the theoretical novelty. The authors have a strong foundation but must prioritize communication to make their contribution visible to the community.
