## Review of “Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes”

This paper presents a novel computational model of visual attention, framing scanpath generation as a variational problem based on a Least Action Principle. The authors propose a Lagrangian incorporating terms for trajectory boundedness, curiosity (driven by image detail), and brightness invariance. By deriving Euler-Lagrange equations, they aim to model attentional dynamics in a biologically plausible and computationally tractable way. This approach differentiates itself from many current saliency models that focus on static saliency map generation, instead prioritizing the *process* of scanpath creation. The paper then validates the model on image and video datasets, comparing its performance to established benchmarks.

The core idea of utilizing a Least Action Principle to model scanpaths is intriguing and offers a potentially unifying framework. The inclusion of “affordances” like curiosity and brightness invariance is a commendable attempt to inject meaningful principles into the model.  However, the justification for these affordances feels somewhat *ad hoc*. While intuitively plausible, the paper lacks a deeper exploration of *why* these specific terms should govern attention, and how they relate to known neural mechanisms. The connection to the substantial body of work from the Wolpert lab and others on optimal control and the Least Action Principle in motor control (and, by extension, eye movements) is surprisingly weak; a more detailed discussion of these links would significantly strengthen the theoretical foundation.

Performance-wise, the results are mixed. While the model demonstrates reasonable performance, it generally lags behind deep learning-based saliency models, which is unsurprising given the current state-of-the-art. Crucially, the paper largely focuses on reporting aggregate statistics (like NSS and AUC) and lacks a detailed analysis of the generated scanpaths themselves. The most significant weakness is the absence of visualizations demonstrating the *dynamic* characteristics of the model's scanpaths – how they unfold over time, how they respond to different stimuli, etc. Showing representative examples of generated trajectories would greatly enhance understanding of the model’s behavior. 

The biological motivation, while acknowledged as limited, could be further bolstered. Though the authors position their work as less reliant on strict biological fidelity, grounding the model more firmly in neurophysiological findings would improve its appeal.  

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Interesting and novel approach to modeling scanpaths; potential for a unifying theoretical framework; provides a computational model of dynamic attention.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** Affordances lack strong justification; limited connection to relevant literature (e.g., motor control); performance lags behind benchmarks; inadequate demonstration of dynamic scanpath characteristics; limited biological grounding.

**Overall:** This is an enjoyable and thought-provoking paper with a promising core idea. However, it requires substantial revision to strengthen its theoretical motivation, empirical validation, and presentation of results before it could be considered a significant contribution to the field. I recommend rejection in its current form, with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the identified weaknesses.
