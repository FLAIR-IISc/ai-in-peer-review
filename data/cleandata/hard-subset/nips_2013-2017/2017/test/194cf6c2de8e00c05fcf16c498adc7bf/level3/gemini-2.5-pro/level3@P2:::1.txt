### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel model of bottom-up visual attention, framing the generation of attentional scanpaths as a variational problem. The core idea is to apply a generalized Least Action Principle, a concept from analytical mechanics, to define the trajectory of the focus of attention. The authors construct a Lagrangian function composed of kinetic energy and a generalized potential energy. This potential energy is ingeniously designed to incorporate three principles: a boundary constraint to keep the scanpath on the "retina," a "curiosity" principle that attracts attention to regions with high detail (image gradients) at both local and peripheral scales, and a "brightness invariance" principle (akin to the optical flow constraint) that encourages fixation and tracking. By finding the stationary point of the action integral, the authors derive the Euler-Lagrange equations of motion, which are a set of differential equations that govern the scanpath. Saliency maps, used for evaluation against standard benchmarks, are generated as a byproduct by aggregating the locations visited over many simulated scanpath trajectories. This approach differs significantly from classic feature-integration models (e.g., Itti et al.) and modern deep learning methods, which typically compute a static saliency map directly rather than modeling the underlying dynamics.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Quality**

The paper is technically sound in its mathematical development. The derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations from the proposed Lagrangian is detailed and appears correct. The work is presented as a complete system, from theoretical formulation to parameter estimation and experimental validation.

However, the quality of the experimental validation is a significant weakness. The central claim of the paper is a model of scanpath *dynamics*, yet the evaluation relies almost exclusively on static saliency map metrics (AUC, NSS, etc.). The results show that the model is competitive with some older methods but does not match the performance of more recent deep learning models, which is a crucial point that is not sufficiently discussed. More importantly, there is a mismatch between the paper's contribution (a dynamic model) and its evaluation (static metrics). The paper would be far more convincing if it included analyses of the generated scanpaths themselves—for example, comparing saccade amplitude distributions, fixation durations, or scanpath similarity scores with human data. Without this, the primary claim of modeling eye movement dynamics remains unsubstantiated.

#### **Clarity**

The paper is well-organized and generally easy to follow. The core concepts of the model are introduced systematically.

The main issue with clarity lies in the motivation of the model's core principles. The "curiosity" and "brightness invariance" terms, while functionally plausible, feel somewhat ad hoc. For instance, the specific form of the curiosity term `C(t, x) = b2x cos 2(ωt) + p2x sin 2(ωt)` with its oscillating weights is justified simply to "avoid trapping," which feels more like an engineering hack than a deeply principled component. The paper would benefit from a more thorough justification of these choices, perhaps grounded in cognitive or neuroscientific literature.

Furthermore, a major omission is the lack of visualizations of the model's primary output. The paper desperately needs figures in the main text showing example scanpaths generated by the model on both static images and videos. Such figures would provide crucial qualitative evidence for the model's claims of producing realistic saccades and tracking behavior.

#### **Originality**

The central idea of using a variational, Least Action Principle to generate attention scanpaths is highly original and provides a refreshing, physics-inspired perspective in a field increasingly dominated by deep learning. The formulation of a single Lagrangian that unifies saccadic exploration ("curiosity") and fixation/tracking ("brightness invariance") is an elegant and novel conceptual contribution.

However, the paper's originality is diminished by its failure to connect with highly relevant prior art. The idea of using optimal control and variational principles to model motor behavior, including saccadic eye movements, has been extensively explored for decades (e.g., the work of Harris & Wolpert on optimal feedback control in sensorimotor systems). By not citing or discussing this substantial body of literature, the authors miss an opportunity to properly situate their work and highlight its specific novelties within that broader context.

#### **Significance**

The paper has the potential for high significance by offering an interpretable, principled alternative to black-box models of attention. The theoretical framework is elegant and could inspire new ways of thinking about the underlying laws of attention.

Unfortunately, its current impact is limited by the weaknesses outlined above. Without a stronger theoretical and biological motivation for its core components, a more thorough comparison to relevant literature in motor control, and—most critically—a more compelling empirical demonstration of its ability to replicate human scanpath dynamics, it is unlikely that the specific model proposed will be widely adopted or built upon by other researchers. The performance on standard saliency benchmarks is not state-of-the-art, which limits its immediate practical utility.

### **Arguments for Acceptance**

*   Presents a highly novel and elegant theoretical framework for modeling attention dynamics based on a variational principle.
*   Offers a unified model for saccadic movements and object tracking within a single mathematical formulation.
*   The principled, physics-inspired approach is a valuable and thought-provoking alternative to purely data-driven methods.

### **Arguments for Rejection**

*   The core principles ("curiosity," "brightness invariance") are insufficiently motivated and appear ad hoc.
*   The paper fails to cite or relate its approach to the extensive literature on optimal control models of eye movements.
*   The experimental evaluation is weak and poorly matched to the paper's central claims. It focuses on static saliency maps while the contribution is a dynamic scanpath model, and provides no analysis of the generated dynamics.
*   The model's performance on standard benchmarks is not competitive with the state of the art.

In conclusion, this is an enjoyable paper with a genuinely interesting and original core idea. However, the work in its current form is not yet a complete scientific contribution. The motivation needs to be strengthened, the relationship to prior work in motor control must be established, and the experimental validation needs to be fundamentally re-oriented to actually test the model's claims about scanpath dynamics.
