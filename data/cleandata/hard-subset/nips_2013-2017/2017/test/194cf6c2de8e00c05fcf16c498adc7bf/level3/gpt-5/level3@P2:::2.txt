Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a variational, energy-based model of bottom-up visual attention that yields a dynamical system for the focus of attention (FOA) in static and dynamic scenes. The Lagrangian combines: (i) a boundary potential that confines the trajectory to the image “retina,” (ii) a curiosity term that alternates attraction to local vs. peripheral structure through time (via cos²/sin² weighting of gradient magnitudes of the raw and blurred images), and (iii) a brightness-invariance term akin to the optical flow brightness constancy constraint (Horn & Schunck, 1981) to promote fixation/tracking behavior. The stationary point of the action gives Euler–Lagrange equations for the FOA. Parameters are tuned via simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000 using NSS. Saliency maps are obtained a posteriori by counting pixel visitations over multiple simulated scanpaths. Experiments on MIT1003/CAT2000 (images) and SFU (videos) report performance comparable to several classical and recent baselines.

The work connects to classic saliency-map models (Koch & Ullman; Itti & Koch), machine learning approaches (e.g., Judd et al., CNN-based methods), and to scanpath simulation heuristics (winner-take-all with inhibition-of-return). It differs by deriving a principled dynamical law from a variational principle, unifying saccadic exploration and (putative) tracking within the same framework. Related cognitive/optimal control perspectives (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler on optimal eye movements for search; Le Meur & Liu’s saccade models) are relevant comparators that would help position this contribution.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a clear, physics-inspired variational formalization of FOA dynamics that yields interpretable forces and constraints (curiosity vs. brightness constancy vs. boundary).
- Unified treatment of static and dynamic scenes, with derivations for both exact and approximate brightness-invariance terms and a well-posedness condition (λ < m/4).
- Empirical results show competitive saliency performance on standard datasets despite a relatively simple, hand-crafted model.
- Saliency emerges as a byproduct of FOA dynamics rather than being explicitly optimized, which is scientifically appealing for bottom-up attention modeling.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- Clarity and writing: The paper is difficult to follow and needs careful proofreading. Many variables and functions are introduced without explicit definitions (e.g., ω, m, exact ranges for parameters, neighbor definition in SA, numerical integration details). The use of Iverson brackets in V(x) makes Vx piecewise; smooth approximations would aid analysis and numerics.
- Center bias and evaluation confounds: The method multiplies inputs by a center Gaussian and also initializes all runs near the image center; both induce strong center bias, which inflates AUC-Judd and NSS. sAUC and Information Gain against a center-prior baseline should be reported. Also report performance without the explicit center weighting and quantify the impact of initialization.
- Experimental transparency: Tables are referenced but numbers are not included here; the paper should include full metrics (AUC-Judd, sAUC, NSS, CC, KL, IG) with confidence intervals, compared to strong modern baselines (e.g., DeepGaze II/Salicon/MLNet). For videos, add standard dynamic saliency baselines and sAUC.
- Ablations/sensitivity: No ablation is reported for key design choices—e.g., removing curiosity, removing brightness invariance, varying ω, turning off the cos²/sin² alternation vs. static multi-scale weighting, eliminating the Gaussian center weighting, or varying the number/duration of simulated runs. Parameter sensitivity and robustness (ηb, ηp, λ, m) and the impact of the λ < m/4 constraint should be shown.
- Scanpath validity: Claims about saccade realism and tracking would be stronger with scanpath-level analyses: saccade amplitude/direction distributions, main sequence, fixation durations, MultiMatch/string-edit distance vs. humans, and quantitative tracking benchmarks rather than anecdotal examples.
- Optimization details: The SA procedure is under-specified (α, proposal distribution/step sizes, acceptance criterion P, domain D, stopping criterion); report the selected parameter values and cross-validate to reduce overfitting to CAT2000-TRAIN.
- Numerical details: The exact model requires higher-order derivatives (bxx, etc.); describe spatial derivative estimation (filters, boundary conditions), solver choice and tolerances, step sizes, and runtime (fps), and include a stability analysis.
- Positioning within cognitive literature: A deeper connection to optimal/active vision (Najemnik & Geisler 2005/2009), foveation/oculomotor constraints, and classic findings (e.g., Yarbus) would contextualize how the proposed “variational laws” relate to known behavioral regularities.

Quality
- Theoretical development is sound in spirit and produces explicit EL equations with a clear well-posedness condition. However, some mathematical choices (e.g., non-smooth boundary penalties; time-oscillating curiosity term) feel ad hoc and need justification and ablations. Experimental methodology raises concerns about center bias and insufficiently detailed reporting. The work appears reproducible in principle but needs more specifics and code/data to ensure replicability.

Clarity
- Currently below NeurIPS standards. The paper would benefit from: defining every symbol upon first use; consolidating notation; specifying all hyperparameters; improving figure/table self-containment; and moving the two-page Appendix to the supplement to allocate space for clear definitions and experiments.

Originality
- The variational, least-action framing for FOA is original in this exact form and unifies elements often treated separately. The brightness constancy integration into a Lagrangian with velocity-dependent “potential” is novel in this domain. Prior dynamic/saccadic models exist, but the specific formulation is new.

Significance
- Potentially significant as a mechanistic, interpretable alternative to black-box saliency predictors, with plausible extensions to active vision and control. Empirical performance is competitive, but broader impact depends on stronger validation (scanpaths, ablations), clearer comparisons to state-of-the-art deep models, and demonstrated utility beyond saliency maps.

Suggestions for improvement
- Improve exposition; define all variables; proofread thoroughly.
- Report sAUC and Information Gain, and include a strong center-prior baseline.
- Provide full ablations (center bias, curiosity alternation, brightness invariance, ω, parameter sensitivity) and scanpath analyses.
- Justify the cos²/sin² alternation; compare to static multi-scale aggregation.
- Smooth the boundary penalty to ensure C1 continuity; discuss implications for solvers.
- Detail SA and ODE integration settings; release code.
- Situate the work within optimal eye movement literature (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2005 Nature Neuroscience; 2009 J Vis) and discuss integration.

Questions for the authors
- What values/ranges were used for m, λ, ηb, ηp, and ω? How sensitive are results to these?
- How much of the reported performance is attributable to the explicit and implicit center bias?
- Do trajectories reproduce human saccade statistics and main sequence?
- How many runs/time are needed for saliency convergence? Why 199 runs for images?
- Runtime and solver details for videos?

Pros
- Conceptually novel, interpretable variational model.
- Unified treatment of static and dynamic attention.
- Competitive saliency performance without heavy learning.

Cons
- Writing/clarity issues; missing definitions.
- Potential evaluation confounds (center bias); limited metrics.
- Lack of ablations and scanpath-level validation.
- Under-specified optimization/numerical details.

Overall recommendation: Borderline (leaning weak accept if clarity, evaluation metrics, and ablations are addressed). Confidence: Medium.
