- Proposal: Introduces EYe MOvement Laws (EYMOL), a variational/Lagrangian model where scanpaths arise from "curiosity" and brightness invariance, with dynamics given by derived differential equations and parameters estimated via simulated annealing.  
- Novelty: Uses a physics-inspired least-action framework to model scanpath dynamics directly, rather than applying winner-take-all to precomputed saliency maps.  
- Unified framework: Claims to derive both saccadic eye movements and object tracking from the same principled model.  
- Mathematical rigor: Provides detailed derivation of Euler–Lagrange equations (appendix) demonstrating formal rigor.  
- Experimental validation: Evaluated quantitatively on established datasets (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, SFU) for static and dynamic saliency tasks.  
- Clear presentation: Paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  
- Limited biological motivation: The choice of "curiosity" and brightness invariance as affordances is under-justified and lacks strong neurophysiological grounding or connections to predictive/optimal control literature.  
- Insufficient scanpath analysis: Lacks visual and quantitative analyses of generated scanpaths (e.g., example scanpaths, speed profiles, human comparisons) to assess behavioral plausibility.  
- Performance vs SOTA: Model is competitive but generally underperforms current deep learning saliency models and doesn’t fully discuss trade-offs or advantages.  
- Parameter sensitivity: Reliance on simulated annealing raises concerns about sensitivity and generalizability; description of the search space and SA is brief.  
- Brightness invariance approximation: The static-image approximation (Eq. 6) needs clearer justification and discussion of its impact on results.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, mathematically grounded approach that broadens perspectives beyond data-driven methods and achieves reasonable saliency predictions.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Weak biological grounding, missing scanpath behavioral analysis, and inferior performance compared to state-of-the-art without sufficient discussion.  
- Recommendation/condition: Overall interesting and well-written; recommend weak accept contingent on addressing reviewer requests—add scanpath visualizations/analysis, cite motor control/optimal control literature, and discuss limitations relative to deep learning.
