This paper proposes a novel variational energy-based model for predicting human attention, framing scanpaths as solutions to a generalized least action principle. The model incorporates principles of bounded trajectories (harmonic oscillator constraint), curiosity-driven multi-scale brightness change, and brightness invariance. While the core idea – grounding attention in fundamental physical principles – is intriguing and potentially impactful, the presentation suffers significantly from a lack of clarity and mathematical rigor, hindering a full assessment of its merits.

The work builds upon decades of research in visual attention, beginning with early feature-integration theories (Koch & Ullman, Itti et al.) and progressing to more recent machine learning approaches (Judd et al., convolutional neural networks). A key distinction is the authors’ attempt to move beyond purely data-driven approaches and derive attention from first principles. This contrasts with most contemporary methods that focus on *predicting* saliency maps, rather than *modeling* the underlying scanpath dynamics as done here. The link to the concept of Least Action, borrowed from physics, provides a unique perspective. However, the paper doesn’t sufficiently connect to existing work in optimal eye movement control (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2008) which explores efficient information gathering, a closely related idea.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The variational approach to modeling eye movements is a genuinely novel contribution. It offers a compelling framework for understanding attention as an emergent property of underlying physical principles.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** Grounding the model in the Least Action Principle and attempting a formal derivation are admirable goals.
* **Potential Significance:** A successful model of this type could provide valuable insights for both vision and cognitive science, potentially informing areas like robotics and human-computer interaction.
* **Comparable Performance:** The quantitative results demonstrate performance on par with state-of-the-art saliency models, despite the model's fundamentally different approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Exposition:** The paper is extremely difficult to follow. The writing is often vague, and the mathematical derivations lack sufficient explanation. Critical terms and functions are introduced without definition, making it challenging to understand the underlying logic (particularly in Section 3 and the equations).
* **Mathematical Rigor:** While aiming for a formal approach, the mathematical development feels incomplete. The justification for certain choices (e.g., the form of the potential energy terms) is weak.
* **Lack of Detail:** The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail. The parameters used for numerical integration are not specified. The hyperparameter tuning process is briefly mentioned but lacks explanation.
* **Limited Connection to Psychological Literature:** The paper fails to adequately connect its findings to relevant psychological research on eye movement control and optimal foraging theory (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2008). This limits the interpretability and impact of the work.
* **Appendix Placement:** The two-page appendix contains crucial technical details that should be integrated into the main text for clarity.



**Specific Suggestions:**

* **Define All Variables and Functions:** A comprehensive glossary of all variables and functions is essential. This is particularly critical for equations (3), (4), (5), (6), (7), (9), (11), (13), (14), and the equations in the appendix.
* **Expand Mathematical Derivations:** Provide a more detailed and step-by-step derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations, explaining the rationale behind each step.
* **Move Appendix to Supplementary Material:** The technical details in the appendix are essential for understanding the model. Relocating it to supplementary material would free up space in the main text for clearer explanations and definitions.
* **Discuss Optimal Eye Movements:** Explicitly connect the results to psychological literature on optimal eye movements (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2008). Discuss how the model aligns with or deviates from established theories of efficient information gathering.
* **Improve Writing Clarity:**  Rewrite the paper with a focus on clarity and conciseness. Break down complex ideas into smaller, more manageable pieces.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The core idea is genuinely novel and potentially impactful.
* The model demonstrates comparable performance to state-of-the-art approaches.
* The work attempts a theoretically grounded approach, which is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper suffers from significant clarity issues, making it difficult to understand and evaluate.
* The mathematical derivations are incomplete and lack sufficient rigor.
* The lack of connection to relevant psychological literature limits the interpretability of the results.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially important idea, but its execution falls short. The lack of clarity and mathematical rigor significantly hinders a full assessment of its merits. While the performance is promising, the presentation needs substantial improvement before this work can be considered a significant contribution to the field. I recommend **rejection** in its current form, but encourage the authors to revise and resubmit, addressing the concerns outlined above. A major rewrite focusing on clarity, mathematical detail, and connection to existing literature is essential.
