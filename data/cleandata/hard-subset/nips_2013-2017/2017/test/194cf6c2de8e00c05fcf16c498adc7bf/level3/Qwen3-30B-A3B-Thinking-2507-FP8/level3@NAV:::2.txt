

This paper proposes a novel variational energy-based model for predicting eye movements in both static and dynamic scenes, grounded in a generalized Least Action Principle from physics. The authors derive a cost function and differential equations from three key principles: a harmonic-oscillator eye-movement constraint, curiosity-driven multi-scale brightness-change, and brightness-invariance term. Unlike most prior work that relies on centralized saliency maps (e.g., Itti & Koch, 1998), this model generates saliency maps as a byproduct of simulating scanpaths, offering a unified framework for both attention and eye movement modeling. The approach is evaluated on standard static image datasets (MIT1003, CAT2000) and a dynamic video dataset (SFU), showing competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a well-constructed theoretical framework and solid experimental validation. The variational approach represents a significant original contribution to the field, providing a formal mathematical foundation for visual attention that bridges cognitive science and computational modeling. The significance of this work is substantial, as it offers a potentially more biologically plausible mechanism for attentional processes compared to existing models.

However, the paper suffers from poor clarity that severely hinders understanding. Key variables and functions (especially in Section 3 and the equations) are undefined, making it difficult to follow the mathematical derivations. The two-page appendix should be moved to supplementary material to free up space in the main text for proper definitions and explanations. The writing is convoluted, with grammatical errors and unclear exposition that detract from the otherwise strong technical contribution. The authors should also explicitly connect their findings to psychological literature on optimal eye movements (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2008) and discuss how their model might integrate with existing cognitive theories.

While the technical contribution is strong and novel, the poor writing quality significantly undermines the paper's impact. With careful revision to improve clarity, define all variables, and better contextualize the work within psychological literature, this would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. As written, the clarity issues prevent the paper from reaching its full potential. I recommend rejection due to clarity issues, but with strong encouragement for resubmission after substantial revision.
