- Proposes a novel bottom-up variational model that generates scanpaths for static and dynamic scenes via a generalized Least Action Principle.  
- Models attention trajectory as a dynamical system by deriving Euler–Lagrange equations from a Lagrangian.  
- Introduces "affordances" in the Lagrangian: a curiosity term (attraction to high-gradient regions) and a brightness-invariance term (promotes fixation/tracking).  
- Produces saliency maps as a byproduct by accumulating fixation locations over time.  
- Strength: original, elegant, and principled framework that moves beyond static saliency prediction.  
- Strength: ambitious attempt to unify saccadic exploration and object tracking within one dynamical model.  
- Weakness: the defined "affordances" appear ad hoc and lack rigorous cognitive-science or information-theoretic motivation.  
- Weakness: fails to cite or connect to extensive prior work on optimal-control/minimum-cost models of eye movements (e.g., Wolpert, Simoncelli).  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation relies mainly on static saliency metrics (AUC, NSS) where performance is mixed and generally below deep benchmarks.  
- Weakness: crucially missing analyses and figures characterizing scanpath dynamics (saccade amplitude/direction distributions, comparison to human scanpath statistics).  
- Overall: promising and enjoyable idea but needs stronger motivation for core principles, better literature grounding, and direct empirical validation of dynamics.  
- For acceptance: novel variational scanpath framework and focus on attention dynamics.  
- Against acceptance: ad hoc principles, missing related-work engagement, and insufficient empirical support for dynamic claims.
