This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling eye movements, dubbed EYMOL (Eye Movement Laws), which differs significantly from current state-of-the-art models. The authors provide a clear and concise summary of the existing literature, correctly highlighting the limitations of saliency-map based models with fixation selection via optimization/maximization. In contrast, EYMOL models gaze trajectories directly using a "Least Action Principle," a generalized view of the principle in physics.

The mathematical formulation of the model is sound, and the authors provide a detailed derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations in the appendix. However, some of the mathematical details, particularly in the appendix, were beyond my physics background to fully evaluate. Nevertheless, I was able to grasp the key approach and how it differs from prior models.

The model is evaluated on real eye-movement datasets and compared to several alternative models. The results are favorable overall, with EYMOL performing well on various metrics, although not dominant on every metric. This suggests that the model has validity and can be a useful contribution to the field.

The paper is well-written, and the organization is clear. The authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results, and the experimental setup is well-described. The use of simulated annealing for parameter estimation is a good choice, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the algorithm.

One of the strengths of the paper is its originality. The proposed model offers a novel interpretation of curiosity-driven movements and brightness invariance term for fixation and tracking, which are regarded as mechanisms that jointly contribute to optimize the acquisition of visual information. The model's ability to capture mechanisms behind saccadic eye movements and object tracking within the same framework is also a significant contribution.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to inspire further empirical research and its merit for perception sciences. The model's simplicity and real-time performance make it a promising tool for various applications, including human-computer interaction, robotics, and virtual reality.

However, I am less convinced that the paper fits the NIPS significance criteria. While the model is novel and has potential applications, its impact on the broader machine learning community may be limited. Nevertheless, I defer to more expert reviewers in this regard.

In conclusion, the paper is a valuable contribution to the field of visual attention modeling, offering a novel and sound approach to modeling eye movements. Its originality, clarity, and potential significance make it a strong candidate for publication.
