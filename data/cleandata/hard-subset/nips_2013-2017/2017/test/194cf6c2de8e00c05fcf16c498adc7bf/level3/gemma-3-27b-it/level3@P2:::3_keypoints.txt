- Presents EYMOL, a novel computational model where eye movements follow a generalized Least Action Principle producing scanpaths via variational laws.  
- Defines potential energy from image details and brightness invariance and kinetic energy for movement, yielding Euler–Lagrange differential equations.  
- Provides an algorithm for parameter estimation and evaluates on standard saliency datasets (MIT300, CAT2000, SFU).  
- Positions work as a departure from saliency-map heuristics by directly modeling attention trajectories.  
- Good historical framing and motivation linking early fixations and foundational attention principles.  
- Novelty: applies a physics-inspired variational principle to model eye movements, offering a fresh perspective.  
- Strong theoretical foundation: clear mathematical formulation and derivation of attention dynamics.  
- Empirical validation: competitive performance on static and dynamic saliency benchmarks.  
- Unified framework: integrates curiosity-driven attraction to detail and brightness invariance into one model.  
- Clear presentation and organization, making core concepts and experiments understandable.  
- Mathematical complexity may limit accessibility for readers without calculus of variations or physics background.  
- Parameter sensitivity concerns: reliance on tuned parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) with limited robustness analysis.  
- Limited evaluation scope: focuses on fixation prediction/saliency maps rather than detailed scanpath statistics (e.g., saccade amplitudes, scanpath length).  
- Questionable fit for NIPS: relevance to core machine learning/neural information processing community may be indirect.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theory, solid empirical results, and potential to inspire further attention modeling research.  
- Arguments against acceptance: accessibility issues, limited NIPS-specific impact, and incomplete scanpath evaluation.  
- Overall assessment: conceptually interesting and well-executed work with significant strengths; recommended for acceptance (rating 6/7) with suggested improvements on accessibility and expanded evaluation.
