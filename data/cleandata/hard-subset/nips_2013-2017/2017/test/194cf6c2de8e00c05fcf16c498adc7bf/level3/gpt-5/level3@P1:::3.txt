Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces EYMOL, a dynamical model of visual attention that generates scanpaths as solutions of Euler–Lagrange equations derived from a generalized least action principle. The Lagrangian comprises: (i) a boundary potential to keep gaze within the retinal image, (ii) a “curiosity” potential that alternates sensitivity to high local gradients and their blurred (peripheral) counterpart via a time-varying modulation, and (iii) a brightness-invariance term that softly enforces the classical optical-flow constraint ḃ = 0, encouraging fixation on edges for static images and tracking in videos. The resulting second-order ODEs are integrated to produce trajectories; saliency maps are obtained as visits accumulated over time and random initializations. Parameters are estimated via simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a training subset.

Relative to prior work, this departs from the dominant saliency-map paradigm (e.g., Itti & Koch; Bruce & Tsotsos; Itti & Baldi; Judd et al.; deep CNN-based models) that compute a global saliency map and then rely on winner-take-all with inhibition-of-return to synthesize scanpaths. The authors instead model the generative physics of gaze motion directly, unifying saccades, fixation, and motion tracking under one variational principle. The brightness-invariance connection to Horn–Schunck optical flow is appropriate, and the inclusion of a time-modulated local vs. peripheral gradient field addresses attraction to detailed regions while avoiding local trapping. Experiments on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video show competitive performance on standard saliency metrics; two formulations of the brightness-invariance term (exact vs. upper-bound approximation) yield comparable results on static images.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The variational setup is coherent and the Euler–Lagrange derivations are carefully laid out in the appendix, including the velocity-dependent “potential,” resulting forces, and conditions for well-posedness (e.g., constraints on λ to keep the system stable). While some derivation details go beyond my physics background to fully verify, the formulation appears internally consistent and grounded in standard analytic mechanics and optical flow theory. Numerical integration choices (SciPy) and stability considerations are reasonable.
- Empirical support: The model is evaluated on widely used benchmarks with appropriate metrics (AUC-Judd, NSS, etc.), and compared against representative baselines including CNN-based methods. Results are favorable overall, though not uniformly dominant across all metrics/datasets. The equivalence of the exact vs. approximate brightness term on static images is a useful negative result that simplifies computation.
- Reproducibility: The paper describes parameter estimation via simulated annealing, the use of grayscale, Gaussian blur for a peripheral channel, and an explicit center bias. However, some implementation details are under-specified (e.g., precise parameter ranges/domains D, final selected parameter values and ω, initialization distributions, integration step size/tolerance, and runtime FPS), which could hinder exact reproduction.

Clarity:
The manuscript is generally well-written and organized. The main ideas are clearly introduced and connected to prior work. The appendix is dense but helpful; still, a brief intuitive summary of each force term’s qualitative effect (with vector field visualizations) in the main text would aid readers not fluent in variational calculus. A few copyediting issues remain (e.g., minor typos). The distinction between the two brightness-invariance variants (V1/V2) is clear, as is the protocol for converting trajectories to saliency maps.

Originality:
Modeling attention as least-action dynamics with a velocity-dependent brightness-invariance term is novel within the saliency literature, which typically uses static energy maps plus selection heuristics. The unification of saccades, fixation, and tracking in one principled dynamical system is original and conceptually appealing. The work is well-differentiated from previous saliency-map and saccade-synthesis approaches.

Significance:
The contribution is likely of interest to the vision science and computational neuroscience communities, offering a mechanistic, physics-inspired alternative to map-centric pipelines. For the NeurIPS audience, the significance is moderate: the theory is elegant and the empirical results competitive, but not state-of-the-art across the board. The primary value may be in stimulating further research on energy-based/dynamical formulations of attention and in providing a bridge between optical flow constraints and scanpath generation.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled formulation of gaze dynamics via a generalized least action.
- Unifies curiosity-driven exploration with fixation/tracking through brightness invariance.
- Generates scanpaths directly; saliency maps emerge as a byproduct.
- Competitive results on standard image and video benchmarks with simple inputs (grayscale).
- Useful analysis showing approximate brightness term suffices for static images.
- Real-time feasibility claimed and plausible given ODE integration.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Reliance on explicit center bias and post-hoc blur tuning can inflate certain metrics; please report metrics with and without these components to quantify their contribution.
- Limited ablation: quantify the effect of each term (remove curiosity, remove brightness invariance, vary ω) and provide sensitivity analyses for ηb, ηp, λ, m.
- Scanpath evaluation is missing: beyond heatmaps, compare generated trajectories to human scanpaths using established metrics (e.g., MultiMatch, string-edit distance, saccade amplitude and fixation duration distributions), and compare against winner-take-all scanpath baselines.
- Dynamic scenes: SFU is small (12 clips). Consider additional datasets or cross-validation, and evaluate tracking explicitly (e.g., object/feature-following success).
- Reproducibility: release code and report final parameter values, integration settings, and runtime; clarify the parameter domain D and selection of ω and temperature schedule α.
- Scope: Paper uses only grayscale; discuss extension to feature channels or learned features while retaining the variational framework.
- Clarify initialization scheme and number of runs per image; explore robustness to initial conditions.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Conceptually innovative, principled dynamical model that challenges the prevailing saliency-map paradigm.
- Solid math-meets-vision contribution with clear links to optical flow and variational mechanics.
- Competitive empirical performance with simple inputs; practical and interpretable.
- Likely to inspire follow-up work across CV, vision science, and computational neuroscience.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical gains are not consistently state-of-the-art; dynamic-scene evaluation is limited.
- Heavy reliance on center bias/blur tuning without thorough ablations may confound conclusions.
- Missing scanpath-level validation, which is central to the paper’s core claim (trajectory modeling).
- Some reproducibility and implementation details are underspecified.

Overall assessment:
A thoughtful and original contribution proposing variational laws for gaze dynamics, with promising results and a compelling unifying perspective. While I am not fully equipped to validate every mathematical detail, the approach appears sound. I see strong interest for the vision science community; for NeurIPS, I am positive but would welcome additional empirical rigor (ablations, scanpath metrics, broader video evaluation) to solidify the case.
