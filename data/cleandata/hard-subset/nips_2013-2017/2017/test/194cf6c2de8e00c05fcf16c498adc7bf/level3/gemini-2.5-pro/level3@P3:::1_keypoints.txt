- Proposes a novel bottom-up variational model of visual attention that frames scanpath generation as a Least Action Principle minimizing an action functional.
- Uses a Lagrangian with kinetic energy and a generalized potential combining curiosity (image gradients), brightness invariance (optical-flow-derived tracking), and a bounding potential.
- Derives scanpath dynamics via Euler–Lagrange equations and produces saliency maps by aggregating simulated fixation locations over time.
- Highly original and conceptually elegant, offering a principled, physics-inspired framework instead of heuristic rules.
- Provides a unified mathematical framework able to generate both saccade-like and smooth-pursuit-like behaviors.
- Strong emphasis on temporal dynamics of attention, addressing a gap where most work focuses on static spatial saliency.
- The curiosity and brightness-invariance terms lack rigorous motivation; specific mathematical forms (e.g., oscillating weights) feel ad hoc.
- Fails to situate the approach within existing variational/optimal-control motor-control literature (e.g., Harris & Wolpert, Todorov & Jordan), missing important theoretical grounding.
- Empirical evaluation neglects the paper’s core claim: no visualizations or quantitative analyses of generated scanpaths (saccade amplitudes, fixation durations, path similarity) vs human data.
- Evaluation focuses on saliency-as-byproduct and shows only modest benchmark performance, not competitive with contemporary deep learning methods.
- The claim of “best score in half of the cases” is based on a selective comparison set and is potentially misleading.
- Uses cognitive terminology like “curiosity” without linking to neurobiological oculomotor mechanisms, weakening biological plausibility.
- Overall: an interesting, ambitious idea with potential, but weakened by ad hoc model choices, missing literature connections, and insufficient empirical validation of scanpath dynamics.
