This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling eye movements, termed EYMOL, grounded in the principle of Least Action from physics. Current state-of-the-art models typically rely on saliency maps and optimization-based fixation selection, whereas EYMOL directly models gaze trajectories. The authors correctly summarize the existing landscape of visual attention modeling, as far as can be assessed by a non-expert. Their core idea – applying a variational principle to eye movement modeling – is intriguing and represents a significant departure from conventional approaches.

The strength of the paper lies in its originality. The application of the Least Action Principle to scanpath prediction is a genuinely new direction. While the mathematical details, particularly in the appendix, were challenging to fully evaluate given my background, the core approach and its distinction from prior models were clear. The paper’s attempt to unify curiosity-driven movements and brightness invariance within a single framework is particularly compelling. The evaluation on real eye-movement datasets (MIT300, CAT2000, SFU) and comparison with several alternative models demonstrate a conscientious effort to validate the model. Results are generally favorable, supporting the validity of the proposed approach, even if not consistently dominant across all metrics.

However, the paper does have weaknesses. The heavy reliance on mathematical formalism, while indicating a rigorous approach, hinders accessibility for readers less familiar with the underlying physics. The appendix, containing crucial derivations, is particularly dense. A more intuitive explanation of the key mathematical concepts would broaden the paper's impact. While the authors acknowledge potential limitations and perform a thorough empirical evaluation, the significance of the work within the NIPS community is debatable. The problem addressed is more central to vision science and cognitive neuroscience than to core NIPS topics. The improvement over existing methods, while present, isn’t consistently substantial enough to warrant a high NIPS rating. The parameters estimation using simulated annealing, while standard, could benefit from more detailed justification of the chosen parameter space and annealing schedule.

**Quality:** The paper appears technically sound, based on my understanding. The claims are supported by experimental results, although a deeper dive into the mathematical details would be needed for a definitive assessment. The work seems complete, providing a fully functional model and comprehensive evaluation. The authors are careful to present both the strengths and limitations of their approach.

**Clarity:** The paper's clarity is hampered by the mathematical complexity. While the core ideas are articulated reasonably well, the detailed derivations are difficult to follow without a strong physics background. Improved explanations and perhaps visual representations of the mathematical concepts would enhance readability. The organization is logical, and the paper is well-structured.

**Originality:** The originality of the approach is the paper's strongest suit. The application of the Least Action Principle to eye movement modeling is a novel contribution. The authors adequately reference related work, demonstrating an awareness of the existing literature.

**Significance:** The significance of the work is less clear-cut. While the model offers a potentially valuable new perspective on visual attention, its immediate impact on core NIPS research areas seems limited. However, the paper's potential to inspire further empirical research and its relevance to perception sciences are substantial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and original approach to modeling eye movements.
*   Strong theoretical foundation based on the Least Action Principle.
*   Comprehensive evaluation on multiple datasets.
*   Favorable results, supporting the model's validity.
*   Potential to inspire further research in vision science and computational neuroscience.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Mathematical complexity may limit accessibility.
*   Significance to the NIPS community is questionable.
*   Improvements over existing methods are not consistently dominant.
*   Lack of detailed justification for parameter selection in simulated annealing.

**Overall:**

I believe this paper presents a valuable and innovative contribution, particularly to the field of vision science. While its significance to NIPS may be limited, its originality and potential for inspiring future research are noteworthy. I recommend acceptance, but defer to more expert reviewers for a definitive judgment on the technical soundness of the mathematical derivations. The paper would benefit from a more accessible presentation of its mathematical concepts and a clearer articulation of its potential impact on core NIPS research areas.
