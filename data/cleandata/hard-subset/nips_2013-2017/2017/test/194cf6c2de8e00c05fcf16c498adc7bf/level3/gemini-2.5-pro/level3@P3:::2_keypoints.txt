- Proposes a novel variational formulation of scanpath generation, framing eye movements as trajectories that minimize a generalized action functional.  
- Constructs a Lagrangian from three principles: harmonic oscillator potential (image-bound constraint), curiosity-driven potential (multi-scale high-gradient attraction), and brightness invariance (encourages contour fixation and object tracking).  
- Derives Euler–Lagrange equations yielding second-order differential equations that directly govern focus-of-attention motion; saliency maps obtained by aggregating fixations.  
- Evaluated on static (MIT300, CAT2000) and dynamic (SFU) datasets, achieving competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods.  
- Highly original and significant: elegant, principled alternative to feature-map + winner-take-all paradigms with potential impact on vision and cognitive science.  
- Theoretical soundness: mathematically grounded derivation linking energy terms to exploration, fixation, and tracking behaviors.  
- Empirical validation: simple, principled model performs comparably to complex/data-driven approaches on benchmarks.  
- Major clarity/exposition problems: paper is difficult to read with many grammatical and presentation issues.  
- Critical notation issues: many variables and derivatives (e.g., b_x, p_x, b_tt, ḃ, b̈, etc.) are used without explicit definitions, impeding reproducibility.  
- Appendix is too dense: core derivations presented as compact equations making verification and understanding difficult.  
- Strong recommendation to move detailed derivations to supplementary material and expand Section 2 to clearly define all notation and provide step-by-step derivations.  
- Lacks sufficient connection to cognitive science literature on optimal eye movements; should discuss links to information-theoretic optimal search (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler) and interpret curiosity/invariance as possible proxies for information maximization.  
- Pros: novel variational framework, unified model for saccades and tracking, competitive benchmark performance, high interdisciplinary impact potential.  
- Cons: poor writing and mathematical exposition, undefined notation, insufficient grounding in existing cognitive-science theories.  
- Recommendation: reject in current form but encourage major revision focusing on clarity, full definition of variables, expanded derivations, and stronger links to related cognitive-science work—resubmission would be promising.
