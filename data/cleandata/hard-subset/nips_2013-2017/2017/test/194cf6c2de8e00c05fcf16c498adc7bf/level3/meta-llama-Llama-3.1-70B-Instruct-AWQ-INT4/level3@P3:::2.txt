This paper proposes a novel paradigm for modeling visual attention based on a few unifying functional principles. The authors introduce a variational approach, called EYe MOvement Laws (EYMOL), which relies on a generalized view of the Least Action Principle in physics. The model is built from three principles: harmonic-oscillator eye-movement constraint, curiosity-driven multi-scale brightness-change, and brightness-invariance term. The authors derive a cost function and differential equations to predict eye movements from initial position and velocity.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear overview of the state-of-the-art in visual attention modeling. The introduction effectively sets the stage for the proposed model, highlighting the importance of understanding visual attention mechanisms. The mathematical description of the model is thorough, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the underlying principles.

The experimental results are promising, with the model performing comparably to state-of-the-art models on static and dynamic eye-tracking datasets. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the model's performance, discussing the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.

However, the paper suffers from poor writing and organization. The text is often dense and difficult to follow, with many sentences being overly long and convoluted. The authors could benefit from breaking up the text into smaller paragraphs and using clearer headings to guide the reader through the paper.

Additionally, the authors could provide more context and explanation for the mathematical derivations. While the equations are presented clearly, the underlying assumptions and justifications are not always explicit. This makes it difficult for non-experts to follow the mathematical arguments.

Furthermore, the authors could benefit from providing more detail on the parameter estimation process. The use of simulated annealing is mentioned briefly, but the authors do not provide a clear explanation of how the parameters were chosen or optimized.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to visual attention modeling. The use of a variational approach and the incorporation of brightness-invariance term are new and interesting contributions to the field.

However, the paper could benefit from more discussion of related work and how the proposed model differs from existing approaches. The authors mention a few relevant papers, but a more thorough review of the literature would help to situate the paper in the context of the field.

Overall, the paper presents a promising new approach to visual attention modeling. However, the writing and organization could be improved to make the paper more accessible to a wider audience.
