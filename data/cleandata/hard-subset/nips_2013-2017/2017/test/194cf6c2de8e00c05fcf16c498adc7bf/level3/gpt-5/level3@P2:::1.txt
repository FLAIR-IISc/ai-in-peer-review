Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a bottom-up dynamical systems model of overt visual attention in which scanpaths arise as stationary points of a variational principle. The Lagrangian includes: (i) a boundary potential that confines the “eye” within the retinal frame; (ii) a “curiosity” potential that attracts the trajectory toward high spatial detail, modeled as a mixture of local (image gradient magnitude) and peripheral (blurred) gradients modulated by cos2/sin2 in time; and (iii) a brightness-invariance term corresponding to the optical-flow brightness constancy residual, encouraging fixation or motion tracking. The Euler–Lagrange equations yield a second-order ODE with velocity-dependent terms. The authors integrate these equations from near-center initial conditions to produce scanpaths and, by counting visits, derive saliency maps. Parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) are tuned by simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a subset of CAT2000. Results on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU videos are reported against several classical and a few learning-based baselines.

The variational framing is interesting and relates to longstanding ideas in optimal control and physics-inspired formulations of sensorimotor behavior (e.g., Wolpert & Ghahramani; Todorov & Jordan’s optimal feedback control), and to variational/free-energy principles in active vision (Friston). For scanpath generation, there is also relevant work on dynamical field/oculomotor models (e.g., Engbert’s SceneWalk, Le Meur’s saccadic models), ideal-observer accounts (Najemnik & Geisler), and smooth pursuit (Lisberger). The paper would benefit from explicitly positioning itself with respect to these.

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing, interpretable dynamical formulation that unifies saccade-like exploration (curiosity) and fixation/pursuit (brightness invariance) in one Lagrangian.
- Clear derivations (with an appendix) culminating in solvable ODEs; the model is simple and fast to simulate.
- One framework applied to both static images and dynamic scenes; reasonable performance for a non-deep, bottom-up approach.
- Reports on standard datasets and metrics, and explores an exact vs approximated brightness-invariance term.

Weaknesses
- The “affordances” are ad hoc: the cos2/sin2 alternation and choice of ω, the specific mixture of raw and blurred gradients, and the boundary potential lack principled justification or empirical grounding. No ablations quantify their necessity.
- Limited biological plausibility: no constraints from oculomotor physiology (main sequence, refractory periods, motor noise, latencies), no inhibition of return, and no validation against scanpath statistics (e.g., saccade amplitude distributions, MultiMatch/ScanMatch).
- Empirical evaluation is focused on static saliency metrics; dynamic/scanpath behavior—central to the contribution—is not visualized or quantitatively analyzed in the main paper.
- Comparisons omit the strongest contemporary deep saliency models (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON, ML-Net/DeepFix, SAM-ResNet). On current benchmarks, the proposed model is generally below SOTA deep methods.
- Center bias and blur are explicitly injected and tuned to maximize AUC/NSS, which can inflate scores; fairness vs baselines and cross-dataset generalization are unclear.
- Technical clarity issues: the kinetic energy term appears nonstandard (K is typically (m/2)||ẋ||2, here written as (1/m)||ẋ||2). The well-posedness condition on λ is confusing (Eq. 40–47). The boundary potential uses indicator functions, making forces nondifferentiable at image borders.

Quality
- Soundness: The derivations are largely correct, and the ODE is implementable. However, some formulae (kinetic energy, constraints on λ) and the use of Iverson brackets merit correction or justification. Claims about tracking would be stronger with quantitative pursuit metrics.
- Empirics: Reported scores are mixed; competitive with older baselines but generally below modern deep models. Lack of scanpath validation weakens the central claim about dynamics.

Clarity
- Generally well organized, with detailed appendix. Improve notation consistency, fix the kinetic energy expression, and clarify all hyperparameters (ω, mass m, step sizes). Include main-paper figures illustrating representative scanpaths on static and dynamic scenes, and energy term contributions over time.

Originality
- Applying a least-action formulation to derive attention dynamics is novel within saliency modeling, though related to established optimal control/variational perspectives in motor control and active vision. The specific curiosity/peripheral alternation is new but appears heuristic.

Significance
- As a path toward interpretable, physics-inspired attention dynamics, this is promising. However, without stronger empirical validation of scanpaths and competitive saliency accuracy, immediate impact is limited relative to current SOTA.

Specific suggestions
- Positioning: Discuss connections and differences to optimal control (Todorov & Jordan), active inference (Friston), oculomotor/scanpath models (Engbert/SceneWalk, Le Meur), and smooth pursuit literature.
- Ablations: Quantify the effect of each term (remove curiosity, remove brightness invariance, remove alternation, vary ω) on both saliency metrics and scanpath statistics.
- Scanpath evaluation: Report MultiMatch/ScanMatch, saccade amplitude/velocity distributions, fixation durations, sequence similarity to humans; show qualitative scanpath overlays and trajectory curvature.
- Fair comparisons: Use shuffled AUC, IG, and sAUC to control center bias; standardize center bias across models; add stronger deep baselines; report runtime.
- Modeling refinements: Correct kinetic energy; smooth boundary forces; learn ω and weights from data; consider scale weighting via a contrast sensitivity function instead of ad hoc alternation; incorporate simple IOR and saccade constraints.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, interpretable variational formulation; unifies fixation and tracking in one dynamical law.
- Fast, simple, and potentially useful as a generative scanpath baseline.
- Cross-domain applicability (images and videos) with one model.

Cons for acceptance
- Heuristic design choices insufficiently justified; limited biological grounding.
- Missing key baselines; performance generally below SOTA deep models.
- Central claim about scanpath dynamics not empirically demonstrated with appropriate metrics and figures.
- Some technical ambiguities (energy terms, parameter constraints).

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. Interesting and enjoyable ideas with a clear derivation, but the paper needs stronger motivation, tighter connections to prior dynamical/optimal-control work, and substantially more empirical evidence (especially on scanpath dynamics) to support its claims.

Confidence
High on evaluation of empirical/methodological gaps; medium on the completeness of related-work coverage in oculomotor modeling.
