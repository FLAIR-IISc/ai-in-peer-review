- Novel application of the Least Action Principle to model visual attention, deriving eye-movement dynamics via variational principles.
- Unified mathematical framework integrating curiosity-driven exploration and brightness invariance, claimed to align with biological plausibility.
- Generates scanpaths in real time, which is practically useful for robotics and HCI.
- Achieves competitive saliency detection results on some benchmarks despite model simplicity.

- Selection and mathematical formulation of affordances (curiosity, brightness invariance) appear ad hoc and lack deeper theoretical or empirical justification.
- Limited biological validation: no sufficient visualizations or analyses comparing generated scanpaths to human data beyond static saliency metrics.
- Poorer connection to prior literature on eye movement dynamics (e.g., Wolpert lab), weakening theoretical grounding and context.
- Mixed empirical performance, underperforming deep learning baselines on several datasets and raising questions about practical utility.
- Dense and potentially inaccessible mathematical derivations; key equations should be simplified or summarized for broader readership.

- Recommend acceptance only if authors: strengthen theoretical motivation for affordances.
- Require inclusion of visualizations/analyses of generated scanpaths to validate biological plausibility.
- Require fuller discussion and connection to prior work on eye movement dynamics.

- Arguments for acceptance: novel LAP application, competitive results on some benchmarks, and potential to inspire interdisciplinary work.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited theoretical/empirical justification for key components, mixed benchmark performance, and insufficient linkage to prior work and biological validation.

- Overall: creative and original contribution that needs stronger theoretical grounding, biological validation, and literature connections to increase impact.
