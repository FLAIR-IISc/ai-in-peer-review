- Proposal: Introduces EYMOL, a physics-inspired variational model that generates continuous-time gaze trajectories via Euler–Lagrange equations from a least-action Lagrangian.  
- Lagrangian terms: Uses boundary potential (retina constraint), curiosity term (attracts high-gradient regions with local/peripheral alternation), and a velocity-dependent brightness-invariance term (fixation/tracking).  
- Output: Produces scanpaths directly and derives saliency maps by aggregating spatial visitations across runs.  
- Relation to prior work: Differs from classical saliency pipelines and CNN-based models by deriving dynamics from physical principles rather than optimizing saliency maps or fixation policies.  
- Technical soundness: Derivation is careful and well-posed (e.g., λ < m/4); computational implementation and simulated annealing for parameter search are reasonable.  
- Empirical support: Competitive results on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video, with approximate and exact formulations performing similarly on static images.  
- Evaluation concerns: Use of explicit center bias, near-center initialization, and blur tuning may inflate metrics and obscure the model’s true contribution.  
- Missing analyses: Lacks statistical significance tests and necessary ablations (e.g., remove center bias, isolate curiosity vs brightness-invariance).  
- Reproducibility: Integration is standard but paper should report exact parameters, SA domains, blur/kernel sizes, center-bias σ, runtimes, and step-sizes; code release recommended.  
- Clarity: Generally well written, but should move more intuition for each term into main text and clarify separation of dataset-driven biases from model dynamics.  
- Numerical details: Needs clearer reporting of numerical stability choices, boundary handling, and sensitivity to parameter changes.  
- Originality: Novel and thought-provoking framing of attention/scanpaths as a single least-action dynamical principle with a velocity-dependent term.  
- Significance: Conceptually appealing and potentially influential for theory, but moderate significance for NIPS due to not being state-of-the-art empirically.  
- Pros: Elegant unified variational formulation, interpretable parameters, real-time feasibility, and competitive performance; saliency maps emerge naturally.  
- Cons: Confounding center-bias effects, limited comparison to top deep models and robust metrics, no scanpath-level behavioral validation, and some math/numerical details may be hard for general readers.  
- Requested additions: Report exact parameters and ranges, provide ablations (center prior/init, term-wise removals, fixed vs alternating), include shuffled AUC/Information Gain, compare to DeepGaze II, add scanpath analyses, and show robustness/failure cases.  
- Overall recommendation: Borderline — conceptually strong and interesting but needs additional ablations, stronger baselines, and scanpath validation; reviewer leans slightly against acceptance but would not object if accepted.
