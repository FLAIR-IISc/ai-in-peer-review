This paper proposes a novel model of visual attention, termed EYe MOvement Laws (EYMOL), grounded in a variational approach inspired by the Least Action Principle in physics. The central idea is that scanpaths emerge from foundational principles – a drive for detail (“curiosity”) and brightness invariance – formalized within a Lagrangian framework. The resulting differential equations define the dynamics of attention, and the authors present a simulated annealing method for parameter estimation, validating the model on static and dynamic scene saliency detection tasks.

The work builds upon decades of research in visual attention, initially stemming from feature integration theory [14, 9, 10]. While early models primarily focused on bottom-up saliency map generation, this paper distinguishes itself by directly modeling scanpath dynamics rather than relying on a winner-take-all strategy applied *to* a pre-computed saliency map [9]. The attempt to frame attention as a physics-inspired dynamical system is a departure from contemporary approaches dominated by deep learning [26, 18], and evokes related work in motor control and optimal control of movement [20]. However, unlike models rooted in predictive coding or efficient coding, the theoretical motivation for the specific "affordances" (curiosity and brightness invariance) isn’t strongly articulated. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The application of a variational principle to model scanpath dynamics offers a unique perspective within the field. It's a bold attempt to move beyond purely data-driven saliency prediction and formulate attention as a fundamental process.
* **Unified Framework:** The model provides a potentially unifying framework for understanding both saccadic eye movements and object tracking, deriving them from a shared set of principles.
* **Mathematical Rigor:** The derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations, while detailed and relegated to the appendix, demonstrates a commitment to mathematical precision.
* **Experimental Validation:** The authors conduct experiments on several established datasets (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, SFU), providing a quantitative assessment of the model's performance.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow from introduction to experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Biological Motivation:**  While the authors acknowledge the interplay between bottom-up and top-down processes [2, 3, 4], the choice of "curiosity" and brightness invariance as the driving principles feels somewhat ad hoc. A more compelling connection to known neurophysiological or behavioral observations would strengthen the argument. Relevant work by Wolpert and colleagues on the brain as a prediction machine and minimizing energy expenditure in motor control [e.g., see reviews on optimal control in movement] is conspicuously absent and could offer a valuable grounding.
* **Insufficient Scanpath Analysis:** The primary results are presented in terms of saliency maps and quantitative metrics (AUC, NSS). The paper lacks a visual or quantitative analysis of the *generated scanpaths themselves*.  Figures illustrating typical scanpaths, their speed profiles, or comparison to human scanpaths would be invaluable for assessing the model’s behavioral plausibility. 
* **Performance Compared to State-of-the-Art:**  While the model achieves competitive results, its performance is generally worse than current deep learning-based saliency models [26, 18]. This isn't necessarily a fatal flaw, given the different underlying philosophy, but the authors should more thoroughly discuss the trade-offs and potential advantages of their approach.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The reliance on simulated annealing for parameter estimation raises concerns about sensitivity and generalizability. The description of the search space (domain D) and the SA algorithm itself is somewhat brief.
* **Brightness Invariance Approximation:** The use of an approximated form of the brightness invariance term (equation 6) for static images should be more clearly justified, and the impact of this approximation on the results should be discussed in detail.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and mathematically grounded approach to modeling visual attention, offering a unique perspective compared to dominant deep learning methods.
* The experimental results demonstrate that the model is capable of generating reasonable saliency predictions, even if it doesn't outperform state-of-the-art techniques.
* The work contributes to the ongoing effort to understand the underlying principles governing visual attention and scanpath dynamics.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The biological motivation for the chosen affordances is weak, and the connection to existing neurophysiological findings is limited.
* The lack of detailed scanpath analysis hinders a comprehensive evaluation of the model's behavioral plausibility.
* The performance falls short of current state-of-the-art methods, and the paper doesn’t sufficiently address this gap.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is an interesting and well-written paper that proposes a novel approach to modeling visual attention. While the biological motivation could be strengthened and the empirical evaluation broadened, the work represents a valuable contribution to the field.  I recommend **weak accept** contingent upon the authors addressing the reviewers’ comments, particularly including visualizations and analysis of the generated scanpaths to support their claims about the model’s dynamic behavior and including relevant citations from the motor control and optimal control literature.  Adding a discussion of the limitations relative to modern deep learning approaches would also be beneficial.
