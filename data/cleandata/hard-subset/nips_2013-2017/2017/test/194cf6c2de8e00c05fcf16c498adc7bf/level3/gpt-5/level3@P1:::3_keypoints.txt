- Introduces EYMOL, a dynamical model generating scanpaths via Euler–Lagrange equations from a generalized least-action principle with boundary, curiosity, and brightness-invariance potentials.  
- Produces trajectories by integrating second-order ODEs; saliency maps formed from accumulated visits and random initializations; parameters learned via simulated annealing maximizing NSS.  
- Departures from prior work: models gaze dynamics directly rather than relying on global saliency maps plus winner-take-all/inhibition-of-return heuristics.  
- Brightness-invariance term appropriately connects to Horn–Schunck optical flow; time-modulated local vs. peripheral gradients help avoid local trapping.  
- Evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video with competitive but not uniformly dominant performance; exact vs. approximate brightness terms similar on static images.  
- Variational formulation and Euler–Lagrange derivations are coherent and grounded in analytic mechanics; stability constraints (e.g., on λ) are discussed.  
- Numerical integration choices (SciPy) and stability considerations are reasonable, though some derivation details exceed the reviewer’s physics verification.  
- Empirical support uses standard metrics (AUC-Judd, NSS) and CNN baselines; results are favorable overall but not state-of-the-art across the board.  
- Reproducibility concerns: under-specified parameter ranges/domains, final parameter values, ω, initialization distributions, integration step/tolerance, and runtime FPS.  
- Manuscript clarity: generally well-written and organized; appendix is dense but helpful; would benefit from intuitive summaries/vector-field visualizations of forces.  
- Originality: novel framing of attention as least-action dynamics with a velocity-dependent brightness-invariance term; unifies saccades, fixations, and tracking.  
- Significance: likely of interest to vision science and computational neuroscience; moderate significance for NeurIPS because empirical gains are mixed.  
- Strengths: principled, interpretable formulation; generates scanpaths directly; competitive performance with simple grayscale inputs; approximate brightness term suffices for images; plausible real-time feasibility.  
- Weakness: reliance on explicit center bias and post-hoc blur tuning could inflate metrics; metrics should be reported with/without these components.  
- Weakness: limited ablation and sensitivity analyses—need removal tests for curiosity/brightness terms and parameter sweeps for ηb, ηp, λ, m, ω.  
- Weakness: missing scanpath-level evaluation (e.g., MultiMatch, string-edit, saccade amplitude, fixation duration distributions) and comparisons to winner-take-all scanpath baselines.  
- Weakness: dynamic-scene evaluation limited (SFU is small); recommend more video datasets and explicit tracking success metrics.  
- Weakness: reproducibility gaps—release code, report final parameter values, integration settings, runtime, domain D, and annealing schedule α.  
- Weakness: scope limited to grayscale; discuss extensions to feature channels or learned features within the variational framework.  
- Suggestion: clarify initialization scheme, number of runs per image, and robustness to initial conditions.  
- Argument for acceptance: conceptually innovative, mathematically principled, interpretable, and likely to inspire follow-up work.  
- Argument against acceptance: empirical gains not consistently state-of-the-art, reliance on tuned components without ablations, missing scanpath validation, and underspecified reproducibility.  
- Overall: thoughtful and original contribution with promising results, but needs stronger empirical rigor (ablations, scanpath metrics, broader video evaluation) to fully justify acceptance.
