- Proposes a novel variational energy-based model framing scanpaths via a generalized Least Action principle.  
- Model components include bounded trajectories (harmonic oscillator), curiosity-driven multi-scale brightness change, and brightness invariance.  
- Core idea is intriguing and potentially impactful but presentation prevents full assessment due to poor clarity and rigor.  
- Attempts to move beyond data-driven saliency prediction to principled modeling of scanpath dynamics.  
- Insufficient engagement with related work on optimal eye-movement control (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2008).  
- Strength: Novel variational approach offers a new framework for understanding attention.  
- Strength: Theoretical grounding in the Least Action Principle is a notable contribution.  
- Strength: Potential significance for vision science, cognitive science, robotics, and HCI.  
- Strength: Quantitative performance is comparable to state-of-the-art saliency models.  
- Weakness: Paper is hard to follow; writing is vague and introduces terms/functions without definition.  
- Weakness: Mathematical development feels incomplete; justifications for potential energy forms are weak.  
- Weakness: Experimental methodology lacks detail (numerical integration parameters, hyperparameter tuning).  
- Weakness: Limited connection to psychological theories like optimal foraging and eye-movement control.  
- Weakness: Crucial technical details relegated to a short appendix instead of main text or full supplementary material.  
- Suggestion: Provide a comprehensive glossary defining all variables and functions used in key equations.  
- Suggestion: Expand and clarify mathematical derivations, including step-by-step Euler–Lagrange development.  
- Suggestion: Move essential appendix material to supplementary and incorporate necessary details in the main paper.  
- Suggestion: Explicitly discuss and compare the model to literature on optimal eye movements (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler).  
- Suggestion: Rewrite for clarity and conciseness, breaking complex ideas into smaller steps.  
- For acceptance: Novelty, theoretical ambition, and competitive performance argue in favor.  
- Against acceptance: Major clarity, rigor, and literature-connection deficiencies argue against acceptance.  
- Overall: Interesting and promising idea but currently undermined by poor exposition and incomplete mathematics; recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit.
