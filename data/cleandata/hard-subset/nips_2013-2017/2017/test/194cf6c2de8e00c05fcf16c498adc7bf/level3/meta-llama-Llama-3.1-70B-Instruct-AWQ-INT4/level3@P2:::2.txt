The paper "Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes" proposes a novel model of visual attention based on the principle that there are foundational laws that drive the emergence of visual attention. The authors devise variational laws of eye-movement that rely on a generalized view of the Least Action Principle in physics. The model is built from three fundamental principles: boundedness of the trajectory, curiosity-driven principle, and brightness invariance term. These principles lead to the introduction of the corresponding terms of the Lagrangian of the action, which is used to derive differential equations of visual attention.

The proposed model is related to previous work in the field of visual attention, including the feature integration theory of attention and the saliency map approach. However, the authors' approach is distinct in that it provides a unified interpretation of curiosity-driven movements and the brightness invariance term for fixation and tracking. The model is also novel in that it uses a variational approach based on a generalization of least action, which is not commonly used in the field of visual attention.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their model and its underlying principles. However, the writing is sometimes unclear, and the authors could benefit from proofreading and editing to improve the clarity and readability of the text. Additionally, some of the variables and functions used in the equations are not explicitly defined, which can make it difficult for readers to follow the mathematical derivations.

The experimental results presented in the paper are promising, showing that the model performs comparably to state-of-the-art models on both static and dynamic eye-tracking datasets. However, the authors could provide more details about the experimental setup and the evaluation metrics used to compare the performance of their model to other models.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to modeling visual attention, and the authors' use of a variational approach based on a generalization of least action is a significant contribution to the field.
