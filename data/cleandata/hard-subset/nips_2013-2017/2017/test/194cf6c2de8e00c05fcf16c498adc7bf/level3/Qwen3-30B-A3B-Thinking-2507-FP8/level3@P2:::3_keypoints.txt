- Proposes EYMOL: a novel framework modeling visual attention as dynamic scanpaths from variational principles (Least Action), producing saliency maps as a byproduct.  
- Unifies two driving principles—curiosity (attraction to high-gradient regions with peripheral modulation) and brightness invariance—via a Lagrangian with potential, curiosity, and invariance terms.  
- Derives Euler-Lagrange differential equations that produce real-time scanpath dynamics.  
- Evaluated on standard datasets (MIT1003/MIT300, CAT2000, SFU) and compares favorably to five SOTA methods on about half the metrics.  
- Strength — originality: first to derive gaze dynamics from variational principles, offering a fresh alternative to saliency-map-first approaches.  
- Strength — quality: thorough experiments on static and dynamic scenes, rigorous justification of brightness invariance, and sensible parameter tuning (simulated annealing).  
- Strength — clarity: well-structured exposition and compelling argument for scanpath-first modeling, though appendix is physics-heavy.  
- Weakness — ML significance: limited demonstrated impact on machine learning methods; claims about integration with variational learning are speculative.  
- Weakness — accessibility: Euler-Lagrange derivation assumes familiarity with analytical mechanics and needs a simpler intuition for a broad ML audience.  
- Weakness — comparison analysis: quantitative metrics reported, but lacks qualitative explanation for when/how EYMOL outperforms specific prior models.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify ML contributions and simplify Appendix exposition.  
- Overall: high-quality, theoretically grounded contribution more directly valuable to vision science than core ML; suitable as a weak accept for NIPS but a stronger fit for venues like Neural Computation or Journal of Vision.
