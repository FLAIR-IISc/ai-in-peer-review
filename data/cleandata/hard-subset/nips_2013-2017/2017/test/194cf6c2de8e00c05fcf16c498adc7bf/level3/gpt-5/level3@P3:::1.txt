Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a bottom-up dynamical-systems model for visual attention that treats the focus of attention as a particle following Euler–Lagrange equations derived from a generalized Least Action Principle. The Lagrangian includes: (i) a boundary potential to keep the trajectory within the image; (ii) a “curiosity” potential that alternates between local detail (brightness gradient magnitude) and a peripheral/blurred gradient via cos2/sin2 modulation; and (iii) a brightness invariance term (akin to the optical-flow constraint) encouraging ḃ ≈ 0, intended to produce fixation (static images) and tracking (videos). The resulting second-order ODEs are integrated to generate scanpaths; saliency maps are obtained as visitation heatmaps over many runs. Parameters are fitted via simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000, with additional post-hoc blur and center-bias optimization for some metrics. Experiments on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU compare to classic and deep saliency baselines.

Relative to classic saliency-map frameworks (e.g., Itti & Koch) and machine learning/deep approaches (e.g., Judd et al., SALICON, LSTM-based models), this work shifts focus from static saliency maps to scanpath dynamics. There is related dynamical work in the eye-movement literature (e.g., SceneWalk/Engbert et al.; Le Meur & Liu; optimal control and minimum-jerk/variance work from the Wolpert lab and Todorov & Jordan), and the paper’s variational derivation offers a fresh angle that could unify saccades and tracking within one formalism.

Strengths
- Novel framing: A Lagrangian/Least-Action perspective on scanpath generation is original for the NIPS saliency space and could stimulate cross-talk with control theory.
- Unified dynamics: One set of equations aims to produce saccades, fixation, and tracking behavior; saliency maps arise as a byproduct.
- Clear math in the appendix: The Euler–Lagrange derivation is explicit; the brightness-invariance term connects to classical optical flow.
- Practicality: The ODEs are simple enough for real-time scanpath generation in videos.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Affordances appear ad hoc. “Curiosity” is operationalized as gradient magnitude plus a blurred gradient with an exogenous cos2/sin2 temporal alternation; the oscillation, frequency choice, and combination lack principled justification or ablation. The brightness-invariance term is plausible but not tied to a measurable cost (e.g., inference accuracy, retention of object identity).
- Least Action link to eye-movement theory is underdeveloped. There is a rich optimal-control literature on saccades and eye movements (e.g., minimum-variance/jerk, signal-dependent noise, optimal feedback control; Harris & Wolpert 1998; Todorov & Jordan 2002; Flash & Hogan 1985) that could anchor the Lagrangian’s form and constraints. Currently the derivation is mathematically sound but conceptually floating.
- Physical interpretation issues. The kinetic energy is defined as K = (1/m)‖ẋ‖2 rather than the standard (m/2)‖ẋ‖2; if intentional, please justify, as this choice affects the dimensions and the λ < m/4 condition. More generally, the mix of true potentials and velocity-dependent “potentials” would benefit from a careful dimensional and stability analysis.
- Evaluation is limited. Performance is mixed and typically worse than top deep models; improvements on AUC-like metrics may reflect the learned center bias and blur. There is no quantitative scanpath evaluation (e.g., MultiMatch, ScanMatch, string-edit distance), no saccade-statistics comparison (amplitude distribution, main sequence, velocity profiles), and no temporal metrics for videos.

Clarity
- The main paper lacks figures showing exemplar scanpaths, saccades, and tracking behavior over time; results focus on static saliency metrics. This is a major omission given the paper’s core claim about dynamics.
- Some hyperparameters (e.g., ω, k, bounds in simulated annealing) and preprocessing steps (center bias, blur optimization) are not sufficiently justified, and the impact on different metrics (especially sAUC, IG) is not dissected.
- V1 vs. V2 (approximate vs. exact brightness invariance) is presented, but the practical differences and computational tradeoffs could be explained more clearly.

Originality
- Using a variational/Least-Action derivation for scanpaths is original in this context. However, the components (detail-seeking, peripheral modulation, brightness invariance) overlap with established ideas in saliency and optical flow, and dynamical scanpath models exist. The main novelty rests on the specific Lagrangian construction and its purported unification.

Significance
- As a saliency predictor, the method does not advance state of the art relative to benchmark deep models. The potential significance lies in offering a mechanistic model of eye-movement dynamics, but the paper does not provide the scanpath-level behavioral validation necessary to realize that impact.

Detailed suggestions
- Ground the Lagrangian in optimal-control and motor-planning literature on eye movements (e.g., Harris & Wolpert 1998; Todorov & Jordan 2002; minimum-jerk/variance; connections to the Wolpert lab’s work). Clarify how your “action” encodes costs relevant to saccadic precision, latency, and energy.
- Motivate and ablate the “affordances.” Justify the cos2/sin2 alternation and peripheral term biologically (e.g., cortical rhythms, foveal–peripheral trade-offs) or replace with a learned schedule; report ablations (ηb, ηp, λ, with/without oscillation).
- Provide scanpath analyses: show sample trajectories vs. human scanpaths; report MultiMatch/ScanMatch, saccade amplitude distributions, main sequence, fixation durations, and the effect of inhibition-of-return-like dynamics (if any).
- Ensure evaluation fairness: report sAUC and IG; control for center bias; analyze robustness to initial conditions (not just near-center starts).
- Clarify and correct the kinetic-energy definition and units; discuss stability and the λ < m/4 constraint. Provide sensitivity analyses.
- Extend biological links: map terms to SC/FEF/LIP computations or retinal/contrast sensitivity; discuss how noise or signal-dependent variability could be incorporated.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually interesting, interdisciplinary formulation with potential to inspire further dynamical models of attention.
- Clear mathematical derivation; unifies fixation and tracking in a single system.
- Lightweight and real-time capable; educational value as a mechanistic alternative to black-box deep models.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical support for claimed scanpath dynamics; lack of dynamic/temporal analyses and figures in the main paper.
- Affordances feel hand-crafted and insufficiently justified; weak connection to established optimality principles in eye-movement research.
- Mixed performance, generally below leading deep saliency models; gains on some metrics likely driven by center-bias tuning.
- Clarity gaps around hyperparameters, preprocessing choices, and physical interpretation of the Lagrangian terms.

Overall evaluation
An enjoyable paper with an intriguing idea—deriving eye-movement dynamics from a variational principle—but in its present form it lacks strong motivation for the specific “affordances,” a tighter connection to the eye-movement/control literature, and decisive empirical evidence at the scanpath level. With deeper biological/theoretical grounding and richer dynamic evaluations, this could become an impactful contribution.
