- Proposes EYMOL, a physics-inspired variational model that predicts gaze trajectories directly rather than via saliency maps plus selection.
- Lagrangian combines a bounding potential, a curiosity term (local detail vs. peripheral blurred image), and a brightness-invariance term akin to optical-flow constancy.
- Euler–Lagrange equations yield second-order ODEs for gaze dynamics; parameters fitted via simulated annealing on a CAT2000 subset.
- Saliency maps are produced post hoc by aggregating simulated trajectories; approximate brightness-invariance works for static images.
- Novelty: models full scanpaths as a dynamical system, producing saliency maps as a byproduct, distinct from typical saliency pipelines.
- Technical soundness: variational formulation is coherent, well-motivated connections between terms, and parameter conditions discussed.
- Empirical support: tested on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, SFU videos with standard metrics; results competitive but not uniformly superior.
- Main limitation: evaluation focuses on aggregate saliency maps rather than validating predicted scanpath dynamics against humans.
- Missing quantitative trajectory-level comparisons (MultiMatch, ScanMatch, saccade distributions) and ablations for priors like center bias and blur.
- Parameter fitting and sensitivity analyses are incomplete—no study of generalization, initialization effects, annealing settings, or ω sensitivity.
- Video evaluation limited (small SFU dataset) and lacks analysis of cases violating brightness constancy (illumination changes).
- Clarity: paper is generally well written and organized, but some mathematical details need proofreading and numerical integration details are unclear.
- Reproducibility concerns: integrator choice, step size, boundary handling, parameter ranges, ω, initialization distributions, and code release are not specified.
- Originality: least-action formulation and combination of curiosity and brightness-invariance provide a novel theoretical unification of saccades and tracking.
- Significance: interesting to vision/perception community; broader NeurIPS impact uncertain given limited empirical gains and missing trajectory validation.
- Strengths: principled, interpretable model that directly produces scanpaths, unifies saccades/tracking, competitive results, and real-time applicability.
- Weaknesses: overemphasis on saliency maps, benchmark-tuned priors, limited sensitivity/robustness analysis, small video benchmark, and minor math typos.
- Key questions for authors: sensitivity to ω and relative weights, impact of removing center bias/blur, scanpath-level metrics vs. humans, behavior under luminance changes.
- Suggested improvements: add trajectory-level evaluations, ablation studies (no center bias/blur/terms), expand video benchmarks, discuss top-down/task effects, and release code/parameters.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical framing, unifying model for eye movements, competitive without heavy learning, and potential to inspire cross-disciplinary work.
- Arguments against acceptance: central claim of trajectory realism under-demonstrated, reliance on tuned priors, limited video evaluation, and incomplete reproducibility/sensitivity analyses.
- Overall: creative and thoughtful contribution with encouraging validation; borderline for NeurIPS pending stronger scanpath validation and ablation/sensitivity analyses.
