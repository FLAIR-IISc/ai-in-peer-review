- Paper introduces EYMOL, a novel physics-inspired scanpath model using a generalized Least Action Principle to model eye movements.  
- Lagrangian combines kinetic energy with a potential comprising boundedness, curiosity-driven oscillation between fine/coarse details, and brightness-invariance for fixation/tracking.  
- Euler–Lagrange equations yield second-order dynamics; saliency maps are produced by aggregating simulated scanpath visits.  
- Evaluated on static (MIT1003, CAT2000, MIT300) and dynamic (SFU) datasets, showing competitive or superior performance to state-of-the-art models.  
- Strength: highly original, principled approach reframing attention as a generative scanpath process rather than direct saliency computation.  
- Strength: unifies multiple attentional behaviors (exploration, fixation, pursuit) within a single differential-equation framework.  
- Strength: achieves strong empirical results despite theoretical simplicity and no direct saliency training.  
- Strength: significant potential impact for computer vision, computational neuroscience, and cognitive science.  
- Weakness: poor clarity and presentation—dense writing, grammatical errors, and readability issues hinder comprehension and reproducibility.  
- Weakness: undefined variables/parameters and missing precise mathematical forms (e.g., boundary notation, Gaussian blob, blur/σ), plus undefined abbreviations like NSS.  
- Weakness: insufficient intuitive explanation for modeling choices (e.g., sinusoidal modulation to avoid trapping).  
- Weakness: limited engagement with cognitive/neuroscience literature—fails to relate to optimal observer/visual search models (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler).  
- Suggestion: major rewrite for clarity, explicit definitions for all variables/functions, and thorough proofreading.  
- Suggestion: restructure by moving detailed derivations to supplement and use freed space for intuitive model explanation and experimental details for reproducibility.  
- Suggestion: strengthen related-work discussion contrasting variational laws with information-theoretic optimality and add a Limitations section on bottom-up scope and top-down extensions.  
- Conclusion: conceptually strong and empirically promising, but requires substantial revision on clarity, reproducibility, and literature connection before acceptance.
