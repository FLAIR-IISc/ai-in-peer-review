This paper proposes a novel computational model of visual attention, framing scanpath generation as a problem of finding the stationary point of a generalized action integral inspired by the Least Action Principle in physics. The authors motivate this approach by suggesting foundational laws govern attention, incorporating principles of curiosity (driven by image detail) and brightness invariance into a Lagrangian formulation. This results in differential equations describing the focus of attention, solved numerically to generate scanpaths and, as a byproduct, saliency maps. While the approach is intriguing, its grounding in established cognitive or neurophysiological literature requires strengthening, and the empirical validation, while thorough, reveals performance competitive with but not exceeding state-of-the-art methods.

The work builds upon decades of research into visual attention, starting with the early feature-integration theory of Koch and Ullman [9] and the implementation by Itti et al. [10], which established saliency maps as a central component of attention models. More recent work has leveraged machine learning, particularly convolutional neural networks [18, 26], to achieve high performance on saliency prediction tasks. This paper distinguishes itself by attempting a more principled, dynamic systems approach, eschewing the global computation of saliency in favor of a process driven by local principles.  Unlike many recent models focusing on learning complex feature representations, this work attempts to derive attention directly from first principles. The authors correctly position their work as a departure from traditional saliency map approaches, where scanpaths are typically generated *after* the saliency map is computed [9, 10].

The strengths of the paper lie in its original framing of visual attention as an optimization problem and the elegant mathematical formulation. The attempt to link scanpath dynamics to fundamental physical principles like the Least Action Principle is ambitious and potentially insightful. The inclusion of brightness invariance as a driving force is a reasonable constraint, particularly for fixation behavior. The authors also provide a reasonably comprehensive experimental evaluation on standard datasets (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU), comparing their model to several baselines. The method for parameter estimation via simulated annealing is also well-described.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The “affordances” of curiosity and brightness invariance, while plausible, feel somewhat *ad hoc*. The connection between these principles and underlying cognitive or neurophysiological mechanisms is weak. While the Least Action Principle is invoked, a more detailed discussion of its relevance to existing theories of motor control, particularly work from labs like those of Wolpert [e.g., Haruno et al., 2009], would significantly strengthen the theoretical motivation. The paper states that newborns exhibit attention mechanisms despite lacking world knowledge [5], hinting at a primitive form of the proposed principles, but this connection isn’t fleshed out. The biological justification is limited, which, while less critical in a field increasingly dominated by data-driven deep learning models, remains a valuable aspect of attention research.

Furthermore, the empirical results are not overwhelmingly compelling. While competitive, the model's performance on saliency detection generally lags behind state-of-the-art deep learning approaches. More crucially, the paper lacks illustrative examples of the generated scanpaths themselves. Presenting visual comparisons of predicted vs. human scanpaths, beyond the summary statistics reported for saliency maps, would significantly enhance the impact of the results. The claim of capturing object tracking “within the same framework” is not convincingly demonstrated with appropriate visualizations or quantitative analysis.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel framing of visual attention as an optimization problem.
*   Elegant mathematical formulation based on the Least Action Principle.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation on standard datasets.
*   Potential for a more principled and interpretable model of attention compared to black-box deep learning approaches.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak theoretical motivation for the chosen “affordances.”
*   Limited biological grounding and connection to relevant neurophysiological literature.
*   Empirical performance is competitive but not state-of-the-art.
*   Lack of illustrative scanpath visualizations to demonstrate claimed dynamic behavior.
*   Limited discussion connecting the model to existing eye-movement literature.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and ambitious approach to modeling visual attention. However, the lack of strong theoretical grounding and the limited demonstration of dynamic scanpath behavior prevent it from achieving a higher rating. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit, addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should strengthen the biological motivation, explore connections to existing motor control theories, include compelling visualizations of generated scanpaths, and conduct more detailed analysis of the model’s dynamic properties.
