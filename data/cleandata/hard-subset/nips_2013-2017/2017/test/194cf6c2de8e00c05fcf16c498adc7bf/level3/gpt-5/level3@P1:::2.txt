This paper proposes a variational, energy-based formulation of bottom-up visual attention that generates a scanpath by solving Euler–Lagrange equations derived from a generalized action. The Lagrangian includes (i) a harmonic boundary potential confining the trajectory within the retinal/image domain, (ii) a curiosity term that alternates between local brightness gradients and a blurred “peripheral” gradient, and (iii) a brightness-invariance term enforcing the optical-flow constraint to promote fixation and motion tracking. The authors derive explicit second-order differential equations of motion (EYMOL), estimate parameters via simulated annealing on CAT2000-TRAIN, and compute saliency maps by aggregating visit counts across multiple simulated runs. On MIT1003/CAT2000/MIT300 (images) and SFU (videos), the method achieves performance comparable to several baselines, despite using only grayscale information and simple features. The work contrasts with saliency-map-first approaches (e.g., Itti-Koch, info-max, surprise, CNN-based models) by making saliency a byproduct of a mechanistic scanpath generator grounded in a variational principle.

Relation to prior work: The setup is reminiscent of classical scanpath simulations “driven” by saliency maps (Itti et al.) but inverts the pipeline by deriving movement laws from an action functional. The brightness-invariance term naturally connects to Horn–Schunck optical flow. Compared to recent deep models (e.g., SAM-LSTM), this model is interpretable and lightweight. A broader connection to computationally rational theories of eye movements (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler’s optimal search/active sensing framework) would strengthen the scientific positioning and help articulate when least action approximates optimal behavior.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The derivation of Euler–Lagrange equations is careful, including the velocity-dependent “potential” and a well-posedness condition λ < m/4. However, (a) the kinetic energy expression appears nonstandard (K is typically 1/2 m||ẋ||^2; the manuscript uses 1/m||ẋ||^2), which affects units and the interpretation of m; please verify and correct. (b) The cosine/sine modulation in C(t, x) and the choice of ω are not justified nor analyzed (sensitivity, stability). (c) The brightness-invariance approximation used for static images (V1 vs V2) is validated empirically; an error bound or clearer conditions for equivalence would be helpful.
- Experimental design: Parameter fitting via SA on a CAT2000-TRAIN subset is reasonable, but the additional grid search for blur/center bias tuned on the same dataset (and on SFU for videos) risks inflating metrics that are known to benefit from center bias and blur (AUC, NSS). Please (i) report results without the added center bias and with fixed blur; (ii) add ablations for ηb/ηp/λ; and (iii) clarify whether video hyperparameters were tuned on a held-out subset. The multiple-initialization strategy near the image center also induces center bias; quantify its effect.
- Evaluation scope: Results focus on saliency-map metrics; since the model’s core output is a scanpath, include scanpath-level metrics (e.g., saccade amplitude distribution, fixation duration, MultiMatch, string-edit distance) and motion-tracking success to substantiate claims about fixation and tracking behavior.

Clarity:
- The manuscript is hard to follow in places and needs careful proofreading and restructuring. Many symbols and abbreviations (e.g., bx, px, bt, ω, m, λ, ηb, ηp, li) are introduced informally or late; define all variables on first use and maintain consistent notation.
- Move the lengthy derivations to the supplementary material and use the freed space to clarify the modeling choices (why each term, units, parameter ranges), initial/boundary conditions, numerical integration details (step size, solver, runtime), and dataset splits.
- Explicitly describe the preprocessing pipeline and its implications (grayscale conversion, Gaussian peripheral channel, multiplicative center bias) and discuss how these steps interact with evaluation metrics.

Originality:
- The idea of deriving attention dynamics from a least-action Lagrangian that unifies curiosity and brightness-invariance is novel in the attention literature, even though the brightness-invariance component is classical in optical flow. The alternating local/peripheral gradient drive is an interesting mechanism to avoid local traps. Framing attention as physics-like dynamics is conceptually appealing and may inspire new active-vision models.

Significance:
- Scientifically, the work offers a principled, interpretable model that can generate continuous-time scanpaths and produce competitive saliency maps as a byproduct. For engineering, performance is respectable given the simplicity and real-time feasibility. The lack of color/orientation/mid-level features and the reliance on tuned center bias likely cap state-of-the-art performance; nonetheless, the framework seems extensible (e.g., adding feature channels or top-down terms in U).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide an ablation study: remove each Lagrangian term (V only; V+C; V+B; V+C+B), vary ω, and quantify effects on metrics and trajectory behavior.
- Report results with and without externally added center bias and blur; quantify the effect of initialization choices.
- Correct and justify the kinetic energy term; include units and typical parameter magnitudes.
- Add scanpath metrics and qualitative analyses (trajectory overlays, saccade statistics).
- Situate within optimal/active sensing literature and discuss potential integration (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler, Nature 2005; J Vis 2008), RL-based active vision, and connections to Bayesian surprise/information gain.
- Release code and parameter settings for reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel variational formulation of attention dynamics producing scanpaths; saliency as a secondary outcome.
- Interpretable, unified account of curiosity and brightness-invariance, with clear equations and stability conditions.
- Competitive performance on multiple benchmarks with a simple, fast model.

Cons for acceptance:
- Clarity issues and missing definitions; some equations likely contain typos (kinetic energy).
- Heavy reliance on center bias/blur and tuning; limited ablations; potential metric inflation.
- Evaluation centered on saliency maps rather than scanpath behavior; limited connection to broader cognitive/optimality literature.
- Uses only grayscale; limited feature repertoire.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on addressing clarity issues, adding ablations and scanpath analyses, and clarifying the kinetic energy term and hyperparameter tuning.
