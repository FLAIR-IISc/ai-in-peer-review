## Review of "Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes"

This paper proposes a novel computational model of visual attention based on a variational energy approach, drawing inspiration from principles in physics. The core idea is to model scanpaths as the result of minimizing an action integral, constructed from terms representing harmonic oscillator constraints for retinal boundaries, curiosity driven by multi-scale brightness changes, and brightness invariance. The authors derive differential equations governing eye movements and validate the model quantitatively on both static and dynamic eye-tracking datasets, achieving performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods. 

The approach is interesting and potentially valuable as it offers a formal, principled framework for understanding bottom-up visual attention, moving beyond purely feature-based or learning-based methods. The connection to the Least Action Principle is a creative attempt to ground attention mechanisms in fundamental physical laws. The quantitative evaluation demonstrating competitive performance is a definite strength. This work builds upon the foundation laid by early saliency models like Itti et al. (2000) and Judd et al. (2009), but distinguishes itself through its theoretical framework rather than purely empirical optimization. 

However, the paper suffers significantly from a lack of clarity and organization. The writing is dense and difficult to follow, especially in Section 3 and the mathematical derivations. Many abbreviations and variables are introduced without proper definition, hindering comprehension. The two-page appendix should indeed be moved to the supplementary material to free up space for more detailed explanations within the main text. 

Furthermore, the connection to psychological literature on optimal eye movements is missing. The authors should consider comparing their results to established findings, such as those by Najemnik & Geisler (2008) on minimizing information gain during saccades. Discussing how their model aligns with, or diverges from, these findings would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**
* Novel and principled approach to saliency modeling.
* Competitive performance on standard datasets.
* Potential for valuable insights in vision and cognitive science.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
* Poorly written and difficult to understand.
* Lack of clarity in variable/function definitions.
* Missing connections to relevant psychological literature.

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a promising but currently underdeveloped idea. I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit, addressing the clarity, completeness, and contextualization issues outlined above. A significant rewrite is necessary to make the work accessible and fully appreciate its potential contributions.
