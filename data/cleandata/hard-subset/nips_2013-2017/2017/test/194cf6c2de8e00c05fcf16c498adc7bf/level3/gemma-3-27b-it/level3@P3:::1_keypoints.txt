- Proposes a novel dynamic model of visual attention using a Least Action Principle to generate scanpaths from "curiosity" and brightness invariance.
- Novelty: attempts to model scanpaths directly from underlying principles rather than using static saliency maps plus ad-hoc scanpath rules.
- Motivation weak: chosen affordances (boundedness, curiosity, brightness invariance) feel arbitrary and lack compelling justification for their combination.
- "Curiosity" term is particularly ad-hoc, alternating local and peripheral information without clear rationale.
- Brightness invariance has some grounding (optical flow) but is isolated and not well integrated into a broader cognitive framework.
- Use of the Least Action Principle is interesting but insufficiently connected to literature on optimal control, predictive coding, and eye movement theories.
- Mathematical derivation is reasonable but unclear in relating potential/kinetic energies to neural processes; notation could be improved.
- Empirical results are mixed: competitive on some saliency datasets but generally underperform deep learning saliency models.
- Major experimental omission: no visual examples or quantitative analysis of generated scanpaths despite the paper's dynamical focus.
- Needs detailed ablation studies to isolate contributions of each Lagrangian term and comparison between V1/V2 brightness terms.
- Paper clarity and organization are generally good, but deeper explanations for key choices are missing.
- Arguments for acceptance: principled, novel approach; interesting application of Least Action; competitive dataset performance; potential for unified theory.
- Arguments against acceptance: ad-hoc affordances, weak connection to control/neuroscience literature, limited biological grounding, mixed empirical support, lack of scanpath-level analysis.
- Recommendation: reject but encourage revision — strengthen theoretical justification, add scanpath analyses and ablations, and connect to existing neuroscience/control literature.
