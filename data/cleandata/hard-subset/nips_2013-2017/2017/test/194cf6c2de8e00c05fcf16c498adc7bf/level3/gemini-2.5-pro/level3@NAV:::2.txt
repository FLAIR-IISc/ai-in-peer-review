This paper presents a novel, physics-inspired variational model for predicting bottom-up visual attention scanpaths in both static and dynamic scenes. Departing from common approaches that directly compute saliency maps, this work proposes that eye movements emerge from a few fundamental principles. The model is elegantly constructed from a Lagrangian combining three terms: a harmonic oscillator to constrain eye movements within the image bounds, a "curiosity" term attracted to multi-scale brightness changes, and a brightness-invariance term to encourage fixation and tracking. By applying the principle of least action, the authors derive a set of differential equations that govern the scanpath dynamics. The model is evaluated on several standard static and dynamic eye-tracking datasets, where it generates saliency maps as a byproduct of simulated scanpaths, achieving performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this paper is its originality and formal approach. Grounding a model of attention in variational principles is a significant contribution that offers a unifying framework for understanding saccades and smooth pursuit. This principled methodology is a valuable alternative to purely heuristic or black-box deep learning models, providing deeper potential insights for the fields of computer vision and cognitive science. The technical quality appears sound, and the experimental validation demonstrates that this formal approach can compete with established, highly-engineered systems. The potential significance is high, as such foundational models could inspire new directions in attention research.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's most significant weakness is its lack of clarity. The manuscript is poorly written and difficult to follow, which severely obscures its novel contributions. Many variables, functions, and abbreviations are used without clear, upfront definitions, making the mathematical exposition in Section 2 and the Appendix particularly challenging to parse. This lack of clarity compromises the paper's quality and reproducibility.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
I strongly recommend acceptance, contingent on a major revision to address the clarity issues. The authors should perform a thorough proofreading and rewrite to improve the exposition. Crucially, every variable and function must be explicitly defined upon its first appearance. I suggest moving the lengthy two-page derivation in the appendix to supplementary material. The two pages freed in the main text should be used to meticulously define the model's components and parameters, making the core contribution accessible. Furthermore, the paper would be strengthened by connecting its findings to the psychological literature on optimal eye movement strategies, for example, by discussing how the model's behavior relates to theories of ideal observer models (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler, 2008) and whether these frameworks could be integrated.
