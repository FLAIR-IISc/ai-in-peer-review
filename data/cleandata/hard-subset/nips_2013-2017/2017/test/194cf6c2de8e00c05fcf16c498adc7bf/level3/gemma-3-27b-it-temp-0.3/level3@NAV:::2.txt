## Review of "Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes"

This paper proposes a novel model of visual attention, termed EYMOL, grounded in a variational, physics-inspired approach. The core idea is to model scanpaths as the result of minimizing an action integral, incorporating principles of bounded trajectories, curiosity driven by image detail, and brightness invariance. The resulting model yields differential equations predicting eye movements, and is evaluated on both static and dynamic eye-tracking datasets, achieving performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods. This work represents a unique and potentially valuable contribution, moving beyond saliency map-centric approaches to directly model scanpath generation, which aligns with recent interest in understanding the underlying mechanisms of attention and its link to optimal movement strategies.

The strength of this paper lies in its originality and the formal, mathematically rigorous approach. The attempt to unify attention mechanisms under a least-action principle is compelling and offers a fresh perspective. The inclusion of brightness invariance as a key driver is particularly insightful, potentially bridging the gap between bottom-up saliency and more goal-directed, stable fixations. The quantitative results, while not dramatically surpassing existing methods, demonstrate the viability of the approach and its competitive performance.

However, the paper suffers significantly from clarity and exposition issues. The writing is dense and difficult to follow, with numerous undefined variables and functions, particularly within Section 3 and the equations themselves. The authors rely heavily on mathematical notation without sufficient explanation, hindering understanding. The two-page appendix should be moved to the supplementary material, and the freed space used to explicitly define all variables and functions used throughout the paper. The connection to psychological literature on optimal eye movements (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2008) is crucial; discussing how EYMOL aligns with or diverges from established findings regarding efficient information gathering would significantly strengthen the work. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically grounded approach to modeling visual attention.
*   Competitive performance on standard datasets.
*   Potential to advance understanding of the underlying mechanisms of scanpath generation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Poorly written and difficult to understand.
*   Lack of clear definitions for variables and functions.
*   Insufficient connection to relevant psychological literature.



**Overall Recommendation:** Reject with request for major revisions. The core idea is promising, but the paper requires substantial improvements in clarity, exposition, and contextualization to be considered a significant contribution to the field. Addressing the issues outlined above is crucial for making the work accessible and impactful.
