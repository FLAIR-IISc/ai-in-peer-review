This paper proposes EYMOL, a dynamical, bottom-up model of visual attention derived from a variational (least-action) formulation. The Lagrangian comprises a kinetic term, a potential that confines the trajectory within the retinal field, a “curiosity” term that alternates attraction to local and peripheral gradients, and a brightness-invariance term derived from the optical flow constraint. The resulting Euler–Lagrange equations yield scanpaths; saliency maps are computed as visitation counts over many simulated trajectories. Parameters are selected via simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a subset of CAT2000-TRAIN. The model is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and the SFU video dataset; an approximate and an exact brightness-invariance variant perform similarly on static images. Compared with classical saliency baselines and one CNN model, results are competitive on some metrics/datasets; on others, performance lags modern deep saliency methods.

Relation to prior work: The model contrasts with saliency-map-centric pipelines [9,10,16,24] and learning-based saliency (e.g., Judd et al. [1], CNN approaches [26,18]). It connects loosely to dynamic scanpath generators (e.g., winner-take-all with inhibition-of-return [9], saccadic models [6]) but replaces heuristics with a single variational principle. The authors do not situate their least-action approach within optimal control and motor control accounts of eye movements (e.g., minimum-jerk/variance and optimal feedback control in the Wolpert/Todorov literature), which would strengthen the conceptual bridge between physics-inspired objectives and oculomotor behavior.

Strengths
- A clear, principled dynamical formulation that produces scanpaths and saliency as a byproduct, unifying fixation, saccades, and tracking within one set of equations.
- Derivation is technically careful; the Appendix makes the implicit dependence on ẍ explicit and the well-posedness condition clear.
- The brightness-invariance term is a nice connection to classic optical flow constraints and plausibly explains fixation on contours and motion tracking.
- Empirical coverage across static and dynamic datasets; the V1 vs V2 comparison shows the approximate invariance works well for static scenes.
- Computationally light and potentially real-time.

Weaknesses
- The “affordances” (curiosity alternating local/peripheral, brightness invariance) feel ad hoc and lack biological grounding; ω selection and alternation are not supported by data or ablations.
- Limited connection to established eye-movement optimality principles (e.g., effort/noise tradeoffs), despite the least-action framing; missing discussion of relevant motor-control literature from the Wolpert/Todorov line.
- The kinetic term is unconventional: K(ẋ)=1/m||ẋ||² departs from the usual (m/2)||ẋ||²; this flips the role of m and complicates the interpretation of λ < m/4. Dimensional and stability implications need clarification.
- Evaluation emphasizes saliency map scores but lacks analysis of the generated scanpaths (e.g., saccade amplitude distributions, fixation durations, MultiMatch or string-edit metrics, temporal correlations with human scanpaths).
- Center-bias and blur are tuned to optimize AUC-Judd/NSS, which may inflate metrics; fairness vs learned baselines is not fully addressed.
- Comparisons omit several state-of-the-art deep models current at the time (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON, ML-Net/SAM variants), and performance appears mixed and generally below the best deep models on MIT300/CAT2000.
- Details of simulated annealing (acceptance function, neighbor proposal, bounds D) and sensitivity analyses are sparse; stability issues noted for ηb, ηp are not quantified.

Quality
- The mathematical development is mostly sound, but the definition of kinetic energy and the resulting scaling of parameters deserve a careful justification, ideally with a units analysis and an ablation showing that the choice does not drive results.
- The well-posedness condition λ < m/4 is derived under specific normalizations; its practical enforcement and its interaction with ω and blur should be documented.
- The learning/tuning pipeline risks overfitting to a small training subset; include cross-validation and report variance across runs.

Clarity
- The paper would benefit from figures showing representative trajectories, fixation heatmaps over time, and tracking examples, as well as histograms of saccade amplitudes and fixation durations vs human data.
- Clarify the SA procedure (acceptance probability P, neighbor generation, α schedule) and provide pseudocode with concrete settings.
- Consolidate notation (e.g., consistent use of vector norms) and correct minor typos.

Originality
- The least-action formulation for attention dynamics and the periodic mixing of local vs peripheral gradients are novel in the saliency literature.
- However, the paper could better differentiate itself from dynamical saliency/scanpath models and from optimal control perspectives on eye movements by explicit comparisons and citations.

Significance
- As a mechanistic, interpretable alternative to black-box saliency predictors, this line has promise. However, without stronger empirical scanpath analyses and head-to-head benchmarks against top deep models, the impact on state-of-the-art prediction appears limited.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to ω, blur σ, and the center-bias strength? Please provide ablations and sensitivity plots.
- What happens if the curiosity alternation is removed or replaced by a static convex combination? Does it still avoid “trapping”?
- Can you report scanpath metrics (e.g., MultiMatch) and human-model temporal correlations?
- How robust is tracking in videos where bt is noisy or when multiple moving objects are present?

Suggestions
- Add explicit comparisons to stronger contemporary deep models on MIT300/CAT2000.
- Provide comprehensive scanpath characterizations and visualizations.
- Justify the kinetic term and revisit parameter scaling; relate the action to known oculomotor optimality principles (e.g., minimum-variance, effort costs), citing the Wolpert/Todorov literature.
- Include failure cases and discuss when the model excels (e.g., high-contrast motion) or fails (e.g., strong semantic cues).

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, interpretable dynamical framework unifying saliency and scanpaths.
- Technically neat variational derivation with a plausible brightness-invariance mechanism.
- Competitive performance against classic baselines with low computational cost.

Arguments against acceptance
- Mixed performance and missing comparisons to top deep methods.
- Ad hoc components insufficiently motivated biologically; limited connection to related optimal control literature.
- Lack of scanpath-level evaluation and limited ablations/sensitivity analyses.
- Ambiguity around the kinetic term and parameter scaling.
