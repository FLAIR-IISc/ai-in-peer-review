- Proposal: variational, energy-based model generating scanpaths via Euler–Lagrange ODEs from a least-action Lagrangian.  
- Lagrangian components: retinal harmonic potential, curiosity term alternating local/peripheral gradients, and brightness-invariance term akin to optical-flow constancy.  
- Outputs: explicit second-order ODEs for eye position; saliency maps computed as visit counts across trajectories.  
- Empirical claim: competitive AUC/NSS on static (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and dynamic (SFU) datasets despite model simplicity.  
- Prior work: builds on established global saliency, surprise-based accounts, and heuristic/biologically inspired scanpath models; novelty is the variational least-action framing.  
- Technical soundness: derivation is nontrivial and conceptually sound; brightness-constancy inclusion is well-motivated; alternation avoids local traps.  
- Evaluation gap: emphasis on saliency maps rather than the model’s primary output (scanpaths); no scanpath metrics or temporal analyses reported.  
- Possible confounds: center-bias and post-hoc blur tuning could inflate results; sAUC/IG and center-bias controls needed.  
- Reproducibility issues: missing details on parameter choices (m, k, λ, ηb, ηp, ω), annealing, initial-condition distributions, integration method/step size, and parameter domain.  
- Modeling detail concerns: kinetic term normalization deviates from convention; boundary potential uses Iverson brackets causing piecewise nonsmooth forces—numerical implications unaddressed.  
- Statistical rigor: risk of dataset leakage from tuning on CAT2000-TRAIN; need variance across initializations and parameter sensitivity/ablation studies.  
- Clarity problems: poor exposition, undefined symbols/notation, missing definitions for p(t,x), center-bias Gaussian, cos2/sin2 alternation, and ω’s relation to timescales.  
- Presentation fixes: extensive proofreading, define all variables on first use, move lengthy derivations to supplementary, and present static-image simplification first.  
- Originality: novel formal variational framing linking curiosity and brightness invariance into an explicit dynamical scanpath generator.  
- Literature positioning: should relate more clearly to optimal search/active-sensing literature (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler).  
- Significance: conceptually strong and interpretable bridge between physics-inspired principles and eye-movement dynamics; practical gains modest vs modern deep models.  
- Suggested analyses: add scanpath-level comparisons to human data, ablations for each Lagrangian term, sensitivity to ω/λ/η, and stability analysis (e.g., λ < m/4).  
- Broader evaluation: test on more dynamic datasets (DIEM, DHF1K) and report center-bias-controlled metrics (sAUC, IG).  
- Pros: elegant principled formulation, explicit ODEs for scanpaths, unifies saccades/fixations/motion tracking, competitive simple model.  
- Cons: readability and organization issues, limited evaluation of scanpath claims, potential center-bias/blur confounds, reproducibility gaps, weak connection to optimal eye-movement literature.  
- Recommended fixes: release code and exact parameters, justify modeling choices (K scaling, boundary smoothness, ω selection), provide integration/solver details, and add ablations/scanpath metrics.  
- Overall recommendation: weak reject (borderline) due to clarity, missing definitions, and limited scanpath evaluation despite an original and promising core idea.
