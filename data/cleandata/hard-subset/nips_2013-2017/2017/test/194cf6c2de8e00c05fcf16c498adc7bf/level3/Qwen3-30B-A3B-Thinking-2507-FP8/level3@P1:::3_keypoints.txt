- Introduces EYMOL, a framework modeling gaze trajectories via a generalized Least Action Principle and Euler-Lagrange equations.  
- Differs from traditional saliency-map methods by directly modeling scanpaths and computing saliency as a byproduct.  
- Attention in the model arises from three principles: bounded trajectory motion (retina constraints), curiosity-driven detail attraction, and brightness invariance.  
- Theoretical novelty: unifies curiosity and brightness invariance under a variational principle, offering a fresh perspective beyond saliency maps.  
- Thorough experimental validation on MIT1003, CAT2000, SFU using NSS and AUC-Judd metrics.  
- Competitive empirical performance, ranking among top methods on several metrics without bespoke saliency optimization.  
- Efficient, real-time scanpath generation and simple implementation using an approximated brightness term (V1) that avoids overfitting.  
- Mathematical derivations (Appendix) are highly technical and rely on physics concepts that may be opaque to many readers.  
- Key theoretical constraints (e.g., λ < m/4) are not transparently verified, limiting confidence in theoretical soundness.  
- Results are favorable but not transformative, falling short of top CNN-based saliency models (e.g., Cornia et al. 2016).  
- Unclear direct impact for NIPS / broader ML community; more immediately relevant to vision science and cognitive modeling.  
- Overall: solid, novel contribution with strong empirical grounding; recommend acceptance despite moderate theoretical and impact-related caveats.
