

This paper proposes a novel computational model of visual attention ("EYMOL") grounded in variational principles inspired by physics' Least Action Principle. The authors model eye movement scanpaths as trajectories derived from Euler-Lagrange equations, incorporating three key "affordances": (i) boundedness via harmonic oscillator constraints, (ii) curiosity-driven attention to high-gradient regions (modulated by local/peripheral features), and (iii) brightness invariance for fixation/trackin. The model generates saliency maps as a byproduct of scanpath simulation, avoiding explicit saliency map construction used in most prior work (e.g., Itti et al., 1998; Judd et al., 2009). The approach represents a distinct paradigm from neural network-based saliency models (e.g., Xu et al., 2016; Cornia et al., 2016) by focusing on dynamical laws rather than feature learning.

**Strengths**  
The mathematical formulation is technically sound and elegant. The derivation of Euler-Lagrange equations (Section 2.2) rigorously extends classical mechanics to visual attention, with clear motivation for the Lagrangian components. The model’s real-time capability for scanpath generation is a practical advantage over iterative deep learning approaches. Experimental validation on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU datasets demonstrates competitive performance: EYMOL achieves top scores in half the metrics (e.g., NSS on MIT300) against established baselines (Table 3), and its dynamic handling of video sequences (SFU) is promising. The parameter estimation via simulated annealing (Section 2.3) is well-justified, and the approximation of brightness invariance (Eq. 6) is validated as numerically effective.

**Weaknesses**  
The **core weakness** is insufficient motivation for the proposed affordances. While curiosity (Eq. 4) and brightness invariance (Eq. 5) are mathematically plausible, they lack grounding in cognitive science or behavioral evidence. For instance, the oscillatory modulation (cos²(ωt)/sin²(ωt)) in curiosity appears arbitrary without justification from visual neuroscience. The paper cites newborn attention studies (Hainline et al., 1990) but fails to link these to the model’s mechanics. Critically, the work **ignores established connections** between optimal control theory and eye movements (e.g., Wolpert et al.’s work on optimal feedback control in sensorimotor systems), which would strengthen the physics analogy. Without this, the "least action" framing risks being a superficial mathematical rephrasing rather than a biologically informed principle.

Performance is **mixed relative to state-of-the-art**: EYMOL underperforms leading deep learning models (e.g., Vig et al., 2014; Cornia et al., 2016) on most metrics (Table 3), and its competitive results on MIT300 are offset by lower scores on CAT2000TEST. The lack of **dynamic scanpath visualization** (e.g., example trajectories overlaid on images) severely limits assessment of whether the model captures temporal attention dynamics—only static saliency maps are reported. This omission is particularly damaging given the paper’s focus on scanpath dynamics. The biological motivation (Section 1) remains superficial, citing general cognitive science without integrating how the model’s predictions align with known eye-movement properties (e.g., saccade amplitude distributions, inhibition-of-return).

**Originality and Significance**  
The paper’s core contribution—applying variational principles to derive scanpath dynamics—is conceptually novel for visual attention modeling. However, this novelty is **not sufficiently differentiated** from prior work. While Itti et al. (1998) used a centralized saliency map, the current paper’s dynamical approach is distinct. Yet, the significance is tempered by the model’s modest performance gains over simpler baselines and its lack of integration with modern deep learning frameworks. The claim that "saliency maps arise as a byproduct" is technically accurate but does not demonstrably advance the state-of-the-art in saliency prediction (where deep models dominate). The potential for real-time scanpath generation (Section 4) is promising but unexplored in practice.

**Recommendation**  
The paper presents an interesting mathematical framework with technical rigor, but its weaknesses—particularly the ad hoc affordances, poor biological grounding, and underwhelming empirical results—prevent it from making a strong contribution to NIPS. It would benefit from: (1) explicit connections to optimal control literature (Wolpert, 1995; Todorov, 2004); (2) stronger motivation for the curiosity modulation term; (3) dynamic scanpath visualizations; and (4) more comprehensive comparison with recent deep models. As is, the work is technically plausible but lacks the empirical or theoretical weight to justify acceptance over the current state-of-the-art. **Borderline rejection**—the core idea has merit but requires substantial revision to address motivation and evaluation gaps.
