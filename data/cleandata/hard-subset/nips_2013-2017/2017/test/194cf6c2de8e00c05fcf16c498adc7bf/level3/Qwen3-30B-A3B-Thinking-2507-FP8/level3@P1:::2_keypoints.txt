- Proposes a novel variational framework (EYMOL) for modeling scanpath dynamics using a generalized Least Action Principle.  
- Derives Euler–Lagrange differential equations combining harmonic oscillator constraints, curiosity-driven attention (local + peripheral gradients), and brightness invariance.  
- Produces saliency maps as a byproduct of simulated scanpaths, unlike methods that directly optimize saliency.  
- Theoretical contribution is original and technically sound; derivations in the Appendix are correct.  
- Unifies curiosity-driven exploration and brightness invariance into a single bottom-up attention model, offering a fresh alternative to feature-integration and learning-based approaches.  
- Generates realistic scanpaths and competitive saliency results on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video datasets, with strong results on some CAT2000 metrics.  
- Capable of real-time dynamic scene tracking, a practical strength over many prior methods.  
- Major clarity problems: key variables and parameters (e.g., b(t,x), ω, λ, ηb/ηp) are introduced without clear definitions in the main text.  
- Appendix contains essential derivations but should be moved to supplementary material to free space for clearer definitions and exposition.  
- Experimental descriptions are vague (e.g., “a Gaussian blob,” “grid search”), hindering reproducibility.  
- Claims about approximation quality lack quantitative justification beyond a statistical table.  
- Misses important connections to psychological literature on optimal eye movements (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2008), weakening the significance and biological plausibility arguments.  
- Technical quality is high but undermined by poor exposition; empirical performance is comparable to, not clearly better than, state of the art.  
- Recommendation: Reject in current form; encourage resubmission after clarifying definitions, moving derivations, improving experimental detail, and connecting to psychological studies.
