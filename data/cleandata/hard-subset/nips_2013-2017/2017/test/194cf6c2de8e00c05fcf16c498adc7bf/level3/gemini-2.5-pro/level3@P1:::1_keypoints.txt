- Proposes EYMOL, a novel variational model framing scanpath generation via a Principle of Least Action with Euler–Lagrange dynamics.  
- Lagrangian combines kinetic energy and a generalized potential with three terms: boundedness, curiosity (image-gradient attraction), and brightness invariance (optical-flow constraint).  
- Produces scanpaths as primary output and derives saliency maps by aggregating simulated fixations.  
- Evaluated on image (MIT1003, CAT2000, MIT300) and video (SFU) saliency datasets with competitive but not state-of-the-art results.  
- Strength: original, principled, and elegant mathematical framework replacing heuristic fixation-generation schemes.  
- Strength: unified account of saccadic exploration and fixation/tracking via the brightness invariance term.  
- Strength: focuses on dynamics and generative scanpath trajectories rather than only static saliency maps.  
- Weakness: the chosen potential terms (especially the sinusoidal “curiosity” modulation) appear ad hoc and lack strong justification.  
- Weakness: insufficient connection to existing variational/optimal-control literature in motor control and biological movement.  
- Weakness: evaluation is mismatched to claims — no qualitative or quantitative analysis of generated scanpath dynamics (e.g., saccade lengths, fixation durations, angles).  
- Weakness: saliency benchmark performance lags behind modern deep-learning approaches and should be framed as theoretical rather than SOTA benchmarking.  
- Accept arguments: introduces a principled, generative, and conceptually advanced model of eye-movement dynamics worthy of interest.  
- Reject arguments: model foundations need stronger motivation, related-work grounding is weak, and empirical validation of scanpath claims is critically lacking.  
- Overall: promising and creative framework that requires stronger theoretical justification and direct empirical validation of scanpath dynamics before publication.
