- Frames scanpath generation as a variational problem using a generalized Least Action Principle.
- Constructs a Lagrangian with kinetic energy plus a potential combining boundary constraint, curiosity (image-gradient attraction, local + peripheral), and brightness-invariance (optical-flow-like) terms.
- Derives Euler–Lagrange differential equations to govern attention trajectories; produces saliency maps by aggregating simulated trajectories.
- Distinct from classic feature-integration and modern deep saliency models by modelling dynamics rather than directly computing static saliency maps.
- Mathematical derivation is detailed and appears technically sound; presents a complete system from theory to experiments.
- Experimental validation is weak: evaluation relies almost exclusively on static saliency metrics (AUC, NSS) despite claiming a dynamic scanpath model.
- Performance is competitive with older methods but not with recent deep learning models, and this shortfall is insufficiently discussed.
- Major mismatch between contribution and evaluation: no analyses of generated scanpath dynamics (e.g., saccade amplitudes, fixation durations, scanpath similarity to humans).
- Paper is well-organized and readable, but core principles (especially the oscillatory curiosity term) feel ad hoc and under-motivated.
- Lacks visualizations of generated scanpaths in the main text; needs example trajectories on images/videos for qualitative validation.
- Highly original and elegant physics-inspired approach that unifies saccadic exploration and tracking within one framework.
- Fails to cite or relate to extensive prior work on optimal control/variational models of motor behavior and eye movements (e.g., Harris & Wolpert), missing important context.
- Potentially significant as an interpretable alternative to black-box models, but impact is limited by weak motivation, missing literature connections, and insufficient empirical support for dynamics.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel variational framework, unified model for saccades and tracking, principled alternative to data-driven methods.
- Arguments for rejection: ad hoc core terms, lack of connection to motor-control literature, mismatched and weak experimental evaluation, and non–state-of-the-art benchmark performance.
- Conclusion: interesting and original idea that requires stronger theoretical/biological motivation, proper literature positioning, and dynamic-focused experiments to be a complete scientific contribution.
