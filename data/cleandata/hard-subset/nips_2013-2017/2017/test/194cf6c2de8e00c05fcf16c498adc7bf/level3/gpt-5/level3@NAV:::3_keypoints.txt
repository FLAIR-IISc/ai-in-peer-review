- Proposes EYMOL, a variational framework modeling eye-movement scanpaths as particle trajectories from Euler–Lagrange equations derived from a generalized Least Action Principle.
- Contrasts with saliency-map paradigms by explicitly modeling scanpaths rather than using winner-take-all selection and heuristics for fixation sequencing.
- Lagrangian combines three terms: (i) boundary potential, (ii) time-alternating curiosity driven by local and peripheral gradients, and (iii) brightness-invariance tied to optical-flow for fixation/tracking.
- Saliency maps are obtained as a byproduct by aggregating visited locations across multiple trajectory runs.
- Evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU videos against multiple baselines (including CNN models); results are generally favorable but not uniformly state-of-the-art.
- Approach is coherent and experiments are sound for saliency benchmarks, but some mathematical derivations (especially in the appendix) are hard to verify for non-physicists.
- Stability constraints (e.g., λ < m/4), parameter sensitivity, and numerical robustness are discussed but need deeper empirical probing.
- Use of center bias and post-hoc blur tuning may inflate metrics; ablation studies are needed to validate contributions.
- Paper is well-organized and provides useful intuition for each term, aiding clarity.
- Needs clearer specification of annealing schedules, parameter ranges, and initial-condition distributions (center initialization introduces bias).
- Recommends summarizing key mathematical assumptions in the main text for accessibility.
- Modeling gaze as a variational trajectory system is novel within the community and distinct from heuristic scanpath simulators and deep saliency predictors.
- Unifying fixation and tracking via a brightness-invariance term is an interesting and original idea.
- Potentially impactful for vision science and computational neuroscience; may inspire scanpath-focused empirical work.
- For NeurIPS, significance is borderline because empirical gains are not dominant and the contribution skews toward perception science rather than core ML.
- Pros: conceptually novel trajectory formulation, broad evaluation on standard datasets, and potential to catalyze scanpath-level research.
- Cons: limited dominance on benchmarks, reliance on center-bias/blur tuning, heavy math that’s not fully accessible, no scanpath-level comparisons to human trajectories, and small video evaluation set.
- Suggested additions: ablations (remove brightness term; local vs peripheral; with/without center bias), sensitivity and stability analyses, scanpath-similarity metrics versus human data, and public release of code and parameters for reproducibility.
