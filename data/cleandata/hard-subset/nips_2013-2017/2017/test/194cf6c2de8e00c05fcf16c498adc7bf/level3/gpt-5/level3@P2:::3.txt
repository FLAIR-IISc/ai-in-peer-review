Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a dynamical, physics-inspired model of visual attention (EYMOL) that directly generates gaze trajectories as solutions of Euler–Lagrange equations derived from a generalized least-action principle. The Lagrangian uses: (i) a boundary potential that keeps trajectories within the “retina,” (ii) a “curiosity” term that attracts gaze to detailed regions (high brightness gradients) while alternating local versus peripheral gradients via a cos^2/sin^2 modulation, and (iii) a brightness-invariance term motivated by the optical-flow constraint (favoring fixation and motion tracking). The resulting second-order ODEs define continuous-time scanpaths; saliency maps are produced as a byproduct by counting spatial visitations across multiple simulated runs.

Relative to classical saliency-map pipelines (e.g., Koch & Ullman; Itti & Koch; information maximization; surprise; and more recent CNN-based models), this work does not optimize a saliency map or a fixation-selection policy. Instead, it derives scanpaths from a small set of physical principles expressed variationally. The paper evaluates two instantiations (exact and approximate brightness invariance) on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video. Results are competitive with several baselines across multiple metrics, though not uniformly dominant.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivation is careful and the appendix provides the required Euler–Lagrange details and well-posedness condition (e.g., λ < m/4). While some mathematical steps are advanced (and may stretch readers without a mechanics background), the core modeling choices and their computational implementation are clear. The simulated annealing procedure for parameter selection is reasonable, with search bounded for stability.
- Empirical support: Image and video benchmarks are standard. The paper reports that the approximate and exact formulations perform similarly on static images, supporting the approximation. The comparisons suggest the method is competitive. However, several practical choices (explicit center bias via Gaussian modulation, near-center initialization, grid-search blur tuning) risk inflating certain metrics (e.g., AUC-Judd, NSS) and make it difficult to isolate what the dynamical model contributes beyond bias and blur. Statistical significance and ablation analyses (e.g., removing center bias, turning off curiosity vs brightness-invariance terms, replacing peripheral alternation with a constant weight) would strengthen claims.
- Reproducibility: The integration is standard (SciPy ODE solver). The paper would benefit from listing exact parameter values (m, k, ω, λ, ηb, ηp), the domains explored by SA, blur/kernel sizes, and center-bias σ chosen after grid search, along with runtime and step-size details. Code availability would help.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally well written and organized. The high-level intuition is clear, and the mathematical development—including the role of the velocity-dependent brightness term—is explicit in the appendix. Suggestions for clarity:
- Move more intuition for each term into the main text; briefly describe how the alternating local/peripheral gradient modulation avoids texture trapping.
- Clearly separate dataset-driven center bias from model dynamics; explain the effect of near-center initialization versus the multiplicative center prior.
- Clarify how parameters were tuned across datasets to avoid inadvertent leakage (e.g., tuning on CAT2000 then evaluating on MIT300).
- Expand on numerical stability choices (step sizes, handling of boundary conditions) and report sensitivity to parameter changes.

Originality
Formulating visual attention and scanpaths as least-action dynamics with an explicit velocity-dependent brightness-invariance term is original and thought-provoking. Prior saccadic/attentional models have used winner-take-all selection, inhibition of return, or probabilistic policies and, more recently, deep regression to saliency maps. Casting fixation and tracking within a single variational principle is a novel contribution. To contextualize further, it would help to discuss related dynamical systems approaches to attention/oculomotor control and deep saliency models that incorporate strong priors (e.g., DeepGaze II), highlighting differences.

Significance
The proposed paradigm could influence future theoretical work in vision science by offering a compact, interpretable dynamical alternative to saliency-map optimization. For NIPS, the significance is moderate: results are competitive but not clearly state of the art on the strongest benchmarks, and the contribution is more modeling/theoretical than algorithmic/learning-centric. Nonetheless, the unification of saccades and tracking through a single Lagrangian is conceptually appealing and may spur empirical and computational follow-ups.

Pros
- Elegant, unified variational formulation of scanpaths with intuitive terms (curiosity and brightness invariance).
- Competitive performance on standard image and video datasets; approximate and exact formulations agree on static images.
- Produces scanpaths directly; saliency maps arise naturally from trajectory statistics.
- Small number of interpretable parameters, real-time feasibility, and clear theoretical constraints.

Cons
- Confounding effects of explicit center bias and center initialization on evaluation metrics; limited ablation to isolate core mechanisms.
- Limited comparison to strongest contemporary deep saliency models and metrics robust to center bias (e.g., shuffled AUC, Information Gain).
- No scanpath-level behavioral validation (e.g., saccade amplitude distributions, MultiMatch, Levenshtein-based scanpath similarity).
- Some mathematical and numerical details may be challenging for general NIPS readership; clearer guidance and parameter reporting would help.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please report exact parameter settings (m, k, ω, λ, ηb, ηp) selected by SA and the final blur/center-bias parameters; include ranges explored and sensitivity.
- Provide ablations: (i) without center prior, (ii) without near-center initialization, (iii) curiosity only vs brightness-invariance only, and (iv) fixed vs alternating local/peripheral terms. Report metrics including shuffled AUC and Information Gain to control for dataset biases.
- Include comparisons to top-performing deep models on MIT300 (e.g., DeepGaze II), and discuss where EYMOL wins/loses.
- Add scanpath analyses (e.g., saccade length distributions, direction biases, fixation duration proxies, and MultiMatch) to support the claim of modeling eye movements rather than only saliency.
- Discuss robustness to noise, parameter variation, and highly textured scenes; show failure cases.
- Consider alternative boundary conditions (e.g., reflective) and report their effect.

Overall recommendation
Borderline. The paper presents a novel and elegant dynamical formulation that is likely to interest the vision science and computational neuroscience communities. Empirically, it is competitive but not clearly superior to the strongest baselines, and some evaluation choices conflate center bias with model dynamics. With additional ablations, stronger baselines, and scanpath-level validation, this could be a strong contribution. As is, I lean slightly against acceptance for NIPS, but I would not object to acceptance given the conceptual novelty.

Reviewer confidence: moderate.
