- Paper proposes a novel variational model of visual attention using a generalized Least Action Principle to drive saccadic trajectories.  
- Model components: boundedness (harmonic oscillator), curiosity (multi-scale brightness gradients), and brightness invariance (fixation).  
- Leads to differential equations governing attention trajectories and uses simulated annealing for parameter estimation.  
- Experimental results show competitive performance on standard static and dynamic saliency datasets.  
- Novelty: physics-based Least Action framing is a fresh departure from saliency-map–centric approaches.  
- Unified framework: integrates boundedness, curiosity, and fixation within a single mathematical model.  
- Strong theoretical foundation: derivation of governing differential equations enables further analysis and extensions.  
- Potential for insight: offers a tool to investigate mechanisms of visual attention and perceptual processes.  
- Clarity/readability issues: dense writing and many unexplained variables make the model hard to follow.  
- Insufficient elaboration on the rationale and implications of using the Least Action Principle.  
- Limited psychological validation: lacks comparisons to established eye-movement theories (e.g., information gain/optimal search).  
- Parameter robustness concerns: reliance on simulated annealing without sensitivity analysis undermines confidence in results.  
- Appendix overload: lengthy derivations should be moved to supplementary material to improve main-text clarity.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel theory, competitive empirical performance, and potential scientific contribution.  
- Rejection arguments: poor clarity, weak explanations and psychological linkage, and robustness concerns.  
- Overall recommendation: reject with request for major revisions—improve clarity, expand theoretical/psychological discussion, add sensitivity analysis, and relocate appendix material.
