This paper proposes EYMOL, a variational framework for modeling eye-movement trajectories as solutions to Euler–Lagrange equations derived from a generalized Least Action Principle. In contrast to the dominant saliency-map paradigm with winner-take-all selection and heuristics for fixation sequencing, EYMOL explicitly models scanpaths as the motion of a particle under a Lagrangian combining: (i) a boundary potential, (ii) a curiosity term driven by local and peripheral gradients that alternates over time, and (iii) a brightness-invariance term tied to optical-flow constraints for fixation/tracking. Saliency maps are then obtained as a byproduct by aggregating visited locations over multiple runs. The paper evaluates on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU videos against several baselines (including CNN-based models). Results are generally favorable though not uniformly state-of-the-art.

Quality: The approach is coherent and the experiments are soundly executed at the level of saliency benchmarks; however, some mathematical derivations (especially the appendix) are beyond my physics background to fully verify. The role of stability constraints (e.g., λ < m/4), parameter sensitivity, and numerical robustness is discussed but could be probed more deeply. The use of center bias and post-hoc blur tuning may inflate some metrics; ablations would strengthen claims.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly contrasts with prior saliency-map models. The intuition behind each term is helpful. I recommend clarifying the exact annealing schedule, parameter ranges, and initial-condition distributions (center-initialization adds bias), and summarizing the key mathematical assumptions in the main text.

Originality: Modeling gaze as a variational trajectory system is novel in this community and distinct from both heuristic scanpath simulators and deep saliency predictors. The unification of fixation and tracking via brightness invariance is particularly interesting.

Significance: The work is likely impactful for vision science and computational neuroscience and could inspire new empirical studies of scanpaths. For NeurIPS, the empirical gains are not dominant and the scope may feel more perception-science than core ML; I defer to domain experts but view significance for NeurIPS as borderline.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually novel trajectory-based formulation with clear departure from saliency-map pipelines.
- Broad evaluation on standard datasets; competitive results.
- Potential to catalyze scanpath-level research beyond static saliency.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited dominance on benchmarks; reliance on center-bias/blur tuning.
- Math heavy; some derivations and stability analysis not fully accessible.
- No scanpath-level comparison to human trajectories; small video set.

Suggestions: Add ablations (remove brightness term; local vs peripheral; with/without center bias), sensitivity/stability analysis, scanpath-similarity metrics against human data, and release code/parameters for reproducibility.
