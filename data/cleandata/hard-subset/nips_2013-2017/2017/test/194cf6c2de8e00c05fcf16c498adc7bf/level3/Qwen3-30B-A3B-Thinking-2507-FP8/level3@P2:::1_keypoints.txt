- Paper proposes a novel theoretical framework applying a generalized Least Action Principle to model visual attention and derive scanpath dynamics.
- Introduces two "affordances" (curiosity for detail and brightness invariance) to derive differential equations governing attention.
- Produces saliency maps as a byproduct of simulated attention dynamics rather than optimizing maps directly.
- Evaluated on MIT1003, CAT2000, SFU datasets and shows competitive but modest results compared to baselines.
- Builds on classic saliency literature but diverges by focusing on scanpath dynamics instead of static maps.
- Lacks thorough connection to substantial eye-movement and optimal-control literature (e.g., Wolpert’s work).
- Theoretical formulation is elegant and mathematically sound; Euler–Lagrange derivation and parameter estimation are appropriate.
- Implementation details are clear, computationally efficient, and can run in real-time.
- Major weakness: the two affordances lack sufficient motivation and theoretical/neuroscientific grounding.
- Empirical evaluation is weak: performance falls short of state-of-the-art deep models and is reported only via overall saliency metrics.
- Critical omission: no visualizations or quantitative comparisons of the model’s predicted scanpaths to human fixations.
- Biological plausibility is unaddressed; no evidence that derived equations align with neural mechanisms or behavioral data.
- Paper is well-written with a useful appendix for replication, but the claimed significance is not convincingly demonstrated.
- Arguments for acceptance: elegant theory, scanpath-focused modeling, efficient implementation, clear theoretical contribution.
- Arguments against acceptance: poorly motivated affordances, inadequate empirical validation of dynamics, lower performance than SOTA, missing scanpath figures, and lack of biological grounding.
- Recommendation: strengthen links to eye-movement/optimal-control literature, provide scanpath visualizations and behavioral comparisons, and better justify affordances.
