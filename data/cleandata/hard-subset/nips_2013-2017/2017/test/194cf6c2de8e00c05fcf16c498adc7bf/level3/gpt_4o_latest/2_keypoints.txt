- Proposes a novel physics- and cognitive-science-inspired model for predicting eye movements using harmonic oscillator constraints, curiosity-driven attention to brightness changes, and brightness invariance.  
- Formulates these principles in a variational framework (Least Action Principle) and derives differential equations to model attentional scanpaths.  
- Evaluated on human eye-movement datasets and shows competitive performance with state-of-the-art saliency detection methods.  
- Originality: introduces a refreshing, non-ML approach by leveraging physics principles for visual attention modeling.  
- Unified framework: combines curiosity-driven exploration and brightness invariance to explain saccades and object tracking coherently.  
- Experimental validation: achieves competitive results on multiple datasets (MIT1003, CAT2000, SFU) despite model simplicity.  
- Potential impact: could spur interdisciplinary research linking computational vision, cognitive science, and physics.  
- Clarity issues: paper is poorly written with unclear equations, inconsistent abbreviations, and dense Euler–Lagrange derivations lacking intuitive explanation.  
- Contextualization gap: insufficient connection to relevant psychological studies (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler) limiting cognitive-science relevance.  
- Limited evaluation scope: focuses mainly on saliency maps, not temporal scanpath dynamics, and is not best across all metrics.  
- Parameter tuning concerns: reliance on simulated annealing raises scalability and generalizability issues for larger datasets or real-time use.  
- Suggested fixes: improve manuscript clarity and mathematical explanations; discuss related psychological literature; include temporal scanpath comparisons; adopt more efficient parameter optimization.  
- Recommendation: accept with major revisions contingent on addressing clarity problems and strengthening literature contextualization.
