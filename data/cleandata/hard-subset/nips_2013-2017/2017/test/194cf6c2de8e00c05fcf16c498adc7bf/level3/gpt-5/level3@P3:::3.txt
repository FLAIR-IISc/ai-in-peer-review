Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes EYMOL, a physics-inspired model of eye movements that predicts gaze trajectories directly, rather than computing a saliency map and then selecting fixations via a winner-take-all/inhibition-of-return scheme. The model posits that scanpaths result from a Least Action Principle: the Lagrangian combines (i) a bounding potential to keep gaze within the image frame, (ii) a curiosity term that alternates between local detail (brightness gradient) and a peripheral, blurred version of the image, and (iii) a brightness-invariance term analogous to the optical-flow brightness constancy constraint, which encourages fixation and motion tracking. The Euler–Lagrange equations yield second-order ODEs for gaze dynamics. Parameters are fit via simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a subset of CAT2000. Saliency maps are produced post hoc by aggregating visited locations across many simulated trajectories; for static images, an approximate brightness-invariance term performs as well as the exact version.

Compared to most prior saliency models (Koch-Ullman/Itti-Koch, AIM/information maximization, Bayesian surprise, learned CNN-based predictors), EYMOL’s novelty is to model the full trajectory as a dynamical system, with saliency maps emerging as a byproduct. The paper evaluates on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU videos, reporting competitive scores against representative baselines, though not best on every metric.

I am not an expert in analytic mechanics; some derivations (especially in the appendix) are beyond my physics background to fully evaluate. That said, the main idea, assumptions, and how the approach differs from conventional saliency-map pipelines are clear.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The variational formulation is coherent and the connection between brightness invariance and fixation/tracking is well-motivated. The derivations appear careful; conditions on parameters (e.g., λ for well-posedness) are discussed.
- Empirical support: The model is tested on standard fixation datasets, with sensible metrics (AUC-Judd, NSS, etc.) and baseline comparisons. Results are favorable overall and confirm that the approach is viable, though not dominant across all metrics or datasets.
- Limitations: Evaluation focuses on aggregate saliency maps rather than the core promise—predicting scanpath dynamics. There is no quantitative scanpath comparison to human trajectories (e.g., MultiMatch, ScanMatch, string-edit distance, saccade amplitude/angle distributions). The model introduces explicit center bias and post-hoc blurring tuned via grid search, which can inflate AUC/NSS; ablations of these priors are needed. Parameter fitting uses a 100-image CAT2000 subset; generalization and sensitivity to initialization, annealing settings, and ω are not analyzed. For videos, the dataset (SFU) is relatively small, and there is limited analysis of cases where brightness constancy fails.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with an informative introduction and clear motivation for each Lagrangian term. Some mathematical details (e.g., constants in the kinetic energy expression, determinant simplifications) would benefit from careful proofreading. For reproducibility, please clarify integrator choice and step size, boundary handling, precise parameter ranges, ω, initialization distributions, and whether code will be released.

Originality
Modeling gaze as a variational dynamical system is distinct from the saliency-map paradigm and plausibly unifies saccades and tracking within one framework. While dynamical models of saccades exist, the least-action formulation and the specific combination of curiosity and brightness-invariance terms constitute a novel theory.

Significance
The work is likely to interest the vision science/perception community (e.g., VSS attendees) and may stimulate empirical studies linking physical principles to eye-movement behavior. For NeurIPS, the broader significance is less clear: empirical gains over strong learned saliency models are limited, and the paper stops short of validating trajectory realism or top-down effects. Nonetheless, the conceptual contribution could seed cross-disciplinary follow-ups.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation that directly produces scanpaths; saliency emerges naturally.
- Unifying treatment of saccades and tracking via brightness invariance.
- Competitive results on standard image and video datasets despite model simplicity.
- Real-time applicability and interpretable components.
- Solid and succinct review of related work; position relative to saliency-map models is clear.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Evaluation emphasizes saliency maps, not scanpath dynamics; no trajectory-level human comparison.
- Center bias and blur are tuned to benchmarks; fairness and ablations are missing.
- Limited analysis of parameter sensitivity, stability, and robustness to initial conditions.
- Small video benchmark; no test of violations of brightness constancy (illumination changes).
- Reproducibility details (integrator, ω, code) not fully specified; minor typos in math.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to ω and to the relative weights ηb/ηp/λ? Please provide ablation curves and stability ranges.
- What is the impact of removing learned center bias and blur on each metric?
- Can you report scanpath-level metrics against human trajectories?
- How does the model behave under dynamic luminance changes where brightness constancy is violated?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add trajectory-level evaluations and human-comparison metrics; analyze saccade amplitude, inter-saccade intervals, and turning angles.
- Provide ablations: no center bias, no blur, and single-term knockouts (no B, no C, no V).
- Expand video evaluation and include dynamic benchmarks with diverse motion/illumination.
- Discuss top-down/task effects and how they could be incorporated (e.g., as additional potentials).
- Release code and parameter settings; specify numerical integration details.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel theoretical framing that directly models gaze trajectories.
- Conceptually unifies key behaviors (fixation, saccades, tracking) in one interpretable system.
- Competitive empirical results without heavy learning machinery; potential to inspire cross-disciplinary research.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited demonstration of the model’s central claim (trajectory realism) and modest gains vs. strong saliency baselines.
- Dependence on benchmark-tuned priors (center bias, blur) and small video evaluation.
- Reproducibility and sensitivity analyses are incomplete.

Overall assessment
A creative and thoughtful theoretical contribution with encouraging empirical validation. I find it of clear interest to vision/perception researchers. For NeurIPS, I am borderline on significance given the current evaluation emphasis and missing trajectory analyses, and would defer to more expert reviewers on the derivational rigor. With stronger scanpath validation and ablations, this could be a compelling acceptance.
