- Paper proposes a scanpath model of visual attention derived from a generalized Least Action Principle, framing eye movements as variational laws.

- The model uses a Lagrangian with kinetic energy and a generalized potential comprising boundary confinement, a "curiosity" term (attraction to high-detail regions, oscillating between scales), and a brightness-invariance term for fixation/tracking.

- Euler-Lagrange equations from the action functional yield trajectory dynamics; saliency maps are produced by aggregating visited locations.

- Strength: Highly original and elegant principled formulation that unifies saccades and smooth pursuit within a single variational framework.

- Strength: Focuses on scanpath dynamics rather than static fixation prediction, addressing a fundamental aspect of visual attention.

- Strength: Model is interpretable—the driving factors are explicit terms in the Lagrangian, unlike black-box deep models.

- Weakness: Formulation of principles (e.g., oscillatory curiosity term, brightness-invariance form) is somewhat axiomatic and lacks strong cognitive/neuroscientific justification.

- Weakness: Experimental evaluation focuses on aggregate saliency maps rather than direct comparison of generated trajectories to human scanpath data (e.g., saccade amplitudes, fixation durations, velocities).

- Weakness: Important related work on variational principles and optimal motor control for eye movements is not adequately cited or discussed.

- Weakness: Benchmark performance is competitive with classical methods but not state-of-the-art versus recent deep learning approaches, questioning practical utility.

- Argument for acceptance: Introduces a novel, interpretable, first-principles framework moving the field toward dynamic modeling of attention.

- Argument for acceptance: Offers a unified treatment of saccades and tracking that serves as a principled alternative to deep learning.

- Argument against acceptance: Experimental validation does not substantiate claims about dynamics and lacks trajectory-level analyses.

- Argument against acceptance: Model principles are insufficiently grounded in existing cognitive/neuroscientific and motor-control literature.

- Argument against acceptance: No clear performance advantage on benchmarks, leaving practical relevance unclear.
