This computational neuroscience paper proposes a bottom-up visual feature saliency model, modified by dynamical systems modelling, that aims to capture the dynamics of scanpaths. I enjoyed reading this paper, which presents a set of nice ideas in defining "affordances" (curiosity, brightness invariance) that drive the scan-path, as well as the idea of the Least Action Principle. Although these "affordances" are perhaps a bit ad hoc and not further motivated, they are an interesting contribution.

In the context of the Least Action Principle and its derivation, it would have been interesting to relate it to actual work in eye movement research, such as past and recent work by the Wolpert lab. While the lack of biological motivation may not be a significant issue in the era of benchmark-busting deep learning models of static visual salience, the model's mixed performance with respect to these models is a concern. Given that the model claims to capture biological eye movement dynamics, it would have been interesting to see actual figures characterising the generated scan paths in the main paper, not only in terms of their overall statistics at matching eye movements, but also in terms of their temporal dynamics.
