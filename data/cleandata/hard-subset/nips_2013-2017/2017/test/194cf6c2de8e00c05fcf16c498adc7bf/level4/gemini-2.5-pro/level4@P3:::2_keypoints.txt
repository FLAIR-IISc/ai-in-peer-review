- Proposes a first-principles variational (least-action) model that generates scanpaths by solving Euler–Lagrange equations from a Lagrangian with kinetic and three potential terms (bounding, multi-scale curiosity to high spatial gradients, brightness invariance); saliency maps arise from aggregated simulated scanpaths and parameters tuned via simulated annealing, with competitive results on standard image/video benchmarks.

- Originality: novel, elegant framing of visual attention dynamics via calculus of variations rather than feature engineering or deep nets.

- Theoretical foundation: principled physics-based model unifying saccades (curiosity) and fixation/tracking (brightness invariance) within one formalism.

- Performance: despite simplicity, achieves competitive performance on multiple saliency datasets for static and dynamic scenes.

- Clarity and presentation: manuscript is difficult to follow, with grammatical issues, dense math, and many undefined variables that impede comprehension.

- Cognitive connection: insufficient linkage to established psychological and neuroscientific theories of attention (e.g., optimal foraging/information maximization).

- Improve writing and variable definitions; add intuitive motivations for each Lagrangian term and have the paper proofread for English.

- Restructure by moving derivations to supplementary material and use main text space for clearer model walkthrough, parameter rationale, and expanded results discussion.

- Explicitly connect and compare the proposed principles to psychological literature (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2008) to situate the model relative to optimal search/information-gain theories.

- Clarify experimental details such as what "1 second of visual exploration" means in integration steps and briefly define evaluation metrics/abbreviations (AUC-Judd, NSS) in the results.
