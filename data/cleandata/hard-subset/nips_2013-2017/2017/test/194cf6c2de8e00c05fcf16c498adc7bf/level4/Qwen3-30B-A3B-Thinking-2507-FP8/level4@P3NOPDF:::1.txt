

This paper proposes a dynamical systems-based model for predicting visual scanpaths, integrating bottom-up feature saliency with novel "affordances" (curiosity, brightness invariance) and the Least Action Principle (LAP). While the core ideas present an intriguing theoretical framework for modeling eye movement dynamics, significant concerns regarding motivation, biological relevance, and empirical validation limit the paper's contribution.

The most compelling aspects are the conceptualization of "affordances" and the application of LAP to scanpath generation. The use of curiosity-driven exploration and brightness invariance as driving forces for attentional shifts is novel and potentially insightful for modeling adaptive visual exploration. Similarly, framing scanpath optimization through LAP offers a principled alternative to heuristic motion models. However, these concepts are introduced with insufficient theoretical grounding. The justification for selecting these specific affordances lacks clear connection to established cognitive or neurobiological principles. For instance, the role of "curiosity" as a formalized affordance requires stronger motivation beyond its intuitive appeal—how does it relate to known neural mechanisms of exploration (e.g., dopamine signaling, hippocampal novelty detection)? Similarly, the derivation of the LAP in this context appears disconnected from prior work in computational vision or oculomotor control. A critical omission is the failure to engage with seminal work on the physical and computational principles underlying eye movements, particularly the extensive research by the Wolpert lab on optimal control and motor planning. Explicitly linking the LAP derivation to these foundational concepts would have significantly strengthened the model's theoretical basis and positioned it within the broader literature.

The paper's primary limitation, however, is its empirical performance relative to state-of-the-art benchmarks. The model claims to capture *biological* eye movement dynamics, yet its quantitative results are mixed and, crucially, inferior to current deep learning models for *static* visual salience (e.g., models like SaliencyNet, ITTI, or deep learning baselines from the 2017 era). This is a significant issue. If the model cannot reliably predict static salient locations—where most benchmark salience models excel—it undermines its core premise of modeling biological visual processing. The paper reports "mixed performance" without providing clear comparative metrics (e.g., AUC, NSS, CC) against relevant baselines. More importantly, the authors do not demonstrate *why* their dynamical model should outperform static salience predictors for scanpath prediction. The lack of strong empirical validation against the very benchmarks it claims to improve upon is a major weakness.

Furthermore, the paper fails to provide adequate evidence for its central claim of capturing *biological dynamics*. The review of scanpath characteristics is superficial. The main text relies on static metrics (e.g., fixation density maps) without presenting actual, visually comparable scanpaths generated by the model versus human data. Figures showing example scanpaths (e.g., overlaid on images with human and model paths) are essential to demonstrate the *dynamics*—not just the endpoint distribution—of the model's predictions. Without such figures, it is impossible to assess whether the model reproduces key biological properties like saccade amplitude distributions, fixation durations, or sequence dependencies. The supplementary material (if it exists) should not substitute for this core validation in the main paper. The current presentation leaves the reader unable to judge whether the model genuinely captures the temporal structure of eye movements or merely produces plausible-looking fixation maps.

The paper's focus on dynamical modeling also creates a tension with its evaluation. While the model is presented as a *dynamical* system, the evaluation metrics primarily assess static salience properties (e.g., fixation count maps), which are insufficient for validating dynamics. To credibly claim biological relevance for scanpath *dynamics*, the authors must evaluate metrics directly measuring temporal sequence fidelity (e.g., saccade sequence statistics, dwell time distributions, path smoothness, or predictive accuracy of the next fixation location). The absence of these metrics is a critical gap.

In summary, the paper offers an interesting theoretical angle on scanpath modeling through affordances and LAP, but this potential is undermined by weak theoretical motivation, poor empirical performance against relevant baselines, and inadequate validation of the claimed biological dynamics. The model's performance on standard salience benchmarks is not competitive, and the lack of visual evidence for scanpath dynamics makes it difficult to assess its core contribution. To strengthen the paper, the authors should: (1) provide a rigorous justification for the chosen affordances, linking them to cognitive/physiological evidence or prior computational models; (2) explicitly compare their dynamical model against the best-performing *static* salience models on standard metrics to establish a baseline; (3) include visual comparisons of generated scanpaths against human data, alongside quantitative metrics specific to scanpath dynamics (e.g., saccade amplitude distributions, sequence entropy); and (4) engage deeply with the optimal control literature (e.g., Wolpert lab) to situate the LAP derivation.

As currently presented, the model's empirical shortcomings and lack of clear biological validation prevent it from making a strong contribution to the field of computational visual attention. The theoretical ideas are promising but require significantly more substantiation to support the claims made. The paper would benefit greatly from a more focused evaluation centered on validating the *dynamics* it purports to model, rather than relying on static salience metrics alone.
