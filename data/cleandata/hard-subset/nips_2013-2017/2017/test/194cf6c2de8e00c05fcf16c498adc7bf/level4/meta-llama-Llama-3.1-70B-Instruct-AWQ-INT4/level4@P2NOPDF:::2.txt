Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes

The authors present a novel approach to understanding visual attention in static and dynamic images by deriving a model based on three fundamental principles. These principles are defined as an energy function that is minimized by the movements of the eye: (1) a harmonic oscillator constraint at the image borders within a limited-sized retina, (2) a "curiosity-driven principle" that highlights regions with large changes in brightness in both fine and blurred versions of the image, and (3) brightness invariance, which increases as a function of changes in brightness. By combining these principles into a cost function, the authors derive differential equations to predict eye movements across static or dynamic images, taking into account the starting location and initial velocity.

The authors evaluate their technique quantitatively on datasets of static and dynamic scenes, along with human eye movements, and demonstrate comparable performance to state-of-the-art methods.

The formal definitions of saliency and modeling eye movements are critical issues in computational vision and cognitive science. Psychologists have long struggled with vague definitions of saliency, and the authors propose a novel and innovative model that could aid in developing a better understanding of what makes something salient and provide a formal model for eye movements within the bottom-up tradition. Although the method may not be state-of-the-art on every metric for every dataset, it performs well and offers a refreshingly different perspective on the problem.

However, the paper's poor writing and lack of clarity make it challenging to follow. I recommend that the authors have others proofread the paper and expand on abbreviations, both within the equations and in Section 3. Additionally, I suggest moving the 2-page Appendix to supplementary material and utilizing the extra pages to define each variable and function used, even if they are conventions within the field. This would facilitate understanding for the NIPS audience, which comes from diverse disciplines.

As a psychologist, I would have appreciated the authors connecting their work to the psychological literature on eye movements as optimal steps for gathering information. For example, Najemnik and Geisler (2008) demonstrate that eye movement statistics in humans are consistent with an optimal search strategy. The authors could have discussed how their approach compares to these findings, whether they are restatements of similar models or provide independent information that could be integrated to produce a better model.
