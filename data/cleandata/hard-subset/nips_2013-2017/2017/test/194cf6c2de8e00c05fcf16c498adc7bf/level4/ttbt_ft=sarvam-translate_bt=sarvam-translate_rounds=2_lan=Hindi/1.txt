This computational neuroscience paper proposes a bottom-up visual feature prominence model, modified by dynamic systems modeling, aimed at capturing the dynamics of the scanpath. I enjoyed reading this paper because it contains a good set of ideas for defining "affordance" (the ability to perceive or respond to a stimulus) that drives the scanpath, as well as the idea of a minimal action principle. These "affordances" are perhaps considered a bit ad hoc and not further motivated.

In this context, regarding the minimal action principle and its derivation, it would be interesting to connect it to real-world work in eye-movement research, such as past and recent work by the Volper Lab. While the lack of biological motivation is, in itself, a minor issue in the era of benchmark-busting deep learning models (static) visual significance, the issue with this model is that the mixed performance it achieves in relation to these models is poor. Given that the model claims to capture biological eye movements, it would be interesting to see real data demonstrating the generated scanpath in the main paper, not only in terms of their overall position but also in terms of matching eye movements.
