Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes EYMOL, a physics-inspired model of visual attention that generates scanpaths by minimizing a generalized action functional. The Lagrangian contains: (i) a confining potential to keep the trajectory within the image bounds; (ii) a “curiosity” term that favors regions with strong local gradients and a peripheral (blurred) gradient, alternated over time via cos^2/sin^2 modulation; and (iii) a brightness-invariance term (a soft version of the optical-flow constraint bt + bx·ẋ = 0) to encourage fixation and motion tracking. The stationary point of the action yields Euler–Lagrange equations that define attention dynamics. Parameters are fit with simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000. Saliency maps are obtained post hoc by counting trajectory visits over many runs. The model is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000 (images), and SFU (videos), and compared against several saliency models, including classic saliency maps and more recent learning-based methods. An approximate brightness term (upper bound) performs on par with the exact one for static images.

Relative to much prior work that computes a global saliency map followed by winner-take-all and post hoc heuristics (inhibition-of-return, proximity), this work directly models the dynamics of attention and produces saliency as a byproduct. This is aligned with dynamic/saccadic models (e.g., Le Meur & Liu), and with optimal control/active vision approaches that formulate gaze as the solution to a principled objective (e.g., information maximization, Bayesian surprise, or optimal search models such as Najemnik & Geisler). The variational mechanics framing is novel in this context and unifies saccadic movements and (some) tracking behavior within one set of equations.

Strengths:
- Conceptually elegant and unified: a compact set of principles yields a dynamical system that generates scanpaths; saliency maps emerge rather than being explicitly optimized.
- Covers both static and dynamic scenes, with a single framework and few parameters.
- Competitive empirical performance on standard benchmarks for several metrics, despite the model’s simplicity and lack of high-level features.
- Clear link to the optical-flow constraint for brightness invariance, offering a plausible mechanism for fixation/tracking behaviors.
- Theoretical attention to well-posedness (e.g., bounds on λ) and an interpretable role for each term.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
Quality/technical:
- The kinetic energy is written as K(ẋ) = (1/m) ẋ^2; traditionally it is (m/2)∥ẋ∥^2. If this is not a typo, please justify the scaling; otherwise correct it and propagate changes to the derivation and parameter interpretation.
- The time-varying curiosity modulation via cos^2(ωt)/sin^2(ωt) is ad hoc. How is ω chosen? Provide sensitivity analysis and ablations (constant weights; no alternation; local-only vs peripheral-only).
- The brightness-invariance approximation for static images is helpful computationally; include a formal discussion of when the bound is tight and its implications on the dynamics (it acts like a spatially varying velocity penalty).
- Stability/well-posedness: the bound λ < m/4 assumes b ∈ [0,1]; after Gaussian weighting and blurring, is this normalization guaranteed per dataset? Clarify units and rescaling to ensure D > 0 across images/videos.

Evaluation/design:
- Center bias and blur tuning: both b and p are multiplied by a central Gaussian, and additional blur and center-bias parameters are grid-searched to optimize AUC-Judd and NSS. This risks conflating model gains with generic priors known to boost certain metrics. Please report sAUC (shuffled AUC) and other center-bias–robust metrics, and include ablations without explicit center bias and without post hoc blur tuning.
- Initialization: runs are initialized “almost at the center.” Show results with broader/different initialization distributions and report sensitivity. Also report how many steps/saccades per second and whether multiple runs approximate inter-subject variability.
- Comparisons: Include more recent deep saliency baselines prevalent on MIT300/CAT2000 at the time, and ensure comparable post-processing across methods. Report statistical significance and variance across images/categories.
- Scanpath evaluation: since the main contribution is a dynamical scanpath model, it is important to evaluate temporal behavior directly (e.g., MultiMatch, ScanMatch, string-edit distance), not only heatmap metrics.
- Video: SFU has 12 clips; performance comparisons are suggestive but limited. Consider reporting dynamic-specific metrics (sAUC over time, tAUC) and qualitative analyses of claimed tracking behavior with quantitative tracking scores when possible.

Clarity and presentation:
- The manuscript needs editing for grammar and consistency (e.g., “psedo-code,” “the the model,” equation numbering, footnotes in text). Define all symbols at first use and keep notation consistent (e.g., gradients, dot products, vector vs scalar).
- Several references are incomplete; please provide full citations and include related dynamic/optimal-control/active inference literature.
- Provide intuition alongside derivations: short paragraphs explaining the physical meaning of each term, how forces interact, and example trajectories illustrating their effects. A component ablation figure would help.
- Reproducibility: specify all parameters (m, k, λ, ηb, ηp, ω), solver details (step size, integrator), preprocessing (normalizations), and release code and parameter files.

Originality:
- The variational mechanics framing for attention dynamics is novel and interesting. That said, please better situate it relative to existing dynamical/optimal control models of eye movements and active vision, and clarify how EYMOL differs in assumptions and predictions.

Significance:
- As a principled, interpretable alternative to black-box saliency predictors, this work could interest researchers in computational neuroscience and vision. The empirical performance is respectable given the model’s simplicity, and the ability to generate scanpaths is valuable. However, broader impact at NIPS would be strengthened by (i) stronger, bias-robust evaluations; (ii) direct scanpath metrics; and (iii) clearer theoretical grounding and ablations.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant, unified dynamical formulation with clear intuitions.
- Produces scanpaths and saliency as a byproduct; applicable to images and videos.
- Competitive results on several benchmarks despite simplicity.
- Potential to inspire further work at the intersection of physics-based modeling and attention.

Cons for acceptance:
- Empirical evaluation is confounded by center bias/blur tuning and limited in scanpath analysis.
- Some technical details (kinetic term scaling, parameter normalization, ω choice) are unclear.
- Writing/presentation and references require significant polishing.
- Limited comparison to the strongest contemporary baselines and limited video evaluation.

Overall:
This is a creative and promising direction with an appealing theoretical underpinning and encouraging results. With clearer derivations, stronger bias-robust and scanpath-centric evaluations, and improved presentation, it could make a solid contribution. In its current form, I view it as borderline for NIPS: interesting and novel, but in need of additional clarity and more rigorous evaluation to meet the bar.
