

The paper proposes a variational framework for modeling visual attention in static and dynamic scenes, grounded in three core principles: (1) a harmonic oscillator constraint limiting eye movements near image boundaries within a finite retina model; (2) a curiosity-driven mechanism prioritizing regions with significant brightness changes across both fine and blurred image scales; and (3) brightness invariance, which scales with local brightness variations. These principles jointly define an energy-minimizing cost function, yielding differential equations to predict eye movement trajectories. The authors validate their approach quantitatively on static and dynamic scene datasets with human fixation data, demonstrating competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods on most metrics.

This work addresses a fundamental challenge in computational vision and cognitive science: the need for formal, psychologically grounded models of saliency and eye movement dynamics. Historically, saliency has been defined ambiguously, hindering progress. The authors present a conceptually distinct framework that advances formal modeling within the bottom-up attention tradition. While not universally superior across all datasets or metrics, their model offers a compelling alternative perspective and strong empirical performance, suggesting significant potential for advancing understanding of visual attention mechanisms.

However, the paper’s execution undermines its contributions. The writing is frequently opaque, with inconsistent notation, unexplained abbreviations (e.g., in Section 3 and equations), and insufficient mathematical exposition. The 2-page Appendix should be moved to supplementary material to free space for essential definitions of all variables and functions. Given NIPS’ interdisciplinary audience, the authors must clarify conventions that may be familiar within their subfield but opaque to broader readers. A thorough proofread by multiple colleagues is strongly recommended to resolve these issues.

A critical gap lies in the lack of engagement with established psychological literature on the *functional purpose* of eye movements. The authors should explicitly connect their model to evidence that human eye movements optimize information gain during visual search. For instance, Najemnik & Geisler (2008) demonstrated that human eye movement statistics align with an optimal search strategy for gathering information efficiently. The paper would benefit significantly from a discussion addressing: How does the proposed energy-minimizing framework relate to or extend such optimal search models? Does it offer independent insights that could be integrated with existing theories of attentional control (e.g., from Geisler’s work), or does it merely reframe similar principles? This connection is essential for situating the work within cognitive science and justifying its broader significance beyond computational performance.

In summary, the paper presents a novel and promising theoretical framework for visual attention with strong empirical validation. Its core ideas—particularly the integration of harmonic constraints, multi-scale brightness change detection, and brightness invariance—offer a fresh perspective. However, the current presentation is inadequate for NIPS. The authors must substantially improve clarity through rigorous editing, expanded definitions, and explicit linkage to psychological theory on the *functional role* of eye movements. Addressing these points would transform a competent technical contribution into a significant advance for both computational vision and cognitive science. The work has potential but requires significant revision to meet NIPS standards.
