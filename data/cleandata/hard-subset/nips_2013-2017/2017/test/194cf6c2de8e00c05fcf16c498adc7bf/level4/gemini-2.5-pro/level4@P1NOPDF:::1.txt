This paper proposes a bottom-up visual saliency model that incorporates dynamical systems to capture the dynamics of human scanpaths. I enjoyed reading the paper, as it presents several interesting ideas. Particularly noteworthy are the concepts of "affordances" (such as curiosity and brightness invariance) used to drive the scanpath, and the application of the Principle of Least Action.

However, these "affordances" are introduced in a somewhat ad-hoc manner and lack sufficient motivation. In the context of the Principle of Least Action, it would have been valuable to connect its derivation and application to established work in eye movement research, such as the past and recent contributions from the Wolpert lab.

While a lack of strong biological motivation might be a minor issue in the current era of high-performing deep learning models for static visual saliency, the model presented here achieves poor performance relative to these state-of-the-art approaches. This underperformance is a significant concern.

Finally, given that the model's primary claim is to capture the dynamics of biological eye movements, it is surprising that the main paper lacks a detailed characterization of the generated scanpaths. The evaluation focuses on static metrics for matching eye movement locations, rather than analyzing the dynamic properties of the scanpaths themselves. Including figures and metrics that characterize these dynamics would be essential for validating the model's core contribution.
