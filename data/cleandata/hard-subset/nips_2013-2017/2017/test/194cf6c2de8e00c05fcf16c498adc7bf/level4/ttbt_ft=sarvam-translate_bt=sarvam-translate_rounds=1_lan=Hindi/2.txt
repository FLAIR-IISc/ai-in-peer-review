**Dynamic Visuals: Variable Rules of Visual Attention**

The authors investigate the locations where humans pay attention in static and dynamic images. They derive a model by defining three basic principles for visual attention (defined as an energy function to be minimized by eye movements): (1) Eye movements are constrained by a harmonic oscillator on the borders of the image within a limited size retina. (2) A "curiosity-driven theory" that highlights areas with large changes in brightness in both fine and blurred versions of the image, and (3) brightness constancy, which increases as a function of brightness changes. Using a cost function derived from these three functions, the authors obtain differential equations to predict eye movements in static or dynamic images (based on starting location and starting velocity). The authors quantitatively evaluate their technique on a data set of static and dynamic scenes paired with human eye movements. They demonstrate that their method performs at par with state-of-the-art technology.

Formal definitions of salience and modeling eye movements are important issues in computational vision and cognitive science. Psychologists have long been plagued by vague definitions of salience, and the authors propose a novel and innovative model (as far as I know) that may help develop a better understanding of what makes something salient and a formal model for eye movements (within the bottom-up tradition). While it is not state-of-the-art on every metric for every data set, it performs well and provides a fresh different perspective on the problem.

Unfortunately, some of what I have written above is based on assumption because the paper is poorly written and difficult to follow. I recommend that the authors have the paper proofread by others and expand on the abbreviations (both within the equations and in Section 3). I would recommend that they move the 2-page appendix to the supplementary material and use those additional pages to define each variable and the functions used (even if this is a tradition within your own field – NIPS audiences come from many different disciplines and some will have trouble following the math otherwise).

As a psychologist, I would like the authors to link their work to some psychological literature on eye movements, which are optimal steps for gathering information. See, for example, Nazmanik, J., and Geisler, W. S. (2008). Figures of eye movement in humans conform to an optimal search strategy. Journal of Vision, 8(3), 1-14. There are other relevant articles as well (especially from Geisler's lab, but this should give the authors a clue to follow that literature). I would be interested to see how their approach compares to their findings (for example, are they reiterations of similar models or provide independent information that can be integrated to create a better model?).
