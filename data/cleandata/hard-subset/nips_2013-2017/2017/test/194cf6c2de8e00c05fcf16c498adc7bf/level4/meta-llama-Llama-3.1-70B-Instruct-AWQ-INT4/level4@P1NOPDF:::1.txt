This computational neuroscience paper proposes a bottom-up visual feature saliency model, modified by dynamical systems modelling, that aims to capture the dynamics of scanpaths. I found the paper to be an enjoyable read, with a set of intriguing ideas in defining "affordances" (such as curiosity and brightness invariance) that drive the scan-path, as well as the concept of the Least Action Principle. However, these "affordances" are somewhat ad-hoc and lack further motivation.

In the context of the Least Action Principle and its derivation, it would have been interesting to relate it to actual work in eye movement research, such as past and recent studies by the Wolpert lab. Although the lack of biological motivation may be a minor issue in the era of high-performing deep learning models of static visual salience, the model's mixed performance in comparison to these models is a concern. Given that the model claims to capture biological eye movement dynamics, it would have been beneficial to include actual figures characterizing the generated scan paths in the main paper, not only in terms of their overall statistics but also in terms of their temporal dynamics.
