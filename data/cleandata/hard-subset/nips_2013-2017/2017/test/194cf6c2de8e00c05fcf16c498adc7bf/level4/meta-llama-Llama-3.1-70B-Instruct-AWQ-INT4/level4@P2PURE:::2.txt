Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes

The authors investigate human visual attention in static and dynamic images, deriving a model based on three fundamental principles: (1) eye movements are constrained by a harmonic oscillator within the image borders, considering a limited-sized retina; (2) a "curiosity-driven principle" emphasizing regions with significant brightness changes in both fine and blurred image versions; and (3) brightness invariance, which increases with changes in brightness. By combining these principles into a cost function, the authors obtain differential equations to predict eye movements across static or dynamic images, depending on the initial location and velocity. The proposed technique is evaluated quantitatively on datasets of static and dynamic scenes with human eye movements, demonstrating comparable performance to state-of-the-art methods.

Formal definitions of saliency and modeling eye movements are crucial issues in computational vision and cognitive science. The authors propose a novel and innovative model that addresses the long-standing problem of vague saliency definitions in psychology, providing a potential foundation for understanding saliency and formalizing eye movements within the bottom-up tradition. Although the method may not be state-of-the-art on every metric for every dataset, it performs well and offers a distinct perspective on the problem.

However, some of my understanding is based on conjecture due to the paper's poor writing and organization. I recommend that the authors have their work proofread and expand on abbreviations, both within equations and in Section 3. Moving the two-page Appendix to supplementary material and utilizing the extra pages to define each variable and function (even if they are conventional within the field) would improve clarity. As the NIPS audience comes from diverse disciplines, explicitly defining these elements is essential for readers to follow the mathematics.

As a psychologist, I would have appreciated a connection between the authors' work and the existing psychological literature on eye movements as optimal information-gathering strategies. For example, Najemnik and Geisler (2008) demonstrated that human eye movement statistics are consistent with an optimal search strategy. Exploring the relationship between the proposed approach and findings from this literature (e.g., Geisler's lab) would be valuable. A discussion on how the authors' approach compares to or integrates with existing models would be particularly interesting, shedding light on whether their model provides independent information or restates similar concepts.
