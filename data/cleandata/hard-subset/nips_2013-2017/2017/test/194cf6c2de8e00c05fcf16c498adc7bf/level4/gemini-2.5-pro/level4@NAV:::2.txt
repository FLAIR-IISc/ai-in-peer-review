This paper investigates the principles governing human visual attention in static and dynamic scenes. The authors propose a model based on minimizing an energy function derived from three foundational principles: (1) Eye movements are modeled as a harmonic oscillator constrained by the image borders and a finite retinal field. (2) A "curiosity-driven principle" emphasizes regions exhibiting high-contrast brightness changes at both fine and coarse scales. (3) A brightness invariance principle is included, which increases as a function of brightness variations. By formulating a cost function from these principles, the authors derive differential equations that predict eye movement scanpaths based on an initial position and velocity. The model is evaluated quantitatively on datasets containing static and dynamic scenes with corresponding human eye-tracking data, demonstrating performance on par with state-of-the-art methods.

The formal definition of saliency and the predictive modeling of eye movements are central problems in computational vision and cognitive science. The field of psychology has long struggled with ambiguous definitions of saliency, and this paper presents a novel and innovative model (to my knowledge) that contributes to a more formal, bottom-up understanding of what captures attention. Although the method does not consistently outperform all competing approaches on every metric for every dataset, its performance is strong, and it provides a refreshingly different theoretical perspective on the problem.

Unfortunately, my understanding of the work is partly based on inference, as the manuscript is poorly written and difficult to follow. I strongly recommend that the authors conduct a thorough proofread and expand all abbreviations, both within the equations and in the main text (e.g., Section 3). I also suggest moving the two-page Appendix to supplementary material. The additional space should be used to explicitly define every variable and function. This is critical for a multidisciplinary audience, as readers from different backgrounds may not be familiar with the authors' field-specific conventions.

As a psychologist, I would have liked to see the authors connect their framework to the psychological literature on eye movements as an optimal strategy for information gathering. The work by Najemnik & Geisler (2008, Journal of Vision) and other related research provides a key entry point into this literature. A discussion is needed to clarify how the proposed variational approach relates to these findings. For example, are these different formulations of similar underlying models, or do they provide independent information that could be integrated to build a more powerful, unified theory of visual search?
