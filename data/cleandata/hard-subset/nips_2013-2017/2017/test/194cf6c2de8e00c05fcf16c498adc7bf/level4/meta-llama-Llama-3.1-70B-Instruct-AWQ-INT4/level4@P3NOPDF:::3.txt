The paper proposes a novel approach to the study of eye movements, providing a thorough summary of the current state of the art in this field. The authors accurately describe existing approaches, which primarily rely on saliency maps defined over an image, with fixation points selected through optimization or maximization methods. In contrast, the proposed model, EYMOL, operates under a distinct assumption, defining eye movements directly on trajectories of gaze based on the "Least Action Principle." Although some mathematical details, particularly those in the extensive appendix, were beyond my expertise due to my limited physics background, I was able to grasp the key elements of the approach and its differentiation from past models.

The authors apply their model to a real dataset of eye movements and compare its performance against various alternative models. While EYMOL does not outperform existing models on every measure, the results are generally favorable, demonstrating the validity of the new approach. This application to real data strengthens the paper's contribution, showcasing the model's potential in explaining eye movement patterns.

I believe the paper may be of interest to certain members of the vision science community, such as attendees of the Vision Sciences Society (VSS) conference. However, I am less convinced that it makes a significant contribution to the NIPS community, and I suggest that this assessment be evaluated in conjunction with the opinions of more expert reviewers. Nevertheless, the paper likely advances a new and interesting theory that could inspire further empirical research, thereby possessing both novelty and merit for the field of perception sciences.
