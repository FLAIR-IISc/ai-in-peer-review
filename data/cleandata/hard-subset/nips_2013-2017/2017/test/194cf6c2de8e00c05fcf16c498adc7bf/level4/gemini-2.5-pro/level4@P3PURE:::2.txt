This paper investigates the principles governing human visual attention in both static and dynamic scenes. The authors propose a model derived from three core principles, which are formulated as an energy function to be minimized by eye movements: (1) eye movements are modeled as a harmonic oscillator, constrained by the image boundaries and a finite retinal size; (2) a "curiosity-driven" principle directs attention toward regions with large, multi-scale changes in brightness; and (3) a brightness invariance principle introduces a cost that increases as a function of brightness changes. By minimizing a cost function derived from these principles, the authors derive differential equations that predict eye movement scanpaths given a starting location and initial velocity. The model is evaluated quantitatively on datasets of static and dynamic scenes with corresponding human eye-tracking data, where it performs comparably to state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**

The formal modeling of saliency and eye movements is a critical challenge in computational vision and cognitive science. The authors address this challenge with a novel and innovative model that provides a formal, bottom-up framework for understanding visual attention. This work is a welcome departure from purely descriptive models and offers a refreshingly different perspective on the problem. While the model does not achieve state-of-the-art performance on every metric for every dataset, its performance is strong and competitive, validating the underlying theoretical principles.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The primary weakness of the paper is its clarity. The writing is dense and difficult to follow, which forced me to make conjectures about some of the model's details. I strongly recommend that the authors perform a thorough proofreading and revision of the manuscript. Specifically:
    *   **Expand Abbreviations:** All abbreviations, both in the text (e.g., in Section 3) and in the equations, should be clearly defined upon their first use.
    *   **Define Variables:** The authors should explicitly define every variable and function used in their mathematical formulation. The NIPS audience is broad and interdisciplinary; one cannot assume familiarity with conventions from a specific subfield.
    *   **Restructure Content:** I suggest moving the two-page appendix to supplementary material. The two pages gained should be used to improve the clarity of the main text by elaborating on the model's components and definitions, as mentioned above.

2.  **Connection to Psychological Literature:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by connecting its theoretical framework to the existing psychological literature on eye movements. For instance, a large body of work treats eye movements as optimal steps for information gathering. The authors should discuss how their proposed principles relate to or differ from these theories. A relevant starting point would be the work from Geisler's lab (e.g., Najemnik, J., & Geisler, W. S. (2008). Eye movement statistics in humans are consistent with an optimal search strategy. *Journal of Vision*, 8(3), 1-14). A discussion comparing the current approach to such findings would be valuable. For example, are the proposed principles a restatement of similar optimal-search models in a different mathematical language, or do they provide independent constraints that could be integrated to produce a more comprehensive model of visual attention?
