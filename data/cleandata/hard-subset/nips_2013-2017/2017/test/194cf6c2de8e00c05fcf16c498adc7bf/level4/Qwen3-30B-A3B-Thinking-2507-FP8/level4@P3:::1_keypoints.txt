- Proposes a novel attention model using the Least Action Principle, treating scanpaths as particle trajectories in a potential field.  
- Introduces three affordances driving attention: boundedness (retina constraint), curiosity (attraction to high-detail gradients), and brightness invariance (fixation/tracking).  
- Encodes these principles in a Lagrangian; stationary point yields Euler–Lagrange equations for attention dynamics.  
- Produces saliency maps as a byproduct of simulated scanpaths rather than as primary outputs.  
- Validated on MIT1003, CAT2000 (images) and SFU (video), reporting competitive performance with state-of-the-art.  
- Strong theoretical motivation linking visual attention to fundamental physics, a compelling NIPS angle.  
- Clear positioning relative to prior computational attention models and distinction from centralized saliency-map approaches.  
- Mathematical derivations are sound and supported by technical appendix.  
- Model is simple and runs in real time, an advantage over data-hungry deep learning methods.  
- Theoretical originality—unifying curiosity and brightness invariance in a variational framework—is the paper’s main strength.  
- Provides a genuine dynamic model of scanpaths, aligning with neuroscience perspectives on attention.  
- Experimental presentation is weak and obscures actual performance details.  
- Critically missing visual comparisons between human and model-generated scanpaths.  
- Overall performance is mediocre compared to recent deep-learning methods and inconsistent across metrics.  
- The biological justification for the three affordances, especially the curiosity term, is underdeveloped and somewhat ad hoc.  
- Parameter estimation (simulated annealing) is vaguely described; parameter ranges and validation are unclear.  
- Main text lacks intuitive explanation of key equations despite detailed appendix.  
- Significance limited: interesting theoretical contribution but insufficient evidence of advancing practical or biological plausibility.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, principled framework that models scanpath dynamics rather than static saliency.  
- Arguments against acceptance: mediocre performance, missing visual scanpath comparisons, and weak biological grounding of assumptions.  
- Recommended improvements: add direct visual human vs. model scanpath comparisons, strengthen biological/cognitive justification for affordances, and clarify parameter estimation and intuitive explanations.
