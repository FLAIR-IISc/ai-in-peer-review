Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes

Summary
The paper investigates which locations in static and dynamic images attract human attention. The authors define visual attention via an energy function minimized by eye movements, grounded in three principles:
- Eye movements are constrained by a harmonic oscillator at the image borders within a limited-sized retina.
- A curiosity-driven principle highlights regions with large brightness changes in both a fine and a blurred version of the image.
- A brightness-invariance term increases as a function of changes in brightness.

From a cost function combining these components, the authors derive differential equations to predict eye movements over static and dynamic images, conditioned on starting location and initial velocity. They evaluate the model quantitatively on datasets of static and dynamic scenes paired with human eye-movement recordings, showing performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods.

Significance and novelty
Formalizing saliency and modeling eye movements are central challenges in computational vision and cognitive science. Psychologists have long grappled with vague definitions of saliency; this paper proposes a novel and innovative model (to the best of my knowledge) that could advance our understanding of what makes a location salient and provide a formal, bottom-up model of eye movements. While it is not state-of-the-art on every metric across all datasets, it performs well overall and offers a refreshingly different perspective on the problem.

Clarity and presentation
Some of the above assessment is necessarily tentative because the paper is poorly written and difficult to follow. I strongly recommend:
- Thorough proofreading by colleagues to improve clarity and fix grammatical issues.
- Expanding all abbreviations and symbols, both in the equations and especially in Section 3.
- Moving the two-page Appendix to the supplementary material, and using the freed space to define every variable and function used. Even if certain notations are standard in your subfield, the NeurIPS audience is multidisciplinary, and many readers will struggle to follow the mathematics without explicit definitions.

Relation to psychological literature
As a psychologist, I would like to see the work connected to the literature on eye movements as optimal steps for information gathering. For example:
- Najemnik, J., & Geisler, W. S. (2008). Eye movement statistics in humans are consistent with an optimal search strategy. Journal of Vision, 8(3), 1–14.

There are other relevant papers, particularly from Geisler’s lab. A discussion comparing your approach to these findings would strengthen the paper. Are your principles restatements of similar models, or do they provide independent information that could be integrated to produce a better combined model?

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and potentially impactful contribution that formalizes saliency and eye-movement dynamics within a variational framework and achieves competitive performance. However, the current presentation severely limits accessibility. With substantial revisions to writing and notation, and with a clearer connection to the psychological literature on optimal search, the paper’s contributions would be much more compelling and broadly understandable.
