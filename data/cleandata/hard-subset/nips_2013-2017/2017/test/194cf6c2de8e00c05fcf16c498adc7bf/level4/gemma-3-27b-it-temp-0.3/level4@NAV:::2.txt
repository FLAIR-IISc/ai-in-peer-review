## Review - Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes

This paper presents a model for predicting human visual attention in both static and dynamic images. The authors ground their approach in three core principles – constraints on eye movements modeled as a harmonic oscillator, a “curiosity” principle based on brightness changes at multiple scales, and brightness invariance – which are combined into an energy function and subsequently used to derive differential equations governing eye movements. The resulting model is quantitatively evaluated against human eye-tracking data for both static and dynamic scenes, demonstrating performance competitive with current state-of-the-art methods.

The formalization of saliency and the modeling of eye movements represent fundamental challenges in computational vision and cognitive science. Existing definitions of saliency often lack precision, and this work offers a novel and potentially impactful model – to the best of my knowledge – for addressing both the theoretical understanding of salience and the creation of a formal model for bottom-up driven eye movements. While the method doesn’t uniformly achieve state-of-the-art results across all metrics and datasets, its overall performance is strong and it offers a distinctive and valuable perspective on the problem.

However, the clarity of the presentation currently hinders full appreciation of the work. The paper is difficult to follow, and I strongly recommend thorough proofreading and a more explicit explanation of all abbreviations, both within the equations and throughout Section 3. The current two-page Appendix would be better suited as supplementary material; the space gained should be used to provide a comprehensive definition of each variable and function employed, recognizing that the NIPS audience is multidisciplinary and may not be familiar with all notational conventions. 

From a psychological perspective, it would be beneficial to connect this work to the literature on eye movements as an information-gathering strategy. For instance, the authors might consider Najemnik & Geisler (2008, *Journal of Vision*) and related work from Geisler’s lab. A discussion of how the proposed approach relates to, or potentially integrates with, findings from this literature – specifically, whether it replicates existing models or offers complementary insights – would strengthen the paper considerably.
