

The paper introduces EYMOL, a novel framework for modeling eye movements that diverges from conventional saliency-based approaches. Current methods typically generate fixation predictions by optimizing over saliency maps defined on static images. In contrast, EYMOL posits that gaze trajectories adhere to a "Least Action Principle," modeling eye movements directly as continuous paths rather than discrete fixation points. The authors provide a clear summary of prior work, though their expertise in eye movement science exceeds mine; I acknowledge this limitation upfront. While the mathematical appendix contains physics-intensive derivations beyond my immediate comprehension, the core conceptual innovation and model mechanics are sufficiently articulated for evaluation. The approach is empirically validated on a real-world eye-tracking dataset and rigorously compared against multiple established baselines. Although EYMOL does not uniformly outperform all competitors across every metric, its results consistently demonstrate superior or competitive performance, establishing empirical validity for the proposed trajectory-centric framework.  

The work offers a fresh theoretical perspective for vision science, potentially inspiring new empirical investigations into gaze dynamics. It aligns with interests in the Vision Sciences Society (VSS) community, where trajectory-based models are an emerging focus. However, its contribution to NIPS 2017 warrants careful consideration. While the model exhibits clear novelty and methodological rigor, its primary impact appears more substantive for perception science than for machine learning or neural computation—NIPS' core domain. The paper's strength lies in its theoretical innovation and empirical support, but it lacks explicit connections to broader ML challenges (e.g., scalability, integration with deep learning, or computational efficiency). Without such bridges, its significance for NIPS may be limited compared to other submissions. That said, this judgment should be weighed against reviewers with deeper expertise in computational vision.  

In summary, EYMOL advances a compelling theory for eye movement modeling with demonstrable empirical support. It merits attention from vision scientists but requires stronger justification for its relevance to NIPS' machine learning and AI-focused audience. The work is well-executed and contributes meaningfully to its specific domain, yet its alignment with NIPS' mission is less definitive than the authors suggest. I recommend acceptance if the authors can better articulate how this framework addresses core ML challenges or enables new algorithmic insights beyond perception science. Otherwise, it may be more appropriately suited for a vision or neuroscience venue.
