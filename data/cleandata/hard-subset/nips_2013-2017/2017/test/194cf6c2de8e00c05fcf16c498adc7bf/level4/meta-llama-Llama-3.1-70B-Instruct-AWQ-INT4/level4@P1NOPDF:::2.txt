Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes

The authors investigate what locations in static and dynamic images tend to attract human attention. They derive a model by first defining three fundamental principles for visual attention, which is formalized as an energy function to be minimized by eye movements. These principles are: (1) eye movements are constrained by a harmonic oscillator at the image borders within a limited-sized retina, (2) a "curiosity-driven principle" that highlights regions with large changes in brightness in both fine and blurred versions of the image, and (3) brightness invariance, which increases as a function of changes in brightness. Using a cost function derived from these three principles, the authors derive differential equations for predicting eye movements across static or dynamic images, depending on the starting location and initial velocity. The authors evaluate their technique quantitatively on datasets of static and dynamic scenes coupled with human eye movements, demonstrating that their method performs comparably to the state-of-the-art.

Formal definitions of saliency and modeling eye movements are critical issues in computational vision and cognitive science. Psychologists have long struggled with vague definitions of saliency, and the authors propose a novel and innovative model that could aid in the development of a better understanding of what makes something salient and provide a formal model for eye movements within the bottom-up tradition. Although their method may not be the state-of-the-art on every metric for every dataset, it performs well and offers a refreshingly different perspective on the problem.

Unfortunately, some of my understanding of the paper is based on conjecture, as the writing is poor and difficult to follow. I recommend that the authors have others proofread the paper and expand on abbreviations, both within the equations and in Section 3. I would also suggest moving the two-page Appendix to supplementary material and using the extra pages to define each variable and function used. This would be particularly helpful, as the NIPS audience comes from many different disciplines, and some may struggle to follow the mathematics otherwise.

As a psychologist, I would have liked to see the authors connect their work to the psychological literature on eye movements as optimal steps for gathering information. For example, Najemnik and Geisler (2008) demonstrate that eye movement statistics in humans are consistent with an optimal search strategy. There are other relevant articles, particularly from Geisler's lab, that could provide a useful starting point for exploring this literature. I would be interested to see a discussion of how the authors' approach compares to these findings, such as whether they are restatements of similar models or provide independent information that could be integrated to produce a better model.
