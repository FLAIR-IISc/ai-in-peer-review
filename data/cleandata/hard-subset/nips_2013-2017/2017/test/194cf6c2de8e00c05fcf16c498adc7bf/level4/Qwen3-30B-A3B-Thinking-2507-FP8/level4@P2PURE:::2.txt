

Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes  
The authors investigate human attention patterns in static and dynamic images by deriving a model based on three foundational principles for visual attention (formulated as an energy function to be minimized by eye movements): (1) Eye movements are constrained by a harmonic oscillator near image borders within a retina of limited size; (2) a "curiosity-driven principle" emphasizing regions with significant brightness changes in both fine and blurred image versions; and (3) brightness invariance, which increases with brightness changes. From these principles, they derive a cost function yielding differential equations to predict eye movements across static or dynamic scenes (depending on initial location and velocity). Quantitative evaluation on datasets of static/dynamic scenes paired with human eye movement data demonstrates performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods.  

Formal definitions of saliency and eye movement modeling are critical in computational vision and cognitive science, where psychologists have long grappled with vague saliency concepts. The authors propose a novel and innovative model (to my knowledge) that advances understanding of saliency mechanisms and provides a formal framework for eye movements within the bottom-up tradition. While not state-of-the-art on every metric across all datasets, the method performs robustly and offers a refreshing alternative perspective.  

Unfortunately, the paper’s poor writing and lack of clarity hinder full assessment. I strongly recommend the authors have the manuscript proofread by others and expand all abbreviations (including those in equations and Section 3). I also suggest moving the two-page Appendix to supplementary material and using those pages to define every variable and function—critical for a cross-disciplinary NIPS audience unfamiliar with field-specific conventions.  

As a psychologist, I would have valued stronger connections to psychological literature on eye movements as optimal information-gathering steps. For instance, Najemnik & Geisler (2008) demonstrated that human eye movement statistics align with optimal search strategies (Journal of Vision, 8(3), 1–14). I encourage the authors to discuss how their approach relates to such findings—whether it restates similar models or offers complementary insights for a more integrated framework.
