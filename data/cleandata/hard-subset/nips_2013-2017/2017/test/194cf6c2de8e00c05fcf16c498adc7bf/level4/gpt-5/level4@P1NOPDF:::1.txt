This computational neuroscience paper proposes a bottom-up visual-feature saliency model combined with dynamical-systems modeling to capture scanpath dynamics. I enjoyed reading it; it presents several appealing ideas, particularly the definitions of “affordances” (curiosity, brightness invariance) that drive the scanpath, as well as the use of a Least Action Principle. However, these “affordances” feel somewhat ad hoc and are not further motivated.

In the context of the Least Action Principle and its derivation here, it would have been interesting to relate it to established work on eye-movement control, including past and recent work from the Wolpert lab. While the lack of strong biological motivation might be a minor issue in an era of benchmark-busting deep learning models of static visual salience, a more pressing concern is the model’s performance: its results are poor relative to these models.

Given that the model claims to capture biological eye-movement dynamics, I would have liked to see, in the main paper, actual figures characterizing the generated scanpaths, not only their overall statistics.
