Variability of Formula for Visual Attention in Static and Dynamic Scenes

The authors have investigated which areas of static and dynamic images attract human attention. They developed a model defining three fundamental principles for visual attention (defined as a power function with a minimum by eye movement): (1) eye movement is bounded by a harmonic oscillator within the boundaries of the image on a limited-sized retina. (2) a "curiosity-driven principle" that identifies areas with large changes in brightness, both subtle and ambiguous versions of the image, and (3) brightness constancy, which increases as a function of brightness changes. Using an expenditure function derived from these three functions, the authors developed differential equations to estimate eye movement across static or dynamic images (depending on the starting position and initial speed). The authors quantified their technique in data sets of static and dynamic scenes related to human eye movement. They showed that their method is comparable to state-of-the-art methods.

Formal definitions of salience and eye movement modeling are important topics in computational vision and cognitive science. Psychologists have long been plagued by vague definitions of salience, and the authors have proposed a new and innovative model (as far as I know) that can help create a better understanding of what makes something salient and how to create a formal model for eye movement (within the tradition of below-above). Although it may not be state-of-the-art in every metric for every data set, it works well and provides a refreshingly different perspective on the problem.

Unfortunately, some of what I wrote above is speculative, as the paper is poorly written and difficult to follow. I recommend that the authors have the paper proofread by someone else and expand the summaries (both in equations and section 3). I would recommend that they remove the 2-page appendix of supplementary materials and use those additional pages to define each variable and function used (even if it is a convention within your own field - NIPS' audience comes from various disciplines, and some of them may have difficulty following mathematics).

As a psychologist, I would have liked the authors to link their work to some psychological literature on eye movement as an optimal step for data collection. For example, see Najemanik, J., and Geisler, W. S. (2008). Statistical measures of eye movement in humans are consistent with an optimal search strategy. Journal of Vision, 8 (3), 1-14. There are other relevant articles as well (especially from Geisler's lab, but this may give the authors a formula to follow that literature). I would be interested to see how their results compare to their method (e.g., do they rewrite similar models or provide independent data that can be combined to create a better model?).
