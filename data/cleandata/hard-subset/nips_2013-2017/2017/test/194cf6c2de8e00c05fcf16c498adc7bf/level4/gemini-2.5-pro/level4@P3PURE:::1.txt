This paper presents a computational neuroscience model of bottom-up visual saliency that incorporates dynamical systems to capture the dynamics of scanpaths. The paper is interesting and enjoyable to read, and it introduces several compelling ideas, notably the use of "affordances" (such as curiosity and brightness invariance) to drive the scanpath and the application of the Least Action Principle.

However, the work has several weaknesses. First, the proposed "affordances" seem somewhat ad-hoc and are not sufficiently motivated by existing literature or theory. In its discussion of the Least Action Principle, the paper would be significantly strengthened by connecting its derivation and application to established work in eye movement research, such as the past and recent contributions from the Wolpert lab.

While a lack of deep biological motivation might be acceptable for a model that achieves state-of-the-art performance, this model's performance is mixed and appears poor when compared to contemporary deep learning models for static visual salience. A more significant concern is the mismatch between the paper's central claim and its evaluation. The model claims to capture the *dynamics* of biological eye movements, yet the main paper lacks a detailed characterization of the generated scanpaths. The evaluation appears to be limited to static metrics of how well the aggregate fixations match human eye movement data, rather than providing a dynamic analysis of the scanpaths themselves.
