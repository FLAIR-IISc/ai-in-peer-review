- Proposes a novel variational, physics-inspired model of visual attention that generalizes the Least Action Principle to eye-movement trajectories.
- Defines three core principles: boundedness via harmonic oscillators at image borders; curiosity-driven attraction to high brightness gradients at fine and blurred scales; and brightness invariance favoring object boundaries.
- Builds a Lagrangian from these principles and derives Euler–Lagrange equations to model attention dynamics.
- Generates scanpaths by numerically solving the resulting differential equations; saliency maps emerge as a byproduct.
- Evaluates on static (MIT1003, CAT2000) and dynamic (SFU) datasets and shows competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods.
- Conceptually original and potentially unifying bottom-up and top-down attentional mechanisms under a single mathematical framework.
- Strong technical quality: sound mathematical derivation, comprehensive experiments, parameter estimation via simulated annealing, and informative ablation study.
- Major clarity issues: dense mathematics, many variables introduced without definition or motivation (e.g., ω in Equation 4).
- Implementation details unclear for key terms, notably the brightness invariance component (Equations 5–6).
- Paper should better differentiate itself from prior saccadic models (e.g., Le Meur & Liu, 2009).
- Needs stronger linkage to psychological literature on eye-movement optimization (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler) and discussion of the “eggs-chicken dilemma.”
- Presentation suggestions: move full Euler–Lagrange derivation from appendix to supplementary material to free space for clearer explanations.
- Significance: offers a biologically plausible, scanpath-generating framework that can bridge computational vision and cognitive science and suggests integration with variational learning.
- Recommendation: accept with major revisions focused on clarity, variable explanation, and stronger connections to psychological findings.
