- Proposes a dynamical systems model combining bottom-up saliency, novel "affordances" (curiosity, brightness invariance), and the Least Action Principle (LAP), but motivation and grounding are weak.  
- Novelty: conceptualizing affordances and applying LAP to scanpath generation is interesting and potentially insightful.  
- Theoretical grounding is insufficient; selection of affordances lacks clear links to cognitive/neurobiological mechanisms (e.g., dopamine, hippocampal novelty detection).  
- LAP derivation appears disconnected from prior computational vision and oculomotor control literature, notably work on optimal control (e.g., Wolpert lab).  
- Empirical performance is mixed and inferior to state-of-the-art static salience models, undermining the model’s core premise.  
- Paper fails to provide clear comparative metrics (AUC, NSS, CC) against relevant baselines.  
- No convincing argument or evidence for why the dynamical model should outperform static salience predictors for scanpath prediction.  
- Inadequate validation of claimed biological dynamics: relies on static fixation maps and lacks example overlaid scanpaths comparing model and human sequences.  
- Missing temporal/dynamical metrics (saccade amplitude distributions, fixation durations, sequence dependencies, sequence entropy, next-fixation prediction) necessary to validate dynamics.  
- Evaluation mismatch: model is dynamical but primarily evaluated with static metrics, which is insufficient.  
- Recommended fixes: justify affordances with cognitive/physiological evidence, compare to top static salience models on standard metrics, include visual and quantitative scanpath comparisons, and engage deeply with optimal-control literature.  
- Overall: promising theoretical angle but undermined by weak motivation, poor empirical validation, and inadequate demonstration of biological relevance.
