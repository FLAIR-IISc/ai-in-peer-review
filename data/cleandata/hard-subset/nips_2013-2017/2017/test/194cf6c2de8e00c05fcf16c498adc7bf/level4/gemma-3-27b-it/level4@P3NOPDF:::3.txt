This paper introduces a novel approach to modeling eye movements, diverging from traditional saliency map-based methods. Current approaches typically construct a saliency map over an image and then select fixation points through optimization or maximization strategies. In contrast, this work – EYMOL – is grounded in the “Least Action Principle,” defining a model directly on trajectories of gaze. 

While the mathematical details, particularly within the extensive appendix, required a level of physics background beyond my expertise to fully evaluate, I grasped the core principles of the approach and its key distinctions from existing models. The authors provide a clear explanation of the underlying theory, allowing understanding even without specialized knowledge of the applied physics.

The model is thoughtfully applied to a real-world eye movement dataset and rigorously compared against a variety of alternative models. The results are generally encouraging, demonstrating the validity of the proposed approach, though it does not consistently outperform all baselines across all metrics. The performance gains, however, suggest a worthwhile contribution to the field.

The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical novelty and potential to inspire further research. It advances an interesting and potentially fruitful theory for understanding gaze trajectories, moving beyond pixel-based saliency. This novelty, coupled with the empirical validation presented, indicates merit within the broader perception sciences.

However, I am less certain about the paper’s fit for NIPS. While the work is technically sound and potentially impactful, its primary relevance appears to lie within vision science (e.g., it would likely be well-received at the Vision Sciences Society conference). Therefore, I defer to the judgment of reviewers with more specialized knowledge in perception and eye movement research to determine its suitability for this conference. 

Overall, I believe this paper presents a valuable contribution to understanding eye movements and warrants consideration, particularly given its potential to stimulate future empirical investigation.
