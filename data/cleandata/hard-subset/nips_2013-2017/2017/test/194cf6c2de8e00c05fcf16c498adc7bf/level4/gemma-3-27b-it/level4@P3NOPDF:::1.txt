This paper presents a computational neuroscience model of visual attention that combines a bottom-up saliency map with dynamical systems modeling to capture the dynamics of scanpaths. I found the paper engaging, particularly its novel approach to defining “affordances”—specifically, curiosity and brightness invariance—as drivers of scanpath generation, and the application of the Least Action Principle to this process. However, while these affordances are conceptually interesting, their formulation feels somewhat *ad hoc* and lacks further theoretical justification.

Within the context of the Least Action Principle and its derivation, it would have been valuable to connect this work to existing research in eye movement control, such as the substantial body of work from the Wolpert lab and more recent studies. While a strong grounding in biological plausibility is arguably less critical in the current landscape of deep learning-based static visual salience models focused primarily on benchmark performance, the central claim of this model—that it captures *biological* eye movement dynamics—necessitates a more direct comparison to and motivation from established findings in the field. 

The primary weakness of the paper lies in the mixed performance of the proposed model when compared to existing state-of-the-art methods. Given the claim of capturing dynamic aspects of eye movements, it's surprising that the performance doesn’t demonstrably surpass or even significantly compare favorably with static saliency models. Crucially, the paper lacks sufficient visualization of the generated scanpaths within the main text. While overall statistics are reported regarding matching eye movements, a qualitative and quantitative characterization of the *properties* of the generated scanpaths themselves – such as saccade amplitudes, velocities, fixation durations, and patterns of sequential fixations – would greatly strengthen the argument for the model's biological relevance. Figures illustrating example scanpaths generated by the model, alongside comparisons to human scanpaths, are essential.

Specifically, I would like to see:

*   **Justification for Affordance Selection:** A more thorough discussion of *why* curiosity and brightness invariance were chosen as key affordances. What makes these particularly relevant to scanpath generation, and are there alternative affordances that could be explored?
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** A detailed discussion of how the Least Action Principle, as applied here, relates to established theories of motor control and eye movements. Referencing work on optimal control, minimum jerk, and related principles would be beneficial.
*   **Scanpath Visualization and Analysis:** Inclusion of figures showing example scanpaths generated by the model, and a quantitative analysis of their key characteristics (saccade length, velocity, fixation duration, etc.). Comparison of these characteristics to those of human scanpaths is essential.
*   **Performance Breakdown:** A more granular analysis of the model’s performance. Where does it succeed, and where does it fail? Are there specific types of images or scenes where the model performs particularly well or poorly?
*   **Ablation Study:** A brief ablation study investigating the contribution of each affordance to the overall scanpath generation process would further clarify their importance.



In summary, this paper presents an interesting, though somewhat underdeveloped, approach to modeling scanpath dynamics. The core ideas are promising, but the lack of strong biological motivation, the *ad hoc* nature of the affordances, and the mixed empirical results necessitate significant revisions. Addressing the points outlined above—particularly providing detailed visualizations and analyses of the generated scanpaths—would substantially improve the quality and impact of this work. As it stands, the paper represents a starting point for further investigation rather than a significant advance in the field.
