- Proposes EYMOL: a variational/physics-inspired model of visual attention using a Lagrangian with three potential terms (retina bounding, curiosity to high-detail regions, brightness invariance) and Euler–Lagrange–based scanpath generation.  
- Evaluated by generating scanpaths, creating saliency maps, and comparing to human fixations on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000 (images) and SFU (video), showing competitive state-of-the-art performance.  
- Strength — Originality: novel framing of attention as variational calculus, offering a principled theoretical foundation distinct from feature-engineering or end-to-end deep learning.  
- Strength — Unifying framework: clean integration of exploration (saccades) and fixation/tracking mechanisms within one mathematical model, with brightness invariance aiding stability and tracking.  
- Strength — Strong empirical results: simple, theory-driven model achieves impressive results comparable to more complex saliency models.  
- Weakness — Clarity/presentation: dense mathematical derivations and poorly defined variables obscure the method; motivations for specific term forms (e.g., C(t,x)) are insufficiently explained.  
- Weakness — Limited interdisciplinary grounding: insufficient connection to cognitive-psychology literature on eye movements as optimal information-gathering (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler), leaving questions about theoretical overlap or novelty.  
- Suggestion — Improve exposition: clarify and define all variables/functions, give intuitive step-by-step translation of the three principles into Lagrangian terms, and expand explanation of experimental abbreviations (NSS, AUC-Judd).  
- Suggestion — Reorganize material: move detailed derivations to supplementary material to free space for clearer main-text explanations and intuition.  
- Suggestion — Strengthen related work: discuss and cite optimal search/ information-gain eye-movement literature and explicitly compare conceptual relations or differences.  
- Editorial suggestion — Proofread and fix grammatical issues to improve flow.  
- Overall conclusion: highly original and promising with strong results, but requires substantial revision for clarity and better cognitive-science grounding to realize its contribution.
