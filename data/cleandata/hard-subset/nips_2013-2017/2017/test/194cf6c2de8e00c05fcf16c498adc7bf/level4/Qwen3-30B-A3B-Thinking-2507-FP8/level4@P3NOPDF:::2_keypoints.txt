- Proposes a variational model for human visual attention based on three principles: retinal boundary (harmonic oscillator), change-driven multi-scale brightness variation, and brightness invariance scaling with luminance.

- Derives differential equations from those principles to predict eye movement trajectories in static and dynamic scenes.

- Evaluated on human fixation datasets for static and dynamic scenes and is competitive with state-of-the-art, though not consistently superior across all metrics.

- Fills a gap by offering a formal, psychologically grounded alternative to purely data-driven saliency and eye-movement models.

- Novel integration of multi-scale brightness changes and boundary constraints, with thorough empirical validation.

- Paper’s writing is opaque with insufficient explanation of key terms, equations, and assumptions, making it hard to follow for a diverse NIPS audience.

- Notation issues: many abbreviations (e.g., S, E) and variables are undefined; mathematical derivations lack intuitive scaffolding.

- Recommendation to move the 2-page appendix to supplementary material and use main paper space to define all variables and clarify equations (e.g., explain the harmonic oscillator’s k).

- Lacks engagement with foundational psychological literature on attentional optimization (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2008) and doesn’t relate the model to optimal search theories.

- Authors should explicitly discuss how their energy-minimization framework relates to, extends, or diverges from optimal search and sequential visual search models.

- Evaluation reporting is unclear about which metrics/datasets show underperformance; needs a comparative table (e.g., AUC, NSS) against top baselines.

- Dynamic scene evaluation lacks ablation studies to quantify each principle’s contribution to performance.

- Overall assessment: conceptually promising and technically sound but undermined by poor exposition and weak links to cognitive literature.

- Recommendation: Major Revision — address clarity and notation, expand psychological context, and refine/evaluate experiments to clarify contributions.
