- Introduces EYMOL, a novel model that extends the Least Action Principle to eye movement, modeling scanpaths as trajectories minimizing a generalized action functional.  
- Uses Euler–Lagrange-derived differential equations to generate gaze trajectories instead of traditional saliency-map optimization.  
- Incorporates three core principles: bounded trajectory motion via harmonic oscillator constraints; curiosity-driven attention to high-detail regions using gradient features; and brightness invariance (optical flow) for fixation stability.  
- Produces saliency maps as a byproduct of simulated scanpaths, avoiding explicit saliency computation.  
- Validated on static (MIT1003, CAT2000) and dynamic (SFU) datasets and compared to state-of-the-art CNN-based saliency methods.  
- Technical foundation is sound with rigorous derivations (Appendix) and simulated annealing parameter estimation to maximize NSS.  
- Brightness-invariance approximation is justified and V1/V2 equivalence demonstrated on static images, but its limitations in dynamic scenes (e.g., motion blur) need explicit discussion.  
- Experimental design is robust (AUC-Judd, NSS metrics) and reproducible (preprocessing details, 199 trajectory runs), though using only 100 images for parameter tuning raises overfitting concerns.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written; mathematical precision is good but the Appendix may deter readers without physics background.  
- Suggests adding a concise main-text summary linking variational terms to “curiosity” and “brightness invariance” for accessibility.  
- Originality: significant conceptual shift from static saliency maps to dynamical variational modeling, novel integration of optical-flow-based brightness invariance.  
- Significance: real-time scanpath generation, competitive saliency performance, strong dynamic-scene behavior (object tracking), and potential to bridge computational vision and neuroscience.  
- Main cons: limited treatment of dynamic approximation limits, Appendix accessibility, and small parameter-tuning dataset.  
- Recommendation: Accept — conceptually novel, rigorously supported, and a valuable contribution despite not being a pure performance breakthrough.
