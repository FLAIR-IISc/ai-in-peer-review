- Proposes a novel variational framework for visual attention dynamics inspired by the Least Action Principle.  
- Defines two driving "affordances": curiosity-driven gradient magnitude (local detail + blurred periphery) and a brightness invariance constraint for fixation/tracking.  
- Unifies these affordances into a Lagrangian whose Euler–Lagrange equations produce differential equations for attentional dynamics.  
- Generates saliency maps as a byproduct of simulated scanpaths instead of optimizing saliency maps directly.  
- Extends prior saliency models by grounding attention in dynamical principles rather than static feature integration.  
- Strength: conceptually novel framing of attention as a principled variational process.  
- Strength: rigorous mathematical derivation and careful treatment of brightness invariance (Eq. 5–6).  
- Strength: real-time scanpath generation and model simplicity (no deep learning), aiding interpretability.  
- Strength: competitive experimental performance on MIT1003, CAT2000, and SFU, topping half the tested metrics and outperforming several baselines.  
- Weakness: modest performance relative to state-of-the-art CNN-based saliency predictors, notably in AUC-Judd.  
- Weakness: disconnect between biological motivation and benchmark results weakens the claim of modeling biological eye-movement dynamics.  
- Major flaw: absence of visual/qualitative scanpath comparisons against human fixation data—figures show only static saliency maps.  
- Weakness: "affordances" lack deeper biological/neurophysiological grounding and links to existing motor control or predictive coding literature.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject; paper is theoretically interesting but needs direct scanpath comparisons, stronger biological justification, and clearer alignment between biological claims and benchmark results.
