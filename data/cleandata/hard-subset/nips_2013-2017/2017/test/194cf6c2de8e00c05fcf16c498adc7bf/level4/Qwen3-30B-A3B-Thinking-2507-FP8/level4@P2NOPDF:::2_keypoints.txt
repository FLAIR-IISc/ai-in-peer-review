- Proposes a variational framework for visual attention based on three principles: harmonic oscillator boundary constraint, curiosity-driven multi-scale brightness change detection, and brightness invariance.  
- Formulates an energy-minimizing cost function yielding differential equations that predict eye movement trajectories.  
- Validated quantitatively on static and dynamic scene datasets with human fixation data, showing competitive performance against state-of-the-art on most metrics.  
- Advances formal, psychologically grounded bottom-up attention modeling and helps address longstanding ambiguity in saliency definitions.  
- Offers a conceptually distinct and promising alternative perspective that could advance understanding of visual attention mechanisms.  
- Presentation problems: writing is frequently opaque with inconsistent notation and unexplained abbreviations (notably in Section 3 and equations).  
- Insufficient mathematical exposition and essential variable/function definitions are missing; the 2-page Appendix should be moved to supplementary material to free space for these definitions.  
- Needs clearer conventions and explanations for NIPS’ interdisciplinary audience; recommends thorough proofreading and colleague review.  
- Critical theoretical gap: lacks engagement with psychological literature on the functional purpose of eye movements and optimal information-gathering models.  
- Specifically should relate and compare to Najemnik & Geisler (2008) and other optimal search/attentional control theories to situate the model within cognitive science.  
- Must clarify whether the framework extends, integrates with, or merely reframes existing optimal-search theories and explain its independent insights.  
- Overall: novel and empirically strong contribution but requires substantial revision in clarity, definitions, and theory linkage to meet NIPS standards.
