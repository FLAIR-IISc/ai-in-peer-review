## Review: Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes

This paper investigates human visual attention – specifically, which locations in static and dynamic images attract gaze. The authors present a novel model grounded in three core principles: (1) a biomechanical constraint modeled as a harmonic oscillator operating within the boundaries of the image and a limited retinal area; (2) a “curiosity” principle emphasizing regions exhibiting significant brightness changes, assessed at both fine and blurred scales; and (3) a brightness invariance principle that rewards increased brightness variation. These principles are formalized as a cost function, from which the authors derive differential equations to predict eye movements across both static and dynamic scenes, contingent on initial position and velocity. Quantitative evaluation against human eye movement data from static and dynamic scenes demonstrates performance comparable to current state-of-the-art methods.

The formalization of saliency and the modeling of eye movements represent fundamental challenges in computational vision and cognitive science. Existing work often suffers from imprecise definitions of saliency. This paper offers a unique and potentially impactful approach – to the best of my knowledge – that could contribute to a more rigorous understanding of what drives salience and provide a formal, bottom-up model of eye movements. While the method doesn’t uniformly achieve state-of-the-art results on all metrics and datasets, its overall performance is strong and it offers a distinct perspective on the problem.

However, the paper’s impact is significantly hampered by its clarity. The writing is dense and, at times, difficult to follow. I strongly recommend the authors engage external reviewers for thorough proofreading and significantly expand on all abbreviations used, both within the equations and throughout Section 3. The current Appendix (spanning two pages) should be moved to the supplementary material. The recovered space within the main body should be used to provide a comprehensive definition of each variable and function employed – even if these are standard conventions within the authors’ specific field. The NIPS audience is highly interdisciplinary, and many readers will struggle to parse the mathematical formulations without this clarification.

From a psychological perspective, it would strengthen the work to connect it with existing literature on eye movements as an information-gathering strategy. For instance, Najemnik & Geisler (2008, *Journal of Vision*, 8(3), 1-14) demonstrated that human eye movement statistics align with an optimal search strategy. Further exploration of the work from Geisler’s lab, and related literature, would be valuable. A discussion of how the authors’ approach relates to these findings – whether it replicates, extends, or offers independent insights that could be integrated into a more comprehensive model – would be highly beneficial.
