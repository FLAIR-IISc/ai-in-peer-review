Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes

This paper explores the locations in static and dynamic images that tend to capture human attention. The authors develop a model based on three fundamental principles of visual attention, formulated as an energy function that guides eye movements: (1) a harmonic oscillator constraint at the image borders within a limited-sized retina, (2) a "curiosity-driven principle" emphasizing regions with significant brightness changes in both fine and blurred image versions, and (3) brightness invariance, which increases with changes in brightness. By combining these principles into a cost function, the authors derive differential equations that predict eye movements across static or dynamic images, depending on the initial location and velocity. The proposed technique is evaluated quantitatively using datasets of static and dynamic scenes with human eye movements, demonstrating comparable performance to state-of-the-art methods.

The formal definitions of saliency and modeling eye movements are crucial in computational vision and cognitive science. Psychologists have long struggled with ambiguous definitions of saliency, and the authors propose a novel and innovative model that could facilitate a deeper understanding of saliency and formalize eye movement modeling within the bottom-up tradition. Although the method may not be state-of-the-art on every metric for every dataset, it performs well and offers a unique perspective on the problem.

However, some of my understanding is based on inference due to the paper's poor writing and difficulty in following the content. I recommend that the authors have their work proofread and expand on abbreviations, both within equations and in Section 3. Consider moving the 2-page Appendix to supplementary material and utilizing the extra pages to define each variable and function used, even if they are conventional within the field. This would facilitate understanding for the NIPS audience, which encompasses diverse disciplines.

As a psychologist, I would have appreciated connections to the psychological literature on eye movements as optimal information-gathering strategies. For example, Najemnik and Geisler (2008) demonstrate that human eye movement statistics are consistent with an optimal search strategy. The authors could explore relevant articles, particularly from Geisler's lab, to discuss how their approach compares to existing findings. I am interested in seeing a discussion on whether their model restates similar concepts or provides independent information that could be integrated to produce a better model.
