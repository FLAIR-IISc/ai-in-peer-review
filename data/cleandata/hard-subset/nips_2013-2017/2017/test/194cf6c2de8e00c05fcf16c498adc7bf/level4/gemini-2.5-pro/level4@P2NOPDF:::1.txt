This computational neuroscience paper proposes a bottom-up visual saliency model, modified by a dynamical systems approach, to capture the dynamics of human scanpaths. The paper is well-written and introduces several interesting ideas, including the use of "affordances" (e.g., curiosity, brightness invariance) and the Principle of Least Action to drive the simulated scanpath.

However, the work has several weaknesses that limit its impact. First, the proposed "affordances" seem to be postulated in an ad hoc manner and lack sufficient motivation. The manuscript would be stronger if the authors provided a more rigorous justification for why these specific factors were chosen. Second, while the application of the Principle of Least Action is a compelling idea, the paper misses an opportunity to connect its formulation to relevant prior work in eye movement research, such as the past and recent contributions from the Wolpert lab. Situating the work within this established context would significantly strengthen its theoretical grounding.

The most significant issue is the model's performance and evaluation. In an era of high-performing deep learning models for static visual salience, a new model with weaker biological motivation must justify its existence with strong benchmark performance. The proposed model, however, achieves mixed and generally poor results compared to these state-of-the-art methods.

Furthermore, there is a critical mismatch between the paper's central claim—that it models the *dynamics* of eye movements—and the evaluation metrics presented. The results focus on static measures of fixation prediction, which do not sufficiently validate a dynamic model. To substantiate its claims, the paper should have included a quantitative characterization of the generated scanpaths, comparing their statistical properties (e.g., saccade amplitude, orientation, and fixation duration distributions) against human eye movement data. The absence of such dynamic analysis is a major omission.
