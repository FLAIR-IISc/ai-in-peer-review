Summary
This paper proposes a dynamical model of visual attention in which scanpaths emerge as trajectories that minimize a generalized action functional, rather than being derived from a static, global saliency map. The Lagrangian includes: (i) a bounding potential that confines motion within the retinal field, (ii) a curiosity term that attracts gaze toward regions with strong local gradients as well as peripheral structure via an alternating local/peripheral modulation, and (iii) a brightness-invariance term derived from the optical flow constraint, encouraging fixation and motion tracking. The stationary conditions yield Euler–Lagrange equations governing gaze dynamics. Model parameters are estimated via simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000 using NSS as the objective. Saliency maps are produced post hoc by aggregating visitation counts from multiple simulated trajectories. Two variants are evaluated on static images (an exact and an approximated brightness-invariance term), with comparable performance; the approach is also tested on videos (SFU). Results are competitive with several baseline saliency methods, sometimes leading on standard metrics, and the model can run in real time.

Relation to prior work
The paper contrasts with classical saliency-map frameworks (e.g., Itti & Koch, information maximization, Bayesian surprise) and with recent CNN-based saliency predictors. It also relates to models that simulate scanpaths via winner-take-all plus inhibition-of-return heuristics. Here, the scanpath is the primary object, derived from a variational principle that blends bottom-up image structure with a constraint widely used in optical flow estimation.

Quality
- Theory: The variational formulation and derivations appear coherent, though the appendix is mathematically dense. I was not able to verify every step of the physics/variational calculus, but the high-level rationale is clear and consistent.
- Empirics: Experiments cover common static (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and dynamic (SFU) benchmarks using standard metrics (e.g., AUC-Judd, NSS). The model is competitive and sometimes best, though not uniformly state-of-the-art. The comparison between the exact and approximated brightness-invariance terms is informative and suggests the approximation is adequate for static images.
- Methodological details: The pipeline uses grayscale input, Gaussian blur for peripheral signal, an explicit center bias (tuned via grid search), and parameter optimization via simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000. Numerical integration is straightforward.

Clarity
The paper’s core idea is well-motivated, and the main components of the Lagrangian are explained. Some sections would benefit from clearer exposition:
- Provide more intuition for the alternating local/peripheral modulation (cos2/sin2) and the choice/sensitivity of ω.
- Specify the parameters and bounds used in simulated annealing (domain D, acceptance rule P, α), and discuss sensitivity to initialization.
- Clarify whether blur/center-bias tuning was applied uniformly to baselines for a fair comparison.
- Streamline notation and fix minor grammatical issues to improve readability.

Originality
Framing attention as the stationary point of a generalized action and unifying saccades with fixation/tracking via brightness invariance is novel within the saliency literature. The connection to optical-flow constraints is elegant, and the emergence of saliency maps as a byproduct of the dynamics is conceptually distinct from mainstream approaches.

Significance
The approach offers a principled alternative to map-based saliency and could influence future work on scanpath modeling and attention dynamics. Empirical performance is promising, especially given the model’s simplicity and real-time capability. However, the immediate impact may be tempered by the absence of top-down/task effects and by performance parity (rather than dominance) versus strong deep-learning baselines.

Strengths
- Conceptually fresh dynamical formulation grounded in a variational principle.
- Unified treatment of saccades, fixation, and tracking via brightness invariance.
- Competitive results on multiple benchmarks; real-time simulation of trajectories.
- Saliency maps arise naturally from the model rather than being hand-designed.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Fairness and tuning: Center bias and blur are tuned; clarify whether comparable tuning was allowed for baselines. Report ablation studies (remove brightness term, remove curiosity, remove peripheral modulation) to quantify each component’s contribution.
- Sensitivity and stability: Provide parameter sensitivity analyses (ηb, ηp, λ, m, ω) and discuss the λ constraint more concretely. Report variability across different initial conditions.
- Scope of evaluation: Include scanpath-level comparisons (e.g., MultiMatch, string-edit measures), not only saliency metrics, to test the model’s central claim about trajectory generation.
- Generalization: Discuss extensions to color and higher-level feature channels, and how top-down influences or task demands might be incorporated into the variational framework.
- Clarity: Improve the intuitive explanation of the brightness-invariance term’s role in fixation vs. tracking; add runtime details and release code for reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled formulation with a clear link to established constraints in vision.
- Unified model that generates scanpaths and saliency with competitive performance.
- Simple, fast, and potentially extensible framework.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited ablations and fairness clarifications; reliance on heuristic center bias.
- Dense mathematical presentation that may limit accessibility.
- Mixed dominance on benchmarks; limited treatment of top-down factors.

Overall
This is a creative and well-motivated contribution that bridges physics-inspired modeling with visual attention. While not uniformly superior to the best saliency predictors, it offers a compelling alternative that could stimulate further research on dynamic attention and scanpaths. I view it as borderline for NeurIPS; expert opinions from vision/attention specialists will be valuable.
