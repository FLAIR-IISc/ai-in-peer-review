This computational neuroscience paper presents a novel bottom-up visual feature saliency model, enhanced by dynamical systems modelling, to capture the dynamics of scanpaths. The paper offers an engaging read, introducing intriguing concepts such as "affordances" (curiosity and brightness invariance) that influence scan-path generation, as well as the Least Action Principle. Although these "affordances" are somewhat ad hoc and lack further justification, they demonstrate potential.

In the context of the Least Action Principle and its derivation, it would be beneficial to draw connections to relevant research in eye movement studies, such as the work by the Wolpert lab. While the absence of biological motivation may be a minor concern in the current era of deep learning models for static visual salience, the model's inconsistent performance compared to these models is a notable limitation. Given the model's claim to capture biological eye movement dynamics, it would be valuable to include figures in the main paper that characterize the generated scan paths, not only in terms of their overall statistics but also in terms of their matching eye movement patterns.
