- Introduces EYMOL, a variational model of visual attention based on a generalized Least Action Principle that models gaze trajectories via Euler-Lagrange equations.  
- Offers an alternative to saliency-map-based methods by directly generating scanpaths, with saliency maps emerging as a byproduct.  
- Novelty: frames attention dynamics (not just static maps) using physics-inspired Lagrangian components (retinal boundaries, curiosity, brightness invariance).  
- Technical soundness: mathematically well-derived (Appendix) and implemented with a clear Euler-Lagrange formulation.  
- Empirical results: competitive performance on MIT1003, CAT2000, SFU; V1 approximation matches V2 in static scenes and is faster.  
- Experimental rigor: comparisons against five baselines using standard metrics (NSS, AUC-Judd) are well-designed.  
- Significance: enables real-time scanpath simulation and opens paths for behavioral validation and mechanistic models of attention.  
- Simplicity and generality of the framework could inspire further work in dynamic scene modeling.  
- Clarity concern: physics-heavy appendix and opaque exposition of the brightness invariance approximation may impede reproducibility for non-physicists.  
- Limited impact on benchmarks: novelty is theoretical rather than delivering dominant benchmark gains, especially for static scenes.  
- Dynamic-scene weakness: competitive on SFU but not clearly superior to specialized video saliency methods.  
- Acceptance pros: groundbreaking theoretical unification and rigorous validation; cons: modest benchmark gains and potentially exclusionary technical exposition.
