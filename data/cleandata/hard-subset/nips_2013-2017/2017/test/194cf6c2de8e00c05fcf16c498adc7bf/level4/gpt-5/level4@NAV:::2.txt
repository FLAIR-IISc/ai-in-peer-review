Review- Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes
The paper explores which regions of static and dynamic images humans tend to attend to. The authors propose a model by introducing three fundamental principles of visual attention, formulated as an energy minimized by eye movements: (1) eye motions are constrained by a harmonic oscillator near image borders within a finite retinal extent; (2) a curiosity-driven term that accentuates regions with large luminance changes in both a high-resolution and a blurred rendition of the image; and (3) brightness invariance that increases as a function of luminance changes. From a cost function synthesizing these three components, they derive differential equations to predict eye trajectories over static or dynamic imagery (given initial position and velocity). They evaluate the approach quantitatively on datasets of static and moving scenes paired with human eye movements, showing performance comparable to the state of the art.

Precise definitions of saliency and explicit models of eye movements are crucial in computational vision and cognitive science. Psychologists have long contended with ambiguous notions of saliency, and the authors offer a novel and inventive framework (to the best of my knowledge) that could help clarify what makes a stimulus salient and provide a formal bottom-up model of eye movements. Although it may not be state of the art on every metric across all datasets, it performs well and brings a refreshingly different perspective to the problem.

Regrettably, some of the above rests on inference because the manuscript is difficult to read and follow. I recommend thorough proofreading and the expansion of abbreviations (both within the equations and those used in Section 3). I also suggest moving the two-page Appendix to supplementary material and using the extra pages to define each variable and function employed (even if standard in your field—the NIPS audience is multidisciplinary, and some readers will otherwise struggle with the mathematics).

As a psychologist, I would like to see stronger connections to the psychological literature on eye movements as optimal information-gathering steps. For example, see Najemnik, J., & Geisler, W. S. (2008). Eye movement statistics in humans are consistent with an optimal search strategy. Journal of Vision, 8(3), 1–14. There are other relevant papers (particularly from Geisler’s lab), which could guide the authors into that literature. I would be interested in a discussion comparing their approach to these findings (e.g., are they reformulations of similar models, or do they provide complementary information that could be integrated into a better model?).
