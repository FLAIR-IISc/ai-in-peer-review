Summary
The paper introduces EYMOL, a novel framework for modeling eye movements that departs from standard saliency-map approaches. Rather than predicting isolated fixation points via maximization on a static saliency landscape, EYMOL models full gaze trajectories under a Least Action Principle. While I am not an expert in perception or eye-movement modeling, the core idea and its contrast with prior work are clearly conveyed: the model treats gaze as a dynamical process governed by a principled objective, not just a sequence of pointwise selections.

Theoretical contribution
The shift from saliency-based selection to trajectory-based modeling grounded in a least-action formulation is conceptually appealing and potentially impactful. Some of the more technical details—particularly in the appendix—are beyond my expertise in physics and thus difficult for me to fully verify. Nevertheless, the main assumptions, objective, and modeling pipeline are explained well enough to follow the high-level logic and to understand how EYMOL differs from existing models.

Empirical evaluation
The authors apply EYMOL to a real eye-movement dataset and compare it against several established alternatives. Although EYMOL does not outperform competitors on every metric, its performance is generally strong and competitive, lending support to the plausibility and usefulness of the proposed framework. The evaluation appears fair and sufficiently broad to demonstrate validity.

Strengths
- Novel theoretical framing that models gaze as a trajectory, not a set of isolated fixations.
- Clear articulation of how this approach differs from mainstream saliency-map methods.
- Empirical comparisons on real data against multiple baselines, with favorable overall results.
- Potential to stimulate new research directions in vision science and computational perception.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Accessibility: The heavy physics-based derivations in the appendix may limit accessibility for a typical NIPS audience. A more intuitive, self-contained explanation in the main text would help.
- Validation depth: While results are favorable, it would be useful to better understand the conditions under which EYMOL excels or struggles. For instance, clarifying the model’s sensitivity to parameter choices, noise, and stimulus characteristics would strengthen the case.
- Interpretability: More discussion of how the least-action formulation maps onto known visual or cognitive mechanisms (e.g., attentional priorities, task demands) would improve interpretability and relevance for perception researchers.
- Computational considerations: A brief analysis of computational cost and scalability relative to saliency-based models would be informative for practical adoption.

Significance and venue fit
I expect this work to interest the vision science community, particularly those attending meetings like VSS, because it frames eye movements within a principled dynamical systems perspective. Its novelty and coherence are strengths. My uncertainty lies in its fit for NIPS: the contribution is more theoretical and domain-specific than many NIPS papers, and some technical portions may be difficult for the broader machine learning audience to evaluate. I would defer to reviewers with deeper expertise in eye-movement modeling and physics-based formulations.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a concise, intuitive overview of the Least Action Principle as applied here, with minimal math, early in the paper.
- Include ablations or sensitivity analyses to reveal which components of the objective most drive performance.
- Clarify links between model components and established findings in perception/attention to situate the approach within the literature.
- Report or discuss runtime and scalability to guide future applications.
- If possible, expand on how the framework could incorporate task goals or context to handle goal-directed gaze.

Overall assessment
This paper advances a fresh and well-motivated perspective on eye-movement modeling, supported by solid empirical comparisons. While not uniformly state of the art across metrics, the results are competitive and validate the approach. I view the work as a meaningful contribution to perception science with the potential to inspire further empirical and theoretical research. Its suitability for NIPS may depend on the program’s appetite for theory-driven, domain-focused contributions; I would lean toward a borderline recommendation and encourage input from area experts.
