This computational neuroscience paper proposes a bottom-up visual feature saliency model, modified by dynamical systems modeling, to capture the dynamics of scanpaths. The paper presents a set of intriguing ideas, particularly in defining "affordances" such as curiosity and brightness invariance that drive the scan-path, as well as the concept of the Least Action Principle. Although these "affordances" are somewhat ad hoc and lack further motivation, they contribute to the paper's appeal.

In the context of the Least Action Principle and its derivation, it would have been beneficial to relate it to existing work in eye movement research, such as past and recent studies by the Wolpert lab. While the lack of biological motivation might be a minor issue in the current era of deep learning models for static visual salience, the model's mixed performance compared to these models is a concern. Given the model's claim to capture biological eye movement dynamics, it would have been interesting to see figures in the main paper characterizing the generated scan paths, not only in terms of their overall statistics at matching eye movements but also providing a more detailed analysis.
