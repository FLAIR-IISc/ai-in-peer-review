

This computational neuroscience paper proposes a bottom-up visual feature saliency model enhanced by dynamical systems modeling to capture scanpath dynamics. The work presents a compelling set of ideas, particularly in defining "affordances" (curiosity, brightness invariance) that drive scanpaths and the application of the Least Action Principle. However, these affordances are somewhat ad hoc and lack deeper theoretical motivation.  

Regarding the Least Action Principle and its derivation, it would have been valuable to connect this framework to established work in eye movement research, such as the seminal and recent studies from the Wolpert laboratory. While the absence of explicit biological motivation is a minor concern in an era dominated by benchmark-busting deep learning models for static visual salience, this model's primary limitation lies in its subpar performance relative to state-of-the-art approaches. Given the paper's claim to model biological eye movement dynamics, the main text should include visualizations of the generated scanpaths—not merely static statistics—to rigorously evaluate their temporal and spatial fidelity. The current presentation omits this critical validation.
