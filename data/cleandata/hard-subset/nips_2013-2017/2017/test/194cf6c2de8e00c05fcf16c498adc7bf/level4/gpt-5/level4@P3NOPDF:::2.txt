Summary
This paper proposes a variational formulation of bottom-up visual attention for static and dynamic scenes. Eye movements are modeled as trajectories that minimize an energy function composed of: (1) a harmonic-oscillator term that constrains motion within image borders and a finite retinal field; (2) a curiosity-driven term emphasizing regions with large luminance changes at both fine and blurred scales; and (3) a brightness-invariance term tied to local changes in brightness. From this objective, the authors derive differential equations governing gaze dynamics and predict scanpaths given initial position and velocity. Quantitative evaluation on datasets of static and dynamic scenes with human eye-tracking shows performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a clear variational principle and dynamical system for gaze, unifying static and dynamic scenes.
- Formalization of saliency as energy terms grounded in image measurements and motion constraints.
- Empirical performance that is competitive with leading approaches.
- Potential to bridge computational vision and cognitive science by providing a principled framework for eye-movement modeling.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- The paper is difficult to follow. Many symbols and abbreviations (especially in Section 3) are not defined at first use, and equations are not self-contained.
- Some key ideas are ambiguously described (e.g., “brightness invariance increases as a function of changes in brightness” needs sign and role clarification).
- Important details of the derivation and implementation are either missing or relegated to an appendix that could be better used for clarifying notation.

Requests for clarification
- Energy functional: Provide the explicit form, including all terms, signs, weighting coefficients, and units; list and define every symbol and operator.
- Harmonic-oscillator/border term: Specify the potential near image boundaries, retina size, damping, and whether the dynamics are under/over-damped. Explain how the model accounts for saccades versus smooth pursuit.
- Curiosity-driven term: Detail the multiscale construction (filters, blur kernel, pyramid design), normalization across scales, and relation to local contrast.
- Brightness-invariance term: Make the formulation explicit. Is it a brightness-constancy penalty using temporal derivatives (e.g., I_t + v·∇I), or another measure? How is it estimated in noise?
- Variational derivation: Include Euler–Lagrange equations and boundary conditions used for the trajectories, and how saccade onset/offset is treated.
- Parameters: Describe how weights are chosen (fixed a priori, tuned, or learned) and provide sensitivity analyses and ablations showing each term’s contribution.
- Initial conditions: Clarify how initial fixation and velocity are set and report sensitivity to these choices.
- Numerics: Describe the integration method (solver, step size, stability) and provide runtime information.

Evaluation methodology
- Datasets and splits: Specify which static and dynamic benchmarks are used, with training/validation/test splits if applicable.
- Baselines: Ensure fair comparisons with clear handling of center bias and identical input modalities for all models.
- Metrics: Report standard metrics (e.g., AUC, sAUC, NSS, KL, CC, scanpath similarity), with confidence intervals and statistical tests. Include qualitative examples and failure cases.
- Dynamic evaluation: Distinguish frame-wise saliency maps from full scanpath prediction and evaluate both where relevant.

Relation to prior work
- Situate the model more explicitly relative to classic bottom-up saliency (e.g., Itti–Koch–Niebur), variational saliency formulations, and dynamical/optimal-control models of eye movements.
- Connect to the psychological literature that frames eye movements as information gathering under uncertainty. In particular, discuss Najemnik & Geisler (2008) and related ideal-observer/optimal-search work. Are your variational laws approximations to, or complementary with, expected information gain or Bayesian decision-theoretic objectives? Could your energy be interpreted as a surrogate cost in that framework? A conceptual comparison and, if feasible, an empirical comparison on search datasets would strengthen the paper.
- Clarify the bottom-up scope and limitations. How might top-down goals or task demands be incorporated (e.g., by adding a task prior term to the energy)?

Presentation suggestions
- Thorough proofreading; expand all abbreviations at first use; ensure equations are fully self-contained.
- Move the two-page appendix to the supplementary material and use the space to provide: a notation table, explicit definitions of all variables/functions, parameter values, and a concise algorithm/pseudocode for prediction.
- Improve figure captions; add schematics of the energy landscape and example trajectories in static and dynamic scenes.
- Release code and scripts to support reproducibility.

Overall assessment
- Quality: A promising and principled approach with competitive results, but the current exposition obscures the contribution and omits key details.
- Clarity: Needs substantial revision for readability, completeness, and accessibility to a broad NIPS audience.
- Originality: High within bottom-up and dynamical modeling of attention.
- Significance: Moderate to high, especially if connections to optimal-search literature are articulated and methodological details are clarified.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on major revisions to clarify notation and derivations, strengthen the empirical evaluation, and position the approach relative to optimal-control/ideal-observer models of eye movements.
