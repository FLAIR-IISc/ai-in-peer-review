- Proposes a novel variational framework modeling visual attention from three principles: bounded trajectory motion, curiosity-driven attention, and brightness invariance.  
- Derives Euler-Lagrange differential equations from a generalized Least Action Principle to predict eye movements in static and dynamic scenes.  
- Computes saliency maps as a byproduct of attention dynamics rather than as a primary output.  
- Demonstrates competitive performance on MIT1003, CAT2000, and SFU video datasets without training on fixation data.  
- Theoretical derivations are rigorous and technically sound, with well-defined mathematics in Section 2.2.  
- The approximation of the brightness invariance term (Equation 6) is under-justified and its impact on performance across image types is not analyzed.  
- Parameter estimation via simulated annealing is appropriate but lacks detailed parameter sensitivity analysis.  
- Exposition is unclear: many mathematical terms/variables are introduced without sufficient explanation, hindering accessibility.  
- Essential technical details are in the appendix and should be moved to supplementary material to improve readability.  
- The connection between the variational model and psychological mechanisms (e.g., the meaning of "curiosity") is insufficiently explained.  
- Original contribution: novel unified framework linking attention dynamics to physics and integrating curiosity and brightness invariance.  
- Needs stronger distinction from related computational neuroscience work, especially Najemnik & Geisler’s optimal foraging/search models.  
- Significance is promising—predicts scanpaths and saliency maps from one model—but the advance beyond competitive benchmark performance is not clearly articulated.  
- Pros: novel variational approach, physics-based theoretical grounding, competitive results without fixation training, produces both scanpaths and saliency maps.  
- Cons: unclear mathematical exposition and psychological links, unjustified brightness invariance approximation, limited engagement with optimal search literature, unclear overall advancement.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions; requested fixes include moving appendix to supplementary material, clarifying variables and psychological links, strengthening ties to Najemnik & Geisler, and better articulating how the model advances the state of the art.
