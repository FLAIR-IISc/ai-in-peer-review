### Summary

This paper introduces a novel, physics-inspired model of bottom-up visual attention, termed EYMOL (EYe MOvement Laws). The central thesis is that attentional scanpaths emerge from variational laws, analogous to the Principle of Least Action in classical mechanics. The authors formulate a Lagrangian where the kinetic energy has its standard mechanical interpretation, and the potential energy is a composite of three principles: (1) a "boundedness" term that confines the trajectory to the visual field, (2) a "curiosity" principle that attracts attention to regions with high-detail (image gradients), and (3) a "brightness invariance" constraint, borrowed from optical flow estimation, which promotes fixation on static scenes and tracking of moving objects. By finding the stationary point of the associated action functional, the authors derive the Euler-Lagrange equations that govern the scanpath's dynamics. Saliency maps, used for evaluation, are generated as a byproduct by aggregating the locations visited by simulated scanpaths over time. The model is evaluated on standard static image and dynamic video saliency datasets.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Conceptual Elegance:** The core idea of framing scanpath generation as a variational problem is highly original and provides an elegant, principled alternative to the more heuristic or purely data-driven approaches common in the field. The connection to fundamental principles in physics is both intellectually appealing and offers a unifying mathematical framework.

2.  **Unified Model of Saccades and Fixation/Tracking:** A significant strength is the model's ability to account for different aspects of eye movements within a single framework. The "curiosity" term naturally drives saccadic exploration, while the "brightness invariance" term provides a mechanism for both fixation and smooth pursuit, which are often handled by separate modules in other models.

3.  **Focus on Dynamics:** The paper correctly shifts the focus from predicting static saliency maps to modeling the underlying dynamics of the scanpath. This is a more ambitious and arguably more fundamental goal for a model of visual attention.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Justification of a Priori Principles:** The "variational laws" that form the basis of the model—specifically the "curiosity" and "brightness invariance" terms—are postulated without strong grounding in cognitive science or neuroscience. While intuitively plausible, they feel somewhat ad-hoc. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion motivating why these specific principles, among many other possibilities, should be considered foundational laws of attention.

2.  **Lack of Connection to Related Work in Motor Control:** The application of variational principles and optimal control theory to model biological movement, including saccadic eye movements, has a rich history (e.g., the work of Harris and Wolpert). The paper misses an important opportunity to situate its contribution within this broader literature and discuss how its proposed principles relate to or differ from established theories of motor control optimality.

3.  **Insufficient Validation of the Core Claim:** The central contribution is a model of scanpath *dynamics*. However, the entire experimental validation rests on comparing aggregate, time-averaged saliency maps against human fixation density maps. This evaluation method discards all temporal information and fails to assess the model's primary claim. The paper presents no analysis of the generated scanpaths themselves (e.g., distributions of saccade amplitudes, velocities, or fixation durations) or a quantitative comparison of these dynamic properties against human eye-tracking data. This is a critical omission.

4.  **Moderate Performance on Saliency Benchmarks:** While the goal is not solely to achieve state-of-the-art performance, the model's results on standard saliency benchmarks are not competitive with contemporary, particularly deep learning-based, models. This performance gap raises questions about the descriptive power of the proposed "laws," especially since the model is designed as a bottom-up system, which is precisely what these benchmarks evaluate.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions

*   To address the validation gap, I strongly urge the authors to include an analysis that directly compares the dynamic properties of the generated scanpaths with human scanpaths. This could involve comparing saccade length and orientation distributions, or using more advanced scanpath similarity metrics.
*   It would be highly beneficial to include figures in the main paper showing examples of generated scanpaths overlaid on images and video frames. This would provide crucial qualitative insight into the model's behavior and help readers assess its plausibility.
*   The authors should consider adding a discussion that connects their work to the optimal control literature in motor neuroscience. This would provide valuable context and strengthen the paper's theoretical foundations.
*   The motivation for the periodic modulation between local (`b_x`) and peripheral (`p_x`) fields in the curiosity term (Eq. 4) is brief. Expanding on its role in preventing the model from getting "trapped" and its potential biological correlates would improve clarity.
