## Review - Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes

This paper presents a novel investigation into the mechanisms underlying human visual attention in both static and dynamic imagery. The authors propose a computational model of attention, framed as an energy minimization problem governing eye movements, built upon three key principles: (1) constraints on eye movements modeled as a harmonic oscillator operating within retinal boundaries, (2) a “curiosity” principle that prioritizes regions exhibiting significant brightness changes at multiple scales, and (3) a brightness invariance component sensitive to luminance variations. From these principles, the authors derive differential equations to predict eye movements across images, contingent on initial conditions. The model’s performance is quantitatively evaluated against human eye-tracking data from static and dynamic scenes, demonstrating competitive results compared to existing state-of-the-art approaches.

The formal definition of visual saliency and the modeling of eye movements represent fundamental challenges in computational vision and cognitive science. The authors address these challenges with an innovative model – to the best of my knowledge, a unique contribution – that offers a promising avenue for advancing our understanding of the factors driving salience and providing a formal, bottom-up framework for modeling eye movements. While the method doesn’t consistently achieve state-of-the-art performance across all metrics and datasets, its overall performance is strong and offers a distinctive perspective on this complex problem.

However, the clarity of the presentation currently hinders a full evaluation of the work’s merits. The paper is difficult to follow, and I strongly recommend thorough proofreading by colleagues. Furthermore, the authors should expand upon all abbreviations used, both within the mathematical equations and throughout Section 3. The current Appendix (spanning two pages) would be more appropriately placed as supplementary material; the reclaimed space should be used to explicitly define each variable and function employed – even those considered standard within the field. The NIPS audience is highly interdisciplinary, and many readers will require these definitions to fully grasp the mathematical details. 

From a psychological perspective, it would be valuable for the authors to contextualize their work within the broader literature on eye movements as strategies for efficient information gathering. Specifically, I suggest engaging with work such as Najemnik & Geisler (2008, *Journal of Vision*) which demonstrates the consistency of human eye movement statistics with optimal search strategies. The authors may find further relevant research within Geisler’s lab. A discussion comparing and contrasting their approach with these findings – for example, whether the model replicates existing results or provides complementary insights – would strengthen the paper considerably.
