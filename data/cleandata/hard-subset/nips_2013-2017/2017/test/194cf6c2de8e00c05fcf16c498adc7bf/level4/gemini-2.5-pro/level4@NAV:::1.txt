This paper presents a computational neuroscience model that integrates a bottom-up visual saliency framework with dynamical systems principles to simulate scanpath dynamics. The manuscript was an enjoyable read and contains several compelling concepts, particularly the definition of "affordances" (e.g., curiosity, brightness invariance) and the application of the Least Action Principle to drive the scanpath.

However, the justification for these "affordances" appears somewhat ad-hoc, and the paper would be stronger if their selection was more thoroughly motivated. In its discussion of the Least Action Principle, the work would also be significantly enhanced by connecting its derivation to relevant literature in eye movement research, such as the foundational and contemporary work from the Wolpert lab.

While a lack of deep biological motivation can be a minor point for deep learning models that achieve state-of-the-art performance on static saliency benchmarks, a critical issue here is that the model's performance is poor compared to these established methods. Furthermore, given the central claim of capturing biological eye movement dynamics, the evaluation is insufficient. It is essential for the main paper to present figures and metrics that characterize the generated scanpaths themselves, moving beyond aggregate static measures to analyze their dynamic properties.
