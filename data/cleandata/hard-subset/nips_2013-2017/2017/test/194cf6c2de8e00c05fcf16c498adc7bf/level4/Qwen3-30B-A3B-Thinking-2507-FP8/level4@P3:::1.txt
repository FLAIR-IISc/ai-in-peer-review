

This paper proposes a novel model of visual attention based on a generalized Least Action Principle from physics, framing eye movement scanpaths as trajectories of a particle in a potential field. The authors introduce three key "affordances" driving attention: boundedness (constrained to the retina), curiosity (attraction to regions with high detail gradients), and brightness invariance (for fixation and tracking). These principles are encoded in a Lagrangian whose stationary point yields Euler-Lagrange equations describing attention dynamics. The model computes saliency maps as a byproduct of scanpath simulation, rather than as a primary output. The authors validate their approach on static image datasets (MIT1003, CAT2000) and a video dataset (SFU), reporting competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical motivation by connecting visual attention to fundamental principles in physics, which is a compelling angle for NIPS. The authors clearly relate their work to prior computational attention models (e.g., Itti-Koch, Judd et al.) while distinguishing their variational approach from centralized saliency map models. The derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations is mathematically sound, with appropriate technical details in the appendix. The model's simplicity and real-time execution capability represent a notable advantage over complex deep learning approaches that require extensive training.

The primary strength of the paper lies in its theoretical originality. The integration of curiosity-driven exploration and brightness invariance into a unified variational framework is conceptually novel and provides a principled alternative to ad hoc saliency models. This approach offers a genuine model of scanpath dynamics rather than merely predicting static saliency maps, which aligns well with the neuroscience perspective on attention. The connection to physical principles like the Least Action Principle is intellectually satisfying and provides a clear theoretical foundation.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses in evaluation and presentation. While the authors claim their model "competes well with state-of-the-art models," the experimental results are presented in a way that obscures the actual performance. The paper reports metrics like NSS and AUC-Judd but lacks direct visual comparisons between human and model-generated scanpaths. For a paper claiming to model biological eye movement dynamics, this is a critical omission - readers cannot assess whether the generated scanpaths resemble human behavior beyond aggregate metrics.

The model's performance appears mediocre compared to recent deep learning approaches. Table 2 shows the model achieves "best score in half of the cases" across different metrics, but this is not impressive given the current state of the field. The paper does not clearly explain why their simple model outperforms more sophisticated methods on some metrics while falling behind on others. The authors acknowledge their model was "not designed for the specific task" of video saliency, which weakens the significance claim.

The theoretical motivation for the three affordances is somewhat underdeveloped. While the mathematical formulation is sound, the authors do not convincingly establish why these particular principles should govern visual attention. The "curiosity" term, for instance, appears somewhat ad hoc without deeper biological justification. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how these principles relate to known cognitive mechanisms or neurophysiological evidence.

Regarding clarity, the paper is generally well-written, but the experimental section could be more precise. The description of the parameter estimation process using simulated annealing is somewhat vague, and the paper does not sufficiently explain why the authors chose specific parameter ranges or how they validated their approach. The appendix provides valuable technical details, but the main paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the key equations.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the modest performance gains. While the theoretical framework is interesting, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate how this model advances the state of the art in a meaningful way. The authors claim their model "offers a truly model of eye movements," but without clear evidence of superior biological plausibility or predictive power, this claim is difficult to support.

In summary, the paper presents an intellectually interesting theoretical approach that connects visual attention to principles in physics. The model's mathematical formulation is sound, and the approach to saliency as a byproduct of scanpath dynamics is conceptually novel. However, the experimental validation is insufficient, the performance does not match the best current methods, and the biological motivation for the key principles is underdeveloped.

Arguments for acceptance:
- The theoretical framework is novel and well-motivated
- The model offers a principled alternative to ad hoc saliency models
- The approach provides a genuine model of scanpath dynamics

Arguments against acceptance:
- Performance is mediocre compared to state-of-the-art methods
- Critical lack of visual comparisons between human and model-generated scanpaths
- Key theoretical assumptions (the three affordances) lack sufficient biological justification
- The paper does not convincingly demonstrate how this work advances the field beyond existing approaches

Overall, this paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution with potential but falls short in experimental validation and significance. It would be strengthened by more direct comparisons of actual scanpaths, better justification of the theoretical principles, and a more compelling demonstration of how this approach advances the field beyond existing methods. While the theoretical angle is appealing, the current evidence does not sufficiently support the claim that this model offers a significant advance in visual attention modeling.
