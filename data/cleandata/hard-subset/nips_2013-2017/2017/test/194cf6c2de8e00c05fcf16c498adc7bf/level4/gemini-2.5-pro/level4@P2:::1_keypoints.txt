- Paper proposes EYMOL: a physics-inspired variational model where scanpaths arise from a Lagrangian with kinetic energy and a composite potential.  
- Potential combines three terms: boundedness (keep gaze in frame), curiosity (attract to high-detail regions), and brightness invariance (fixation/tracking via optical-flow-like constraint).  
- Euler–Lagrange equations derive the scanpath dynamics; saliency maps are produced by aggregating simulated gaze locations.  
- Evaluated on standard static image and dynamic video saliency datasets.  
- Strength: Highly original framing—scanpath generation as a principled variational problem linked to classical mechanics.  
- Strength: Single unified model accounts for saccades, fixation, and smooth pursuit via different terms in the potential.  
- Strength: Emphasis on modeling temporal dynamics rather than only static saliency maps.  
- Weakness: The curiosity and brightness-invariance priors are postulated without strong cognitive/neuroscience justification and feel ad-hoc.  
- Weakness: Lacks engagement with optimal control/motor-control literature (e.g., Harris & Wolpert) that also uses variational principles for biological movement.  
- Weakness: Experimental validation focuses on aggregated saliency maps, discarding temporal information and failing to test the core dynamic claim.  
- Weakness: Moderate performance on saliency benchmarks compared to contemporary (especially deep) models, raising concerns about descriptive power.  
- Suggestions: Add quantitative comparisons of generated vs. human scanpath dynamics (saccade lengths, velocities, fixation durations, scanpath similarity metrics).  
- Suggestions: Include qualitative figures showing example scanpaths overlaid on images and video frames.  
- Suggestions: Discuss connections to optimal control literature and expand the motivation/biological plausibility for the periodic local/peripheral modulation in the curiosity term.
