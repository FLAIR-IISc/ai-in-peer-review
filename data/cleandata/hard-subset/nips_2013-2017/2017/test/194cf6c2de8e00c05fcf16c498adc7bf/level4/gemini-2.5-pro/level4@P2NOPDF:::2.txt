This paper investigates the principles governing human visual attention in both static and dynamic scenes. The authors propose a model based on three core principles, which are formulated as an energy function to be minimized by eye movements. These principles are: (1) a spatial constraint, modeled as a harmonic oscillator, that confines gaze within the image boundaries; (2) a "curiosity-driven" principle that prioritizes regions with high spatial frequency content at multiple scales; and (3) a principle of brightness invariance that favors regions with significant temporal changes in luminance.

By minimizing a cost function derived from these principles, the authors obtain a set of differential equations that predict gaze trajectories given an initial position and velocity. The model is evaluated quantitatively on several datasets of static and dynamic scenes with corresponding human eye-tracking data. The results demonstrate that the proposed method performs comparably to other state-of-the-art techniques.

**Strengths**

The formal modeling of saliency and saccadic eye movements addresses a critical challenge in computational vision and cognitive science. The field has long struggled with vague or purely operational definitions of saliency, and this paper presents a novel and principled approach to the problem. By deriving scanpaths from a clear set of first principles, this work offers a potential path toward a more formal, theoretical understanding of bottom-up attention.

While the model's performance does not uniformly surpass state-of-the-art methods across all metrics and datasets, it is highly competitive. More importantly, its primary contribution lies in offering a refreshingly distinct theoretical perspective on a well-studied problem.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

A significant weakness of this submission is its lack of clarity. The paper is difficult to follow, which forced me to make some conjectures when summarizing the approach. The manuscript would benefit greatly from thorough proofreading and a comprehensive review of its exposition. Specifically:

1.  **Clarity of Mathematical Formulation:** The authors should explicitly define every variable and function used in their equations. Abbreviations and symbols, both within the mathematical derivations and in Section 3, should be expanded and clearly explained.
2.  **Accessibility for a Broad Audience:** Given the interdisciplinary nature of the NIPS audience, it is crucial that the mathematical formalism is self-contained. Relying on field-specific conventions without explicit definitions makes the work inaccessible to a large portion of potential readers.
3.  **Paper Structure:** I strongly recommend moving the current two-page Appendix to the supplementary material. The two pages gained should be used to expand the explanations in the main body of the paper, particularly regarding the mathematical definitions and the intuition behind the chosen principles.

**Connecting to Broader Literature**

As a psychologist, I believe the paper would be substantially strengthened by connecting its theoretical framework to the existing psychological literature on eye movements as an optimal information-gathering process. For instance, the work of Najemnik & Geisler (e.g., "Eye movement statistics in humans are consistent with an optimal search strategy," Journal of Vision, 2008) and others from that research group provides a rich context for this type of modeling.

A discussion is needed to contextualize this model with respect to these established theories. Such a comparison would be highly valuable: Does the proposed model offer a different formulation of similar optimal principles, or does it provide complementary insights that could be integrated to produce a more comprehensive model of visual attention? Addressing these connections would significantly increase the impact and relevance of the work.
