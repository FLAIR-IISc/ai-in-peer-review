Paper summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a continuous-time, variational model of visual attention that generates eye-movement trajectories (scanpaths) as stationary points of a generalized action functional. The Lagrangian combines: (i) a boundary potential that constrains motion within the retinal field via harmonic penalties at image borders; (ii) a “curiosity” term favoring regions with strong local and peripheral gradients, alternating over time to avoid getting trapped; and (iii) a brightness invariance term inspired by the optical flow constraint, encouraging fixations and motion tracking. The authors derive Euler–Lagrange equations and solve the resulting second-order ODEs to produce scanpaths. Saliency maps are then obtained by aggregating visitation counts over many trajectories and initial conditions. Parameters are tuned via simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000-TRAIN, and the model is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video datasets using standard saliency metrics. The approach performs competitively with several established methods.

The work is positioned relative to bottom-up saliency models (e.g., Koch & Ullman; Itti et al.; Bruce & Tsotsos; Itti & Baldi) and complements more recent learning-based methods by providing a physics-inspired generative mechanism for scanpaths. It also connects to classic optical-flow formulations via the brightness constancy term. However, the paper would benefit from stronger engagement with optimal-control/active-vision accounts of eye movements in psychology and vision science (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler, 2005/2008), saccadic biomechanics and optimality principles, and dynamic neural field models, to better contextualize the novelty and implications of the variational formulation.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clear, unified variational framework linking curiosity, fixation, and motion tracking via an action principle. This is a fresh alternative to saliency-map-centric pipelines.
- Generative scanpaths: The model directly produces trajectories, with the saliency map arising as a byproduct. This aligns more naturally with behavioral data than post hoc saliency maps.
- Parsimony and interpretability: Each term in the Lagrangian has a transparent behavioral interpretation, and the brightness invariance term is well-motivated by optical flow theory.
- Empirical competitiveness: Despite its simplicity, the model attains performance comparable to strong baselines on multiple datasets, including video.
- Solid mathematical treatment: The derivation of Euler–Lagrange equations and stability conditions (e.g., constraints on λ) is careful and useful.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and presentation: The paper is difficult to follow due to inconsistent notation, undefined variables and abbreviations, and typographical issues. Important modeling and implementation choices (e.g., ω, m, k, solver details) are underspecified.
- Fairness and confounds in evaluation: The preprocessing imposes a center bias (Gaussian weighting) and the trajectories are initialized near the image center; both can inflate center-sensitive metrics (e.g., AUC). The grid search for blur and center bias is tuned on training data; it is unclear whether comparable tuning is applied to baselines.
- Limited ablation: Beyond V1 vs V2, there is no systematic ablation of model components (curiosity vs brightness invariance vs boundary term; local vs peripheral gradient; effect of ω) or of preprocessing choices (center bias, number of runs).
- Parameter estimation: Simulated annealing over a small subset may overfit; details of the search domain, stopping, and selected parameters (ηb, ηp, λ, ω, m, k) are not reported, limiting reproducibility.
- Scope of behavioral validation: Evaluation focuses on saliency metrics. Scanpath statistics (saccade amplitudes, fixation durations, temporal correlations, string-edit distance to human sequences) are not analyzed, missing an opportunity to test the model’s core contribution.

Quality
The mathematical formulation is sound, and the empirical evaluation is broadly adequate for a saliency paper. However, more detail is needed for reproducibility (parameter values, solver settings, runtime), and additional ablations are necessary to isolate the contribution of each term and of center bias. Claims of “real-time” performance should be quantified.

Clarity
The manuscript requires substantial editing. Many symbols are introduced without definition, some equations are hard to parse, and several typographical and grammatical issues impede comprehension. Moving the two-page Appendix to the supplement and using the space to clearly define all variables, functions, and parameters would help the NIPS audience.

Originality
The use of a least-action principle to jointly capture curiosity, fixation, and tracking is original within the saliency literature. The brightness invariance term echoes optical-flow constraints, but its integration into a Lagrangian for eye movements is novel. The paper should distinguish more explicitly from optimal search/active vision frameworks and from prior potential-field/dynamic systems models of gaze.

Significance
The approach offers a principled, interpretable framework that could inspire further work on generative models of attention and on bridging bottom-up and dynamical systems perspectives. While not uniformly state-of-the-art, competitive performance across metrics and datasets suggests practical relevance, particularly for dynamic scenes.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarity and notation
  - Provide a table of symbols with definitions and units; define m, k, ω, α, temperature schedule, solver type/step, integration time, and initialization distribution.
  - Fix typos, standardize equation numbering, and expand all abbreviations on first use.
  - Move detailed derivations to the supplement; use main text space for intuition and model overview.

- Experimental rigor
  - Report exact parameter values selected by SA and sensitivity analyses. Justify search ranges and include the λ < m/4 constraint in practice.
  - Provide ablations: (a) remove center bias and/or initialize uniformly; (b) curiosity-only, brightness-only, and full model; (c) local vs peripheral gradient and the effect of ω.
  - Quantify “real-time” performance (e.g., fps, CPU/GPU).
  - Ensure fair comparison: clarify whether postprocessing (blur/center bias) and tuning are applied equally to baselines.

- Behavioral validation and related work
  - Compare generated scanpaths to human sequences using temporal metrics and distributions. Analyze whether the model over-fixates edges due to brightness invariance in static images.
  - Situate the work within optimal search and active vision literature (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler, 2005/2008) and discuss similarities/differences and potential integration.

Pros
- Elegant, interpretable variational formulation
- Generates scanpaths; saliency is a byproduct
- Competitive results on images and video
- Clear links to optical flow and physical principles

Cons
- Clarity and notation issues hinder accessibility
- Center bias and initialization may inflate metrics
- Limited ablations and parameter reporting
- Weak connection to psychological optimality literature
- Reproducibility details (code/parameters) missing

Overall assessment
A thought-provoking and theoretically grounded contribution with promising empirical results. With improved clarity, more rigorous ablations, and stronger behavioral validation, this work could make a meaningful impact by re-introducing principled dynamical modeling into contemporary attention research. I encourage revision along the lines above.
