This computational neuroscience paper proposes a bottom-up visual feature saliency model, modified by dynamical systems modeling, that aims to capture the dynamics of scanpaths. The paper presents a set of intriguing ideas, particularly in defining "affordances" (such as curiosity and brightness invariance) that drive the scan-path, as well as the concept of the Least Action Principle.

While the idea of incorporating affordances into the model is innovative, it feels somewhat ad hoc and lacks further motivation. The authors could strengthen their argument by providing more context or theoretical justification for these affordances.

The Least Action Principle, a key component of the model, would benefit from a more explicit connection to existing work in eye movement research. For instance, a discussion of the relationship between the proposed model and the work of the Wolpert lab, both past and recent, would add valuable depth to the paper. Although the lack of biological motivation may be a minor issue in the context of current deep learning models of static visual salience, the model's mixed performance compared to these models is a more significant concern.

Given the model's claim to capture biological eye movement dynamics, it would be beneficial to include more detailed characterizations of the generated scan paths in the main paper. Specifically, the authors should provide figures that illustrate the dynamics of the scan paths, rather than just presenting overall statistics. This would allow readers to better understand the model's performance and its ability to replicate real-world eye movements.

Additionally, the paper could be improved by providing more context about the strengths and limitations of the model, particularly in comparison to other state-of-the-art approaches. This would enable readers to better evaluate the model's contributions and potential applications.

In terms of presentation, the paper is generally well-written, and the ideas are clearly expressed. However, some sections could benefit from more detailed explanations or additional examples to facilitate understanding.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting approach to modeling visual salience and eye movements. While it has some limitations, particularly with regards to its biological motivation and performance, it has the potential to contribute to the field of computational neuroscience. With some revisions to address the mentioned concerns, the paper could be even stronger and more impactful.
