- Proposes a novel variational, physics-inspired model for predicting human visual attention in static and dynamic scenes.  
- Frames attention as minimization of an action functional and derives Euler–Lagrange equations to predict scanpaths from initial position and velocity.  
- Bounding potential: uses a harmonic-oscillator term at image borders to constrain eye movements within a "retina."  
- Curiosity-driven term: attracts attention to high brightness-gradient regions computed from sharp and blurred image versions.  
- Brightness-invariance term: penalizes brightness changes along the scanpath to promote fixation and object tracking.  
- Evaluated quantitatively on multiple standard image and video datasets, achieving performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods.  
- Strength: provides a formal, principled definition of saliency and a novel framework that could bridge computational vision and cognitive science.  
- Strength: offers a different, theoretically grounded perspective despite not being top-ranked on every metric.  
- Main weakness: paper presentation and clarity are poor, making technical details hard to follow and requiring reader interpretation.  
- Recommend thorough proofreading, expansion of abbreviations, and explicit definitions for all variables and functions.  
- Suggest moving lengthy derivations from the two-page Appendix to supplementary material to free space for core explanations and intuition.  
- Suggest stronger connection to psychological literature (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2008) to clarify whether the approach restates or extends optimal-search theories of eye movements.
