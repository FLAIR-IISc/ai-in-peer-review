This computational neuroscience paper presents a bottom-up visual feature saliency model, enhanced with dynamical systems modeling, to capture the dynamics of scanpaths. I found the paper engaging, with several interesting ideas regarding the definition of “affordances” – specifically curiosity and brightness invariance – as drivers of scanpath generation, as well as the application of the Least Action Principle. However, these “affordances” feel somewhat *ad hoc* and lack further justification. 

Concerning the Least Action Principle and its derivation within this framework, it would have been valuable to connect the work to relevant research in eye movement control, such as that from the Wolpert lab, both past and present. While a lack of strong biological motivation is a relatively minor concern given the current trend of benchmark-focused deep learning models for (static) visual salience, the model’s performance relative to these baselines is weak.  As the paper claims to capture biological eye movement dynamics, the main text would benefit from including figures characterizing the generated scanpaths, beyond simply reporting overall statistical matches to eye movement data.
