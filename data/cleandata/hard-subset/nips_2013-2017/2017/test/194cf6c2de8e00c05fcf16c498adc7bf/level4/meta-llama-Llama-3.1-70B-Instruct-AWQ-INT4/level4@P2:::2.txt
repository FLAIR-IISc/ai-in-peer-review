Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes

The authors investigate what locations in static and dynamic images tend to be attended to by humans. They derive a model by first defining three basic principles for visual attention (defined as an energy function to be minimized by the movements of the eye): (1) Eye movements are constrained by a harmonic oscillator at the borders of the image within a limited-sized retina. (2) a "curiosity driven principle" highlighting the regions with large changes in brightness in both a fine and blurred version of the image, and (3) brightness invariance, which increases as a function of changes in brightness. Using a cost function derived from these three functions, the authors derive differential equations for predicting the eye movements across static or dynamic images (depending on the starting location and initial velocity). The authors evaluate their technique quantitatively on data sets of static and dynamic scenes coupled with human eye movements. They demonstrate that their method performs comparable to the state-of-the-art.

The proposed model addresses a critical issue in computational vision and cognitive science, providing a novel and innovative approach to formalize the concept of saliency and model eye movements. The authors' work has the potential to aid in the development of a better understanding of what makes something salient and provide a formal model for eye movements within the bottom-up tradition. Although it is not necessarily state-of-the-art on every metric for every data set, it performs well and provides a refreshingly different perspective on the problem.

The paper's technical soundness is evident in the derivation of the differential equations and the evaluation of the model on various data sets. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the underlying principles and provide a clear explanation of their approach. However, the paper's clarity and organization could be improved. The writing is sometimes dense and hard to follow, and the use of abbreviations and technical terms may be unfamiliar to some readers. I recommend that the authors proofread the paper and expand on abbreviations, both within the equations and in Section 3. Additionally, I suggest moving the 2-page Appendix to supplementary material and using those extra pages to define each variable and function used, even if it is a convention within their own field.

From a psychological perspective, the authors' work could benefit from a more explicit connection to the literature on eye movements as optimal steps for gathering information. For example, Najemnik and Geisler (2008) demonstrated that eye movement statistics in humans are consistent with an optimal search strategy. A discussion of how the authors' approach compares to these findings would be valuable, and I would be interested to see an exploration of whether their model provides independent information that could be integrated to produce a better model.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and innovative approach to modeling visual attention, and its technical soundness and performance are notable. With some revisions to improve clarity and organization, and a more explicit connection to the relevant psychological literature, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.

Strengths:

* Novel and innovative approach to formalize the concept of saliency and model eye movements
* Technical soundness and good understanding of underlying principles
* Performance comparable to state-of-the-art on various data sets

Weaknesses:

* Poor writing and organization, making it hard to follow
* Use of abbreviations and technical terms may be unfamiliar to some readers
* Lack of explicit connection to relevant psychological literature

Recommendations:

* Proofread the paper and expand on abbreviations
* Move the 2-page Appendix to supplementary material and use extra pages to define each variable and function used
* Connect the work to the psychological literature on eye movements as optimal steps for gathering information
* Discuss how the approach compares to other models and whether it provides independent information that could be integrated to produce a better model
