- Proposes a bottom-up visual saliency model augmented with dynamical systems to model scanpath dynamics.
- Introduces “affordances” (e.g., curiosity, brightness invariance) and applies the Least Action Principle as core concepts.
- Affordances appear ad hoc and lack deeper theoretical or biological motivation.
- Derivation of the Least Action Principle lacks explicit links to established eye-movement literature (e.g., Wolpert lab).
- Biological motivation is deemphasized but acceptable given current benchmark-driven trends.
- Major weakness: inconsistent and subpar performance compared to state-of-the-art static saliency models.
- Claim of capturing biological eye-movement dynamics is undermined by poorer quantitative results.
- Main text omits visual evidence of generated scanpaths—no trajectory, fixation distribution, or temporal-dynamics figures.
- Without visualizations, alignment with human scanpath properties (sequence complexity, saccade amplitudes, temporal consistency) is unclear.
- Strongest contributions are the conceptual framing of affordances and using the Least Action Principle as a unifying mechanism.
- Lacks rigorous validation against biological data; affordance design remains speculative.
- Current metrics (AUC, F-measure) are insufficient to demonstrate biological relevance, especially versus data-driven models.
- Authors should justify why a dynamics-focused approach may warrant lower static performance and how it advances temporal modeling.
- Required revisions: clarify affordance motivations, include scanpath visualizations in the main text, and contextualize results among dynamic eye-movement models.
- Without these changes, the work risks being viewed as a theoretical curiosity rather than a substantive advance in modeling visual attention.
