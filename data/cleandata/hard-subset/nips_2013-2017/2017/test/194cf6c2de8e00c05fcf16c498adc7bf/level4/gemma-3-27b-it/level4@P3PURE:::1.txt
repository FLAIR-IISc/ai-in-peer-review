This computational neuroscience paper proposes a bottom-up visual feature saliency model combined with dynamical systems modeling, aiming to capture the dynamics of scanpaths. I enjoyed reading this paper; it presents a set of interesting ideas in defining “affordances” (curiosity, brightness invariance) that drive the scanpath, as well as the application of the Least Action Principle. However, these “affordances” are postulated somewhat ad hoc and lack further motivation. 

In the context of the Least Action Principle and its derivation, it would have been interesting to relate this work to existing research in eye movement dynamics, such as past and recent work from the Wolpert lab. While a lack of strong biological motivation is a relatively minor issue given the current focus on benchmark-busting deep learning models of (static) visual salience, the model’s performance relative to these models is poor. Given the claim that the model captures biological eye movement dynamics, it would have been insightful to see figures in the main paper characterizing the generated scanpaths, and not solely their overall statistics when matching eye movements.
