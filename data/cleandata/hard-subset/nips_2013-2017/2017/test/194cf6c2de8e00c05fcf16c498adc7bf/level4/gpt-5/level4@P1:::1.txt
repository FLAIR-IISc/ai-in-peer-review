This computational neuroscience paper proposes a bottom-up visual saliency model coupled with a dynamical-systems formulation to capture scanpath dynamics. I enjoyed reading it: it contains a set of interesting ideas, notably the definition of “affordances” (curiosity, brightness invariance) that drive the scanpath, and the use of the Least Action Principle. That said, these affordances are somewhat ad hoc and not sufficiently motivated.

Within the Least Action framework, it would be valuable to relate the derivation more explicitly to existing eye-movement and motor-control literature (e.g., work from the Wolpert lab). While the lack of biological grounding might be a minor concern in an era dominated by deep learning models of static visual saliency, a more serious issue is the model’s mixed—and in places poor—performance relative to those benchmark models. Given the claim to capture biological eye-movement dynamics, I would also have liked to see, in the main paper, concrete figures characterizing the generated scanpaths, not only in terms of overall statistics and their match to human eye movements.
