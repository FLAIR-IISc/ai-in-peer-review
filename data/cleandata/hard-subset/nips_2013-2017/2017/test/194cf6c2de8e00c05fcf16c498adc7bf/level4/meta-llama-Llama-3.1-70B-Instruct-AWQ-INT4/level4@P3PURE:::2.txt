Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes

The authors investigate the locations in static and dynamic images that tend to capture human attention. They derive a model based on three fundamental principles for visual attention, defined as an energy function that is minimized by eye movements. These principles are: (1) eye movements are constrained by a harmonic oscillator at the image borders within a limited-sized retina, (2) a "curiosity-driven principle" that highlights regions with large changes in brightness in both fine and blurred versions of the image, and (3) brightness invariance, which increases as a function of changes in brightness. Using a cost function derived from these principles, the authors develop differential equations to predict eye movements across static or dynamic images, depending on the starting location and initial velocity. The authors evaluate their technique quantitatively on datasets of static and dynamic scenes coupled with human eye movements, demonstrating that their method performs comparably to the state-of-the-art.

Formal definitions of saliency and modeling eye movements are critical issues in computational vision and cognitive science. Psychologists have long been plagued by vague definitions of saliency, and the authors propose a novel and innovative model that could aid in developing a better understanding of what makes something salient and provide a formal model for eye movements within the bottom-up tradition. Although the method may not be state-of-the-art on every metric for every dataset, it performs well and offers a refreshingly different perspective on the problem.

Unfortunately, some of my understanding of the paper is based on conjecture, as the writing is poor and difficult to follow. I strongly recommend that the authors have others proofread the paper and expand on abbreviations, both within the equations and in Section 3. I would also suggest moving the two-page Appendix to supplementary material and using the extra pages to define each variable and function used, even if they are conventional within the authors' field. As the NIPS audience comes from diverse disciplines, some readers may struggle to follow the mathematics without clear definitions.

As a psychologist, I would have appreciated a connection between the authors' work and the psychological literature on eye movements as optimal steps for gathering information. For example, Najemnik and Geisler (2008) demonstrate that eye movement statistics in humans are consistent with an optimal search strategy. The authors could explore relevant articles, particularly from Geisler's lab, to provide a pointer to this literature. I would be interested in seeing a discussion of how their approach compares to existing findings, such as whether they are restatements of similar models or provide independent information that could be integrated to produce a better model.
