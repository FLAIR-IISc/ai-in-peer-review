This paper investigates the principles governing human visual attention in both static and dynamic scenes. The authors derive a model from three foundational principles, which are formulated as an energy function that is minimized by eye movements: (1) a constraint, modeled as a harmonic potential, that keeps the gaze within the image boundaries to simulate a finite retina; (2) a "curiosity-driven" principle that favors regions with high luminance contrast at both fine and coarse scales; and (3) a principle of invariance to global brightness, which responds to local changes in luminance. From a cost function derived from these principles, the authors obtain differential equations that predict eye movement trajectories, given an initial gaze position and velocity. The model is evaluated quantitatively on datasets of static and dynamic scenes containing human eye-tracking data, and the authors demonstrate that its performance is comparable to state-of-the-art methods.

The formal definition of saliency and the predictive modeling of eye movements are critical open problems in computational vision and cognitive science. In a field that has long struggled with vague definitions of saliency, the authors’ proposal of a formal, principled model for bottom-up attention is both novel and welcome. While the model does not achieve state-of-the-art results on every metric and dataset, its performance is competitive, and it offers a refreshingly different perspective on the problem.

Unfortunately, a significant weakness of the paper is its lack of clarity. The writing is often difficult to follow, which makes a full understanding of the technical details challenging; my summary above, therefore, involves some degree of interpretation. I strongly recommend that the authors have the paper thoroughly proofread by a native English speaker. Furthermore, all abbreviations and symbols should be explicitly defined, both in the text (e.g., those in Section 3) and within the equations. I would also suggest moving the current two-page Appendix to the supplementary material. The space gained should be used to clearly define each variable and function. This is crucial for the NIPS audience, which comes from many different disciplines. Even if certain notations are standard in the authors' specific field, many readers will struggle to follow the mathematics without these explicit definitions.

From a psychological perspective, the paper would be significantly strengthened by connecting its proposed principles to the extensive literature on eye movements as a mechanism for optimal information gathering. For example, the work by Najemnik and Geisler (2008) on optimal search strategies provides a relevant starting point, and there are many other related articles from this line of research. A discussion of how the proposed model relates to these findings would be valuable. For instance, is the authors' framework a restatement of similar information-theoretic models, or does it provide complementary principles that could be integrated to create a more comprehensive theory of visual attention?
