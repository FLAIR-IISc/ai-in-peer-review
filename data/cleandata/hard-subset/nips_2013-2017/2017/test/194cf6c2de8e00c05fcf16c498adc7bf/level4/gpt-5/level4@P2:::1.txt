Summary
The paper proposes a physics-inspired, bottom-up model of visual attention that generates scanpaths by minimizing a generalized action functional. The Lagrangian combines: (i) a boundary potential that confines the trajectory within the image (retina), (ii) a “curiosity” term that alternates between local and peripheral detail (gradient magnitude on the original image and on a blurred version), and (iii) a brightness-invariance term akin to the optical-flow constraint to encourage fixation and motion tracking. The resulting Euler–Lagrange equations define a second-order dynamical system (EYMOL) whose numerical integration yields eye-movement trajectories. Saliency maps are then obtained as a byproduct by counting trajectory visits over time. Parameters are selected via simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000. Experiments on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU videos report competitive results on some metrics compared to classical saliency models, with the claim that the method is simple, real-time, and unifies saccades and tracking.

Relation to prior work
The formulation is related to classic saliency-map approaches (e.g., Itti–Koch–Ullman), information-theoretic and surprise-based models, and leverages the optical flow brightness-constancy constraint. The least-action perspective is interesting; however, the paper would benefit from a clearer connection to the optimal control literature on eye movements (e.g., work by Wolpert and colleagues on motor control and oculomotor behavior), which offers principled cost formulations and empirical links to saccade statistics.

Strengths
- Provides a unified, explicit dynamical system for scanpath generation that naturally handles both saccades and tracking.
- Elegant variational formulation grounded in a generalized least-action principle; derivations are substantial and placed in the appendix.
- Saliency emerges as a consequence of the dynamics rather than being explicitly optimized.
- Simple to implement and fast to simulate; reproducible methodology with standard numerical integration.
- Evaluated on multiple datasets (static and dynamic scenes).

Weaknesses
- The proposed “affordances” (curiosity term and brightness invariance) feel ad hoc and only weakly motivated biologically; their weights are tuned for benchmark performance rather than grounded in behavior.
- Empirical evaluation focuses mostly on saliency-map metrics; there is little analysis of scanpath properties (e.g., saccade amplitude/duration distributions, fixation clustering, inhibition-of-return) despite the central claim of modeling eye-movement dynamics.
- Mixed performance relative to modern deep learning saliency models; the paper does not convincingly demonstrate state-of-the-art accuracy or a clear trade-off (e.g., interpretability vs. performance).
- Limited discussion of parameter sensitivity and stability; the need for λ < m/4 and instability with large curiosity weights are acknowledged but not deeply analyzed.
- The link to prior oculomotor optimal-control literature is missing, which weakens the theoretical positioning.

Quality
- The mathematical development appears sound, and the appendix provides details. Some terms (e.g., the cosine/sine alternation between local and peripheral cues) would benefit from justification or ablation to show necessity.
- Numerical issues (e.g., constraints on λ, the effect of ω, integration step size, and initial conditions) should be reported more systematically alongside sensitivity analyses.
- The parameter search via simulated annealing on a small subset raises concerns about overfitting and generalization.

Clarity
- Generally well written, though dense. The main text would benefit from figures illustrating exemplar scanpaths and qualitative tracking cases, not just saliency maps.
- Clarify implementation details: initialization distribution, integration time step, ω setting, exact preprocessing (blur radius, center bias), and runtime.
- Tighten presentation of the curiosity term and the rationale for its time-varying mixing.

Originality
- Applying a generalized least-action framework to derive attention dynamics that produce scanpaths and saliency as a byproduct is a novel and conceptually appealing angle.
- The brightness-invariance term is well known in optical flow, but its integration with a curiosity potential within a Lagrangian system is original.

Significance
- The conceptual contribution is interesting, but practical impact is limited by mixed performance relative to contemporary deep models and the lack of scanpath-level validation. Strengthening the empirical case (especially for dynamic scenes and tracking) would enhance significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, principled formulation that unifies saccades and tracking within one dynamical model.
- Clear derivations and a simple algorithmic pipeline; potentially useful as an interpretable baseline.
- Competitive results against several classical saliency methods on some benchmarks.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited biological grounding and missing connections to established oculomotor optimal-control literature.
- Insufficient analysis of the generated scanpaths vis-à-vis human eye movements.
- Mixed performance compared to strong modern baselines; unclear advantage beyond conceptual elegance.
- Limited sensitivity/ablation studies and risk of overfitting from parameter tuning on a small subset.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add scanpath analyses: saccade amplitude/duration distributions, fixation density over time, path similarity metrics, and comparisons to human scanpaths.
- Provide ablations for each Lagrangian term (boundary, curiosity local vs. peripheral, brightness invariance), and report sensitivity to λ, ηb, ηp, and ω.
- Compare against current deep saliency models and dynamic saliency/tracking methods, including scanpath-based metrics where possible.
- Discuss connections to optimal control and motor planning in eye movements (e.g., cost functions used in the Wolpert line of work) and relate your Lagrangian terms to those hypotheses.
- Expand implementation details and release code/settings to support reproducibility.

Overall, this is a thought-provoking and well-crafted theoretical model with clean implementation, but the empirical validation—especially of scanpath dynamics—falls short of the paper’s central claims, and performance lags behind modern deep baselines. Strengthening biological grounding and providing thorough scanpath-level evaluation would substantially improve the paper.
