- Proposes EYMOL, a novel variational physics-based framework modeling eye movements as a particle in a potential field derived from a generalized Least Action Principle.  
- Defines three principles shaping the potential: boundedness at image edges, curiosity-driven attraction to high image gradients, and brightness invariance for fixation/tracking.  
- Derives Euler–Lagrange equations to generate continuous attention trajectories, with saliency maps emerging as a byproduct rather than a primary computation.  
- Contrasts with traditional saliency-map approaches by modeling attention as continuous real-time trajectories instead of global feature maps plus winner-take-all selection.  
- Evaluates performance on standard datasets (MIT1003, CAT2000, SFU) and shows competitive results (AUC-Judd, NSS) despite model simplicity.  
- Originality: offers a genuinely new theoretical unification of curiosity-driven attention and brightness invariance with a physics-based principle.  
- Significance: provides a continuous trajectory model of attention and conceptually elegant emergence of saliency from dynamics.  
- Quality: thorough experiments, statistically significant results, and justified parameter estimation (simulated annealing); acknowledges computational approximations.  
- Clarity: well-structured exposition with a detailed appendix and clear experimental methodology.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of biological plausibility and relation to known neurobiological attention mechanisms (top-down vs bottom-up).  
- Weakness: lacks theoretical analysis of the model’s mathematical properties (stability, convergence, optimality).  
- Weakness: comparisons omit several recent deep learning-based saliency methods, weakening claims about relative standing.  
- Weakness: some mathematical details in the appendix may be hard for readers without a physics background to verify.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical framework, competitive empirical performance, continuous trajectory modeling, natural saliency emergence, clear presentation and validation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited biological grounding, missing theoretical guarantees, incomplete comparison to modern deep methods, and potentially inaccessible math details.  
- Overall: a significant theoretical contribution valuable to computational vision and cognitive science researchers, meriting acceptance despite limitations for practical deep-learning–dominated applications.
