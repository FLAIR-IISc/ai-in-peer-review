The authors investigate what locations in static and dynamic images tend to be attended to by humans. They derive a model by first defining three principles for visual attention (defined as an energy function to be minimized by the movements of the eye): (1) Eye movements are constrained by a harmonic oscillator at the borders of the image within a limited-sized retina. (2) a "curiosity driven principle" highlighting the regions with large changes in brightness in both a fine and blurred version of the image, and (3) brightness invariance, which increases as a function of changes in brightness. Using a cost function derived from these three functions, the authors derive differential equations for predicting the eye movements across static or dynamic images (depending on the starting location and initial velocity). The authors evaluate their technique quantitatively on data sets of static and dynamic scenes coupled with human eye movements. They demonstrate that their method performs comparably to the state-of-the-art. 

This paper addresses fundamental questions in computational vision and cognitive science regarding saliency and modeling eye movements. The proposed model, leveraging a variational approach and principles of least action, offers a novel and potentially insightful framework, particularly within the bottom-up tradition of attention modeling. While not uniformly state-of-the-art across all metrics and datasets, the approach presents a refreshingly different perspective on the problem and demonstrates competitive performance. The formulation, grounding attention in physical principles, is a significant contribution.

However, the paper suffers from issues in clarity and presentation. The writing is dense and challenging to follow, hindering a full appreciation of the technical details. Several abbreviations are used without adequate definition, both within the equations and in Section 3. I recommend a thorough proofread by colleagues and a relocation of the 2-page Appendix to supplementary material. The space gained should be used to explicitly define each variable and function used in the equations, recognizing that the NIPS audience is multidisciplinary and may lack familiarity with specialized conventions.

Additionally, while the paper establishes a strong technical foundation, it lacks sufficient connection to the broader psychological literature on eye movements. Integrating findings from research emphasizing eye movements as an optimal information-gathering strategy—such as the work of Najemnik & Geisler (2008) on optimal search strategies [Najemnik & Geisler, *Journal of Vision*, 2008]—would strengthen the paper’s contextualization and impact. A discussion comparing the proposed approach to existing psychological models would be highly valuable. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach: The variational framework and grounding of attention in physical principles represent a novel contribution.
*   Competitive performance: The model achieves competitive results on standard saliency datasets.
*   Potential for impact: The framework offers a potentially powerful approach to modeling visual attention and could influence future research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Clarity and readability: The paper is poorly written and difficult to follow.
*   Limited connection to psychological literature: The lack of engagement with relevant psychological research weakens the paper’s broader context.
*   Presentation of technical details: The technical details are dense and require significant effort to understand.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound and potentially significant contribution to the field of visual attention. However, substantial improvements in clarity, presentation, and contextualization are needed. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon addressing the concerns raised regarding clarity, definitions, and connections to the psychological literature. The authors should prioritize making the paper more accessible to a broader audience within the NIPS community.
