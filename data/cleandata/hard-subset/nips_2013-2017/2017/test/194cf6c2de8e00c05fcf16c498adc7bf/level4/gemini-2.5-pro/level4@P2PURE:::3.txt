The paper introduces a novel approach for modeling eye movements. The authors provide a competent summary of the current state-of-the-art, which typically relies on saliency maps over an image to select fixation points via an optimization process. In contrast, the proposed model (EYMOL) is founded on a different assumption, defining gaze trajectories directly according to a "Least Action Principle."

A full evaluation of the mathematical derivations, particularly those in the extensive appendix, was challenging given my own background in physics. Despite this, the core concepts of the approach and its key distinctions from previous models were presented clearly and were understandable. The authors validate their model on a real-world eye-movement dataset, comparing its performance against several alternative models. Although the proposed model does not outperform the alternatives on every metric, the overall results are favorable and provide evidence for its validity.

This work will likely be of significant interest to the vision science community (e.g., VSS attendees). I am less certain, however, about its specific contribution to the NIPS audience and recommend weighing the opinions of reviewers with more expertise in this domain. Nonetheless, the paper advances a novel and interesting theory that could inspire considerable future empirical research. As such, it possesses clear novelty and scientific merit, particularly for the perception sciences.
