This computational neuroscience paper proposes a bottom-up visual feature saliency model, enhanced by dynamical systems modeling, to capture the dynamics of scanpaths. I enjoyed reading this paper; it presents a set of compelling ideas in defining “affordances” – such as curiosity and brightness invariance – that drive scanpath generation, as well as the application of the Least Action Principle. However, these “affordances” feel somewhat *ad hoc* and could benefit from further motivation.

In the context of the Least Action Principle and its derivation, it would have been interesting to see a connection made to existing work in eye movement research, particularly from groups like the Wolpert lab, which have explored related concepts. While a lack of strong biological motivation is perhaps a minor concern given the current trend of benchmark-busting deep learning models for *static* visual salience, the model’s mixed performance relative to these models is a significant issue. Because the model claims to capture biological eye movement dynamics, the main paper would have been strengthened by including figures characterizing the generated scanpaths, beyond just overall statistics matching eye movements. 

The quantitative results, while demonstrating some improvement over baseline methods, do not convincingly establish a substantial advantage. Specifically, the gains appear modest when considering the increased model complexity. Further analysis comparing the computational cost of the proposed model with simpler, existing approaches would be valuable.

The experimental evaluation primarily focuses on datasets designed for static saliency prediction. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it highlights the need for evaluation on datasets specifically designed to assess dynamic saliency and scanpath prediction. Performance on such datasets would more directly support the claim of capturing biological eye movement dynamics. A more detailed ablation study, examining the contribution of each component (e.g., the different affordances) to the overall performance, would also strengthen the analysis. It's not entirely clear how sensitive the model is to the weighting of different affordances, and such an analysis could offer insights into their relative importance.

The writing is generally clear, but could be improved in places with more concise phrasing. For example, the description of the dynamical system components could be streamlined. A clearer articulation of the relationship between the Least Action Principle and the implemented cost function would also be beneficial. 

Overall, this paper presents an interesting approach to modeling scanpath dynamics, combining ideas from computational neuroscience and dynamical systems. However, the limited performance gains, the somewhat *ad hoc* nature of the affordances, and the lack of comprehensive evaluation on dynamic saliency datasets weaken the paper's overall impact. I recommend rejection with encouragement to address these concerns in future work.
