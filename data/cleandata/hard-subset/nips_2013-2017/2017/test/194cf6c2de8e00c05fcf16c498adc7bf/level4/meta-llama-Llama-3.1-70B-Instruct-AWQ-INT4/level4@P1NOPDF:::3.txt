The paper proposes a novel approach to the study of eye movements, providing a thorough summary of the current state of the art in the field. The authors accurately describe existing approaches, which primarily rely on saliency maps defined over an image, with fixation points selected through an optimization or maximization process. In contrast, the proposed model, EYMOL, operates under a different assumption, defining gaze trajectories directly according to the "Least Action Principle." While some of the mathematical details, particularly those in the extensive appendix, were challenging for me to evaluate due to my limited physics background, I was still able to grasp the key elements of the approach and its distinction from previous models.

The authors apply their model to a real dataset of eye movements and compare its performance to various alternative models. Although the results do not overwhelmingly favor the new approach on every measure, they are generally favorable and demonstrate the model's validity. The comparison to existing models is helpful in understanding the strengths and limitations of EYMOL.

I believe the paper may be of interest to researchers in the vision science community, such as attendees of the Vision Sciences Society (VSS) conference. However, I am less convinced that it makes a significant contribution to NIPS, and I think this should be evaluated in conjunction with the opinions of other reviewers who are more expert in the field. Nevertheless, I think the paper is likely to advance a new and interesting theory that could inspire further empirical research, and thus has both novelty and merit in the context of perception sciences.
