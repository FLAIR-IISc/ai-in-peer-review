The paper proposes a novel approach to studying eye movements, providing a thorough summary of the current state of the art in this field. The authors accurately describe existing methods, which typically rely on saliency maps defined over images and select fixation points using optimization or maximization techniques. In contrast, the proposed Eye Movement Optimization by Least Action (EYMOL) model operates under a distinct assumption, applying the "Least Action Principle" directly to gaze trajectories. Although some mathematical details, particularly in the extensive appendix, were challenging for me to evaluate due to my limited physics background, I was still able to grasp the key elements of the approach and its differences from previous models. The authors apply their model to a real dataset of eye movements and compare its performance to various alternative models. While the results do not consistently favor the new approach, they demonstrate its validity and potential benefits. 

I believe this paper may be of interest to certain members of the vision science community, such as attendees of the Vision Sciences Society (VSS). However, I am less convinced of its contribution to NIPS, and I think this assessment should be considered in conjunction with the opinions of more expert reviewers. Nevertheless, the paper likely presents a new and intriguing theory that could inspire further empirical research, exhibiting both novelty and merit in the field of perception science.
