This paper presents a bottom-up visual saliency model augmented with a dynamical systems formulation intended to capture scanpath dynamics. I found the paper engaging and appreciated several elegant ideas, notably the definition of “affordances” (e.g., curiosity drive, brightness invariance) to guide scanpaths, and the application of a Least Action Principle (LAP).

However, the proposed affordances feel somewhat ad hoc and insufficiently motivated. Clearer theoretical grounding or empirical justification would strengthen the contribution. In particular, the derivation and use of LAP would benefit from a deeper connection to the eye-movement and motor-control literature, including classic and recent work from the Wolpert lab and related studies on optimal control of saccades. Even a brief discussion clarifying how the proposed cost functions map onto established models of oculomotor planning would help situate the approach.

Empirically, the model’s performance appears mixed when compared with modern deep-learning saliency baselines. While limited biological plausibility can be a forgivable omission for state-of-the-art static saliency models, it becomes a liability here given the paper’s claim to capture biological dynamics. The evaluation should more convincingly demonstrate added value over static saliency methods and recent scanpath predictors, ideally via standardized benchmarks, clear ablations of each affordance term, and sensitivity analyses of key hyperparameters.

Finally, given the focus on dynamics, the main paper should include explicit visualizations and quantitative characterizations of generated scanpaths—not only aggregate statistics. Examples include trajectory plots overlaid on images, distributions of saccade amplitudes and directions, fixation durations, inter-saccadic intervals, and temporal alignment with human sequences. Such analyses would clarify whether the model captures both where and how observers look.

Overall, the paper combines appealing concepts with a principled dynamical formulation, but it needs stronger theoretical grounding, tighter links to prior oculomotor work, and more compelling, dynamics-focused evaluations to substantiate its claims.
