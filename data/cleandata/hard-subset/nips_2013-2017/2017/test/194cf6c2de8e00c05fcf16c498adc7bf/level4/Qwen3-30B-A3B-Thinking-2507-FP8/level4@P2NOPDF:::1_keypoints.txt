- Proposes a dynamical bottom-up saliency model with novel affordances (curiosity, brightness invariance) and uses the Least Action Principle for scanpath trajectories.  
- Affordances are presented as ad hoc postulates with insufficient theoretical grounding or motivation.  
- Derivation of the Least Action Principle lacks explicit connection to established oculomotor/optimal-control literature (e.g., Wolpert lab).  
- Model performance is weak relative to state-of-the-art static saliency deep models (e.g., MIT1003, SALICON).  
- Dynamic scanpath prediction performs worse than both static salience models and dedicated oculomotor prediction frameworks.  
- Failure to outperform or match static salience baselines undermines the claim of modeling biological scanpath dynamics.  
- Main text omits visualizations of generated scanpaths, relying only on summary statistics rather than trajectory-level comparisons.  
- Without sequence, duration, and spatial-coherence comparisons to human scanpaths, temporal validity of the model is unclear.  
- Dynamical-systems framing is theoretically interesting but empirical validation and links to oculomotor literature are superficial.  
- To improve: compare to dynamic oculomotor models, include scanpath visualizations vs. humans, and rigorously justify the affordances biologically or computationally.
