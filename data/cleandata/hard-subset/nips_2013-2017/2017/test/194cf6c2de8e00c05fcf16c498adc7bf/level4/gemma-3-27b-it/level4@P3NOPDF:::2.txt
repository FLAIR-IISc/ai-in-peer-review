## Review of Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes

This paper investigates the mechanisms underlying human visual attention in both static and dynamic images. The authors propose a novel model grounded in three core principles: (1) constrained eye movements modeled as a harmonic oscillator within the image boundaries and limited retinal field; (2) a “curiosity-driven” principle emphasizing regions exhibiting significant brightness changes at multiple scales; and (3) a brightness invariance principle that favors areas with pronounced changes in illumination. These principles are formalized into an energy function, from which the authors derive differential equations predicting eye movements given an initial location and velocity. The model is quantitatively evaluated against human eye movement data collected on static and dynamic scenes, demonstrating performance comparable to existing state-of-the-art methods.

Modeling saliency and predicting eye movements are fundamental challenges in computational vision and cognitive science. Existing approaches often suffer from imprecise definitions of saliency. This work offers a refreshing and innovative perspective – as far as I can ascertain – that could significantly contribute to a more formal understanding of saliency and a principled model for bottom-up driven eye movements. While the method doesn’t consistently achieve state-of-the-art performance across all metrics and datasets, its strong results coupled with its unique approach are noteworthy.

However, the paper’s impact is significantly hampered by its clarity and presentation. The writing is dense and, at times, difficult to follow, hindering a full assessment of the underlying ideas. I strongly recommend the authors prioritize a thorough revision with careful proofreading by multiple individuals. Furthermore, the frequent use of abbreviations, both within the mathematical equations and throughout Section 3, requires expansion for broader accessibility. 

The current two-page Appendix should be moved to the supplementary material. The space reclaimed should be used to provide a comprehensive definition of each variable and function used in the model. While some notations may be standard within the authors’ field, the NeurIPS audience is diverse, and a clear glossary is crucial for understanding the mathematical derivations. This would greatly enhance reproducibility and facilitate engagement with the work.

From a psychological perspective, it would be valuable for the authors to connect their model to the broader literature on eye movements as an information-gathering strategy. Specifically, the work of Najemnik & Geisler (2008, *Journal of Vision*, 8(3), 1-14) demonstrates that human eye movement statistics align with optimal search strategies. Related work from Geisler's lab and others provides further context. A discussion of how the proposed model relates to these findings – whether it represents a re-formulation of similar principles, offers complementary insights, or provides independent information that could be integrated for improved performance – would strengthen the paper’s contribution and position it within the cognitive science landscape. 

In summary, this paper presents a novel and potentially impactful approach to modeling visual attention. However, significant improvements in clarity and presentation are necessary to fully realize its potential. Addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding notation, explanation, and connection to relevant psychological literature, would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its value to the NeurIPS community.
