This computational neuroscience paper proposes a bottom-up visual feature saliency model combined with dynamical-systems modeling to capture the dynamics of scan paths. I enjoyed reading the paper; it contains a set of appealing ideas, including the definition of “affordances” (e.g., curiosity, brightness invariance) that drive the scan path, as well as the use of the Least Action Principle. That said, these “affordances” feel somewhat ad hoc and are not further motivated.

In the context of the Least Action Principle and its derivation here, it would have been valuable to relate the approach to established work in eye-movement research, such as past and recent work by the Wolpert lab. While the limited biological motivation is, by itself, a minor issue in an era dominated by benchmark-busting deep learning models of (static) visual salience, a more serious concern is performance: the model’s results appear mixed and, relative to these deep models, poor.

Given the claim to capture biological eye-movement dynamics, it would have been interesting to see, in the main paper, actual figures characterizing the generated scan paths—not only in terms of their overall statistics at matching eye movements, but also through qualitative examples that make the dynamics and structure of the scan paths explicit.
