This computational neuroscience paper proposes a bottom-up visual feature saliency model, modified by dynamical systems modeling, to capture the dynamics of scanpaths. The paper presents an intriguing set of ideas, particularly in defining "affordances" such as curiosity and brightness invariance that drive scan-path generation, as well as the concept of the Least Action Principle. However, these "affordances" are somewhat ad-hoc and lack further motivation.

In the context of the Least Action Principle, it would be beneficial to relate it to existing work in eye movement research, such as studies conducted by the Wolpert lab. Although the lack of biological motivation may not be a significant concern in the current era of deep learning models for static visual salience, the model's mixed performance compared to these models is a notable limitation. Given the model's claim to capture biological eye movement dynamics, it would be valuable to include figures in the main paper that characterize the generated scan paths, not only in terms of their overall statistics but also in terms of their temporal dynamics.

Some potential improvements to the paper could include providing a more thorough explanation of the "affordances" and their underlying motivations, as well as comparing the model's performance to existing eye movement research. Additionally, including more detailed analyses of the generated scan paths would strengthen the paper's claims and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the model's capabilities.
