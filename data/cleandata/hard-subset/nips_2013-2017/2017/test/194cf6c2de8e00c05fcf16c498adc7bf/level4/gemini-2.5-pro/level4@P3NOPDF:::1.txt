This paper proposes a model of visual saliency that incorporates a dynamical systems approach to capture the temporal evolution of scanpaths. The model combines bottom-up visual features with a framework governed by the Least Action Principle and driven by novel concepts termed "affordances," such as curiosity and brightness invariance. I found the central ideas in this paper to be creative and thought-provoking.

**Strengths:**

The paper presents several interesting conceptual contributions. The idea of defining "affordances" that guide attentional shifts beyond simple low-level features is a welcome and novel perspective. Furthermore, the application of the Least Action Principle to model the trajectory of attention is a compelling theoretical approach that could potentially unify different aspects of visual exploration.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

Despite its interesting premise, the paper has several weaknesses in its current form that limit its impact.

1.  **Motivation for "Affordances":** While the concepts of "affordances" like curiosity are intriguing, their formulation appears ad-hoc. The paper would be significantly strengthened by providing a more rigorous theoretical or empirical motivation for why these specific affordances were chosen and defined in their particular mathematical form. As it stands, they seem postulated rather than derived from established principles of neuroscience or perception.

2.  **Connection to Prior Work:** The application of the Least Action Principle is a central claim, yet the paper misses an opportunity to connect this framework to the extensive body of literature on motor control and eye movements, particularly the work on optimal control theories (e.g., from the Wolpert lab and others). Situating the proposed model within this existing research would provide crucial context and help to highlight the specific advancements made by the authors.

3.  **Empirical Performance:** A significant concern is the model's performance on standard benchmarks. The paper compares its results against state-of-the-art deep learning models for static saliency, but the performance is described as mixed and appears to be substantially lower than these competitors. While the authors rightly argue that their model aims to capture dynamics—something static models do not—a new model should ideally be competitive on existing benchmarks or clearly justify why its architecture leads to trade-offs. The lack of compelling performance on static saliency prediction weakens the overall contribution, especially given the model's complexity.

4.  **Lack of Dynamic Analysis:** The most critical weakness, given the paper's central claim of modeling *dynamics*, is the lack of a thorough analysis of the generated scanpaths. The evaluation focuses primarily on static metrics that measure the aggregate spatial distribution of fixations, which is what standard saliency models are benchmarked against. However, the paper does not present a quantitative or qualitative analysis of the dynamic properties of its generated scanpaths in the main text (e.g., saccade amplitudes, fixation durations, transition probabilities, or scanpath similarity metrics). Without this analysis, the core claim of capturing the dynamics of biological eye movements remains unsubstantiated. It would be essential to include figures in the main paper that illustrate and characterize these generated scanpaths to validate the model's primary contribution.
