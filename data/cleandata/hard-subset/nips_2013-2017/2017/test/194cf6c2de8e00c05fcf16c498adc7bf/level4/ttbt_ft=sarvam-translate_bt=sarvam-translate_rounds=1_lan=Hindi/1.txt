This computational neuroscience paper proposes a bottom-up visual feature salience model, modified by dynamic system modeling, aimed at capturing the dynamics of scanpaths. I enjoyed reading this paper, as it contains a good set of ideas for defining "affordance" (curiosity, brightness change) that drives scanpaths, as well as the idea of the least action principle. These "affordances" are perhaps considered a bit ad hoc and not further motivated.

In terms of the least action principle and its derivation in this context, it would have been interesting to connect it to real-world work in eye-movement research, such as past and recent work by the Wolper lab. While the lack of biological motivation is in itself a minor issue in the era of benchmark-busting deep learning models (static) visual significance, the issue with this model is that the mixed performance it achieves in relation to these models is poor. Given that the model claims to capture biological eye dynamics, it would have been interesting to see real figures depicting the generated scanpaths in the main paper, not only in terms of their overall position but also in matching eye movements.
