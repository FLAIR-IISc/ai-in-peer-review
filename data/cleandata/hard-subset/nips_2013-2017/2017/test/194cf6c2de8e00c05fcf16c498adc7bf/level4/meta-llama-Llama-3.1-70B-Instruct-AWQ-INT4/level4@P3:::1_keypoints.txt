- Proposes EYe MOvement Laws (EYMOL), a novel computational model of visual attention derived from a generalized Least Action Principle producing differential equations for scanpath dynamics.
- Model driven by three principles: boundedness of trajectory, curiosity-driven exploration, and brightness invariance.
- Mathematical formulation and derivation (Euler–Lagrange equations) are sound and clearly explained.
- Parameters are clearly described and estimated using simulated annealing.
- Demonstrates effectiveness at predicting human fixations on image and video datasets.
- Performance is competitive with state-of-the-art models, notable given the model’s simplicity.
- Unique contribution: explicitly captures the dynamics of scanpaths rather than static saliency only.
- Experimental evaluation is thorough, using multiple metrics and promising results.
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written.
- Lacks sufficient biological motivation linking model assumptions (e.g., Least Action Principle) to neurophysiology.
- Performance is not consistently superior to state-of-the-art; lacks analysis of failure cases.
- Insufficient visualizations of generated scanpaths to illustrate model behavior.
- Needs more discussion on implications of results and relation to existing literature.
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty in modeling scanpath dynamics, competitive performance, thorough evaluation.
- Arguments against acceptance: weak biological grounding, inconsistent SOTA performance, insufficient visualizations.
- Reviewer scores: Quality 8/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 8/10.
