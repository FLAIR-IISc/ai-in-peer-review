The paper proposes a novel approach to modeling eye movements, deviating from the traditional saliency map-based methods. The authors provide a thorough summary of the current state of the art in visual attention modeling, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches. They then introduce their model, EYMOL, which is based on the principle of least action and operates directly on gaze trajectories.

The EYMOL model is founded on three fundamental principles: boundedness of the trajectory, curiosity-driven attention, and brightness invariance. These principles are mathematically formulated and integrated into a Lagrangian, which is then used to derive the Euler-Lagrange equations of motion. The model's parameters are estimated using simulated annealing, and its performance is evaluated on various datasets, including images and videos.

The experimental results show that EYMOL competes well with state-of-the-art models, despite its simplicity. The authors also demonstrate the model's ability to track moving objects in dynamic scenes, which is a promising feature for real-world applications.

The paper's strengths lie in its novel approach to visual attention modeling, its ability to capture the dynamics of eye movements, and its competitive performance on various datasets. However, the technical details of the model, particularly the mathematical derivations in the appendix, may be challenging for non-experts to follow.

In terms of the NIPS evaluation criteria, the paper scores well on novelty, as it presents a fresh perspective on visual attention modeling. The model's performance on various datasets also demonstrates its significance and potential impact on the field. However, the paper's clarity and technical soundness may be improved by providing more intuitive explanations of the mathematical concepts and by addressing potential limitations of the model.

Overall, I believe the paper has the potential to inspire further research in visual attention modeling and perception sciences. While its contribution to NIPS may be subject to debate, I think it is a valuable addition to the field and deserves consideration for publication.
