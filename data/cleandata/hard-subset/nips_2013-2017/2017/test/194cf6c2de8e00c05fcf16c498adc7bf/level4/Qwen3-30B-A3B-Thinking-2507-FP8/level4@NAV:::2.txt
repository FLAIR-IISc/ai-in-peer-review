

The authors investigate human attentional priorities in static and dynamic visual scenes by deriving a model grounded in three foundational principles for visual attention (formulated as an energy function to be minimized by eye movements): (1) Eye movements are constrained by a harmonic oscillator near image borders within a limited retina size; (2) a "curiosity-driven principle" emphasizing regions with significant brightness changes across both fine and blurred image versions; and (3) brightness invariance, which scales with brightness changes. A cost function derived from these principles yields differential equations for predicting eye movements in static or dynamic images, contingent on initial position and velocity. Quantitative evaluation on datasets combining static/dynamic scenes with human eye-tracking data demonstrates performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods.  

The formalization of saliency and eye movement modeling represents a critical advancement in computational vision and cognitive science, addressing longstanding ambiguities in psychological definitions. The authors propose a novel and innovative model (to my knowledge) that could significantly advance understanding of saliency mechanisms and provide a formal framework for bottom-up eye movement prediction. While not consistently top-performing across all metrics or datasets, the method delivers strong results and offers a valuable alternative perspective.  

Regrettably, parts of this assessment rely on conjecture due to the paper’s lack of clarity and difficulty in following the argument. I strongly recommend thorough proofreading by co-authors or peers, expanded definitions of abbreviations (both within equations and Section 3), and relocation of the two-page Appendix to supplementary material. The space freed should be used to explicitly define all variables and functions, as the NeurIPS audience spans diverse disciplines and may struggle with the mathematical conventions.  

As a psychologist, I would have valued deeper engagement with psychological literature on eye movements as optimal information-gathering steps. For instance, Najemnik & Geisler (2008) demonstrated that human eye movement statistics align with an optimal search strategy (Journal of Vision, 8(3), 1-14). I encourage the authors to discuss how their model relates to such findings—whether it restates similar principles, provides complementary insights, or suggests integrative improvements to existing frameworks.
