

This paper proposes a novel model for predicting human visual attention in static and dynamic scenes, grounded in three principles formalized as an energy-minimizing framework for eye movements. The authors define (1) a harmonic oscillator constraint at image borders, modeling eye movement limits within a finite retina; (2) a "curiosity-driven" principle emphasizing regions with significant brightness changes across both high-resolution and blurred image versions; and (3) brightness invariance, where salience increases with brightness change magnitude. From these, they derive differential equations predicting eye movement trajectories for static or dynamic scenes, given initial position and velocity. Evaluation on standard static and dynamic scene datasets with human fixation data shows performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods.

The work addresses a critical challenge in computational vision and cognitive science: formalizing saliency and eye movement modeling. Psychologists have long struggled with vague saliency definitions, and this model offers a fresh, innovative approach within the bottom-up attention framework. While not consistently top-performing across all metrics and datasets, it delivers strong results and provides a novel perspective on the problem.

However, the paper's presentation significantly hinders clarity. The writing is often opaque, making it difficult to verify claims or follow the mathematical derivation. The authors should seek additional proofreading to improve exposition. Crucially, they must expand all abbreviations and explicitly define every variable and function, especially in Section 3 and the equations. The current two-page Appendix should be moved to supplementary material, freeing space to clarify all mathematical components. Given NIPS' interdisciplinary audience, assuming familiarity with field-specific notation risks excluding readers from other domains.

As a psychologist, I note a significant opportunity to strengthen the theoretical grounding. The authors should explicitly connect their model to established psychological work on eye movements as optimal information-gathering steps. For instance, Najemnik & Geisler (2008) demonstrated that human eye movement statistics align with an optimal search strategy. The paper would benefit from a discussion comparing their framework to such findings: Does their model restate similar principles, offer complementary insights, or suggest integrable refinements? Engaging with Geisler’s broader literature (e.g., on efficient search) would contextualize their contribution meaningfully within cognitive science. Without this, the work risks appearing disconnected from the foundational psychological literature it aims to inform.

Overall, the model’s conceptual novelty and competitive performance are promising. Addressing the presentation issues and integrating relevant psychological theory would substantially elevate the paper’s impact and accessibility. The core ideas warrant publication, but the current draft requires significant revision to meet NIPS standards for clarity and interdisciplinary engagement.
