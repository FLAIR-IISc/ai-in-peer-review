- Proposes EYMOL, a framework that models eye movement trajectories using the Least Action Principle instead of saliency-map optimization.
- Clarifies distinction from prior work: previous methods pick fixation points via image-based saliency optimization, whereas EYMOL minimizes a path-based action functional directly on trajectories.
- Core theoretical innovation is well-motivated and clearly presented; appendix mathematical/physics derivations are dense and exceed reviewer’s expertise, but concepts remain accessible.
- Empirical evaluation uses a real-world eye-tracking dataset with comprehensive comparisons to multiple established baselines.
- EYMOL does not dominate all metrics but yields statistically significant improvements on several key trajectory-prediction and human-consistency measures.
- Results support the Least Action Principle as a viable, potentially more biologically plausible alternative to saliency-based paradigms.
- Methodologically sound and well-executed implementation.
- Significant for computational models of visual attention and likely to interest vision-science communities (VSS, Journal of Vision), bridging computational neuroscience and psychophysics.
- Limited contribution to core ML/NeurIPS audience: lacks novel ML algorithms, theoretical guarantees, or major benchmark breakthroughs; ML relevance is secondary.
- Recommendation: interesting and valid work but not a major NeurIPS contribution—recommend rejection unless other reviewers argue stronger ML significance.
