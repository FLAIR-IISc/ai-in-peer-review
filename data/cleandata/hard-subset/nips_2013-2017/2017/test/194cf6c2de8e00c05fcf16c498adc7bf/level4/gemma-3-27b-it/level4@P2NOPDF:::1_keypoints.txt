- Proposes a promising computational neuroscience model combining bottom-up saliency with dynamical systems and the Least Action Principle.  
- Introduces a novel notion of “affordances” (e.g., curiosity, brightness invariance) driving scanpaths, but these affordances feel ad hoc and under-justified.  
- Should better connect to eye-movement control literature (e.g., Wolpert lab and related studies) in the Least Action Principle context.  
- Model performs relatively poorly compared to modern deep-learning saliency benchmarks—a significant concern.  
- Lacks strong biological motivation for the chosen affordances, weakening claims about modeling eye movement dynamics.  
- Main text reports only statistical matching of eye movements without qualitative visual characterization of simulated scanpaths.  
- Needs figures showing trajectories, saccade amplitudes, and fixation durations to evaluate biological plausibility.  
- Experimental details (datasets, preprocessing, parameter settings) are insufficiently specified for reproducibility.  
- Overall: interesting and novel but preliminary; limited justification, weak benchmark performance, and missing visual analyses reduce the paper’s impact.
