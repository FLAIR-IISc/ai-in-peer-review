

This paper proposes a novel computational model of visual attention based on a generalized Least Action Principle from physics, aiming to capture the dynamics of scanpaths in both static and dynamic visual scenes. The authors introduce two key "affordances" – curiosity-driven attention (attracted to regions with high detail gradients) and brightness invariance (for fixation and tracking) – which together form a unified theoretical framework. The model derives differential equations for attention dynamics through variational principles, with the saliency map emerging naturally as a byproduct of the scanpath simulation rather than being the primary focus of the model.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by connecting computational neuroscience with physics-inspired modeling approaches, which is relatively uncommon in the visual attention literature. The authors effectively situate their work within the broader context of visual attention modeling, referencing classic work by Itti & Koch [10] and Treisman & Gelade [14], while also acknowledging the prevalence of centralized saliency map approaches in computer vision. The theoretical foundation is well-developed, with the derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations presented in the appendix, and the model is implemented using a principled parameter estimation approach with simulated annealing.

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical novelty: it offers a unified framework that simultaneously addresses both static image and dynamic video saliency prediction through a single dynamical system. This approach is particularly compelling because it naturally produces saliency maps as a byproduct of the scanpath dynamics, rather than optimizing for saliency map prediction directly as most existing methods do. The experimental validation across multiple datasets (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video) demonstrates that the model achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art approaches, particularly for metrics like NSS and AUC-Judd.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that prevent it from being a strong contribution to NIPS. First, while the theoretical framework is mathematically sound, the motivation for the specific "affordances" (curiosity and brightness invariance) remains somewhat ad hoc. The authors do not sufficiently connect these principles to biological evidence or prior work in eye movement research, such as the extensive literature from the Wolpert lab on optimal control of eye movements. The paper's claim that the model captures "biological eye movement dynamics" is not adequately supported by visual evidence of actual scanpaths generated by the model.

Second, the performance claims are somewhat misleading. While the model achieves competitive results on standard saliency metrics, it does not outperform recent deep learning approaches on these metrics, and the paper fails to provide adequate comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art methods. The authors claim the model "competes well with state-of-the-art models" but fail to show how it compares to the best-performing methods in the field. For instance, the paper does not include comparisons with the recent deep learning approaches that have achieved top performance on the MIT1003 and CAT2000 datasets.

Third, the paper lacks sufficient visualization of the scanpaths themselves. The review mentions that "it would have been interesting to see actual figures characterizing the generated scan paths" – this is a critical omission. The paper's main contribution is a model of scanpath dynamics, but the only evidence of the model's performance comes from static saliency maps, not the actual scanpaths it produces. Without visual comparisons of the generated scanpaths to human eye movements, it's difficult to assess whether the model truly captures biological eye movement dynamics.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound and the mathematical derivations are well-executed, but the experimental evaluation is insufficiently comprehensive. The paper would benefit from additional experiments comparing the actual scanpaths generated by the model to human eye movements, including metrics such as scanpath similarity, fixation duration distributions, and saccade amplitude distributions.

Regarding clarity, the paper is generally well-written, though some technical details in the appendix may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with variational calculus. The main text could better explain the intuition behind the two key affordances, particularly how they relate to known biological mechanisms of visual attention.

The originality of the approach is strong – the application of physics-inspired variational principles to visual attention is novel and represents a meaningful contribution to the field. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by better connecting these principles to existing biological evidence and showing how the model's scanpath dynamics differ from those of human observers.

In terms of significance, the paper offers a theoretically interesting framework that could potentially inform future models of visual attention. However, its practical significance is limited by the model's performance relative to deep learning approaches, which have largely dominated the field in recent years. The paper would be more impactful if it demonstrated how this theoretical approach could be integrated with or improve upon existing deep learning models.

In conclusion, this paper presents an interesting theoretical framework for visual attention that bridges computational neuroscience and physics-inspired modeling. However, the lack of strong biological motivation, insufficient comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art methods, and inadequate visualization of scanpath dynamics significantly limit its contribution to the field. The paper would be much stronger with additional experimental evidence showing how the model's generated scanpaths compare to human eye movements, and with more comprehensive comparisons to the latest deep learning approaches. While the theoretical foundation is sound and novel, the paper's practical significance and contribution to the field would be substantially enhanced with these improvements.
