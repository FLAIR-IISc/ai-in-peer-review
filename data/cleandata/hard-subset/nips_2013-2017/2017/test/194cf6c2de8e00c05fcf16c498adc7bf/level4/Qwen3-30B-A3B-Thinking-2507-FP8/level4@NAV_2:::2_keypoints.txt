- Proposes EYMOL, a novel variational/physics-inspired model deriving eye movement trajectories from a Lagrangian and Euler–Lagrange equations.  
- Defines three core principles: boundedness via a harmonic oscillator at image borders, curiosity-driven attraction to high brightness gradients, and brightness invariance for fixation/tracking.  
- Generates scanpaths directly and produces saliency maps as a byproduct rather than modeling saliency maps as primary.  
- Evaluated on standard static (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and dynamic (SFU) datasets, reporting competitive performance.  
- Theoretical derivation is technically sound with clear Euler–Lagrange development and appropriate parameter estimation via simulated annealing.  
- The brightness invariance approximation used for static images (Eq. 6) is insufficiently justified, weakening theoretical rigor.  
- Mathematical derivations are dense and poorly explained, with many key variables and functions undefined for a broad NIPS audience.  
- The curiosity-driven principle (Eq. 4) and its alternation between local and peripheral fields are confusing and need clearer exposition.  
- Appendix contains important technical details that should be moved to supplementary material to improve readability.  
- Originality is strong: unifies curiosity-driven exploration and brightness invariance under a variational framework, distinct from feature- or learning-based methods.  
- Significance is primarily theoretical, offering a promising foundation for understanding early visual attention but with limited immediate practical advancement.  
- Paper lacks sufficient connection to psychological literature on eye-movement optimization (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler) and previous computational models.  
- Pro acceptance: novel, well-motivated theoretical approach with promising, competitive experimental results.  
- Con acceptance: poor writing/clarity, unjustified approximation of brightness invariance, and underdeveloped links to relevant psychological theories.  
- Recommended revisions: improve clarity (define all variables/functions, move derivations to supplementary material), justify approximations, and expand discussion of connections to psychological models.
