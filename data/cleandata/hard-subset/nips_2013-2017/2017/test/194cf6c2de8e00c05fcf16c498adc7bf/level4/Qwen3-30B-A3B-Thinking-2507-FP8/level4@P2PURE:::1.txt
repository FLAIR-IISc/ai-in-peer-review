

This computational neuroscience paper proposes a bottom-up visual feature saliency model enhanced through dynamical systems modeling to capture scanpath dynamics. I appreciated the paper's novel conceptualization of "affordances" (curiosity, brightness invariance) driving scanpaths, alongside the application of the Least Action Principle. However, these affordances are somewhat ad hoc and lack sufficient theoretical motivation. Regarding the Least Action Principle derivation, it would have strengthened the work to explicitly connect it to established eye movement research, particularly foundational and recent contributions from the Wolpert lab. While the absence of biological motivation is a minor concern in the current era of benchmark-driven deep learning models for static visual salience, the model's primary weakness lies in its poor performance relative to state-of-the-art approaches. Given the paper's claim to model biological eye movement dynamics, the main text should include actual figures characterizing the generated scanpaths—not merely static metrics—to demonstrate how well the model replicates empirical dynamics.
