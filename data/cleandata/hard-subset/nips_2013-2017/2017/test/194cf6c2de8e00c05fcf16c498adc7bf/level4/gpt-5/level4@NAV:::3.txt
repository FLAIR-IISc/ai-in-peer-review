The paper presents a novel approach to studying eye movements. The authors accurately review the current state of the art (to the extent I understand it; I note upfront that I am not a specialist in perception or eye movements). In broad terms, existing methods are built around a saliency map defined over an image, with fixation locations chosen through an optimization/maximization procedure. By contrast, this model (EYMOL) adopts a different premise, being formulated directly on gaze trajectories via a Least Action Principle. Some of the mathematical details, especially in the extensive appendix, were beyond my ability to assess due to my limited physics background. Nonetheless, I was able to grasp the central elements of the method and how it departs from previous models. The model is evaluated on real eye-movement datasets and compared against a range of alternative models. Although it does not outperform competitors on every metric, the results are favorable for the proposed approach and support its validity.

I believe the paper will interest segments of the vision science community (e.g., VSS attendees). I am less convinced it represents a strong contribution for NIPS, though this should be judged alongside the views of other, more expert reviewers. It likely advances a fresh and intriguing theory that could spur further empirical research, and thus has both novelty and merit for perception science.
