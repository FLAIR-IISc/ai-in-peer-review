This computational neuroscience paper presents a bottom-up visual-feature saliency model augmented with dynamical-systems modeling aimed at capturing scanpath dynamics. I enjoyed reading it; it offers several appealing ideas, including the definition of “affordances” (curiosity, brightness invariance) that drive the scan path, as well as the use of the Least Action Principle. However, these “affordances” seem somewhat ad hoc and are not further justified. Within the Least Action Principle framework and its derivation here, it would have been informative to relate the approach to established eye-movement research, such as past and recent work from the Wolpert lab. While the limited biological motivation would on its own be a minor issue in an era of benchmark-dominating deep-learning models of (static) visual salience, a more significant concern is the model’s mixed—and overall weak—performance relative to those models. Given the claim to capture biological eye-movement dynamics, it would have been helpful to include in the main paper actual figures characterizing the generated scan paths, not only their overall statistics at matching eye movements.
