This paper introduces EYMOL, a novel computational model of eye movements. The authors provide an accurate summary of the current state-of-the-art, which predominantly relies on saliency maps to predict fixation points via an optimization or maximization process. In a significant departure, EYMOL is not based on static saliency but instead models the entire gaze trajectory directly, governed by a "Least Action Principle" adapted from physics.

The primary strength of this work is its conceptual novelty. By framing eye movements as a trajectory optimization problem under a physical principle, the authors offer a fundamentally different perspective on oculomotor control. The core ideas of the model and its distinctions from previous work are presented clearly. While a detailed evaluation of the mathematical formalism, particularly the physics-based derivations in the appendix, is outside my area of expertise, the central tenets of the approach were comprehensible.

The authors validate their model on a real-world eye-tracking dataset, comparing its performance against a variety of alternative models. The empirical results are promising; while EYMOL does not outperform all competing models on every metric, its performance is highly competitive and demonstrates the viability of this new theoretical framework.

The paper presents a compelling and innovative theory that is likely to stimulate further empirical research in the perception and vision science communities. It would undoubtedly be a strong contribution to venues focused on those fields, such as the Vision Sciences Society (VSS).

I am less certain about its fit for the NIPS community. The work's contribution is primarily in cognitive and perceptual modeling rather than in machine learning methodology or applications, which are typically central to the conference. However, this is a point on which I would defer to the judgment of reviewers with deeper expertise in the intersection of computational cognitive science and machine learning.

In summary, this paper introduces a novel and theoretically interesting model of eye movements. Its novelty and potential to inspire new research directions represent a significant merit for the perception sciences.
