Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes

The authors present a novel approach to modeling visual attention in static and dynamic images. By defining three fundamental principles for visual attention, they derive a model that predicts eye movements as an energy function to be minimized. The principles include: (1) a harmonic oscillator constraint at the image borders within a limited-sized retina, (2) a "curiosity-driven principle" that highlights regions with large changes in brightness in both fine and blurred versions of the image, and (3) brightness invariance, which increases as a function of changes in brightness. Using a cost function derived from these principles, the authors obtain differential equations that predict eye movements across static or dynamic images, depending on the starting location and initial velocity.

The authors evaluate their technique quantitatively on datasets of static and dynamic scenes coupled with human eye movements, demonstrating comparable performance to state-of-the-art methods. This work addresses critical issues in computational vision and cognitive science, providing a formal definition of saliency and modeling eye movements. The proposed model offers a novel and innovative perspective on the problem, which could aid in the development of a better understanding of what makes something salient and provide a formal model for eye movements within the bottom-up tradition.

Although the method performs well and provides a unique perspective, it is not necessarily state-of-the-art on every metric for every dataset. Nevertheless, the authors' contribution is valuable, as it offers a refreshingly different approach to the problem.

Unfortunately, the paper is poorly written and difficult to follow, making it challenging to fully appreciate the authors' work. To improve the manuscript, I recommend that the authors have others proofread the paper and expand on abbreviations, both within the equations and in Section 3. Additionally, I suggest moving the two-page Appendix to supplementary material and using the extra pages to define each variable and function used. This would greatly benefit the NIPS audience, which comes from diverse disciplines and may struggle to follow the mathematics otherwise.

As a psychologist, I would have appreciated a connection between the authors' work and the psychological literature on eye movements as optimal steps for gathering information. For example, Najemnik and Geisler (2008) demonstrated that eye movement statistics in humans are consistent with an optimal search strategy. A discussion of how the authors' approach compares to these findings would be valuable, including whether their model is a restatement of similar models or provides independent information that could be integrated to produce a better model. Exploring this connection could further enrich the authors' contribution and provide a more comprehensive understanding of visual attention.

Overall, the authors' work is a valuable contribution to the field, and with some revisions to improve clarity and connections to relevant literature, it has the potential to make a more significant impact.
