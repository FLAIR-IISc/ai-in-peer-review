The paper introduces a new approach to the study of eye movements. The authors accurately summarize the current state of the art (to the best of my understanding, though I am not an expert in perception or eye movements). Broadly, existing methods rely on a saliency map defined over an image, with fixation points selected via an optimization/maximization procedure. In contrast, the proposed model (EYMOL) adopts a different assumption: it is defined directly over gaze trajectories based on a “Least Action Principle.” Some mathematical details—particularly in the extensive appendix—are beyond my expertise due to my limited physics background. Nevertheless, I was able to grasp the key elements of the approach and how it differs from prior models. The model is evaluated on a real eye-movement dataset and compared against a range of alternative models. While it does not dominate on every metric, the results are favorable and support the validity of the new approach.

I believe the paper will interest members of the vision science community (e.g., VSS attendees). I am less convinced that it represents a strong NIPS contribution, though I defer to reviewers with deeper domain expertise. It appears to advance a novel and interesting theoretical framework that could stimulate further empirical research, and thus has both novelty and merit for perception science.
