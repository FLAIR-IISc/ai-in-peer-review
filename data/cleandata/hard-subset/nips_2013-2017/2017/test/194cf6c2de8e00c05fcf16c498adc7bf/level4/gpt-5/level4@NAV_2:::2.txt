Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes
The authors study which regions in static and dynamic imagery are likely to attract human gaze. They formulate a model by positing three core principles for visual attention, encoded in an energy functional minimized by eye movements: (1) trajectory confinement within a finite retinal area via a harmonic potential at image boundaries; (2) a curiosity-driven term that favors locations with large brightness gradients in both a fine-scale and a blurred representation of the image; and (3) a brightness constancy term that penalizes changes in brightness along the trajectory. From this composite objective, they derive Euler–Lagrange differential equations that generate scanpaths on static or dynamic scenes given an initial position and velocity. The approach is evaluated quantitatively on datasets of images and videos with human eye-tracking and is shown to perform on par with leading methods.

Clear definitions of saliency and mechanistic models of eye movements are central to computational vision and cognitive science. To my knowledge, the paper presents an original and innovative formulation that can help clarify what makes locations salient and offers a principled, bottom-up dynamical model of eye movements. While it is not state-of-the-art on every metric for every dataset, it performs well and provides a refreshingly different perspective on the problem.

That said, some of the above assessment is necessarily inferential because the manuscript is hard to follow. I encourage thorough proofreading and expansion of abbreviations and symbols (both within the equations and in Section 3). I also recommend moving the two-page Appendix to the supplementary material and using the reclaimed space to define all variables and functions explicitly. Given the breadth of the NeurIPS audience, many readers will otherwise struggle with the notation and derivations.

From a psychological standpoint, it would strengthen the work to connect to literature on eye movements as near-optimal information gathering. For example, see Najemnik, J., & Geisler, W. S. (2008). Eye movement statistics in humans are consistent with an optimal search strategy. Journal of Vision, 8(3), 1–14, among other relevant papers from Geisler’s lab. A discussion comparing the proposed variational laws to that line of work would be valuable—are these essentially reformulations of similar ideas, or do they provide complementary insights that could be integrated into a more comprehensive model?
