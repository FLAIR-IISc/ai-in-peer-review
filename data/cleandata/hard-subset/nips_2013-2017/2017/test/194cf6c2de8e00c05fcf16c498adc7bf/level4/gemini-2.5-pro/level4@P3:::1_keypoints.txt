- Proposes a novel bottom-up variational model of visual attention using a Principle of Least Action with a Lagrangian (kinetic + potential) driven by boundary, curiosity, and brightness-invariance terms.  
- Produces scanpaths as the primary output (saliency maps are secondary aggregations); parameters tuned via simulated annealing and evaluated on MIT1003, CAT2000, and SFU.  
- Mathematical derivation is clear and technically sound; Euler–Lagrange equations are well presented.  
- Major weakness: evaluation uses only time-aggregated saliency maps, discarding the model’s claimed temporal dynamics.  
- Lacks analysis/comparison of dynamic scanpath properties (saccade amplitudes, fixation durations, velocities, temporal correlations with human data).  
- Paper is generally well-written; would benefit from showing example scanpath visualizations for images and videos in the main text.  
- Highly original application of the Principle of Least Action to attention, offering a principled, process-oriented alternative to static saliency and deep models.  
- Fails to connect adequately to existing optimal-control literature on eye movements and motor control (e.g., Wolpert et al.), a notable omission.  
- Potentially significant conceptual shift (saliency as emergent from dynamics), but theoretical principles (e.g., cos^2/sin^2 modulation) feel ad-hoc and lack biological justification.  
- Empirical performance on static benchmarks is respectable but does not consistently beat state-of-the-art deep learning methods, limiting practical impact.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, unified saccade/tracking framework, and emphasis on process over static prediction.  
- Arguments against acceptance: evaluation mismatch, weak motivation for chosen mathematical forms, missing key related work, and limited empirical advantage.  
- Recommendation: not ready for acceptance in current form; requires thorough dynamic evaluation and comparisons to human scanpath data to support core claims.
