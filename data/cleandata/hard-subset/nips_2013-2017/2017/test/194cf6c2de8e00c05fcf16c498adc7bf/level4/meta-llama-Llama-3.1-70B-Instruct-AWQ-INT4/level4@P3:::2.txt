Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes

This paper proposes a novel model for predicting human eye movements in static and dynamic images. The authors derive their model from three fundamental principles of visual attention, which are formalized as an energy function to be minimized by the movements of the eye. The principles include: (1) boundedness of the trajectory, modeled by a harmonic oscillator at the borders of the image within a limited-sized retina; (2) a curiosity-driven principle, highlighting regions with large changes in brightness in both a fine and blurred version of the image; and (3) brightness invariance, which increases as a function of changes in brightness. Using a cost function derived from these three principles, the authors derive differential equations for predicting eye movements across static or dynamic images.

The proposed model is evaluated quantitatively on datasets of static and dynamic scenes coupled with human eye movements. The results demonstrate that the method performs comparably to state-of-the-art approaches. The authors' work is significant, as formal definitions of saliency and modeling eye movements are critical issues in computational vision and cognitive science.

The paper's contribution is substantial, as it offers a novel and innovative model that could aid in the development of a better understanding of what makes something salient and provides a formal model for eye movements within the bottom-up tradition. Although the method may not be state-of-the-art on every metric for every dataset, it performs well and provides a refreshingly different perspective on the problem.

Weaknesses:

1. The paper is poorly written and hard to follow, making it challenging for readers to understand the authors' work.
2. The authors could improve the clarity of their writing by expanding on abbreviations and defining each variable and function used.
3. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of the psychological literature on eye movements and how the authors' approach compares to existing findings.
