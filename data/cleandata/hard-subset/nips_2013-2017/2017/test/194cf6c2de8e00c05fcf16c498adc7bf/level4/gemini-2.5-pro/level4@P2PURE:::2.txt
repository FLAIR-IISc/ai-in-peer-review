This paper investigates the principles governing human visual attention in both static and dynamic scenes. The authors propose a model derived from three core principles, which are formulated as an energy function to be minimized by eye movements. These principles are: (1) a spatial constraint, modeled as a harmonic oscillator, that confines eye movements within the image boundaries and a limited retinal field; (2) a "curiosity-driven" principle that directs attention to regions with significant brightness changes across both fine and coarse spatial scales; and (3) a principle of brightness invariance. From a cost function based on these principles, the authors derive a set of differential equations that predict scanpaths across static or dynamic images, given an initial position and velocity. The proposed model is evaluated quantitatively on datasets of static and dynamic scenes with corresponding human eye-tracking data, and the results demonstrate that its performance is comparable to state-of-the-art approaches.

The formal modeling of saliency and eye movements is a critical challenge in computational vision and cognitive science. The field has historically struggled with imprecise definitions of saliency, and this paper presents a novel and innovative model, from a bottom-up perspective, that offers a more formal framework. As such, it has the potential to advance our understanding of what makes a stimulus salient. While the model does not achieve state-of-the-art performance on every metric across all tested datasets, its results are strong and, importantly, it provides a refreshingly different theoretical perspective on the problem.

Unfortunately, the paper's primary weakness is its lack of clarity. It is poorly written and difficult to follow, to the extent that my understanding of the method, as summarized above, is based partly on conjecture. The authors are strongly encouraged to have the manuscript thoroughly proofread. They should also expand all abbreviations, both in the text (e.g., in Section 3) and within the mathematical formulations. To improve clarity, I recommend moving the current two-page appendix to the supplementary material. The space gained should be used to explicitly define every variable and function in the model. Even if these notations are standard in the authors' subfield, the broad NIPS audience requires such definitions to follow the mathematical development.

From a cognitive science perspective, the paper would be strengthened by connecting its principles to the psychological literature on eye movements as a form of optimal information gathering. For example, the work of Najemnik & Geisler (2008, "Eye movement statistics in humans are consistent with an optimal search strategy," Journal of Vision) and related research provides a relevant theoretical framework. A discussion comparing the proposed model to these optimal search strategies would be valuable. For instance, are the authors' variational principles a restatement of similar concepts in a different mathematical language, or do they provide independent constraints that could be integrated with information-theoretic models to create a more comprehensive theory of visual attention?
