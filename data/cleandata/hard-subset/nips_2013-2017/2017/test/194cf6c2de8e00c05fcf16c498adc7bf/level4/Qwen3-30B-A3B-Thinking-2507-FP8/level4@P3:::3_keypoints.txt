- Introduces EYMOL: a novel variational (Least Action) framework modeling visual attention as gaze trajectories rather than map-based fixations.  
- Attention driven by three principles: bounded retinal trajectory, curiosity (local + peripheral detail), and brightness invariance (optical-flow constraint).  
- Scanpaths are generated directly via Euler–Lagrange equations; saliency maps are derived post-hoc from trajectories.  
- Validated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video datasets using standard metrics (AUC-Judd, NSS).  
- Originality rated high: physics-inspired formulation and unified Lagrangian integrating curiosity and brightness is novel and theoretically elegant.  
- Theoretical derivation is rigorous with detailed appendix and sound experimental design comparing diverse baselines.  
- Real-time scanpath generation is a practical strength with applications in robotics and neuroscience.  
- Significance: moderate-high — offers a foundational, unified model for static and dynamic scenes though not a clear empirical breakthrough.  
- Empirical performance is competitive but not state-of-the-art (e.g., 4th/5th on MIT300, 3rd on CAT2000).  
- Lacks comparison to some recent deep-learning scanpath/saliency models (e.g., LSTM-based approaches).  
- Parameter estimation via simulated annealing on only 100 images raises generalizability and sensitivity concerns.  
- Approximation in Eq. (6) validated mainly for static images; justification for dynamic scenes is insufficient.  
- Brightness-invariance term is softly enforced and its biological plausibility relative to alternatives is not fully discussed.  
- Clarity issues: physics formalism may challenge non-physicists; inconsistent notation and need for a concise ML-friendly summary.  
- Recommendation: Accept — strong theoretical contribution and potential impact justify acceptance despite moderate empirical gains.
