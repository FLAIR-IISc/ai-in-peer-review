- Proposes a continuous-time variational model that generates scanpaths as stationary points of an action functional combining boundary, curiosity, and brightness-invariance terms.
- Derives Euler–Lagrange equations and integrates resulting second-order ODEs to produce trajectories and saliency maps via visitation aggregation.
- Positioned relative to classic bottom-up saliency models and optical-flow theory but lacks engagement with optimal-control/active-vision and saccadic biomechanics literature.
- Conceptual novelty: unified least-action framework linking curiosity, fixation, and tracking is a fresh alternative to saliency-map pipelines.
- Model directly generates scanpaths, making behavioral alignment more natural than producing saliency maps post hoc.
- Parsimony and interpretability: each Lagrangian term has clear behavioral meaning; brightness invariance is well-motivated.
- Empirical competitiveness: achieves performance comparable to strong baselines on multiple image and video datasets.
- Solid mathematical treatment with careful derivation and stability conditions (e.g., λ constraints).
- Clarity and presentation are poor: inconsistent notation, undefined variables/abbreviations, and typographical issues impede understanding.
- Important modeling and implementation choices (ω, m, k, solver details) are underspecified, harming reproducibility.
- Evaluation may be biased by preprocessing: Gaussian center bias and center-based initialization likely inflate center-sensitive metrics.
- Grid search for blur/center bias tuned on training data may be unfair if baselines were not equivalently tuned.
- Limited ablation: no systematic tests isolating curiosity, brightness, boundary terms, local vs peripheral gradients, ω, or preprocessing effects.
- Parameter estimation via simulated annealing on a small subset risks overfitting; search domain, stopping criteria, and final values are not reported.
- Behavioral validation is narrow: focuses on saliency metrics and omits scanpath statistics (saccade amplitude, fixation duration, temporal metrics, sequence similarity).
- Reproducibility details missing: exact parameter values, solver settings, runtime, and code availability are not provided.
- Claims of “real-time” performance are unquantified and should be benchmarked (fps, CPU/GPU).
- Manuscript needs substantial editing, a symbol table, and relocation of lengthy derivations to supplement to improve clarity for NIPS audience.
- Originality: integrating brightness-invariance into a Lagrangian for gaze is novel, but distinctions from optimal search/dynamic field models should be clarified.
- Significance: principled, interpretable framework with practical relevance for dynamic scenes, though not uniformly state-of-the-art.
- Suggested improvements: define all symbols, report SA-selected parameters and sensitivity, perform systematic ablations, quantify runtime, and ensure fair baseline tuning.
- Additional suggestions: compare generated scanpaths to human sequences with temporal metrics, test edge-fixation artifacts, and situate within active-vision/optimal-search literature.
- Pros recap: elegant variational formulation, generates scanpaths, competitive results, links to optical flow and physical principles.
- Cons recap: poor clarity/notation, center-bias confounds, limited ablations and parameter reporting, weak psychological grounding, and reproducibility gaps.
- Overall: promising, theoretically grounded contribution that requires clearer presentation, rigorous ablations, and stronger behavioral validation to have meaningful impact.
