This paper proposes a new approach for studying eye movements. The authors have accurately summarized the current state of the art (as far as I understand, but I admit from the outset that I am not an expert on perception or eye movements). Essentially, these approaches are based on a prominence map defined on an image, and selection of fixation points in the optimization/maximum approach. This model (EYMOL) operates according to a different perception and is instead defined directly on the trajectory of gaze according to the "minimum action theory." My ability to evaluate some mathematical details was beyond my scope because I do not have the necessary physics background (especially the extensive appendix). However, I was still able to understand the key elements of the approach and how it differs from previous models. The model has been usefully applied to real data sets of eye movements and compared with various alternative models. Although it does not dominate every measure, the results are favorable to the new approach and demonstrate that it has some validity.

I think this paper may be of interest to some in the vision science community (e.g., attendees of VSS). I am not entirely convinced that it makes a major contribution to the NIPS, although I think it should be evaluated against the opinions of other reviewers who are more specialized. I think it possibly advances a new and interesting theory that may inspire further empirical research, and therefore has both novelty and merit for vision science.
