- Proposes EYMOL, a novel variational (least-action) model of visual attention as an alternative to saliency-map approaches.
- Positions the work within prior models (Koch & Ullman, Itti et al.) and contrasts trajectory-based scanpaths vs global saliency computation.
- Core idea: model scanpaths by optimizing an action functional that incorporates boundedness, curiosity, and brightness invariance.
- Paper is well-structured with a clear mathematical formulation; appendix derivations may require specialized background to follow.
- Empirical validation on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU (images and videos) shows competitive performance.
- Novelty: framing attention via a variational least-action principle is a significant departure from existing methods.
- Theoretical foundation: grounding in physical principles offers strong basis for further mathematical analysis.
- Presentation: core concepts and formulation are communicated accessibly despite underlying physics complexity.
- Empirical strength: results demonstrate viability and competitiveness against state-of-the-art techniques.
- Unified framework: integrates curiosity and brightness invariance within a single model to explain eye movements.
- Weakness: mathematical complexity in the appendix limits accessibility, reproducibility, and independent verification.
- Weakness: parameter estimation relies on simulated annealing without thorough justification or sensitivity analysis.
- Weakness: limited discussion of biological plausibility and lack of mapping between model terms and neural processes.
- Weakness: relevance to NIPS is debatable since the contribution centers on visual attention rather than learning.
- For acceptance: novel, theoretically strong approach with supportive empirical results that could inspire further research.
- For rejection: complexity and limited biological relevance may reduce impact within the broader NIPS community.
- For rejection: primary contribution may be better suited to vision/perception-specialized venues.
- Overall assessment: technically sound and interesting; reviewer leans weak accept but recommends additional expert opinions in visual attention and physics/neuroscience.
