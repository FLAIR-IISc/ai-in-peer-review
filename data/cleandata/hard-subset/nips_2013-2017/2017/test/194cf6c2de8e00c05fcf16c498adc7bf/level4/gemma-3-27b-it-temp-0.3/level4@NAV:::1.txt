This paper presents a computational neuroscience model of visual feature saliency, integrating bottom-up processing with dynamical systems to model scanpath dynamics. The work introduces compelling concepts – specifically, the definition of “affordances” (including curiosity and brightness invariance) and the application of the Least Action Principle – to guide scanpath generation. However, the justification for these particular “affordances” feels somewhat arbitrary and could benefit from further motivation. 

Regarding the Least Action Principle, connecting the derivation to established research in eye movement control, such as work from the Wolpert lab, would strengthen the paper. While a lack of strong biological grounding is increasingly common in high-performing deep learning models of static visual salience, this model’s performance relative to those benchmarks is comparatively weak. Crucially, given the claim of capturing biological eye movement *dynamics*, the main paper would be significantly improved by including visualizations of the generated scanpaths, beyond just overall statistical comparisons to observed eye movements.
