- Proposes a novel variational model of visual attention using a generalized Least Action Principle with "affordances" (curiosity, brightness invariance) to generate scanpaths.  
- Theoretical framework is conceptually interesting and integrates physics-based principles into attention modeling.  
- Model generates scanpaths rather than just saliency maps, distinguishing it from many existing approaches.  
- Inclusion of brightness invariance is a notable addition that may stabilize fixations.  
- Achieves competitive (but not state-of-the-art) results on MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU benchmarks.  
- Motivation for the specific Lagrangian terms appears arbitrary and lacks strong biological justification.  
- Limited connection to relevant literature on saccade control, optimal control, and Bayesian decision-making (e.g., Wolpert lab).  
- Experimental results are underwhelming; modest improvements do not clearly justify the model’s increased complexity.  
- Paper lacks detailed qualitative analysis of generated scanpaths (saccade amplitudes, velocities, fixation durations) and comparisons to human data.  
- Evaluation relies mainly on NSS and AUC-Judd, which is insufficient to demonstrate biological plausibility.  
- The static-image approximation (Eq. 6) is justified but its broader implications are not fully discussed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel Least Action framework, scanpath generation focus, brightness invariance, competitive benchmark performance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: weak biological grounding, limited literature integration, missing qualitative scanpath analysis, modest empirical gains, insufficiently compelling experiments.  
- Reviewer recommendation: lean reject but would consider weak accept if authors add biological justification, detailed scanpath analyses, stronger empirical improvements/refined experiments, and a clearer discussion of limitations and future work.
