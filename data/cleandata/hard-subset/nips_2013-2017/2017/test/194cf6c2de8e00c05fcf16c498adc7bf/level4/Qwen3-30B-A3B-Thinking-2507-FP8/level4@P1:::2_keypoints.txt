- Proposes a variational framework modeling visual attention as a physical system governed by least-action principles.  
- Defines three core principles: bounded eye movements via a harmonic oscillator at image boundaries.  
- Curiosity-driven mechanism: attention attracted to high-gradient regions in both fine and blurred brightness representations.  
- Brightness invariance enabling fixation and object tracking.  
- Formalizes these into a Lagrangian; Euler–Lagrange equations yield differential equations for scanpath prediction.  
- Generates saliency maps as a byproduct of simulated eye movements rather than optimizing them directly.  
- Empirical validation on MIT1003, CAT2000, and SFU video shows competitive performance (e.g., top-2 on MIT300 NSS).  
- Strength: novel theoretical foundation unifying curiosity-driven exploration and brightness invariance in a single dynamical system.  
- Strength: strong empirical results and realistic scanpath generation, including generalization to video.  
- Strength: theoretical clarity via rigorous derivation (Appendix) linking physical principles to attentional dynamics.  
- Weakness: exposition is mathematically dense and key terms/notations lack intuitive explanation.  
- Weakness: insufficient engagement with cognitive literature on optimal foraging and information-theoretic models (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2008).  
- Weakness: methodological gaps—simulated annealing tuned on only 100 images and possible score inflation from center-bias adjustment.  
- Pro: strong theoretical innovation and unified framework for attention and tracking.  
- Con: dense presentation, limited cognitive integration, and opaque parameter-tuning methodology.  
- Recommendation: Weak Accept.  
- Requested revisions: move Appendix to supplementary and clearly define variables in main text.  
- Requested revisions: expand discussion comparing to optimal-foraging/information-gain models and clarify biological plausibility of approximations.  
- Requested revisions: justify Eq.(6) approximation and address potential impact of center-bias adjustment on evaluation metrics.
