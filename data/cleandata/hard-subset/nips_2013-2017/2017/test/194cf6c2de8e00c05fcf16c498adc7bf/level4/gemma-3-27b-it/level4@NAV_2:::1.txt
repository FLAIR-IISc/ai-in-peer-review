This paper proposes a novel computational model of visual attention, framing scanpath generation through a variational approach inspired by the Least Action Principle in physics. The core idea is to define a generalized action whose stationary point yields differential equations governing attention dynamics, driven by principles of curiosity (detail seeking) and brightness invariance. The model is evaluated on standard saliency detection datasets (images and video) and compared to existing methods.

The paper’s strength lies in its attempt to bridge principles from physics and cognitive science to model visual attention. The formulation based on a generalized action is a unique and potentially powerful approach, offering a different perspective compared to traditional saliency map-based methods. The inclusion of both bottom-up (detail) and invariance-based principles is a commendable effort to capture essential aspects of human attention. The authors clearly present the mathematical formulation and the algorithm for parameter estimation. Furthermore, the evaluation on both static images and dynamic scenes demonstrates the model’s applicability to different scenarios.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the “affordances” (curiosity, brightness invariance) guiding the scanpath are intuitively appealing, their justification remains somewhat ad hoc. Further motivation connecting these principles to underlying cognitive or neurophysiological mechanisms would strengthen the work. The connection to established work in eye movement research, such as the computational models developed by the Wolpert lab and others, is not adequately explored. Despite the model’s claim to capture biological eye movement dynamics, the paper lacks detailed characterization of the generated scanpaths beyond standard saliency metrics. Visualizations comparing the qualitative properties of the simulated scanpaths (e.g., saccade lengths, fixation durations, scanpath smoothness) with human data would be highly valuable. While the experimental results are reported, the performance of the model is not competitive with state-of-the-art deep learning-based saliency models, particularly given the model's complexity. Further analysis is needed to understand the limitations of the current formulation and potential avenues for improvement. The approximation used in V1, while simplifying the equations, should be further justified regarding its impact on the overall behavior of the model. Finally, while the authors acknowledge limitations, a more in-depth discussion of the model’s potential biases and sensitivities to parameter settings would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to modeling visual attention based on variational principles.
*   Clear mathematical formulation and algorithmic description.
*   Evaluation on both image and video datasets.
*   Integration of bottom-up and invariance-based principles.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Ad hoc justification of guiding principles (curiosity, brightness invariance).
*   Limited connection to existing work in eye movement research.
*   Lack of detailed characterization of generated scanpaths.
*   Subpar performance compared to state-of-the-art saliency models.
*   Limited discussion of model limitations and potential biases.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting and ambitious attempt to model visual attention through a physics-inspired framework. However, the lack of strong empirical results, limited connection to established work in the field, and incomplete characterization of the model’s behavior raise concerns. While the work is not currently of sufficient impact to be accepted, it has the potential to be a valuable contribution with further development and refinement. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the identified weaknesses and explore more extensive validation and analysis.
