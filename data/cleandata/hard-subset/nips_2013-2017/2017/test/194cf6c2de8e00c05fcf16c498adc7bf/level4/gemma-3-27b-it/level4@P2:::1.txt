This paper presents a novel computational model of visual attention, aiming to capture the dynamics of scanpaths through a bottom-up approach informed by principles from dynamical systems. The authors propose variational laws of eye movement, grounded in a generalized Least Action Principle, where “affordances” – curiosity and brightness invariance – shape the scanpath. This is a conceptually interesting approach, and the attempt to connect attention to foundational principles like Least Action is commendable. However, while the ideas are appealing, the paper suffers from a lack of strong empirical support and a limited connection to relevant biological literature.

The core idea is to define a Lagrangian that incorporates potential energy related to image details and peripheral features, and kinetic energy representing eye movement. The resulting differential equations then describe the dynamics of attention. This approach differs from many existing saliency models, which typically rely on global computation and winner-take-all algorithms (e.g., Itti et al., [10], Koch & Ullman [9]). The authors correctly position their work against this backdrop, highlighting that their model attempts to *generate* scanpaths rather than simply estimate saliency maps as a post-hoc analysis.  Recent deep learning approaches (e.g., [18, 26]) have achieved state-of-the-art performance in saliency estimation, but often lack a clear mechanistic explanation of the underlying processes. 

The paper’s strengths lie in its theoretical framework and the attempt to integrate principles from physics into a computational model of attention. The use of a Least Action Principle is intriguing, and the defined "affordances" – while somewhat ad-hoc – provide a potential basis for understanding the driving forces behind scanpaths. The inclusion of brightness invariance is also a notable addition, potentially contributing to more stable fixations.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The motivation for the specific form of the Lagrangian terms feels somewhat arbitrary.  While the authors mention curiosity and brightness invariance, a more thorough exploration of the biological basis for these affordances would strengthen the argument. Specifically, it would be valuable to connect this work to existing research on the neural mechanisms of saccade generation, such as the work from the Wolpert lab and others investigating optimal control and Bayesian decision-making in the context of eye movements.  Furthermore, the experimental results are underwhelming. While the model achieves competitive results on standard saliency datasets (MIT300, CAT2000, SFU), the improvements over existing methods are not substantial, especially given the increased complexity of the model. Critically, the paper lacks a detailed qualitative analysis of the *generated scanpaths themselves*.  Figures illustrating the characteristics of the simulated eye movements—saccade amplitudes, velocities, fixation durations—and a comparison to human data would be crucial for demonstrating the biological plausibility of the model. The reliance on NSS and AUC-Judd scores alone provides an insufficient evaluation. Finally, while the approximation made for static images (Eq. 6) is justified, its implications for the broader applicability of the model are not fully discussed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical framework based on the Least Action Principle.
*   Attempts to model scanpath generation rather than just saliency estimation.
*   Integration of brightness invariance into the model.
*   Competitive (though not state-of-the-art) performance on standard benchmarks.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Weak biological motivation for the Lagrangian terms.
*   Limited connection to existing literature on saccade control.
*   Lack of detailed qualitative analysis of generated scanpaths.
*   Modest improvements over existing saliency models.
*   Experimental results are not sufficiently compelling to justify the complexity of the model.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting idea but falls short of delivering a convincing demonstration of its value. While the theoretical framework is promising, the lack of strong empirical support and the limited connection to biological data are significant concerns. I currently lean towards **reject**, but would consider a weak accept if the authors could address the following points: 1) provide a more thorough biological justification for the chosen Lagrangian terms, 2) include a detailed qualitative analysis of the generated scanpaths, and 3) demonstrate a more substantial improvement over existing methods, potentially through more targeted experiments or a refinement of the model parameters. A more detailed discussion of the limitations of the model and potential avenues for future research would also be beneficial.
