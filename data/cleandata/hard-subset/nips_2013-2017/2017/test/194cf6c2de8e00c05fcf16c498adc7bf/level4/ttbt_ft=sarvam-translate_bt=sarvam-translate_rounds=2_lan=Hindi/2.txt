Dynamic Vision: The Variable Rules of Visual Attention

The authors examine the places where humans pay attention to static and dynamic images. They derive a model by defining three basic principles for visual attention (defined as an energy function that must be minimized by eye movement): (1) Eye movement is constrained by a harmonic oscillator on the borders of the image within a limited size retina. (2) A "curiosity-driven theory" that highlights areas with large changes in brightness in both subtle and blurry versions of the image, and (3) brightness constancy, which increases as a function of brightness change. Using the cost function derived from these three functions, the authors obtain differential equations to predict eye movement in static or dynamic images (based on starting position and starting velocity). The authors quantitatively evaluate their technique on a data set of static and dynamic scenes paired with human eye movements. They demonstrate that their method performs at par with state-of-the-art technology.

Salins' formal definitions and modeling of eye movement are important issues in computational vision and cognitive science. Psychologists have long been plagued by Salins' vague definitions, and the authors propose a novel and innovative model (as far as I know) that can help develop a better understanding of what makes something what it is and a formal model for eye movement (within the down-up tradition). While it is not state-of-the-art on every metric for every data set, it performs well and provides a fresh, different perspective on the problem.

Unfortunately, some of what I have written above is based on assumption because the paper is poorly written and difficult to follow. I recommend that the authors have the paper proofread by others and expand on the condensation (both within the equations and in Section 3). I would recommend that they take the 2-page appendix into the supplementary material and use those additional pages to define each variable and the functions used (even if this is a tradition within your own field – the NIPS audience comes from many different disciplines and some will have trouble following the math otherwise).

As a psychologist, I would like the authors to link their work to some psychological literature on eye movement, which are optimal steps for gathering information. See Nazmanik, J., and Geissler, W. S. (2008). Figures of eye movement in humans conform to an optimal search strategy. Journal of Vision, 8 (3), 1-14. There are other relevant articles as well (especially from Geissler's lab, but this should give the authors a clue to follow that literature). I would be interested to see how their approach compares to their findings (for example, are they iterations of similar models or provide independent information that can be integrated to create a better model?).
