

This paper introduces EYMOL, a novel framework for modeling eye movement trajectories grounded in the "Least Action Principle" rather than conventional saliency map optimization. The authors effectively contrast their approach with existing methods, clarifying that while prior work selects fixation points via optimization over image-based saliency maps, EYMOL operates directly on gaze trajectories by minimizing a path-based action functional. The core theoretical innovation is well-motivated and clearly articulated, though the mathematical formalism in the appendix (particularly its physics-inspired derivations) exceeds my expertise. Despite this, I found the key conceptual contributions and model implementation accessible and logically presented.

The empirical evaluation demonstrates the model's validity through application to a real-world eye-tracking dataset, with comprehensive comparisons against multiple established baselines. While EYMOL does not universally dominate all performance metrics, it achieves statistically significant improvements on several critical measures of trajectory prediction accuracy and consistency with human fixation patterns. The results provide compelling evidence that the Least Action Principle offers a viable and potentially more biologically plausible alternative to prevailing saliency-based paradigms.

Regarding significance, the work represents a meaningful theoretical advance for computational models of visual attention, with clear potential to stimulate new empirical investigations in vision science. The model's foundation in physical principles offers a fresh perspective that could bridge computational neuroscience and psychophysics, making it highly relevant for the Vision Sciences Society (VSS) community. However, its direct contribution to machine learning—particularly the core NeurIPS audience—requires careful consideration. While the methodological approach (trajectory modeling via variational principles) possesses ML-adjacent elements, the primary innovation lies in perceptual theory rather than algorithmic or statistical advances typical of NeurIPS. The paper's strongest appeal is to vision science researchers, though its theoretical framework may resonate with ML researchers interested in biologically inspired models.

I remain unconvinced that EYMOL constitutes a *major* contribution to NeurIPS, as it does not present novel ML algorithms, theoretical guarantees, or substantial performance breakthroughs in standard ML benchmarks. That said, the work is methodologically sound, well-executed, and offers a genuinely novel theoretical perspective. It is likely to be of interest to a subset of NeurIPS attendees focused on vision and cognitive modeling, but its broader relevance to the conference’s core ML community is limited. The paper would be a stronger fit for venues like *Journal of Vision* or *Vision Sciences Society* proceedings. Ultimately, while the contribution is valid and potentially influential within perception science, it does not meet the threshold for a high-impact NeurIPS paper. I recommend rejection unless other reviewers with deeper expertise in eye movement modeling strongly endorse its significance for the ML community.
