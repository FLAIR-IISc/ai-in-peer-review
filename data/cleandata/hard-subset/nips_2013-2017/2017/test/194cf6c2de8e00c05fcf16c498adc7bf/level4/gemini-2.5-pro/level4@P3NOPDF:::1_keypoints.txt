- Proposes a dynamical systems model of visual saliency combining bottom-up features with a Least Action Principle and novel "affordances" (e.g., curiosity, brightness invariance) to generate scanpaths.  
- Central ideas are creative and thought-provoking, offering a novel perspective on attentional guidance.  
- Novelty: defining "affordances" that guide attention beyond low-level features is an interesting and welcome contribution.  
- Theoretical appeal: applying the Least Action Principle to attention trajectories is a compelling approach that could unify aspects of visual exploration.  
- Affordances motivation is ad-hoc: the chosen affordances and their mathematical forms lack rigorous theoretical or empirical justification and appear postulated.  
- Weak linkage to prior work: the paper fails to situate the Least Action framework within existing motor-control and eye-movement literature (e.g., optimal control theories).  
- Empirical performance is mixed and substantially below state-of-the-art static saliency models, weakening the contribution unless trade-offs are clearly justified.  
- Critical missing analysis: no thorough quantitative or qualitative evaluation of dynamic scanpath properties (saccade amplitudes, fixation durations, transition probabilities, scanpath similarity), and no illustrative figures in the main text to validate the dynamics claim.
