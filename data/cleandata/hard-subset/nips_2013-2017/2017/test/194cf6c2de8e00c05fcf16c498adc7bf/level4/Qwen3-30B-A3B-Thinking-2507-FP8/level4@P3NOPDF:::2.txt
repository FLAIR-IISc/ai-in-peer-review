

The paper proposes a variational model for predicting human visual attention in static and dynamic scenes, grounded in three novel principles: (1) retinal boundary constraints modeled via a harmonic oscillator, (2) a "change-driven" principle emphasizing regions with significant brightness variations across multi-scale image representations, and (3) brightness invariance that scales with luminance changes. The authors derive differential equations from these principles to predict eye movement trajectories, evaluated against human fixation data from static and dynamic scene datasets. Results show competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods, though not consistently superior across all metrics.

The work addresses a critical gap in computational vision: the lack of formal, psychologically grounded models for saliency and eye movement dynamics. By framing attention as an energy-minimization process constrained by biologically plausible principles, the authors offer a refreshing alternative to purely data-driven approaches. This formalism could significantly advance both computational models and cognitive theories of bottom-up attention. The model’s novelty—particularly the integration of multi-scale brightness changes and boundary constraints—appears well-motivated, and the empirical validation is thorough.

However, the paper’s execution severely undermines its contributions. The writing is opaque and difficult to follow, with insufficient explanation of key terms, equations, and assumptions. For instance, Section 3 introduces numerous unexplained abbreviations (e.g., "S" for saliency, "E" for energy) without definitions, and the mathematical derivations lack intuitive scaffolding. This is especially problematic for the NIPS audience, which spans diverse disciplines. The authors should relocate the 2-page appendix to supplementary material and dedicate those pages to defining *all* variables, functions, and notation explicitly. A single line like "where *k* is the spring constant" would clarify the harmonic oscillator constraint, which currently reads as a physics abstraction without psychological grounding.

Furthermore, the work lacks meaningful engagement with foundational psychological literature on attentional optimization. The authors cite no studies on how eye movements minimize information-gathering costs—despite the model’s clear alignment with optimal search theories. For example, Najemnik & Geisler (2008) demonstrated that human eye movements follow statistically optimal search strategies in visual scenes. The authors should explicitly discuss: (a) how their energy-minimization framework relates to these optimal strategies, (b) whether their "change-driven" principle reinterprets or extends such models, and (c) how their dynamic scene approach complements or diverges from work on sequential visual search (e.g., from Geisler’s lab). Without this, the paper risks appearing disconnected from the cognitive science context it aims to inform. A brief literature discussion would strengthen the contribution and clarify the model’s novelty.

The evaluation also warrants clarification. The paper claims "comparable performance" to SOTA, but does not specify which metrics or datasets it underperforms on. A table comparing key results (e.g., AUC, NSS) against top baselines would strengthen this claim. Additionally, the dynamic scene evaluation lacks ablation studies—e.g., how much each principle contributes to performance?—which would help isolate the model’s core innovations.

In summary, the paper presents a conceptually promising model with strong empirical grounding, but its impact is hampered by poor exposition and missed connections to psychological theory. The core ideas are valuable, yet the current presentation risks alienating both computational and cognitive audiences. With significant revisions—particularly to clarify notation, integrate key literature, and refine the evaluation—the work could make a meaningful contribution. As written, it is technically sound but fails to communicate its significance effectively.

**Recommendation:** Major Revision. The authors must address the clarity issues (notation, exposition) and substantially expand the discussion of psychological context. The model’s potential is evident, but its realization requires careful polishing for NIPS’ interdisciplinary audience.
