This paper proposes a novel paradigm for modeling visual attention, grounded in the principle that there are foundational laws driving the emergence of visual attention. The authors devise variational laws of eye movement based on a generalized view of the Least Action Principle in physics. The model incorporates three fundamental principles: boundedness of the trajectory, curiosity-driven attention, and brightness invariance. These principles are given a mathematical expression through a variational approach, leading to differential equations that describe the dynamics of visual attention.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise description of their model. The use of mathematical derivations and equations is appropriate for the subject matter, and the authors do a good job of explaining the underlying principles and assumptions. The experimental results, which validate the model on tasks of saliency detection, are also clearly presented.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to capture the dynamics of scanpaths in a unified framework. The model's performance on saliency detection tasks is promising, especially considering that it is a relatively simple model compared to other state-of-the-art approaches. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of the related work in the field, which helps to situate their contribution within the broader context of visual attention research.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One potential issue is that the "affordances" driving the scan-path, such as curiosity and brightness invariance, are somewhat ad hoc and not further motivated. While the authors provide some justification for these choices, it would be helpful to see more theoretical or empirical evidence supporting their selection. Additionally, the lack of biological motivation for the model is a minor issue, but it would be interesting to see how the authors' approach relates to actual work in eye movement research, such as past and recent work by the Wolpert lab.

In terms of the model's performance, while it achieves promising results on saliency detection tasks, its performance is not consistently better than other state-of-the-art models. The authors could benefit from providing more detailed analysis and visualization of the generated scanpaths, not just in terms of their overall statistics but also in terms of their temporal characteristics.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and intriguing approach to modeling visual attention. While there are some limitations and areas for improvement, the authors' contribution is a valuable addition to the field, and their model has the potential to capture important aspects of human eye movement dynamics.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and unified framework for modeling visual attention.
* The model's performance on saliency detection tasks is promising, especially considering its relative simplicity.
* The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work and clearly explain the underlying principles and assumptions of their model.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The "affordances" driving the scan-path are somewhat ad hoc and not further motivated.
* The lack of biological motivation for the model is a minor issue, but it would be helpful to see more connection to actual work in eye movement research.
* The model's performance is not consistently better than other state-of-the-art models, and more detailed analysis and visualization of the generated scanpaths would be beneficial.
