This paper proposes a novel paradigm for modeling visual attention, grounded in the principle that there are foundational laws driving the emergence of visual attention. The authors devise variational laws of eye-movement based on a generalized view of the Least Action Principle in physics, incorporating terms for boundedness of the trajectory, curiosity-driven principle, and brightness invariance. The model is then used to estimate the model parameters and evaluate its performance in tasks of saliency detection.

The main idea of the paper is to capture the dynamics of scanpaths using a set of "affordances" (curiosity, brightness invariance) that drive the scan-path. The use of the Least Action Principle is an interesting approach, and the authors provide a clear derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations of motion that describe attention dynamics.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. Firstly, the "affordances" postulated by the authors seem a bit ad hoc and lack further motivation. While the authors provide some references to previous work on visual attention, they do not fully explore the relationship between their model and existing work in eye movement research. For example, it would have been interesting to relate their model to past and recent work by the Wolpert lab.

Furthermore, the performance of the model with respect to benchmark-busting deep learning models of (static) visual salience is mixed. While the authors report some promising results, they do not provide a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art models. Additionally, the paper would have benefited from actual figures characterizing the generated scan paths, not only in terms of their overall statistics but also in terms of their temporal dynamics.

In terms of the criteria for evaluation, I would rate the paper as follows:

* Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations of motion. However, the lack of biological motivation and the mixed performance of the model are drawbacks. (7/10)
* Clarity: The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their model and its components. However, some of the mathematical derivations could be improved for clarity. (8/10)
* Originality: The use of the Least Action Principle to model visual attention is an original approach. However, the "affordances" postulated by the authors seem a bit ad hoc, and the relationship between their model and existing work in eye movement research is not fully explored. (6/10)
* Significance: The paper proposes a novel paradigm for modeling visual attention, which could have implications for our understanding of human vision and attention. However, the mixed performance of the model and the lack of biological motivation limit its significance. (7/10)

Overall, I would recommend accepting the paper for publication, but with some revisions to address the limitations mentioned above.
