Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces EYMOL, a variational model of visual attention that predicts scanpaths by framing eye movements as the stationary point of a generalized least-action functional. Unlike most saliency-based approaches that compute a global saliency map and then apply selection heuristics (e.g., winner-take-all and inhibition-of-return), this work models trajectories directly. The Lagrangian includes kinetic energy, a potential term to keep the trajectory within image bounds, a “curiosity” component that favors regions with strong local gradients and peripheral features, and a brightness-invariance term that encourages fixation and motion tracking. The authors derive the corresponding Euler–Lagrange equations (with an exact and an approximated brightness-invariance form) and estimate parameters via simulated annealing. They evaluate the resulting trajectories indirectly by aggregating visits into saliency maps and compare performance against several established models on standard datasets. While not best on every metric, the results are competitive and generally favorable.

Quality:
I am not a specialist in perception or eye movements, and parts of the mathematical appendix were beyond my expertise in physics. That said, the main ideas and derivations in the body of the paper were understandable, and the modeling assumptions were clearly stated. The empirical evaluation appears reasonable: the model is tested against multiple baselines and metrics, and the results support the claim that the approach is viable. Because I cannot fully assess the technical rigor of the derivations, I would value input from a reviewer with stronger background in the calculus of variations and dynamical systems.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written. The high-level motivation and contrasts with saliency-map methods are clear. The extensive appendix is thorough, but some readers (including me) may struggle with the physics-based formalism. The presentation of the two versions (exact vs. approximate brightness invariance) and their comparable performance on static images was helpful.

Originality:
The core contribution—modeling attention scanpaths via a least-action principle and deriving eye-movement dynamics from a Lagrangian with curiosity and brightness-invariance terms—is novel relative to the dominant saliency-map paradigm. Even if related ideas have been explored in computational neuroscience, this specific formulation and its application to saliency prediction is a fresh contribution.

Significance:
The work offers a coherent theoretical framework that could influence how scanpaths and attention are modeled, especially in early vision. It is likely to interest vision science (e.g., VSS attendees). For NIPS, I am less certain about the breadth of impact, as the empirical gains are not uniformly dominant and the theory may be more compelling to a specialized audience. Nonetheless, it presents a new perspective with the potential to inspire follow-up studies.

Strengths:
- Clear conceptual departure from standard saliency-map approaches by modeling trajectories directly.
- Principled formulation integrating curiosity and brightness invariance within a single variational framework.
- Competitive empirical results that support the viability of the model.
- Sensible comparison to multiple baselines and metrics.

Weaknesses:
- The mathematical derivations are heavy; I cannot fully verify their correctness and would defer to a specialist.
- The model does not consistently outperform alternatives on all metrics.
- The contribution may be more aligned with vision science than the core NIPS audience.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and principled modeling framework for scanpaths with competitive performance.
- Potential to stimulate new research directions in attention modeling.
- Clear contrast to and complementarity with saliency-map methods.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited accessibility and verifiability of the technical appendix for the broader NIPS community.
- Empirical results, while favorable, are not decisively superior across the board.
- Unclear whether the impact will extend beyond specialized perception/attention research.

Overall assessment:
This is an interesting and original paper that proposes a well-motivated alternative to saliency-map modeling and demonstrates encouraging results. I believe it will be of particular interest to the vision science community. For NIPS, I would welcome additional expert opinions on the technical derivations and on the potential broader impact.
