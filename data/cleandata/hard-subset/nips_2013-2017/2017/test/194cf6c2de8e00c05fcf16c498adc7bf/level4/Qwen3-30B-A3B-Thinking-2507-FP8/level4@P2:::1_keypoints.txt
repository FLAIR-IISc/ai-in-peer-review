- Proposes a novel visual-attention model based on a generalized Least Action Principle to simulate scanpath dynamics in static and dynamic scenes.
- Introduces two key affordances: curiosity-driven attention (attracted to high-detail gradients) and brightness invariance (for fixation and tracking).
- Derives attention dynamics via variational principles; saliency maps emerge as a byproduct of simulated scanpaths.
- Connects computational neuroscience with physics-inspired modeling; includes Euler–Lagrange derivations and simulated-annealing parameter estimation.
- Offers a unified dynamical system that handles both images and video, producing saliency without directly optimizing saliency maps.
- Demonstrates competitive performance on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video for metrics like NSS and AUC-Judd.
- Motivation for the chosen affordances feels ad hoc and lacks sufficient biological grounding or links to prior eye-movement/control literature (e.g., Wolpert lab).
- Claim that the model captures biological eye-movement dynamics is not supported by visual evidence of generated scanpaths.
- Performance claims are misleading: the model does not surpass recent deep-learning approaches and lacks comparisons to current state-of-the-art methods.
- Critical omission of scanpath visualizations — paper reports static saliency maps but not the actual trajectories it aims to model.
- Experimental evaluation is incomplete; should include scanpath-similarity metrics, fixation-duration distributions, and saccade-amplitude analyses.
- Paper is generally clear but some appendix variational-calculus details may be hard for non-expert readers; affordance intuition needs clearer explanation.
- Originality is strong: applying physics-inspired variational principles to visual attention is a novel contribution.
- Practical significance is limited by weaker performance relative to deep models; potential value if integrated with or informing deep-learning approaches.
- Recommended improvements: better biological justification, visual and quantitative scanpath comparisons to humans, and comparisons against the latest deep-learning baselines.
