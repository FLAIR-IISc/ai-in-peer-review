This paper presents a computational neuroscience model of visual feature saliency that integrates bottom-up processing with dynamical systems modeling to simulate scanpath dynamics. I found the core ideas to be promising, particularly the novel definition of “affordances” – encompassing concepts like curiosity and brightness invariance – as drivers of scanpaths, and the application of the Least Action Principle. However, the proposed affordances feel somewhat *ad hoc* and lack strong justification. 

Within the context of the Least Action Principle and its derivation, connecting the work to relevant research in eye movement control – specifically, studies from the Wolpert lab and others – would have strengthened the paper. While a lack of extensive biological motivation is increasingly common in the field of deep learning-based static visual salience models, the comparatively weak performance of this model relative to those benchmarks is a significant concern. The paper aims to model biological eye movement dynamics, and yet the main text lacks comprehensive visual characterization of the generated scanpaths beyond overall statistical matching of eye movements. 

Specifically, including figures illustrating the *qualitative* properties of the simulated scanpaths – their trajectories, saccade amplitudes, and fixation durations – would have been highly valuable. This would allow for a more direct assessment of how well the model captures the nuances of human visual exploration. Without such visualization, it is difficult to fully evaluate whether the model’s success relies solely on quantitative metrics and whether the simulated scanpaths *appear* biologically plausible.

Further details regarding experimental setup would be useful. The paper mentions using the same datasets as previous work but doesn’t fully specify preprocessing or parameter settings. A clearer description of these details will help reproduce the results.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting, albeit preliminary, approach to modeling scanpath dynamics. The combination of bottom-up saliency with dynamical systems and the Least Action Principle is a novel contribution. However, the limited biological justification for the affordances, the relatively poor performance compared to existing models, and the lack of detailed visual analysis of the generated scanpaths significantly weaken the paper’s impact. Addressing these points would substantially improve the work.
