

This paper proposes a bottom-up visual saliency model incorporating dynamical systems to capture scanpath dynamics. The authors introduce novel "affordances" (curiosity, brightness invariance) and apply the Least Action Principle to model eye movement trajectories. While these concepts are intellectually stimulating, the affordances lack sufficient theoretical grounding and are presented as ad hoc postulates without deeper motivation or justification. The derivation of the Least Action Principle would benefit from explicit connection to established work in oculomotor control, such as the pioneering eye movement research from the Wolpert lab (e.g., studies on optimal control and movement planning).  

The primary limitation lies in the model’s performance relative to contemporary benchmarks. The paper claims to capture biological scanpath dynamics but fails to demonstrate competitive results against state-of-the-art deep learning models for *static* visual salience (e.g., those achieving high accuracy on datasets like MIT1003 or SALICON). Crucially, the model’s performance on dynamic scanpath prediction is subpar compared to both static salience models and dedicated oculomotor prediction frameworks. This undermines the paper’s central claim, as a model purporting to model biological dynamics must outperform or at least match the performance of models designed for static salience—a standard expected in the field.  

The absence of visualizations of generated scanpaths in the main paper is particularly problematic. The authors rely on summary statistics (e.g., fixation distributions) but omit direct comparisons of actual scanpath trajectories against ground-truth data. Without figures showing how the model’s predicted paths align with human scanpaths in terms of sequence, duration, and spatial coherence, it remains unclear whether the dynamical model genuinely captures the *temporal* structure of eye movements. Supplementary materials could address this, but the core argument requires these visualizations to be presented in the main text for credibility.  

While the dynamical systems approach offers a theoretically interesting perspective on scanpath generation, the paper’s failure to demonstrate robust performance against relevant baselines and its lack of empirical validation for the claimed biological plausibility weaken its contribution. The connection to existing oculomotor literature is superficial, and the affordances require stronger theoretical justification. The model’s value as a *biologically plausible* framework for scanpath prediction is not convincingly established given its performance gap with static salience models, which are the baseline for comparison in this domain.  

In summary, the paper presents an intriguing theoretical framework but falls short on empirical validation and benchmarking. To strengthen the work, the authors should: (1) provide explicit comparisons to dynamic oculomotor models (not just static salience models), (2) include visualizations of predicted vs. human scanpaths, and (3) rigorously justify the affordances through biological or computational principles. Without these, the contribution remains limited to a niche dynamical systems interpretation rather than a meaningful advance in computational models of visual attention.
