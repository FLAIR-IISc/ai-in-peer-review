This paper in computational neuroscience introduces a bottom-up saliency approach coupled with a dynamical-systems formulation intended to model scanpath dynamics. I enjoyed reading it; it presents appealing ideas in defining “affordances” (curiosity, brightness invariance) that guide the scanpath, along with the use of the Least Action Principle. However, these “affordances” feel somewhat ad hoc and are not further substantiated.

Within the Least Action framework and its derivation, it would have been valuable to relate the approach to established eye-movement research, for example past and recent work from the Wolpert lab. While the limited biological grounding might be a minor concern in an era of high-performing deep models of (static) visual saliency, the more substantive issue is that the model’s performance relative to such methods is mixed to poor. Given the claim to capture biological eye-movement dynamics, it would also have been useful to include, in the main paper, figures that characterize the generated scanpaths, not only their overall statistics in matching eye-movement data.
