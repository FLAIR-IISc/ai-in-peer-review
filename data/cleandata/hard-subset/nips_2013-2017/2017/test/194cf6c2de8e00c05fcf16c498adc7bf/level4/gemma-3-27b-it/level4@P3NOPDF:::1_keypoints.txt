- Novel approach combining bottom-up saliency with dynamical systems and Least Action Principle; interesting definition of affordances (curiosity, brightness invariance).
- Affordances feel ad hoc and lack theoretical justification or motivation.
- Need stronger connection to existing eye-movement and motor-control literature (e.g., Wolpert lab, optimal control, minimum-jerk).
- Claim of capturing biological eye-movement dynamics requires direct comparison to established findings and models.
- Empirical performance is mixed and does not convincingly outperform or match static saliency SOTA.
- Main text lacks visualizations of generated scanpaths and qualitative examples compared to human scanpaths.
- Missing quantitative characterization of scanpath properties (saccade amplitudes, velocities, fixation durations, sequential patterns).
- Request for justification of why curiosity and brightness invariance were chosen and consideration of alternative affordances.
- Request for a detailed performance breakdown to identify where the model succeeds or fails and image types affecting performance.
- Recommend an ablation study to assess each affordance’s contribution to scanpath generation.
- Overall: promising but underdeveloped; needs stronger biological motivation, theoretical grounding, visualizations, analyses, and revisions.
