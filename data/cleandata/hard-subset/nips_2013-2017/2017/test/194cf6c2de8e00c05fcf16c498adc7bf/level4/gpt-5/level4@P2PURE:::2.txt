Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes
The paper investigates which locations in static and dynamic images attract human attention. The authors formulate a model by positing three basic principles of visual attention, expressed as an energy function that eye movements seek to minimize: (1) eye movements are constrained by a harmonic-oscillator-like potential at the image borders within a limited retinal extent; (2) a curiosity-driven term that emphasizes regions with large brightness changes in both high-resolution and blurred versions of the image; and (3) a brightness-invariance term whose value increases with changes in brightness. From a cost function built from these three components, they derive differential equations to predict scanpaths over static or dynamic scenes, conditioned on the starting position and initial velocity. The method is evaluated quantitatively on datasets of static and dynamic scenes with human eye movements and is shown to perform comparably to the state of the art.

Providing formal definitions of saliency and models of eye movements is a central challenge in computational vision and cognitive science. Psychologists have long grappled with ambiguous notions of saliency, and the authors offer a novel and, to my knowledge, innovative model that could help clarify what makes stimuli salient and furnish a formal, bottom-up account of eye movements. Although it may not be state-of-the-art on every metric for every dataset, it performs well and brings a refreshingly different perspective to the problem.

However, parts of this assessment rest on inference because the paper is poorly written and difficult to follow. I strongly recommend thorough proofreading and the expansion of abbreviations and acronyms, both in the equations and in Section 3. I also suggest moving the two-page Appendix to the supplementary material and using the recovered space to define every variable and function used. Even if these notations are standard in your subfield, the NIPS audience is multidisciplinary and many readers will struggle to follow the mathematics without explicit definitions.

As a psychologist, I would also like to see the work situated within the literature on eye movements as optimal information-gathering steps. For example, see Najemnik, J., & Geisler, W. S. (2008). Eye movement statistics in humans are consistent with an optimal search strategy. Journal of Vision, 8(3), 1–14. There are additional relevant papers (particularly from Geisler’s lab). A discussion comparing your approach to those findings would be valuable—e.g., whether your principles restate similar models or offer complementary insights that could be integrated to yield a stronger model.
