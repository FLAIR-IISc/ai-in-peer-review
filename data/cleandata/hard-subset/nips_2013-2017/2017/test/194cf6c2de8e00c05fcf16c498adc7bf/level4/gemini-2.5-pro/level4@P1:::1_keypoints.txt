- Introduces EYMOL, a model of visual attention for dynamic scenes that frames scanpaths via a variational Least Action Principle.  
- Formulates a Lagrangian with kinetic energy and a generalized potential energy to derive Euler–Lagrange motion equations for scanpaths.  
- Potential energy decomposed into three terms: harmonic oscillator (image boundary constraint), "curiosity" (attraction to high-detail gradients), and "brightness invariance" (fixation/tracking via optical-flow-like constraint).  
- Model parameters are estimated using simulated annealing; saliency maps are produced by aggregating fixations from multiple runs.  
- Core conceptual contribution is novel and elegant, offering an interpretable, physically principled framework for scanpath generation.  
- The choice of principles ("curiosity" and "brightness invariance") is somewhat ad-hoc and lacks strong grounding in cognitive science or neuroscience.  
- Paper misses connections to relevant motor-control and eye-movement optimality literature (e.g., Harris & Wolpert and related models).  
- Empirical saliency performance is not competitive with current deep-learning state-of-the-art methods.  
- Major mismatch between the claimed contribution (scanpath dynamics) and evaluation, which focuses on time-collapsed static saliency maps.  
- Lacks analysis comparing generated scanpath statistics (saccade amplitudes, fixation durations, velocity profiles) to human data and example visual comparisons.  
- Acceptance pro: novel, theoretically principled departure from data-driven approaches.  
- Acceptance con: weak theoretical grounding for components, poor benchmark performance, and inadequate evaluation of the model’s primary output (dynamics).
