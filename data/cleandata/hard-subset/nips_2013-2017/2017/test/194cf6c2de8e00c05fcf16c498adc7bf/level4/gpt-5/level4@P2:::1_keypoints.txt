- Summary: Proposes a physics-inspired bottom-up model (EYMOL) that generates scanpaths by minimizing a generalized action with boundary, curiosity (local vs. peripheral), and brightness-invariance terms; saliency maps arise from trajectory visit counts and parameters are tuned by simulated annealing, with experiments on multiple datasets showing competitive results on some metrics.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on classic saliency, information-theoretic, and optical-flow ideas but lacks engagement with oculomotor optimal-control literature (e.g., Wolpert) that could ground cost formulations.  
- Strength: Provides a unified, explicit dynamical system that naturally handles both saccades and tracking.  
- Strength: Elegant variational/least-action formulation with substantial derivations and reproducible numerical integration.  
- Strength: Saliency emerges as a byproduct; simple to implement, fast to simulate, and evaluated on multiple static and dynamic datasets.  
- Weakness: Curiosity and brightness-invariance terms feel ad hoc and weakly biologically motivated, with weights tuned for benchmarks.  
- Weakness: Empirical evaluation emphasizes saliency-map metrics but lacks thorough scanpath analyses (saccade statistics, fixation clustering, IOR).  
- Weakness: Mixed performance versus modern deep saliency models with no clear trade-off demonstrated (e.g., interpretability vs performance).  
- Weakness: Limited analysis of parameter sensitivity and stability; some instability constraints noted but not deeply explored.  
- Quality: Mathematical development seems sound and documented, but key terms need justification or ablation and numerical issues should be systematically reported.  
- Clarity: Generally well written but dense; needs exemplar scanpath figures and clearer implementation details (initialization, timestep, ω, preprocessing, runtime).  
- Originality: Novel application of a generalized least-action framework to attention dynamics and the integration of brightness-invariance with curiosity within a Lagrangian is original.  
- Significance: Conceptually interesting but limited practical impact due to mixed empirical results and missing scanpath-level validation.  
- Pros for acceptance: Principled unified model, clear derivations, simple pipeline, and competitive results against classical methods on some benchmarks.  
- Cons against acceptance: Weak biological grounding, insufficient scanpath validation, mixed performance versus strong baselines, and limited sensitivity/ablation studies with overfitting risk.  
- Suggestions: Add detailed scanpath analyses and path-similarity metrics, perform ablations for each Lagrangian term and parameter sensitivity, compare to modern deep/dynamic models with scanpath metrics, connect to optimal-control/motor-planning literature, and expand implementation details and code release.  
- Overall: Thought-provoking, well-crafted theoretical model with clean implementation, but requires stronger empirical, scanpath-level, and biological validation to substantiate its central claims.
