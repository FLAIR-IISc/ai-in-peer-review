This paper introduces a model of visual attention for dynamic scenes, named EYMOL (EYe MOvement Laws). The central idea is that scanpaths can be described by variational laws, analogous to the Principle of Least Action in physics. The authors formulate a Lagrangian that includes kinetic energy and a generalized potential energy. This potential energy is composed of three main terms: (1) a harmonic oscillator to constrain the scanpath within the image boundaries, (2) a "curiosity" term that attracts attention to regions with high detail (image gradients), and (3) a "brightness invariance" term, inspired by optical flow constraints, to encourage fixation and tracking. By finding the stationary point of the action integral, the authors derive the Euler-Lagrange equations of motion that govern the scanpath. The model's parameters are estimated using simulated annealing. While the model's primary output is a scanpath trajectory, the authors generate saliency maps by aggregating fixation locations over multiple runs and compare their performance against state-of-the-art models on static image and dynamic video datasets.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The core conceptual contribution of the paper is novel and elegant. Framing the problem of scanpath generation through the lens of a generalized Least Action Principle is an interesting and principled approach. The decomposition of the potential function into terms representing physical constraints (retina boundaries), curiosity (attraction to detail), and stability (brightness invariance for fixation/tracking) provides an intuitive and interpretable framework.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that detract from its overall contribution:

1.  **Motivation and Related Work:** The principles driving the model (e.g., "curiosity" and "brightness invariance") are postulated in a somewhat ad-hoc manner. While intuitive, they lack strong grounding in the cognitive science or neuroscience literature. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion motivating why these specific principles, out of many possibilities, were chosen to model early, bottom-up attention. Furthermore, the paper misses an opportunity to connect its theoretical framework to existing research on motor control and eye movements that also employs principles of optimality. For instance, the work of Harris & Wolpert on optimal control models of saccades, or related research that models eye movements as minimizing some form of cost or uncertainty, would be highly relevant context. Citing and discussing this literature would better situate the current work and clarify its novel contributions (e.g., Harris, C. M., & Wolpert, D. M. (1998). Signal-dependent noise determines motor planning. *Nature*).

2.  **Empirical Performance:** The model's performance on standard saliency prediction benchmarks is not competitive with current state-of-the-art methods, particularly deep learning-based models (e.g., [18], [24] in their own tables). While the authors argue that saliency prediction is a byproduct of their scanpath model, the presented results in Tables 2, 3, and 4 show a significant performance gap. This raises questions about the model's practical significance for the task of saliency prediction.

3.  **Mismatch between Claim and Evaluation:** The most significant weakness is the disconnect between the paper's central claim—modeling the *dynamics* of eye movements—and its empirical validation. The evaluation is almost entirely focused on static saliency maps, which are an aggregate, time-collapsed representation of these dynamics. The paper would be much more convincing if it included analysis comparing the statistics of the generated scanpaths (e.g., saccade amplitude distributions, fixation durations, velocity profiles) with those of human eye movements. Without this analysis, the central claim of modeling scanpath dynamics remains largely unsupported. A visual comparison of a few example scanpaths from the model versus human data would also be very informative.

**Arguments for and against acceptance**

**Pro:**
*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically elegant framework for modeling eye movements based on physical principles, which is a departure from mainstream data-driven approaches.

**Con:**
*   The core components of the model ("curiosity," "brightness invariance") are weakly motivated and lack sufficient connection to relevant literature in motor control and cognitive science.
*   The empirical performance on standard saliency benchmarks is poor compared to state-of-the-art methods.
*   Most importantly, the evaluation does not validate the main contribution of the paper. The analysis is limited to static saliency maps and fails to assess the model's primary output: the scanpath dynamics.
