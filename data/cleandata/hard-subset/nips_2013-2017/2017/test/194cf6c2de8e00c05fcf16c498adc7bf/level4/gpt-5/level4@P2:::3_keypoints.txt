- Proposes a dynamical model where scanpaths minimize a generalized action; Lagrangian has bounding, curiosity (local/peripheral), and brightness-invariance terms.
- Euler–Lagrange equations govern gaze dynamics; parameters fit by simulated annealing on CAT2000; saliency maps from aggregated visitation counts.
- Evaluates exact and approximate brightness-invariance variants on static (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and dynamic (SFU) benchmarks; competitive and sometimes top-performing; runs in real time.
- Contrasts with classic saliency-map and CNN predictors and relates to WTA+IOR scanpath models; novelty is deriving scanpaths from a variational principle tied to optical-flow constraints.
- Theoretical formulation is coherent but appendix is dense; reviewer couldn’t verify all derivations though high-level rationale is consistent.
- Empirics use standard datasets/metrics and show competitive results, but not uniformly state-of-the-art; approximation performs adequately for static images.
- Method uses grayscale input, Gaussian peripheral blur, explicit center bias tuned by grid search, and simulated annealing for parameter optimization.
- Clarity concerns: need more intuition for alternating local/peripheral modulation and the choice/sensitivity of ω.
- Missing methodological details: specify simulated annealing parameters (domain, acceptance rule, α) and sensitivity to initialization.
- Fairness question: clarify whether blur/center-bias tuning was applied equally to baseline methods.
- Originality: novel framing of attention as stationary action, unifies saccades/fixation/tracking, and elegantly connects to optical-flow constraints.
- Significance: principled, simple, extensible alternative to map-based saliency with promising empirical performance, but limited by lack of top-down/task modeling.
- Strengths: conceptually fresh variational approach, unified gaze behaviors, competitive multi-benchmark results, and saliency maps emerge naturally.
- Weaknesses: reliance on tuned center bias and heuristics, limited ablations, dense math reducing accessibility, and mixed benchmark dominance.
- Key suggested experiments: ablation studies (remove brightness, curiosity, peripheral modulation), parameter sensitivity analyses (ηb, ηp, λ, m, ω), and scanpath-level comparisons (MultiMatch, string-edit).
- Suggested extensions: evaluate color/high-level features and integrate top-down/task influences into the variational framework.
- Reproducibility requests: clarify brightness-term role in fixation vs. tracking, provide runtime details, and release code.
- Pros for acceptance: principled and novel formulation, unified and fast model, potentially influential for scanpath/attention research.
- Cons against acceptance: insufficient ablations/fairness clarifications, dense presentation, and only parity (not dominance) with strong deep-learning baselines.
- Overall: creative and well-motivated contribution, borderline for acceptance; expert vision/attention reviewers recommended.
