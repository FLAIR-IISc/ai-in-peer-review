- Paper proposes an energy-minimization model of human visual attention in static and dynamic scenes based on three principles.
- Principle 1: Eye movements modeled as a harmonic oscillator constrained by image boundaries and finite retinal size.
- Principle 2: Curiosity-driven attention favors regions with large, multi-scale brightness changes.
- Principle 3: Brightness invariance introduces a cost that increases with brightness changes.
- The model yields differential equations predicting scanpaths given a start location and initial velocity.
- Quantitative evaluation on static and dynamic eye‑tracking datasets shows performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods.
- Strength: Provides a novel, formal bottom-up framework for saliency and eye-movement modeling, offering a different perspective from descriptive models.
- Strength: Competitive empirical performance validates the theoretical principles despite not beating SOTA on every metric.
- Weakness: Clarity and presentation are poor—writing is dense and some model details are ambiguous.
- Suggestion: Expand and define all abbreviations and explicitly define every variable and function in the mathematical formulation.
- Suggestion: Restructure content—move the two-page appendix to supplementary material and use freed space to clarify the main text.
- Weakness: Lacks connection to psychological literature on eye movements and optimal information-gathering models.
- Suggestion: Discuss relations to optimal-search theories (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2008) and clarify whether the principles restate or extend those models.
