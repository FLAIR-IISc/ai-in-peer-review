- Proposes a novel energy-minimizing model predicting human visual attention in static and dynamic scenes based on three formalized principles.

- Principle 1: harmonic oscillator constraint at image borders to model eye movement limits within a finite retina.

- Principle 2: "curiosity-driven" salience emphasizing regions with large brightness changes across high-resolution and blurred image versions.

- Principle 3: brightness invariance where salience increases with the magnitude of brightness change.

- From these principles, the authors derive differential equations that predict eye movement trajectories given initial position and velocity.

- Evaluation on standard static and dynamic fixation datasets yields performance comparable to state-of-the-art, though not uniformly top across metrics.

- The work addresses the important challenge of formalizing saliency and eye movement modeling, offering a fresh bottom-up attention perspective.

- Paper presentation is often opaque; mathematical derivations and claims are difficult to follow and verify.

- Authors must expand all abbreviations and explicitly define every variable and function, especially in Section 3 and the equations.

- Move the current two-page Appendix to supplementary material to free space for clearer mathematical exposition.

- Assuming familiarity with field-specific notation risks excluding NIPS’ interdisciplinary audience; rewrite for broader accessibility.

- Strengthen theoretical grounding by connecting to established psychological work on eye movements as optimal information gathering (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2008) and discuss how the model relates to or differs from that literature.

- Overall: conceptually novel and promising with competitive results, but requires significant revision for clarity and integration with cognitive science literature before publication.
