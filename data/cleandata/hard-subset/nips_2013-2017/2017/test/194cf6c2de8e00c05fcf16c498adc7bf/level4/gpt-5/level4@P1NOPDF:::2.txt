Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes

The authors investigate which locations in static and dynamic images attract human attention. They formulate a model by defining three basic principles for visual attention, operationalized as an energy function minimized by eye movements: (1) eye movements are constrained by a harmonic-oscillator term at the image borders within a limited-sized retina; (2) a curiosity-driven principle that highlights regions with large brightness changes in both a high-resolution and a blurred representation of the image; and (3) a brightness-invariance term whose contribution increases as a function of brightness changes. From a cost function combining these three components, the authors derive differential equations that predict scan paths over static or dynamic images, given a starting location and initial velocity. They evaluate the method quantitatively on datasets of static and dynamic scenes paired with human eye-tracking data, showing performance comparable to the state of the art.

Formalizing saliency and modeling eye movements are central problems in computational vision and cognitive science. Psychologists have long grappled with vague definitions of saliency. To my knowledge, this paper proposes a novel and innovative model that could help clarify what makes a location salient and offers a formal account of eye movements within the bottom-up tradition. Although it may not be state of the art on every metric across all datasets, the approach performs well and provides a refreshingly different perspective on the problem.

However, parts of my interpretation above are based on inference, as the paper is poorly written and difficult to follow. I strongly recommend thorough proofreading and clearer exposition. Please expand all abbreviations and symbols, both in the equations and in Section 3, and define every variable and function used. Even if certain notations are standard in your subfield, the NeurIPS audience is diverse, and many readers will struggle to follow the mathematics without explicit definitions. I also suggest moving the two-page Appendix to the supplementary material and using the freed space in the main text to provide these definitions and additional explanations of the modeling choices and their implications.

As a psychologist, I would also like to see your work connected more directly to the psychological literature that frames eye movements as (approximately) optimal steps for information gathering. For example: Najemnik, J., & Geisler, W. S. (2008). Eye movement statistics in humans are consistent with an optimal search strategy. Journal of Vision, 8(3), 1–14. There are additional relevant papers, especially from Geisler’s lab. It would be valuable to discuss how your variational formulation relates to these findings: for instance, whether it restates similar principles under different assumptions, or whether it captures complementary aspects that could be integrated with optimal-search models to yield a stronger hybrid account. A brief qualitative comparison—highlighting similarities, differences, and potential synergies—would significantly strengthen the paper’s positioning and impact.

In summary, this is a creative and promising contribution that brings a principled, dynamical perspective to saliency and scan-path prediction, with competitive empirical performance. Its main weakness is clarity of presentation. Improving the writing, fully defining the notation, and situating the work within the broader psychological literature would make the paper much more accessible and persuasive to the NeurIPS community.
