- Proposes a novel, physics-inspired model (EYMOL) that models scanpaths as particle trajectories via a variational Principle of Least Action.  
- Integrates kinetic energy, curiosity-driven potential, and brightness invariance into a Lagrangian to produce Euler–Lagrange dynamics for attention.  
- Unifies saccadic movements and object tracking within a single principled differential-equation framework.  
- Generates saliency maps by aggregating simulated trajectories and achieves competitive performance on MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU datasets.  
- Conceptual novelty and elegant theoretical derivation differentiate it from saliency-map and deep-learning approaches.  
- Well-written and clearly motivated, making core ideas accessible even to non-physics readers.  
- Main venue-fit concern: stronger alignment with computer vision or computational neuroscience (CVPR, VSS) than with NIPS due to limited connection to machine learning.  
- Mathematical accessibility issue: calculus-of-variations derivations may be hard for parts of the NIPS audience to verify or follow.  
- Scope limitation: model focuses on bottom-up early vision and omits top-down, task-dependent attentional influences.  
- Overall positive assessment: high-quality, original contribution with strong empirical results, but some reservations about audience fit.
