The paper proposes a novel approach to modeling visual attention based on variational principles, specifically a generalized Least Action Principle. The authors accurately summarize existing work in the field, noting the common reliance on saliency maps and optimization-based fixation selection, contrasting this with their trajectory-focused method. This model (EYMOL) formulates attention as the result of minimizing an action integral, incorporating terms for trajectory boundedness, curiosity driven by image detail, and brightness invariance. While a full assessment of the mathematical derivations—particularly those in the appendix—required expertise beyond my own in physics, I followed the core logic and appreciated the distinctiveness of the approach. The model is rigorously tested on established image and video datasets, with performance compared against several state-of-the-art methods. Although EYMOL does not universally outperform existing techniques, the results are competitive and suggest the validity of the proposed framework.

The paper's strengths lie in its theoretical novelty and its attempt to bridge principles from physics with computational models of visual attention. The concept of deriving scanpaths directly from a variational principle is a compelling alternative to saliency-map-based approaches. The experimental validation, utilizing multiple datasets and comparison with established models, adds credibility to the findings. The clarity of the writing is generally good, though the mathematical detail in the appendix might present a barrier to some readers.

Potential weaknesses include the reliance on parameters estimated via simulated annealing and the complexity of the resulting equations, which could limit real-time applicability. While the authors demonstrate competitive performance, a deeper analysis of *why* the model succeeds or fails in specific scenarios could further strengthen the work. Additionally, the connection to underlying neural mechanisms, while hinted at, could be more explicitly explored. It is also worth noting that the approximations made to simplify the equations (particularly related to brightness invariance) may warrant further investigation into their impact on overall performance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical framework grounding visual attention in variational principles.
*   Competitive performance on standard datasets demonstrates empirical validity.
*   Potential to inspire new research directions in both computer vision and neuroscience.
*   Well-written and clearly presented (with the exception of the appendix’s mathematical detail).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Mathematical complexity may limit accessibility and real-time application.
*   Limited discussion of the biological plausibility of the model's assumptions.
*   Performance, while competitive, does not represent a substantial breakthrough over existing methods.
*   The paper may be more appropriate for a vision-focused conference (e.g., VSS) than NIPS.

Overall, I believe this is a worthwhile contribution, but its suitability for NIPS is debatable. A decision should be made considering feedback from reviewers with more expertise in perception and related fields.
