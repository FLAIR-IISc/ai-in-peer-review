The paper "Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes" proposes a novel approach to modeling visual attention, deviating from the traditional saliency map-based methods. The authors provide a thorough review of the current state of the art in visual attention modeling, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches. They then introduce their new model, EYMOL, which is based on the principle of least action and defines visual attention as a trajectory of gaze.

The EYMOL model is founded on three fundamental principles: boundedness of the trajectory, curiosity-driven attention, and brightness invariance. These principles are mathematically formulated and combined to form a Lagrangian, which is then used to derive the Euler-Lagrange equations of motion. The model is applied to real-world datasets of eye movements, and its performance is compared to that of other state-of-the-art models.

The paper's strengths lie in its originality, clarity, and thoroughness. The authors provide a detailed explanation of their model, including the mathematical derivations, and demonstrate its effectiveness in capturing human visual attention. The results show that EYMOL performs competitively with other models, particularly in tasks involving dynamic scenes.

However, the paper's technical depth, particularly in the appendix, may be challenging for non-experts to follow. Additionally, while the model's performance is promising, it is not clear whether it provides a significant improvement over existing approaches.

In terms of quality, the paper is well-written, and the authors have made a clear effort to explain complex concepts in an accessible manner. The experimental setup and results are also well-presented. However, the paper's contribution to the field of machine learning, specifically NIPS, is not immediately apparent. The model's novelty and potential impact on perception sciences are undeniable, but its relevance to the broader NIPS community may be limited.
