The paper proposes a novel approach to modeling eye movements. The authors provide a sound summary of existing techniques, which, to the best of my understanding (though I admit to not being an expert in perception or eye movements), typically rely on saliency maps defined over images, with fixation points selected via optimization or maximization procedures. In contrast, this model (EYMOL) operates under a different assumption, defining gaze trajectories directly according to a “Least Action Principle.” While the mathematical details, particularly within the extensive appendix, were beyond my ability to fully evaluate due to a lack of background in the relevant physics, I was still able to grasp the core elements of the approach and its distinctions from prior work. The model is thoughtfully applied to a real-world eye-movement dataset and benchmarked against a variety of alternative models. Although it does not outperform others on every metric, the results are generally favorable and demonstrate the validity of the proposed approach. 

I believe this paper may be of particular interest to researchers in the vision science community (e.g., attendees of VSS). Its suitability as a contribution to NIPS is less clear, and I recommend that assessment be informed by the opinions of reviewers with more specialized expertise. However, the paper likely advances a new and interesting theory capable of inspiring further empirical research, and thus possesses both novelty and merit within the broader field of perception science.
