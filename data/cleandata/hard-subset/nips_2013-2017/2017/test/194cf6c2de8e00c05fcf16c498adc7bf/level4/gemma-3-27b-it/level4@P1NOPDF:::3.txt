The paper proposes a novel approach to modeling eye movements. The authors provide a sound summary of the current state of the art (to the best of my understanding, though I admit I am not an expert in perception or eye-movement research). Existing approaches generally rely on saliency maps defined over images, with fixation points selected through optimization or maximization techniques. This model, EYMOL, operates under a different assumption, defining gaze trajectories directly according to a “Least Action Principle.” 

While the mathematical details – particularly within the extensive appendix – were beyond my ability to fully evaluate due to a lack of background in the relevant physics, I was still able to grasp the key elements of the approach and its distinction from previous models. The application of the model to a real-world eye-movement dataset, and its comparison against several alternative models, is particularly helpful. Although EYMOL doesn’t outperform the alternatives across all metrics, the results are generally favorable and demonstrate the model’s validity.

I believe this paper may be of interest to researchers in the vision science community, such as attendees of the Vision Sciences Society (VSS) conference. I am less certain about its suitability as a strong contribution to NIPS, though I believe that assessment should be weighed against the opinions of reviewers with more specialized expertise. Nevertheless, I find that the paper likely advances a new and interesting theory that could stimulate further empirical research, suggesting both novelty and merit within the broader field of perception science.
