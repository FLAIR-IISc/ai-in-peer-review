This paper introduces EYMOL, a novel approach for modeling eye movements. The authors provide a competent summary of the current state-of-the-art, which typically involves constructing a saliency map over an image and selecting fixation points through an optimization or maximization process.

In contrast, the proposed model, EYMOL, operates on a fundamentally different premise. It is grounded in the "Least Action Principle," a concept from physics, and is defined directly on the trajectories of gaze rather than on a sequence of discrete points. While a detailed evaluation of the mathematical formalism, particularly the physics-based derivations in the appendix, is beyond the scope of my expertise, the paper's core concepts are presented with sufficient clarity. I was able to grasp the key elements of the approach and appreciate its departure from established models.

A significant strength of the paper is its application of the model to a real-world eye-movement dataset. The authors conduct a thorough comparison against a variety of alternative models. While EYMOL does not uniformly outperform all alternatives across every metric, the results are broadly favorable and effectively demonstrate the viability and merit of this new theoretical perspective.

The primary contribution of this work is the introduction of a new and interesting theory for eye-movement analysis. Its novelty has the potential to inspire further empirical research and open new lines of inquiry within the perception sciences. The paper is well-argued, and its empirical validation lends strong support to its central claims.

My main reservation concerns the paper's fit for the NIPS conference. The work seems highly relevant to the vision science community (e.g., attendees of the Vision Sciences Society conference). However, its contribution to the core machine learning community may be less direct. I believe this is a point that should be carefully weighed against the opinions of reviewers with more specialized expertise in this area.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and well-supported theory for modeling eye movements that challenges the dominant paradigm in the field. Despite my questions about its alignment with the NIPS audience, the work possesses clear novelty and scientific merit. It advances an intriguing new framework that warrants attention from the broader scientific community.
