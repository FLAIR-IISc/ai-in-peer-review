Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes

The authors investigate human attention patterns in both static and dynamic images. They present a model grounded in three principles of visual attention – formulated as an energy function to be minimized by eye movements – namely: (1) constraints on eye movements modeled as a harmonic oscillator at image boundaries within a limited retinal area; (2) a “curiosity” principle that emphasizes regions exhibiting significant brightness changes at multiple scales; and (3) a brightness invariance principle that increases with changes in illumination. From these principles, they derive differential equations to predict eye movements across static and dynamic scenes, contingent on initial location and velocity. The model’s performance is quantitatively evaluated against human eye movement data on static and dynamic datasets, demonstrating results comparable to state-of-the-art methods.

Modeling saliency and eye movements remains a crucial challenge in computational vision and cognitive science. Existing definitions of saliency often lack precision, and the authors offer a novel and, to my knowledge, innovative model that could contribute to a more rigorous understanding of salience and provide a formal framework for modeling eye movements within a bottom-up processing paradigm. While the method does not achieve state-of-the-art performance on every metric and dataset, it performs favorably and offers a distinct perspective on the problem.

However, the paper’s clarity is hindered by its writing style, making it difficult to follow. I recommend thorough proofreading and expanded definitions of abbreviations—both within the equations and in Section 3. The two-page Appendix should be moved to the supplementary material, and the resulting space used to explicitly define all variables and functions, even if conventionally understood within the field. The NIPS audience is highly interdisciplinary, and greater mathematical clarity is essential for broader accessibility.

From a psychological perspective, it would strengthen the work to connect it to the literature on eye movements as an optimal information-gathering strategy. For example, Najemnik & Geisler (2008, *Journal of Vision*, 8(3), 1-14) demonstrates consistency between human eye movement statistics and optimal search strategies. Further relevant work can be found, particularly from Geisler’s lab. I suggest a discussion of how the authors’ approach relates to these findings – whether it represents a restatement of existing models or provides complementary information that could be integrated for improved performance.
