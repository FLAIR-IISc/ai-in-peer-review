Summary
This paper proposes a computational model of visual attention that combines a bottom-up saliency map with dynamical systems to generate scanpaths. The key idea is to define several “affordances” (e.g., curiosity, brightness invariance) and to derive eye-movement trajectories via a Least Action Principle (LAP). The goal is to move beyond static saliency toward biologically plausible gaze dynamics.

Strengths
- Ambitious and timely attempt to model temporal scanpath dynamics rather than only static saliency.
- Clear mechanistic framing: affordances are encoded as terms in an energy or cost functional, and LAP provides a principled way to derive motion.
- Interpretability: the contributions of different affordances are, in principle, separable and testable.
- If validated, the approach could bridge optimization-based accounts of behavior with saliency-driven attention.

Main concerns
1) Biological grounding and related work. The current set of affordances feels ad hoc and insufficiently motivated by prior eye-movement or motor-control literature. The LAP framing invites a comparison to optimal control models of saccades and signal-dependent noise (e.g., work from the Wolpert lab and others on minimum-variance/effort control, main-sequence constraints, and LQG-like treatments). Without this connection, the biological plausibility claim remains weak. Likewise, “curiosity” is introduced with little reference to related constructs such as Bayesian surprise, novelty, or intrinsic motivation; similarly, “brightness invariance” is not tied to known photoreceptor adaptation or Weber–Fechner principles.

2) Empirical validation and baselines. Relative to modern deep saliency models, the reported performance appears mixed to poor. Given that many strong baselines exist for static fixation prediction, the manuscript should more carefully position its contributions: if the primary claim is dynamic plausibility, then evaluation must foreground temporal/sequence metrics, not only static saliency metrics.

3) Missing analysis of scanpath dynamics. For a model whose central contribution is trajectory generation, the paper lacks convincing analyses and visualizations of the resulting scanpaths. Beyond aggregate fixation prediction metrics, readers should see:
- Representative scanpath overlays on images.
- Distributions of saccade amplitudes and directions, fixation durations, inter-saccadic intervals.
- The saccadic main sequence (amplitude–peak velocity relationship) and landing distributions relative to salient objects.
- Sequence similarity metrics (e.g., MultiMatch, ScanMatch, string-edit distance) versus human scanpaths.
- Temporal evolution of saliency sampling (e.g., entropy or coverage over time).

4) Ablations and sensitivity. The manuscript should quantify the contribution of each affordance and the LAP formulation:
- Remove or vary the weight of curiosity and brightness invariance terms.
- Compare LAP-derived dynamics to simpler alternatives (e.g., gradient ascent on saliency with inhibition of return, random-walk with IOR, reinforcement-learning baselines).
- Report sensitivity to hyperparameters, noise, and integration step sizes, and show robustness across datasets.

5) Clarity of the mathematical formulation. The dynamical system and its derivation require more detail:
- What is the exact Lagrangian: state variables, potential terms from saliency/affordances, kinetic term, and any dissipation?
- How are stochasticity and signal-dependent noise handled?
- What are the boundary conditions and constraints (oculomotor range, fixation stability, refractory periods)?
- How is inhibition of return modeled, if at all?
- How are brightness invariance and curiosity operationalized at the feature level?

Suggestions for improvement
- Situate the approach within established motor-control and eye-movement frameworks (e.g., minimum-variance/effort, optimal feedback control, LATER/accumulator models, SceneWalk/CRISP for scanpaths). Explain how LAP relates to these and what unique predictions it makes.
- Relate “curiosity” to prior computational definitions (e.g., Bayesian surprise, information gain, intrinsic motivation) and justify the chosen formulation. For brightness invariance, connect to known luminance/contrast adaptation mechanisms and show that the term improves generalization across luminance conditions.
- Expand evaluation to include robust static metrics (AUC-Judd, sAUC, NSS, CC, KL) and dynamic metrics (MultiMatch, ScanMatch), with appropriate statistical testing. Include cross-dataset tests to assess generalization.
- Provide qualitative figures of generated scanpaths, with side-by-side human and baseline model comparisons, and report key distributions and the main sequence.
- Add comprehensive ablations and sanity checks as outlined above. A “no-LAP” or “no-affordance” variant would help isolate where the performance comes from.
- Ensure fair baseline comparisons with strong deep models current to the field (e.g., DeepGaze variants, SALICON, ML-Net), and clarify training/testing protocols to avoid leakage.
- Discuss computational cost and stability: can the dynamics be integrated in real time? How often do trajectories exhibit unrealistic oscillations or dead-ends, and how are these prevented?

Minor comments
- Clarify terminology: “affordances” is loaded; if used in a Gibsonian sense, motivate; otherwise consider “priors” or “regularizers.”
- Improve notation consistency and define all variables when first introduced.
- Several typographical errors and grammatical issues should be addressed for readability.

Overall assessment
The paper proposes an appealing, principled route to modeling scanpath dynamics via a Least Action formulation with interpretable affordance terms. However, in its current form the work is under-motivated biologically and empirically under-validated, especially relative to strong static baselines and existing dynamic models. With deeper connections to motor-control literature, stronger dynamic evaluations and visualizations, and thorough ablations, this could become a compelling contribution. At present, the evidence does not yet support the central claims.
