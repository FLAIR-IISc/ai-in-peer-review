Summary
This paper proposes a principled, bottom-up model of visual attention for static and dynamic scenes based on a variational formulation. The authors posit three driving principles encoded in a Lagrangian: (i) boundedness of eye trajectories via a harmonic potential at image borders; (ii) a curiosity term that alternates between local and peripheral detail, implemented as gradient energy of the original and blurred images with a time-varying modulation; and (iii) a brightness-invariance term (akin to the optical-flow constraint) that encourages trajectories consistent with fixation and motion tracking. Minimizing the generalized action yields Euler–Lagrange equations governing scanpaths. Parameters for the curiosity weights and brightness-invariance weight are tuned by simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000. Saliency maps are then obtained as a byproduct by counting trajectory visits over multiple runs with randomized initial conditions. The model is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000 (images) and SFU (videos), showing competitive performance with several established baselines.

Relation to prior work
Most saliency models compute a centralized saliency map (e.g., Itti & Koch; information maximization; Bayesian surprise; learned models including CNNs) and then derive scanpaths via winner-take-all with heuristics such as inhibition of return. In contrast, this paper derives the scanpath itself from a least-action principle; the saliency map emerges from trajectory statistics. The brightness-invariance term connects to classical optical-flow constraints. The work would benefit from a clearer comparison to optimal eye-movement models in vision science (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler, 2008, and related work from Geisler’s group), which frame gaze as an optimal information-gathering process.

Quality
- Theory: The variational derivation is soundly presented, with an exact and an approximate formulation of the brightness-invariance term. For static images, the approximation appears justified empirically (V1 vs. V2 yield similar results).
- Algorithms: Numerical integration is standard, and stability constraints on λ are discussed. Parameter search via simulated annealing is reasonable, but details of the acceptance function and neighborhood definition should be clarified for reproducibility.
- Experiments: The evaluation on multiple datasets and metrics is appropriate. However, several design choices (center bias, blur tuning, initialization near center) can materially affect scores; it would help to report ablations and sensitivity analyses (e.g., without center bias; different initializations; varying ηb, ηp, λ).
- Reproducibility: Many symbols and preprocessing steps are not fully defined in the main text (e.g., exact meanings of bt, bx, px, NSS; construction of the blurred peripheral map; boundary conditions; initial velocity distribution; integration step sizes and tolerances). Please define all variables and provide enough detail for an expert reader to reproduce results.

Clarity
The paper is difficult to follow in places due to notation and editorial issues. I strongly recommend careful proofreading, expanding abbreviations on first use, and unifying notation across sections and equations. Consider moving the 2-page Appendix to the supplement and using the space to define all variables, functions, and parameters in the main text. Section 3 should expand all metric names and abbreviations, and describe the simulated annealing schedule, acceptance probability, temperature decay, domain D, and stopping criteria. Please also clarify how the “center bias” and blur are tuned and applied to ensure fairness across comparisons.

Originality
Framing attention dynamics as Euler–Lagrange equations under a generalized action that unifies curiosity and brightness invariance is a novel and elegant contribution within the bottom-up paradigm. The idea that saliency is a byproduct of scanpath dynamics provides a fresh perspective relative to conventional saliency-map-first pipelines.

Significance
The approach offers a conceptually unified model that can produce scanpaths in real time and naturally handle both saccades and tracking. While not uniformly state-of-the-art across all benchmarks and metrics, the performance is competitive and the framework could inspire further work integrating bottom-up physics-inspired dynamics with learned features or top-down goals.

Suggestions for improvement
- Connect to optimal search literature: Discuss how your least-action formulation relates to normative models of gaze as optimal information gathering (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler, 2008). Are the derived trajectories consistent with their reported statistics? Could your curiosity term be interpreted as an implicit utility or information gain?
- Reproducibility: Define all symbols in a dedicated table; specify preprocessing (grayscale conversion, blurring kernel parameters), initial conditions, integration details, runtime, and stability constraints. Describe the acceptance probability P(·) used in simulated annealing and the neighborhood proposal.
- Analysis: Provide ablations for the curiosity subterms (local vs. peripheral), brightness-invariance weight, center bias, and initialization strategy. Report sensitivity to parameter choices and to the blur/center-bias grid search.
- Fair comparison: Clarify whether competing baselines also include center bias and post-hoc blur; if not, include variants without these enhancements to assess intrinsic model performance.
- Broader scope: Briefly discuss limitations (absence of top-down/task effects) and possible extensions (e.g., integrating learned feature hierarchies or task priors within the same variational framework).

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually novel variational formulation with clear links to physical principles.
- Unified treatment of saccades and tracking; saliency arises from scanpath dynamics.
- Competitive empirical performance across several datasets and metrics.
- Potentially fast and interpretable model with few parameters.

Cons for acceptance
- Clarity and notation issues hinder accessibility and reproducibility.
- Limited analysis of design choices (center bias, initialization) and parameter sensitivity.
- Comparison to relevant psychological/normative models is missing.
- Performance is not consistently state-of-the-art, especially relative to modern deep models.

Overall, this is an original and promising contribution that would be strengthened by improved exposition, clearer empirical analyses, and a deeper connection to the vision science literature on optimal eye movements.
