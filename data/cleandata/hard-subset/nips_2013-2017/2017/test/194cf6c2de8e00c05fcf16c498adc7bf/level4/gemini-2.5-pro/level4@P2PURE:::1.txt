This computational neuroscience paper proposes a bottom-up visual saliency model incorporating a dynamical systems component to capture the dynamics of scanpaths. I enjoyed reading the paper, which presents several interesting ideas, notably the concepts of "affordances" (e.g., curiosity, brightness invariance) that drive the scanpath and the application of the Least Action Principle.

However, a weakness is that these "affordances" are postulated in a somewhat ad hoc manner without sufficient motivation. Furthermore, the discussion of the Least Action Principle would have been strengthened by connecting its derivation to existing work in eye movement research, such as the past and recent contributions from the Wolpert lab.

While a lack of direct biological motivation is not necessarily a critical flaw in the current landscape of high-performing deep learning models for static visual saliency, a significant issue with the proposed model is its poor performance relative to these state-of-the-art methods. Finally, given the model's central claim of capturing biological eye movement dynamics, the evaluation is insufficient. It would have been important to include figures in the main paper that characterize the generated scanpaths dynamically, rather than relying solely on static metrics of overall correspondence with human fixation data.
