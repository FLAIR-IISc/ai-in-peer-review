

Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes  
The authors investigate human attention patterns in static and dynamic images by deriving a novel model based on three foundational principles for visual attention (formulated as an energy function to be minimized during eye movements): (1) Eye movements are constrained by a harmonic oscillator near image borders within a limited retina size; (2) a "curiosity-driven principle" prioritizing regions with significant brightness changes in both high-resolution and low-resolution image representations; and (3) brightness invariance, which increases with brightness changes. From these principles, the authors derive a cost function yielding differential equations to predict eye movements across static or dynamic scenes (depending on initial position and velocity). Quantitative evaluation on datasets pairing static/dynamic scenes with human eye-tracking data shows performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods.  

Formal definitions of saliency and eye movement modeling are critical in computational vision and cognitive science, where psychologists have long struggled with vague saliency definitions. The authors propose a novel and innovative model (to the best of my knowledge) that advances formal understanding of saliency and bottom-up eye movement dynamics. While not consistently state-of-the-art across all metrics and datasets, the method performs robustly and offers a refreshingly distinct perspective on the problem.  

Unfortunately, the paper’s poor writing and lack of clarity hinder comprehension. The authors should have others proofread the manuscript and expand all abbreviations (including those in equations and Section 3). I strongly recommend moving the two-page Appendix to supplementary material and using those pages to rigorously define every variable and function—this is essential for the NIPS audience, which spans diverse disciplines and may lack familiarity with field-specific conventions.  

As a psychologist, I recommend explicitly connecting this work to key psychological literature on eye movements as optimal information-gathering steps. For instance, Najemnik & Geisler (2008) demonstrated that human eye movement statistics align with optimal search strategies (Journal of Vision, 8(3), 1–14). The authors should discuss how their model relates to such findings—whether it restates similar principles or offers complementary insights that could integrate into a more comprehensive framework.
