- Paper models human eye movements by minimizing an energy function built from three principles: harmonic boundary constraints, curiosity (multi-scale brightness changes), and brightness invariance.
- Authors derive differential equations from the cost function to predict eye trajectories on static and dynamic images.
- Method is evaluated quantitatively on human eye-tracking datasets and performs comparably to state-of-the-art saliency models.
- Variational/least-action formulation grounding attention in physical principles is a novel and significant contribution.
- The approach offers a fresh, bottom-up perspective with potential to influence future research in visual attention.
- Writing is dense and difficult to follow, hindering comprehension of technical details.
- Several abbreviations and variables are undefined (especially in equations and Section 3), reducing accessibility for a multidisciplinary audience.
- The 2-page Appendix should be moved to supplementary material and its space used to explicitly define all variables and functions.
- The paper lacks sufficient engagement with psychological literature on optimal eye-movement strategies (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2008).
- A comparative discussion with existing psychological models of eye movements is needed to strengthen contextualization and impact.
- Recommend thorough proofreading and clearer presentation to make the work accessible to the broader NIPS community.
- Overall assessment: technically sound and promising; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing clarity, definitions, and literature connections.
