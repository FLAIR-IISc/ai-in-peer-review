- Introduces EYMOL, a variational model that predicts scanpaths by framing eye movements as the stationary point of a least-action functional, modeling trajectories directly instead of using saliency-map heuristics.  
- Lagrangian combines kinetic energy, an image-bound potential, a “curiosity” term favoring strong local gradients/peripheral features, and a brightness-invariance term; Euler–Lagrange equations derived (exact and approximate forms).  
- Parameters are estimated via simulated annealing; trajectories are evaluated indirectly by aggregating visits into saliency maps and compared to established models.  
- Empirical results are competitive and generally favorable but not uniformly superior across all metrics.  
- Reviewer is not a perception specialist and found parts of the mathematical appendix beyond their expertise, requesting specialist verification of derivations.  
- Main ideas and body derivations are understandable and modeling assumptions are clearly stated.  
- Empirical evaluation is reasonable with multiple baselines and metrics that support the model’s viability.  
- Paper is generally well written, though the physics-based formalism may be difficult for some readers.  
- Presentation of exact vs. approximate brightness-invariance variants and their comparable performance on static images is useful.  
- The approach is novel relative to dominant saliency-map paradigms and offers a fresh formulation for scanpath modeling.  
- Theoretical framework could influence scanpath/attention modeling and is likely to interest vision science communities, but broader NIPS impact is less certain.  
- Strengths: clear conceptual departure, principled integration of curiosity and brightness invariance, competitive empirical results, sensible baseline comparisons.  
- Weaknesses: heavy mathematical derivations needing expert verification, inconsistent outperforming of alternatives, and potentially limited appeal to a general NIPS audience.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel principled framework, potential to stimulate follow-up work, and clear complementarity to saliency methods.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited accessibility and verifiability for a broad audience, empirical gains not decisively superior, and uncertain broader impact.  
- Overall: interesting and original with encouraging results; recommends additional expert review of technical derivations and consideration of broader impact for NIPS suitability.
