This paper proposes a new approach to studying eye movements. The authors, to my understanding, correctly summarize the current state of the art (though I am not an expert in perception or eye movements). Broadly, existing approaches rely on saliency maps defined over images, with fixation points selected via optimization—typically by maximizing a saliency function. In contrast, the proposed model (EYMOL) is built on a different assumption: it operates directly on gaze trajectories and is derived from a “Least Action Principle.”

Some of the mathematical details—especially in the extensive appendix—were beyond my ability to fully evaluate due to my limited physics background. Nevertheless, I was able to grasp the key elements of the approach and how it departs from prior models. The paper applies the model to a real eye-movement dataset and compares it against a range of alternative models. While the proposed method does not dominate on every metric, the results are generally favorable and support the approach’s validity.

I believe the paper will interest segments of the vision science community (e.g., VSS attendees). I am less certain it represents a strong contribution for NIPS; however, this judgment should be weighed against the opinions of reviewers with deeper expertise in the relevant areas. Overall, the work likely advances a novel and interesting theoretical framework that could stimulate further empirical research, and thus it offers both novelty and merit for the perception sciences.
