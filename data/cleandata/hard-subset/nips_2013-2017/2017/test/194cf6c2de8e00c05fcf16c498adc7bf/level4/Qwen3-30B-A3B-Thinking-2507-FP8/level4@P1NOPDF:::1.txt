

This computational neuroscience paper proposes a bottom-up visual feature saliency model enhanced by dynamical systems modeling to capture scanpath dynamics. The authors present several compelling ideas, particularly in defining "affordances" (curiosity, brightness invariance) that drive scanpaths and the application of the Least Action Principle. However, these affordances are somewhat ad hoc and lack deeper theoretical motivation.  

The derivation of the Least Action Principle would have benefited from explicit connections to established work in eye movement research, such as the foundational and recent studies from the Wolpert lab. While the absence of biological motivation is less critical in the current deep learning era—where benchmark-driven static saliency models dominate—the paper’s primary weakness lies in its inconsistent performance relative to state-of-the-art methods. The model claims to capture biological eye movement dynamics yet achieves subpar results compared to contemporary benchmarks. This discrepancy undermines its core contribution, as the paper fails to demonstrate how the proposed approach quantitatively improves upon existing models in capturing real-world scanpath behavior.  

Crucially, the main text omits visual evidence of the generated scanpaths. Readers are not provided with actual figures characterizing the model’s scanpaths beyond static performance metrics. Without such illustrations—showing trajectory patterns, fixation distributions, or temporal dynamics—it remains unclear how the model’s dynamics align with biological observations. For instance, how do the predicted scanpaths compare to human eye movements in terms of sequence complexity, saccade amplitude distributions, or temporal consistency? The supplementary materials might address this, but the main paper should include these critical visualizations to substantiate the biological plausibility claim.  

The paper’s strongest elements are its conceptual framing of affordances and the Least Action Principle as a unifying mechanism. Yet, without rigorous validation against biological data or clearer justification for the affordances’ design, these ideas remain speculative. The current performance metrics (e.g., AUC, F-measure) are insufficient to support the model’s biological relevance, especially when contrasted with purely data-driven approaches that achieve higher accuracy on static saliency benchmarks. The authors should explicitly address this gap: *why* their dynamics-focused approach justifies lower static performance, and how it advances beyond existing methods in capturing the temporal aspects of eye movements.  

In summary, the paper offers an interesting theoretical perspective but falls short in empirical validation. To strengthen its contribution, the authors should: (1) clarify the motivation for each affordance, (2) directly compare scanpath dynamics with human data via visualizations in the main text, and (3) contextualize performance results within the broader landscape of dynamic eye movement models. Without these revisions, the work risks being perceived as a theoretical curiosity rather than a meaningful advance in modeling biological visual attention.
