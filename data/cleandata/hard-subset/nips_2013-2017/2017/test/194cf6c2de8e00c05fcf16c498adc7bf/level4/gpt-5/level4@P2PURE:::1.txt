This computational neuroscience paper proposes a bottom-up visual-feature saliency model, augmented with dynamical-systems modeling, to capture the dynamics of scanpaths. I enjoyed reading it; it presents several appealing ideas, including the definition of “affordances” (curiosity, brightness invariance) that drive scan paths, as well as the use of a Least Action Principle. However, these affordances seem somewhat ad hoc and are not further justified.

In the context of the Least Action Principle and its derivation here, it would have been helpful to relate it to established eye-movement research, such as past and recent work from the Wolpert lab. While the limited biological motivation would be a minor concern on its own—especially in an era of benchmark-dominant deep learning models for static visual salience—the more serious issue is the model’s performance, which appears mixed and overall poor relative to those approaches. Given the claim to capture biological eye-movement dynamics, it would also have been useful to include, in the main paper, figures that characterize the generated scan paths themselves, not only aggregate statistics for matching eye movements.
