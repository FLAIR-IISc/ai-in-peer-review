Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a physics-inspired, bottom-up model of visual attention in which scanpaths arise as trajectories that extremize a generalized action. The Lagrangian includes: (i) a boundary potential that confines trajectories to the retinal/image extent; (ii) a “curiosity” term that favors regions with strong local gradients and peripheral structure, implemented by alternating weights on the gradient magnitude of the original and a blurred image via cos^2(ωt)/sin^2(ωt); and (iii) a brightness-invariance term derived from the optical flow constraint (bt + ∇b · ẋ = 0) to encourage fixation and target tracking. The resulting Euler-Lagrange equations (EYMOL) are second-order ODEs governing the focus of attention. Parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) are tuned by simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a subset of CAT2000. Saliency maps are produced as a byproduct by counting visits over many simulated trajectories with randomized initial conditions; a center bias and output blur are tuned via grid search. Experiments on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video suggest competitive performance with classical saliency baselines and mixed results relative to modern deep models.

This work builds on classic saliency models (Itti & Koch; Bruce & Tsotsos; Judd et al.), saccade generation work (e.g., Le Meur & Liu), optical flow–based constraints (Horn & Schunck), and, in spirit, optimal control perspectives in motor neuroscience. The novelty is to recast attention dynamics under a unified variational principle with interpretable “energies” for saccades, fixation, and tracking.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: A clear variational formulation that ties together exploration (curiosity) and stabilization (brightness invariance) in a single dynamical system that produces scanpaths directly.
- Derivation and transparency: Detailed Euler-Lagrange derivations and a discussion of well-posedness constraints (e.g., bounds on λ) are helpful.
- Generality: The same framework handles static and dynamic scenes and yields scanpaths without hand-coded winner-take-all, inhibition-of-return, or explicit saliency maps.
- Empirical scope: Evaluations cover multiple image and video datasets and standard saliency metrics; an approximate vs exact brightness-invariance comparison is informative.

Weaknesses
- Mixed empirical performance: Relative to state-of-the-art CNN-based saliency models (e.g., recent MIT300 leaderboard methods), the reported results appear generally weaker or, at best, competitive with older baselines. The paper would benefit from clearer, up-to-date comparisons to deep models and human inter-observer upper bounds.
- Heuristics and potential confounds: The added center bias and output blur are tuned to maximize benchmark metrics, which can inflate AUC/NSS and obscure the contribution of the core dynamics. Saliency maps also depend on many runs initialized near image center. Reporting with and without these components (and using sAUC) is important for fair assessment.
- Limited scanpath analysis: Despite being a scanpath generator, the main text focuses on saliency maps. There is no quantitative analysis of saccade amplitude distributions, inter-fixation distances, fixation durations, or scanpath similarity to humans (e.g., MultiMatch, ScanMatch). Claims about tracking are qualitative.
- Ad hoc modeling choices: The cos^2/sin^2 alternation between local and peripheral gradients, the specific form and scaling of the kinetic energy, and parameter scheduling (ω, m, k) are not sufficiently motivated or ablated. The acceptance probability P(·) and neighborhood definition in simulated annealing are unspecified; the parameter domain D is only vaguely bounded.
- Reproducibility and clarity: Important details are missing (ODE solver settings, time step, runtime; precise initial condition distributions; choice of mass m; units; exact value of ω). Several typographical issues and notational inconsistencies remain (e.g., kinetic energy as 1/m · ||ẋ||^2 instead of the standard ½ m ||ẋ||^2; “eggs-chicken,” “psedo-code,” stray “st Conference...” line).
- Biological grounding: The model’s biological interpretation (e.g., relation to optimal control/feedback control frameworks in motor neuroscience, Wolpert/Todorov) is only loosely discussed. If the claim is partly biological plausibility, closer engagement with that literature would strengthen the framing.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a coherent variational setup and reasonable numerical strategy. However, the strength of the empirical support is limited by reliance on tuned center bias/blur, small-scale parameter search on 100 images, and lack of scanpath-level analyses and ablations. Proofreading and clarification of modeling choices are needed.

Clarity
The paper is readable but hampered by typos and missing definitions. The appendix is useful but could be condensed with clearer notation and a cleaner derivation. A figure illustrating the trajectory dynamics under different term weights would help.

Originality
The explicit least-action formulation for attention dynamics and the brightness-invariance coupling in a single Lagrangian are novel and interesting, although related ideas (energy-based active vision; dynamical saccade models) exist.

Significance
Conceptually promising, especially for unifying saccades and tracking in a principled way. Practical impact on saliency prediction is limited unless empirical performance and scanpath fidelity improve and are demonstrated against strong baselines.

Specific suggestions
- Report results with and without center bias and output blur; add sAUC and shuffled-NSS to reduce center-bias confounds; include human inter-observer scores.
- Provide scanpath analyses: distributions of saccade amplitudes, fixation durations, inter-fixation distances; temporal saliency metrics; scanpath similarity (MultiMatch/ScanMatch); first-fixation performance.
- Add ablations: no brightness invariance; no curiosity term; fixed vs alternating local/peripheral weighting; sensitivity to ω, λ, m, k; number and placement of initial conditions.
- Clarify modeling choices: justify kinetic energy form and units; specify ω and its effect; detail SA (P(·), neighbor definition, bounds and priors); ODE solver, time step, runtime.
- Reduce grammatical errors and standardize notation; define Iverson brackets on first use; fix stray lines and typos.
- Include broader related work and discussion linking to optimal control/feedback control approaches in eye movement research.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, unified dynamical formulation with interpretable components.
- Generates scanpaths directly and extends naturally to dynamic scenes.
- Clear mathematical derivations; potentially useful for mechanistic modeling.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical performance appears below modern deep baselines; reliance on center bias/blur undermines claims.
- Missing scanpath-level validation, ablations, and sensitivity analyses.
- Several ad hoc choices insufficiently justified; clarity and reproducibility issues.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical framework is appealing and could be impactful with stronger empirical validation, clearer justification of modeling choices, and thorough scanpath analyses. I encourage the authors to address the above issues; a revised version with robust, bias-controlled evaluations and scanpath metrics would be much more compelling.
