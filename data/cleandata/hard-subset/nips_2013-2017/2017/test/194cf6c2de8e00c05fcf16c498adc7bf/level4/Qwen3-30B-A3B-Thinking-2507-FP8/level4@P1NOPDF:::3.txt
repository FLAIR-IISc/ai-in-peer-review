

The paper introduces a novel framework for modeling eye movements, grounded in a distinct theoretical premise. The authors accurately contextualize existing approaches, which typically rely on saliency maps computed over images and select fixation points via optimization-based methods. In contrast, their proposed model (EYMOL) operates directly on gaze trajectories under a "Least Action Principle" framework, offering a fundamentally different theoretical perspective. While some mathematical details in the extensive appendix exceeded my expertise due to my limited background in physics, I found the core methodology and its distinction from prior work sufficiently clear to assess. The model is rigorously evaluated on a real-world eye-movement dataset and benchmarked against multiple established alternatives. Though it does not consistently outperform all competitors across every metric, the results demonstrate favorable performance for the proposed approach, validating its empirical relevance.

The work appears timely and potentially valuable for researchers in vision science, particularly those attending conferences like the Vision Sciences Society (VSS). However, I remain unconvinced that this contribution represents a significant advance for NeurIPS, given the conference’s focus on machine learning and computational neuroscience. While the model advances a compelling theoretical perspective that could stimulate further empirical research in perception science, its primary contribution lies in a specialized domain (eye movement modeling) rather than in novel machine learning techniques, theoretical frameworks, or algorithmic innovations that would strongly align with NeurIPS’ broader scope. This judgment should be weighed against the assessments of reviewers with deeper expertise in vision science. The paper demonstrates novelty within its niche and offers a meaningful theoretical contribution to perception science, but its direct relevance to the NeurIPS community requires careful consideration.
