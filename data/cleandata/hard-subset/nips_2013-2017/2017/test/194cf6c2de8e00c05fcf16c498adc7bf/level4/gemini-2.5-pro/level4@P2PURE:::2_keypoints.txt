- Paper studies human visual attention in static and dynamic scenes and proposes a model from three core principles formulated as an energy function minimized by eye movements.  
- Principle 1: a spatial constraint modeled as a harmonic oscillator that confines eye movements within image boundaries and a limited retinal field.  
- Principle 2: a curiosity-driven principle directing attention to regions with significant brightness changes across fine and coarse spatial scales.  
- Principle 3: a brightness invariance principle.  
- From the cost function the authors derive differential equations that predict scanpaths given an initial position and velocity.  
- Quantitative evaluation on static and dynamic eye-tracking datasets shows performance comparable to state-of-the-art approaches.  
- Contribution: novel, formal bottom-up framework for saliency and eye movements with potential to advance theoretical understanding.  
- Major weakness: poor writing and lack of clarity; the method is difficult to follow and partly inferred by conjecture.  
- Recommended fixes: thorough proofreading, expand all abbreviations, and explicitly define every variable and function in the main text.  
- Suggested structural change: move the current two-page appendix to supplementary material to free space for clearer definitions.  
- Recommend relating the model to cognitive science literature on optimal information gathering (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2008).  
- Question for authors: are the proposed variational principles a restatement of optimal search strategies or independent constraints that could integrate with information-theoretic models?
