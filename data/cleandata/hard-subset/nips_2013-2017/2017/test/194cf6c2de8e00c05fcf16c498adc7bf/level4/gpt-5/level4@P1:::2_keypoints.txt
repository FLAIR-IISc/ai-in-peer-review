- Proposes a variational, bottom-up model of visual attention using a Lagrangian whose stationary path yields Euler–Lagrange equations for eye-movement trajectories.  
- Encodes three principles: (1) retinal boundary potential for boundedness, (2) curiosity term favoring high brightness gradients at multiple scales, (3) brightness-invariance term akin to the optical flow constraint.  
- Defines scanpaths via differential equations conditioned on initial position and velocity; saliency maps produced by aggregating multiple simulated runs.  
- Evaluated on static (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and dynamic (SFU) datasets; performance is competitive with strong baselines.  
- Compares exact brightness-invariance formulation to a simplified upper bound; they behave similarly on static images.  
- Distinct from classic saliency pipelines by deriving scanpaths directly from a principled action functional rather than computing saliency first.  
- Conceptual novelty: unified least-action variational framework that combines saccadic exploration and fixation/tracking.  
- Technical contribution: clear derivation of motion equations and interpretable curiosity and brightness-invariance terms grounded in vision constraints.  
- Practicality: saliency maps emerge as a byproduct; model can run in real time and qualitatively tracks moving objects.  
- Empirical strength: competitive across multiple datasets despite relative simplicity and lack of saliency-specific tuning.  
- Clarity issue: paper is hard to follow; key variables, operators, and abbreviations are inconsistently defined and sometimes only appear in the appendix.  
- Organization suggestion: move long appendix to supplementary and use space to define variables, restate core equations with verbal explanations, and include an algorithmic summary (initialization, integration, runs, aggregation).  
- Literature positioning: needs deeper connection to psychological and optimal-search accounts (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler 2008) and discussion of compatibility or integration.  
- Claims scope: temper claims of state-of-the-art performance; highlight specific strengths such as dynamic scene tracking and interpretability.  
- Quality assessment: derivations seem sound and evaluation is broad, but poor exposition reduces reproducibility and confidence—require clearer definitions and algorithmic details.  
- Clarity assessment: main text must be made self-contained with no unexplained symbols or abbreviations.  
- Originality assessment: high—distinctive combination of variational least-action framework with curiosity and brightness invariance.  
- Significance assessment: potentially substantial if future work connects dynamics to information-gathering theories and validates scanpath statistics against human data.  
- Pros for acceptance: innovative principled formulation, competitive benchmarks, interpretable components, direct generation of scanpaths.  
- Cons for acceptance: poor clarity/organization, limited discussion of psychological/optimal-search literature, and not uniformly SOTA across metrics.  
- Overall: promising and refreshing perspective that would be a valuable contribution after substantial improvements to writing, positioning, and comparisons to optimal-search theories.
