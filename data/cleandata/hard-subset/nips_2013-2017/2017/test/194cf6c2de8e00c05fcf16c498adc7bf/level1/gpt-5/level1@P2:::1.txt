Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a physics-inspired, variational formulation of visual attention. The authors define a Lagrangian L = K − U over the scanpath x(t), with kinetic energy K and a generalized potential U composed of: (i) a boundary potential V that confines the trajectory to the retinal rectangle; (ii) a “curiosity” term C favoring locations with large brightness gradients and including a peripheral component via a blurred image p; and (iii) a brightness-invariance term B that penalizes violation of the optical-flow brightness constancy constraint bt + bx ẋ = 0. The stationary condition of the action S = ∫ L dt yields Euler–Lagrange equations that govern scanpath dynamics (EYMOL). For static images, the authors also study an approximation B̄ that removes the ẋ–x coupling. Parameters for the model are tuned by simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000-TRAIN to maximize NSS. Saliency maps are computed by counting trajectory visits over many randomized runs, with additional post hoc blur and center-bias tuning to optimize AUC-Judd and NSS. Experiments on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU videos show competitive metrics compared to a set of classical and recent methods (Itti & Koch, AIM, BMS, and a CNN-based model).

The work is related to extensive prior literature that computes saliency as a centralized map (Itti & Koch 1998), information-theoretic approaches (AIM: Bruce & Tsotsos), and saccadic/scanpath simulators (e.g., Le Meur & Liu). The model also borrows the brightness constancy constraint from optical flow (Horn–Schunck). Unlike most saliency models, this paper claims to derive scanpath dynamics from first principles, with saliency as a byproduct.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The variational setup is reasonable and the derivations are mostly standard; however, there are several notational/derivation issues that should be corrected. For instance, K(ẋ) in Eq. (8) appears to miss the 1/2 factor. In the Appendix, the determinant D simplifying to m(m − 2λ|bx|^2) is correct, but the intermediate algebra contains typos (e.g., duplicating indices). While these issues likely do not invalidate the implementation, they reduce confidence in the exact form and conditions. The well-posedness condition λ < m/4 is justified under |bx|^2 ≤ 2 but the argument is presented inconsistently.
- Modeling choices: The curiosity term C alternates between local and peripheral gradients via cos^2(ωt)/sin^2(ωt), which is intriguing but under-motivated; the choice and value of ω are not specified or validated. The boundary potential is ad hoc but effective.
- Evaluation: The experimental protocol introduces multiple sources of bias and tuning that confound conclusions. The model uses: (a) initial conditions “almost at the center,” (b) explicit multiplicative center bias on inputs b and p, and (c) post hoc grid search over blur and center-bias parameters to maximize metrics. These collectively make it difficult to attribute performance to the proposed dynamics rather than to center bias and metric-specific tuning. Moreover, parameter tuning (ηb, ηp, λ) via simulated annealing on a small subset (100 images) may overfit; cross-dataset generalization is not carefully tested (e.g., one set of parameters fixed across MIT300 and CAT2000, without per-benchmark tuning).
- Comparisons: The baselines include classical models and one CNN, but several top-performing contemporary deep models (e.g., SALICON, DeepGaze II, MLNet/SAM-ResNet/SalGAN) are not reported. Claims of top performance thus feel premature.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable and the Appendix helps, but important implementation details are missing: values or ranges for m, k, ω, integration step/solver, number of integration steps per second, and the parameter bounds used in simulated annealing. Some equations contain typographical errors and ambiguous notation (e.g., Eq. (8); algebra in Eqs. (41–42); Bx derivation). Citations are incomplete in the references list.
- The role of each term and its qualitative effect on trajectories are described, but an ablation study is needed in the main text to make this concrete.

Originality
- The use of a variational/Lagrangian formalism to unify curiosity-driven saccades and brightness-invariance-driven fixation/tracking is novel within the saliency literature. Prior dynamics-based models exist, but this explicit least-action framing is a fresh angle.
- The curiosity term—essentially gradient magnitude with a peripheral variant—is not new per se; originality lies more in the unifying dynamical formulation than in the features.

Significance
- If validated convincingly, a principled, low-parameter dynamical model that yields scanpaths and competitive saliency as a byproduct would be impactful, connecting computational neuroscience and computer vision.
- However, the empirical evidence is not yet compelling: performance appears to benefit substantially from center bias and metric tuning; comparisons miss several state-of-the-art models; and there is no analysis at the scanpath level (e.g., saccade amplitude distributions, fixation durations, scanpath similarity to humans), which is where this model should shine. As presented, practical utility beyond existing saliency methods remains uncertain.

Strengths
- Conceptually elegant variational formulation; unifies saccades and tracking mechanisms.
- Derivation of explicit Euler–Lagrange dynamics; clear condition for well-posedness.
- Competitive results reported on both images and videos with a simple grayscale input.
- Real-time potential and interpretability.

Weaknesses
- Derivation and notation issues; several typos and missing constants.
- Heavy reliance on center bias (both via initialization and explicit multiplicative bias) and metric-specific blur tuning; limited ablation.
- Parameter-selection details are under-specified; risk of overfitting on small tuning sets.
- Incomplete comparisons to the strongest contemporary deep saliency models.
- Lack of scanpath-level behavioral validation, despite being a core claim.
- Some modeling choices (cos/sin gating, ω) insufficiently justified.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a rigorous ablation: remove explicit center bias; randomize initial conditions over the whole image; turn off the post hoc blur/center tuning; and quantify the contribution of each term (V, C, B) and the frequency ω.
- Fix derivation and notation errors; state all parameters (m, k, ω), solver details, and annealing bounds; release code.
- Expand comparisons to top 2015–2017 deep models (SALICON, DeepGaze II, MLNet/SAM-ResNet/SalGAN), using the MIT benchmark.
- Evaluate scanpaths against human data (e.g., MultiMatch, string-edit distance, saccade amplitude distributions).
- Test cross-dataset generalization with a single parameter set learned once.
- Analyze sensitivity to λ and the well-posedness condition near high-gradient regions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and principled dynamical perspective on attention.
- Simple, interpretable model with potential for unifying saccades and tracking.
- Promising empirical results across images and videos.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical validation confounded by center bias and tuning; missing strong baselines.
- Derivational/notation issues; insufficient implementation detail for reproducibility.
- Lack of scanpath-level behavioral evaluation undermines the central claim.

Overall, this is an intriguing, original idea with promise, but the current empirical and methodological gaps make the contribution premature for NeurIPS without substantial revision.
