This paper proposes EYMOL, a novel, physics-inspired model of visual attention that generates scanpaths for both static and dynamic scenes. The core idea is to frame the trajectory of the focus of attention as the solution to a variational problem governed by a generalized Least Action Principle. The model’s Lagrangian combines standard kinetic energy with a generalized potential energy composed of three key terms: a boundary constraint, a "curiosity" term attracting attention to detailed regions (image gradients), and a "brightness invariance" term, adapted from optical flow principles, to promote fixation and object tracking. By solving the resulting Euler-Lagrange equations, the model produces a dynamic scanpath. Saliency maps, used for evaluation, are generated as a byproduct by aggregating fixation locations over time. This approach presents a significant conceptual departure from classic feature-integration models like Itti & Koch's and the now-dominant end-to-end deep learning methods, offering a principled, generative model of attentional dynamics rather than a direct predictor of a static saliency map.

The primary strength of this work is its originality and elegance. The application of analytical mechanics to model the fundamental drivers of attention is a refreshing and highly novel perspective in a field increasingly dominated by large-scale, data-driven models. A major contribution is the unification of saccadic movements (driven by curiosity) and fixation/tracking behaviors (driven by brightness invariance) within a single, coherent mathematical framework. Furthermore, the model is surprisingly effective, achieving competitive and sometimes superior performance on several standard image (CAT2000, MIT300) and video (SFU) saliency benchmarks. This demonstrates that a compact, principled model can rival complex learned systems, highlighting the potential significance of the underlying proposed laws. The paper is technically sound, with clear derivations provided in the appendix.

However, the paper has a few weaknesses. The motivation for some specific modeling choices could be stronger. For instance, the oscillatory weighting in the curiosity term is justified only briefly as a mechanism to avoid "trapping," but its functional form and the frequency parameter ω are not deeply explained or analyzed. The clarity could be improved with a more intuitive discussion of this mechanism. Secondly, the model’s parameters are optimized on a small data subset using simulated annealing. A more thorough analysis of the model's sensitivity to these parameters would increase confidence in its robustness. Finally, as an explicitly bottom-up model, it cannot account for top-down, task-dependent attentional control, which is a significant component of human vision. While this is a reasonable scope for a model of early vision, this limitation should be noted.

In summary, this is a high-quality, original, and significant contribution that introduces a new paradigm for modeling visual attention. Its strong empirical performance, coupled with its theoretical elegance, makes a compelling case for acceptance.
