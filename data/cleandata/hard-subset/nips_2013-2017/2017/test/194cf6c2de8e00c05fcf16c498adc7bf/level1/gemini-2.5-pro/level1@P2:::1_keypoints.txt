- Proposes EYMOL, a variational model of visual attention that derives scanpaths from a generalized Least Action Principle.  
- Models scanpaths as trajectories minimizing an action functional with a Lagrangian combining kinetic and potential energy.  
- Constructs potential energy from three principles: boundedness (confines to image), curiosity (attracts to image gradients with local/peripheral alternation), and brightness invariance (promotes edges/tracking).  
- Derives Euler–Lagrange differential equations and solves them numerically to generate scanpaths.  
- Produces saliency maps by aggregating simulated trajectories; parameters estimated via simulated annealing.  
- Uses a Gaussian preprocessing step that introduces an explicit center bias.  
- Validates on standard image/video benchmarks (MIT300, CAT2000, SFU) using AUC, NSS, CC, showing competitive performance.  
- Mathematical derivations are rigorous and detailed, with supporting appendix material.  
- Paper is clearly written and well organized; theoretical principles are explained before formalization.  
- The periodic curiosity term (cos²/sin²) appears somewhat ad-hoc and lacks stronger theoretical justification.  
- Parameter fitting on a small batch (~100 images) raises concerns about robustness and generalizability.  
- Novel and original: unifies fixation and tracking, offering an interpretable, generative alternative to black‑box saliency models.  
- Significant for shifting emphasis from static saliency maps to scanpath dynamics, potentially inspiring further research.  
- Key limitations: does not consistently beat top deep-learning methods and evaluation focuses on aggregate saliency rather than direct scanpath dynamics comparison.  
- Overall recommendation favors acceptance for its novelty, elegance, and competitive results despite some ad‑hoc choices and limited dynamic validation.
