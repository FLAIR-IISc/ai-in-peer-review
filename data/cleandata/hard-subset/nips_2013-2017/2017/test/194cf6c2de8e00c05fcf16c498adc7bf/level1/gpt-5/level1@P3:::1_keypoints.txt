- Paper proposes a dynamical, bottom-up visual-attention model where scanpaths are stationary points of a Lagrangian action, yielding a second-order ODE for focus-of-attention trajectories.
- Lagrangian components: boundary potential V(x), curiosity potential C(t,x) alternating local/peripheral gradients, and brightness-constancy velocity-dependent term B(t,x,ẋ).
- Saliency maps obtained by counting trajectory visits over many randomized runs; parameters tuned via simulated annealing on CAT2000-TRAIN.
- Evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video datasets and reported competitive saliency metrics against classical and some CNN baselines.
- Connects to classic saliency models, machine-learning saliency work, and dynamic eye-movement simulators; should also discuss optimal control/active-inference literature.
- Strength: technically competent derivation with correct Euler–Lagrange treatment and well-posedness conditions.
- Strength: brightness-constancy term plausibly unifies fixation and tracking; local/peripheral alternation helps avoid high-frequency traps.
- Strength: computationally light, ODE solvable in real time, yields reasonable saliency maps without heavy learning.
- Weakness: kinetic energy defined as (1/m)‖ẋ‖2 deviates from standard (m/2)‖ẋ‖2; needs justification or correction and dimensional analysis.
- Weakness: strong center bias injected via central Gaussian and center initialization, and further tuned to optimize metrics, confounding intrinsic model contribution.
- Weakness: parameter estimation uses only 100 images with unspecified simulated-annealing details, raising reproducibility and overfitting concerns.
- Weakness: alternation frequency ω is fixed and unanalyzed; sensitivity of ηb/ηp/λ/ω and peripheral blur is not explored.
- Weakness: equivalence claim for exact vs approximated brightness term on static images needs dynamic testing for videos, given bt noise.
- Weakness: evaluation focuses on saliency maps; lacks scanpath-level validation (saccade amplitudes, inter-fixation distances, temporal correlations).
- Clarity issue: kinetic-energy expression and dimensional consistency need fixing throughout the paper.
- Clarity issue: minor notation/typo (determinant simplification Eq.41–42) and missing definitions for SA acceptance, parameter domains, and ω.
- Clarity issue: preprocessing choices (grayscale conversion, Gaussian blur) should be explicitly reported and their metric impact shown.
- Originality: novel use of variational least-action framework and velocity-dependent brightness-constancy in saliency/scanpath modeling.
- Significance: offers an interpretable, physics-inspired alternative and a compact dynamical model, but lacks comparisons to top deep models and debiased/scanpath evaluations.
- Pro for acceptance: elegant unified derivation, interpretable dynamics, competitive lightweight performance, and potential for integration with learned features.
- Con for acceptance: reliance on center bias and dataset tuning, missing strongest deep-model comparisons, lack of scanpath tests, and technical/reproducibility gaps.
- Main suggestions: correct/justify kinetic energy, run debiased metrics (sAUC, IG) and center-bias ablations, and perform ηb/ηp/λ/ω/blur sensitivity studies.
- Additional suggestions: provide scanpath-level validation, compare to top deep saliency models (DeepGaze II, SALICON/MLNet, SAM), detail SA settings, and release code/parameters.
- Video suggestions: consider robust constancy terms (e.g., gradient constancy) and temporal smoothing of bt; quantify tracking performance.
- Overall recommendation: interesting and original but insufficient empirical validation and methodological clarity; recommend rejection now but encourage revision and stronger evaluations.
