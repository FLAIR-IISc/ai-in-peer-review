- Proposes a novel variational model of visual attention based on the Least Action Principle, defining a Lagrangian with boundedness, curiosity, and brightness invariance terms.  
- Derives differential equations for scanpath trajectories and treats saliency maps as a byproduct of the model.  
- Evaluated on static and dynamic scenes using standard datasets (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, SFU) with competitive performance in saliency detection.  
- Well-motivated interdisciplinary positioning (cognitive science, neuroscience, computer vision) and strong literature grounding (Koch & Ullman, Itti et al., deep learning work).  
- Clear and sound mathematical formulation, with detailed material appropriately deferred to the Appendix.  
- Improvement over state-of-the-art is present but modest given the model’s complexity.  
- Parameter estimation via simulated annealing is under-detailed: search space, initialization, convergence criteria, and reason for using 100 images are not justified.  
- Brightness invariance term and its biological plausibility are weakly justified; approximation in equation (6) needs rigorous analysis of its impact.  
- Claim that the saliency map is merely a “byproduct” is misleading since evaluation relies heavily on saliency comparisons.  
- Limited analysis of scanpaths and lack of detailed comparison with human eye-movement data is a missed opportunity.  
- Quality: technically sound but practical significance and impact are limited.  
- Clarity: generally well-written and organized, but some sections (notably parameter estimation) need more detail.  
- Originality: novel application of Least Action to attention; unification of known components is original.  
- Significance: moderate potential, promising conceptual step but requires stronger empirical advantages.  
- Recommendation: reject with encouragement to revise—address parameter estimation details, justify approximations, analyze scanpaths, clarify saliency-map role, and better demonstrate advantages over existing methods.
