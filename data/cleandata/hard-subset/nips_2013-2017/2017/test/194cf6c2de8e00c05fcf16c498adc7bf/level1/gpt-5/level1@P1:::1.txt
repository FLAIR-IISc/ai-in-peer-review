Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes EYMOL, a variational model of visual attention which treats the eye’s focus as a particle obeying Euler–Lagrange dynamics derived from a generalized action. The Lagrangian L = K − U includes: (i) a boundary potential V(x) that confines the trajectory within the retinal/image area; (ii) a “curiosity” term C(t, x) that alternates between local and peripheral spatial detail via the squared gradient magnitude of the raw image b and a blurred image p, with time modulation cos^2(ωt) and sin^2(ωt); (iii) a brightness invariance penalty B(t, x, ẋ) enforcing optical-flow-like brightness constancy ḃ = bt + bx ẋ ≈ 0. The resulting second-order ODEs govern the scanpath. Saliency maps are obtained indirectly by counting trajectory visits over multiple randomized initializations. Parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) are tuned via simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a subset of CAT2000-TRAIN. The model is evaluated on MIT1003/MIT300/CAT2000 for static images and SFU for videos, and compared to classic saliency baselines (Itti-Koch, AIM, Judd SVM, BMS) and one CNN/LSTM method.

This departs from mainstream saliency, which typically constructs a saliency map via feature integration [Itti et al., Koch & Ullman], information maximization (AIM), or learned regressors/CNNs (Judd; recent deep nets), often with winner-take-all scanpath simulation. Related dynamic models (e.g., Le Meur & Liu’s saccadic model) inject biological constraints, but not via a unifying variational principle. The optical-flow-inspired brightness constancy term follows Horn & Schunck.

Quality:
- The variational formulation and derivation of the Euler–Lagrange equations are generally sound, with clear identification of how each term contributes to forces. The condition λ < m/4 for well-posedness is useful.
- The exact and approximate brightness-invariance terms are compared, and equivalence for static images is empirically supported.
- However, several empirical and methodological aspects are under-specified or raise concerns:
  - Parameter learning: Simulated annealing maximizes NSS on human fixations from CAT2000; thus the model is not “parameter-free” nor purely principled. Details of the SA neighborhood, acceptance function P, domain D, α, number of iterations, and runtime are insufficient for reproducibility.
  - Center bias and postprocessing: Images are explicitly multiplied by a centered Gaussian before dynamics, and additional blur/center-bias parameters for the final saliency map are grid-searched to maximize metrics. This confounds the contribution of the proposed dynamics and raises fairness concerns relative to baselines (which may not be equally tuned for center bias). Moreover, modifying the image used for dynamics compromises the physical interpretation of the brightness-constancy term.
  - Comparisons: The baselines are dated; state-of-the-art contemporaries (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON/MLNet/DeepFix variants) are missing. Without those, claims of top performance “in half of the metrics” are hard to assess. Statistical testing is not consistently reported, and exact scores are not fully presented here.
  - Analysis: No ablation of the individual terms (V only, C only, B only, with/without time modulation) or sensitivity to ω, m, k, ηb/ηp/λ. No scanpath-level evaluation (e.g., MultiMatch, string-edit distances) despite the model’s emphasis on trajectories.
  - Dynamic scenes: While the B term is motivated by tracking, the experimental section does not quantify tracking quality (e.g., object-following success), nor analyze failure modes under illumination changes (where brightness constancy breaks). Initialization only at the first frame is simplistic.

Clarity:
- The paper is mostly readable, with derivations deferred to the appendix. Some notational slips/typos appear (e.g., determinant simplification in Eq. 41, repeated indices; footnotes in running text). The role and intuition of the time-modulated curiosity term could be better motivated and explained (why cos^2/sin^2; how ω is set).
- Experimental protocol lacks detail on numerical integration (step, solver tolerance), number of SA iterations, and wall-clock runtime; “real-time” claims are not quantified. The description of evaluation metrics relies on external benchmark reports without showing key numbers or errors.

Originality:
- Modeling attention scanpaths as stationary points of an action with physically interpretable terms is novel for saliency/attention modeling. The joint treatment of saccades (curiosity) and fixation/tracking (brightness invariance) in one variational framework is conceptually appealing.
- The curiosity term combining local and peripheral gradients via time modulation is an interesting idea, though ad hoc and not directly tied to known physiology or learned from data.

Significance:
- If competitive with modern baselines without heavy learned features, the approach could inspire alternative, interpretable models of attention and unified scanpath/saliency generation.
- However, given reliance on center bias and tuning, limited comparisons, and absence of strong scanpath analyses, the current empirical evidence is insufficient to claim clear state-of-the-art impact for NIPS.

Specific suggestions:
- Provide ablations for V/C/B terms; study ω’s effect; test without explicit center bias to see whether center emerges from V(x).
- Report full benchmark numbers with standard deviations and significance; include stronger CNN baselines from recent benchmarks.
- Evaluate scanpaths directly against human sequences (MultiMatch, saccade amplitude distributions, fixation durations).
- Clarify SA details and release code/parameters (m, k, λ bounds, ηb/ηp, ω, Gaussian sigmas).
- Analyze robustness to illumination changes and motion blur; assess real-time performance quantitatively.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually elegant, unified variational framework linking attention dynamics, fixation, and tracking.
- Interpretable components with a plausible physical/cognitive grounding.
- Derivation is careful; approximate and exact formulations compared.

Cons against acceptance:
- Empirical evaluation is not sufficiently strong or fair: missing state-of-the-art baselines, heavy reliance on center bias/blur tuning, limited statistical rigor.
- Insufficient ablations and scanpath-level validation, undermining the central claim about dynamic laws of eye movement.
- Reproducibility gaps and ambiguous training/tuning protocol.
- Some ad hoc choices (time-modulated curiosity) lack justification or analysis.

Recommendation:
Borderline reject. The paper presents an intriguing and original modeling perspective with solid mathematical grounding, but the experimental validation and analysis fall short of NIPS standards. Strengthening comparisons, removing confounds (center bias), adding ablations and scanpath evaluations, and improving clarity/reproducibility would make this a much stronger submission.
