- Proposes a novel variational/Least Action physics-based framework (EYMOL) modeling eye movements as a particle in a potential field that generates scanpaths and saliency maps.
- Integrates three principles: boundedness via a harmonic oscillator, curiosity-driven attraction to high gradients, and brightness invariance for fixation/tracking.
- Offers a dynamical-systems perspective on attention, differing from static saliency-map approaches and building on classic models.
- Originality: highly novel unification of curiosity and brightness invariance within a principled mathematical framework.
- Mathematical quality: derivations are sound, implementation details clear, and simulated annealing for parameter estimation appropriate.
- Significance: moderate — provides a new theoretical lens but not a definitive advance in understanding human cognition.
- Clarity: well-written with clear derivations and a reproducible appendix.
- Experimental weakness: parameter tuning used only 100 CAT2000-TRAIN images, which is insufficient for robust model selection.
- Evaluation limited to NSS and AUC-Judd without error analysis, qualitative comparisons, or statistical significance testing.
- Claim of "best score in half of the cases" lacks statistical validation.
- Contribution significance limited by routine metrics and lack of demonstrated computational or biological advantages over existing methods.
- Minor clarity gap: the specific form of the "curiosity" term and psychological interpretation of parameters are underexplained.
- Specific concern: "real-time" claim is unsupported by timing measurements or runtime comparisons.
- Specific concern: approximation used for brightness invariance is not thoroughly justified for static images.
- Specific concern: paper does not clearly differentiate itself from prior physics-inspired vision methods (optical flow, energy minimization).
- Specific concern: validation focuses on saliency maps rather than scanpaths, limiting connection to actual human eye-movement data.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical framework, sound math, competitive benchmark performance, and appealing link to physical principles.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited experimental validation, unclear biological plausibility, and lack of clear advantages over existing methods.
- Overall assessment: interesting and compelling theoretical contribution worthy of consideration but needs stronger experimental analysis and validation.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions contingent on improved parameter tuning, statistical analyses, timing experiments, and deeper scanpath/behavioral comparisons.
