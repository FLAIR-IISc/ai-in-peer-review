### Summary

This paper proposes a novel, principled model of visual attention scanpaths, named EYMOL (EYe MOvement Laws), derived from a generalization of the Least Action Principle from physics. Instead of directly computing a static saliency map, the core contribution is a set of differential equations that describe the trajectory of the focus of attention over time. The scanpath is modeled as the trajectory that minimizes a generalized action functional, whose Lagrangian incorporates kinetic energy and a generalized potential energy. This potential energy is ingeniously constructed from three core principles: 1) a "boundedness" term that confines the scanpath to the image area, 2) a "curiosity" term that attracts attention to regions of high detail (image gradients), alternating between local and peripheral information, and 3) a "brightness invariance" term, borrowed from optical flow literature, that promotes fixation on object edges in static scenes and tracking of moving objects in dynamic scenes.

The resulting Euler-Lagrange equations of motion are solved numerically to generate scanpaths. A saliency map is then computed as a byproduct by aggregating the locations visited over many simulated trajectories. The model's parameters are estimated using simulated annealing. The authors validate their approach by comparing the generated saliency maps against human fixation data on standard image (MIT300, CAT2000) and video (SFU) benchmarks, showing competitive performance against several well-known methods. This work distinguishes itself from the dominant paradigm of computing a static saliency map (e.g., Itti et al.) and from modern deep learning approaches by providing a generative, dynamic, and interpretable model of the attentional scanpath itself.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations from the proposed Lagrangian is mathematically sound and detailed in the appendix. The experimental validation is rigorous, employing standard public benchmarks and metrics (AUC, NSS, CC), which allows for a direct and fair comparison with existing work. The authors are careful to evaluate two versions of their model (with an exact and an approximate brightness invariance term) and demonstrate that the approximation is sufficient for static images, which is a good scientific practice. The work is presented as a complete system, from theoretical formulation to parameter estimation and empirical validation.

A minor weakness lies in the parameter estimation procedure. Using simulated annealing on a small, randomly selected batch of 100 images may raise questions about the robustness and generalizability of the chosen parameters. Furthermore, the model relies on a pre-processing step that introduces a center bias by multiplying the input brightness fields by a Gaussian blob. While center bias is a well-known phenomenon and its inclusion is common practice, this explicit addition slightly detracts from the elegance of having all behavior emerge purely from the variational laws.

**Clarity**

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The core principles driving the model are enumerated and explained intuitively before their mathematical formulation is presented. This makes the central idea accessible. Separating the detailed mathematical derivations into the appendix is an effective choice that improves the readability of the main text. The experimental setup and results are also presented in a clear and understandable manner.

One area for potential improvement is the motivation for the specific functional form of the "curiosity" term, `C(t, x) = b²ₓ cos²(ωt) + p²ₓ sin²(ωt)`. While the goal of alternating between local and peripheral information is stated, the choice of a periodic `cos²`/`sin²` modulation feels somewhat engineered rather than derived from a deeper principle. A more thorough justification for this specific choice would strengthen the paper.

**Originality**

The paper is highly original. The central idea of framing visual attention as a problem in the calculus of variations and deriving differential equations of motion for the scanpath is a significant and novel departure from mainstream approaches in the field. Most existing models either compute a static saliency map or use black-box machine learning models to predict fixations. This paper, in contrast, proposes a generative and interpretable physics-based model for the *dynamics* of attention. The unified treatment of fixation and tracking through the brightness invariance principle is particularly elegant and novel. While the constituent parts (variational principles, brightness constancy) are known in other fields, their synthesis into a cohesive model of visual attention is a unique and creative contribution.

**Significance**

The work is significant as it introduces a new and thought-provoking paradigm for modeling visual attention. It shifts the focus from "where to look" (saliency) to "how to look" (scanpath dynamics). This principled, interpretable framework is a valuable scientific alternative to the increasingly complex and opaque deep learning models that currently dominate the field. The fact that such a conceptually simple model achieves competitive results on standard benchmarks underscores the potential of its underlying principles. This paper is likely to inspire other researchers to explore dynamic, principle-driven models of cognitive processes.

The primary limitation on its immediate impact is that, while competitive, it does not consistently outperform the top state-of-the-art deep learning methods. Furthermore, the validation is restricted to comparing aggregate saliency maps. The true promise of a scanpath model is in predicting the temporal sequence and geometric properties of saccades. As the authors note for future work, a direct comparison of generated scanpaths with human scanpath statistics would be a more powerful validation of the model's dynamic claims.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Novelty and Elegance:** The paper introduces a highly original and conceptually elegant variational framework for modeling visual attention, a significant departure from existing work.
*   **Principled Approach:** It provides a clear, interpretable model derived from a few fundamental principles, which is a valuable contribution in an era of "black box" models.
*   **Unifying Framework:** The model elegantly unifies the mechanisms for curiosity-driven exploration, fixation, and object tracking within a single set of dynamic equations.
*   **Competitive Performance:** Despite its relative simplicity and lack of large-scale training, the model achieves strong, competitive results on both image and video saliency benchmarks.

### Arguments against Acceptance

*   **Ad-hoc Modeling Choices:** Some specific mathematical formulations, particularly the oscillating curiosity term, feel somewhat ad-hoc and could be better justified.
*   **Limited Validation of Dynamics:** The model's main output is a scanpath, but the evaluation is limited to static saliency maps derived from these paths. A direct comparison with human scanpath dynamics is missing.
*   **Performance:** The model is competitive but does not consistently set a new state-of-the-art compared to heavily-tuned deep learning approaches.

Overall, the strengths, particularly the profound originality and conceptual elegance of the approach, far outweigh the weaknesses. This paper presents a beautiful and inspiring piece of science that opens up a new avenue for research in computational attention models. It is a strong candidate for acceptance.
