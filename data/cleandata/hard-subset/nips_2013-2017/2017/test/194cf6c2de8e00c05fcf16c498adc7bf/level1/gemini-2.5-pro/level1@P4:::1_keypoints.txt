- Proposes EYMOL: a principle-driven model that generates scanpaths by treating eye movements as particle trajectories from a Least Action variational principle.  
- Formulates a Lagrangian L = K - U with kinetic energy K and a generalized potential U governing attention dynamics.  
- Defines three potential terms: bounding potential V(x) to keep gaze in-frame, curiosity C(t,x) attracting high-gradient regions with oscillatory local/peripheral weighting, and brightness invariance B(t,x,ẋ) based on optical-flow constraints.  
- Derives Euler–Lagrange second-order ODEs to produce continuous-time scanpath trajectories x(t).  
- Produces saliency maps as density maps of simulated scanpath visits and optimizes parameters via simulated annealing.  
- Evaluated on standard static (MIT300, CAT2000) and video (SFU) benchmarks, showing competitive performance despite not being end-to-end.  
- Technical soundness: mathematical derivation is detailed and correct; appendix supports reproducibility.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written, principled, and logically structured with clear exposition of core ideas.  
- Originality: novel unification of fixation, boundary following, and tracking within a variational Lagrangian framework—a new paradigm beyond saliency-first models.  
- Significance: potential paradigm shift toward scanpath-first generative models linking vision to optimal control and physics.  
- Biological plausibility concerns: specific choices (e.g., sinusoidal curiosity modulation) lack neurophysiological justification.  
- Evaluation limitation: analysis relies on aggregate saliency maps and omits comparisons of temporal scanpath statistics (saccade amplitudes, fixation durations, ordering) to human eye-tracking.  
- Parameter sensitivity unclear: limited discussion/justification of parameter choices (η_b, η_p, λ, mass m, frequency ω) and robustness to them.  
- Novelty caveat: constituent principles (optical flow, gradient attraction) are established; novelty is primarily in their synthesis within the variational framework.  
- Acceptance arguments: theoretically elegant, generative scanpath model, unifies attentional mechanisms, and achieves competitive benchmark results.  
- Rejection arguments: biological basis for some components not shown, missed temporal validation of scanpaths, and limited parameter sensitivity analysis.
