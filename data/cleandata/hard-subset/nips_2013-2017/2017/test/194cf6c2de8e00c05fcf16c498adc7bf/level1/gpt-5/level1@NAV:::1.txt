This paper proposes EYMOL, a variational formulation of visual attention in which scanpaths arise as stationary points of a generalized action. The Lagrangian combines: (i) a potential that confines motion to the retinal field; (ii) a “curiosity” term that alternates local and peripheral detail via gradients of brightness and a blurred image; and (iii) a brightness-constancy term akin to the optical-flow constraint (Horn–Schunck). The resulting Euler–Lagrange equations define second-order dynamics for the focus of attention. Parameters are tuned by simulated annealing on CAT2000-TRAIN. Saliency maps are obtained by aggregating visited locations over many runs. Experiments on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU videos show performance competitive with several classical models (Itti–Koch, information maximization, BMS) and some learned baselines, with a simplified brightness-invariance yielding similar accuracy to the exact version on static images.

Relation to prior work: The paper draws on classic saliency-map models (Itti et al.), information maximization (Bruce & Tsotsos), Bayesian surprise (Itti & Baldi), and optical flow. The mechanics/least-action view is less explored in NeurIPS attention literature and complements recent deep saliency predictors (e.g., DeepGaze II; SALICON), which are not thoroughly discussed or compared.

Quality: The derivation is careful and the well-posedness condition on λ is noted. However, several modeling choices appear ad hoc or under-justified: the cosine/sine alternation for curiosity, non-smooth boundary forces via indicator functions, and fixing m,k,ω without reported sensitivity. Empirical evaluation is solid but limited: center-bias/blur are tuned, which can inflate AUC-Judd and NSS; sAUC and shuffled metrics should be emphasized. Comparisons largely omit the strongest recent deep models, and ablations isolating the contribution of curiosity vs. brightness-constancy are missing. Reproducibility would benefit from code and full hyperparameter disclosure.

Clarity: Generally well written with a detailed appendix. Some notation and typos could be tightened, and key constants (m,k,ω) and their ranges should be reported. The pre-processing “content rectangle” requires clarification.

Originality: Moderate to high. A unified least-action treatment that yields scanpaths and saliency as a byproduct is novel and intellectually appealing.

Significance: Moderate. The approach is interpretable, fast, and may influence future mechanistic models, but current state-of-the-art saliency is dominated by learned methods; impact will hinge on stronger empirical validation.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually elegant variational framework unifying fixation and tracking
- Interpretable dynamics with competitive results given simplicity
- Clear derivations and potential real-time applicability

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited comparisons to modern deep baselines; missing sAUC emphasis
- Incomplete ablations and hyperparameter reporting (m,k,ω)
- Some heuristic modeling choices lack justification; boundary non-smoothness
- Color cues and top-down influences are excluded, potentially limiting ecological validity
