- Paper proposes a physics-inspired variational/Lagrangian formulation for visual attention (L = K − U) yielding Euler–Lagrange scanpath dynamics (EYMOL) and saliency maps via trajectory counts, tested on several image/video datasets.

- Model components: boundary potential V, curiosity term C (local + peripheral via blurred image p), and brightness-invariance term B from optical-flow brightness constancy; static-image approximation B̄ removes ẋ–x coupling.

- Technical soundness: variational setup is reasonable but contains notational/derivational errors (e.g., missing 1/2 in K, algebra typos) and inconsistent presentation of well-posedness (λ < m/4).

- Modeling choices: curiosity gating (cos^2/sin^2) alternating local/peripheral is under-motivated, ω unspecified/unanalyzed; boundary potential is ad hoc but works.

- Evaluation flaws: multiple sources of bias and tuning confound results—center-biased initialization, multiplicative center bias, and post hoc blur/center-bias grid search—and parameter tuning on a small subset risks overfitting.

- Cross-dataset generalization is not convincingly tested (parameters not consistently fixed across benchmarks).

- Comparisons are incomplete: several strong contemporary deep saliency models (SALICON, DeepGaze II, MLNet/SAM-ResNet/SalGAN) are omitted, making performance claims premature.

- Clarity issues: missing implementation details (m, k, ω, solver step, integration settings, annealing bounds), typographical equation errors, and incomplete citations hinder reproducibility; main-text ablation is lacking.

- Originality: novel application of a least-action/Lagrangian formalism to unify saccades and tracking; the curiosity feature itself is not new but its integration in the dynamical framework is.

- Significance: conceptually impactful if validated, but current empirical evidence is weak due to center-bias/method tuning and lack of scanpath-level behavioral validation.

- Strengths: elegant, interpretable variational formulation with explicit dynamics, competitive reported results using simple grayscale input, and potential for real-time use.

- Weaknesses: derivation/notation mistakes, heavy reliance on center bias and metric-specific tuning, under-specified parameter selection, missing top baselines, and absence of scanpath behavioral analyses.

- Suggested improvements: rigorous ablation (remove center bias, randomize initial conditions, disable post hoc tuning), fix derivations and report all parameters/solver/code, add top deep-model comparisons, evaluate scanpath similarity to humans, test single-parameter cross-dataset generalization, and analyze λ/ω sensitivity.

- Arguments for acceptance: novel principled dynamical perspective, simple interpretable model unifying saccades and tracking, and promising cross-modal results.

- Arguments against acceptance: empirical validation is confounded and incomplete, derivational and reproducibility issues, and lack of scanpath-level evaluation; substantial revision required.
