- Proposes EYMOL, a variational model of visual attention treating gaze as a particle obeying Euler–Lagrange dynamics with terms for boundary confinement, time-modulated curiosity (local vs peripheral gradients), and brightness-invariance tracking.
- Saliency maps are obtained by aggregating trajectory visits; parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) are tuned via simulated annealing to maximize NSS on CAT2000-TRAIN.
- Novelty: framing scanpaths as stationary points of an action with interpretable physical terms is original and conceptually appealing.
- Mathematical derivation is generally sound, with useful well-posedness condition λ < m/4 and comparison between exact and approximate brightness-invariance showing empirical equivalence for static images.
- Parameter learning via simulated annealing is under-specified (neighborhood, acceptance P, domain, α, iterations, runtime) reducing reproducibility.
- Explicit image pre-multiplication by a centered Gaussian and grid-searched blur/center-bias for final maps confound the contribution of the dynamics and compromise fairness versus baselines.
- Modifying input images for dynamics also undermines the physical interpretation of the brightness-constancy term.
- Comparisons omit contemporary deep saliency models (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON/MLNet/DeepFix), making performance claims (“top in half metrics”) hard to assess.
- Statistical testing and full benchmark scores (with variances) are incompletely reported.
- No ablation studies for V/C/B terms, no sensitivity analysis for ω, m, k, ηb/ηp/λ, and no justification for the cosine/sine time modulation.
- Scanpath-level evaluation (MultiMatch, string-edit, saccade distributions) is missing despite the model’s trajectory focus.
- Dynamic scene experiments lack tracking-specific metrics, robustness analysis to illumination changes/motion blur, and use simplistic initialization (only first frame).
- Clarity issues: some notational typos, deferred derivations, and weak motivation/explanation for the time-modulated curiosity term and choice of ω.
- Experimental protocol omits numerical integration details (time step, solver tolerance), SA iteration counts, wall-clock runtimes, and unquantified “real-time” claims.
- Significance: could inspire interpretable attention models if competitive without learned features, but current empirical weaknesses limit impact.
- Specific recommendations include adding ablations, testing without explicit center bias, reporting full benchmark numbers with significance, including modern CNN baselines, providing scanpath evaluations, detailing SA and solver settings, and releasing code/parameters.
- Pros: elegant unified variational framework, interpretable components, careful derivations, and comparison of exact/approx formulations.
- Cons: empirical evaluation insufficient/fairness-confounded, missing SOTA baselines, inadequate ablations and scanpath validation, reproducibility gaps, and some ad hoc design choices.
- Overall recommendation: borderline reject — promising, original approach but requires stronger, fairer, and more reproducible experimental validation to meet conference standards.
