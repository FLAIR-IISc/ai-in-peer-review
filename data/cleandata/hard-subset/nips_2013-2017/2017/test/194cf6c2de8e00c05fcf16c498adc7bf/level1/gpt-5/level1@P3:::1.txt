Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a dynamical, bottom-up model of visual attention in which scanpaths arise as stationary points of a generalized action functional. The Lagrangian includes: (i) a boundary potential V(x) that keeps the trajectory within the image domain; (ii) a “curiosity” potential C(t, x) that attracts the particle to regions of high spatial detail, alternating between local gradient magnitude and a peripheral (Gaussian-blurred) gradient via cos2/sin2 temporal modulation; and (iii) a brightness-constancy term B(t, x, ẋ) ≡ (bt + bx·ẋ)2 that encourages fixation and motion tracking by penalizing departures from the optical-flow constraint. The resulting Euler–Lagrange equations yield a second-order ODE for the 2D focus-of-attention trajectory. Saliency maps are then obtained as a byproduct by counting trajectory visits over many runs with randomized initial conditions. Parameters (ηb, ηp for curiosity; λ for brightness constancy) are tuned via simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a subset of CAT2000-TRAIN. The model is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and the SFU video dataset, reporting competitive saliency metrics versus several baselines, including classical models and a CNN-based method.

The work connects with classic saliency-map models (Itti & Koch; GBVS; information maximization; Bayesian surprise), machine learning approaches (Judd et al.; deep CNN methods such as Vig et al., SAM), and dynamic eye-movement simulators (e.g., Le Meur & Liu). It is also related in spirit to optimal control/variational approaches in attention and active vision (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler’s optimal saccade strategies; active inference/control-as-inference), although these are not discussed.

Quality
Strengths:
- The derivation is technically competent: starting from L = K − U with a velocity-dependent term, the paper correctly carries through Euler–Lagrange to an explicit ODE with conditions ensuring well-posedness (D > 0, implying λ < m/4 under normalization).
- The brightness-constancy term is a principled way to couple fixation and tracking behaviors, and the local/peripheral alternation in C(t, x) is a simple mechanism to avoid traps in high-frequency regions.
- The model is computationally light and the ODE is solvable in real time; empirical results suggest it can yield reasonable saliency maps without heavy learning.

Weaknesses:
- The kinetic energy is defined as K(ẋ) = (1/m)‖ẋ‖2, which deviates from the standard (m/2)‖ẋ‖2. While a constant rescaling can be absorbed into m and λ, the mismatch in physical units and its implications for bounds (e.g., λ < m/4) deserve justification or correction.
- A heavy center bias is injected by multiplying the input with a central Gaussian and initializing trajectories near the center, and further tuning blur/center parameters is performed to optimize AUC-Judd/NSS. This makes it difficult to disentangle the intrinsic contribution of the variational dynamics from standard priors known to inflate certain metrics. Debiased metrics (sAUC, IG) and ablations without center bias are needed.
- Parameter estimation uses only 100 images (5 per CAT2000 category) and unspecified SA details (search bounds, acceptance function, α). Reproducibility and risk of overfitting are concerns, particularly since additional blur/center parameters are tuned per dataset.
- The ω governing alternation between local and peripheral gradients appears fixed; its choice and sensitivity are not analyzed. An ablation on ηb/ηp/λ/ω would clarify each term’s role.
- The claim that the exact and approximated brightness terms perform equivalently on static images is plausible (bt ≈ 0), but the dynamic case should test the advantage of the exact term, especially given potential noise in bt estimates.
- The evaluation focuses on saliency maps; scanpath-level validation (saccade amplitude distributions, inter-fixation distances, temporal correlations, trajectory similarity to human scanpaths) is not provided, despite the model’s central contribution being a scanpath generator.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a detailed appendix. Some notation and derivations need polishing:
- Fix the kinetic energy expression and check dimensional consistency throughout.
- Clarify the determinant simplification (Eq. 41–42; likely a typo b2x1 + b2x2).
- Provide definitions for SA acceptance P(·), parameter domains, and ω.
- Explicitly report preprocessing choices (grayscale conversion, Gaussian blur) and their impact on metrics.

Originality
Using a variational least-action framework to derive attention dynamics that simultaneously produce saccades and tracking is novel in the saliency literature and distinct from mainstream deep saliency approaches. The velocity-dependent “potential” encoding brightness-constancy is an interesting twist on Lagrangian modeling. Related lines in optimal control/active inference are relevant and should be discussed to better situate the contribution.

Significance
Conceptually, the paper offers an interpretable, physics-inspired alternative to black-box saliency estimators. Practically, the reported metrics are competitive with several baselines given a simple grayscale pipeline. However, absent stronger comparisons to top 2015–2017 deep models (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON/MLNet/SAM), debiased evaluation, and scanpath-level validation, it is hard to conclude that this advances the state of the art. The main significance may lie in providing a compact dynamical model that can be extended or combined with learned features.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, unified derivation of scanpaths from a small set of principles.
- Interpretable dynamics linking fixation and tracking via brightness constancy.
- Competitive results with lightweight computation; saliency as a byproduct.
- Potentially valuable foundation for integrating learned features with principled dynamics.

Cons for acceptance
- Evaluation relies on center bias and dataset-specific tuning; limited ablations and debiased metrics.
- Missing comparisons to strongest contemporary deep methods and no scanpath-level tests.
- Some technical issues (nonstandard kinetic energy, incomplete SA details) harm rigor and reproducibility.
- ω and the periodic switching in curiosity are ad hoc and under-analyzed.

Suggestions/questions for authors
- Correct or justify the kinetic energy form; provide a dimensional analysis and revisit the λ bound.
- Report sAUC and IG, and ablate center bias and initialization to assess intrinsic model performance.
- Add ablations for ηb, ηp, λ, ω, and peripheral blur; analyze sensitivity and stability.
- Provide scanpath-level validation against human trajectories on MIT1003/CAT2000.
- Compare against top deep saliency models from the MIT benchmark (DeepGaze II, SALICON/MLNet, SAM).
- Detail SA settings (bounds, acceptance, α, iterations) and release code/parameters.
- For videos, consider robust constancy terms (e.g., gradient constancy) and temporal smoothing of bt; quantify tracking (e.g., object-following metrics).

Overall recommendation
Interesting and original modeling with promising results, but the current empirical validation and methodological clarity are insufficient for NIPS. With stronger, fairer evaluations and clarified technical details, this could make a solid contribution. I recommend rejection in its current form, with encouragement to revise along the lines above.
