- Proposal: Introduces a variational framework using a generalized Least Action Principle to model visual attention and derives Euler–Lagrange equations for attention dynamics.  
- Principles: Model built from three principles—bounded retinal trajectories, curiosity-driven attraction to high gradients, and brightness invariance for fixation/tracking.  
- Model (EYMOL): EYMOL generates scanpaths by solving the differential equations and produces saliency maps as a byproduct.  
- Evaluation: Tested on static (MIT1003, CAT2000) and video (SFU) datasets, reporting competitive performance and top scores on about half the metrics.  
- Relation to prior work: Properly cites feature-integration and saliency-map literature and recent deep learning models, but insufficiently differentiates from earlier energy-minimization/physics-inspired attention models ([22], [23]).  
- Strength — theoretical innovation: Offers an intellectually stimulating unification of attentional principles under a variational/least-action framework with sound mathematical derivations.  
- Strength — computational efficiency: Model is real-time capable and produces saliency maps without separate computation, a practical advantage.  
- Strength — empirical competitiveness: Achieves competitive benchmark results, showing practical utility of the framework.  
- Weakness — cognitive justification: No empirical or neuroscientific evidence that visual attention follows least-action principles; biological plausibility is not established.  
- Weakness — limited scanpath analysis: Claims to model scanpaths but evaluates primarily with saliency metrics (AUC, NSS) and lacks trajectory-level comparison to human eye movements.  
- Weakness — novelty overstated: The contribution appears mainly a mathematical reformulation of existing ideas rather than a fundamentally new concept.  
- Weakness — technical concerns: Approximation of the brightness-invariance term (eq.6) lacks error analysis and the stability constraint λ < m/4 (eq.40) is not discussed in practical terms.  
- Pros for acceptance: Theoretical novelty linking physics and attention, computational practicality, and competitive results on benchmarks.  
- Cons for acceptance: Lack of justification for least-action foundation, missing scanpath validation, overstated novelty, and insufficient biological grounding.  
- Overall assessment: Mathematically solid and empirically competent but limited in significance for understanding cognitive mechanisms; clearer neuroscience links and scanpath evaluation are needed.  
- Recommendation: Recommend rejection as incremental rather than transformative; suggest stronger cognitive/neuroscience evidence, detailed scanpath comparisons, clearer differentiation from prior physics-based work, and analysis of approximation/stability issues.
