Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a dynamical model of visual attention (EYMOL) derived as Euler–Lagrange equations from a generalized action functional. The Lagrangian includes: (i) a boundary potential to constrain gaze within the image; (ii) a “curiosity” term that attracts the trajectory to locations with high spatial detail, combining local and peripheral gradient energies with a time-varying weight; and (iii) a brightness-invariance term derived from the classical optical-flow brightness constancy constraint to encourage fixation and object tracking. The resulting second-order ODE yields scanpaths; saliency maps are obtained as a byproduct by counting spatial visit frequencies over multiple runs. Parameters (weights of curiosity subterms and brightness-invariance) are tuned by simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000-TRAIN to maximize NSS. The model is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video datasets against several baselines (Itti-Koch, information maximization, Judd et al., BMS, and an LSTM-based deep model).

The work differs from the widely used saliency-map-plus-WTA paradigm [Itti & Koch; Bruce & Tsotsos; Judd et al.] by not postulating a centralized saliency map to drive selection. Instead, attention emerges from a physically inspired variational principle, unifying saccadic exploration and motion tracking in the same framework. It relates to prior biologically motivated scanpath simulators (e.g., Le Meur & Liu) but emphasizes a principled variational derivation. Compared to recent CNN-based saliency estimators (e.g., SALICON/ML-Net/DeepFix/SAM, DeepGaze), it trades predictive capacity for interpretability and dynamical modeling.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A principled variational formulation that unifies curiosity-driven exploration and brightness constancy for fixation/tracking is original and appealing. The continuous-time dynamical system provides a coherent alternative to WTA/IOR heuristics.
- Technical contribution: The derivation of the Euler–Lagrange equations with a velocity-dependent “potential” and the analysis of well-posedness (matrix determinant constraint and λ < m/4 condition) are nontrivial. The appendix details the chain of derivatives.
- Practicality: The method is computationally light (simple ODE integration), naturally produces scanpaths, and yields saliency maps as a byproduct.
- Empirical evidence: The model performs competitively with selected baselines on MIT/CAT2000 and SFU, with an ablation showing the brightness-invariance approximation is adequate for static images.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality (soundness and evaluation)
- Some modeling choices appear ad hoc and under-justified: the time-varying curiosity mixture C(t, x) with cos^2/sin^2(ωt) requires specifying ω; its rationale and sensitivity are not analyzed. The boundary potential acts only outside the image (no soft confinement within), which may allow unrealistic edge sliding unless other forces counteract it.
- Kinetic energy is defined as K(ẋ) = (1/m) ẋ^2 (Eq. 8). Standard analytic mechanics uses K = (1/2) m ||ẋ||^2. If this is not a typo, dimensions and the physical analogy become unclear; if it is a typo, it should be corrected, as it affects parameter interpretation and the condition on λ.
- The brightness-invariance term can be minimized by ẋ = 0 (especially for static images), potentially favoring halting; the interplay with K and C determines motion, but the paper does not analyze stability or qualitative phase behavior (e.g., conditions that produce saccades vs. fixations).
- The well-posedness derivation (Eqs. 41–47) is hard to follow and seems inconsistent in places; clarifying the determinant simplification and bounds on λ with correct algebra would strengthen confidence.
- Evaluation risks overfitting to metrics via multiple tuning stages: (i) SA tunes ηb, ηp, λ on CAT2000-TRAIN to maximize NSS; and (ii) additional grid search tunes blur and center-bias parameters to maximize AUC-Judd and NSS. While common in saliency work, this makes unbiased generalization claims harder. Reporting performance on sAUC (which discounts center bias) and CC/KL/IG would better contextualize metric-specific gains.
- Baseline coverage is limited relative to 2017 SOTA. Including DeepGaze II, SALICON/ML-Net/DeepFix/SAM-ResNet, and more recent video saliency baselines would provide a stronger comparison.
- Reproducibility details are insufficient: ω, m, k, ηb, ηp, λ ranges and final values, time discretization, integration method/tolerances, and the domain D for SA are not specified; code availability would help.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable and the appendix is useful, but there are typographical errors (e.g., “psedo-code”), missing parameter definitions (ω, α), and ambiguous equations (K term; determinant steps) that should be corrected.
- The role and implementation of center bias are conflated with input preprocessing (multiplying b and p by a Gaussian), which changes the dynamics beyond a post-hoc map bias; an explicit ablation clarifying this effect would improve clarity.
- The video experiments mention emergent tracking qualitatively; providing quantitative tracking or scanpath similarity analyses would make the claim more concrete.

Originality
- Casting attention dynamics via a generalized action with brightness-invariance coupling is novel within NIPS saliency literature. The explicit unification of saccades and tracking in a single variational framework is a distinctive contribution beyond standard saliency-map models.

Significance
- As a conceptual and mechanistic model, the work is potentially influential for interdisciplinary research linking vision, control, and neuroscience. However, without comparisons to the strongest deep baselines and without broader metrics, the empirical saliency significance remains moderate. The ability to generate scanpaths is valuable; quantifying scanpath similarity to humans (e.g., MultiMatch, string-edit, time-to-first-fixation) would increase impact.

Questions and suggestions
- Specify ω, m, k, ηb, ηp, λ, α, and the SA domain D; provide sensitivity analyses for ω and the curiosity mixture.
- Clarify/correct the kinetic energy definition and the λ < m/4 bound derivation; include unit/scale normalization for b and its gradients.
- Add ablations: remove brightness-invariance; remove peripheral term; remove center bias; vary Gaussian blur; report effects on metrics including sAUC and CC.
- Extend baselines to state-of-the-art deep models circa 2016–2017 (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON/ML-Net/DeepFix/SAM-ResNet), and include modern video saliency baselines.
- Report scanpath-level metrics and dynamic fixation statistics to substantiate the tracking and saccade claims.
- Discuss numerical stability (step sizes, stiffness), and provide runtime on standard hardware.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, interpretable variational formulation yielding a unified dynamical model of attention (scanpaths and tracking).
- Clear mathematical derivation; simple, real-time implementability.
- Competitive results on standard datasets against several well-known baselines.

Arguments against acceptance
- Key modeling details and parameters are under-specified; some equations likely contain errors/typos (kinetic energy; determinant steps).
- Empirical validation lacks strongest contemporary baselines and comprehensive metrics; potential overfitting to center-biased metrics.
- Limited analysis of qualitative behavior, stability, and parameter sensitivity; no quantitative scanpath/tracking evaluation.

Overall, the work is original and promising as a mechanistic model, but clarity and evaluation need strengthening to meet NIPS standards.
