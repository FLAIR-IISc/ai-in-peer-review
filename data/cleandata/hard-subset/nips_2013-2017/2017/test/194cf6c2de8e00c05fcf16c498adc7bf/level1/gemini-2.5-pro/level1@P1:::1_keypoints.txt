- Introduces EYMOL: a physics-inspired variational model that generates scanpaths by minimizing an action via a Lagrangian (K − U) instead of predicting static saliency maps.
- Defines potential U with three principles: boundedness (image confinement), curiosity (attraction to image gradients with oscillation between fine/coarse details), and brightness invariance (optical-flow constraint promoting contour fixation and tracking).
- Derives Euler–Lagrange equations to obtain second-order differential dynamics for scanpaths; saliency maps emerge by aggregating simulated dwell times.
- Technically sound derivations with detailed appendix; model connects theory to algorithm and extensive experiments.
- Empirical results are competitive on standard image (MIT300, CAT2000) and video (SFU) benchmarks using established metrics.
- Treats saliency as an emergent property and models dynamics (exploration, fixation, tracking) within a single principled framework, offering a novel alternative to data-driven deep models.
- Originality is high: applying variational physics-style methods to attentional dynamics is novel and conceptually elegant.
- Model is biologically plausible, generative, and has potential real-time applicability for robotics and active vision.
- Paper is generally clear and well organized, with core principles explained in Section 2.1.
- Reproducibility issues: several key hyperparameters are not reported (ηb, ηp, λ from simulated annealing).
- Additional missing implementation details: mass m, boundary potential constant k, curiosity oscillation frequency ω, and Gaussian kernel size for peripheral blur.
- The center-bias Gaussian blob appears ad-hoc and is not justified by the core variational theory; parameters/justification are unclear.
- Limitation: model addresses only bottom-up attention and does not incorporate top-down or task-driven influences.
- Pro-acceptance points: high originality, conceptual unification of attentional behaviors, strong empirical validation, and direct generation of dynamic scanpaths.
- Contra-acceptance points: reproducibility gaps, ad-hoc center bias weakening first-principles claim, and limited scope regarding top-down attention.
- Conclusion: strong, innovative paper with fixable clarity/implementation issues; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
