This paper proposes a novel paradigm for modeling visual attention based on the principle of least action. The authors devise a variational approach to model the eye-movement scanpath, incorporating three fundamental principles: boundedness of the trajectory, curiosity-driven principle, and brightness invariance. The model, referred to as EYe MOvement Laws (EYMOL), is derived using the Euler-Lagrange equations, resulting in a system of two differential equations that describe the visual attention scanpath.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the model and its components. The mathematical derivations are thorough and well-documented in the Appendix. The experimental setup is well-described, and the results are presented in a clear and easy-to-understand manner.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a unified interpretation of curiosity-driven movements and brightness invariance term for fixation and tracking. The model's performance on both image and video datasets of saliency is promising, especially considering that the proposed theory offers a truly model of eye movements, whereas the computation of the saliency maps only arises as a byproduct.

However, there are some limitations and potential areas for improvement. Firstly, the model's parameters are estimated using simulated annealing, which may not be the most efficient method, especially considering the large number of images used in the training dataset. Secondly, the model's performance on dynamic scenes is not as strong as on static images, which may indicate that the model needs to be adapted or extended to better handle temporal information.

In terms of related work, the authors provide a thorough review of the state-of-the-art in visual attention modeling, highlighting the differences between their approach and existing methods. However, it would be beneficial to include a more detailed comparison with other models, especially those that also use variational approaches or incorporate similar principles.

The paper's clarity is good, and the writing is clear and concise. However, there are some minor issues with formatting and typography, such as inconsistent spacing between sections and equations.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and promising approach to modeling visual attention. While there are some limitations and areas for improvement, the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the problem and provide a thorough and well-organized presentation of their work.
