This paper proposes a novel model of visual attention based on a variational approach, drawing parallels to the least action principle in physics. The core idea is that scanpaths emerge from foundational laws governing attention, specifically boundedness, curiosity, and brightness invariance. The work formulates a Lagrangian and derives differential equations to describe attention dynamics, validated through saliency detection experiments on images and videos.

The project’s strength lies in its ambitious attempt to ground visual attention in a principled, physics-inspired framework. The connection to the least action principle is interesting and potentially insightful. The explicit formulation of the Lagrangian, encompassing terms for boundedness, curiosity, and brightness invariance, provides a clear and mathematically tractable model. The inclusion of brightness invariance, particularly as a mechanism for fixation, is a notable contribution. The experimental validation, though not outperforming state-of-the-art by a large margin, demonstrates the model’s capability to predict saliency, both in static images and dynamic scenes. The use of simulated annealing for parameter estimation is a reasonable approach.

However, several weaknesses require addressing. While the analogy to physics is appealing, the paper lacks a deeper justification for *why* these particular physical principles should govern visual attention. What cognitive or neural mechanisms underlie this connection? The paper gestures towards newborns exhibiting similar mechanisms but doesn't fully develop this line of reasoning. The description of the curiosity-driven principle feels somewhat ad-hoc – the specific form of the `C(t,x)` term lacks a strong rationale beyond fulfilling the desired behavior. The parameter estimation process, while described, lacks detail regarding sensitivity analysis. How robust are the results to changes in the initial parameter values or the SA parameters? The datasets used (MIT300, CAT2000, SFU) are standard, but the comparisons to other state-of-the-art methods could be more comprehensive, especially considering recent advancements in deep learning-based saliency models. The paper claims the saliency map computation is a byproduct of the scanpath model, which is a valid point, but further exploration of the relationship between the scanpath and the resulting saliency map could be beneficial.  The discussion of the approximation made in version V1 (eq. 6) lacks a detailed analysis of its impact on the results beyond stating statistical equivalence. Further, the claim of reaching "best score in half of the cases" is vague – specifying which metrics and datasets are considered is crucial. The appendices contain the mathematical derivations but are not always clearly connected to the main text, potentially hindering reproducibility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and potentially insightful framework for modeling visual attention.
*   Clear mathematical formulation and derivation of the model.
*   Experimental validation on standard datasets.
*   The integration of brightness invariance into the model is a strong point.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of strong justification for the chosen physical principles.
*   Ad-hoc nature of some model components (e.g., the curiosity term).
*   Limited discussion of parameter sensitivity and robustness.
*   Comparisons to state-of-the-art could be more comprehensive.
*   Insufficient detail in the discussion of the approximation made for static images.



**Recommendations:**

*   **Essential:** Provide a more compelling justification for grounding the model in physical principles. Explore the cognitive and neural underpinnings of this analogy.
*   **Essential:** Conduct a thorough sensitivity analysis of the parameter estimation process.
*   **Important:** Expand the comparison to state-of-the-art saliency models, especially those based on deep learning.
*   **Important:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of the impact of the approximation made for static images and its influence on the results.
*   **Nice-to-have:** Elaborate on the relationship between the scanpath and the resulting saliency map.  Explore how changes in the scanpath influence the saliency map, and vice versa.
*   **Nice-to-have:** Improve the clarity of the connection between the main text and the appendices, ensuring reproducibility.



Overall, the paper presents an interesting and innovative approach to modeling visual attention. However, addressing the identified weaknesses is crucial to strengthening the contribution and increasing its impact. The paper is currently borderline, but with significant revisions addressing the points above, it could be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
