This paper proposes EYMOL, a novel, physics-inspired model for generating visual attention scanpaths. The central claim is that scanpaths emerge from a variational principle, akin to the Principle of Least Action in mechanics. The model defines a Lagrangian where the kinetic energy is standard, while the potential energy uniquely combines three principles: a bounding force to keep attention on the "retina," a "curiosity" term that drives attention toward high-gradient details, and a brightness invariance term, borrowed from optical flow literature, to model fixation and object tracking. By finding the stationary point of this action, the authors derive the Euler-Lagrange equations of motion that directly generate a scanpath. This approach is a significant departure from mainstream saliency models (e.g., Itti et al., or modern CNNs), which typically compute a static saliency map first and then simulate a scanpath over it. Here, the saliency map is an emergent property derived from the scanpath's trajectory over time.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this work is its conceptual novelty. Framing attention dynamics within the elegant mathematical formalism of variational principles is a fresh and compelling perspective. It offers a unified theory that can potentially model both saccadic movements (driven by "curiosity") and smooth pursuit/fixation (driven by "brightness invariance") within a single set of differential equations. This is a significant theoretical contribution to the field.
*   **Technical Quality:** The model is technically sound, with a clear derivation of the governing equations from first principles. The experimental evaluation is solid, demonstrating that this principled approach achieves competitive, and in some cases superior, performance on standard image and video saliency benchmarks against well-established models.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Evaluation Mismatch:** The model’s core contribution is the generation of dynamic scanpaths, yet the entire quantitative evaluation rests on aggregate, static saliency maps. The paper would be substantially stronger if it included a direct comparison of the generated scanpaths (e.g., saccade amplitudes, fixation durations, temporal ordering) with human scanpath data using appropriate metrics.
*   **Simplicity of Features:** The model is entirely data-driven by low-level brightness gradients. While effective, it ignores the well-documented influence of other features like color, orientation, and higher-level semantics on human attention, which limits its explanatory power as a complete model of attention.
*   **Clarity on Reproducibility:** While the mathematical derivation is present, more practical details regarding the numerical integration method and the hyperparameter search space for the simulated annealing would improve the paper's reproducibility.

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents a highly original and thought-provoking model of visual attention. Its main weakness is the disconnect between its primary claim (modeling scanpaths) and its evaluation metric (saliency maps). However, the strength and elegance of the theoretical framework, coupled with strong empirical results, make this a valuable contribution. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors acknowledge this evaluation gap and consider adding scanpath-based analysis in a future version.
