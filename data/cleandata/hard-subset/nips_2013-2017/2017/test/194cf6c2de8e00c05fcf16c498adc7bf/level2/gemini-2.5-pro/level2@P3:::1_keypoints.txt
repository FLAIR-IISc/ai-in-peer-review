- Introduces EYMOL, a principled variational model of scanpath generation using a generalized Principle of Least Action.  
- Represents scanpaths as particle trajectories minimizing an action; Lagrangian = kinetic energy + generalized potential.  
- Potential includes three components: bounding (keep within visual field), curiosity (attracts high-gradient regions with foveal/peripheral alternation), and brightness invariance (optical-flow-like).  
- Brightness invariance term explains both static-edge fixation and dynamic object tracking; approximated pragmatically for static images.  
- Derives Euler–Lagrange equations yielding second-order ODEs that govern eye-movement dynamics.  
- Generates saliency maps as an emergent property by simulating many scanpaths.  
- Shows competitive performance on MIT300, CAT2000 (images) and SFU (videos) without end-to-end learning.  
- Technical derivations are clear and correct; experiments are solid and reproducible (appendix derivations helpful).  
- Strength: unifies saccadic exploration and object tracking within a single, coherent mathematical framework.  
- Weakness: missing ablation study to quantify contributions of each potential component.  
- Weakness: lacks parameter sensitivity analysis (ηb, ηp, λ) beyond a single simulated-annealing configuration.  
- Clarify the rationale for the sinusoidal modulation in the curiosity term (biological/cognitive justification).  
- Include qualitative examples (e.g., scanpaths overlaid on video frames) to visually support object-tracking claims.  
- Originality is very high—novel application of Lagrangian/Least Action principles to visual attention.  
- Significance: offers a parsimonious, interpretable framework with potential impact for neuroscience, vision research, and robotics; overall recommendation positive with revisions suggested.
