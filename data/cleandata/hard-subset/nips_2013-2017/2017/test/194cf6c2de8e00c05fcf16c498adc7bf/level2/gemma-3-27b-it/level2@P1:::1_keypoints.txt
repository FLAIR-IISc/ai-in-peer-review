- Proposes a novel variational model of visual attention using the Least Action Principle: scanpaths minimize an action with potential (image detail + peripheral info), kinetic energy (eye movement), and brightness invariance.  
- Derives differential equations for attention dynamics and uses simulated annealing for parameter estimation, validating on static and dynamic saliency tasks.  
- Addresses an important problem and offers a principled alternative to purely feature- or learning-based attention models.  
- The connection to the Least Action Principle is interesting and potentially fruitful for modeling attention.  
- Inclusion of brightness invariance is insightful and may explain fixations on object boundaries.  
- Experimental validation across multiple datasets is commendable and shows competitive performance.  
- Weak justification for the chosen potential energy terms (combining local gradient magnitude and blurred version); lacks physical/neurological/psychological grounding.  
- Simulated annealing description is too brief: missing search space definition, temperature schedule, convergence criteria, and rationale for using only 100 CAT2000-TRAIN images.  
- Performance comparisons are superficial; lacks analysis of when/why the model excels or fails and unclear metric for the “best score in half of the cases.”  
- Appendix mathematical derivations are dense with difficult notation; needs more intermediate steps and clearer links to the paper’s intuition.  
- Insufficient discussion of parameter estimation difficulties, computational cost, and scalability to larger scenes or real-time use.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theory-grounded approach, physics connection, brightness invariance, and multi-dataset competitive results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited justification of model choices, insufficient SA details, shallow performance analysis, dense appendix, and missing scalability discussion.  
- Overall assessment/recommendation: promising but requires major revisions; reviewer recommends weak reject pending improvements in clarity, justification, and empirical analysis.
