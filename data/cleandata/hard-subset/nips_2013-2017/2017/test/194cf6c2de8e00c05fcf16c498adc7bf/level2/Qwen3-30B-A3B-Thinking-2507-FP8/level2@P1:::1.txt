

This paper proposes a novel variational framework for modeling visual attention scanpaths, inspired by the Least Action Principle in physics. The authors derive differential equations of attention dynamics by formulating a generalized Lagrangian that incorporates three key principles: (i) bounded trajectory via harmonic oscillator constraints, (ii) curiosity-driven attention via gradient-based detail detection, and (iii) brightness invariance for fixation/tracking. The model, termed EYMOL, produces saliency maps as a byproduct of simulating scanpaths, contrasting with conventional saliency models that directly optimize for saliency maps. The approach is evaluated on static image (MIT1003, CAT2000) and dynamic video (SFU) datasets using standard metrics (NSS, AUC).

**Strengths**  
1. **Originality**: The physics-inspired variational formulation is a compelling departure from prevailing saliency models (e.g., Itti-Koch, CNN-based methods). Framing attention as a trajectory governed by Euler-Lagrange equations offers a unified theoretical foundation for both curiosity-driven exploration and brightness-invariance-based fixation—a novel perspective not captured in prior work.  
2. **Technical Soundness**: The derivation is rigorous (Appendix provides detailed Euler-Lagrange equations), and the model’s simplicity (only three parameters) is well-justified. The approximation for brightness invariance (Eq. 6) is theoretically grounded (parallelogram law) and validated empirically (Table 2 shows negligible performance difference between V1/V2).  
3. **Empirical Results**: On static images, EYMOL achieves competitive scores (top-3 on MIT300 for NSS, top-2 on CAT2000 for AUC-Judd), outperforming classic models (Itti, Bruce) and matching modern approaches (Judd, SaliencyNet). For video, it competes with state-of-the-art methods on SFU (Table 4), demonstrating real-time applicability and emergent object tracking.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Limited Comparison to Recent SOTA**: The paper compares against 2010–2014 models (e.g., Itti, Judd) but omits recent 2016–2017 CNN-based methods (e.g., [26], [24] cited but not evaluated). For instance, Vig et al. (CVPR 2014) and Cornia et al. (arXiv 2016) achieved higher AUC on CAT2000—this gap needs addressing to contextualize significance.  
2. **Parameter Estimation Scale**: Simulated annealing uses only 100 images (5 per CAT2000 category) for parameter tuning. This risks overfitting to a small sample; larger validation (e.g., 500 images) would strengthen claims.  
3. **Dynamic Scene Analysis**: Video results (SFU) lack statistical significance testing (e.g., p-values for metric differences) and fail to discuss failure cases (e.g., complex motion). The "object tracking" claim is vague—showing a few examples would be more convincing than stating it as a general capability.  

**Constructive Suggestions**  
- **Expand SOTA Comparison**: Include recent CNN-based models (e.g., Vig et al. 2014, Cornia et al. 2016) in Table 3 to clarify where EYMOL stands relative to current best practices.  
- **Strengthen Validation**: Report confidence intervals for NSS/AUC scores across all datasets and add statistical tests (e.g., paired t-tests) for significance.  
- **Clarify Video Contributions**: Specify *how* the model tracks objects (e.g., "fixation on moving regions with high gradient magnitude during brightness invariance") and include a qualitative example.  

**Overall Assessment**  
The paper presents a theoretically elegant and empirically viable model for visual attention. Its core strength—unifying curiosity and brightness invariance via variational principles—offers a fresh perspective with clear potential for integration with future learning frameworks (as noted in the conclusion). While the results are promising, the omission of recent SOTA comparisons slightly weakens the significance claim. With minor revisions to address parameter tuning robustness and comparative context, this work would make a strong contribution to NIPS. The approach is technically sound, clearly presented, and advances the field beyond incremental saliency map optimization.  

**Recommendation**: Accept (with minor revisions)
