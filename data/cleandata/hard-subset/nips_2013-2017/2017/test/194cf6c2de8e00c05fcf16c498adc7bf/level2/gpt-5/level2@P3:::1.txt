Summary
The paper proposes a principled, dynamical model of visual attention in which scanpaths emerge as solutions of Euler–Lagrange equations derived from a generalized least-action formulation. The Lagrangian combines: (i) a boundary potential to keep trajectories within the retinal/image domain; (ii) a “curiosity” potential that alternates between local detail (squared brightness gradient) and peripheral information (squared gradient of a blurred image) through a time-varying cos2/sin2 modulation; and (iii) a brightness-invariance (optical-flow) term encouraging ḃ = 0, argued to promote fixation on edges for static scenes and motion tracking in videos. Parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) are tuned via simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000 to maximize NSS. Saliency maps are obtained as byproducts by accumulating visitation counts from multiple simulated trajectories. Two variants are evaluated: an exact brightness-invariance term and a simplified upper bound for static images. Experiments on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video datasets report competitive saliency metrics against several classical and learning-based baselines.

Relation to prior work
The study relates to classic saliency-map frameworks (Itti–Koch [10], Bruce & Tsotsos [16], Itti & Baldi [17]) and modern learning-based saliency (e.g., Judd et al. [1], Vig et al. [17], Cornia et al. [21]). It also connects to eye-movement and saccade models (Le Meur & Liu [6]) and to the use of brightness constancy from optical flow (Horn & Schunck [18]) and its link to saliency (García-Díaz et al. [22]). Casting attention as a variational mechanics problem that unifies fixation and tracking is less explored in this literature and constitutes the key conceptual contribution.

Strengths
- Conceptually elegant unification: a compact variational formulation yields scanpaths, with saliency emerging naturally as visitation counts, rather than being explicitly optimized.
- Clear mathematical development: Euler–Lagrange derivations are presented with conditions for well-posedness (e.g., constraint on λ), and the approximation for static images is justified and empirically validated.
- Interpretable forces: the roles of curiosity and brightness invariance are transparent, offering insight into saccades vs. pursuit within a single framework.
- Empirical scope: results reported on standard image (MIT, CAT2000) and video (SFU) datasets; competitive performance despite model simplicity.
- Practicality: reported ability to simulate scanpaths in real time.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Kinetic energy inconsistency: K is defined as (1/m) ẋ2, but subsequent derivations behave as if K = (m/2) ẋ2 (yielding ∂K/∂ẋ = m ẋ). This should be corrected and units/scaling clarified; conclusions may depend on the effective mass term and thus on λ constraints.
- Center bias and initialization: the model multiplies inputs by a central Gaussian, initializes trajectories near the center, and includes a boundary potential. These design choices can inflate AUC-type metrics. sAUC/IG should be emphasized, and ablations without explicit center bias and with broader initializations should be reported.
- Limited baselines: comparisons omit several 2015–2017 state-of-the-art deep saliency models on MIT300/CAT2000 (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON, MLNet, SAM-ResNet). Including these or referencing leaderboard numbers would better situate performance.
- Parameterization and sensitivity: the choice and range of ω, k, m, ηb, ηp, λ are only partially specified; sensitivity analyses and final selected values are missing. The alternating curiosity term via cos2/sin2 lacks empirical grounding and its frequency is not analyzed.
- Color neglected: all inputs are grayscale; color-opponent features are known to matter for human fixations. The impact of excluding color is not assessed.
- Video evaluation is mostly saliency-centric: the tracking claim is qualitative. No scanpath-level comparisons to human sequences (e.g., MultiMatch) or motion-alignment analyses (e.g., relation to optical flow) are provided.
- Reproducibility and details: no code link is provided; numerical integration method, step sizes, runtime, and stability behavior under different parameter regimes are not reported.

Quality
The theoretical development is mostly sound and insightful, with one notable inconsistency in the kinetic energy term that should be addressed. The approximation for static images is well motivated and empirically close to the exact form. Experimental methodology is reasonable but would benefit from stronger controls for center bias, more comprehensive baselines, and additional analyses supporting the tracking claim.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The intuition behind each term in the Lagrangian is clear. Some implementation details are sparse (parameter ranges, ω selection, integrator settings). Explicitly listing final parameter values and providing a brief runtime/stability report would improve clarity and reproducibility.

Originality
Framing attention dynamics via a generalized least-action principle with a brightness-invariance term is novel relative to common saliency-map pipelines and typical dynamical saccade models. The alternation between local and peripheral gradients is an interesting, albeit heuristic, addition.

Significance
If strengthened empirically, the approach could influence how attention models generate scanpaths and integrate fixation and pursuit. Even if not surpassing top deep saliency models across the board, the interpretability and efficiency make it a useful complement and a potential building block.

Recommended improvements (prioritized)
Essential:
- Fix the kinetic energy definition and propagate corrections through the derivations; report the final, consistent parameterization (m, λ) and their units.
- Add center-bias–robust metrics (sAUC, IG) and ablations without explicit center bias and with diverse initializations; report sensitivity to ω, ηb/ηp/λ.
- Include stronger baselines from the 2015–2017 MIT300/CAT2000 leaderboards or reference official scores where re-running is impractical.

Valuable but optional for camera-ready:
- Extend curiosity to color-opponent gradients or report the effect of adding color channels.
- Provide scanpath-level evaluations against human data and quantitative motion-tracking analyses in video.
- Release code and parameter configurations; document numerical integrator, step size, runtime, and stability behavior.

Overall assessment
An elegant and promising contribution that offers a principled dynamical perspective on attention with competitive results given its simplicity. With corrections to the mechanics, stronger bias controls, and more comprehensive comparisons, the work would be substantially strengthened and of broad interest.
