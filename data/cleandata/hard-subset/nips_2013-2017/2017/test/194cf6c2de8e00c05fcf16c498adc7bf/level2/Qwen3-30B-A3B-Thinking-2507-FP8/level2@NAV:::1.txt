

This paper proposes a novel variational model of visual attention derived from a generalized Least Action Principle, framing eye movements as trajectories minimizing a Lagrangian incorporating curiosity-driven detail-seeking (via gradient magnitude) and brightness invariance (for fixation/fixation tracking). The core claim is that these principles unify bottom-up attention dynamics, yielding differential equations for scanpaths whose saliency maps emerge as a byproduct.  

**Strengths:** The physics-inspired formulation offers an elegant theoretical framework distinct from conventional saliency models (e.g., Itti & Koch, 2000; Judd et al., 2009). The model generates real-time scanpaths and produces competitive saliency predictions on MIT1003/MIT300/CAT2000 (Table 2) and SFU video data (Table 4), with V1 (approximated brightness invariance) performing robustly. The derivation of Euler-Lagrange equations (Appendix) is technically sound, and the use of simulated annealing for parameter tuning is appropriate.  

**Weaknesses:** Critical gaps undermine the claims. First, the SOTA comparison (Table 2) excludes recent deep learning models (e.g., Vig et al., 2014; Cornia et al., 2016), which dominate current saliency benchmarks—only older methods like [10,16,1] are used. Second, experimental validation lacks statistical significance testing (e.g., p-values for metric improvements), and the "byproduct" saliency claim is under-supported since most SOTA models directly optimize saliency. Third, the brightness invariance term’s application to video (SFU) is superficially justified without analyzing *why* it works for dynamic scenes. The paper also overlooks key recent work on attention dynamics (e.g., [28] in references), weakening novelty claims.  

**Constructive Suggestions:** 1) Expand comparisons to include top-performing deep models (e.g., [18,24]), reporting metrics like AUC-Judd and NSS. 2) Add statistical tests for performance differences and analyze failure cases (e.g., cluttered scenes where model diverges from human fixations). 3) Clarify the theoretical advantage over existing scanpath models (e.g., [6])—does the variational approach enable tracking beyond [6]’s biological biases?  

**Verdict:** The theoretical insight is intriguing and well-executed mathematically, but insufficient empirical validation against current SOTA limits significance. The paper would strengthen considerably with broader comparisons and rigorous statistical analysis. *Recommendation: Weak Reject (due to inadequate SOTA benchmarking and missing statistical validation).*
