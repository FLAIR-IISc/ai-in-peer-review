1. Proposes a variational framework for visual attention, modeling eye movements via Euler–Lagrange equations from a generalized action functional with three components: boundedness, curiosity (gradient magnitude), and brightness invariance.  
2. Generates saliency maps as a byproduct of simulated scanpaths, avoiding explicit saliency-map construction.  
3. Evaluated on MIT1003, CAT2000 (images) and SFU (video); results competitive and top-ranked on half the metrics versus five baselines.  
4. Conceptually original: unifies attention dynamics under a physics-inspired variational principle bridging mechanics and cognitive science.  
5. Technical derivations (Appendix) are sound; brightness invariance theoretically supports fixation/tracking via optical-flow-like constraints.  
6. Real-time scanpath generation is a practical advantage over training-intensive deep learning approaches.  
7. Novelty claim needs stronger contextualization and explicit contrast with prior dynamics-based and neural scanpath models (e.g., LSTM-based [22]).  
8. Lacks an ablation study to isolate the individual contributions of curiosity, brightness invariance, and boundedness.  
9. Video “object tracking” claims are unsupported by qualitative examples or per-clip tracking analyses—only aggregate metrics are provided.  
10. Fixed parameters across datasets may limit adaptability to diverse scenes, but this limitation is not discussed.  
11. Recommended revisions: clarify novelty vs prior work, add ablation experiments, include qualitative SFU scanpath examples, and expand baselines to recent methods (e.g., 2017 LSTM-based models).  
12. Overall assessment: significant theoretical contribution with competitive empirical results and practical strengths; minor revisions required before final acceptance.
