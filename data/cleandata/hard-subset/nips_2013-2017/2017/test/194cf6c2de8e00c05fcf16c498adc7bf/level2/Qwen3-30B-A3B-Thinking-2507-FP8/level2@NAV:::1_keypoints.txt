- Proposes a variational model of visual attention from a generalized Least Action Principle, modeling eye movements as Lagrangian-minimizing trajectories with curiosity (gradient magnitude) and brightness invariance terms.  
- Claims saliency maps emerge as a byproduct while generating differential-equation-based scanpaths.  
- Strength: Elegant, physics-inspired theoretical framework distinct from conventional saliency models.  
- Strength: Produces real-time scanpaths and competitive saliency results on MIT1003/MIT300/CAT2000 and SFU video; V1 (brightness invariance) performs robustly.  
- Strength: Derivation of Euler–Lagrange equations is technically sound; simulated annealing for parameter tuning is appropriate.  
- Weakness: SOTA comparison omits recent deep learning models that dominate current benchmarks, using only older methods.  
- Weakness: Experimental validation lacks statistical significance testing (no p-values reported).  
- Weakness: The claim that saliency is a “byproduct” is under-supported since most SOTA models directly optimize saliency.  
- Weakness: Application of the brightness invariance term to video is superficially justified without analysis of why it works in dynamic scenes.  
- Weakness: Overlooks recent work on attention dynamics, weakening the novelty claim.  
- Suggestion: Expand comparisons to include top-performing deep models and report standard metrics (e.g., AUC-Judd, NSS).  
- Suggestion: Add statistical tests for performance differences and analyze failure cases (e.g., cluttered scenes).  
- Suggestion: Clarify theoretical advantages over existing scanpath models (e.g., does the variational approach enable tracking beyond known biological biases?).  
- Verdict: Intriguing and well-executed theoretically but insufficient empirical validation against current SOTA; recommendation: Weak Reject.
