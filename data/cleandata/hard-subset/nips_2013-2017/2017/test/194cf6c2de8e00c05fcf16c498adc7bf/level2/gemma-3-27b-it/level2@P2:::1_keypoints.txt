- Proposes a novel variational model of visual attention that models scanpaths by minimizing an action functional (potential + kinetic terms) and derives Euler–Lagrange dynamics.  
- Validates the model on static image and video saliency datasets (MIT, CAT2000, SFU) showing competitive results.  
- Positions the approach against global saliency-map methods and emphasizes emergent attention from fundamental principles.  
- Connects visual attention modeling to the Least Action Principle from physics, offering a strong theoretical grounding.  
- Integrates concepts like trajectory boundedness, curiosity (gradient-driven attraction), and brightness invariance within a unified framework.  
- Mathematical derivations are clear, with useful Appendix details on the Euler–Lagrange formulation.  
- The Least Action Principle connection is interesting but its biological plausibility and neural implementation are not convincingly argued.  
- Parameter estimation (simulated annealing) appears complex and potentially sensitive to initialization and parameter ranges.  
- The brightness invariance approximation (Eq. 6) may reduce accuracy, especially in dynamic scenes with illumination changes.  
- The curiosity term based on gradient magnitude is simplistic and omits richer contextual or feature-based cues humans use.  
- Comparisons to state-of-the-art deep learning saliency models are limited and should be expanded.  
- Paper is technically sound and experimentally well-defined, but robustness and biological relevance need further analysis.  
- Writing and organization are generally good, though mathematical notation could be made more accessible.  
- The variational/Least Action approach is highly original in the attention literature.  
- If limitations are addressed, the work could significantly impact fundamental understanding and practical vision systems.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, theoretical foundation, competitive experimental performance, and clear mathematical formulation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: questionable biological plausibility, parameter sensitivity, limited SOTA comparisons, and simplistic curiosity modeling.  
- Overall recommendation: weak accept—worth publishing for originality and potential, but authors should address biological plausibility, robustness, and comparative evaluation.
