- Proposes a principled dynamical model of visual attention where scanpaths arise as Euler–Lagrange solutions from a generalized least-action formulation.  
- Lagrangian combines a boundary potential, a time-varying “curiosity” potential (local vs. peripheral gradients via cos^2/sin^2 modulation), and a brightness-invariance (optical-flow) term.  
- Parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) tuned by simulated annealing on CAT2000 subset; saliency maps produced by accumulating visitation counts from simulated trajectories.  
- Two model variants: exact brightness-invariance and a simplified static-image upper bound.  
- Evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video datasets with competitive saliency metrics against several baselines.  

- Relates to classic saliency-map frameworks (Itti–Koch, Bruce & Tsotsos) and modern learning-based saliency; links to saccade/pursuit models and optical-flow literature.  
- Novel contribution: casting attention as a variational mechanics problem that unifies fixation and tracking.  

- Strength: conceptually elegant unification producing scanpaths and saliency as byproducts rather than optimized outputs.  
- Strength: clear mathematical derivation with well-posedness conditions and justified static-image approximation.  
- Strength: interpretable forces clarifying saccade vs. pursuit behavior within one framework.  
- Strength: empirical scope across image and video datasets with competitive performance given model simplicity.  
- Strength: practical — reported ability to simulate scanpaths in real time.  

- Weakness: inconsistency in kinetic energy definition (K specified as (1/m) ẋ^2 but used as (m/2) ẋ^2), affecting derivatives, scaling, and λ constraints.  
- Weakness: explicit center bias (central Gaussian weighting, center initialization, boundary potential) may inflate AUC metrics; sAUC/IG and ablations needed.  
- Weakness: comparisons omit several 2015–2017 deep saliency state-of-the-art models (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON, MLNet, SAM-ResNet).  
- Weakness: incomplete parameterization and sensitivity analysis — ranges and selection for ω, k, m, ηb, ηp, λ are not fully reported and curiosity frequency not analyzed.  
- Weakness: color information ignored despite known importance for fixation; impact of grayscale only not assessed.  
- Weakness: video evaluation focused on saliency metrics; tracking claims lack scanpath-level quantitative comparisons and motion-alignment analyses.  
- Weakness: reproducibility issues — no code, missing numerical integrator details, step sizes, runtimes, and stability reports.  

- Quality: theoretical development mostly sound aside from kinetic energy inconsistency; static-image approximation is well motivated and empirically close.  
- Clarity: paper is generally well written with clear Lagrangian intuitions, but implementation and parameter details are sparse.  
- Originality: novel framing of attention via least-action with brightness-invariance and alternating curiosity; the alternation is heuristic but interesting.  
- Significance: interpretable, efficient approach with potential impact if empirically strengthened and properly controlled for biases.  

- Essential improvements: correct kinetic energy and update derivations/parameterization; add center-bias–robust metrics (sAUC, IG) and ablations with diverse initializations; report sensitivity to ω, ηb/ηp/λ; include stronger baselines or leaderboard references.  
- Optional but valuable: extend curiosity to color-opponent gradients or report color effects; provide scanpath-level human comparisons and motion-tracking analyses for video; release code and full numerical/ runtime/ stability details.  

- Overall assessment: an elegant, promising, and interpretable dynamical attention model that requires corrections to mechanics, stronger bias controls, and more comprehensive comparisons to be broadly compelling.
