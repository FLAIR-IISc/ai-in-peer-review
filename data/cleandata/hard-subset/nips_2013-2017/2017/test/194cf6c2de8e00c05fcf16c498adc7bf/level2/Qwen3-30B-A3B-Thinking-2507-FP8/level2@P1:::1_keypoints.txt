- Proposal: Introduces EYMOL, a variational framework modeling scanpaths via Euler–Lagrange dynamics inspired by the Least Action Principle, producing saliency maps as a byproduct.  
- Core model principles: bounded trajectories (harmonic oscillator), curiosity-driven attention (gradient-based detail detection), and brightness invariance for fixation/tracking.  
- Evaluation: Tested on MIT1003, CAT2000 (static) and SFU (video) with standard metrics (NSS, AUC).  

Strengths
- Originality: Physics-inspired variational formulation unifies exploration and fixation in a novel theoretical framework.  
- Technical soundness: Rigorous derivation, simple three-parameter model, and validated brightness-invariance approximation.  
- Empirical results: Competitive performance on static benchmarks (top-3/top-2 ranks) and real-time video applicability with emergent tracking behavior.  

Weaknesses
- Limited SOTA comparison: Omits recent 2016–2017 CNN-based methods, weakening contextual significance.  
- Parameter tuning scale: Simulated annealing uses only 100 images, risking overfitting to a small validation set.  
- Video analysis gaps: Lacks statistical significance testing, failure-case discussion, and concrete examples for the claimed object-tracking behavior.  

Suggestions
- Expand comparisons to recent CNN-based SOTA (e.g., Vig 2014, Cornia 2016) in results tables.  
- Strengthen validation with larger tuning sets, confidence intervals, and statistical tests (e.g., paired t-tests).  
- Clarify and illustrate video/object-tracking mechanisms with qualitative examples.  

Overall
- Assessment: Theoretically elegant and empirically viable contribution that advances attention modeling beyond saliency-map optimization.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address comparative context and validation robustness.
