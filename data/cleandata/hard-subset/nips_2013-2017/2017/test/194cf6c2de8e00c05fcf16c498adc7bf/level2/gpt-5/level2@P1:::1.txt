Summary
This paper proposes a dynamical model of visual attention in dynamic scenes grounded in a variational formulation. The authors posit that scanpaths arise as stationary points of a generalized action functional, combining: (i) a boundary “potential” that constrains trajectories within the retinal field; (ii) a curiosity-driven term that alternates between local detail (image gradient magnitude) and peripheral (blurred) features via a time-varying modulation; and (iii) a brightness invariance term akin to the optical flow constraint to encourage fixation and motion tracking. The resulting Euler–Lagrange equations yield a second-order ODE governing the focus-of-attention. Parameters are tuned via simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a subset of CAT2000. Saliency maps are obtained as a byproduct by counting visits of multiple simulated trajectories, with additional post-processing (center bias and blur) selected by grid search. Experiments on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and the SFU video dataset show the model performs competitively on several metrics; the approximation of the brightness-invariance term performs on par with the exact form for static images.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A unified, physics-inspired variational framework that naturally yields scanpaths, fixations, and (in dynamic scenes) tracking behavior is original and interesting. It offers a compact model that contrasts with prevalent saliency-map–first approaches.
- Clear derivation: The appendix provides a reasonably thorough derivation of the Euler–Lagrange equations, including conditions for well-posedness (determinant positivity and λ bounds).
- Practicality: The model is simple to integrate numerically and can run in real time; the approximation of the brightness term for static images is supported empirically and reduces complexity.
- Empirical scope: Evaluation on both static images and videos is welcome; use of MIT300 official benchmarking indicates attention to fair testing.

Weaknesses
- Experimental confounds and limited ablations: Performance appears to rely substantially on strong center biases and blur added both to the input (multiplying by a center Gaussian to attenuate periphery) and to the saliency maps via post-processing optimized on training data. This double center bias makes it hard to assess the intrinsic contribution of the proposed dynamics. Ablations removing (a) input-level center bias, (b) map-level blur/center bias, (c) the curiosity term, and (d) the brightness term are needed. Reporting sAUC/shuffled-AUC is important to control for center bias.
- Incomplete comparisons: The comparisons omit several strong deep baselines available by 2016–2017 (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON, DeepFix/MLNet). For videos, comparisons to contemporary dynamic saliency models are limited. Without these, the claim of state-of-the-art competitiveness is difficult to evaluate.
- Parameter tuning and overfitting risk: Simulated annealing and grid search are optimized for specific metrics on subsets; the search space, schedule, acceptance probability, and selected configurations are not fully specified. This raises questions about robustness and generalization across datasets and metrics.
- Heuristic design elements: The alternating curiosity term using cos^2/sin^2(ωt) between local and peripheral gradients is intriguing but ad hoc. The paper does not analyze sensitivity to ω or justify why this time modulation should arise from the proposed “laws.”
- Clarity and technical details: Several implementation details are missing or unconventional. For instance, the kinetic energy is written as K(ẋ) = (1/m)‖ẋ‖^2 rather than the standard (m/2)‖ẋ‖^2; units and implications for the λ < m/4 bound should be clarified. Numerical integration method, step sizes, initial condition distribution, and mass m/ω values are not specified. The cropping step to “the rectangular zone containing the totality of information” could inadvertently impose additional center bias.
- Behavioral validation: While the model purports to generate realistic scanpaths, there is no quantitative comparison to human scanpath statistics (e.g., saccade amplitude distributions, fixation durations, temporal correlations), nor to established dynamical models of saccade generation.

Quality
- The derivations are generally sound but include nonstandard choices (kinetic term) and a set of heuristic modeling decisions (time modulation, center bias) whose effects are not isolated.
- The empirical section demonstrates competitive results on some metrics but lacks robust baselines and ablations to convincingly attribute gains to the variational principles rather than post-processing and biases.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly well organized, with a useful appendix. However, important implementation details are omitted; notation has occasional typos; and several hyperparameters (m, ω, α in SA, domain D) and acceptance function P are unspecified. Figures illustrating trajectories and forces would help intuition.

Originality
- Framing attention dynamics via least action and explicitly incorporating an optical-flow–style constraint into the Lagrangian is novel within saliency modeling. Related dynamical and optimal control formulations (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler; Butko & Movellan; Engbert-type models) should be discussed and differentiated.

Significance
- The work could stimulate research on principled, dynamical models of attention that produce scanpaths and saliency as outputs. However, its immediate impact is tempered by reliance on heuristic biases, limited comparisons, and missing behavioral validation.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, unified variational formulation connecting curiosity, fixation, and tracking.
- Simple, interpretable dynamics with promising results on both static and dynamic datasets.
- Potentially valuable alternative to black-box deep saliency predictors and a basis for future extensions.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on center bias and post-processing; limited ablations and robustness analysis.
- Missing comparisons to several strong contemporary methods and lack of sAUC/shuffled-AUC results.
- Incomplete technical details (energy terms, parameterization, optimization) and heuristic design choices not justified empirically.
- No quantitative scanpath-level validation.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify the kinetic energy definition and units; if K should be (m/2)‖ẋ‖^2, how does this affect λ bounds and dynamics?
- What are the exact values/ranges for m, ω, λ, ηb, ηp, α (SA), and how are neighbors and acceptance probabilities defined?
- How sensitive are results to ω and to the alternation scheme (e.g., constant weights instead of cos^2/sin^2)?
- Can you report sAUC/shuffled-AUC and ablations removing input/map center bias?
- Can you provide scanpath-level evaluations and compare basic saccade statistics to human data?

Suggestions
- Add comprehensive ablations (remove/add each term; vary ω; remove center bias at input and output; report sAUC).
- Compare with strong deep baselines and dynamic saliency models from 2016–2017.
- Provide implementation details and release code for reproducibility.
- Include behavioral validation of scanpaths.
- Discuss related optimal control/active vision models and clarify distinctions.

Overall, this is an original and thought-provoking formulation with promise, but the current empirical and methodological gaps make it difficult to assess its true contributions. With stronger ablations, fairer evaluations, and clearer technical details, it could be a compelling contribution.
