This paper proposes a novel model of visual attention based on a variational approach inspired by the Least Action Principle in physics. The core idea is to formulate scanpaths as the result of minimizing an action functional, where potential energy captures image details and peripheral information, and kinetic energy represents eye movement. A brightness invariance term is also included. The authors present differential equations governing attention dynamics and an algorithm for parameter estimation via simulated annealing, validating their model on static and dynamic scene saliency detection tasks.

The paper tackles an important and challenging problem in computational vision. The connection to the Least Action Principle is an interesting and potentially fruitful avenue for modeling visual attention, offering a principled alternative to purely feature-based or learning-based approaches. The inclusion of brightness invariance is a particularly insightful addition, potentially explaining fixation behavior on object boundaries. The experimental validation on multiple datasets is commendable.

However, the paper suffers from a few weaknesses. While the theoretical framework is presented, the physical intuition behind the chosen potential energy terms (particularly the combination of local gradient magnitude and blurred version) is not fully explained. Why this particular combination? Is there a clear link to neurological or psychological findings regarding attention mechanisms? More justification and discussion would strengthen the rationale.

The description of the simulated annealing process is somewhat brief. While the algorithm is mentioned, details about the search space, temperature schedule, and convergence criteria are lacking. Including this information would improve reproducibility. It's also unclear why a relatively small batch of images (100 from CAT2000-TRAIN) is used for SA, while the final evaluation is performed on the full datasets. A justification for this choice is needed.

The performance comparisons to state-of-the-art methods are good, but the analysis could be more in-depth. A discussion of *why* the proposed model performs well or poorly compared to others would be valuable. For example, does it excel in specific types of scenes or scenarios? Also, the claim of reaching "best score in half of the cases" needs clarification – best according to which metric? A table summarizing the best score *per metric* would be more informative.

The appendix provides the mathematical derivations, which is a positive aspect, but the notation is dense and can be difficult to follow. A clearer presentation, perhaps with more intermediate steps or explanations, would enhance readability. The connection between the derived equations and the intuitive principles described in Section 2 is not always apparent.

Finally, while the authors acknowledge the limitations related to parameter estimation and computational cost, a more thorough discussion of these challenges is warranted. The scalability of the method to larger, more complex scenes or real-time applications is not addressed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically grounded approach to modeling visual attention.
*   Interesting connection to the Least Action Principle.
*   Inclusion of brightness invariance, potentially explaining important fixation behaviors.
*   Experimental validation on multiple datasets demonstrating competitive performance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited justification for the chosen potential energy terms.
*   Insufficient details regarding the simulated annealing process.
*   Superficial performance analysis.
*   Dense mathematical notation in the appendix.
*   Lack of discussion regarding scalability and computational cost.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising new approach to modeling visual attention. The theoretical framework is intriguing, and the experimental results are encouraging. However, several weaknesses, particularly regarding justification, clarity, and analysis, need to be addressed. I recommend **weak reject** with the expectation that the authors will significantly revise the paper to address the points raised in this review. A major revision would be required to increase the paper's clarity and provide further justification for the model's design choices and empirical performance.
