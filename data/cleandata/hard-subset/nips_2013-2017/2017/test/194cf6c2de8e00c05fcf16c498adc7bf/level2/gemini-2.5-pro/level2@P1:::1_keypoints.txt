- Introduces EYMOL, a generative scanpath model framing attention as a particle trajectory from a variational principle with a Lagrangian of kinetic + generalized potential terms.  
- Potential encodes three principles: boundary confinement, curiosity (image-gradient attraction with foveal/peripheral alternation), and brightness invariance (optical flow constraint).  
- Derives Euler–Lagrange second-order ODEs to generate scanpaths; saliency maps produced by aggregating simulated scanpath locations; parameters tuned via simulated annealing.  
- Evaluated on standard image (MIT300, CAT2000) and video (SFU) benchmarks, showing competitive saliency performance.  
- Quality: technically rigorous with detailed derivations, transparent methodology, and supported experimental claims.  
- Clarity: well-written and organized; motivations and math are clear; distinguishes generative scanpath modeling from direct saliency prediction.  
- Originality: novel and innovative application of variational/analytical mechanics to model saccades, fixations, and tracking in a unified framework.  
- Significance: proposes a true generative process model for visual exploration that could shift focus from pure prediction to underlying dynamics.  
- Weakness: Quantitative evaluation focuses on saliency maps rather than directly assessing generated scanpaths (saccade amplitudes, fixation durations, temporal order).  
- Weakness: Limited comparison to contemporary deep-learning saliency models—only one CNN included, lacking more recent state-of-the-art baselines.  
- Weakness: Some design choices (e.g., cos^2/sin^2 modulation) and several hyperparameters (m, k, ω) lack theoretical/empirical justification and sensitivity analysis.  
- Suggestions: Add direct scanpath-to-human comparisons using appropriate metrics and analyze sensitivity/justification of model components and hyperparameters.  
- Recommendation: Despite evaluation gaps, the paper’s originality, rigor, and promising results make it a valuable contribution and merit acceptance.
