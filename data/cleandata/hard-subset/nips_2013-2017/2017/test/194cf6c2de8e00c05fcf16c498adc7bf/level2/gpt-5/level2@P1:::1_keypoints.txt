- Proposes a variational, physics-inspired dynamical model of visual attention where scanpaths are stationary points of a generalized action functional.
- Action functional combines a boundary potential, a time-varying curiosity term (local vs peripheral), and a brightness-invariance (optical-flow–style) term; Euler–Lagrange yields a second-order ODE for attention.
- Parameters tuned via simulated annealing on CAT2000 subset; saliency maps produced by aggregating simulated trajectories with post-processing (center bias and blur).
- Evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video dataset; claims competitive performance and shows brightness-term approximation works for static images.
- Strength: original, unified variational framework that naturally yields scanpaths, fixations, and tracking behavior as opposed to saliency-map–first approaches.
- Strength: derivations are clear in the appendix with conditions for well-posedness (determinant positivity, λ bounds).
- Strength: numerically simple and real-time capable; brightness-term approximation reduces complexity.
- Strength: empirical evaluation covers both images and videos and includes MIT300 official benchmarking.
- Weakness: heavy reliance on double center biases (input-level and map-level blur/post-processing) that confound attribution of gains to the dynamics.
- Weakness: lacks crucial ablations (remove input center bias, map-level blur/center bias, curiosity term, brightness term) and lacks sAUC/shuffled-AUC reporting to control for center bias.
- Weakness: incomplete comparisons—omits strong deep baselines (DeepGaze II, SALICON, DeepFix/MLNet) and more video saliency contemporaries, weakening claims of competitiveness.
- Weakness: parameter tuning via simulated annealing and grid search is under-specified (search space, schedule, acceptance) raising overfitting/generalization concerns.
- Weakness: alternating curiosity term (cos^2/sin^2(ωt) scheme) is ad hoc without sensitivity analysis or theoretical justification for ω or the modulation form.
- Weakness: missing/unclear implementation details (nonstandard kinetic energy formulation, numerical integration, step sizes, initial condition distribution, m/ω values, cropping) that may affect reproducibility and introduce bias.
- Weakness: no quantitative behavioral validation—no scanpath-level comparisons to human saccade statistics or established dynamical saccade models.
- Quality: derivations are mostly sound but contain nonstandard modeling choices and heuristics whose effects are not isolated experimentally.
- Clarity: paper is mostly well organized with a useful appendix but omits many hyperparameters and has some notation typos; more figures of trajectories/forces would aid intuition.
- Originality: novel framing using least action and an optical-flow–style constraint, but needs clearer differentiation from related optimal-control/active-vision and dynamical models.
- Significance: could stimulate principled dynamical attention modeling, but immediate impact limited by heuristic biases, weak comparisons, and missing behavioral validation.
- Arguments for acceptance: elegant, interpretable variational formulation with promising image/video results and potential as an alternative to black-box deep predictors.
- Arguments against acceptance: overreliance on center bias and post-processing, insufficient ablations and baselines, incomplete technical details, and lack of scanpath validation.
- Key questions for authors: clarify kinetic energy definition and effect on λ bounds; provide exact parameter values/ranges and SA details; report sensitivity to ω and alternation scheme; provide sAUC and ablations removing center biases; supply scanpath-level evaluations.
- Suggested fixes: add comprehensive ablations and sAUC, compare to strong deep and dynamic baselines (2016–2017), release code and full implementation details, include behavioral scanpath validation, and discuss/contrast related optimal-control and active-vision models.
