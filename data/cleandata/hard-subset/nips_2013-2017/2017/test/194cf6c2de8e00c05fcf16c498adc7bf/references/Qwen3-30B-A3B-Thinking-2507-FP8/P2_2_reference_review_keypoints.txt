- Proposes EYMOL: a variational, physics-inspired framework for visual attention deriving dynamics from boundedness, curiosity (high gradients), and brightness invariance.  
- Models attention via a least-action principle yielding Euler–Lagrange differential equations; saliency maps emerge as a byproduct of simulated scanpaths.  
- Evaluated on static (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and dynamic (SFU) datasets and reports competitive results.  

- Mathematical derivation is rigorous and well-documented (detailed appendix).  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive across images and videos.  
- Authors compare model variants (with/without brightness invariance) and justify approximations for static images.  
- Paper acknowledges limitations like parameter tuning via simulated annealing and not being designed specifically for video saliency.  

- Does not compare against several recent deep-learning saliency methods (missing SOTA baselines).  
- Parameter estimation (simulated annealing) is ad hoc; sensitivity analysis and reproducibility details are lacking.  
- Claims of real-time performance are unsubstantiated due to missing timing and complexity data.  
- Theoretical motivation for specific Lagrangian terms (e.g., curiosity term form) is weak and could be better justified.  

- Paper is well-organized and clearly written; derivations are accessible to the audience.  
- Experimental setup (preprocessing, saliency computation, parameter estimation) is well-described.  
- Some technical details (e.g., peripheral input p(t,x)) need clearer explanation for non-experts.  
- Lacks qualitative analysis/examples of generated scanpaths to illustrate model behavior.  

- Using a variational principle to unify fixation and tracking is a novel and significant theoretical contribution.  
- Successfully integrates curiosity-driven attention and brightness invariance in one framework, shifting focus from static saliency maps to dynamic attention.  
- Approach differs fundamentally from centralized saliency-map models.  

- Needs clearer distinction from other physics-inspired vision approaches and stronger positioning against recent deep-learning paradigms.  
- The paper’s claimed contribution to understanding human attention is not clearly articulated beyond existing models.  
- Significance is weakened by omission of comparisons to the latest state-of-the-art methods.  

- Overall assessment: technically sound, novel theoretical contribution with promising results.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  

- Requested revisions: add comparisons to 2–3 recent deep-learning saliency methods; provide concrete timing/complexity numbers; include qualitative scanpath examples; expand parameter sensitivity analysis and justify estimation method; clarify theoretical connections to other physics-inspired models.
