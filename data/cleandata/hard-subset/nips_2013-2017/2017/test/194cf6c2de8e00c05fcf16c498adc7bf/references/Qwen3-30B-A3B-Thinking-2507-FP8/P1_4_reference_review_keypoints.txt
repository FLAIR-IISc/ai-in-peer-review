- Proposes a novel variational framework (EYMOL) modeling visual attention dynamics via a least-action principle and Euler-Lagrange equations.  
- Models eye movements as a particle in a potential field; saliency maps emerge from simulated scanpath dynamics rather than being predicted directly.  
- Incorporates three principles: bounded eye movements, curiosity-driven attention to detail-rich regions, and brightness invariance for fixation/tracking.  
- Evaluated on static (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and video (SFU) datasets, showing competitive performance against state-of-the-art.  
- Originality: highly novel physics-inspired approach that departs from typical static saliency models and unifies attention mechanisms theoretically.  
- Theoretical quality: sound mathematical derivation with clear Euler-Lagrange treatment and appropriate use of simulated annealing for parameter estimation.  
- Experimental quality: comprehensive evaluation across static and dynamic scenes; acknowledges limitations like brightness invariance approximations.  
- Significance: moderate–high—offers a fresh theoretical perspective that achieves competitive results without large training datasets.  
- Clarity: generally well-organized and well-written, though some appendix sections could use more intuitive explanations.  
- Weakness—empirical novelty: performance is modest compared to recent deep learning state-of-the-art and doesn’t clearly outperform leading DL models.  
- Weakness—biological plausibility: insufficient discussion/comparison to known neurobiological mechanisms and human saccade patterns.  
- Weakness—implementation: lacks details on computational efficiency, scalability, and substantiation of real-time claims.  
- Weakness—comparisons: limited benchmarking against the most recent (last 2–3 years) deep learning models such as transformer-based approaches.  
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretically novel, connects physics and attention, competitive without big data, unified static/dynamic model, technically sound.  
- Arguments against acceptance: only modest empirical gains, limited neuroscientific grounding, unclear advance beyond existing knowledge in practice.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to improve biological discussion, recent DL comparisons, computational efficiency details, and clarification of dynamic-scene limitations.
