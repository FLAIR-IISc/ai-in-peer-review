Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a dynamical, variational model of early visual attention (EYMOL) that generates scanpaths by minimizing an action functional. The Lagrangian combines: (i) a border “potential” enforcing boundedness on the retina; (ii) a curiosity term that alternates attraction to high local spatial gradients and to a blurred (peripheral) version of the image, via a cos^2/sin^2 temporal modulation; and (iii) a brightness-invariance term inspired by the optical flow constraint (bt + bx·ẋ ≈ 0), argued to support fixation and tracking. Euler–Lagrange equations yield second-order ODEs whose numerical integration produces trajectories; saliency maps are then obtained by visit counts over many runs. Parameters are set via simulated annealing using NSS on a small training subset. The model is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video, and reported to be competitive with several classic and some learning-based baselines.

This relates to classic saliency (Itti & Koch), information/surprise-based models (Bruce & Tsotsos; Itti & Baldi), scanpath simulators with heuristics (Le Meur & Liu), and optical-flow-based constraints in vision. The novelty is casting both “curiosity” and fixation/tracking as a unified least-action principle, with saliency a byproduct of simulated dynamics rather than the trained target.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The variational derivation is mostly coherent, and the resulting equations are carefully worked out in the appendix. However, key modeling choices are heuristic and under-justified:
  - The curiosity term C uses bx^2 and px^2 with a deterministic time modulation (cos^2/sin^2). The choice of ω, the alternation schedule, and why this form avoids trapping are not theoretically analyzed or empirically ablated.
  - The brightness-invariance term is treated as a “potential” dependent on velocity; that is acceptable in a generalized Lagrangian sense, but its consequences (e.g., stability, damping-like effects) are not analyzed.
  - There appears to be a mismatch in Eq. (8): K(ẋ) = 1/m ẋ^2, whereas the subsequent EL equations use mẍ as if K = (m/2) ẋ^2. This should be corrected/clarified.
- Empirical support: The paper reports competitive metrics, but the comparison set omits the strongest deep saliency models prevalent on MIT300/CAT2000 at the time (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON, SAM/MLNet variants). Without these, claims of competitiveness are hard to assess. No ablation is provided to quantify the contribution of each term (curiosity, brightness invariance, border potential) or the time-modulation. For videos, SFU is small (12 clips); tracking claims are anecdotal and not measured with scanpath/kinematic metrics.
- Evaluation design: The authors inject a strong center bias by multiplying inputs with a central Gaussian and then tune blur and center parameters by grid search to maximize AUC-Judd/NSS on training. This can inflate scores and conflates the contribution of the dynamics with post-hoc priors. Initial conditions are near-center, further reinforcing central bias. Reproducibility details (values for m, k, ω; time step/integrator; number of steps; acceptance function in SA; ranges of parameter search) are incomplete.

Clarity
- The manuscript is generally readable and equations are explicit, but there are several typos/formatting issues (missing footnotes, incomplete references, inconsistent equation numbering) and the kinetic energy expression inconsistency noted above.
- Important hyperparameters and implementation details are missing or scattered (ω, Gaussian kernel sizes, exact SA schedule and acceptance function, numerical integration specifics).
- Claims such as “real-time” are not quantified (no complexity/runtime measurements).

Originality
- Casting bottom-up attention and fixation/tracking within a unified least-action framework is novel for saliency modeling, even if individual ingredients (gradient-based saliency, optical-flow constraints, scanpath dynamics) are known. The alternation between local and peripheral drives is an interesting design idea.
- Related dynamic/variational active vision work could be better covered; nonetheless, the formulation is a distinct contribution.

Significance
- Conceptual: Provides a principled dynamic model that yields scanpaths and saliency as a byproduct. This is appealing for integrating attention with motion/oculomotor constraints.
- Practical: Given the current state of deep saliency on benchmarks, the impact on SOTA prediction may be limited unless comparisons to top models are added and the central-bias confounds are addressed. The absence of scanpath-level validation limits significance for modeling eye movements.

Strengths
- Unified, interpretable dynamical formulation with explicit equations.
- Derivation and stability condition on λ are worked out; exact vs approximated brightness-invariance versions assessed.
- Simple, data-driven early-vision focus; saliency emerges from dynamics, not directly trained.
- Competitive results against several baselines, with both image and video evaluations.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient comparison to contemporary top-performing deep models; unclear standing on leaderboards.
- Heavy reliance on tuned center bias and initial central starting point; no ablation.
- Lack of ablations for model components and ω modulation.
- Missing details for reproducibility; questionable kinetic energy expression.
- Claims about scanpath realism/tracking not quantitatively evaluated.
- Parameter fitting on small subset; risk of overfitting; unspecified acceptance function in SA.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide ablations: remove center bias; vary initial conditions; disable curiosity/brightness-invariance terms; test fixed vs time-modulated C.
- Report sensitivity to ω, ηb, ηp, λ, m and integration step; include stability analysis beyond the λ bound.
- Compare against strong deep baselines from MIT300/CAT2000 leaderboards of the same period; report ranks.
- Add scanpath metrics (e.g., saccade amplitude distributions, fixation durations, string-edit distance to human scanpaths) and tracking metrics on videos.
- Clarify kinetic energy definition and reconcile with EL derivation; provide full parameter values and code.
- Quantify runtime for a single scanpath and for saliency map generation (199 runs).

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, interpretable variational framework unifying multiple attention drives.
- Clear mathematical formulation; interesting idea that saliency emerges from dynamics.
- Competitive performance vs several classic baselines; works on images and videos.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is not competitive enough relative to modern deep saliency models; key comparisons missing.
- Central bias and initialization confounds make it hard to attribute gains to the proposed dynamics.
- Lack of ablations and scanpath-level validation; reproducibility gaps; minor technical inconsistencies.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical framing is interesting and potentially impactful, but the current empirical evaluation and analysis are insufficient for NIPS. With stronger comparisons, thorough ablations, clarified math/implementation, and scanpath validation, this could become a solid contribution.
