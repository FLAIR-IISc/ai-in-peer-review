Summary
The paper proposes a bottom-up, physics-inspired model of visual attention where scanpaths are derived as trajectories of a particle governed by a variational (least-action) principle. The Lagrangian includes: (i) a boundary potential to confine motion within a rectangular “retina”; (ii) a “curiosity” term promoting regions with large brightness gradients, alternating between local and blurred gradients via cos^2/sin^2 modulation; and (iii) a brightness-constancy (optical flow) term intended to induce fixation/tracking. The Euler–Lagrange equations yield second-order ODEs for the focus of attention. Saliency maps are produced as a byproduct by counting pixel visits over many simulated trajectories. Parameters are tuned via simulated annealing on CAT2000-TRAIN; additional blur/center-bias postprocessing is selected by grid search. The model is evaluated on MIT1003/MIT300/CAT2000 for static saliency and SFU for videos, with claims of competitive performance relative to several baselines.

Strengths
- Ambitious, unified dynamical formulation of attention that produces scanpaths and saliency maps within one framework.
- Derivation of explicit Euler–Lagrange dynamics with velocity-dependent terms (brightness constancy), yielding a nontrivial ODE system.
- Claimed competitive performance on multiple datasets (including a video dataset), with a very lightweight model compared to deep methods.
- Authors openly discuss the role of center bias and blur in evaluation, and provide two versions of the model (approximate vs exact brightness term).

Weaknesses and detailed concerns

Quality (technical soundness and empirical support)
- Kinetic energy is incorrect in Eq. (8): K(ẋ)=1/m ẋ^2. Subsequent derivations use ∂L/∂ẋ ≈ m ẋ (yielding m ẍ), which is only consistent with K=(m/2) ẋ^2. This inconsistency is fundamental (dimensional and mathematical) and undermines the derivation.
- Several derivation steps and conditions are error-prone or opaque. In Appendix, determinants (Eqs. 37–47) contain typos (duplicated indices) and the bound λ<m/4 is asserted without a clear, correct normalization argument (is ||b_x||∞≤1 per component or magnitude?).
- The “brightness invariance” term is repeatedly conflated with fixation. For static images (b_t=0), minimizing (b_x·ẋ)^2 encourages motion tangential to level sets (i.e., along edges), not fixation (ẋ≈0). The proposed interpretation needs correction and clearer discussion.
- The curiosity term alternates via cos^2(ωt)/sin^2(ωt). ω is not defined, tuned, or analyzed. Its effect on dynamics and metrics is unexplained.
- Several key hyperparameters are unspecified or under-specified: mass m, boundary stiffness k, ω, numerical solver, step sizes, tolerances, initialization distribution (“almost at center”), number of steps per second, etc. Without these, reproduction is difficult.
- SA procedure is not reproducible: acceptance probability P(·) is undefined; neighborhood structure and domain D are unspecified; α is not given; stopping criterion is vague. The choice to optimize on only 100 CAT2000 images (five random subsamples) and then select the best configuration invites overfitting.
- Additional blur and center-bias parameters are tuned by grid search to maximize AUC-Judd/NSS on training sets; for videos, tuning appears to be on SFU (12 clips), which is particularly risky. No sAUC/ shuffled-AUC results are reported, which are critical to control for center bias.
- Saliency map computation uses 199 (images) / 100 (videos) rollouts initialized “almost at center,” injecting strong center bias at the dynamical level; the input is additionally multiplied by a central Gaussian (second source of center bias), and the final saliency map is postprocessed by another center bias and blur (third source). No ablation quantifies their contributions vs the dynamics. A “gradient magnitude + center bias” baseline is notably missing.
- No scanpath validation (saccade amplitude distributions, fixation durations, temporal correlations) despite proposing a scanpath model; tracking claims are anecdotal with no quantitative tracking metrics.
- Tables/metrics referred to in the text (Tables 2–4) are not present; thus the “competes well/best score in half the cases” claim cannot be verified. Runtime claims (“real-time”) are not quantified.

Clarity
- Numerous typos and grammar issues impede readability (e.g., “eggs-chicken dilemma,” “interwound,” “psedo-code,” inconsistent indexing). Footnotes and numbering are broken or misplaced (“retina1,” “A straightforward extension…” mid-flow).
- Several definitions are imprecise (e.g., how “rectangular zone of information” is found; bounds used to define D in SA; precise normalization assumptions for b and b_x).
- The connection between the assumed principles and human oculomotor behavior is asserted but not substantiated (e.g., saccadic character of trajectories). The model produces smooth ODE trajectories; whether these produce realistic saccades is not analyzed.

Originality
- The unification of “curiosity” (gradient magnitude) with brightness-constancy in a least-action framework is interesting. However, dynamics-based attention models and saccadic models exist (e.g., Le Meur & Liu; WTA dynamics in Itti & Koch), and brightness constancy is standard in optical flow. The cos^2/sin^2 alternation and visit-count saliency construction feel heuristic. The paper would benefit from clearer positioning vs prior dynamic/variational approaches (e.g., active vision/control formulations, Bayesian surprise dynamics, active inference).

Significance
- If solidly validated and clearly presented, a compact, interpretable dynamical model that yields competitive saliency could be impactful. However, heavy reliance on center bias (threefold), lack of sAUC and ablations, missing tables, and irreproducible optimization severely limit confidence in the reported advances.

Pros
- Conceptually appealing physics-based unification.
- Simple, potentially efficient model with closed-form dynamics.
- Attempts to handle both static and dynamic scenes in one framework.

Cons
- Fundamental error in kinetic energy and multiple derivation/notation issues.
- Heavy, confounded use of center bias with no sAUC controls or ablations.
- Incomplete experimental reporting (missing tables, runtime), under-specified methods (SA, solvers), and limited reproducibility.
- Misinterpretations (brightness invariance vs fixation) and unclear hyperparameters (ω, m, k).
- No scanpath analyses despite being a central claim.

Recommendation: Reject

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the Lagrangian (use K=(m/2) ẋ^2) and check all derivations; clean up notation and indices; clearly state parameter domains and normalization.
- Specify all hyperparameters (m, k, ω), solver details, and provide code.
- Report full benchmark tables, include sAUC/HIT/IG metrics, and perform ablations: without center bias, without blur, varying initialization, with/without B and C terms, and a “gradient+center bias” baseline.
- Separate training/validation/test rigorously for both images and videos; avoid tuning on SFU test clips.
- Quantify scanpath properties vs human data; if aiming for biological plausibility, address saccades (ballistic jumps), not continuous ODE drift.
- Justify the cos^2/sin^2 alternation, or learn the modulation from data.
- Clarify the boundary potential and replace discontinuous Iverson brackets with smooth barriers.
