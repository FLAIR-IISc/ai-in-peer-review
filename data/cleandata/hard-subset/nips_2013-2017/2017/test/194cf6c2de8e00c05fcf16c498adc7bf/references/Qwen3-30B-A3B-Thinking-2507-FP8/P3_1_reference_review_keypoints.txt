- Paper proposes a variational model (EYMOL) for visual attention combining "curiosity" (high gradients) and "brightness invariance" inspired by least-action principles.
- Derives Euler-Lagrange equations and generates saliency maps as a byproduct of simulated scanpaths.

- Strength: Mathematically rigorous derivation with a thorough appendix.
- Strength: Conceptually simple model with only three main parameters.
- Strength: Unified framework integrating exploration and fixation/tracking principles.
- Strength: Saliency maps emerge naturally from simulated trajectories rather than being directly optimized.

- Weakness: Novelty is overstated and insufficiently differentiated from prior physics-inspired and variational attention models (e.g., Itti & Baldi, Horn & Schunck, Bruce & Tsotsos).
- Weakness: Misrepresents cited biological evidence about infant saccades.
- Weakness: Lacks biological plausibility given no direct comparison to human eye movement trajectories.

- Weakness: Parameter tuning used only 100 images, risking unreliable estimation and overfitting.
- Weakness: Evaluation limited to saliency maps; no quantitative scanpath or tracking comparisons to human data.
- Weakness: Video evaluation uses a very small SFU dataset (12 clips) without per-clip analysis.
- Weakness: No failure analysis to highlight model limitations across scenes or motion types.
- Weakness: Implementation and numerical solution details (integration method, timestep) are missing, hurting reproducibility.

- Significance: Achieves competitive but not state-of-the-art saliency results; improvement over existing methods is not demonstrated.
- Significance: Claims of competing with state-of-the-art on dynamic scenes are based on limited comparisons and minor gains.
- Significance: Practical impact is limited with no demonstrated applications beyond saliency prediction.

- Suggestion: Reframe novelty and provide a more comprehensive literature review distinguishing this work from related models.
- Suggestion: Add biological validation by comparing generated scanpaths to human eye movements using scanpath similarity metrics.
- Suggestion: Expand experimental evaluation—use more data for parameter tuning, provide failure analysis, and analyze video clips by complexity.
- Suggestion: Clarify how scanpaths produce saliency maps and detail the numerical integration/implementation steps for reproducibility.

- Overall assessment: Mathematically elegant and conceptually appealing but overclaims novelty and lacks sufficient experimental and biological validation.
- Recommendation: Reject due to insufficient novelty, limited evaluation, and unsupported biological claims.

- Pro: Elegant variational formulation, unified curiosity and tracking framework, and simplicity relative to deep models.
- Con: Overstated novelty, insufficient experimental validation (especially scanpaths), and results not superior to state-of-the-art.
