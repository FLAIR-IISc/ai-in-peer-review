- Proposes a variational/least-action framework (EYMOL) for visual attention deriving Euler-Lagrange dynamics from three principles: retina boundedness, curiosity (detail gradients), and brightness invariance; saliency maps emerge from simulated scanpaths.
- Strength: Provides a rigorous, physics-inspired theoretical foundation for attention dynamics.
- Strength: Unifies static and dynamic scene attention within a single mathematical framework.
- Strength: Computationally efficient and real-time capable compared to many deep learning approaches.
- Strength: Achieves competitive benchmark results across image and video datasets despite being non-learning-based.
- Strength: Experimental design and parameter estimation (simulated annealing) are clearly described.
- Weakness: Lacks in-depth comparative analysis explaining which model components drive performance.
- Weakness: Limited biological validation against actual eye movement data (scanpaths, fixation durations).
- Weakness: Uses an approximation for brightness invariance (eq. 6) without sufficient justification for dynamic scenes.
- Weakness: Insufficient discussion of limitations (e.g., multiple moving objects, highly cluttered scenes).
- Weakness: Novelty not fully contextualized relative to other physics-inspired/variational vision methods.
- Evaluation (Quality): Technically sound derivation and comprehensive experiments, but needs more analysis of why it works.
- Evaluation (Clarity): Well-organized and clearly written though mathematically dense; could use a simple conceptual diagram.
- Evaluation (Originality): Novel combination of curiosity-driven attention and brightness invariance under a variational principle, but needs clearer contrast to related work.
- Evaluation (Significance): Principled, efficient unified approach with potential impact, especially as a training-free alternative to data-driven methods.
- Arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical foundation, unified framework, competitive results, computational efficiency, and useful alternative to data-hungry methods.
- Arguments against acceptance: Weak biological validation, insufficient explanation for success, unjustified brightness approximation for dynamics, and incomplete contextualization among physics-inspired models.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address biological validation, analysis of model behaviour, and justification of key approximations.
- Suggested revision 1: Add explicit comparison with other physics-inspired attention models to highlight differences and novelty.
- Suggested revision 2: Provide detailed performance analysis and case studies showing successes and failures.
- Suggested revision 3: Compare generated scanpaths to human eye movement data, not only saliency maps.
- Suggested revision 4: Better justify the brightness invariance approximation for dynamic scenes and discuss its limitations.
- Final recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
