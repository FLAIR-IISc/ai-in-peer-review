Summary
The paper proposes a variational/dynamical model of visual attention (EYMOL) in which scanpaths arise as solutions of Euler–Lagrange equations for a Lagrangian with three components: (i) a boundary potential to confine the trajectory to the “retina,” (ii) a “curiosity” attraction to regions of high local or peripheral gradient magnitude with a time-oscillating weighting, and (iii) a brightness-invariance penalty motivated by the optical-flow constraint to encourage fixation/tracking. The model generates 2D trajectories via numerical integration; saliency maps are produced post hoc by counting visits across many runs. Parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) are tuned with simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a CAT2000 training subset. The approach is evaluated on MIT1003/MIT300/CAT2000 (images) and SFU (videos), and claimed to be competitive with several prior methods.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: Casting attention, fixation, and (rudimentary) tracking within a single variational framework is interesting and potentially impactful if made rigorous.
- Clear linkage to classical vision: The brightness-constancy term connects to optical flow (Horn–Schunck) and provides a plausible mechanism for motion-following without an explicit flow estimator.
- Produces scanpaths, not just saliency maps: This is valuable because scanpaths are richer behavioral outputs, and many saliency models only produce static maps.
- Computationally light: Integrating second-order ODEs is cheap, and real-time simulation seems feasible.
- The empirical protocol attempts blind testing (via MIT benchmark) rather than only in-house metrics.

Weaknesses (technical and methodological)
- Inconsistent/incorrect kinetic term: K(ẋ) is written as 1/m ||ẋ||^2 (Eq. 8) but subsequent derivations assume the standard K = 1/2 m ||ẋ||^2 (since d/dt ∂K/∂ẋ = m ẍ in Eq. 12). This is not a trivial typo: the Lagrangian’s scaling affects all subsequent equations and parameter interpretation.
- Velocity-dependent “potential”: The brightness-invariance penalty B depends on x and ẋ. Calling it a potential is nonstandard; a clearer treatment (e.g., as a Lagrangian with velocity-dependent term or via a Rayleigh dissipation functional) and discussion of physical meaning/units is missing.
- Missing damping: A second-order conservative system plus time-varying forcing tends to oscillate. No damping/friction term is present, yet stability is asserted. The ad hoc stability condition for λ (below) is insufficient.
- λ well-posedness condition is unjustified: The determinant D simplification is correct, but the bound λ < m/4 is derived by assuming b ∈ [0,1] implies ||∇b||^2 ≤ 2, which is false; bounding brightness does not bound spatial gradients. The subsequent inequalities (Eqs. 40–47) are garbled (missing denominators) and mathematically sloppy.
- Notation inconsistencies and ambiguities:
  - b2x denotes what—||∇b||^2 or component-wise squares? Later bx1, bx2 are components, but earlier b2x is overloaded.
  - cos 2(ω t) appears to mean cos^2(ω t); similarly for sin 2(ω t). Please fix.
  - C uses a single weight η but later the method uses ηb, ηp. Equations never updated accordingly.
  - “Parallelogram law” is cited where (a+b)^2 ≤ 2(a^2+b^2) + Cauchy–Schwarz/Young would be appropriate.
- Key hyperparameters unspecified: ω (oscillation frequency) is crucial and not reported; integration step size, solver, mass m, domain D for SA, neighbor proposal distribution, acceptance probability P, and annealing α are all underspecified. Reproducibility is not possible from the present description.
- Center bias and blur are tuned post hoc to maximize AUC-Judd/NSS on training data; combined with central initialization, this heavily bakes in center bias. Yet fairness w.r.t. center-bias-controlled metrics (sAUC) is not discussed, and the extent of overfitting is unclear.
- Evaluation reporting is insufficient: The paper claims state-of-the-art or competitive results and “best in half of the cases,” but the actual tables with numbers/metrics are not included in the text provided. It is impossible to verify significance without them. Confidence intervals or statistical tests are not discussed.
- Comparators are dated/incomplete: Recent strong saliency baselines (e.g., DeepGaze II/III, SALICON, SAM-ResNet, MLNet, GBVS variants) and modern video-saliency models are largely missing; the work cites one 2016 LSTM model but does not compare comprehensively. The rationale for selected baselines is thin.
- Design choices degrade biological plausibility: Converting to grayscale discards color, a known contributor to saliency; oscillating between local/peripheral gradients with cos^2/sin^2 lacks justification (why periodic toggling rather than a principled peripheral weighting?); no modeling of saccadic dynamics, inhibition of return, or oculomotor constraints beyond a box potential.
- Claims about fixation behavior: For static images, bt=0 and minimizing (∇b · ẋ)^2 implies motion perpendicular to edges; this encourages edge-following, not fixation. The assertion that it “favors coherent fixation of objects” needs analysis and empirical evidence (e.g., dwell time around object centers).
- Saliency from visit counts depends heavily on number of runs (199 for images, 100 for videos) and initialization “almost at the center.” The choice 199 is arbitrary and not justified; sensitivity analyses are absent.
- Clarity/editing: Numerous typos (“eggs-chicken,” “interwound,” “psedo-code,” “diferently by,” etc.), missing years in references, inconsistent footnotes, and formatting issues impede readability.

Quality
- The variational derivation is promising but marred by inconsistencies (K term), shaky bounds (λ), and missing key details. Empirical claims cannot be evaluated without the actual results tables. Overall, the technical and experimental rigor is insufficient.

Clarity
- The paper is difficult to reproduce and has multiple notation/grammar issues. Important parameters are unspecified.

Originality
- The idea of least-action for attention dynamics is interesting. However, building saliency from gradient-based curiosity and an optical-flow penalty is closely related to longstanding ideas (e.g., Horn–Schunck, information-maximization/surprise). The oscillatory mixing of local/peripheral cues is novel but ad hoc.

Significance
- If made rigorous and empirically validated against strong baselines, a unifying dynamical model producing scanpaths could be impactful. In current form, significance is limited by methodological weaknesses and incomplete evaluation.

Questions and suggestions
- Fix the kinetic term (use K = 1/2 m ||ẋ||^2) and re-derive all equations with correct scaling.
- Provide a principled stability/well-posedness analysis (or add explicit damping).
- Specify ω, m, solver details, SA settings, and release code.
- Report comprehensive metrics including sAUC and CC/IG, with confidence intervals, and include recent baselines.
- Justify/ablate center bias, initialization, number of runs, grayscale conversion, and oscillatory curiosity mixing.
- Discuss how video b(t,x) is constructed from discrete frames and whether inter-frame timing matches ω.
- Clean up notation, typos, and references.
