Summary
This paper proposes a physics-inspired, variational formulation of overt visual attention. The authors define a Lagrangian whose terms embody three principles: (i) boundedness of gaze within the image via a border potential, (ii) “curiosity” via attraction to high spatial gradients of both the raw and a blurred image (alternated with cos²/sin² weighting), and (iii) brightness invariance via the optical-flow constraint, used either exactly or via an upper bound in static images. The Euler–Lagrange equations yield second-order ODEs that define scanpaths. Saliency maps are then obtained as visit-frequency maps over many runs with random initializations. Parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) are tuned by simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000-TRAIN to maximize NSS; blur and center-bias post-processing parameters are chosen by grid search. The model is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU videos, with claims of competitive performance against several baselines.

Relation to prior work
The work departs from the standard global saliency-map paradigm (e.g., Itti-Koch, AIM, deep CNNs) by directly modeling scanpath dynamics. The use of the optical-flow constraint for fixation/tracking and gradient magnitude as a bottom-up drive aligns with prior saliency/flow links (e.g., Garcia-Diaz et al.), while the least-action framing is less common in saliency. However, recent deep-learning baselines (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON/MLNet) appear to be missing from the comparisons and discussion.

Strengths
- Clear, interpretable dynamical model with few intuitive components; can produce scanpaths and works on static and dynamic scenes.
- Variational derivation is interesting and unifies saccadic selection and motion tracking within one framework.
- No heavy learning; parameters tuned on a small batch; potentially real-time and lightweight.
- Comparison on standard datasets; use of MIT Benchmark for MIT300.

Weaknesses
- Mathematical inconsistencies/typos: Kinetic energy is defined as K=1/m‖ẋ‖² but later yields d/dt ∂L/∂ẋ = m ẍ, which is only consistent with K=(m/2)‖ẋ‖². The derivation and constants need correction and clarity. The determinant/λ-condition derivation (λ<m/4) is not rigorous; b gradients are not guaranteed to be ≤1 after normalization; several algebraic steps and footnotes contain errors/typos.
- Strong and potentially unfair center bias: (a) input images are multiplied by a Gaussian centered blob; (b) initial conditions are near the center; (c) additional blur and center-bias are applied to the final saliency maps via grid search to maximize metrics. This triple center-bias confound can inflate AUC-Judd/NSS, and should be controlled (e.g., with shuffled-AUC, IG) and ablated.
- Empirical validation is insufficient: numbers referred to in Tables 2–4 are not shown; “best in half the cases” is unverifiable. Video evaluation is on only 12 clips; no scanpath-level metrics (e.g., MultiMatch, string-edit distance) or tracking metrics are reported, despite claims of tracking behavior.
- Limited baselines: the compared methods omit several strong contemporary models (e.g., DeepGaze II, MLNet/SALICON), and fairness of post-processing across methods is unclear.
- Lack of ablations and sensitivity analysis: no systematic study of the role of (i) curiosity vs brightness-invariance vs boundary terms, (ii) the alternating cos²/sin² modulation and ω, (iii) the blurred peripheral channel p, or (iv) parameter ranges and stability.
- Possible metric overfitting: simulated annealing maximizes NSS on CAT2000-TRAIN and grid search tunes blur/center for AUC-Judd/NSS; cross-dataset generalization and held-out validation strategy are not convincingly documented.
- Reproducibility: key details are missing (exact parameter values, ω, m, k, SA domain D and schedule α, integrator type/step, runtime). Code/data are not provided.

Quality
Theoretical derivation is appealing but currently error-prone and imprecise in constants and conditions. The model plausibility is reasonable, yet the experimental evidence is not strong enough: results are incompletely reported, baselines are limited, and confounds (center bias) are substantial. Without ablations and stronger, bias-resistant metrics, claims about the mechanisms (e.g., fixation via brightness invariance, alternation avoiding traps) are not supported.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but suffers from numerous typos, missing/incorrect equations, dangling footnotes, and references to absent tables. The experimental section lacks crucial details for reproduction. The description of parameter constraints (e.g., λ) and numerical stability is unclear.

Originality
Moderately original in framing attention dynamics via a least-action principle with a specific combination of gradient- and brightness-invariance-driven terms. However, the ingredients themselves (gradient-based saliency, optical-flow constraint) are well-known; the main novelty is the unifying variational formalism.

Significance
Potentially interesting for interpretable, lightweight attention modeling and for generating scanpaths. As presented, the empirical significance is limited: it does not convincingly advance state-of-the-art or establish robust benefits over established baselines. Demonstrations on tracking and scanpath realism would be valuable but are missing.

Questions for the authors
- Please reconcile the kinetic energy definition with the subsequent derivation; provide a corrected, consistent Lagrangian and all constants.
- What are the exact values/ranges for m, k, ω, ηb, ηp, λ, the SA domain D, α, integration step/solver, and runtime? Is the λ<m/4 condition ever violated in practice?
- How much do results degrade without each source of center bias (input Gaussian, initializations, post-processing)? Please report sAUC, IG, CC, SIM, EMD, KL, and NSS.
- Can you report full numerical results for all datasets and baselines, with statistical tests and cross-dataset generalization? Include stronger contemporary baselines.
- Provide ablations for curiosity vs brightness-invariance terms, and an analysis of ω’s role.
- Can you evaluate scanpath metrics and demonstrate object tracking performance quantitatively?

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix mathematical inconsistencies, clean up notation, and provide a stability/identifiability analysis.
- Remove or strictly control center-bias manipulations; use bias-resistant metrics and ablations.
- Expand baselines and report complete tables; release code for reproducibility.
- Add scanpath-level evaluations and tracking metrics to substantiate the claimed unified mechanism.
- Provide sensitivity analyses and clear descriptions of all hyperparameters and numerical integration details.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the current manuscript has technical inaccuracies and insufficient, confounded empirical validation. With corrected derivations, stronger, bias-controlled experiments, comprehensive baselines, and ablations, I would be more positive.

Confidence: High.
