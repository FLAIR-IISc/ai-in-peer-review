- Proposes a novel variational framework (least action generalization) for visual attention, deriving Euler-Lagrange equations called EYe MOvement Laws (EYMOL).  
- Argues attention arises from bounded retinal constraints, curiosity-driven attraction to detail, and brightness invariance for fixation/tracking.  
- Positions saliency as a byproduct of scanpath simulation rather than a centralized map.  
- Evaluates model on static (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and dynamic (SFU) datasets, reporting competitive results.  
- Mathematical derivation is rigorous and well-documented, with detailed appendix.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive across multiple standard datasets.  
- Conceptually interesting shift from centralized saliency maps to scanpath-based saliency.  
- Parameter estimation via simulated annealing is well-justified and numerically stable.  
- Comparison excludes recent deep learning-based saliency models dominating current benchmarks.  
- Lacks ablation studies to isolate contributions of model components (e.g., curiosity vs. brightness invariance).  
- Insufficient explanation linking variational principles to observed empirical performance.  
- No statistical significance testing provided for reported performance differences.  
- Some key terms (e.g., "curiosity-driven principle") are underdefined and need clearer distinctions from related work.  
- Choices like Gaussian blur parameters for peripheral input are not fully justified or validated.  
- The approximated brightness invariance (Equation 6) vs. exact form in appendix is not convincingly justified for practice.  
- Novelty: variational least-action framing is original and theoretically motivated.  
- Novelty caveat: similarities to information-theoretic and scanpath-generating models are not adequately discussed, reducing perceived novelty.  
- Simplicity and lack of integration with modern deep learning methods limit impact.  
- Significance: provides a principled theoretical bridge between physics and attention and offers a simple, real-time-capable model.  
- Significance caveat: performance gains are modest and not clearly shown to advance state of the art.  
- Overall assessment: interesting and well-executed theory but needs stronger empirical comparisons and analysis — recommendation: weak acceptance with major revisions.  
- Suggested improvements: include comparisons to recent deep models, add ablation studies, clarify principle-to-behavior links, relate to information-theoretic work, provide statistical tests, compare to other scanpath models, detail parameter selection, and discuss limitations handling complex dynamic scenes.
