Summary
The paper proposes EYMOL, a variational/dynamical systems formulation of visual attention. A Lagrangian combines: (i) a boundary “potential” to keep the trajectory within the image; (ii) a “curiosity” term that attracts the trajectory to large image gradients, alternating between a local and peripheral (blurred) gradient via a cos2/sin2(ωt) modulation; and (iii) a brightness-invariance penalty (the classic optical flow brightness constancy) to promote fixation and tracking. The Euler–Lagrange equations yield second-order ODEs for the scanpath. Saliency maps are obtained post hoc by counting pixel visitations over many simulated trajectories with randomized near-center initial conditions. Parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) are tuned via simulated annealing on a small subset of CAT2000-TRAIN to maximize NSS. The method is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and the SFU video dataset, with claims of competitive performance relative to several baselines.

Relation to prior work
The model sits among long-standing saliency/attention frameworks (Itti & Koch; information maximization; surprise; learning-based models) and draws directly on the optical flow brightness constancy constraint. The idea of deriving saccade dynamics from variational principles is appealing and has appeared in active vision/dynamical attention literatures, though this particular combination is less common. Modern deep models (e.g., SALICON, DeepGaze II, SAM) set the current state of the art circa 2016–2017, and only some are cited and compared.

Strengths
- Conceptually unified dynamical formulation that ties curiosity and (approximate) fixation/tracking via a single Lagrangian.
- Produces scanpaths directly (rather than only a static saliency map), potentially enabling richer temporal analyses.
- Simple ingredients; equations can be integrated with standard solvers; model is fast per trajectory.
- The V1 vs V2 comparison suggests the proposed brightness-invariance approximation is adequate for static images.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and rigor (Quality)
- The kinetic energy in Eq. (8) is written as K(ẋ)=1/m ẋ^2, yet later derivations use d/dt ∂K/∂ẋ = m ẍ, which corresponds to K=(m/2) ẋ^2. This inconsistency is not a minor typo because it underpins the Euler–Lagrange equations and dimensions of parameters.
- The determinant simplification used to derive the constraint λ < m/4 (Eqs. 41–47) contains algebraic errors and repeated symbols (b2x1 appears twice); the conclusion does not follow as written. Claims of well-posedness should be carefully rederived (including regularity of the piecewise boundary “potential,” which is non-smooth).
- The “curiosity” alternation via cos2/sin2(ωt) is ad hoc; ω is neither justified nor ablated. It is unclear that periodic toggling is preferable to a learned or adaptive mixing.
- The boundary potential based on Iverson brackets is non-differentiable at the borders; this conflicts with the smoothness assumed in the variational derivation and could affect numerical stability.
- The paper acknowledges numerical instabilities for large η values; there is no systematic stability analysis or parameter sensitivity study.

Experimental methodology and baselines (Quality/Significance)
- Center bias and blur are explicitly injected by multiplying b and p with a Gaussian and then further tuned via grid search per dataset to optimize AUC-Judd/NSS. This makes it hard to attribute gains to the dynamics rather than priors/post-processing. Modern benchmarks emphasize separating center-bias effects.
- Parameter tuning via simulated annealing is performed on only 100 images (5 per CAT2000 category), and then multiple hyperparameters (including center-bias strength and blur) are tuned as well. Risk of overfitting is high; no cross-validation, robustness analysis, or confidence intervals are reported.
- Comparisons omit several strong contemporary baselines (e.g., SALICON, DeepGaze II), and for video, dynamic-specific deep models. Claims of “best in half the cases” are not persuasive without comprehensive, up-to-date baselines and statistical testing.
- Saliency maps are aggregates over 199 randomized trajectories starting near the center. This design favors metrics like AUC-Judd and may conflate model dynamics with initial-condition/center prior effects. Variance across runs and sensitivity to the number of trajectories are not reported.
- No evaluation of scanpath statistics (saccade amplitudes, fixation durations, temporal correlations) against human data, despite scanpath modeling being a central claim.

Clarity
- Numerous typographical and formatting issues (e.g., missing years in references, dangling “st Conference…”, inconsistent notation). Key implementation details are underspecified: ω, integration step size and solver, mass m, units/scaling of b, exact initial-condition distributions, and runtime measurements for “real-time.”
- The appendix contains nontrivial algebraic inconsistencies, reducing confidence in the derivation.

Originality
- The variational framing is interesting, but the specific terms largely restate known cues (gradient magnitude, blurred gradient, brightness constancy, center prior) in a physics-inspired package. Without stronger theoretical justification or unique empirical benefits, the novelty feels incremental.

Significance
- Given the current saliency landscape dominated by learned models, the practical impact hinges on either superior or clearly competitive performance with strong baselines, or compelling cognitive/biophysical validation. The present evidence is insufficient on both fronts.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the kinetic energy and derivations; provide a correct, self-consistent EL system and a well-posedness/stability analysis (including smooth boundary potentials).
- Report full implementation details and ablation studies: remove center bias, vary ω, remove the curiosity alternation, remove brightness-invariance; quantify each component’s contribution.
- Evaluate scanpaths against human temporal statistics in addition to standard saliency metrics; report variability across runs.
- Expand baselines to include leading deep saliency models (images and video); use current benchmark protocols; report multiple metrics with statistical tests.
- Analyze parameter sensitivity and provide cross-dataset generalization without dataset-specific tuning of center bias.
- Measure and report actual runtimes for single-trajectory and multi-trajectory inference.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually elegant unification of fixation and motion tendencies in a single dynamical framework.
- Produces scanpaths and saliency as a byproduct, which is attractive for modeling.

Cons for acceptance
- Derivational inconsistencies and algebraic errors harm technical credibility.
- Heavy reliance on center bias and dataset-specific tuning; limited, outdated baselines.
- Lack of rigorous ablations, robustness analyses, and scanpath validation.
- Novelty and impact relative to current state of the art are unclear.

Overall recommendation
Leaning toward rejection. The idea is intriguing, but the paper needs stronger theoretical correctness, clearer exposition, and substantially more rigorous and up-to-date empirical validation to meet NeurIPS standards.
