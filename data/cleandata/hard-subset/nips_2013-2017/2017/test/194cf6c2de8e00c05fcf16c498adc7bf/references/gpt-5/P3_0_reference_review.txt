Summary
The paper proposes a variational formulation of bottom-up visual attention in dynamic scenes. The scanpath is modeled as the trajectory of a point mass following Euler–Lagrange equations derived from an action with three terms: (i) a confining “retina” potential to keep the trajectory within image bounds; (ii) a curiosity term encouraging visits to high-detail regions via the squared gradient magnitude of brightness and its blurred version, alternated over time by cos^2/sin^2; (iii) a brightness-invariance term penalizing the time derivative of brightness along the trajectory, akin to the optical flow constraint, promoting fixation and motion tracking. Saliency maps are produced as a byproduct by aggregating pixel visit counts over multiple runs with randomized initial conditions. Parameters are tuned via simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a subset of CAT2000. The method is compared to baselines on MIT1003/CAT2000 (training), MIT300/CAT2000 (test), and SFU videos, and is claimed to be competitive with classic and learning-based saliency models.

Strengths
- A principled dynamical perspective: casting attention as a stationary point of a variational principle is elegant and unifies saccadic exploration and fixation/tracking mechanisms within one model.
- The brightness-invariance term connects to well-known optical flow constraints and provides an interpretable mechanism for border-following in static images and tracking in videos.
- Generating scanpaths first and deriving saliency maps as visit densities is a refreshing contrast to end-to-end saliency prediction; it aligns with active vision/trajectory-based accounts.
- The model is lightweight, uses grayscale only, and can be integrated in real time according to the authors; the derivation for both exact and upper-bounded “B” terms and their empirical equivalence (for static images) is informative.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
Technical correctness and derivations:
- The kinetic energy is written as K(ẋ)=1/m ẋ^2 (Eq. 8). Standard Lagrangians use K=(m/2)||ẋ||^2. As written, units and scaling are inconsistent and affect all subsequent conditions (e.g., λ<m/4). Please clarify whether this is a typo or a deliberate reparameterization and reconcile with the stability analysis.
- Several algebraic/notation issues in the Appendix (e.g., duplicated indices in Eq. 42; inconsistent vector/scalar notation; missing factors) impede reproducibility. The generalized potential depends on ẋ; the use of Euler–Lagrange with velocity-dependent potentials is valid, but the derivation should be checked carefully.
- The alternation C(t,x)=||∇b||^2 cos^2(ωt)+||∇p||^2 sin^2(ωt) is ad hoc. The choice of ω, its sensitivity, and the rationale for deterministic alternation vs. learned/stochastic scheduling are not analyzed.

Experimental design and fairness:
- The saliency map is heavily shaped by preprocessing: grayscale conversion, a Gaussian center weighting applied to the image before gradient computation, random initializations near center, and a post-hoc grid search over blur and center bias to maximize AUC-Judd/NSS on training data. These steps can inflate certain metrics (e.g., AUC) and obscure the contribution of the proposed dynamics. An ablation without center bias (and with “shuffled AUC”) is needed.
- Parameter tuning via simulated annealing on a small subset (100 images) using NSS, followed by per-dataset grid search for blur and center parameters, risks overfitting. Clear train/val/test separation and reporting on validation vs. test are required.
- Quantitative results are insufficiently documented in the manuscript (no numbers in the text; reliance on tables that are not included here). Claims like “best score in half of the cases” are hard to assess without full metrics across AUC, sAUC, NSS, CC, KL, and with appropriate baselines (including a strong center prior and deep models).
- For videos, the evidence for “tracking moving objects” is qualitative. Quantitative tracking proxies (e.g., correlation of scanpath with target trajectories, time-lagged overlap) or dynamic saliency metrics beyond AUC would strengthen the claims.

Modeling assumptions and scope:
- The brightness-invariance term enforces motion perpendicular to ∇b in static images, pushing trajectories along edges. While the curiosity term attracts to high gradients, the joint dynamics may produce oscillations or edge-following rather than fixation on semantically meaningful regions; stability analysis and qualitative trajectory plots would help.
- The model ignores color and mid/high-level features. Given that state-of-the-art saliency is dominated by deep features, demonstrating competitive performance with only low-level cues requires rigorous benchmarking and strong baselines.
- The center bias is effectively built into the model via preprocessing; this undermines the claim that the saliency map is an emergent byproduct. Consider removing pre-imposed center bias and reporting shuffled AUC to control for dataset center tendencies.

Relation to prior work:
- While classic saliency and “surprise” models are cited, there is limited discussion of dynamical/optimal-control/active vision approaches (e.g., ideal observer models for gaze allocation, dynamic neural fields, reinforcement learning for gaze control) and saccade generation models that incorporate motion dynamics and inhibition-of-return. Positioning the variational approach relative to such frameworks (and discussing differences/advantages) would improve originality and context.
- There is a body of work linking attention/gaze to information gain or Fisher information; a connection between the proposed Lagrangian terms and information-theoretic objectives would strengthen the theoretical motivation for the chosen form of C and B.

Quality, clarity, originality, significance
- Quality: The core idea is sound and potentially impactful, but the current derivations contain inconsistencies and the experimental methodology leaves room for confounds. Reproducibility is limited: parameter values, ω, integrator details, and exact datasets splits are not fully specified.
- Clarity: The paper is readable but marred by typos, incomplete references, and notation issues. The Appendix needs careful editing. Explicit algorithmic details (neighborhood in SA, acceptance probability, bounds D, integrator step size) should be included.
- Originality: Using a least-action principle to derive attention dynamics is relatively novel in the saliency community, though related in spirit to active vision and optimal control. The curiosity-plus-brightness-invariance combination is interesting but somewhat heuristic in its current form.
- Significance: If validated rigorously, a lightweight dynamic model producing both scanpaths and saliency could be useful. As presented, the empirical gains are unclear and the reliance on center bias limits impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the kinetic energy expression and re-derive stability conditions; clean up all equations and notations.
- Provide full quantitative results with standard metrics and strong baselines, including no-center-bias and shuffled AUC. Add ablations: remove C or B terms; vary ω; remove image-level center weighting; vary initialization.
- Report parameter values learned by SA, sensitivity analyses, and cross-dataset generalization without retuning center/blur.
- For videos, add quantitative tracking/gaze dynamics analyses and compare scanpath statistics (saccade amplitudes, fixation durations) to human data.
- Discuss connections to information-theoretic objectives and to active vision/optimal control literature; clarify how your approach differs and what it buys.

Recommendation
Borderline reject in current form. The variational perspective and unification of curiosity and brightness invariance are appealing, but technical inconsistencies and evaluation confounds need to be addressed. With corrected derivations, stronger and fairer experiments, and richer analysis of scanpaths, this could become a solid contribution.
