Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a variational/dynamical systems approach to model human visual attention as the trajectory of a particle moving over a visual stimulus under a generalized least action principle. The Lagrangian combines: (i) a boundary potential V to confine the trajectory within the image; (ii) a “curiosity” term C favoring regions with high spatial gradients, alternating between local (original image) and peripheral (Gaussian-blurred image) gradients via a cos^2/sin^2 temporal modulation; and (iii) a brightness-invariance term B that penalizes deviations from the optical flow constraint ḃ = b_t + ∇b · ẋ = 0, encouraging fixation and tracking. The resulting Euler-Lagrange equations yield second-order ODEs for the focus-of-attention trajectory. Saliency maps are produced as a byproduct by counting pixel visits over multiple simulated runs with randomized initial conditions.

The approach connects to classic saliency models (Koch & Ullman; Itti & Koch), information-theoretic accounts (Bruce & Tsotsos; Itti & Baldi), machine learning approaches (Judd et al.), and recent deep saliency models, while differing in that scanpaths are primary and the saliency map is derived from the dynamics. The fixation/optical-flow constraint links to the Horn–Schunck optical flow formulation. The paper evaluates on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000 (images), and SFU (videos), reporting competitive performance against selected baselines.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: A single variational formulation yields both saccade-like exploration (via gradient-based curiosity) and fixation/tracking behavior (via brightness-invariance), and naturally produces scanpaths and saliency without a central saliency map.
- Analytical clarity of the core mechanism: The derivation of Euler-Lagrange equations and reduction to a solvable 2×2 linear system for accelerations (with explicit invertibility conditions) makes the model interpretable and computationally lightweight.
- Applicability to dynamic scenes: The same framework extends to videos without architectural changes, with preliminary evidence of object-following behavior.
- Empirical evaluation on standard datasets and metrics, with parameter selection described (simulated annealing), and attention to practicalities (SciPy integration, multiple runs, run-time feasibility).

Weaknesses

Quality (technical soundness)
- Inconsistency in kinetic energy: The text defines K(ẋ)=1/m · ||ẋ||^2 but subsequently uses d/dt ∂K/∂ẋ = m ẍ, which would require K=(m/2)||ẋ||^2. This should be corrected; as written, units and derivatives are inconsistent.
- Determinant/invertibility condition: The matrix mI−2λ(b_x b_x^T) is correctly of the form “identity minus rank-1 update,” implying det = m^2 − 2λ m ||∇b||^2. The manuscript’s derivation contains typographical errors (duplicated terms, missing x2) and a specific bound λ < m/4 that seems unjustified without a bound on ||∇b||^2. Provide a rigorous, correct condition (e.g., λ < m/(2 sup ||∇b||^2)).
- Non-smooth boundary potential: V uses indicator functions [xi>li], [xi<0], introducing force discontinuities at borders. This is at odds with the Euler-Lagrange smoothness assumptions; a smooth barrier or soft penalty would be preferable, or piecewise-smooth analysis should be stated.
- Approximate brightness-invariance: The bound B̄(t,x,ẋ) ≤ 2 b_t^2 + 2 ||∇b||^2 ||ẋ||^2 is reasonable using Cauchy–Schwarz, but the text elides the inner product vs. norm distinction. Clarify the inequality and its implications on the resulting equations.
- Missing or unspecified parameters: The temporal modulation frequency ω, mass m, stiffness k, and the acceptance function P() and neighborhood definition in simulated annealing are not specified, hindering reproducibility and interpretability. Stability and sensitivity to these parameters are not analyzed.
- Post-processing and center bias: The preprocessing multiplies images by a Gaussian blob (explicit center bias) and the saliency maps are further tuned by grid-search blur/center parameters on training data. This complicates attribution of gains to the proposed dynamics versus bias/post-processing.

Clarity
- Numerous typographical and notation errors (e.g., repeated indices, missing terms, dimension mismatches) and incomplete references (years, venues) impede readability and verification.
- References to tables and quantitative results (Tables 2–4) are made without including the data in the text. Claims such as “best score in half of the cases” cannot be independently assessed from the manuscript alone.
- Some modeling choices lack motivation or ablation: the cos^2/sin^2 temporal alternation between local and peripheral gradients, the choice of Gaussian blur parameters, and the number of runs/initialization strategy.

Originality
- The combination of a variational principle with an optical-flow-like constraint to unify exploration and fixation/tracking is a fresh perspective in the saliency literature. However, dynamical systems for attention and scanpath simulation have prior art; the paper would benefit from a deeper positioning versus those (e.g., biologically-inspired saccade generators, dynamical WTA circuits).
- The idea of deriving saliency from visitation statistics of scanpaths is known; the particular Lagrangian design and alternating local/peripheral drive appear novel.

Significance
- Empirical gains are asserted but not substantiated in the text with numbers, confidence intervals, or statistical tests. Comparisons to modern deep saliency models seem limited; the most recent cited deep model is an LSTM variant, and many top-performing 2016–2017 methods are not discussed.
- Without ablations disentangling the contribution of the dynamic laws from center bias and post-processing, it is difficult to assess the scientific or practical impact.
- The model’s ability to predict scanpath sequences (not just saliency maps) is a key promise, but the paper does not evaluate scanpath metrics against human data.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, interpretable variational framework producing attention dynamics and saliency as a byproduct.
- Unified handling of static and dynamic scenes with a single set of equations.
- Potentially useful for real-time applications requiring light-weight attention mechanisms.

Cons for acceptance
- Technical inconsistencies and derivation errors (kinetic energy, determinant bound, notation).
- Insufficient quantitative evidence in the manuscript; missing tables/figures and lack of ablations.
- Heavy reliance on center bias and post-processing muddies core contributions.
- Limited comparison to strong contemporary baselines; no scanpath-level evaluation.
- Reproducibility concerns due to unspecified parameters and incomplete experimental details.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the Lagrangian and derivations; provide a clean, rigorous statement of the well-posedness condition and a stability analysis.
- Report full quantitative results (MIT300, CAT2000, SFU) with multiple metrics, plus ablations: without center bias, without peripheral term, without brightness-invariance, and with/without blur post-processing.
- Specify and justify all parameters (m, k, ω), SA details (neighborhood, acceptance probability), integrator settings, and release code.
- Evaluate scanpaths against human data using standard sequence metrics; include analysis of initial condition sensitivity.
- Compare against a broader set of contemporary deep saliency models and control for center bias to ensure fair comparisons.
- Motivate and analyze the temporal modulation between local and peripheral gradients; explore alternatives (e.g., scale-space without time modulation).

Overall, while the conceptual framing is appealing, the current submission has technical and empirical gaps that limit confidence in its contributions.
