This paper proposes a novel model of visual attention, termed EYMOL, framed within a variational least-action principle. The core idea is to model attention as driven by curiosity (regions with high detail) and brightness invariance, resulting in differential equations governing scanpath dynamics. The authors compare their model’s generated saliency maps to human fixations on image and video datasets. While the approach is mathematically interesting and attempts a unifying framework, numerous shortcomings, particularly concerning implementation details, justification of choices, and presentation, lead me to recommend rejection.

The introduction adequately frames the problem and motivates the exploration of alternative attention models. However, the phrasing is occasionally imprecise ("trapped into a sort of eggs-chicken dilemma") and the transition between biological motivation and the proposed mathematical framework feels abrupt. The literature review is reasonably comprehensive but lacks critical assessment of the cited works; it primarily *lists* related approaches without highlighting their limitations or specific contributions relevant to the proposed model.

The mathematical formulation in Section 2, while utilizing familiar concepts like the Lagrangian, suffers from several issues. The boundedness term (V(x)) using Iverson’s notation is needlessly complex and lacks justification for the chosen harmonic oscillator form. Simply constraining coordinates seems sufficient, and a more direct, simpler implementation would be preferred. The “curiosity driven principle” (C(t, x)) introduces ad-hoc alternating cosine and sine functions modulating gradients. The rationale for this specific functional form is unclear; why is this alternation crucial for avoiding “trapping”? This feels arbitrarily designed rather than derived from underlying principles. The brightness invariance term (B(t, x, ẋ)) is similarly problematic. While drawing inspiration from optical flow is reasonable, the soft-satisfaction approach is not thoroughly justified. The approximation in (6), while presented as a simplification, needs a stronger justification—is it a valid approximation for the intended image characteristics? The transition to using this approximation throughout the paper is not adequately motivated. 

The parameters estimation with simulated annealing (SA) is described vaguely. The search space “D” is mentioned but not defined explicitly. This is a crucial detail for reproducibility and understanding the limitations of the parameter search. The justification for using only a batch of 100 images for SA, then testing on the whole dataset, is weak; the batch might not be representative. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is also terse, lacking sufficient detail for implementation. The discussion around numerical instability and parameter selection in the final paragraph is concerning and suggests potential fragility of the model.

The experimental section is riddled with minor but collectively significant flaws. The pre-processing steps (grayscale conversion, blurring, center bias) are described, but the choices of parameters (Gaussian kernel size, sigma for blurring, center bias parameters) are not justified. The claim that the saliency map calculation is a byproduct of the scanpath model is somewhat overstated; it *requires* running numerous simulations with random initial conditions, effectively turning it into a post-hoc analysis. The lack of a clear ablation study to assess the contribution of each term in the Lagrangian is a major weakness. The comparison with state-of-the-art methods is presented in tables, but the differences in methodology (e.g., data pre-processing, parameter tuning) are not adequately addressed, making direct comparison difficult. The statement that the model can "conveniently be simplified" for static images and then claiming the approximation doesn’t cause loss in score feels disingenuous without a detailed error analysis. The claim of “evidences of tracking moving objects” in the video experiments is a weak assertion without quantitative validation or detailed examples.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting idea but suffers from a lack of rigor in both the mathematical formulation and the experimental evaluation. The ad-hoc choices in the Lagrangian, the insufficiently justified approximations, the vague description of the SA algorithm, and the numerous minor flaws in the experimental setup raise serious concerns about the validity and reproducibility of the results. While the novelty of the approach is acknowledged, it is overshadowed by the numerous issues.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Numerous unjustified design choices and approximations in the mathematical model.
*   Vague description of the parameter estimation process (SA).
*   Lack of ablation studies to assess the contribution of individual terms.
*   Insufficient justification for experimental parameters and choices.
*   Weak experimental validation and overly optimistic interpretation of results.
*   Collectively, the numerous minor flaws indicate a lack of thoroughness.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty of the variational approach to modeling visual attention.
*   Attempts to unify curiosity and brightness invariance as driving principles.
*   Competitive performance on benchmark datasets (though this is weakened by methodological concerns).
