- Proposes a variational framework for visual attention based on a least-action generalization with three principles: boundedness, curiosity (high gradient attraction), and brightness invariance, yielding Euler-Lagrange scanpath equations and saliency as a byproduct.

- Novelty is overstated: core principles overlap with prior work (e.g., Bayesian surprise, optical flow), so the approach feels like rebranding rather than a fundamental breakthrough.

- Strength — Conceptual novelty: unified mathematical treatment for static and dynamic scenes is conceptually appealing.

- Strength — Computational efficiency: model runs in real-time, an advantage over many deep learning saliency models.

- Strength — Experimental breadth: evaluated on multiple standard image datasets (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and SFU video with standard metrics (NSS, AUC-Judd) showing competitive results.

- Strength — Clear derivation: solid mathematical formulation with detailed appendices enabling replication.

- Weakness — No ablation study: fails to quantify the relative contribution of boundedness, curiosity, and brightness invariance.

- Weakness — Superficial video evaluation: dynamic scene claims tested on only SFU (12 clips) with no tracking metrics or comparisons to tracking models.

- Weakness — Parameter tuning concerns: simulated annealing used only 100 CAT2000-TRAIN images, raising generalization doubts.

- Weakness — Missing comparisons to recent deep learning SOTA methods, undermining significance of results.

- Weakness — Theoretical justification weak: connection to the least-action principle is not convincingly motivated or compared to alternative frameworks.

- Critical — Claims about modeling human attention emergence are unsupported: no scanpath temporal/spatial similarity analysis to human eye movements.

- Critical — Dataset and center-bias issues: use of Gaussian center bias and standard datasets’ biases weaken the claim of being completely data-driven.

- Recommendation — Reject in current form due to insufficient novelty and incomplete evaluation; suggest major revisions addressing DL comparisons, ablation studies, scanpath analysis, and stronger theoretical justification.

- Pro/Con summary: Pros — theoretically grounded, efficient, competitive, reproducible; Cons — overstated novelty, missing ablations, weak dynamic eval, omitted DL baselines, focus on saliency maps not scanpaths.
