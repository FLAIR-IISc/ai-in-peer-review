- Summary: Proposes a physics-inspired variational model of visual attention using three principles (boundedness, curiosity, brightness invariance) producing Euler–Lagrange ODEs (EYMOL) whose simulated scanpaths yield saliency maps; tuned by simulated annealing and evaluated on multiple datasets with competitive results.

- Relation to prior work: Connects to classic saliency, surprise/information-theoretic methods, scanpath models, and optical flow; variational least-action and velocity-dependent brightness term are distinct but comparisons to modern deep models are limited.

- Strengths: Principled, interpretable framework unifying saccades, fixations, and tracking; novel derivation and well-posedness discussion; saliency as scanpath byproduct is behaviorally meaningful; lightweight and real-time capable with competitive performance.

- Weakness — Math/Derivations: Mathematical expressions contain inconsistencies/typos (e.g., kinetic energy definition, unstated gradient bounds) and the λ stability bound is unjustified without proper normalization.

- Weakness — Missing sensitivity analyses: Key parameters (ω, mass m, stiffness k) are introduced but neither specified nor sensitivity-tested.

- Weakness — Optimization/reproducibility: Simulated annealing details (acceptance function, neighborhood, iterations, α) are omitted, hindering reproducibility.

- Weakness — Center-bias and evaluation: Input multiplication by a central Gaussian and post-hoc tuning of blur/center likely inflate center-biased metrics; sAUC and center-bias baseline are not reported.

- Weakness — Ablations and statistics: No ablation studies isolating each model term or cos/sin alternation; statistical tests and variances for reported comparisons are missing.

- Weakness — Scanpath validation: No quantitative scanpath-level comparisons to human sequences (e.g., MultiMatch), undermining claims about modeling eye movements.

- Clarity issues: Grammar errors, missing parameter values, integrator/step-size details, incomplete pseudo-code, missing footnotes/references, and possible equation typos reduce clarity and reproducibility.

- Originality: The velocity-dependent brightness-invariance in a least-action variational framework is novel, but related dynamic attention and active inference literature are not sufficiently discussed.

- Significance: Could be valuable as an interpretable, learning-light saliency model, but impact depends on stronger baselines, center-bias controls, scanpath metrics, and ablations.

- Questions for authors: Provide exact ω, m, k ranges and sensitivity; clarify gradient normalization and λ bound; specify integrator, step sizes, and time scaling; quantify effect of removing post-hoc center tuning and report center-only baseline; supply sAUC, CC, SIM, KL across datasets.

- Suggested experiments: Add ablations for each term and cos/sin alternation; provide parameter sensitivity plots; include sAUC and center-bias baseline; compare to strong deep and dynamic scanpath baselines; add scanpath metrics and release code.

- Pros for acceptance: Conceptually novel, interpretable variational formulation; unifies saccades/fixation/tracking with saliency as a byproduct; competitive multi-dataset results with a lightweight model.

- Cons against acceptance: Unclear/shaky mathematical details; inadequate control for center bias and limited ablations; incomplete comparisons to strong baselines; reproducibility concerns.

- Overall assessment: Borderline reject — promising and potentially impactful idea but needs clearer theory, stronger controlled experiments (sAUC, scanpath metrics), thorough ablations, and reproducibility fixes.
