## Review of "Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel computational model of visual attention, termed EYMOL, based on a variational approach and a least-action principle. The core idea is that attention emerges from balancing “curiosity” (regions with high detail) and “brightness invariance” (fixation). The model is mathematically formulated, resulting in differential equations that govern the scanpath. The authors demonstrate the model's performance on static image saliency detection (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and dynamic scene saliency detection (SFU) datasets, comparing it to existing state-of-the-art methods. The saliency map is computed as a byproduct of the scanpath simulation, rather than being the primary objective.

**Relationship to Previous Work:**

The paper positions itself within the broader field of computational visual attention modeling, contrasting with approaches based on feature integration (Itti et al.) and saliency map optimization. It acknowledges the debate between bottom-up and top-down attention, framing its work as a data-driven, early-stage model inspired by observations of infant attention. The use of a variational approach is less common in this area, offering a different perspective compared to purely feature-based or learning-based methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The variational approach to modeling visual attention is a genuinely novel contribution. Framing attention as a result of optimizing an action functional is a compelling idea.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations provides a strong theoretical underpinning for the model. The connection to concepts from physics (least action) is insightful.
* **Unified Framework:** The model elegantly combines curiosity and brightness invariance into a single framework, offering a potential explanation for both saccadic movements and object tracking.
* **Competitive Performance:** Despite its simplicity, the model achieves competitive results on standard saliency detection benchmarks, particularly considering the saliency map is a byproduct of the scanpath simulation.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the mathematical formulations are clearly presented (though some details are relegated to the appendix).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The model's performance relies on careful parameter tuning using simulated annealing.  The sensitivity to these parameters and the robustness of the learned parameter configurations aren't thoroughly discussed. While SA is used, the justification for the search space and the algorithm’s convergence are not completely clear.
* **Biological Plausibility:** While inspired by observations of infant attention, the connection to known neurophysiological mechanisms of attention is limited. The "curiosity" and "brightness invariance" principles are reasonable, but a more detailed discussion of their neural correlates would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** The comparison between the two versions of the model (V1 and V2) is limited to assessing whether the approximation made in V1 impacts performance. A more thorough ablation study, examining the contribution of each term in the Lagrangian, would provide further insight into the model's behavior.
* **Simplifying Assumptions:** The model makes several simplifying assumptions (e.g., grayscale images, center bias). The impact of these assumptions on the model's generalizability is not fully explored.
* **Lack of Scanpath Visualization:** While the paper focuses on saliency maps, visualizing representative scanpaths generated by the model would offer valuable qualitative insight into its behavior and comparison to human scanpaths.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** Technically sound. The mathematical derivations appear correct, and the experimental setup is reasonable. The claim that it's a "complete piece of work" is debatable – further ablation studies and analysis of parameter sensitivity would improve it.
* **Clarity:** Generally clear, though the appendix contains dense mathematical derivations that might be challenging for some readers.
* **Originality:** Highly original in its approach. The variational framework is a novel contribution to the field.
* **Significance:** Potentially significant. The model offers a new perspective on visual attention and achieves competitive performance. However, the impact will depend on its ability to be extended and integrated with more complex cognitive models.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically grounded approach to visual attention.
* Competitive performance on standard benchmarks.
* Clear presentation of the model and mathematical derivations.
* Potential to advance our understanding of the underlying mechanisms of visual attention.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Parameter sensitivity and limited analysis of parameter robustness.
* Limited discussion of biological plausibility and neural correlates.
* Need for more comprehensive ablation studies and sensitivity analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of computational visual attention. The variational approach is novel and offers a promising alternative to existing methods. While the paper has some weaknesses, particularly regarding parameter sensitivity and biological plausibility, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggested Revisions:**

* Conduct a more thorough ablation study to assess the contribution of each term in the Lagrangian.
* Discuss the limitations of the model and the potential impact of simplifying assumptions.
* Provide visualizations of representative scanpaths generated by the model.
*  Elaborate on the connection to known neurophysiological mechanisms of attention.
*  Provide more detailed justification for the simulated annealing parameter space and algorithm convergence.
