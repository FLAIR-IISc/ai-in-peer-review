Summary
The paper proposes a physics-inspired, variational formulation of gaze behavior in dynamic scenes. The authors define a Lagrangian over the gaze trajectory x(t) with three components: (i) a boundary potential to confine the trajectory within the retina/image, (ii) a “curiosity” term that attracts the trajectory to high-gradient regions of the raw and a blurred image (modulated by cos^2 and sin^2 to alternate local/peripheral influences), and (iii) a brightness-invariance term penalizing deviations from ḃ = bt + bx ẋ = 0 to promote fixation and motion tracking. The scanpath is obtained by solving the Euler–Lagrange equations; saliency maps are derived as visitation counts over many simulated runs. Parameters are tuned via simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a subset of CAT2000-TRAIN. The model is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and the SFU video dataset, reporting competitive saliency metrics relative to a small set of baselines.

Relation to prior work
This work connects to long-standing bottom-up saliency models (Itti & Koch; AIM; BMS), saccadic models (e.g., Le Meur & Liu), and Bayesian/info-theoretic approaches (Bruce & Tsotsos; Itti & Baldi), as well as optical-flow/brightness constancy constraints for motion. The least-action formulation is an interesting unification and resembles other physics/energy-minimization approaches in vision (e.g., active contours; variational optical flow). Contemporary deep saliency models (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON, MLNet/SAM) are only partly represented; the paper cites Cornia et al. but omits several strong baselines widely used on MIT300/CAT2000.

Strengths
- A principled, compact dynamical system that jointly yields scanpaths and (as a byproduct) saliency maps.
- Unifying idea: curiosity (detail seeking) and brightness invariance (fixation/tracking) are combined within a single variational framework.
- The model is simple and fast to integrate numerically; equations are explicit and tractable.
- Evaluates on both static and dynamic datasets; shows that a lightweight, unsupervised model can be competitive with several classical baselines.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and clarity
- Kinetic energy is written as K(ẋ) = 1/m ẋ^2 (Eq. 8), yet subsequent derivations use ∂K/∂ẋ = m ẋ, which corresponds to K = (m/2)||ẋ||^2. This inconsistency affects dimensional correctness and the Euler–Lagrange equations.
- Several algebraic steps in the Appendix contain typos or questionable simplifications: the determinant D and its bound, repeated indices (b^2x1 twice), and the derivation of the λ < m/4 condition rely on unstated normalization and appear error-prone.
- The “potential” U includes a velocity-dependent term B; while a generalized potential is fine, the sign conventions and the rationale for −ηC in U (with L = K − U) are confusingly explained.

Modeling choices
- The curiosity term is essentially squared gradient magnitude of b and a blurred p, with an ad hoc sinusoidal alternation (cos^2/sin^2) governed by ω. The biological and empirical justification for time-sharing via a fixed frequency is unclear. ω is not reported nor analyzed for sensitivity.
- Brightness constancy ḃ = 0 typically promotes motion along iso-brightness contours; on static images, it biases motion tangential to edges. While this can stabilize fixation on edges, it does not address objectness, faces, or text, which often dominate human fixations.
- The model omits inhibition-of-return, saccade amplitude priors, and oculomotor constraints known to shape scanpaths; alternation between local/peripheral fields alone is unlikely to prevent stickiness in complex scenes.
- A multiplicative center bias is injected into the input (reducing gradients toward the periphery), and then an additional grid search for output blur and a center parameter σ is performed to maximize AUC/NSS. This double center-bias tuning undermines the claim that saliency emerges “as a byproduct” of the dynamics.

Evaluation and reproducibility
- Parameter fitting via simulated annealing is done on 100 images from CAT2000-TRAIN to maximize NSS; details of domains D, ω, m, k, λ ranges, neighbor generation, and final chosen values are not reported. No code or parameter values are provided, limiting reproducibility.
- The MIT300 and CAT2000-TEST comparisons include only a small selection of baselines. Strong contemporary deep methods (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON) that dominate these benchmarks are missing; thus “best score in half of the cases” is not informative relative to the state of the art.
- For SFU videos, it is unclear whether tuning was performed on a separate training split. The pre-processing for videos and the use of bt are under-specified (temporal filtering, frame rate, integrator step size).
- The main promise of the approach is scanpath generation and tracking, yet evaluation is exclusively via static saliency metrics. No scanpath-level metrics (e.g., string edit distance, MultiMatch, saccade amplitude distributions) or tracking accuracy are reported.
- The choice of 199/100 trajectories initialized near the image center induces further center bias. The sensitivity to initialization and the number of trajectories is not analyzed.

Originality and significance
- The least-action framing for gaze dynamics is elegant and potentially impactful, but conceptually overlaps with prior energy-based or dynamical accounts of attention/oculomotor control. The specific combination here is reasonably novel, though the curiosity term is quite rudimentary.
- Empirical significance is limited by the evaluation methodology, heavy reliance on center bias/blur tuning, and absence of competitive baselines. As presented, it is difficult to conclude that the proposed principles, rather than the center bias and metric-specific tuning, drive the performance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the Lagrangian inconsistencies; provide a clean derivation with correct kinetic energy and determinants, and state all assumptions (normalization of b, bounds on bx).
- Report all hyperparameters (m, k, λ, ηb, ηp, ω), SA settings, and provide code. Analyze sensitivity to ω and initial conditions.
- Provide ablations: remove brightness-invariance term; remove cos^2/sin^2 alternation; use only b vs p; remove center-bias multipliers and only apply the dynamics; analyze the effect on both scanpaths and saliency metrics.
- Evaluate scanpaths against human data (e.g., MultiMatch, KiT, saccade amplitude distributions), and quantify tracking of moving objects where ground truth is available.
- Compare against strong modern baselines on MIT300/CAT2000 (DeepGaze II, SALICON, SAM/DeepFix/MLNet), and use standardized evaluation servers/splits for video (e.g., DHF1K).
- Reduce or justify post-hoc center bias and blur tuning, or clearly position them as baselines; alternatively, fix them on a training set and evaluate on disjoint test sets.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The conceptual unification via a variational principle is appealing, and the model is simple and fast. However, technical derivations need correction, several modeling choices are ad hoc and under-justified, reproducibility is low, and the empirical evaluation does not convincingly establish advantages over strong baselines or validate scanpath predictions. With stronger theory, clearer reporting, ablations, and comprehensive evaluations (including scanpaths), this line could become impactful.

Reviewer confidence: High on modeling/evaluation, medium on specific dataset details.
