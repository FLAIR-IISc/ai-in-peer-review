- Proposal: Introduces EYMOL, a variational (least-action) model that derives differential equations for attention, producing scanpaths with saliency maps as byproducts.
- Evaluation: Tested on image (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and video (SFU) benchmarks and shows competitive performance, best in ~half the cases.
- Theoretical grounding: Builds on feature integration theory and Itti’s model but uses a variational principle rather than feature combination or learned approaches.
- Strength — Novel math: Elegant variational derivation linking attention dynamics to physics (least action principle).
- Strength — Scanpaths: Generates dynamic scanpaths (not just static saliency) providing a fuller attention representation.
- Strength — Unified framework: Integrates curiosity-driven attention and brightness invariance in a single model.
- Strength — Experiments & speed: Adequate benchmark validation and real-time computational efficiency.
- Weakness — Theoretical depth: Lacks analysis of stability, uniqueness, and full parameter sensitivity of the differential equations.
- Weakness — Related work: Insufficient differentiation from prior physics-inspired attention models (missing/ref unclear [28]).
- Weakness — Overstated claims: Claims about newborns and developmental neuroscience are not supported by the model.
- Weakness — Scanpath analysis: Paper focuses on static saliency metrics instead of detailed comparisons of generated scanpaths to human scanpaths.
- Weakness — Parameter justification: Simulated annealing tuning and metric choices (e.g., NSS) and bounds lack justification; instability claims unsupported.
- Weakness — Limitations discussion: Missing analysis on complex scenes, low-contrast/ambiguous images, and trade-offs vs. CNN-based methods.
- Tech concern — Curiosity term: Alternating local/peripheral modulation (cos² and sin²) lacks justification or neuroscientific evidence.
- Tech concern — Approximation (6): No quantitative analysis of error introduced or conditions of validity.
- Tech concern — Eq. (40) constraint: λ < m/4 requirement is stated but its practical impact on behavior is unexplored.
- Tech concern — Video evaluation: SFU test is small (12 clips, 15 observers); temporal coherence and tracking claims are weakly supported.
- Comparison: Modest gains over SOTA; learning-based models often outperform, and the paper should justify why its simpler/physics-based approach is preferable.
- Overall scores: Quality 7/10, Clarity 8/10, Originality 6/10, Significance 6/10.
- Recommendation: Weak Accept.
- Suggested revisions: Add rigorous theoretical analysis (stability/parameter sensitivity), clearer comparison to physics-inspired models, thorough scanpath similarity analyses, and a fuller limitations discussion.
- Final takeaway: Promising principled approach with elegant math and scanpath focus, but needs stronger theoretical, empirical, and neuroscientific grounding to establish novelty and practical advantage.
