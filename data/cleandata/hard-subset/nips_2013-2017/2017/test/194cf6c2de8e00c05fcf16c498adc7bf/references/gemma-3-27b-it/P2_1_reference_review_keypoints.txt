- Proposes EYMOL, a variational model of visual attention using a least-action principle where saliency maps emerge as a byproduct of scanpath generation.  
- Model combines three principles: boundedness, curiosity (image detail), and brightness invariance.  
- Authors derive differential equations governing eye movements and generate scanpaths from those dynamics.  
- Saliency maps produced by the model perform competitively on MIT, CAT2000, and SFU benchmarks despite not being explicitly optimized.  
- Claims the model can run in real time, enabling practical applications.  
- Novel variational approach offers a fresh, potentially more biologically plausible perspective compared to typical saliency-focused models.  
- Strong theoretical foundation with mathematical derivations provided in the appendix.  
- Unified framework integrates exploration and fixation mechanisms within a single model.  
- Parameter estimation via simulated annealing raises concerns about sensitivity to initial conditions and search space choices.  
- Solving differential equations imposes computational overhead that may limit scalability in resource-constrained settings.  
- Limited biological validation; weak linkage to neurophysiological data like single-cell or fMRI studies.  
- Presentation can be dense and mathematical; needs more intuitive explanations, especially for cost functions C and B.  
- The claim that saliency is “only” a byproduct is somewhat overstated and defensive.  
- Technically sound overall: correct derivations and a well-defined experimental setup.  
- Clarity is generally acceptable but hampered by dense notation and insufficient discussion on parameter tuning.  
- Originality is high: a novel variational framing and proper citation of related work.  
- Significance is promising: models both static and dynamic scenes and could have substantial impact if validated and optimized.  
- Pro-accept reasons: novelty, solid theory, competitive results, unified framework, and real-time potential.  
- Con-accept concerns: parameter sensitivity, lack of biological validation, computational complexity, and accessibility of presentation.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Suggested revisions: provide parameter sensitivity analysis, expand discussion linking principles to neural mechanisms, improve intuitive explanations, and soften the “saliency as byproduct” framing.
