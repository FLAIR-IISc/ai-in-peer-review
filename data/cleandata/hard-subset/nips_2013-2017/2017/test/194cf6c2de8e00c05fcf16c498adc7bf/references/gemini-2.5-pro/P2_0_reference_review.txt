### Summary

This paper presents a novel model of visual attention, named EYMOL, based on variational principles from classical mechanics. Instead of computing a saliency map directly, the authors model the scanpath of the eye's focus as the trajectory of a particle governed by the principle of least action. The corresponding Lagrangian is constructed from three core principles: 1) a bounding potential to confine the trajectory to the "retina", 2) a "curiosity" potential that attracts the focus to regions of high detail (image gradients), and 3) a "brightness invariance" term, identical to the optical flow constraint, that encourages fixation on static objects and tracking of moving ones. By solving the resulting Euler-Lagrange equations, the model generates a dynamic scanpath. Saliency maps are then derived as a byproduct by aggregating the visitation frequency of locations over multiple simulated trajectories. The model is evaluated on standard image (MIT300, CAT2000) and video (SFU) saliency datasets, where it achieves competitive, and in some cases state-of-the-art, performance compared to established models.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations from the proposed Lagrangian is a standard procedure in calculus of variations and appears to be correctly executed in the appendix. The experimental methodology is solid: the authors use well-established public datasets and standard evaluation metrics for saliency prediction. The use of simulated annealing to find model parameters is a reasonable approach.

A major strength is that the empirical results strongly support the model's validity. Despite its relative simplicity and lack of end-to-end training on saliency maps, EYMOL performs remarkably well against complex, data-hungry deep learning models (e.g., SALICON, SAM-ResNet). This suggests that the underlying principles (curiosity and brightness invariance) are powerful explanatory concepts for bottom-up attention. The authors are also careful to test an approximation (V1) against the full model (V2) for static images, justifying their use of the simpler version.

However, a key weakness in the evaluation is the disconnect between the model's primary output and the metrics used. The model's core contribution is a dynamic *scanpath*, yet the evaluation is entirely based on static, aggregated *saliency maps*. The paper would be significantly stronger if it included an analysis of the generated scanpaths themselves, comparing properties like saccade length distribution, fixation durations, and scanpath similarity to human data. Without this, the claim of providing a "truly model of eye movements" is not fully substantiated by the experiments.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly laid out in the introduction, contrasting the proposed approach with the dominant "saliency-map-first" paradigm. The three driving principles are explained intuitively before being formalized mathematically in Section 2. The mathematical formulation, while dense, is clear for a reader familiar with variational mechanics. The detailed derivation in the appendix is a welcome addition that aids reproducibility.

However, several crucial details are missing, which hinders full reproducibility. The values of fixed parameters like the particle mass `m` and the frequency `ω` for the curiosity term are not specified. The exact procedure for pre-processing, such as the parameters for the Gaussian blur to create the peripheral input `p(t, x)` and the central bias blob, is not detailed. While the parameters `(ηb, ηp, λ)` are learned, reporting their final values would be beneficial.

**Originality:**

The paper is highly original. While individual components have appeared elsewhere (e.g., attraction to high-contrast edges is a classic saliency feature, and the brightness invariance term is the foundation of optical flow), synthesizing them within a unifying variational framework of least action is a novel and elegant contribution. The conceptual shift from predicting saliency maps to modeling the underlying dynamics of the scanpath is a significant departure from mainstream research in computational attention. This physics-inspired approach provides a fresh perspective on a well-studied problem. It clearly distinguishes itself from prior work, which is adequately referenced.

**Significance:**

The work is significant for several reasons. First, it offers a compelling, principled, and compact model for bottom-up visual attention that is not just a black-box function approximator. Its success suggests that simple, physically-grounded laws can explain a substantial component of human gaze behavior. Second, the framework elegantly unifies mechanisms for exploring detailed regions (saccades via the curiosity term) and stabilizing gaze (fixation/tracking via the brightness invariance term) which are often treated separately. This unification is a key theoretical contribution. Third, the fact that a non-learning-based model can compete with state-of-the-art deep models is an important result, suggesting that current benchmarks may be solvable without necessarily capturing the full complexity of human cognition, but rather by approximating a few core underlying principles. Researchers in both computational neuroscience and computer vision are likely to find these ideas interesting and build upon them.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  **High Originality and Conceptual Novelty:** The variational framework provides a fresh, principled, and elegant alternative to the dominant saliency map paradigm.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The model achieves surprisingly competitive results on standard benchmarks, lending strong support to the validity of its underlying principles.
3.  **Unifying Framework:** The model successfully integrates mechanisms for both saccadic exploration and fixation/tracking within a single set of differential equations.
4.  **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper clearly articulates its core ideas and provides the necessary mathematical derivations.

### Arguments for Rejection:

1.  **Mismatch between Model Output and Evaluation:** The primary claim is about modeling scanpaths, but the evaluation is solely on static saliency maps. This is a significant missed opportunity to validate the model's core dynamic predictions.
2.  **Missing Details for Reproducibility:** Several key hyperparameters and pre-processing steps are not specified, making it difficult for other researchers to reproduce the results exactly.

### Overall Assessment:

This is a strong, original, and thought-provoking paper. The central idea of modeling visual attention via a principle of least action is both elegant and effective, as demonstrated by the impressive experimental results. The main weakness is the lack of evaluation on the scanpath dynamics themselves. However, the strength of the conceptual contribution and the quality of the saliency prediction results outweigh this limitation. This work presents a significant contribution that is likely to inspire new research directions. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors include more implementation details for reproducibility and consider adding a scanpath analysis, if possible, for the final version.
