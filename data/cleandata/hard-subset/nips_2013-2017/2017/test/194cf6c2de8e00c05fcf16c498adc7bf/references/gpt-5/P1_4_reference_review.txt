Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a variational formulation for bottom-up visual attention in dynamic scenes. The authors define a Lagrangian whose stationary points yield Euler–Lagrange equations governing the eye’s focus of attention (scanpath). The energy has: (i) a bounding potential to keep trajectories within the image; (ii) a “curiosity” term that attracts the trajectory to high-detail regions, combining local and peripheral gradients via a time-varying cos^2/sin^2 schedule; and (iii) a brightness-invariance penalty that encourages the optical-flow brightness constancy constraint bt + ∇b·ẋ ≈ 0, argued to induce fixation in static scenes and facilitate tracking in dynamic scenes. The resulting ODEs are integrated numerically; saliency maps are obtained as a byproduct by counting trajectory visits over multiple runs. Parameters are tuned by simulated annealing on a small training subset to maximize NSS. The method is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000 (images) and SFU (videos) and compared against several classical and recent models.

The work connects to long-standing bottom-up saliency (Itti & Koch) and machine learning approaches (Judd et al., CNN-based). It also relates to variational optics (brightness constancy) and dynamic saliency. The novelty claimed is a unified physics-inspired ODE for scanpaths and tracking, with saliency emerging from trajectories.

Strengths
- Conceptually elegant, unified variational framework that links curiosity-driven exploration and brightness constancy into a single dynamical system for scanpaths.
- Produces full scanpaths rather than only static saliency maps, enabling analysis of fixation and tracking behaviors in one model.
- Simple, largely parameter-light formulation built from low-level signals; numerically tractable and reportedly real-time.
- Empirical results reported across both image and video datasets; the approximation to brightness invariance in static images is shown to behave similarly to the exact form.

Weaknesses and technical concerns (Quality)
- Derivational inconsistency in the kinetic energy: Eq. (8) sets K(ẋ)=1/m ||ẋ||^2, yet the subsequent Euler–Lagrange derivation uses ∂K/∂ẋ = m ẋ (leading to mẍ). This mismatch suggests a mistake; typically K=(m/2)||ẋ||^2. The correctness of the derived dynamics depends on this.
- The determinant/stability condition derivation (Eqs. 37–47) assumes b normalized in [0,1] and yields λ < m/4, but this uses gradient magnitudes while bounding the signal range; ∥∇b∥ can far exceed 1 even if b∈[0,1]. The stated bound may not ensure D>0 in practice. This calls into question well-posedness guarantees.
- The curiosity term alternation C(t,x)=||∇b||^2 cos^2(ωt)+||∇p||^2 sin^2(ωt) is ad hoc; ω is not specified, and there is no ablation justifying this particular time-scheduling versus a more principled combination.
- Simulated annealing details are insufficient for reproducibility: acceptance probability P(), neighborhood definition, domain D and α are not specified; only three parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) appear tuned while other impactful constants (m, k, ω, blur σ, integration step, initial condition variance) are unspecified.
- The saliency pipeline introduces strong center bias twice: multiplying b and p by a central Gaussian and initializing trajectories near the center. On top of that, blur and center bias are tuned via grid search to maximize AUC-J and NSS. These choices risk inflating scores on center-biased datasets and undercut the claim that saliency is a byproduct of the dynamics.
- Evaluation lacks key baselines and analyses: no comparison with stronger modern models (e.g., DeepGaze/SALICON/MLNet/GBVS), limited metrics reported, no ablations (e.g., without center bias; curiosity vs brightness-invariance only), and no scanpath-level comparisons (e.g., saccade amplitudes, sequence similarity to human paths). The “tracking” claim is qualitative; no quantitative tracking measure is provided.
- For videos, bt estimation (temporal derivatives), numerical differentiation, and robustness to noise are not described.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but there are multiple typos and some equations are ambiguous or incorrect (noted above). Important implementation details are missing (parameter values, ω, integrator settings, runtime). Some references are incomplete, and tables mentioned are not shown in the provided text.

Originality
- The specific unification via a Lagrangian and derivation of explicit EL equations for attention is interesting and, to my knowledge, not common in the saliency literature. However, the components (gradient-based attraction, brightness constancy) are classical, and the alternation mechanism is heuristic. Related work on dynamic saliency/variational formulations could be discussed more comprehensively.

Significance
- If technically sound and more rigorously evaluated, a principled dynamical model that yields scanpaths and competitive saliency could be useful for both cognitive modeling and practical attention modules. As presented, technical inconsistencies and evaluation choices (center bias; limited baselines) reduce confidence in the empirical gains and limit impact.

Questions for the authors
1. Please correct/clarify the kinetic energy definition and re-derive the EL equations consistently. What exact L do you use in code?
2. How are ω, m, k, and the Gaussian center-bias parameters chosen? Please provide all hyperparameters and sensitivity analyses.
3. How is bt computed for videos, and how robust is the model to temporal noise?
4. What happens without the pre-multiplication center bias and center-initialization? Provide results and ablations for fairness.
5. Justify the cos^2/sin^2 alternation and provide ablations over ω and over using fixed convex combinations.
6. Provide quantitative scanpath analyses and tracking metrics to support the behavioral claims.
7. Expand baselines to include strong recent deep saliency models and report standard metrics (AUC variants, NSS, CC, KL, SIM).

Pros
- Elegant variational formulation; unified treatment of fixation and tracking.
- Produces scanpaths directly; simple, fast ODE integration.
- Competitive results claimed on multiple datasets.

Cons
- Mathematical inconsistencies (kinetic term; stability bound).
- Heavy and tuned center bias and limited ablations undermine empirical claims.
- Missing implementation details; reproducibility concerns.
- Limited baselines and metrics; lack of scanpath/tracking evaluation.

Overall assessment
Borderline reject. The core idea is appealing and potentially impactful, but the current version has derivational errors and an evaluation protocol that likely overstates performance due to center bias and limited baselines. With corrected mathematics, full implementation details, stronger and fairer evaluations (including ablations and scanpath analyses), this could become a solid contribution.
