Summary
This paper proposes a physics-inspired, variational formulation of visual attention in dynamic scenes. The authors define a Lagrangian with three components: (i) a confining potential that keeps the gaze within the retinal bounds; (ii) a curiosity term that alternates between local detail (|∇b|2) and peripheral, blurred detail via a cos2/sin2 temporal modulation; and (iii) a brightness invariance penalty (the optical flow constraint) to encourage fixation and enable motion tracking. From this, they derive Euler–Lagrange equations governing a continuous-time scanpath (EYMOL). Saliency maps are obtained a posteriori by counting visits across multiple simulated trajectories. Parameters (weights for curiosity and brightness invariance) are tuned by simulated annealing on CAT2000-TRAIN to maximize NSS. The model is evaluated on static (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and dynamic (SFU) datasets, reporting competitive performance vs. classical and some modern baselines; they also argue that V1 (using an upper bound to the brightness-invariance term) is as effective as the exact formulation on static images.

Relation to prior work
Classical saliency models (Itti & Koch; info-max; surprise) are largely global, computing a saliency map and then applying winner-take-all plus heuristics for scanpaths. Le Meur and others introduced saccade biases. Deep models (Judd; Vig; modern CNNs) learn saliency from data. This paper differs by making scanpath the primitive and the saliency map an emergent property, with a unified dynamical system that can, in principle, explain both saccades and tracking. The use of the optical flow constraint for fixation/tracking aligns with vision literature, but embedding it in a least-action framework for attention is novel.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a unified variational principle for attention that yields explicit Euler–Lagrange dynamics, rather than post hoc scanpath heuristics. Saliency arises as a byproduct.
- Elegant unification: curiosity-driven exploration and brightness invariance for fixation/tracking are combined cleanly in one Lagrangian, applicable to both static and dynamic scenes.
- Some analytical care: explicit derivation, discussion of stability/well-posedness (constraints on λ), and a tractable approximation (V1) for static images.
- Empirical evidence that this simple, minimally trained model is competitive on established benchmarks, including videos, with real-time potential.
- Minimal supervision: only a few parameters tuned on a small subset; the rest follows from the dynamics.

Weaknesses and questions
- Clarity/rigor: Several typographical/notation issues (e.g., unusual kinetic term K=1/m|ẋ|2 rather than m|ẋ|2/2; repeated bx1 in Eq. (42); inconsistent references/footnotes; incomplete citations). These should be corrected; currently they impede reproducibility and raise doubt about some derivations.
- Design choices insufficiently justified: the cos2/sin2 alternation between local and peripheral fields and the choice of ω are intriguing but ad hoc; no ablation on their necessity/sensitivity. Likewise, the role of mass m is underexplained.
- Center bias and blur: the input is multiplied by a Gaussian center bias and an additional grid search over blur/center parameters is used to optimize metrics. This confounds claims that saliency “emerges” solely from the variational dynamics; please quantify performance without these post hoc tweaks.
- Limited ablations: No systematic study of each term’s contribution (disable curiosity, disable brightness invariance, change ηb/ηp/λ), nor of V1 vs V2 on dynamic scenes.
- Experimental scope: The reported comparisons are promising but somewhat sparse for 2017-era saliency (many strong deep baselines exist). Tables aren’t included here; please ensure fair, recent baselines on MIT300/CAT2000/SFU with standard metrics, and provide details about runtime and number of trajectories per image (199) vs. accuracy trade-offs.
- Reproducibility: No code or parameter values are provided; the SA procedure is described but not sufficiently to reproduce exact results.

Quality
Technically sound in spirit and mostly correct, but some derivational typos and the nonstandard kinetic term need to be addressed. Empirical validation is encouraging yet would benefit from stronger baselines, ablations, and bias controls.

Clarity
The paper is readable and well-motivated, but clarity suffers from typographical errors, inconsistent notation, and incomplete references. The appendix derivation is helpful; tightening it and fixing errors would substantially improve readability.

Originality
High. The least-action framing of attention dynamics that unifies saccades and tracking, with saliency as a byproduct, is a fresh and bold idea compared to global-map-centric approaches.

Significance
Moderate to potentially high. If validated more extensively and made reproducible, this could spur a line of work on physics-inspired, learning-light attention models and on integrating top-down terms into a principled dynamical system.

Pros
- Novel and elegant formulation; unifies exploration and fixation/tracking.
- Saliency emerges from dynamics; applicable to videos without redesign.
- Competitive performance with very few tuned parameters.

Cons
- Clarity/derivation issues; some questionable equations.
- Reliance on center bias/blur post-processing and metric tuning.
- Limited ablation and scope of comparisons; reproducibility concerns.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix derivational typos and clarify the kinetic term and parameter roles (m, ω).
- Provide ablations (term contributions, V1 vs V2 on video, sensitivity to ηb/ηp/λ/ω).
- Report results without center bias/extra blur; quantify their effects.
- Compare to stronger contemporary baselines and include runtime/trajectory budget analysis.
- Release code and parameter settings for reproducibility.
- Explore scanpath-level comparisons to human sequences (as you note for future work).

Recommendation
Weak accept. Despite imperfections in clarity and evaluation breadth, the core idea is novel, bold, and potentially impactful. With improved presentation and more thorough experiments, this could be a valuable contribution to NeurIPS.
