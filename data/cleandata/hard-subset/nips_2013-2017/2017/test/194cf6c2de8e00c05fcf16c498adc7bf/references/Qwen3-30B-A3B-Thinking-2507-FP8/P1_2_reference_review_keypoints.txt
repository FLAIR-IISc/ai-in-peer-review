- Proposal: Introduces EYMOL, a variational, physics-inspired model of visual attention combining boundedness (harmonic oscillator), curiosity (gradient attraction), and brightness invariance, with saliency as a byproduct of simulated dynamics.  
- Method: Derives a Lagrangian and Euler–Lagrange motion equations to drive attention trajectories; implements and simulates dynamics to produce saliency.  
- Evaluation: Tested on standard image (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and video (SFU) datasets and shows competitive performance versus existing saliency models.  
- Practical advantage: Model is computationally simple and can run in real time compared to complex, trained neural models.  
- Unified scope: Same framework handles both static images and dynamic videos.  
- Experimental design: Uses standard datasets and metrics; parameter estimation via simulated annealing is reasonable.  
- Theoretical grounding: Mathematical derivations are sound and detailed in the appendix for reproducibility of the theory.  
- Novelty claim: Conceptually novel application of variational principles that unifies curiosity-driven attention and brightness invariance.  
- Limited theoretical insight: Unclear why least-action/variational principle is the correct or uniquely informative choice for modeling attention beyond existing psychological/neuroscience accounts.  
- Lack of statistical testing: Results are presented without statistical significance analysis to show differences are meaningful.  
- Superficial related-work comparison: Insufficient comparison to prior physics-inspired and variational attention models (notably [29]) and limited differentiation from prior work.  
- Limited limitations discussion: Paper does not thoroughly analyze shortcomings, especially regarding how well temporal dynamics of human eye movements are captured.  
- Reproducibility concerns: Implementation details and exact parameter values used in experiments are insufficiently specified.  
- Modest impact: Contribution is practical and modestly useful (efficiency, unified framework) but does not substantially outperform or revolutionize the field.  
- Clarity issues: Paper is well-organized and derivations clear, but motivation for least-action principle and links to attention literature could be better explained for broader readers.  
- Originality nuance: Approach is novel in framing but the competitive performance may stem from simplicity rather than the variational formulation itself.  
- Acceptance arguments: Novel theoretical framing, efficiency, unified static/dynamic handling, and competitive empirical results support acceptance.  
- Rejection arguments: Incremental novelty, missing statistical rigor, incomplete distinction from prior work, and reproducibility gaps argue against acceptance.  
- Final decision: Reviewer recommends weak acceptance, contingent on addressing statistical analysis, clearer positioning vs prior work, and better discussion of limitations.  
- Concrete improvements: Add statistical significance tests, expand comparison with [29] and other physics/variational models, clarify differences from other physics-inspired approaches, provide full implementation parameters, analyze temporal dynamics fidelity, and compare to recent neural saliency models.
