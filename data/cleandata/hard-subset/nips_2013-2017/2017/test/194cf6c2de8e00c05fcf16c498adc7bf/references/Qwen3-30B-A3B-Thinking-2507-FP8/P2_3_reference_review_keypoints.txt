- Proposes a novel variational framework modeling eye movements as particle trajectories derived from a generalized least action principle.  
- Defines a potential field from three principles: retina boundedness, curiosity-driven attraction to high-detail gradients, and brightness invariance for fixation/tracking.  
- Generates scanpaths via Euler–Lagrange differential equations; saliency maps emerge as a byproduct of simulated attention dynamics.  
- Evaluates on standard benchmarks (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, SFU) and reports competitive performance using NSS and AUC-Judd.  
- Strong mathematical derivation with a detailed appendix and an ablation study comparing approximate (V1) and exact (V2) brightness invariance.  
- Implementation details and parameter estimation (via simulated annealing) are provided, enabling reproducibility.  
- Clear writing and organization, though the physics derivation may challenge readers without classical mechanics background.  
- Originality is high: unifies curiosity-driven attention and brightness invariance under a physics-inspired variational framework.  
- Addresses both static and dynamic scene attention within a single principled model.  
- Practical advantages include simplicity and real-time capability, offering a mechanism for eye movements rather than only saliency prediction.  
- Lacks thorough comparison to recent deep learning/CNN-based methods and unclear statistical significance of performance gaps.  
- Limited justification for using simulated annealing over other optimization methods.  
- "Curiosity" principle needs more precise definition in terms of visual features and biological plausibility.  
- Insufficient discussion of limitations handling complex scenes and of connections to actual human visual attention mechanisms.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to strengthen comparisons with recent methods, clarify limitations, and better justify methodological choices.
