

# Review of "Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes"

This paper proposes a novel model of visual attention based on variational principles, deriving differential equations for eye movement scanpaths using a generalized least action principle. The authors claim their EYMOL (EYe MOvement Laws) model unifies curiosity-driven attention and brightness invariance principles, with the saliency map emerging as a byproduct of the scanpath model. The paper is organized into mathematical formulation, experimental setup, and results on image and video datasets.

## Strengths
The paper presents an interesting theoretical framework that connects visual attention to variational principles, which is conceptually novel. The mathematical derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations is generally sound, and the experimental results on saliency detection are promising, with the model competing well with state-of-the-art approaches on standard benchmarks.

## Critical Weaknesses

### 1. Technical Notation and Formatting Errors
The paper contains numerous technical notation inconsistencies that undermine its credibility. Key examples include:
- Inconsistent use of subscripts (e.g., "b2x" instead of "b_x^2" in Equations 4, 6, and 15)
- Missing mathematical symbols (Equation 3 should be "k ∑_{i=1,2}...")
- Inconsistent derivative notation (Equation 13 uses "d/dt" but Equation 23 uses "d/dt" with inconsistent spacing)
- Unclear notation in Equation 14 where "Vx" is defined without specifying which variable it's differentiating with respect to

### 2. Methodological Flaws
The paper claims the model "competes well with state-of-the-art models" but provides insufficient evidence:
- No statistical significance testing for the performance comparisons (Table 2 and Table 3)
- Algorithm 1 mentions "P()" for acceptance probability but doesn't define it
- The paper states "the approximation is very good" for the brightness invariance approximation (Equation 6) but doesn't provide quantitative evidence of this claim
- The paper claims the model "can conveniently be simplified" but provides no justification for why this approximation is valid

### 3. Theoretical Inconsistencies
The theoretical derivation contains significant errors:
- In Appendix Equation 26: "b̈(t, x, ẋ, ẍ) = d/dt ḃ" should be "b̈(t, x, ẋ, ẍ) = d/dt \dot{b}"
- The derivation of Equation 28 contains an error in the expansion of "d/dt Bẋ"
- The paper claims "D > 0" must hold for well-posedness, but Equation 42 shows D = m(m - 2λ(b_x1^2 + b_x2^2)), which would be negative if λ > m/(2(b_x1^2 + b_x2^2)), contradicting the paper's claim

### 4. Presentation and Clarity Issues
- The paper contains grammatical errors and awkward phrasing (e.g., "it turns out that visual attention is interwound with high level cognitive processes, so as its deep understanding seems to be trapped into a sort of eggs-chicken dilemma")
- The paper references "NIPS 2017" in the header, suggesting it's an older submission that should be updated to current conference standards
- The paper claims "the model can conveniently be simplified" but doesn't explain why this simplification is reasonable or what specific aspects of the model are affected
- The paper states "the proposed approach such a computation is obtained as a byproduct" but doesn't clearly explain why this is novel compared to other scanpath models

## Recommendation

Despite the interesting theoretical framework, the paper suffers from numerous technical errors, inconsistent notation, and insufficient experimental justification that significantly undermine its credibility. The mathematical derivation contains errors that would prevent replication, and the experimental results lack statistical rigor. The paper's claims about the model's novelty and performance are overstated relative to the evidence provided.

While the conceptual approach is intriguing, the numerous technical flaws and lack of sufficient detail to replicate the work make this paper unsuitable for publication at NIPS. The authors should thoroughly revise the mathematical derivations, fix the notational inconsistencies, and provide more rigorous statistical validation of their claims before resubmission.

This paper would be better suited for a journal with more lenient standards for mathematical rigor, but it does not meet NIPS' requirements for technical precision and reproducibility. The number and severity of the technical errors are too significant to overlook, even considering the paper's conceptual novelty.
