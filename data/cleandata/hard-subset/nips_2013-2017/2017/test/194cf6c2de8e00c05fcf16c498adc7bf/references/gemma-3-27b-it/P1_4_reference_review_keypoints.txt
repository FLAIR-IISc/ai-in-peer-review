- Overall assessment: interesting variational model linking curiosity and brightness invariance; reviewer leans weak accept pending major revisions.

- Main contribution: proposes a Lagrangian variational formulation that yields differential equations for a dynamic “focus of attention,” unifying saccades and tracking.

- Relation to prior work: departs from saliency-map approaches and connects attention modeling to the principle of least action and earlier biological theories.

- Strength — novelty: the variational/least-action approach is original and offers a new perspective on attention modeling.

- Strength — unified framework: aims to model both saccadic movements and object tracking within one coherent model.

- Strength — theoretical foundation: derives Euler–Lagrange equations, giving solid mathematical grounding.

- Strength — empirical promise: achieves competitive results on standard static and dynamic benchmarks given model simplicity.

- Strength — practical potential: authors claim real-time capability, a practical advantage.

- Weakness — limited experiments: experimental validation is superficial and needs deeper, more rigorous comparisons to state-of-the-art.

- Weakness — parameter estimation: reliance on simulated annealing is computationally expensive and the choice of a 100-image SA batch then full-dataset testing is arbitrary and possibly biased.

- Weakness — parameter sensitivity: model sensitivity to parameter choices is not sufficiently analyzed.

- Weakness — mathematical clarity: main text lacks intuitive explanations of the equations and the Lagrangian terms; heavy math in appendix needs clearer exposition.

- Weakness — simplifying assumptions: use of grayscale images and Gaussian peripheral blur may limit real-world applicability and need discussion.

- Weakness — lack of qualitative analysis: missing visual comparisons between generated scanpaths and human scanpaths to assess human-likeness.

- Weakness — incomplete justification: brightness invariance implementation and approximation (equation 6) lack justification and discussion of impact.

- Scores summary: Quality 7/10, Clarity 6/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 6/10.

- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, strong math foundation, competitive benchmarks, unified saccade/tracking model.

- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient experimental rigor, parameter/SA concerns, missing qualitative scanpath comparisons, clarity issues.

- Key recommendations: expand experiments and ablations, analyze parameter sensitivity and alternatives to SA, add qualitative scanpath visualizations, improve intuitive exposition of math, and justify approximations.
