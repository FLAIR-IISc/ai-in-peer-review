### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel, physics-inspired model of visual attention, termed EYMOL (EYe MOvement Laws). The central thesis is that visual scanpaths, for both static and dynamic scenes, can be described as the trajectory of a particle that minimizes a generalized action functional. This variational approach is built upon three core principles: 1) **Boundedness**, which confines the scanpath within the visual field using a harmonic potential; 2) **Curiosity**, an attractive force pulling attention towards regions of high detail (large image gradients), with a mechanism to alternate between local and peripheral information to prevent getting stuck; and 3) **Brightness Invariance**, a principle derived from the optical flow constraint (`db/dt = 0`) that encourages fixation on iso-brightness contours in static images and tracking of moving objects in dynamic scenes.

By defining a Lagrangian incorporating these principles, the authors derive the Euler-Lagrange equations of motion, a set of second-order differential equations that govern the scanpath `x(t)`. Unlike most contemporary models in visual attention which directly compute a static saliency map, this work's primary output is the temporal scanpath itself. Saliency maps are then generated as a byproduct by aggregating the locations visited over many simulated trajectories. This approach distinguishes the work from the classic feature-integration framework of Itti & Koch and subsequent saliency models, including recent deep learning methods, which typically treat scanpath generation as a post-processing step. The model's parameters are optimized using simulated annealing, and its performance is evaluated on standard image and video saliency benchmarks, where it demonstrates competitive results.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations from the proposed action functional is detailed in the appendix and appears correct. The claims of competitive performance are well-supported by experimental results on standard public datasets (MIT300, CAT2000, SFU) using established evaluation metrics (AUC, NSS, etc.). The authors are careful to evaluate two versions of their model (an exact and an approximate form for the brightness invariance term) and justify their choice for the main experiments. The work constitutes a complete contribution, presenting a full theoretical framework, its implementation, and a thorough empirical evaluation against relevant baselines.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a good overview of the field and clearly situates the proposed work. The core principles of the model are explained intuitively in Section 2.1 before the mathematical formalism is introduced. However, some aspects could be clarified. The motivation for the specific form of the curiosity term `C(t, x)`—an oscillating combination of local and peripheral gradient information—is stated as avoiding "trapping," but this mechanism is not analyzed or discussed in detail. The role and selection of several model parameters, such as the particle mass `m`, the boundary potential constant `k`, and the oscillation frequency `ω` in the curiosity term, are not discussed, while the tuning of `(ηb, ηp, λ)` is. A more complete discussion of parameter sensitivity would improve the reproducibility and understanding of the model's behavior. The appendix is mathematically dense and would benefit from more explanatory text connecting the steps.

**Originality:** The originality of this work is its primary strength. Framing the problem of visual attention within the calculus of variations and the principle of least action is a highly novel approach in this domain. While individual components are familiar—the optical flow constraint is a classic concept from Horn & Schunck [20], and saliency is often linked to gradients—their integration into a single, unified Lagrangian is new and elegant. This provides a compelling alternative to the dominant paradigms of feature-based saliency maps or end-to-end learned models. The unified treatment of saccadic exploration (driven by curiosity) and fixation/tracking (driven by brightness invariance) within a single dynamical system is a significant conceptual contribution.

**Significance:** The paper's results are significant. It demonstrates that a model derived from a few simple, first principles can achieve performance competitive with, and in some cases superior to, heavily engineered or purely data-driven models. This suggests that the proposed principles may capture fundamental aspects of data-driven attention. The model's main contribution is a generative process for scanpaths, not just a static saliency map. This is a more complete model of attention and opens up new avenues for evaluation, such as comparing scanpath statistics (saccade amplitudes, fixation durations) with human data, a direction the authors rightly identify for future work. The framework is general and could potentially be extended to incorporate top-down influences or other principles, making it a valuable foundation for future research.

A weakness in the evaluation, however, is that despite the model's primary output being a scanpath, the comparison to other work relies entirely on static saliency maps. This fails to fully showcase the unique temporal dynamics of the model. A direct comparison against other scanpath models (e.g., Le Meur & Liu, 2012) on metrics that evaluate scanpath similarity would have been more compelling and better aligned with the paper's core contribution.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **High Novelty:** The variational framework based on a least action principle is a genuinely new and elegant perspective on modeling visual attention.
*   **Conceptual Unification:** The model provides a single, coherent mathematical system that naturally accounts for both exploratory saccades and fixation/tracking behaviors.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The model achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results on multiple standard benchmarks for both images and video, validating the power of its underlying principles.
*   **Generative Scanpath Model:** The work moves beyond static saliency to produce dynamic scanpaths, representing a more comprehensive model of the attention process and a significant contribution to the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   **Clarity on Model Details:** The justification for certain modeling choices (e.g., the specific formulation of the "curiosity" term) and the selection of key hyperparameters (`m`, `k`, `ω`) are not adequately explained or analyzed.
*   **Evaluation Mismatch:** The experimental evaluation focuses on static saliency maps, which does not fully exploit or validate the model's primary strength as a temporal scanpath generator. A comparison of scanpath dynamics would be more fitting.
*   **Biological Plausibility:** The model is presented as a physical analogy (a particle moving in a potential field), and the link between minimizing a global action integral and the underlying neural computations in the brain is not discussed, limiting its explanatory power from a neuroscience perspective.
