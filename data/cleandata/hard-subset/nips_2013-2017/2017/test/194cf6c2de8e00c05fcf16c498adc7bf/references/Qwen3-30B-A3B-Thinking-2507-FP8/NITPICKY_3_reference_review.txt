

# Review of "Variational Laws of Visual Attention for Dynamic Scenes"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling visual attention using variational principles, inspired by physics' least action principle. The authors model eye movements as trajectories governed by three principles: boundedness (retina constraints), curiosity (attracted to high-gradient regions), and brightness invariance (for fixation and tracking). The resulting Euler-Lagrange equations form the basis of their "EYMOL" model, which generates saliency maps as a byproduct of scanpath simulation. The paper evaluates the model on image and video datasets, claiming competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods.

## Strengths
- The paper presents a theoretically grounded approach that unifies several aspects of visual attention under a single variational framework.
- The model's simplicity and real-time capability are appealing for practical applications.
- The experimental setup is well-structured with appropriate datasets (MIT1003, CAT2000, SFU) for evaluation.

## Weaknesses

### Technical and Notational Inconsistencies
The paper suffers from severe notational inconsistencies that undermine its technical credibility. In Equation (4), "b2x" is ambiguous - does it represent b²x or b_x²? This appears repeatedly throughout the paper (Equations 4, 15, 16, 17, 20, etc.). In Equation (3), the summation index is written as "i=1,2" instead of "i=1,2" with a comma, which is a formatting error that could confuse readers. The treatment of partial derivatives is inconsistent: sometimes "bt" is used for ∂b/∂t, while "bx" represents ∂b/∂x, but these notations are not clearly defined.

### Mathematical Errors
The appendix contains several mathematical errors. In Equation (21), the derivative of B with respect to ẋ is incorrectly calculated. The correct derivative should be ∂B/∂ẋ = 2(bt + bxẋ)bx, but the paper states it as "2 (bt + bxẋ) bx" without clarifying that B = (bt + bxẋ)². This leads to errors in Equation (23) where the time derivative is computed. The derivation of Equation (26) is particularly problematic - it claims "b̈ ≡ b̈(t, x, ẋ, ẍ)" but fails to properly express the second time derivative of b, which should account for both direct time dependence and dependence through x(t) and ẋ(t).

### Theoretical and Conceptual Issues
The paper claims novelty in unifying curiosity and brightness invariance principles, but this is not clearly demonstrated. The model's reliance on the approximation in Equation (6) for static images (B̄(t, x, ẋ)) is not sufficiently justified. The paper states this approximation "significantly simplifies the motion equations," but the resulting equations (Equation 39) are still complex and require careful parameter selection (Equation 40) to maintain well-posedness.

The paper's claim that "the computation of the saliency maps only arises as a byproduct of a model of scanpath" is misleading. The saliency map is computed by summing visited locations over multiple runs, which is a standard approach in scanpath models. This is not a unique feature of their method.

### Experimental Design and Reporting
The parameter estimation procedure using simulated annealing is described but lacks sufficient detail. The paper states they "used a batch of a 100 images" (redundant "a"), but doesn't explain why 100 images were sufficient for parameter estimation. The choice of 199 runs for image data (Section 3.2) is arbitrary and unexplained.

The paper claims "despite its simplicity, our model reaches best score in half of the cases," but without the actual tables (Table 2 and 3), this claim cannot be verified. The evaluation metrics (NSS, AUC-Judd) are mentioned but not thoroughly explained or justified.

### Formatting and Presentation
The paper contains numerous formatting errors that distract from its technical content:
- "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017), Long Beach, CA, USA." is an obvious typo
- Inconsistent spacing around mathematical symbols (e.g., "b2x" vs "b_x²")
- Inconsistent use of italics for variables (e.g., "x" vs "x")
- The reference list has incomplete information (e.g., missing years and page numbers)

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework, the numerous technical inconsistencies, mathematical errors, and unclear notations significantly undermine its credibility. The paper's claims about novelty and performance are not sufficiently supported by the presented evidence. The writing is often ambiguous, and the mathematical derivations contain errors that would prevent reproducibility. The paper's quality falls below the expected standard for NIPS, where technical precision and clarity are paramount.

I recommend rejection due to these substantial technical and presentation flaws that prevent the paper from being evaluated as a sound scientific contribution. The authors would need to completely revise the paper to address these issues before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.
