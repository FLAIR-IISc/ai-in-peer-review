Summary
The paper proposes a variational model of visual attention (EYMOL) in which scanpaths arise as stationary points of an action functional. The Lagrangian comprises: (i) a boundary potential to keep the trajectory within the “retina,” (ii) a curiosity term that alternates between local gradient magnitude of brightness and a blurred (peripheral) version, and (iii) a brightness-constancy penalty to promote fixation and tracking. The Euler–Lagrange equations yield a second-order ODE in 2D controlling gaze. Saliency maps are computed as visit-counts over many runs. Parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) are tuned with simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a subset of CAT2000-TRAIN. The method is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU videos, with claims of competitive or superior results on several metrics, and the authors argue the model runs in real time.

Strengths
- A conceptually coherent, physics-inspired dynamical formulation that unifies saccades, fixation, and tracking in one system.
- Saliency emerges as a byproduct of explicit scanpath dynamics rather than being optimized directly.
- The model is simple and potentially efficient; it operates on low-level cues and claims to work on both images and videos.
- The derivation attempts to be explicit, with an appendix providing the EL equations.

Major concerns and weaknesses
Technical correctness and derivations
- Kinetic energy is given as K(ẋ) = (1/m) ẋ^2, but later d/dt ∂L/∂ẋ produces m ẍ. This is inconsistent (the standard is K = (m/2)‖ẋ‖^2). The current form is dimensionally and analytically inconsistent with subsequent equations.
- The boundary potential V uses Iverson brackets, making Vx discontinuous at the boundaries, violating smoothness assumptions needed for EL derivations; no smoothing is provided.
- The inequality used for the approximation B̄ appears to invoke the “parallelogram law,” but the bound used is a simple algebraic/Young’s inequality; the justification is imprecise.
- Several derivation typos/mistakes in the appendix: e.g., D = m(m − 2λ(bx1^2 + bx1^2)) should be bx1^2 + bx2^2; several symbols are reused ambiguously (bx both as vector and scalar magnitude), and there are sign/notation inconsistencies. The claimed bound λ < m/4 assumes normalized gradients and constant m, but b and its gradients are later multiplied by a Gaussian “center bias,” changing their scale; this affects the bound.
- The curiosity term C mixes squared gradient magnitudes with cos^2(ωt) and sin^2(ωt), but ω is not specified, learned, or justified; alternation is asserted to avoid trapping without analysis.
- Video case: how bt is computed is unspecified (temporal smoothing, finite differences, frame rate dependence), and stability of the velocity-dependent terms is not analyzed.

Experimental design and evaluation
- Heavy center bias: both (a) multiplying inputs by a Gaussian blob and (b) initializing each run near the center introduce strong center bias. Then blur and center parameters are grid-searched to maximize AUC-Judd/NSS on training data. This likely inflates AUC/NSS, which are known to be sensitive to center bias; sAUC/shuffled-AUC or IG should be reported to control for this.
- Saliency maps are constructed from 199 runs initialized “almost at the center” with small velocities; no justification for 199, no analysis of sensitivity to initialization, and this further hard-codes early central fixations.
- Parameter tuning: only ηb, ηp, λ are tuned via SA on a small subset of CAT2000; other critical parameters (m, k, ω, Gaussian blur widths, solver tolerances, step size) are not specified. The acceptance probability P() in SA is undefined; neighborhood definition, proposal distribution, and α are underspecified.
- Claims of “statistically equivalent” between V1/V2 are made without reporting numbers, confidence intervals, or tests. Tables 2–4 are referenced but not included; numerical results are absent, preventing verification.
- Baselines are limited and somewhat dated given NeurIPS timelines (e.g., no comparisons to strong contemporaneous deep models such as DeepGaze II, SALICON variants, SAM-ResNet, MLNet on MIT300/CAT2000 with officially reported scores; in video, no comparison to recent deep video saliency models). Fairness of comparisons (with or without center bias) is unclear.
- SFU evaluation is on 12 clips only; “evidence of tracking moving objects” is anecdotal; no tracking metrics, nor qualitative figures here.
- No ablations: the contributions of the curiosity term, brightness constancy (exact vs approximate), boundary potential, center bias, and number of runs are not analyzed.
- Reproducibility: solver choice (SciPy which integrator?), tolerances, numerical stability strategies, preprocessing details (the algorithm that defines the “rectangular zone”), and runtime are not specified, yet “real-time” is claimed.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., “interwound,” “eggs-chicken,” “psedo-code,” subject–verb agreement), and formatting artifacts (e.g., “st Conference …”).
- Notation is often ambiguous (bx sometimes vector, sometimes magnitude; ḃ, b̈ overloaded; ∂bt appears). Footnotes are referenced but missing/garbled.
- References are incomplete (years/venues missing), inconsistent formatting, and some key related works (dynamic gaze models, contemporary deep saliency) are under-cited.
- Important modeling choices (ω, m, k, normalization ranges, image/video preprocessing) are not specified. The “retina” rectangle detection is vague.

Originality and significance
- The variational/Lagrangian framing for gaze dynamics is interesting and, to my knowledge, underexplored in recent saliency literature. However, potential field/gaze dynamics models have been studied (e.g., Le Meur and others). The novelty claim would benefit from a clearer contrast to prior dynamical systems approaches and modern probabilistic scanpath models.
- Without robust, transparent, and up-to-date empirical validation (including bias-controlled metrics and strong baselines), significance is limited. The current evaluation and reporting do not convincingly demonstrate state-of-the-art performance or unique insights.

Pros
- Unified dynamical formulation connecting fixation and tracking.
- Simple, interpretable components; saliency arises from scanpaths.
- Potentially efficient and applicable to both images and videos.

Cons
- Multiple technical inconsistencies in the derivation; missing parameters and unclear numerical details.
- Heavy, implicit center bias and metric choices likely inflate scores; missing bias-controlled metrics and ablations.
- Missing quantitative results in the paper body; outdated baselines; unclear statistical testing.
- Clarity, notation, and reference issues hinder reproducibility and assessment.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is appealing, but the paper in its current form has substantial technical, experimental, and clarity issues. I encourage the authors to:
- Fix the Lagrangian/EL derivations (correct K, smooth boundary potentials, rigorously handle velocity-dependent terms), and provide a stability analysis.
- Specify all parameters and numerical settings; define SA acceptance and proposals.
- Evaluate with bias-controlled metrics (sAUC, shuffled-AUC, IG), remove/ablate center bias, and report full tables with CIs.
- Include strong, current baselines; add ablations and sensitivity analyses; provide qualitative scanpaths and tracking metrics.
- Substantially improve writing, notation, and references.
