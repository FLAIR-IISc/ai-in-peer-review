This paper proposes a novel variational approach to modeling visual attention, framing it as a problem of finding scanpaths driven by “curiosity” (detail) and “brightness invariance.” The model utilizes a least-action principle, resulting in differential equations that govern the focus of attention. The authors evaluate the model on static images (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and dynamic scenes (SFU) by comparing predicted saliency maps with human fixation data.

The work builds upon the established idea of attention as a bottom-up process initiated by salient features (e.g., Itti et al., Koch & Ullman). However, it deviates from the dominant paradigm of constructing explicit saliency maps as an intermediate step. Instead, saliency emerges as a byproduct of simulating scanpaths governed by the proposed variational principles. This contrasts with recent learning-based approaches (e.g., Judd et al., convolutional neural networks for saliency) that directly predict saliency maps. The use of a least-action principle, drawing analogies to physics, provides a distinct theoretical foundation compared to previous models. The authors appropriately cite relevant work, including foundational papers in visual attention and more recent advancements.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The variational approach, framing attention as a continuous optimization problem guided by “curiosity” and “brightness invariance,” is a genuinely novel contribution. The least-action principle offers a unique theoretical perspective.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The mathematical formulation and derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations provide a solid theoretical basis for the model. The paper explicitly outlines the derivation in the appendix.
*   **Unified Framework:** The model attempts to address both saccadic eye movements and object tracking within a single framework, which is commendable.
*   **Competitive Performance:** Despite its simplicity, the model achieves competitive results on standard saliency benchmarks, sometimes surpassing other approaches, indicating the viability of the proposed principles.
*   **Clear Mathematical Formulation:** The equations are presented clearly and concisely, enabling reproduction and further investigation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The model's performance relies on carefully tuned parameters (ηb, ηp, λ). The simulated annealing procedure, while used, introduces complexity and potential for suboptimal parameter selection. A more systematic analysis of parameter sensitivity is needed.
*   **Biological Plausibility:** While inspired by biological principles, the specific mathematical formulation of "curiosity" and "brightness invariance" may not directly correspond to known neural mechanisms. Further investigation of the biological plausibility is warranted.
*   **Limited Insight into Scanpath Diversity:** The model primarily focuses on generating plausible scanpaths that align with human fixations. However, it does not provide much insight into *why* scanpaths differ between individuals or under different task conditions.
*   **Simplifications:** The use of a Gaussian blob to introduce a center bias and the approximations made in the brightness invariance term (e.g., inequality (6)) represent simplifications that might limit the model's accuracy in certain scenarios.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** While the math is clear, some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the concepts and their biological relevance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to modeling visual attention.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the viability of the proposed principles and competitive performance on standard benchmarks.
*   The work offers a unique perspective on attention as a continuous optimization process.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The model's reliance on parameter tuning and the lack of detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity are concerns.
*   The biological plausibility of the specific mathematical formulation needs further investigation.
*   The model’s ability to explain complex scanpath variations is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a strong contribution to the field of visual attention modeling. It offers a novel and theoretically sound approach that yields promising results. While some weaknesses exist, particularly regarding parameter sensitivity and biological plausibility, the strengths of the work outweigh these concerns. The work is suitable for NIPS, given its originality, theoretical depth, and empirical validation. Minor revisions addressing the clarity of explanations and a more thorough discussion of parameter sensitivity are recommended.
