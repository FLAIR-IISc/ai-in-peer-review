- Proposes EYMOL, a variational model of visual attention deriving scanpaths from a least-action principle, with saliency maps as a byproduct.  
- Grounds model in curiosity (detail seeking) and brightness invariance principles and formulates dynamics via Euler–Lagrange equations.  
- Demonstrates competitive performance on static image and dynamic video benchmarks, comparing to established saliency methods.  
- Technical approach is interesting and mathematically sound, with a correct derivation of the governing differential equations.  
- Connection between chosen Lagrangian terms and neurophysiological mechanisms is weak and somewhat tenuous.  
- Simulated annealing for parameter estimation is reasonable but limited by small batch sizes and unclear parameter justification.  
- Brightness invariance term is simplified; the impact of this approximation is acknowledged but not fully explored.  
- Claim of being a "true model of eye movements" is overstated; the model is computationally inspired by eye movements rather than biologically proven.  
- Mathematical sections are dense and hard to follow; rapid introduction of many parameters reduces clarity.  
- Experimental section is better but missing crucial implementation and preprocessing details (numerical integration, blur/center-bias specifics).  
- Originality is high: variational scanpath generation and treating saliency as emergent are novel contributions.  
- Significance is moderate: results are promising but improvements over existing methods are not substantial enough for a major breakthrough.  
- Limited experimental validation, lacking thorough ablation studies and quantitative tracking evaluation.  
- Strengths: novel, mathematically grounded framework; competitive benchmarks; fresh perspective on saliency dynamics; potential for biologically inspired extensions.  
- Weaknesses: sparse experiments, dense math, weak neuro justification, moderate gains, and no quantitative proof of tracking claims.  
- Recommendation: Reject with encouragement to revise.  
- Suggested revisions: improve mathematical and experimental clarity, add ablation studies and quantitative tracking results, strengthen neurophysiological justification, and discuss brightness invariance limitations in more depth.
