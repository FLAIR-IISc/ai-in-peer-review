Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a dynamical systems model of visual attention derived from a variational (least-action) principle. The state x(t) ∈ R2 denotes the gaze location; an action integrates a Lagrangian with kinetic energy and three potential-like terms: (i) a boundary “retina” confinement, (ii) a curiosity term that attracts gaze to high local/peripheral brightness gradients with a time-varying alternation, and (iii) a brightness-invariance term corresponding to the standard optical-flow brightness constancy constraint, intended to promote fixation and, in videos, motion tracking. Euler–Lagrange equations (with a velocity-dependent “potential”) yield second-order ODEs for the focus of attention (EYMOL). Parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) are tuned via simulated annealing on a 100-image subset to maximize NSS. Saliency maps are obtained as a byproduct by counting trajectory visits over multiple runs; a center-bias Gaussian and blur are tuned by grid search. The method is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000 (images) and SFU (videos), claiming competitive results against classic and CNN-based models.

This connects to early saliency models (Itti & Koch), information/surprise-based saliency, and learning-based saliency (e.g., Judd et al., Vig et al., CNN/LSTM models), while also relating to optical-flow methodology. Using variational mechanics to derive scanpath dynamics is less common in saliency modeling, though dynamical and potential-field approaches to saccadic generation exist (e.g., Le Meur & Liu), and the brightness constancy term is standard in motion estimation.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The Lagrangian and resulting EL derivations appear correct, including the handling of the velocity-dependent brightness term and the determinant condition on λ. The approximation B̄ for static images is reasonable and the empirical claim that it matches the exact form is plausible.
- Modeling choices: The curiosity term C(t,x) = |∇b|^2 cos^2(ωt) + |∇p|^2 sin^2(ωt) and its periodic alternation are not theoretically justified beyond an intuitive anti-trapping rationale; ω is not motivated, estimated, or analyzed. The boundary potential uses indicator functions that create non-smooth forces at edges; implications for integration stability are not discussed.
- Empirical evaluation: The method’s main claim is scanpath dynamics, yet the quantitative evaluation is almost entirely via saliency-map metrics. There is no comparison to human scanpath statistics (e.g., saccade amplitude distributions, fixation durations, MultiMatch, string-edit distances) or temporal correlations. The added Gaussian center bias and blur are tuned to improve AUC/NSS and the initial conditions are “almost center,” which inflate center-biased metrics; sAUC is not reported despite being designed to control for center bias. Ablations are limited to exact vs. approximate brightness term; contributions of curiosity vs. brightness invariance vs. center bias vs. initialization are not quantified. Runtime claims are not backed by timings.
- Reproducibility: Key details (ω, m, k, exact parameter ranges, numerical solver tolerances, preprocessing “zone” detection) are missing; code availability is not stated.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, with a clear high-level narrative and an appendix for derivations. However, several presentation issues remain: occasional grammatical errors, incomplete/inconsistent references, and some vague descriptions (e.g., “an algorithm identifies the rectangular zone...”). Figures/tables are referenced but not shown here; assuming they exist, the text still needs complete parameter listings to enable reproduction.

Originality
The unification of curiosity (edges/texture) and brightness constancy within a Lagrangian framework for scanpath generation is a novel combination. The idea that saliency maps arise as a byproduct of explicit scanpath dynamics is appealing. That said, the components are largely hand-crafted and individually known (gradient-based saliency, brightness constancy), and dynamical systems for saccade generation have precedent. Novelty is thus moderate rather than groundbreaking.

Significance
If robust and well-validated, a principled, lightweight ODE model yielding scanpaths and saliency would be useful for explainable attention modeling and potentially for resource-constrained settings. The reported competitiveness on benchmarks is promising. However, without stronger, center-bias-controlled evaluation and scanpath-level validation, and given the prevalence and strength of modern deep saliency methods, the current impact is limited. The paper reads more as an interesting modeling proposal with suggestive results than a definitive advance.

Strengths
- Conceptually elegant variational formulation; closed-form EL equations and parameter constraints.
- Unified treatment of fixation and motion tracking via brightness invariance.
- Saliency maps derived as a byproduct of scanpath dynamics.
- Competitive numbers reported on multiple datasets despite model simplicity and minimal learned features.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on hand-crafted gradient features, periodic alternation, and tuned center bias; limited theoretical justification for ω and alternation.
- Evaluation focuses on saliency-map metrics, with no scanpath-level comparisons to human data.
- Center bias and initialization likely inflate AUC/NSS; sAUC not reported; limited ablation.
- Reproducibility gaps: missing hyperparameters, preprocessing specifics, runtime, and code.
- Limited novelty relative to prior dynamical and feature-based saliency models.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report sAUC, shuffled NSS, CC, SIM, KL, and IG; explicitly analyze the effect of center bias and initializations. Include results with and without Gaussian center bias and with non-central initializations.
- Provide scanpath-level validation against human data (e.g., MultiMatch, trajectory similarity, saccade amplitude/direction distributions, fixation durations).
- Add ablations: remove curiosity term, remove brightness term, vary ω, and vary λ, ηb, ηp to demonstrate each component’s contribution and sensitivity.
- Justify or learn ω; consider asynchronous or state-dependent switching between local and peripheral fields rather than fixed cos^2/sin^2.
- Clarify numerical integration details, parameter ranges, units, normalization, and stability; provide timing.
- Ensure fair comparisons (use benchmark servers whenever possible) and include stronger contemporary baselines.
- Improve references (complete bibliographic info; include recent deep saliency methods) and release code.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The modeling idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the current empirical validation is not sufficiently rigorous (especially regarding center bias and scanpath metrics), and some modeling choices are ad hoc without analysis. With the suggested evaluations and clarifications, this could become a solid contribution. My confidence is moderate.
