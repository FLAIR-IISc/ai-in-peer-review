Summary
This paper proposes a physics-inspired, variational formulation of visual attention for dynamic scenes. The authors define a Lagrangian whose stationary point yields Euler–Lagrange equations governing the scanpath. The model combines: (i) a confinement potential to keep gaze within the retinal bounds; (ii) a “curiosity” drive favoring regions with high brightness gradients, alternated with a peripheral (blurred) field via cos2/sin2 modulation; and (iii) a brightness invariance penalty akin to the optical-flow constraint, encouraging fixation and motion tracking. Solving the resulting second-order ODE generates a trajectory; saliency maps arise as a byproduct by counting visit frequencies over many runs. Parameters are tuned via simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000. The method is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000 (images), and SFU (videos), with competitive results against classic and deep saliency models. An exact and an approximate (upper-bound) version of the brightness term perform similarly on static images.

Relation to prior work
Most prior saliency models postulate a centralized saliency map (Itti & Koch and many successors), with scanpaths produced by heuristic WTA and inhibition-of-return rules. Learning-based models (SVMs, CNNs/LSTMs) learn map predictors directly. There are also information-theoretic (surprise/information maximization) and bio-inspired dynamic saccade simulators. In contrast, this work derives scanpath dynamics from a unifying least-action principle; the saliency map is not primary but emerges from dynamics. The use of brightness constancy links to classical variational optical flow (Horn–Schunck). The paper could also be related to optimal control/active sensing/active inference formulations, which are not cited.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a compact, principled variational formulation that unifies curiosity, fixation, and tracking in a single dynamical system. Saliency maps emerge as a consequence rather than a design target.
- Scope: the same equations apply to static and dynamic scenes, with plausible qualitative behavior (saccades, object tracking).
- Simplicity and interpretability: few components, clear physical analogies, no massive training; competitive performance compared to more complex learned systems.
- Empirical signal: on standard saliency benchmarks, the model is competitive and sometimes best on several metrics, despite being lightweight.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Clarity/technical correctness: Several equations and derivations contain inconsistencies or typos that undermine confidence. Examples: K(ẋ)=1/m ẋ^2 is unconventional (one expects 1/2 m|ẋ|^2); repeated indices and possible algebraic slips in the determinant D and the bound λ<m/4; vocabulary calls B a “potential” though it is velocity-dependent (closer to a dissipation/Rayleigh term). These should be corrected and units/sign conventions made consistent.
- Missing details: ω for the cos2/sin2 alternation, values of m, k, numerical integration step/solver and stability considerations are not specified. Only ηb, ηp, λ are tuned; what about m,k,ω and Gaussian parameters before the final blur/center-bias grid search?
- Evaluation fairness and biases: The model applies a center bias and post-blur tuned to maximize AUC-Judd/NSS, initializes trajectories near the center, and aggregates 100–199 runs per stimulus. This risks inflating performance on center-biased datasets. Ablations (no center bias, single-run vs multi-run, curiosity-only vs brightness-only) are necessary to assess the contribution of the proposed dynamics.
- Limited behavioral validation: Since the output is fundamentally a scanpath generator, it is a missed opportunity not to evaluate scanpath similarity to human data (saccade amplitude distribution, temporal order, MultiMatch, string-edit measures). The reliance on saliency map metrics alone weakens the claim of modeling eye movements.
- Video evaluation is on a small dataset (12 clips), and claims of real-time should be quantified. The cost of 100 runs per video should be reported.
- Related work coverage: Missing connections to active sensing/optimal control (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler’s optimal search), active inference, and modern dynamic attention models.

Quality
The idea and high-level derivation are sound and appealing. However, several mathematical details need correction/clarification, and the experimental protocol should include stronger ablations and behavioral tests. Despite these issues, the core proposal is coherent and potentially impactful.

Clarity
The paper is mostly readable but contains grammatical issues, typos, and notation inconsistencies. The appendix is helpful but also where several confusions appear. A careful pass to fix equations, define all symbols, and report hyperparameters is needed.

Originality
High. Framing attention scanpaths as solutions to Euler–Lagrange equations with a curiosity term and brightness invariance in a single Lagrangian is, to my knowledge, novel in the saliency literature.

Significance
Moderate to high. If polished, this could open a fruitful direction for physics-based generative models of attention that connect to control/estimation and potentially integrate with learned feature extractors. Even if not SOTA across all metrics, the conceptual contribution is valuable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the kinetic energy term, the determinant/bound derivations, and clarify the role of velocity-dependent terms (e.g., via Lagrange–d’Alembert/Rayleigh dissipation).
- Report all hyperparameters (m,k,ω, integration scheme/step), computational costs, and sensitivity analyses.
- Provide thorough ablations: remove center bias/post-blur, vary number of runs, curiosity vs brightness components.
- Evaluate scanpath similarity to human data on MIT1003/CAT2000 (e.g., MultiMatch, scanpath KL, amplitude distributions), not only saliency metrics.
- Compare to dynamic/active models and discuss relations to active inference/optimal control; consider learning the Lagrangian weights end-to-end.
- Release code.

Pros for acceptance
- Bold, principled, and unified formulation of attention dynamics.
- Generates scanpaths and saliency as a byproduct.
- Competitive results without heavy learning.
- Potential to stimulate new research at the intersection of variational methods, control, and attention.

Cons against acceptance
- Mathematical/notation issues that need correction.
- Evaluation relies on center bias and multi-run aggregation; lacks behavioral scanpath analysis.
- Missing hyperparameter and computational details; limited video evaluation.

Recommendation: Weak Accept (6/10)
Confidence: 3/5
