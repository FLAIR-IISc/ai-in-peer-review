Summary
The paper proposes a mechanistic, variational account of overt visual attention. The authors define a Lagrangian with three components: (i) boundary confinement via a quadratic “retinal” potential; (ii) curiosity via attraction to high-gradient regions that alternates over time between local and peripheral (blurred) gradients; and (iii) brightness invariance via a soft optical-flow constraint. The Euler–Lagrange equations yield a second-order ODE system (EYMOL) whose numerical integration generates scanpaths. Saliency maps are obtained as occupancy counts over many simulated runs. Parameters are tuned by simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000-TRAIN to maximize NSS. The model is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video, claiming competitive results and emergence of tracking behavior.

Relation to prior work
The paper positions itself against global saliency-map computation (Itti-Koch, Bayesian surprise, information maximization, SVM- and CNN-based models) by offering a dynamical, physics-inspired model where saliency maps are byproducts. It cites classical feature integration theory, early saliency models, and recent deep methods (e.g., SAM). It also references saccadic models (Le Meur) and optical flow. However, comparisons appear limited to a small set of older baselines; many strong contemporary saliency models (e.g., DeepGaze II/III, SALICON, MLNet/SAM-ResNet, EML-NET) are not discussed or compared.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The variational setup is reasonable and the derivations in the Appendix are mostly consistent with velocity-dependent “potential” terms. However, there is at least one apparent error: K(ẋ) is written as (1/m) ẋ^2 in the main text, yet the subsequent EL equations treat K as if it were (1/2) m ẋ^2 (consistent with Eq. 12). This should be corrected since it affects the form of the inertial term. The curiosity term’s periodic modulation (cos^2, sin^2) and the choice of ω are ad hoc and under-justified.
- Empirical support: The paper claims competitive or state-of-the-art results “in half the cases,” but the actual numbers and statistical tests are not present in the text provided (Tables referenced but not shown). The evaluation methodology includes injecting a center bias (multiplying b and p by a Gaussian) and tuning its parameters on training data for AUC/NSS, which can inflate metrics and makes it harder to attribute performance to the proposed dynamics rather than priors or postprocessing. The SA procedure is underspecified (acceptance probability P(), parameter ranges, ω, mass m, λ, ηb, ηp, numerical solver step sizes).
- Completeness: The model evaluation focuses on saliency map metrics, while the central contribution is a scanpath-generating dynamical system. No scanpath-level metrics (e.g., MultiMatch, string-edit distance, temporal NSS, scanpath similarity) or quantitative tracking evaluations are presented, limiting support for the “unified laws” claim.

Clarity
The paper is readable but has numerous notational issues, typos, and formatting glitches. Key hyperparameters (ω, SA details, integration settings) and implementation details are missing, making reproduction difficult. The justification for some design choices (e.g., alternation between local and peripheral gradients) is qualitative; a sensitivity analysis would help.

Originality
Moderately novel. A variational formulation of attention with explicit EL dynamics is conceptually appealing, and the unification of curiosity and brightness-invariance is interesting. Related dynamical/biophysical models exist, but the specific Lagrangian and analysis here are distinctive. However, the alternation mechanism and the center-bias injection reduce the conceptual purity.

Significance
Potentially interesting for researchers seeking mechanistic models of attention and scanpath generation. Empirical significance for saliency prediction is less clear: (i) comparisons to strong contemporary deep models are missing; (ii) gains may hinge on tuned center bias; (iii) the main value may be theoretical elegance rather than practical SOTA. Demonstrating robust scanpath-level agreement and emergent tracking with quantitative metrics would substantially increase impact.

Pros
- Clear, principled dynamical formulation with an appealing connection to least action.
- Unifies saccade generation and fixation/tracking in one ODE system.
- Produces scanpaths in real time; saliency is a byproduct rather than the objective.
- Empirical evaluations across images and videos suggest competitiveness (conditional on better reporting).

Cons
- Missing or incorrect formula (kinetic energy) and several specification gaps (ω, SA details, solver settings).
- Heavy reliance on center bias and post-hoc tuning for metrics; unclear attribution of improvements.
- Lack of scanpath-level and tracking metrics contradicts the central claim.
- Comparisons to strong recent baselines are absent; tables with actual scores are missing from the provided text.
- The curiosity term’s time-oscillatory design is ad hoc; no ablations or sensitivity analysis.

Requested clarifications and suggested experiments
- Correct K(ẋ) and ensure all derivations are internally consistent. Provide all hyperparameters (m, λ, ηb, ηp, ω) and SA details (search space, P(), α, iterations).
- Report complete metrics (AUC-Judd, sAUC, NSS, CC, SIM, KL) with CIs on MIT300 and CAT2000; include modern baselines (DeepGaze II/III, SALICON, SAM-ResNet, EML-NET).
- Provide ablations: remove center bias; remove curiosity or brightness-invariance; V1 vs V2; vary ω; number of trajectories; initial condition dispersion.
- Add scanpath metrics (MultiMatch dimensions, string-edit distance, temporal NSS) and video tracking metrics on SFU or a standard tracking benchmark.
- Analyze robustness and computational cost; release code to ensure reproducibility.

Overall rating: 4 (weak reject)
Justification: The paper offers an elegant and potentially useful mechanistic framework, but the current presentation falls short on technical clarity, empirical rigor, and reproducibility. With corrected derivations, stronger baselines, fuller reporting, ablations, and scanpath/tracking analyses, this could become a compelling contribution. As is, I cannot verify the claimed performance nor the central claim of unified, emergent scanpaths. Confidence: medium.
