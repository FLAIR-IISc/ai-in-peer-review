Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a variational/dynamical-systems model of visual attention (EYMOL) in which a scanpath x(t) evolves as the solution of Euler–Lagrange equations derived from an action functional. The Lagrangian includes: (i) a boundary potential V that confines the trajectory to the retinal/image region; (ii) a “curiosity” term C that alternates between local and peripheral detail via gradient magnitudes of the raw image b and its Gaussian-blurred version p; and (iii) a brightness invariance term B based on the (squared) optical flow constraint, encouraging fixation/tracking along iso-brightness curves. The resulting second-order ODE is integrated numerically; saliency maps arise as a byproduct by counting pixel visits over multiple trajectories and initializations. Parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) are tuned via simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a small batch from CAT2000. Two variants are tested: an exact B for video and an upper-bound approximation B̄ for static images. The model is reported to achieve competitive performance on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video.

This work connects to classic bottom-up saliency (Itti & Koch 1998), information maximization (Bruce & Tsotsos), Bayesian surprise (Itti & Baldi), and dynamical/saccadic models (e.g., Le Meur & Liu; and other physics/Bayesian scanpath models such as Boccignone et al.). The use of the optical-flow constraint for fixation/tracking relates to Horn–Schunck and prior work linking motion/edges to gaze. Recent deep saliency models (e.g., DeepGaze, SALICON, MLNet/SAM) are partially cited (Cornia et al.), though the experimental comparisons to modern deep baselines are limited.

Strengths
- Unified, interpretable framework that generates scanpaths and saliency maps within the same dynamical system, applicable to both static and dynamic scenes.
- Conceptually appealing use of variational principles; the brightness invariance term plausibly ties to fixation and edge/tracking behavior.
- Simple model with few parameters; claimed real-time performance.
- For static images, the approximate B̄ seems to match the exact B empirically, simplifying computation.

Weaknesses
Technical correctness/derivations:
- The kinetic energy is defined as K = (1/m) ||ẋ||^2 (missing 1/2 and with inverted mass), yet subsequent EL equations use mẍ, suggesting inconsistency. Several algebraic steps in the Appendix (e.g., determinant factorization, repeated indices) appear incorrect. The well-posedness condition on λ follows from these steps and is therefore questionable.
- The boundary potential uses indicator functions, inducing non-smooth forces; differentiability and numerical stability are not discussed. No damping term is included; stability of the ODE is not analyzed.

Experimental methodology:
- Center bias and blur are added post hoc and tuned by grid search, which can inflate AUC-type metrics. The video experiment notes tuning on SFU itself, raising train/test leakage concerns. The simulated annealing tuning uses only 100 images; overfitting is plausible, and no validation split or confidence intervals are reported.
- Comparisons omit several strong contemporary deep baselines (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON, SAM-ResNet/MLNet), and there is no scanpath-level evaluation (e.g., MultiMatch, saccade statistics), despite scanpaths being the primary model output.
- No ablation isolating the contribution of C vs. B vs. V, nor sensitivity to ω, λ, ηb, ηp. The paper does not quantify how the brightness invariance term improves fixation/tracking beyond anecdotal statements.

Clarity and presentation:
- Numerous typographical and notation errors, incomplete references, and missing details (e.g., integrator, step size, initial condition distributions, runtime). Tables are described but not shown; claims like “best in half the cases” are difficult to verify.

Originality and significance:
- The conceptual blend of curiosity (gradient energy) and brightness invariance in a least-action framework is interesting but not radically new relative to prior dynamical/physics-based attention models and edge-/motion-driven saliency. The empirical gains over strong modern baselines are not convincingly demonstrated.

Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance
- Quality: Moderate to weak. Theoretical derivations contain inconsistencies; experimental protocol has potential leakage and lacks ablations/statistics.
- Clarity: Below average. The paper contains many typos and mathematical inaccuracies; related work coverage is incomplete.
- Originality: Moderate. Variational framing is elegant, but components (gradient-based curiosity, optical flow constraint) are well-known; similar dynamical models exist.
- Significance: Limited in current form. The idea could be a useful, interpretable baseline; however, without rigorous evaluation against state-of-the-art and scanpath metrics, and with methodological issues, impact is uncertain.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, unified dynamical formulation that directly generates scanpaths.
- Interpretable principles; potentially useful as a fast baseline.
- Promising qualitative behavior (fixations along edges; potential tracking) and some competitive metrics.

Cons for acceptance
- Mathematical inconsistencies and likely errors in derivations.
- Evaluation protocol concerns (center-bias tuning, possible train/test leakage on SFU, small tuning set).
- Missing comparisons to strong deep baselines; no scanpath-level analysis or ablations.
- Clarity/notation issues and incomplete references.

Recommendation: Reject

Constructive suggestions
1) Fix the Lagrangian and derivations: use K = (m/2)||ẋ||^2, re-derive EL equations carefully, and verify factors and conditions (e.g., on λ). Provide a damping term and smooth boundary potential for numerical stability, with an existence/uniqueness and local stability analysis around fixations.
2) Methodology: separate train/val/test splits; avoid tuning on test sets (for SFU, tune on a different video dataset or a held-out split). Report CIs via bootstrapping and do ablations for C, B, and V, as well as ω, ηb, ηp, λ.
3) Comparisons: include strong contemporary saliency models (DeepGaze II, SALICON, MLNet/SAM, EML-NET). Report sAUC/IG to mitigate center bias, and provide results with/without your added center bias.
4) Scanpath evaluation: since the main output is trajectories, add MultiMatch, saccade amplitude/angle distributions, fixation duration distributions, and sequence-level metrics, and compare to human scanpaths.
5) Analysis: quantify when brightness invariance helps (e.g., moving objects, edge-rich scenes). Show sensitivity to initialization and runtime complexity (fps).
6) Related work: expand to include GBVS (Harel et al.), Boccignone’s Bayesian dynamical models, Le Meur’s saccade models, and recent deep saliency literature. Release code to facilitate reproducibility.
