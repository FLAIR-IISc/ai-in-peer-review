Summary
The paper proposes a physics-inspired, variational model of visual attention (EYMOL) in which a scanpath is the trajectory of a particle moving under a Lagrangian composed of: (i) a boundary potential V that keeps the trajectory within the retinal/image bounds; (ii) a “curiosity” term C that attracts the particle to high spatial gradients, alternating between local and peripheral information via a time-varying mixture of gradient magnitudes of the image and its blurred version; and (iii) a brightness-invariance term B that penalizes deviations from the optical flow brightness constancy constraint, intended to promote fixation on edges for static images and tracking in dynamic scenes. The action is minimized (stationary) to yield Euler–Lagrange equations, resulting in a second-order ODE system for gaze. Saliency maps are then computed as visitation heatmaps from many simulated trajectories with random initial conditions. Parameters are tuned via simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a subset of CAT2000. The model is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000 (images) and SFU (videos), and is claimed to be competitive with classical and some learning-based baselines. The paper emphasizes that saliency emerges as a byproduct of an explicit dynamical model of scanpaths rather than an explicit saliency predictor.

Relation to prior work
The paper positions itself against centralized saliency map computation (e.g., Itti & Koch; Bruce & Tsotsos; Judd et al.; more recent CNNs such as Vig et al. and Cornia et al.) and saccadic simulators that post-process a saliency map with winner-take-all and heuristics (inhibition-of-return, proximity). It connects the B term to classic brightness constancy from optical flow (Horn–Schunck) and to psychophysics (edge fixation). There is also related work on generative/active models of gaze and saccades (e.g., Le Meur & Liu), and deep saliency with explicit center bias modeling (DeepGaze, SALICON, MLNet) that could be more comprehensively discussed, especially regarding scanpath prediction vs saliency map prediction.

Quality
- Theory: The variational formulation is interesting and potentially unifying. However, several technical issues need attention.
  - The kinetic energy term K(ẋ) = 1 mẋ2 seems dimensionally incorrect (likely (m/2)|ẋ|^2). Units and constants are not clearly justified.
  - The “potential” B depends on velocity; while the authors acknowledge it’s not a true potential, the consequences for the Euler–Lagrange derivation and stability should be more carefully justified (e.g., relation to generalized forces or Rayleigh dissipation).
  - The determinant D and resulting constraint λ < m/4 are derived under normalization assumptions that are not fully specified; there appear to be typos (b2x1 duplicated), and the bound seems heuristic. A rigorous existence/uniqueness/stability discussion would strengthen the model.
  - The alternating curiosity term C(t,x) using cos^2/sin^2(ωt) is ad hoc; ω selection and its perceptual interpretation are not analyzed, nor is the effect on trapping/escape formally studied.
- Experiments: The empirical evaluation is under-specified in the text provided.
  - Numerical results (exact metrics, tables, statistical tests) are referenced but not shown here. Without explicit numbers and variance across runs, it is hard to verify competitiveness, especially against strong deep baselines.
  - The model introduces a strong center bias twice: by multiplying inputs with a central Gaussian and by initializing trajectories near the center; an additional grid search over blur and center parameters further optimizes AUC/NSS. This raises fairness concerns versus baselines and obscures the contribution of the dynamics. A shuffled-AUC control and a “no center bias” ablation are needed.
  - Parameter tuning uses SA on 100 CAT2000 images, repeated five times; sensitivity analysis, reproducibility across datasets, and robustness to initialization are not reported.
  - No ablation of terms (turning off B, C, peripheral component, boundary term) is reported, nor are runtime and integrator details (time step, ω, m, λ, k) sufficient to reproduce.
  - For videos, the claim of “evidence of tracking” is anecdotal; a quantitative scanpath or motion-tracking metric (e.g., scanpath similarity, MultiMatch) would be valuable.
- Reproducibility: The paper would benefit from code, parameter values, seeds, and precise pre/post-processing details.

Clarity
The paper is readable and the high-level story is clear. The derivations in the appendix are helpful, but there are several typos and notational inconsistencies (e.g., kinetic energy factor, determinant algebra, repeated subscripts), and some steps skip assumptions (smoothness, boundary handling with Iverson brackets). Experimental setup lacks sufficient detail to reproduce results. The role and choice of ω, m, λ, k, and the discretization/integration scheme are not clearly specified.

Originality
The idea of deriving scanpaths from a least-action principle combining curiosity and brightness invariance is novel in this precise form. The ingredients (edge gradients, blurred gradients, brightness constancy) themselves are standard, and scanpath generation via ODEs has precedents, but the unifying variational framing is a creative contribution. The use of an alternating local/peripheral drive is an interesting design choice, though currently heuristic.

Significance
If the model can match modern deep baselines on mainstream benchmarks without training, it would be noteworthy, offering interpretability and real-time simulation of scanpaths. However, the current evidence is insufficiently detailed, and the extensive use of center bias may inflate reported metrics. The contribution may be more conceptual/theoretical than state-of-the-art in accuracy. With stronger, fair evaluations and ablations, the work could impact future attention modeling.

Pros
- Conceptually elegant variational formulation linking curiosity, fixation, and tracking.
- Unified dynamic model producing both scanpaths and saliency maps as a byproduct.
- Simple, interpretable ingredients; potential for real-time simulation.
- Provides explicit equations and some parameter constraints.

Cons
- Technical derivations contain typos/ambiguities; stability and parameter bounds not rigorously justified.
- Heavy reliance on center bias and post-hoc tuning; fairness of comparisons unclear.
- Missing or insufficiently detailed quantitative results and ablations.
- Heuristic choices (alternating C) not theoretically or empirically validated.
- Reproducibility details (parameters, code) absent.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct derivation details (kinetic energy factor, determinant D), clarify assumptions, and provide a stability/existence analysis (e.g., Lyapunov argument or boundedness proof).
- Provide full tables with metrics (AUC-Judd, sAUC, NSS, CC, SIM, KL), confidence intervals, and clear comparative baselines (including deep methods like DeepGaze, SALICON, MLNet) with proper center-bias controls.
- Add ablations: no center bias; no peripheral term; no brightness-invariance term; different ω; initialization away from center; comparison to a pure center Gaussian baseline.
- Report scanpath-level analyses (saccade amplitude and duration distributions, fixation durations, MultiMatch) and quantitative tracking metrics in videos.
- Release code and parameter settings; detail numerical integration and time scaling.
- Discuss biological plausibility and connections to active vision/optimal control literature.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper presents an intriguing and potentially impactful formulation, but the current technical and empirical support is not yet sufficient for NIPS. With clearer derivations, rigorous and fair evaluations, and thorough ablations, this work could become a strong contribution.
