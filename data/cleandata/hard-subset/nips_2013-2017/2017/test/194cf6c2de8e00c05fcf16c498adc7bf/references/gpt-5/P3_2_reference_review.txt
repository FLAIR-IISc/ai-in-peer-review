Summary
The paper proposes a physics-inspired, bottom-up model of visual attention in dynamic scenes. The authors posit three principles that govern gaze trajectories: (i) boundedness within the retinal image via a border “spring” potential, (ii) curiosity for high-detail regions operationalized as local and peripheral squared gradient magnitudes that alternate over time, and (iii) brightness invariance (optical-flow constancy) to support fixation/tracking. These principles define a Lagrangian (kinetic minus a generalized potential), from which Euler–Lagrange (EL) equations describe the scanpath as a second-order dynamical system. Saliency maps are then obtained as a byproduct by counting trajectory visits over multiple runs with random initial conditions. Parameters are tuned by simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000 to optimize NSS; the model is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and the SFU video dataset, claiming competitive results with several baselines.

Relation to prior work
The model contrasts with the dominant saliency-map-first paradigm (Itti & Koch; Bruce & Tsotsos; Bayesian surprise; modern CNNs). It echoes active vision/control perspectives (e.g., brightness constancy from optical flow, energy-based dynamics), and shares with Le Meur & Liu’s saccade modeling the focus on scanpath generation. However, it does not engage with more recent active sensing/optimal control/RL models of gaze or predictive-coding/free-energy accounts, and it largely omits modern deep saliency models beyond a single LSTM-based method.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and interpretability: a compact dynamical system that unifies saccades, fixation, and tracking from a few principles.
- Mathematical transparency: explicit EL derivation and a closed-form system for the accelerations.
- Unifying view: saliency maps arise naturally from the scanpath dynamics rather than being the primary modeled quantity.
- Applicability to video via the brightness-constancy term; real-time feasibility is plausible.
- Empirical results appear competitive on several metrics/datasets despite minimal features.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Technical correctness and modeling choices:
- Kinetic energy is given as K(ẋ) = (1/m) ẋ^2 rather than the standard (1/2) m ||ẋ||^2. This impacts units, the EL equations, and stability conditions; at minimum, justify or correct this.
- The “potential” includes a velocity-dependent term B; in mechanics, this would typically be handled via a Lagrangian with velocity-dependent potentials or with an explicit Rayleigh dissipation function. The interpretation is acceptable, but the paper should position it correctly and discuss implications (e.g., non-conservative forces).
- Boundary potential V uses Iverson brackets, leading to non-differentiability at the borders (the gradient is discontinuous). This is at odds with the smoothness assumptions invoked for the EL derivations and may cause numerical artifacts. A smooth barrier or differentiable soft constraint would be preferable.
- The derivations in the Appendix contain typos/inconsistencies (e.g., repeated indices, missing bx2 in some terms, unclear steps around Eqs. 41–47). The determinant D simplifies to m^2 − 2 m λ ||bx||^2; therefore the stated sufficient condition λ < m/4 is not supported by that algebra unless you bound ||bx||^2 a priori. As written, the bound and the derivation are unclear and likely incorrect.
- The time-varying curiosity term C uses cos^2(ωt) and sin^2(ωt) to alternate between local and blurred gradients. The biological and algorithmic motivation for periodic alternation is not substantiated; the choice of ω and sensitivity to it are not explored. An explicit multi-scale potential without artificial temporal modulation might be more principled.

Experimental design and fairness:
- A strong center bias is injected twice: by multiplying inputs by a central Gaussian before dynamics, and by post-hoc grid search over blur and center-bias parameters to maximize AUC-Judd/NSS. This optimization on training data can greatly inflate center-bias-sensitive metrics and undermines claims that performance arises from the proposed dynamics. Please also report shuffled AUC (sAUC), which controls for center bias, and clearly separate results with and without post-hoc blur/center tuning.
- Parameter tuning via simulated annealing on a small 100-image subset carries overfitting risk; it is not clear whether the final configuration is fixed across all datasets (MIT1003/300, CAT2000, SFU) or re-tuned per dataset. Report the selected parameter values, confidence intervals, and a sensitivity analysis (especially for ηb, ηp, λ, ω).
- Ablation studies are missing. Report performance for: curiosity only; brightness-constancy only; without border term; without pre-imposed center bias; without post-hoc blur. This is crucial to validate each principle’s contribution.
- For video, details on computing bt (temporal derivatives), time step, and numerical integration are absent. The claim of tracking moving objects is anecdotal; include quantitative scanpath/tracking metrics (e.g., dynamic NSS, tAUC, gaze overlap over time) and qualitative examples.
- Reproducibility is limited: code/parameters are not provided, and several implementation choices are unspecified (ω, integration scheme/step sizes, runtime).

Clarity and presentation:
- The manuscript has many typos, incomplete references (missing years/venues), and stray footnote markers. Some equations, indices, and derivations are hard to follow or contain errors. Figures/tables are referenced but not visible here; ensure that final submission has clear tables and properly labeled plots. A careful edit is needed.
- The center bias is a key modeling ingredient but is not integrated into the Lagrangian; presenting it as an external pre/post-processing choice obscures the model’s structure. Consider adding a prior term in U(t, x) to make this explicit.

Originality and significance
- The idea of deriving gaze dynamics from a variational formulation combining brightness constancy and gradient-based attraction is interesting and, to my knowledge, not standard in current saliency literature. The unification of saccades and tracking within one ODE system is a nice conceptual contribution.
- However, the empirical significance is tempered by reliance on center-bias tuning and lack of ablations; without stronger evidence, it is difficult to credit the dynamics (vs. priors/post-processing) for the reported scores. The paper would be more impactful with robust, bias-controlled evaluations and scanpath analyses.

Pros
- Simple, interpretable dynamical model; mathematically grounded.
- Unifies scanpaths, fixation, and tracking; applicable to video.
- Competitive results reported on multiple datasets.

Cons
- Technical derivation issues; unclear/incorrect stability bound; non-smooth boundary potential; unusual kinetic term.
- Heavy reliance on center bias and metric-optimized post-processing; no sAUC; limited ablations.
- Insufficient experimental detail and reproducibility; missing scanpath metrics; limited related work coverage.
- Clarity issues and incomplete references.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The approach is promising and potentially significant, but the paper needs (i) corrected and clarified derivations, (ii) bias-controlled, ablated experiments with reported parameter values and code, and (iii) stronger, clearer empirical evidence (including scanpath evaluation) to support its claims. I encourage a revised submission addressing these points. Confidence: high.
