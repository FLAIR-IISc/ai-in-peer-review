- Proposes a novel variational framework (EYMOL) modeling eye movements as particles in a potential field derived from boundedness, curiosity, and brightness invariance principles.  
- Derives Euler–Lagrange motion equations from a generalized action functional to generate scanpaths; saliency maps are produced as a byproduct.  
- Evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000 (images) and SFU (video), achieving competitive results and top scores in several metrics.  
- Originality: introduces a fresh physics-inspired variational approach unifying curiosity-driven attention and brightness invariance.  
- Theoretical foundation: provides rigorous mathematical derivations and an elegant connection to the least action principle.  
- Competitive performance: strong empirical results given the model’s theoretical simplicity and applicability to static and dynamic scenes.  
- Biological plausibility concern: lacks direct comparison of generated scanpaths’ temporal properties and saccade patterns with human eye-tracking data.  
- Parameter sensitivity concern: heavy reliance on tuned parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) via simulated annealing with insufficient analysis of sensitivity and cross-dataset generalization.  
- Significance: unclear how the model advances understanding of cognitive/neurophysiological attention mechanisms beyond producing saliency maps.  
- Clarity: rigorous but dense mathematical exposition (especially appendix); needs more intuitive explanations and clearer implementation details.  
- Quality: technically sound with careful experiments, but biological justification and scanpath validation are weak.  
- Originality assessment: significant departure from prior work; unification of principles is novel and well-executed.  
- Suggested revisions: add direct scanpath vs. human comparisons, relate predictions to neurophysiology, analyze parameter sensitivity, and clarify contributions to understanding attention.  
- Recommendation: minor revision — publishable after addressing biological validation, parameter analysis, and clarity improvements.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical framework, strong math, competitive results, unified static/dynamic approach.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited biological plausibility evidence, insufficient parameter analysis, and unclear broader significance.
