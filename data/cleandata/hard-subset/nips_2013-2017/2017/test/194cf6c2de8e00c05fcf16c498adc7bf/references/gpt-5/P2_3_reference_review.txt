Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a physics-inspired, variational model of early visual attention. The authors posit three principles: (i) boundedness (harmonic penalties at image borders), (ii) curiosity (attraction to high spatial gradients of brightness at two scales, alternated over time), and (iii) brightness invariance (a soft optical-flow constraint encouraging fixation/consistent tracking). They formulate a Lagrangian L = K − U with a velocity-dependent “potential” for brightness invariance, derive Euler–Lagrange equations for a 2D particle trajectory, and integrate the ODE to generate scanpaths. Saliency maps are then produced as a byproduct by counting visits over multiple runs with random initial conditions. Parameters are tuned by simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a subset of CAT2000; center bias and blur are added via preprocessing and tuned by grid search. The model is evaluated on MIT1003/MIT300/CAT2000 for static images and SFU for videos, and compared to classic and some learning-based saliency baselines.

This connects to classic saliency (Itti & Koch), information/surprise-based models (Bruce & Tsotsos; Itti & Baldi), machine-learned saliency (Judd), and more recent CNN/LSTM approaches. It also relates to optical flow (Horn–Schunck) and to dynamical systems models of saccades. The main novelty is casting curiosity and brightness invariance into a unified variational action whose stationary point yields attention dynamics.

Quality:
- Theory: The derivation follows standard EL machinery for L(x, ẋ, t) with velocity-dependent terms. However, there are technical inconsistencies that raise concerns:
  - Kinetic energy is written as K(ẋ) = 1/m ẋ^2 but later the EL derivation uses ∂K/∂ẋ = m ẋ, which only holds if K = (1/2) m ẋ^2. This inconsistency needs correction.
  - Several algebraic typos (e.g., repeated index in D expression, missing factors of 1/2) and notational slips make it hard to verify the claimed constraint λ < m/4 and the well-posedness condition D > 0.
  - The “brightness invariance” is treated as a generalized potential; more standard mechanics would use a Rayleigh dissipation function or Lagrange–d’Alembert framework for dissipative forces. The paper should justify why a least-action principle is appropriate with velocity-dependent, non-conservative terms and clarify the physical analogy.
  - The curiosity term alternation via cos^2(ωt)/sin^2(ωt) is heuristic; ω is not specified or justified.
- Empirics: The model is simple and fast, and the experiments span static and dynamic datasets. However:
  - Strong center bias is introduced twice (multiplying inputs by a central Gaussian and initializing trajectories near center) and further tuned by grid search to maximize AUC-Judd/NSS. Without sAUC/IG controls, these choices can inflate metrics; sAUC or shuffled-NSS should be reported, and an ablation without center bias is needed.
  - The number of runs (199) and integration time (1s) are arbitrary; no sensitivity analysis is provided.
  - Ablations are minimal. While V1 vs V2 (approximate vs exact brightness term) is compared, the paper lacks ablations for: curiosity-only vs brightness-invariance-only; effect of the two-scale alternation; with/without blur; with/without boundary term; and with/without center bias.
  - Comparisons omit several stronger deep baselines that were state-of-the-art by 2016–2017 (e.g., DeepGaze II/IIE, SALICON variants, MLNet, EML-NET). Claims of being “best in half of the cases” are unconvincing without including stronger baselines and full tables.
  - Video results are qualitative; “tracking” is asserted but not quantitatively assessed (e.g., using scanpath measures such as MultiMatch, string edit distance, saccade amplitude/ISI distributions).
- Reproducibility: Many key details are missing: values/ranges for m, k, ω, ηb, ηp, λ, SA domain D and schedule parameters, ODE solver tolerance/step size, and preprocessing kernel sizes. No code or parameter files are released. As written, experts could not reliably reproduce results.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable and the high-level idea is clear. However, derivations contain errors/typos, and notation is sometimes ambiguous. The appendix should be carefully proofread and corrected. The experimental sections should present full metric tables, statistics, and ablation details. It would help to include pseudo-code for generating scanpaths and saliency maps, and a schematic of the pipeline.

Originality:
- The unifying variational framing of curiosity and brightness invariance is a fresh perspective. While individual components (gradient magnitude, multi-scale cues, brightness constancy) are classic, their combination into a single action functional yielding ODEs for attention dynamics is novel. The idea that saliency maps are a byproduct of simulated scanpaths is also a distinctive stance.

Significance:
- Conceptually interesting and potentially useful as a lightweight, interpretable baseline and as a bridge between control/variational formulations and attention. However, without stronger empirical evidence (state-of-the-art baselines, bias-controlled metrics, ablations, and scanpath validation), the current impact on saliency prediction is limited. The broader significance may lie more in modeling/interpretability than in performance.

Pros:
- Coherent, interpretable model derived from first principles; unifies fixation and tracking.
- Generates scanpaths and saliency in a single framework; efficient, unsupervised.
- Evaluations on multiple datasets; approximation analysis (V1 vs V2) for static images.

Cons:
- Technical inconsistencies in the derivations (kinetic energy, typos) and limited justification for key design choices (ω, parameter constraints).
- Heavy reliance on center bias and lack of bias-controlled metrics/ablations.
- Missing comparisons to stronger modern baselines; incomplete reporting of results.
- Reproducibility concerns (insufficient implementation details, no code).

Questions and suggestions for authors:
- Fix the kinetic energy definition and re-derive EL equations with correct factors; clean up algebra and provide a supplementary with verified symbolic derivations.
- Justify the treatment of the brightness term within a Lagrangian framework or adopt a standard dissipative formulation.
- Report sAUC, shuffled-NSS, IG; include ablations removing center bias and each term in U; provide sensitivity to number of runs/integration time.
- Add stronger baselines and full metric tables with statistical tests; include scanpath metrics for both static and dynamic data.
- Release code and parameters; specify all hyperparameters and solver settings; describe ω selection.
- Explore alternate curiosity terms (e.g., information gain/surprise) and multi-scale schedules beyond fixed sinusoidal alternation.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The paper presents an appealing and original modeling framework, but the current version has technical inconsistencies and insufficient, potentially biased empirical validation. With corrected derivations, stronger and bias-controlled experiments, and improved reproducibility, this work could be a valuable contribution.
