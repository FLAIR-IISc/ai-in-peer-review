- Proposes a variational, physics-inspired Lagrangian model for gaze trajectories combining boundary potential, curiosity (image gradients), and brightness-invariance terms, with scanpaths from Euler–Lagrange solutions and saliency from visit counts.  
- Parameters are tuned via simulated annealing on CAT2000-TRAIN; evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video datasets with competitive results against a few classical baselines.  
- Connects to long-standing bottom-up saliency, saccadic, and Bayesian/info-theoretic models and resembles variational/energy-minimization approaches; contemporary deep saliency baselines are largely omitted.  
- Strength: principled, compact dynamical system that yields scanpaths and saliency maps jointly.  
- Strength: unifies curiosity (detail seeking) and brightness invariance (fixation/tracking) in one framework.  
- Strength: simple, numerically tractable equations and fast integration.  
- Strength: evaluated on static and dynamic data, showing lightweight unsupervised competitiveness with classical baselines.  
- Technical error: kinetic energy expression inconsistent (K written as 1/m ẋ^2 but derivations use (m/2)||ẋ||^2), breaking dimensional correctness and Euler–Lagrange derivation.  
- Technical error: Appendix algebra contains typos/questionable steps (determinant bounds, repeated indices, λ < m/4 derivation) and unstated normalizations.  
- Clarity issue: velocity-dependent term in potential and sign conventions (−ηC in U with L = K − U) are confusingly explained.  
- Modeling concern: curiosity term is ad hoc (squared gradients with cos^2/sin^2 alternation); biological/empirical justification for fixed-frequency time-sharing (ω) is missing and ω sensitivity is unreported.  
- Modeling concern: brightness constancy biases motion along iso-brightness contours, stabilizing edges but not addressing objectness, faces, or text—key drivers of human fixations.  
- Modeling concern: omits inhibition-of-return, saccade amplitude priors, and oculomotor constraints, raising risk of sticky scanpaths in complex scenes.  
- Doubtful claim: center bias is applied multiplicatively to input and then optimized again at output (blur and center σ), undermining the claim that saliency emerges purely from dynamics.  
- Reproducibility issue: simulated annealing parameter ranges, neighbor proposals, final hyperparameters, and code are not reported.  
- Evaluation gap: comparisons exclude strong modern deep saliency models (DeepGaze II, SALICON, SAM/MLNet), making reported “best in half cases” uninformative versus state of the art.  
- Video evaluation unclear: unclear whether SFU tuning used separate splits; bt preprocessing, frame rate, and integrator details are under-specified.  
- Evaluation gap: only saliency metrics reported; no scanpath-level evaluations (MultiMatch, string-edit, saccade amplitude distributions) or tracking accuracy reported.  
- Sensitivity untested: initialization near image center (199/100 trajectories) likely induces center bias; sensitivity to initialization and trajectory count is not analyzed.  
- Originality: least-action framing is elegant and moderately novel but overlaps prior energy-based dynamical attention models; curiosity term is rudimentary.  
- Significance limited: empirical impact weakened by technical errors, ad hoc modeling choices, heavy center-bias/metric tuning, and lack of competitive baselines.  
- Suggested fix: correct Lagrangian derivations, state assumptions/normalizations, and clean up Appendix algebra.  
- Suggested fix: report all hyperparameters, SA settings, provide code, and analyze sensitivity to ω and initial conditions.  
- Suggested experiments: ablations (remove brightness term, cos/sin alternation, center-bias) and scanpath evaluations (MultiMatch, saccade stats, tracking).  
- Suggested benchmarking: compare to strong modern baselines on MIT300/CAT2000, use standard video benchmarks/splits (e.g., DHF1K), and fix training/test splits for tuning.  
- Suggested positioning: reduce or justify post-hoc center-bias and blur tuning or fix them on a training set before testing.  
- Recommendation: borderline reject—appealing conceptual idea but needs corrected theory, clearer justification, ablations, reproducibility, and stronger evaluations to be convincing.  
- Reviewer confidence: high on modeling/evaluation, medium on dataset-specific details.
