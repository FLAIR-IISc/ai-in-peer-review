This paper proposes a novel, physics-inspired model for visual attention scanpaths in dynamic and static scenes, termed EYMOL. The central idea is to formulate the scanpath of the focus of attention as the trajectory of a particle that minimizes a generalized action functional. This action is derived from three core principles: 1) the trajectory is bounded within the retina, 2) attention is driven by a "curiosity" for detailed regions, and 3) the trajectory seeks brightness invariance, facilitating fixation and tracking. The resulting Euler-Lagrange equations describe the motion of the attentional focus. Saliency maps, used for evaluation against standard benchmarks, are generated as a byproduct by aggregating the simulated scanpaths over time.

The primary strength of this work lies in its conceptual originality. Moving away from models that directly compute a static saliency map, the authors propose a generative, dynamic model of the scanpath itself based on first principles. The unification of saccade-like "curiosity" movements and fixation/tracking behavior under a single variational framework is elegant and thought-provoking. If sound, this approach could offer a more fundamental understanding of attentional mechanisms than many existing engineering- or learning-based models.

Despite the novelty of the core idea, the paper is undermined by a significant number of flaws in its technical presentation, experimental methodology, and overall clarity. The execution is sufficiently careless as to cast doubt on the work's technical soundness and reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

**1. Technical Soundness and Clarity:** The mathematical exposition is imprecise and contains several ambiguities and apparent errors.
*   **Ambiguous Notation:** The notation for vector and scalar quantities is inconsistent. For instance, in the curiosity term `C(t, x)` (Eq. 4), the paper uses `b_x^2` and `p_x^2`. Since `b_x` and `p_x` are gradients (vectors), it is unclear if this denotes the squared L2-norm (`||b_x||^2`) or some other operation. This ambiguity persists throughout the paper (e.g., Eq. 6).
*   **Undefined Operations:** In the Appendix, the derivative of the curiosity term `C_x` (Eq. 15) contains the expression `b_x · b_xx`, which is a dot product between a vector (`b_x`) and a matrix (the Hessian `b_xx`). This operation is not standard and its meaning is left undefined.
*   **Errors in Derivations:** The Appendix, which should lend rigor to the model, contains several issues. The simplification of the determinant `D` from Eq. (41) to Eq. (42) appears to contain a typo (`b_x1^2` is written twice instead of `b_x1^2` and `b_x2^2`). More critically, the derivation of the bound on the parameter `λ` (Eq. 47) is unsubstantiated. The paper concludes that `λ < m/4`, but the preceding steps (Eq. 46) show that the bound depends on the image-specific gradient magnitude `||b_x||^2`. The origin of the constant `4` is not explained, making this crucial constraint seem arbitrary.
*   **Misleading Justification:** The approximation for the brightness invariance term `B̄` (Eq. 6) is justified by the "parallelogram law of Hilbert spaces." This is incorrect; the inequality `(a+b)^2 ≤ 2a^2 + 2b^2` is a direct consequence of the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality. While a minor point, it contributes to a general lack of precision.

**2. Methodological Concerns and Reproducibility:** The paper omits critical details required to reproduce the experiments and validate the results.
*   **Ad-hoc Heuristics:** The paper's narrative of being a fundamental, principles-based model is contradicted by the use of several ad-hoc, performance-boosting heuristics. The input is pre-processed with a "Gaussian blob... to produce a center bias," and the final saliency maps are blurred, with parameters for both steps optimized via grid search on the test metrics. These steps make it impossible to disentangle the performance of the core variational model from the performance of standard saliency benchmark tricks.
*   **Missing Parameters:** Key parameters of the experimental setup are not specified: the standard deviation of the Gaussian kernel used to compute the peripheral input `p`; the specific numerical integration routine from SciPy used to solve the differential equations (e.g., integrator type, time step, error tolerances); the precise distribution used for the "almost at the center" random initialization of trajectories.
*   **Vague Evaluation:** The evaluation is described in vague terms. The claim that two model versions are "statistically equivalent" (Sec 3.3) is presented without any reported statistical tests, p-values, or confidence intervals. The claim of object tracking on dynamic scenes is supported only by anecdotal evidence ("In some favorable case...").

**3. Formatting and Writing:** The manuscript is unpolished and contains numerous errors that detract from its professionalism.
*   **Formatting Errors:** A misplaced conference footer appears in the middle of the Introduction. The citation style `[2; 3; 4]` is non-standard. Footnotes are misplaced (e.g., footnote 3 attached to "numerical").
*   **Typos and Grammatical Errors:** The paper is replete with typos ("eggs-chicken dilemma", "interwound", "psedo-code", "Differently by") and grammatical mistakes. These are minor individually but collectively suggest a lack of care in the paper's preparation. Equation (14) is incorrectly referenced when it should be Eq. (3).

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:** The central idea of a unified variational model for scanpaths is highly original and conceptually appealing. It represents a departure from mainstream saliency modeling and could stimulate new research directions.
*   **Arguments for rejection:** The paper suffers from a pervasive lack of rigor. The technical presentation is unclear and contains errors. The experimental methodology relies on unacknowledged heuristics that confound the evaluation of the core model, and critical details for reproducibility are omitted. The manuscript is poorly written and formatted.

**Decision:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the core idea is interesting, the execution is deeply flawed. The sheer number of technical ambiguities, methodological shortcomings, and careless errors prevents a confident assessment of the paper's contribution. The work does not currently meet the high standards of technical soundness and clarity expected for NIPS. The authors should be encouraged to refine their mathematical presentation, conduct a more rigorous and transparent experimental evaluation, and thoroughly proofread the manuscript before resubmission.
