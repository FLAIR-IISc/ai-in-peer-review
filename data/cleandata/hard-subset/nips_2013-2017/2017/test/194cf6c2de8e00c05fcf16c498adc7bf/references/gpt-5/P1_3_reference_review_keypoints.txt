- Summary: Proposes EYMOL, a Lagrangian/least-action model combining border potential, time-modulated curiosity (local gradient + blurred peripheral), and brightness constancy to produce a second-order ODE for scanpaths and saliency maps via visit counts; parameters tuned by simulated annealing and post-processed with blur/center-bias; evaluated on several datasets with competitive classic-baseline metrics.

- Relation to prior work: Connects to Itti-Koch, AIM, saccade simulators and active-vision models; novelty is variational derivation of scanpath dynamics, but lacks discussion/comparison to GBVS and modern deep baselines (DeepGaze II, SALICON, learned saccades).

- Theory quality: Derivation is mostly sound and readable; matrix/determinant condition is helpful, but algebraic typos and missing reporting of key constants (m, k, ω) weaken the presentation.

- Modeling choices: Curiosity uses ||∇b||² and ||∇p||² with cos²/sin² modulation but ω and the modulation rationale are unjustified; no damping/friction term discussed and stability control is insufficiently motivated.

- Experimental concerns: Heavy reliance on multiple runs from near-center starts plus tuned center-bias and blur may inflate AUC/NSS; no ablations for core components or sensitivity to initial conditions; video results lack explicit tracking metrics.

- Reproducibility: Key implementation details are missing (values/ranges for m,k,ω, integrator, step size, SA hyperparameters, runtime, initial condition distribution) and code is not promised.

- Clarity: Generally understandable but marred by formatting glitches, typos, missing reference details; experimental protocol and preprocessing (center bias, peripheral attenuation) need clearer, self-contained description; more figures/scanpaths recommended.

- Originality: Interesting least-action framing that unifies curiosity and brightness constancy, but constituent terms are standard and the contribution is incremental without deeper empirical validation.

- Significance: Model is interpretable and could stimulate physics-inspired attention work, but current empirical validation and missing modern baselines limit impact; video tracking claims are not quantitatively supported.

- Pros: Principled variational formulation; unifies fixation and exploration; produces scanpaths and saliency maps; competitive against several classic baselines.

- Cons: Heavy reliance on tuned priors and near-center initialization; missing ablations and quantitative scanpath comparisons; lack of modern deep baselines; insufficient reproducibility and theoretical justification for time modulation; presentation issues.

- Suggestions: Add ablations (remove center bias, brightness term, vary/omit ω and time modulation, local vs peripheral curiosity, initial-condition sensitivity); compare to strong contemporary baselines and report multiple metrics; evaluate video tracking quantitatively; release full implementation and runtimes; analyze scanpaths vs human data and justify/consider damping.

- Recommendation: Weak reject—interesting and potentially impactful idea but requires stronger experiments, ablations, comparisons, and reproducibility to meet conference standards.
