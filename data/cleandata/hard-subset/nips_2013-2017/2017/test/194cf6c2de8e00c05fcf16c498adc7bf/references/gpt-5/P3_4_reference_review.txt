Summary
The paper proposes a physics-inspired, variational formulation of bottom-up visual attention. The authors posit three principles: (i) boundedness of the scanpath within the retinal image via a confining potential; (ii) “curiosity” for detailed regions, modeled by the squared gradient magnitude of brightness and a blurred version that alternate over time; and (iii) brightness constancy (optical-flow constraint) as a soft penalty to encourage fixation and motion tracking. These terms define a Lagrangian (kinetic minus generalized potential), and the Euler–Lagrange equations yield second-order ODEs governing the gaze trajectory. Saliency maps are computed as a byproduct by counting trajectory visits. Parameters are tuned via simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a small subset of CAT2000. The model is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video, where the authors claim competitive performance with classic and some modern methods, and anecdotal tracking behavior in videos.

Relation to prior work
The variational framing connects to long-standing energy-based and dynamical systems approaches in attention and active vision (e.g., Itti & Koch’s saliency map as a global potential; Le Meur et al. dynamic saccade models; control-theoretic/optimality perspectives). The brightness constancy term is standard (Horn–Schunck). The “curiosity” term is akin to edge-based saliency (high gradient energy), with a peripheral/global component reminiscent of coarse-to-fine schemes and multi-scale saliency (GBVS, AIM). Recent deep saliency models (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON, SAM) are briefly acknowledged (Cornia et al.) but not adequately compared.

Strengths
- Conceptual elegance: a compact variational formulation yields a unified mechanism for saccadic exploration, fixation, and tracking, with interpretable forces.
- Derivation: the Euler–Lagrange development is technically sound at a high level, including attention to velocity-dependent “potential” and explicit solvability conditions.
- Simplicity and efficiency: the dynamics are easy to integrate and can run in real time; saliency maps arise naturally from scanpaths.
- Empirical indication that the approximate brightness-invariance term suffices for static images.

Weaknesses
- Empirical rigor and fairness: heavy reliance on center bias (explicit Gaussian weighting of input, initialization near center, and post-hoc blur/center grid search) confounds reported gains, especially for AUC-family metrics known to be center-bias sensitive. Ablations without center bias are missing.
- Evaluation protocol: parameters are tuned for videos on SFU, and evaluation appears on the same dataset; no held-out split is described, risking overfitting. Tables are not provided for verification. Comparisons omit top-performing 2016–2017 deep models (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON, newer SAM variants), making “state-of-the-art” claims weak.
- Biological and computational plausibility: the time-varying alternation between local and peripheral terms via cos^2/sin^2 lacks justification (what ω, why periodicity, and what data support this mechanism?). The model omits oculomotor constraints (saccade velocity profiles, refractory periods, IOR) that are known to matter for scanpaths.
- Limited analysis: no sensitivity analysis or ablation of the three terms; no scanpath-level validation (e.g., saccade amplitude distributions, temporal correlations) despite claiming to model trajectories; no robustness analysis to noise/contrast scaling.
- Clarity/reproducibility: important implementation details (ω, m, λ, ηb, ηp values, initial-condition distribution, integration step, stopping criteria) are missing. Numerous typos and incomplete references hinder readability. Code/data splits not provided.

Quality
Technically, the variational derivation is mostly correct; the determinant simplification and the bound λ < m/4 follow from D = m(m − 2λ||∇b||^2), though the presentation includes typographical mistakes around inequalities that should be corrected. The empirical section, however, is not strong enough: potential metric inflation from center bias, lack of held-out validation for videos, and missing comparisons to stronger baselines reduce confidence in the claimed competitiveness. The absence of ablations and scanpath analyses leaves key modeling choices untested.

Clarity
The paper is readable at a high level but needs substantial polishing. Equations should be numbered consistently; ω and all hyperparameters must be reported; pre-/post-processing steps and evaluation protocols require clearer, reproducible descriptions. References are incomplete and occasionally incorrect. Several grammatical errors and typos distract.

Originality
Casting bottom-up attention dynamics as a least-action problem is a neat synthesis, though related ideas (energy/dynamics/optimal control) have precedent. The brightness-constancy penalty and gradient-based “curiosity” are standard; the periodic alternation is novel but ad hoc. The main originality lies in integrating these into a single ODE framework and using trajectories to derive saliency maps.

Significance
If validated more rigorously, a fast, principled, interpretable dynamics model that yields competitive saliency and plausible scanpaths would be valuable. As presented, the significance is limited by evaluation shortcomings and reliance on center bias. The lack of scanpath-level validation misses an opportunity to differentiate from saliency-only models.

Questions for the authors
- What value of ω was used, and how sensitive is performance to it? Why periodic alternation rather than a learned or state-dependent schedule?
- How much of the reported performance remains if you remove all center-bias components (input weighting, centered initialization, post-hoc blur/center tuning)?
- For SFU videos, was there a training/validation split? If tuned and tested on the same data, please redo with proper held-out evaluation.
- Can you provide ablations (only V, only C, only B; combinations) and parameter sensitivity?
- Do the generated scanpaths match human saccade statistics (amplitude, duration, velocity profiles)? Any evidence of inhibition-of-return emerging from the dynamics?

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen evaluation: remove/postpone center bias and report all metrics; add ablations and sensitivity; include stronger deep baselines; provide held-out evaluation for videos and scanpath-level metrics.
- Justify or replace the cos^2/sin^2 alternation; consider an adaptive weighting driven by state or learned from data.
- Report all parameter values and implementation details; release code to ensure reproducibility.
- Analyze stability and invariance properties (e.g., contrast scaling) and discuss existence/uniqueness of solutions with your parameter choices.
- Improve writing, fix typos, and complete references.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The conceptual contribution is interesting and potentially impactful, but the experimental methodology and analysis are not yet sufficient for NeurIPS. With stronger, bias-controlled evaluations, ablations, and scanpath validation, this could become a compelling submission.

Confidence
High (familiar with saliency modeling, dynamic/variational methods, and common evaluation pitfalls).
