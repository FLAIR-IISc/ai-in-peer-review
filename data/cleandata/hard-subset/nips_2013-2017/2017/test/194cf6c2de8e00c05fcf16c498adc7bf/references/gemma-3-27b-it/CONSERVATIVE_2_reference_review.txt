This paper proposes a novel model of visual attention, “EYE MOvement Laws” (EYMOL), based on a variational approach and principles of curiosity and brightness invariance. The authors aim to move beyond saliency map-centric models, directly modeling scanpaths as solutions to Euler-Lagrange equations. While the attempt to ground attention in a physically-motivated framework is interesting, the paper suffers from a lack of thorough evaluation and a reliance on established datasets with limited comparison to state-of-the-art approaches that *already* perform well on these benchmarks.

The introduction provides a reasonable overview of visual attention and existing models, correctly identifying the limitations of saliency map approaches and the disconnect between bottom-up and top-down influences. However, the motivation for the specific principles chosen (curiosity and brightness invariance) feels somewhat arbitrary. While plausible, a stronger justification connecting these to known neural mechanisms or perceptual phenomena would be beneficial. 

The mathematical formulation in Section 2 is presented clearly, although the derivation appears lengthy and potentially obscures the core ideas. The use of a Lagrangian and subsequent derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations is mathematically sound, but the connection between this formalization and actual biological plausibility remains weak. The use of Iverson notation is acceptable but can hinder readability for some. The introduction of simulated annealing for parameter estimation is appropriate, but the limited search space and batch size used for the algorithm are concerning. A more rigorous exploration of parameter space is needed.

The experimental section (Section 3) is the most significant weakness of the paper. The authors evaluate the model on standard datasets (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, SFU), but the comparison to other methods is superficial. They mention several state-of-the-art approaches in the introduction, yet only compare against a small subset in the results. Furthermore, the claim that their approach “competes well” despite its simplicity is overstated. While the results are not *bad*, they are not demonstrably better than existing methods, and often comparable, suggesting a lack of significant improvement. The fact that the approximated form (V1) performs similarly to the exact form (V2) is noted, but doesn't add substantial insight. The emphasis on real-time computation is a minor benefit but doesn't outweigh the lack of competitive performance. The discussion of blur and center bias, applied to 'level the playing field', feels like post-hoc adjustments to improve scores, rather than an inherent property of the model.

**Strengths:**

*   Novel approach to modeling attention based on variational principles.
*   Clear mathematical formulation.
*   Real-time computational feasibility.
*   Exploration of a direct scanpath modeling approach, rather than relying on saliency maps.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited justification for chosen principles (curiosity & brightness invariance).
*   Insufficiently rigorous parameter estimation using simulated annealing.
*   Weak comparative evaluation against state-of-the-art methods.
*   Results are comparable to existing methods, but not demonstrably superior.
*   Post-hoc adjustments (blur, center bias) to improve scores.
*   Limited discussion of biological plausibility beyond initial motivation.
*   The lack of ablation studies to determine the contribution of each component.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The approach is novel and explores a different paradigm for modeling attention.
*   The mathematical formulation is sound.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is inadequate and does not demonstrate a significant advance over existing methods.
*   The justification for the chosen principles is weak.
*   The paper lacks a thorough analysis of its limitations.
*   The reliance on standard datasets with minimal comparison to recent work. 



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents an interesting idea, it lacks the rigor and thorough evaluation required for acceptance at NIPS. The claims of novelty and competitive performance are not convincingly supported by the experimental results. The paper feels more like a proof-of-concept than a fully developed contribution. I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the comparative evaluation and justification of their chosen principles, before resubmitting. They should demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods or provide a compelling reason why their approach is fundamentally different and deserving of attention despite comparable performance.
