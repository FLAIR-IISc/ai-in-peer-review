Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a variational/dynamical formulation of visual attention in which scanpaths arise as solutions of Euler–Lagrange (EL) equations derived from a least-action functional. The Lagrangian includes: (i) a boundary “retina” potential to confine motion; (ii) a curiosity term that attracts the trajectory to regions with high local detail (|∇b|2) and to peripheral/blurred detail (|∇p|2), alternating over time via cos2(ωt)/sin2(ωt) to avoid trapping; and (iii) a brightness-invariance term (bt + bx·ẋ)2 motivated by the optical flow brightness constancy constraint, intended to yield fixation on edges for static scenes and tracking in dynamic scenes. The resulting second-order ODEs are integrated to produce scanpaths; saliency maps are then obtained as spatial visit counts across multiple runs. Model parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) are tuned by simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a small subset of CAT2000-TRAIN. The method is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU-video; the paper claims competitive performance against several classical and a recent deep baseline. The approach differs from classical saliency maps with winner-take-all and inhibition-of-return [10] and from information/surprise maximization [16, 17] by generating dynamics from a single variational principle; it is closer in spirit to physics-inspired active vision but with a specific combination of gradient- and flow-based terms.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Derivation consistency: There is an apparent inconsistency between Eq. (8) and the EL derivation. The kinetic energy is given as K(ẋ) = (1/m)|ẋ|2, whereas Eq. (12) and subsequent EL equations require K(ẋ) = (1/2)m|ẋ|2 to yield d/dt(∂L/∂ẋ) = mẍ. This should be corrected; otherwise dimensions and resulting forces are inconsistent.
- Video term computation: The paper relies on bt and bx for B(t, x, ẋ), yet the computation of temporal derivatives (e.g., finite differences between frames, smoothing) is not described. This is critical for dynamic scenes and for numerical stability of the EL integration.
- Approximation for static images: The upper bound B̄ = 2b2t + 2|bx|2|ẋ|2 is reasonable and the claim that it performs on par with the exact form on static images is plausible, but quantitative evidence is not shown in the submission (tables are referenced but not provided). Without the numbers, it is difficult to verify the equivalence.
- Stability and well-posedness: The determinant analysis leading to λ < m/4 (for normalized gradients) is useful. However, other hyperparameters (ω, k, m) and their impact on stability/integration are not reported. No sensitivity analysis is provided.
- Evaluation protocol: The model applies an explicit center bias by multiplying the input with a central Gaussian and initializes trajectories near the center. Blur and center-bias parameters are tuned to maximize metrics on training data. This can substantially inflate AUC-type metrics. Fairness of comparisons requires clarifying whether competing methods were also center-biased and reporting center-bias-controlled metrics (e.g., sAUC).
- Experimental rigor: Ablations (e.g., remove brightness-invariance term, remove alternating peripheral term, remove center bias, approximate vs exact B on dynamic scenes) are missing. Scanpath-level analyses (e.g., saccade amplitude distributions, fixation durations, trajectory similarity to human scanpaths) are not provided, despite being central to the claim of modeling eye movements.
- Reproducibility: Key details are missing (exact parameter values, ω, integrator/time-step, simulated time units, domain D for SA, annealing schedule α and neighborhood definition). References and tables are incomplete; code is not provided.

Clarity
The exposition of the high-level idea is clear and the appendix attempts a full derivation. However, the manuscript contains numerous typos, formatting issues (e.g., broken footnotes, incomplete references), and several undefined or unexplained quantities (ω, units/scales, numerical settings). The missing quantitative tables hinder assessment. The writing would benefit from tightening and a more systematic description of implementation and evaluation protocols.

Originality
The specific combination of (i) curiosity via gradient-based attraction, (ii) an alternating local/peripheral field, and (iii) brightness invariance within a single variational framework that directly yields scanpaths is novel relative to standard saliency map pipelines. However, the constituent ideas (gradient magnitude as “detail,” brightness constancy, confinement) are classical, and the choice of temporal alternation via cos2/sin2 appears ad hoc without analysis or biological justification. Related physics-inspired attentional/dynamical models are not discussed; the related-work section is biased toward saliency-map literature and omits several contemporary deep saliency models (e.g., DeepGaze II, MLNet/SAM, SALICON) common in 2016–2017.

Significance
If robust and competitive, a light-weight, interpretable dynamical model that unifies fixation and tracking would be valuable, especially for real-time applications and for generating scanpaths. However, the current evidence is insufficient: quantitative results are not shown in the submission; center bias is entangled with the core model; and no scanpath-level validation is performed. The significance is therefore uncertain.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually unified variational formulation that directly generates scanpaths and yields saliency maps as a byproduct.
- Interpretable terms with plausible links to fixation (edge-following) and tracking (brightness constancy).
- Lightweight, potentially real-time, and using only grayscale input.
- Preliminary claims of competitive performance on standard datasets.

Cons for acceptance
- Technical inconsistency in the kinetic energy term; unclear computation of temporal derivatives; missing numerical details.
- Heavy reliance on center bias (both in input and initialization) and tuning for metrics; fairness of comparisons unclear.
- Missing tables/quantitative results in the submission; lack of ablations and scanpath-level validation.
- Limited discussion of prior dynamical/physics-inspired attention models and omission of several strong recent baselines.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues (typos, incomplete references, unspecified hyperparameters).

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the kinetic energy inconsistency and thoroughly verify the EL derivations; provide units and parameter scales.
- Specify how bt and bx are computed (smoothing, frame rate, discretization), integrator settings, and stability safeguards.
- Provide full quantitative tables on all metrics (AUC-Judd, sAUC, NSS, CC, SIM, KL), with and without center bias; ensure fair baselines.
- Add ablations for each term (V, C local vs peripheral, alternation, B exact vs approximate), and report sensitivity to ω, λ, k, m.
- Include scanpath analyses comparing generated trajectories to human sequences.
- Expand related work to include contemporaneous deep models and any prior dynamical/variational approaches.
- Release code and parameter settings to ensure reproducibility.

Overall, the idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the current version lacks technical rigor, clarity, and comprehensive evaluation needed for a strong NeurIPS contribution.
