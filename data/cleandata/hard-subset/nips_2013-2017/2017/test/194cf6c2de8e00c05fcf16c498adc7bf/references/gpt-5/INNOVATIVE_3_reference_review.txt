Summary
This paper proposes a physics-inspired, variational formulation of visual attention for dynamic scenes. The authors posit that scanpaths arise as trajectories of a particle governed by a Lagrangian with three terms: (i) a boundary potential that keeps the trajectory within the retinal field, (ii) a curiosity term that alternates between local and peripheral detail (based on image gradients of the raw and blurred brightness) via a time-varying modulation, and (iii) a brightness invariance term that softly enforces the optical flow constraint to promote fixation and, in videos, motion tracking. The Euler–Lagrange equations are derived to produce a system of ODEs (EYMOL) whose integration yields a scanpath. Saliency maps are then obtained as a byproduct by counting visits over multiple trajectory runs. Parameters are tuned via simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000. The approach is evaluated on MIT1003/CAT2000 (images) and SFU (videos), and is reported to be competitive with several classic and recent baselines.

Relation to prior work
Most saliency models aggregate feature evidence into a centralized saliency map and then synthesize scanpaths with winner-take-all plus heuristics (e.g., inhibition of return). Deep learning models have recently dominated saliency prediction. In contrast, this work removes the saliency map as a central object and derives scanpaths as solutions to an action-minimization problem, unifying saccades, fixation, and (some) tracking behavior under a single dynamical law. This continues a line of active/variational vision ideas but is uncommon in contemporary saliency modeling.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clear, unified variational principle that connects curiosity-driven exploration with brightness invariance, yielding a single dynamical system for scanpaths in both static and dynamic scenes.
- Interpretability: Each Lagrangian term has a transparent behavioral interpretation; conditions for well-posedness are discussed.
- Generality: Works with minimal training (only a few scalar parameters), and saliency maps are an emergent byproduct rather than the model’s target.
- Empirical promise: Despite simplicity, reported performance is competitive on standard image and video datasets; real-time scanpath generation is plausible.
- Technical contribution: The Euler–Lagrange derivation is carried through, including handling of velocity-dependent terms.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is underdeveloped relative to current standards. The strongest deep baselines (e.g., DeepGaze II/SALICON-era models) are not included, and the quantitative tables are not shown here. Claims like “best score in half of the cases” need clearer, comprehensive evidence.
- Fairness concerns: The method introduces center bias both via multiplicative Gaussian on the input and via initialization near the center, and further tunes blur/center parameters by grid search to optimize benchmark metrics. This can inflate AUC/NSS and complicate comparisons unless the same center bias is applied to baselines.
- Limited ablations: It is unclear how much each component contributes. There is no analysis of the effect of removing center bias, the alternating curiosity modulation (cos^2/sin^2, with unspecified ω), or the brightness invariance term. V1 vs. V2 equivalence is only shown for static images.
- Scanpath-level validation is missing. Since the central claim is a principled model of eye movements, comparisons to human scanpaths (e.g., saccade amplitude distributions, fixation durations, MultiMatch/sequence metrics, early-fixation agreement) would be much more convincing than saliency heatmaps alone.
- Dynamic scenes: The tracking claim is primarily qualitative; there is no quantitative tracking measure (e.g., overlap/velocity alignment with moving objects), and details of temporal derivative estimation (bt) and robustness to video noise are sparse.
- Mathematical/logistical clarity: Some algebraic steps and conditions (e.g., the determinant condition and λ < m/4 bound) could be presented more cleanly; implementation details (ω, m, k, Gaussian widths, integration step) and sensitivity analysis are missing. References are inconsistent/incomplete.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide comprehensive comparisons with strong contemporary saliency baselines (including deep models circa 2016–2017) and report full metric tables.
- Run ablations: remove center bias and/or use consistent post-hoc center priors across all methods; test single-run vs. multi-run aggregation; remove the alternation (set ω = 0) or vary ω; isolate the contributions of curiosity vs. brightness invariance.
- Add scanpath-based evaluations against human data (MIT1003/CAT2000 offer fixation sequences) and analyze saccade statistics and early vs. late fixations.
- For videos, quantify tracking behavior (e.g., object-following metrics or alignment of gaze velocity with optical flow).
- Clarify how bt is estimated; discuss smoothing and discretization effects needed to ensure C2 assumptions; include sensitivity to parameter choices and stability conditions.
- Clean up the presentation: fix typos, complete citations, and include reproducible details and code.

Significance
This is a bold and original direction: a principled, dynamical account of attention that yields scanpaths and produces saliency only as a side effect. If strengthened empirically and presented with careful ablations and fair baselines, this could stimulate new research at the intersection of attention modeling, variational methods, and active vision.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The conceptual novelty and unifying variational framework are compelling and, in my view, outweigh the current experimental and presentation shortcomings. Strengthening the empirical section (ablations, fairer comparisons, scanpath metrics) and clarifying implementation details would make this a solid NeurIPS contribution.
