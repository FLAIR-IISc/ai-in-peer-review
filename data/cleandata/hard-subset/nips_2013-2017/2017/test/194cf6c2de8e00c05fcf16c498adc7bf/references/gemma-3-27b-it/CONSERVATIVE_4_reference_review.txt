This paper proposes a novel model of visual attention, “EYE MOvement Laws (EYMOL),” based on a variational approach and principles of curiosity and brightness invariance. The authors derive differential equations governing scanpath generation and demonstrate its performance on image and video datasets, comparing it to established saliency models. While the attempt to ground attention in a principled, physics-inspired framework is interesting, the paper suffers from a lack of rigorous evaluation and suffers from novelty without sufficient justification or strong empirical support.

The central idea – formulating attention as a least-action principle – feels somewhat contrived. While appealing from a theoretical perspective, the connection between the chosen Lagrangian terms (boundedness, curiosity, brightness invariance) and actual human attention mechanisms isn’t convincingly established. The justification relies heavily on analogies to newborn attention and general principles, lacking strong neurophysiological grounding. The authors acknowledge this partially, framing the work as a “model” rather than a definitive explanation, but the leap from these principles to the specific mathematical formulation remains unclear.

A major weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. While comparisons to standard saliency models are made, the methodology feels superficial. The parameters are tuned via simulated annealing, but the range of parameters tested and the rationale for the search space are not detailed enough. The use of metrics like NSS and AUC-Judd, while standard, doesn't fully capture the qualitative aspects of scanpaths. The claim of achieving "best score in half of the cases" is qualified but needs stronger evidence given the established performance of existing techniques. Critically, the paper lacks a comprehensive ablation study. How much does each term in the Lagrangian *actually* contribute to the performance? Is the brightness invariance component truly necessary, or is a simple curiosity-driven model sufficient? Furthermore, the qualitative results shown in the supplementary material are not compelling enough to demonstrate significant advantage over existing methods. The model is reported to sometimes track moving objects, but concrete examples and a quantitative assessment of tracking performance are missing.

The simplification offered by the approximated form of brightness invariance (V1) is noted, but the justification for its adoption rests solely on computational efficiency, and its impact on the accuracy of the model deserves further scrutiny. The choice to use only a subset of the datasets for parameter estimation (a 100 image batch from CAT2000-TRAIN) raises concerns about generalizability.

The paper’s clarity is hampered by a somewhat dense mathematical presentation. While the appendix provides derivations, the overall flow could be improved by providing more intuitive explanations of the core concepts and equations. A more detailed discussion of the limitations of the model and potential avenues for future research would also enhance the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to modeling visual attention using a variational framework.
*   Potential for real-time implementation due to the differential equation formulation.
*   Competitive performance on standard benchmarks, though not consistently superior.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of strong theoretical justification for the chosen Lagrangian terms.
*   Insufficiently rigorous experimental evaluation and limited ablation studies.
*   Weak demonstration of superior performance compared to established methods.
*   Limited novelty; the core idea of applying variational principles isn't fundamentally new.
*   The connection to the biological plausibility of attention is tenuous.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting idea but falls short of making a compelling contribution. The lack of robust validation and the absence of a deeper analysis of the model's behavior weaken its claims. While the authors demonstrate that the model *can* generate plausible scanpaths, they haven't convincingly shown that it *does so better* or *in a more insightful way* than existing methods. The paper feels premature for NIPS, and I recommend **rejection**. I encourage the authors to perform more extensive experiments, conduct thorough ablation studies, and provide stronger theoretical grounding for their approach before resubmitting. A more detailed comparison to more recent, state-of-the-art methods would also be beneficial.
