This paper presents a novel model of visual attention, termed EYMOL, based on variational principles. The core idea is to model the scanpath of the eye's focus of attention as the trajectory of a particle that minimizes a generalized action functional. The corresponding Lagrangian is constructed from three principles: a bounding potential to confine the trajectory to the retina, a "curiosity" term that attracts attention to detailed regions by alternating between local and peripheral image gradients, and a "brightness invariance" term, akin to the optical flow constraint, to promote fixation and tracking. The Euler-Lagrange equations derived from this action principle describe the dynamics of the scanpath. Saliency maps are then computed as a secondary output by aggregating fixation densities from multiple simulated trajectories. The model is evaluated on standard image and video saliency datasets.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this work lies in its originality. The attempt to derive the laws of visual attention from a unifying variational principle is elegant and conceptually appealing. It provides a refreshing alternative to the dominant paradigm of directly engineering features to compute a static saliency map. By modeling the dynamics of the scanpath itself, the work has the potential to explain not just *where* we look, but also the temporal sequence of fixations, unifying saccades and smooth pursuit within a single mathematical framework. The connection to classical mechanics is intriguing and offers a principled, physics-based foundation for attention modeling.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the novelty of the core idea, the paper is plagued by a substantial number of flaws, ranging from critical omissions to a general lack of rigor in its presentation and technical details.

1.  **Incomplete Manuscript and Unverifiable Claims:** The most critical flaw is that the paper is incomplete. Tables 2, 3, and 4, which are supposed to contain all the quantitative results comparing the model to the state of the art, are missing from the manuscript. Consequently, all claims regarding the model's performance are entirely unsubstantiated. The text claims the model "reaches best score in half of the cases" and "competes well with state-of-the-art models," but without the data, these assertions are impossible to verify. This omission alone makes the paper unsuitable for publication.

2.  **Lack of Technical and Methodological Rigor:**
    *   **Model Formulation:** The "curiosity" term in Eq. (4) feels arbitrary. The paper provides no strong theoretical or empirical justification for the specific sinusoidal modulation between local (`b_x`) and peripheral (`p_x`) gradients. The frequency `ω` is a critical parameter of this dynamic, yet its origin and selection are not discussed.
    *   **Parameter Estimation:** The description of the simulated annealing procedure in Section 2.3 is cursory. Key details required for reproducibility, such as the neighborhood function, the explicit form of the acceptance probability `P()`, and the value of the cooling parameter `α`, are absent. The procedure of running SA five times on small, random 100-image subsets and then testing an *average* of the resulting parameter configurations is ad-hoc and not well-motivated.
    *   **Experimental Protocol:** The description of the experimental setup is vague. The initialization of trajectories is described as "almost at the center of the image and with a small random velocity," which is not a precise specification. Furthermore, the use of a post-processing grid search to optimize blur and center bias parameters complicates the evaluation. It is unclear if the compared methods were subjected to similar post-hoc optimization, making a fair comparison questionable. An ablation study on the impact of these post-processing steps is necessary.
    *   **Appendix Derivations:** The technical appendix contains confusing and likely erroneous statements. In Eq. (42), the derivation of the determinant `D` appears to contain a typo (`b_x1` squared twice). More seriously, the final condition derived for the parameter `λ` in Eq. (47), `< λ < m/2`, is nonsensical as written and the steps leading to it are not clearly justified. Such errors in the core mathematical derivations cast doubt on the technical soundness of the work.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript suffers from a general lack of polish and attention to detail, which falls below the expected standard for a top-tier conference.
    *   **Formatting and Style:** The citation style `[2; 3; 4]` is non-standard. There are numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasings throughout the text (e.g., "eggs-chicken dilemma," "interwound with," "correspondent equations," "differently by many").
    *   **Factual Errors:** The paper's header incorrectly identifies NIPS 2017 as the "1st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems."
    *   **Unprofessional Artifacts:** The footnotes, particularly 3 and 4, read like internal author notes ("...would be very expensive in terms of time") and are inappropriate for a final submission.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The core conceptual idea of a variational principle for visual attention is highly original and could inspire a new direction of research in the field.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  **Fatal Flaw:** The paper is incomplete. The absence of results in Tables 2, 3, and 4 makes the experimental section meaningless and the paper's central claims unverifiable.
2.  **Lack of Rigor:** The methodological descriptions for parameter estimation and experimental simulation are too vague for reproducibility.
3.  **Technical Soundness:** The motivation for key model components is weak, and the mathematical derivations in the appendix contain errors, undermining confidence in the model's formulation.
4.  **Poor Presentation:** The manuscript is filled with a high density of minor errors, including typos, grammatical mistakes, formatting issues, and factual inaccuracies, which collectively suggest a lack of careful preparation.

**Decision:**
I recommend **rejection**. While the foundational idea is interesting, the execution is deeply flawed. A scientific paper must provide evidence for its claims; this manuscript does not. Beyond the critical omission of results, the multitude of errors in the technical details, experimental description, and overall presentation demonstrates that the work is not yet ready for publication. The paper would require a major, substantial revision to address these numerous and significant issues before it could be considered for a venue like NIPS.
