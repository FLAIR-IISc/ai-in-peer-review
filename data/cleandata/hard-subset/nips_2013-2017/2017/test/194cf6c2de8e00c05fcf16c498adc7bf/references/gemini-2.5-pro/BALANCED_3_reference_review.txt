This paper proposes a novel, physics-inspired model of visual attention, termed EYMOL (EYe MOvement Laws). Departing from mainstream approaches that compute a static saliency map from hand-crafted or learned features, this work models the scanpath of the eye directly as the trajectory of a particle governed by a principle of least action. The authors formulate a Lagrangian with three key components: (i) a potential field to bound the trajectory within the image, (ii) a "curiosity" term that attracts the focus of attention to regions with high image gradients (details), and (iii) a "brightness invariance" term, analogous to the optical flow constraint, that encourages fixation on edges in static scenes and tracking of moving objects in dynamic scenes. The scanpath is determined by solving the resulting Euler-Lagrange equations of motion. Saliency maps are then generated as a byproduct, by aggregating the locations visited over many simulated scanpaths. The model is evaluated on standard image (MIT300, CAT2000) and video (SFU) saliency datasets, where it demonstrates competitive performance against several classic and learning-based methods.

This work relates to a long history of visual attention modeling. It presents a clear alternative to the classic feature-integration architecture of Koch & Ullman and Itti et al., which relies on combining feature maps into a saliency map and using a winner-take-all mechanism. While other models have been based on principles like information maximization (Bruce & Tsotsos) or Bayesian surprise (Itti & Baldi), the use of a variational framework from classical mechanics is highly original. In contrast to modern deep learning approaches that learn complex feature hierarchies from data (e.g., Cornia et al.), this model is theory-driven, deriving its behavior from a small set of explicit, interpretable principles.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Elegance:** The central contribution—modeling visual scanpaths via a least action principle—is highly novel and conceptually elegant. It frames the problem of visual attention in a powerful mathematical language that unifies disparate phenomena like exploration, fixation, and tracking within a single, coherent framework. This is a significant and refreshing departure from the dominant paradigms in the field.

2.  **Principled, Not Just Data-Driven:** In an era dominated by large-scale, data-driven models, this paper presents a compelling theory-driven account of attention. The model's behavior emerges from a few well-defined principles ("curiosity," "brightness invariance") rather than being solely optimized to fit human fixation data. This adds significant explanatory value.

3.  **Unified Model for Static and Dynamic Scenes:** A major strength is the model's ability to handle both static images and dynamic videos without modification. The brightness invariance term (B) naturally transitions from encouraging fixation on object contours in static images (where `b_t = 0`) to facilitating object tracking in dynamic scenes (where `b_t + b_x * x_dot ≈ 0`). This is a powerful and unified view.

4.  **Focus on Scanpaths:** The model's primary output is a temporal scanpath, which is more fundamental to the process of vision than the static saliency maps typically produced by other models. While the evaluation does not fully leverage this, the generative nature of the model is a key strength.

5.  **Competitive Performance:** Despite its relative simplicity and lack of extensive feature learning, the model achieves surprisingly strong results on standard benchmarks. Its performance against established models validates the choice of the underlying principles and demonstrates the potential of this approach.

**Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions:**

1.  **Evaluation Focuses on Saliency Maps, Not Scanpaths:** The paper's core claim is to model *eye movements*, but the entire quantitative evaluation rests on aggregate saliency maps. This is a major missed opportunity. A much stronger validation would involve comparing the temporal and spatial statistics of the generated scanpaths (e.g., saccade lengths, fixation durations, angular distributions) with human scanpath data. Metrics for scanpath similarity could also be employed. As it stands, the evaluation only tests a derived, time-averaged property of the model. I strongly encourage the authors to include a direct scanpath analysis in future work, as it would provide much more direct evidence for their claims.

2.  **Biological Plausibility and Interpretation:** The paper draws motivation from human vision, but the connection between the model's components (mass, Lagrangian, kinetic energy, etc.) and the underlying neural mechanisms is not discussed. The model should be framed more clearly as a computational-level or functional explanation. Furthermore, the physical interpretation of some parameters is unclear. For instance, what is the role of the particle's "mass" `m`? How does it affect the dynamics of attention? A discussion of parameter sensitivity and a more thorough justification for their roles would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Ad-hoc Components:** While the model is largely principled, some design choices feel ad-hoc. The explicit multiplication by a Gaussian blob to enforce a center bias is one example. In a principled model, one might hope for such a bias to emerge more naturally from the formulation, perhaps via an additional potential term in the Lagrangian. Similarly, the oscillatory curiosity term `C` with frequency `ω` is a clever engineering solution to avoid getting trapped, but its theoretical motivation could be stronger.

4.  **Parameter Estimation:** The use of Simulated Annealing to find optimal parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) on a subset of the data blurs the line between a purely principled model and a data-fitted one. While some parameter tuning is unavoidable, the process could be made more transparent. A discussion of how robust the model is to parameter variations would be valuable. Does the qualitative behavior of the model remain consistent across a range of parameter settings, or is the good performance contingent on a very specific, fine-tuned configuration?

5.  **Clarity of the Simplified Model:** The paper introduces an approximation (Eq. 6) for the brightness invariance term to create a simpler model (V1) for static images. However, the resulting simplified motion equations are not shown, making it difficult for the reader to appreciate the simplification. Presenting the final form of the equations for model V1 would improve clarity.

**Decision Rationale:**

This paper presents a highly original and thought-provoking contribution to the field of visual attention. Its main strength lies in its novel variational framework, which provides an elegant, unified, and principled account of scanpath generation. While the empirical results are strong, the evaluation is incomplete as it neglects to analyze the model's primary output: the scanpaths themselves. There are also minor concerns regarding the interpretation of model parameters and the ad-hoc nature of certain components.

However, the novelty and potential significance of the core idea outweigh these weaknesses. This work has the potential to inspire a new line of research into theory-driven models of attention that go beyond static saliency prediction. The paper is well-written, the theory is sound, and the results are compelling. It is a solid piece of scientific work that would be of great interest to the NeurIPS community.

**Final Recommendation:** Accept.
