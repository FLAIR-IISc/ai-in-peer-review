Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a dynamical-systems account of visual attention in which scanpaths emerge as solutions to Euler–Lagrange equations derived from a variational (least-action) formulation. The Lagrangian includes: (i) a boundary potential that keeps the trajectory within the “retina”; (ii) a curiosity term that alternates between local and peripheral detail via the squared gradient of brightness and of a blurred version, modulated in time by cos^2(ωt)/sin^2(ωt); and (iii) a brightness invariance term that softly enforces the optic-flow constraint bt + ∇b · ẋ ≈ 0, intended to support fixation and tracking. The model produces scanpaths x(t) by integrating the resulting second-order ODEs. Saliency maps are obtained as byproducts by counting visited locations over multiple runs with random initial conditions. Parameters are tuned via simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a small training subset. The model is evaluated on image (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and video (SFU) benchmarks, with comparisons to several classic and recent saliency models; the authors report competitive scores and occasional qualitative tracking behavior.

The work relates to classic saliency and attention models that aggregate feature maps into a saliency map [Itti & Koch; Bruce & Tsotsos; Judd et al.], as well as to information-theoretic and surprise-based approaches. Distinctively, it returns to bottom-up, early-vision principles and frames attention as a physical law (least action), linking to variational formulations common in vision (e.g., Horn–Schunck optical flow). The idea of deriving scanpaths from dynamical equations (instead of post hoc WTA/inhibition-of-return) is interesting and less explored within the NeurIPS community.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A unified variational formulation that ties together curiosity, fixation, and tracking, yielding scanpaths and saliency maps from the same dynamics.
- Interpretability: Each term has a clear functional role; the link to the optic-flow constraint is principled.
- Simplicity and efficiency: The ODEs are straightforward to integrate and can plausibly run in real-time.
- Evaluation breadth: Tested on both image and video datasets; includes comparisons to several representative baselines.
- Empirical observation that the brightness-invariance approximation yields similar results to the full term on static images.

Weaknesses
Quality and empirical support
- Heavy reliance on center bias and blurring tuned to optimize AUC/NSS undermines claims of “completely data-driven” bottom-up modeling. Shuffled-AUC (sAUC) or other center-bias-robust metrics should be emphasized to avoid inflated scores.
- Parameter tuning via simulated annealing on a small subset risks overfitting; little is reported about parameter sensitivity, the acceptance function, schedules, or reproducibility.
- Missing or incomplete quantitative details: the paper repeatedly cites tables but does not present numbers here; “statistically equivalent” claims lack tests or confidence intervals; real-time performance is claimed without runtime measurements.
- Limited baselines: Recent state-of-the-art deep saliency models (e.g., SALICON, DeepGaze II) are not clearly compared; this weakens significance claims.
- Scanpath evaluation is absent. Since the core contribution is a scanpath generator, comparisons to human scanpaths (e.g., MultiMatch, string-edit distance, saccade amplitude and fixation duration distributions) are needed.

Modeling and derivations
- The curiosity alternation C(t, x) is ad hoc (periodic cos^2/sin^2 switching) and not biologically or statistically justified; no ablation shows its necessity versus standard multi-scale aggregation.
- The dynamics include no damping/friction. Without dissipation or inhibition-of-return, trajectories may be sensitive to initialization or oscillatory; stability analysis is limited.
- The kinetic energy is written as K = (1) m ẋ^2 (missing 1/2 factor). Several derivations contain typos (e.g., determinants, bounds); the condition λ < m/4 depends on gradient bounds that are neither justified nor scale-invariant, and the algebra in Eqs. (41–47) appears to assume bounded ∥∇b∥ without specifying image normalization of gradients.
- The boundary potential (harmonic at the borders) can artificially inflate visits near edges and may interact with the center-bias preprocessing; its effect is not analyzed.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typographical errors, missing reference details, and formatting issues (e.g., incomplete citations, “st Conference…” artifact, “Iversen” vs. Iverson) detract from clarity.
- The preprocessing (multiplicative center bias to b and p) should be clearly separated from the model and analyzed as a distinct component.
- Important hyperparameters (ω, Gaussian blur, mass m, integration step) and implementation choices are not fully specified.

Originality
- The least-action framing and direct derivation of scanpaths are novel within saliency modeling, although related dynamical and active-vision approaches exist. The specific combination of optic-flow constraint with a time-modulated multi-scale gradient term appears original.

Significance
- If shown to be competitive with strong modern baselines and to reproduce human scanpaths, this could be a useful interpretable alternative to black-box deep models. As presented, the empirical support is incomplete, and performance gains may rely on center bias and metric-specific tuning. The scientific insight is promising but would benefit from deeper validation.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Elegant, interpretable variational framework unifying fixation and tracking.
- Produces scanpaths natively; saliency arises as a byproduct.
- Competitive results suggested on several benchmarks.
- Simple, potentially real-time method.

Cons:
- Incomplete and possibly biased empirical evaluation; missing scanpath analyses.
- Heavy reliance on tuned center bias; lack of sAUC and robustness tests.
- Modeling choices (curiosity alternation, lack of damping) insufficiently justified/ablated.
- Derivational inaccuracies and presentation issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add scanpath evaluations against human data (MultiMatch, scanpath similarity, saccade amplitude distributions) and analyze temporal patterns.
- Report complete benchmark results (including sAUC, CC, SIM, KL, IG) with confidence intervals; include top deep baselines (SALICON, DeepGaze II, etc.).
- Provide ablations: remove center bias; vary/omit curiosity alternation; isolate the contribution of the brightness invariance term; test with/without boundary potential; sensitivity to ω, λ, ηb, ηp, mass m, and integrator settings.
- Include damping/friction or inhibition-of-return and analyze stability. Provide theoretical guarantees (existence/uniqueness, boundedness) under clear assumptions on ∇b.
- Fix derivational errors (kinetic energy factor, λ bound) and clearly state scaling/normalization of images and gradients.
- Report runtimes and code to ensure reproducibility.

Recommendation
At present: weak reject. The conceptual contribution is appealing and potentially impactful, but the empirical validation is insufficient and some derivations/presentation issues need correction. With stronger, bias-robust evaluations (especially scanpaths), clearer comparisons to SOTA, and tighter analysis, this could become a compelling submission. My confidence is moderate.
