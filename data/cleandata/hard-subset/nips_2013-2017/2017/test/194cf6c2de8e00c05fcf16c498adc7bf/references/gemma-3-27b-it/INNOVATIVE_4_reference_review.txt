This paper proposes a novel variational framework – EYe MOvement Laws (EYMOL) – to model visual attention as an emergent property driven by curiosity (detail-seeking) and brightness invariance. It’s a bold attempt to ground attention not in complex, learned saliency maps, but in fundamental principles expressed through differential equations. The connection to physics (least action principle) is intriguing and offers a potentially unifying perspective. The authors demonstrate the model’s ability to generate scanpaths and, as a byproduct, saliency maps, on both static images and dynamic scenes.

The core strength of this work lies in its *novelty*.  Most attention models build *towards* saliency; this paper starts with principles and *derives* scanpaths, with saliency as an observation.  This is a fundamentally different approach, reminiscent of early work on active vision, but framed in a modern variational context. The use of a least-action principle is a particularly compelling aspect, hinting at a deeper, more principled understanding of attention. The fact that the model produces reasonable results on standard benchmarks *despite* its simplicity and lack of dedicated saliency optimization is encouraging.  The connection to brightness invariance for fixation, linking to optical flow estimation, is a clever observation.

However, the paper has weaknesses. The experimental validation, while present, feels somewhat cursory. The parameter tuning via simulated annealing is mentioned but lacks detailed explanation; the search space and justification for its bounds are not clearly articulated. While the results on the datasets are competitive, they don't demonstrably *outperform* state-of-the-art methods. The improvement over existing techniques is not substantial enough to warrant strong acceptance. Furthermore, the limitations of the model regarding more complex cognitive influences (task dependency, prior knowledge) are acknowledged, but not thoroughly discussed. The description of the model’s behavior in dynamic scenes is brief; more detailed analysis and visualization of tracking performance would be valuable. The numerical stability discussion is present but could be expanded.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High novelty:** The variational approach to attention is genuinely different and potentially impactful.
*   **Principled foundation:** Grounding attention in physical principles (least action, brightness invariance) is appealing.
*   **Emergent saliency:** The saliency map arises as a byproduct, not a design goal, which is refreshing.
*   **Competitive performance:** Despite its simplicity, the model achieves reasonable results on standard benchmarks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited validation:** The experimental evaluation is somewhat superficial and doesn't show substantial gains.
*   **Parameter tuning details lacking:** The simulated annealing process isn’t fully explained.
*   **Simplified model:** The model neglects many cognitive aspects of attention.
*   **Incremental improvement:** While novel, the performance increase isn’t transformative.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significantly novel and intellectually stimulating approach to visual attention. While the experimental validation is not exhaustive and the performance gains are modest, the boldness of the idea and the principled foundation outweigh these weaknesses. The paper proposes a new direction for attention research, potentially shifting the focus from complex, learned representations to simpler, physics-inspired models. I lean towards **acceptance**, believing that the potential impact of this work justifies overlooking its current limitations. The paper opens up a promising avenue for future research, and its novel perspective is valuable to the NIPS community. Further refinement and more extensive validation will be crucial for realizing the full potential of this approach. I recommend the authors consider providing a more detailed appendix regarding the parameter tuning process and discussing the implications of their model for more complex attentional phenomena in a revised version.
