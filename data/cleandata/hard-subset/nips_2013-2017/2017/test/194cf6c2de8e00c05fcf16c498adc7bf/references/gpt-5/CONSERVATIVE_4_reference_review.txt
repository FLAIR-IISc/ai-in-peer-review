Summary
The paper proposes a dynamical, variational formulation of visual attention (EYMOL). A scanpath is modeled as the trajectory of a particle under a Lagrangian with three terms: (i) a confining potential to keep the trajectory within the image; (ii) a “curiosity” term that alternates between local and peripheral gradient energy via cos^2/sin^2 modulation; and (iii) a brightness-invariance term derived from the classical optical flow constraint to promote fixation/tracking. The action’s stationary point yields Euler–Lagrange ODEs for the 2D position. Saliency maps are obtained post hoc by counting trajectory visitations across multiple runs. Parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) are tuned via simulated annealing on a small subset. The method is evaluated on MIT1003/MIT300/CAT2000 for images and SFU for videos, claiming competitive scores versus a selection of classic and one deep saliency model.

Strengths
- Presents a coherent dynamical viewpoint that unifies saccades, fixation, and (purportedly) tracking in a single ODE framework.
- Derivation connects to established principles (optical flow brightness constancy) and encourages interpretable components.
- Training-light and capable of producing explicit scanpaths in real time.
- The idea of deriving saliency as a byproduct of a scanpath model is appealing and aligns with bottom-up theories.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness
- Kinetic energy is given as K = (1/m) ||ẋ||^2 rather than the standard (m/2)||ẋ||^2. This raises unit/scale inconsistencies throughout, including the bound λ < m/4 later on. Several derivations in the appendix contain apparent typos and algebraic mistakes (e.g., determinant D, repeated indices, signs). The confining potential uses indicator functions, making V non-smooth at boundaries, contradicting the smoothness assumptions used elsewhere.
- The brightness-invariance approximation B̄ replaces the true objective with an upper bound, changing the variational problem. The paper claims V1 ≈ V2 on static images but offers no deeper analysis of when this approximation preserves behavior or stability; for dynamic scenes, the impact is unclear.
- The alternation C(t, x) = b_x^2 cos^2(ωt) + p_x^2 sin^2(ωt) introduces a time-scale ω that is not specified, justified, or analyzed for sensitivity. This is a key design choice (to avoid “trapping”) yet feels ad hoc without ablation.
- Stability/well-posedness: beyond a simple λ bound, there is no analysis of existence/uniqueness, sensitivity to m, k, ω, or stiffness of the ODE. Practical damping/friction is not discussed; the B-term is velocity-dependent but does not guarantee convergence or bounded accelerations.
- Preprocessing introduces a Gaussian center bias and initializes trajectories near the center. Together these can substantially inflate AUC-type metrics. There is no use of shuffled AUC (sAUC) or reporting of center-bias-sensitive vs center-bias-robust metrics to disentangle these effects.

Empirical validation
- Parameter tuning (simulated annealing) uses a small batch (100 images) from CAT2000-TRAIN, followed by a grid search for additional blur/center-bias parameters. This layered tuning risks overfitting to the training metrics, and there is no cross-validation or sensitivity analysis.
- Comparisons omit many state-of-the-art deep models available by 2017 (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON variants, MLNet, SAM-ResNet), especially on MIT300. The baselines selected are mostly older or mid-level methods. Claims of being “best in half of the cases” are hard to interpret without full, up-to-date leaderboards. In video, SFU has only 12 clips; results there are anecdotal and not competitive evidence.
- No ablations isolating the role of each term (remove B, remove C or p vs b, remove center bias, vary ω, vary initialization). Only V1 vs V2 is shown for static images. This makes it difficult to attribute gains to the proposed principles vs the added center bias and heuristics.
- Saliency-only evaluation is insufficient for a scanpath model. There is no comparison to human scanpaths using established metrics (e.g., MultiMatch, scanpath similarity, time-to-first-fixation, saccade amplitude/velocity distributions). The claim of tracking capability is not evaluated on tracking benchmarks or with appropriate metrics.
- Reproducibility is hindered by missing details (values/ranges for m, k, ω, integration scheme and step size, noise in initial conditions, preprocessing “rectangular zone” detection algorithm). Several citations are incomplete.

Clarity
- The writing contains many typos and grammatical errors; equation numbering and notation have inconsistencies; references are partially incomplete. Key design choices (ω, initialization, parameter ranges) are underspecified.

Originality
- Casting bottom-up attention as a least-action problem is interesting, but many components (gradient-based “curiosity,” optical-flow brightness constancy) are well-known. Dynamical systems for saccades and saliency have been explored. The alternating local/peripheral term is a novel twist but ad hoc. Overall, incremental conceptual novelty without the empirical rigor to establish clear advantages.

Significance
- Without strong, fair comparisons to current SOTA, center-bias-controlled metrics, and comprehensive ablations/scanpath analyses, it is difficult to conclude impact. The approach could be a useful conceptual or pedagogical model, but the present evidence is insufficient for NeurIPS.

Pros
- Conceptually neat, unified dynamical framework.
- Simple, training-light, and real-time friendly.
- Derivation connects to classical vision (optical flow) and yields interpretable terms.

Cons
- Mathematical derivations contain inconsistencies; key design choices are ad hoc and under-justified.
- Heavy reliance on center bias/center initialization; lack of sAUC and scanpath metrics.
- Limited, possibly outdated baselines; small video evaluation; insufficient ablations and sensitivity analyses.
- Reproducibility gaps and clarity issues.

Recommendations to improve
- Fix theoretical inconsistencies (kinetic energy, determinant, smoothness) and provide stability/sensitivity analyses; specify ω and justify its choice.
- Report sAUC, CC, NSS, KL, SIM; evaluate with and without center bias and non-central initializations.
- Add comprehensive ablations per term (b vs p, C vs B, V), and sensitivity to m, k, λ, ω.
- Include modern baselines on MIT300/CAT2000 (DeepGaze, SALICON, SAM, etc.) and use official benchmarks.
- Evaluate scanpaths with established metrics and provide tracking-specific evaluations.
- Release code and detail all preprocessing/integration settings.
