This paper presents a novel, variational approach to modeling visual attention, framing it as a problem of finding scanpaths that balance curiosity (detail) and brightness invariance. The core idea – deriving attention dynamics from a least-action principle – is a bold and interesting departure from traditional saliency-map-based approaches, and a welcome one. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing methods which largely rely on centralized control via saliency and propose a more emergent, dynamic view of attention. The mathematical formulation, while dense, appears sound, and the attempt to connect the model to established concepts like optical flow is commendable.

The primary strength of this work lies in its originality. The variational approach is genuinely new and offers a potentially unifying framework for understanding both saccadic eye movements *and* object tracking. This is a significant conceptual leap. The model isn’t explicitly trying to *reproduce* saliency maps, but rather *explains* scanpaths, with saliency arising as a byproduct. This shift in perspective is valuable. The authors also demonstrate a reasonable experimental setup, evaluating on standard datasets (images and video) and comparing to existing methods.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. While the mathematical derivation is present (mostly in the Appendix), the connection between the chosen Lagrangian terms and their biological plausibility feels somewhat tenuous. The justification for *why* these specific principles (boundedness, curiosity, brightness invariance) should drive attention, beyond their intuitive appeal, could be stronger. The parameters are tuned using simulated annealing, which is a reasonable approach, but the description lacks sufficient detail regarding the search space and convergence criteria. The ablation studies presented, comparing V1 and V2, are limited. While the results are statistically equivalent, a deeper investigation into the impact of the brightness invariance approximation would be helpful. Crucially, the comparison to state-of-the-art is somewhat superficial. While the results are competitive, the authors don’t delve into *why* their model succeeds or fails in specific scenarios, nor do they offer a comparative analysis of computational cost or efficiency. The claim of real-time performance needs more rigorous substantiation. The visualizations of scanpaths in the supplementary material are limited. Demonstrating the model tracking moving objects in a more convincing way would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **High Originality:** The variational approach to visual attention is a genuinely novel contribution.
*   **Conceptual Shift:** Moving from saliency-map prediction to scanpath generation is a valuable perspective.
*   **Mathematical Rigor:** The formulation appears theoretically sound.
*   **Competitive Performance:** Achieves comparable results to existing methods despite its fundamentally different approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Limited Biological Justification:** The rationale for the chosen Lagrangian terms is not fully developed.
*   **Superficial Comparison:** The analysis of results compared to state-of-the-art is lacking depth.
*   **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The impact of the brightness invariance approximation warrants further investigation.
*   **Limited Visualizations:** The supporting visual evidence (scanpaths) is not compelling enough.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the identified weaknesses, I lean towards acceptance. The originality and boldness of the approach are significant. The paper introduces a fundamentally new way of thinking about visual attention, and while further validation and refinement are necessary, it has the potential to be a highly influential work. The authors prioritize a novel concept over methodological perfection, and I believe that is a worthwhile trade-off in this case. I encourage the authors to address the concerns outlined above, particularly concerning the biological justification and comparative analysis, in a revised version. A more in-depth investigation of the model's behavior and limitations, along with stronger visualizations, would substantially strengthen the paper.



**Rating:** Accept (with minor revisions)
