Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a dynamical, variational formulation of visual attention in which scanpaths arise as trajectories that extremize an action. The Lagrangian includes: (i) a boundary potential to keep the trajectory within the “retina” (image domain), (ii) a “curiosity” term that attracts the trajectory to regions with high brightness gradient magnitude at local and peripheral scales, alternated via cos^2/sin^2 modulation, and (iii) a brightness-invariance term derived from the optical-flow brightness constancy constraint to encourage fixation and motion tracking. The resulting Euler–Lagrange equations define second-order ODEs for the 2D focus-of-attention trajectory. Saliency maps are computed a posteriori by counting trajectory visits. Parameters are fit via simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a subset of CAT2000-TRAIN. Experiments on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video report competitive saliency metrics versus several baselines including classical (Itti/Koch [10], information maximization [16]) and learned methods (e.g., CNN-based [18], LSTM-based [21]).

The work relates to classical saliency frameworks with winner-take-all and inhibition-of-return [9,10], and to machine learning-based saliency [1,18]. It differs by treating attention as the solution to a variational principle that unifies saccadic exploration (via curiosity) and fixation/tracking (via brightness constancy), and by producing saliency maps as a byproduct of simulated scanpaths, closer in spirit to dynamical/saccadic models (e.g., [6]) than to purely feed-forward saliency maps.

Quality
- Theory: The variational setup is plausible and the Euler–Lagrange derivation is mostly standard for velocity-dependent “potentials.” However, there are technical inconsistencies:
  - Kinetic energy is written as K(ẋ) = 1/m |ẋ|^2 (Eq. 8) while later derivations use ∂K/∂ẋ = m ẋ ⇒ d/dt∂K/∂ẋ = m ẍ. This only holds if K = (m/2)|ẋ|^2; otherwise the dimensions and derivatives are inconsistent. This should be corrected.
  - The determinant D used to isolate ẍ has algebraic typos and the final condition on λ is unclear/inconsistent (λ < m/4 is stated, then a different inequality is sketched). Please provide a clean, self-contained proof of existence/uniqueness and the precise parameter constraints.
  - The “brightness invariance” approximation for static images (Eq. 6) is reasonable ((a+b)^2 ≤ 2a^2+2b^2), but the empirical claim that it is “statistically equivalent” to the exact form lacks reported numbers and tests.
  - The curiosity term C uses |∇b|^2 and |∇p|^2. In static images, the approximate brightness-invariance term introduces |∇b|^2‖ẋ‖^2, which can be redundant with C; discussion/ablation would clarify their distinct roles.
- Implementation/evaluation:
  - The experiments rely on heavy center bias: inputs are multiplied by a Gaussian centered in the frame; initialization is near center; additional blur/center-bias parameters are tuned by grid search to optimize metrics. Since center bias strongly impacts AUC-type metrics, please report ablations (with/without multiplicative center bias; random vs centered initialization) to quantify its contribution.
  - The paper asserts state-of-the-art competitiveness and “best scores in half of the cases,” but tables/metrics are not included in the submission text. For reproducibility and verification, provide the actual numbers (AUC-Judd, NSS, sAUC, CC, KL, etc.) with standard deviations and significance tests, and ensure fair comparison against strong contemporary baselines (e.g., deep saliency models trained on large datasets).
  - For video, the claim of tracking behavior is qualitative. Please include quantitative motion/scanpath analyses (e.g., dynamic NSS/sAUC, correlation with ground-truth motion, scanpath similarity metrics like MultiMatch or string-edit distances).
  - The simulated annealing protocol optimizes parameters on a 100-image subset of CAT2000-TRAIN; please clarify cross-dataset generalization (e.g., use that configuration on MIT300 without tuning; report any dataset-specific tuning). Also, specify ω, m, λ, ηb, ηp values and sensitivity.
  - Computational cost: 199 runs per image for 1 s of simulated time may be non-trivial. Provide runtime and complexity; contrast “real-time scanpath” claim with the number of Monte Carlo runs needed for a stable saliency map.

Clarity
- The manuscript has many typos/grammar issues and several equations contain notational inconsistencies (e.g., repeated indices, missing factors, ambiguous vector vs scalar notation). References are incomplete (missing years/venues); tables referenced (Tables 2–4) are absent in the text. The appendix derivation mixes notation (bx, bxx, ḃx) without consistently defining vector/matrix quantities. A thorough editing pass is needed.
- Clearly define all symbols (bx as gradient, bxx as Hessian-vector product?), specify norms, and distinguish vectors from scalars. Provide an explicit ODE system with all terms and a compact pseudocode for numerical integration and saliency accumulation.

Originality
- The unifying variational formulation that couples curiosity (edge/texture seeking) with brightness constancy for fixation/tracking is interesting and reasonably original relative to standard saliency map pipelines and WTA dynamics. However, using brightness constancy and gradient magnitude as drivers is well known in optical flow/saliency literature, and prior dynamical/saccadic models exist. The novelty resides in the least-action framing and the specific coupling, which would be strengthened by deeper analysis or unique predictions.

Significance
- Potentially useful as an interpretable, low-level, task-agnostic model that produces both scanpaths and saliency maps. Current significance is limited by unclear empirical gains over strong baselines and by confounds (center bias). Stronger, unambiguous experimental evidence and scanpath-level validation would improve impact.

Pros
- Conceptually unified, interpretable dynamical model grounded in a variational principle.
- Produces scanpaths and saliency maps within one framework; extends to video naturally.
- Lightweight feature requirements (grayscale gradients), no large-scale training.

Cons
- Technical inconsistencies in the derivation (kinetic energy, parameter constraints) and notational clarity.
- Incomplete/missing results tables; limited ablations; strong reliance on center bias and initialization.
- Limited comparison to state-of-the-art deep models; lack of scanpath-level validation.
- Some redundancy between terms; limited analysis of parameter sensitivity and ω’s role.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the Lagrangian (use K = (m/2)|ẋ|^2) and re-derive the EL equations; provide a clear, correct condition ensuring D > 0 and existence/uniqueness.
- Add comprehensive ablations: remove center bias; vary initialization; remove curiosity/brightness terms individually; vary ω; report their effects on metrics.
- Include complete quantitative results with confidence intervals and proper baselines, for both images and videos; add scanpath metrics.
- Discuss computational cost and provide code. Consider evaluating against more recent saliency benchmarks/models.
- Improve writing, notation, and references; ensure all tables/figures are present.

Overall, the paper presents an appealing idea but requires substantial technical and experimental strengthening to meet NeurIPS standards.
