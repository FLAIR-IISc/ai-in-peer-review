Summary
The paper proposes a variational formulation for bottom-up visual attention in dynamic scenes. A Lagrangian is defined with (i) a boundary potential to keep the “particle” within the image, (ii) a “curiosity” potential encouraging motion toward high brightness gradients (with a peripheral/blurred term alternated in time), and (iii) a brightness-invariance term derived from the optical flow constraint to encourage fixation/tracking. Euler–Lagrange equations are derived (EYMOL), numerically integrated to generate scanpaths; saliency maps are then obtained as visit counts. Parameters are tuned via simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000-TRAIN. The method is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video, and is claimed to be competitive with several baselines.

Strengths
- Presents a clean dynamical-systems perspective on attention, with a unified treatment of saccades and (putative) tracking via a variational principle.
- Derives explicit equations of motion and discusses conditions for well-posedness.
- Simple to implement; saliency emerges as a byproduct of simulated scanpaths.
- Includes experiments on both images and videos; leverages established benchmarks.

Weaknesses
Technical correctness and clarity
- The kinetic energy is written as K(ẋ) = 1/m ẋ^2, which is nonstandard (and dimensionally inconsistent) compared to the usual (m/2)‖ẋ‖^2. If this is intentional, it needs justification; otherwise it undermines the physical analogy and the Euler–Lagrange derivation.
- Several derivations contain algebraic inconsistencies/typos (e.g., determinant D lines (41–46) repeat bx1, appear to omit bx2, and simplify to an expression that seems incorrect; a stated bound λ < m/4 is not convincingly derived). These issues cast doubt on the stability/uniqueness claims.
- The “retina” boundary potential is piecewise with Iverson brackets; smoothness and differentiability assumptions for the variational derivations are not reconciled with these discontinuities.
- The time-alternating curiosity term C(t,x) with cos^2/sin^2 is not motivated beyond intuition; its effective average is a simple convex combination unless ω is chosen to resonate with the dynamics. No ablation or sensitivity analysis is provided.

Methodology, evaluation, and baselines
- The model introduces a strong center bias by multiplying the input with a Gaussian and initializing all trajectories near the center, and then further tunes blur/center parameters via grid search to maximize AUC-Judd/NSS. Without shuffled-AUC, IG, CC, or KL (which better control for center bias), performance claims may be inflated. The paper cites these metrics but appears to emphasize AUC-Judd/NSS.
- Parameter tuning uses simulated annealing on a small subset of CAT2000-TRAIN, but the description suggests that “configurations … are tested on the whole dataset, to select the best one,” which risks test-set leakage. Clear train/val/test separation is needed.
- Comparisons omit several strong contemporary baselines in 2016–2017 (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON, MLNet, SAM-ResNet/SAM-VGG, etc.), and do not equalize postprocessing (center bias/blur) across methods.
- The video evaluation is on a very small dataset (12 clips) with limited comparisons; claims of real-time performance lack runtime numbers.
- No ablations are reported to isolate the contributions of boundary term, curiosity (local vs peripheral), brightness-invariance (exact vs approximate), center bias, or number of trajectories. The equivalence of V1 vs V2 on static images is shown, but broader ablations are missing.

Relation to prior work
- The work relates to a long line of dynamic/saccadic models (e.g., Le Meur & Liu 2015; Bruce & Tsotsos; Itti & Baldi; reinforcement/active vision approaches). The novelty of using a least-action derivation is interesting but functionally similar to prior gradient-/surprise-driven dynamics; the paper would benefit from sharper positioning and clearer differences in behavior/bounds guarantees.
- The claim that saliency is a byproduct of a scanpath model is not new per se; many works have generated saliency from simulated fixations. The paper should clarify what is fundamentally gained by the variational formalism.

Reproducibility and completeness
- Important implementation details are missing: actual parameter values (m, λ, ηb, ηp, ω, k), bounds of the search domain D, ODE solver and step size, stopping criteria, number of steps per second, and the algorithm that determines the “rectangular zone” of the retina.
- Code/data availability is not stated.
- Statistical significance claims are made without showing confidence intervals, standard deviations, or appropriate tests across images.

Quality: Mixed. The idea is coherent, but derivational inaccuracies and evaluation choices weaken confidence.
Clarity: Fair to poor. Numerous typos and notation issues; missing details impede reproduction.
Originality: Moderate. The variational framing is interesting, but the underlying forces overlap with established mechanisms; novelty over prior dynamic saliency/scanpath models is not crisply demonstrated.
Significance: Limited at present. Without stronger, bias-controlled benchmarks and ablations, impact is uncertain.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify the kinetic energy form and rectify the derivations around D and the λ bound. Provide a rigorous, error-checked supplement.
- What is ω, how was it chosen, and what is the effect of time modulation vs a static convex combination?
- Can you report shuffled-AUC, IG, CC, KL, and SI metrics, and equalize center bias/blur postprocessing across methods?
- Provide ablations removing the Gaussian center bias and central initialization to demonstrate intrinsic dynamics.
- Clarify the train/val/test protocol to avoid test-set tuning. Are any parameters tuned on MIT300/CAT2000-TEST?
- Provide runtime and ODE solver details.

Pros
- Conceptually elegant, unified dynamical formulation.
- Simple, potentially real-time model that produces scanpaths and saliency.
- Some empirical competitiveness reported.

Cons
- Technical inconsistencies in the derivation; unclear parameterization.
- Strong center bias and tuning strategy may inflate metrics; limited and uneven baselines.
- Missing ablations and details; reproducibility concerns.

Recommendation: Reject
With rigorous corrections to the derivations, stronger and bias-controlled evaluations against contemporary baselines, and thorough ablations demonstrating where the gains come from (and whether the variational structure is crucial), this could become a solid contribution. As it stands, the technical and empirical evidence is insufficient for NeurIPS.
