Summary
The paper proposes a variational/dynamical model of overt visual attention (EYMOL), where the scanpath is generated by Euler–Lagrange equations derived from an action comprising: (i) a boundary “retina” potential, (ii) a curiosity term that alternates between local and peripheral gradient energy via a sinusoidal modulation, and (iii) a brightness-constancy penalty to encourage fixation/tracking. Saliency maps are obtained post hoc by counting pixel visits over many simulated runs. Parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) are tuned with simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000-TRAIN to maximize NSS; additional center-bias and blur are tuned by grid search. The method is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video, and claims to be competitive with several baselines.

Relation to prior work
The model echoes longstanding bottom‑up saliency ideas (e.g., gradient/contrast cues, multi-scale/peripheral processing, center bias) and uses the classical brightness-constancy assumption from optical flow. There is prior work on dynamical saccade generation and inhibition-of-return; here, the novelty is the particular variational formalization of a curiosity/brightness-constancy blend and deriving explicit ODEs. However, comparable physics-inspired or dynamical formulations exist in active vision and saliency; and modern state-of-the-art saliency prediction models are deep networks trained on fixation data.

Strengths
- Unifying framing: a single dynamical system intended to cover saccades, fixation on edges (via perpendicular motion to gradients), and potential tracking in videos.
- Conceptual simplicity: the curiosity term reduces to gradient magnitudes at two scales; the boundary potential is straightforward; saliency maps are a byproduct of simulated trajectories.
- Reported competitive results on multiple datasets; real-time claim for trajectory generation.
- Public benchmarks used; two variants (exact vs approximate brightness-constancy) compared.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and clarity
- Kinetic energy inconsistency: K(ẋ) is stated as (1/m)‖ẋ‖², yet later EL yields mẍ terms; this requires K = (1/2)m‖ẋ‖². As written, the derivation is inconsistent.
- Appendix derivations contain algebraic errors. The determinant D simplification drops λ² terms and incorrectly factors m, leading to an unjustified condition λ < m/4. Also, b is normalized to [0,1] but ∥∇b∥ need not be bounded by 1, making the stability bound suspect.
- Several notational typos and duplicated terms (e.g., b²x1 + b²x1), making it difficult to validate the equations. These issues undermine confidence in the theoretical core.

Methodological choices and validation
- Heavy reliance on post-hoc components known to inflate saliency metrics: explicit center bias (image multiplied by a Gaussian), blur tuning, multiple runs initialized near center. There is no ablation showing performance without these additions or with alternative initializations.
- Parameter tuning uses simulated annealing and grid search over multiple knobs on a training subset; robustness/sensitivity analyses are absent. It is unclear how ω, m, k, integration scheme/step, and SA details (neighbor definition, α) are chosen; reproducibility is low.
- Limited and dated baselines. By 2017, several strong deep saliency models (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON-derived, MLNet variants) were top-ranked on MIT300 and CAT2000. Comparison to only five methods (one deep) is insufficient to establish competitiveness.
- Saliency is computed from 199 stochastic runs per image; computational cost vs “real-time” claim is unclear. No timing or complexity analysis is provided.
- Scanpath modeling claims are not validated: no comparison to human scanpath metrics (e.g., MultiMatch, STED, saccade amplitude distributions), no statistical assessment of “tracking” on video beyond anecdote.

Experimental reporting
- Tables are referenced but not provided, preventing verification of the claim “best score in half of the cases.” Statistical testing and effect sizes are not shown; “statistically equivalent” for V1 vs V2 is asserted without details.
- Preprocessing steps (cropping to “totality of information,” center bias) are likely to reinforce dataset photographer bias and may not generalize.

Originality and significance
- The use of brightness-constancy and gradient-energy cues is standard; the novelty is primarily the specific variational packaging and sinusoidal alternation between scales (whose frequency ω is unspecified and appears ad hoc). Without strong empirical gains or deeper theoretical insight, significance is limited.
- The paper does not convincingly advance state of the art nor provide new conclusions about human attention mechanisms.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the Lagrangian and EL derivations; ensure internal consistency (K, signs, determinants), provide a rigorous and correct stability analysis (with realistic bounds on ∥∇b∥), and specify all parameters (m, k, ω) and numerical integration details.
- Provide comprehensive ablations: remove center bias; remove blur tuning; remove the peripheral term; remove brightness-constancy; vary ω; vary initial fixation distribution. Report sensitivity and generalization (no re-tuning across datasets).
- Expand baselines to contemporary top-ranked models on MIT300/CAT2000; report official benchmark ranks. Include simple strong baselines (e.g., center prior + gradient magnitude) to isolate the benefit of the dynamical system.
- Evaluate scanpath properties against human data with established metrics; quantify tracking on dynamic benchmarks beyond SFU (or provide a careful analysis on SFU).
- Release code and settings to ensure reproducibility; report runtime.

Arguments for acceptance
- Interesting variational perspective that unifies several intuitive components of attention.
- Potentially useful for generating scanpaths in applications requiring lightweight dynamics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Mathematical inconsistencies/errors in the core derivations; questionable stability condition.
- Heavy reliance on center bias/blur tuning without ablations; unclear contribution beyond standard cues.
- Insufficient and outdated baselines; claims of competitiveness not substantiated by comprehensive evidence.
- Reproducibility gaps and missing experimental details; limited analysis of scanpaths and tracking.

Recommendation
Reject. The conceptual framing is intriguing, but the paper falls short on technical soundness, rigor of validation, and clarity. A revised version addressing the derivations, adding thorough ablations and stronger comparisons, and providing scanpath analyses could be much more compelling.

Reviewer confidence: High.
