The paper proposes a novel model of visual attention, termed EYMOL, based on variational principles. It posits that attention emerges from a combination of curiosity for detailed regions and a need for brightness invariance, formalized through a Lagrangian and resulting Euler-Lagrange equations. The model generates scanpaths and, as a byproduct, saliency maps, differing from many existing approaches that directly optimize saliency estimation. Experiments are conducted on static images (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and dynamic scenes (SFU) demonstrating competitive performance.

This work builds upon decades of research in visual attention. Early models, like those by Koch & Ullman [9] and Itti et al. [10], focused on bottom-up saliency based on feature integration. Subsequent work explored top-down influences [3, 4] and learning-based approaches [1, 26, 18]. The proposed model distinguishes itself by its unifying variational framework, aiming to model attention dynamics directly rather than solely focusing on saliency map prediction. The use of a Lagrangian formalism, reminiscent of physics-based approaches to vision (e.g., optical flow estimation [20] as referenced in the paper), is a notable departure from common machine learning techniques.  The idea of tying attention to brightness invariance is also linked to classic work in vision.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The variational approach to modeling attention is a unique contribution. Framing attention as a least-action principle provides a principled foundation and potentially allows for insights into the underlying mechanisms.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The mathematical derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations, while relegated to the appendix, provides a strong theoretical basis for the model.
* **Unified framework:** The model elegantly combines curiosity and brightness invariance into a single framework, potentially addressing the "chicken and egg" dilemma mentioned in the introduction.
* **Scanpath generation:** The model directly generates scanpaths, offering a more complete account of attention than models focused solely on saliency maps.
* **Competitive performance:** The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on standard datasets, especially considering the simplicity of the model.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter sensitivity:** The performance is sensitive to the parameters (ηb, ηp, λ), requiring simulated annealing for optimization. The explanation of parameter tuning is adequate, however, a more thorough analysis of the parameter space and its impact on the results would be valuable.
* **Complexity of equations:**  The Euler-Lagrange equations, while theoretically sound, appear complex and may hinder further analysis or extension of the model.
* **Limited biological plausibility discussion:** While the introduction mentions biological inspiration, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of how the proposed principles and equations relate to known neural mechanisms of attention.
* **Approximation impact:** The approximation of the brightness invariance term (6) is justified for static images, but its effect on performance in dynamic scenes isn't sufficiently discussed.
* **Clarity of notation:**  Some notation (e.g., in the Appendix) could be clarified for better readability. 



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The novel variational framework offers a promising new direction for modeling visual attention.
* The theoretical grounding and mathematical rigor are commendable.
* The competitive performance on standard datasets demonstrates the viability of the approach.
* The work addresses a fundamental question in visual attention research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The complexity of the equations may limit the accessibility and further development of the model.
* The lack of detailed discussion regarding biological plausibility weakens the connection to real neural mechanisms.
* The sensitivity to parameter tuning and the approximation used require further investigation.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution to the field of visual attention. The variational approach is a significant departure from existing methods and offers a principled framework for understanding attention dynamics. While there are some weaknesses related to complexity, biological plausibility, and parameter sensitivity, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. The competitive experimental results provide evidence of the model's effectiveness.  I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to address the clarity of notation and further discuss the limitations of the approximation and the biological relevance of the model.
