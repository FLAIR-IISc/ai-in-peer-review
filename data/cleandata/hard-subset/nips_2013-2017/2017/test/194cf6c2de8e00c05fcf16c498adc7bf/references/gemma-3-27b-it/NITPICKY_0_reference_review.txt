This paper presents a novel model of visual attention, termed EYMOL, grounded in principles of curiosity and brightness invariance, formalized through a variational approach. The authors aim to move beyond saliency map-centric models towards a more biologically plausible mechanism directly predicting scanpaths. While the core idea is interesting and attempts to bridge the gap between bottom-up and top-down attention, a multitude of issues, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to questionable methodological choices and inadequate experimental validation, lead me to recommend rejection.

The introduction establishes the context well, highlighting the interplay between explicit and implicit attentional processes. However, the phrasing is often verbose and lacks precision ("trapped into a sort of eggs-chicken dilemma"). The literature review, while broad, feels somewhat disjointed and lacks critical evaluation of the cited works. For instance, stating that many papers “rely on models that are purposely designed to optimize the approximation of the saliency map” is a broad generalization without substantiation. 

The core of the paper – the model itself – suffers from several weaknesses. The mathematical formulation, while presented formally, feels unnecessarily complex. The use of Iverson’s notation (Eq. 3) is jarring and obscures the underlying concept. More importantly, the justification for the chosen terms in the Lagrangian (brightness invariance particularly) feels weak. Why does *this* particular constraint lead to realistic fixation behavior? A more thorough discussion connecting the mathematical choices to neurophysiological evidence is needed. The term 'soft-satisfaction' of the brightness invariance constraint (Eq. 5) is not clearly defined. The leap from the parallelogram law to inequality (6) feels unjustified and requires a more rigorous derivation.

The experimental setup raises significant concerns. The parameter estimation using simulated annealing (SA) is vaguely described. The size of the training batch (100 images from CAT2000) is insufficient, and the justification for selecting 5 images per category is weak. The limitations of this approach, particularly the computational cost, are acknowledged, but this does not excuse the limited scope. Applying a Gaussian blob to both b and p feels like a post-hoc adjustment to improve performance rather than a principled part of the model. The claim that the model produces saliency maps “as a byproduct” is disingenuous – the entire evaluation hinges on the quality of these maps.

The results are presented without sufficient statistical rigor. Tables 2 and 3 show comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, but the statistical significance of the differences is not assessed. The claim of "statistical equivalence" between V1 and V2 is insufficiently supported – a proper statistical test should be reported. Furthermore, the selection of comparison methods seems somewhat arbitrary. Reporting only AUC-Judd and NSS scores is limiting; other relevant metrics should be included.  The statement about “competing well” in the video experiments is overly optimistic given the limited detail provided.  The observation that the model “shows evidences of tracking moving objects” is anecdotal and requires quantitative validation.

Several minor issues further detract from the paper's quality. The notation is inconsistent throughout. For example, bx is used as both a gradient and a component in the equations, creating ambiguity. The use of subscripts and superscripts is sometimes unclear. There are grammatical errors and awkward phrasing throughout the text. The appendix, while intended to provide technical details, is difficult to follow due to the dense mathematical notation and lack of explanatory text. The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., some citations include pages, others do not).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel attempt to model attention directly as scanpaths, rather than via saliency maps.
*   Intriguing application of variational principles to a computational model of attention.
*   Demonstrated performance comparable to existing methods on standard datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of rigorous theoretical justification for the model’s key components.
*   Insufficiently detailed and validated experimental setup.
*   Limited statistical analysis of results.
*   Numerous minor flaws in notation, writing, and presentation.
*   The model's complexity does not translate to a demonstrably significant improvement over simpler approaches.

In conclusion, while the paper explores a promising research direction, the numerous weaknesses in its theoretical foundation, methodology, and presentation outweigh its strengths. The paper requires substantial revision and further validation before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field. Therefore, I recommend rejection.
