This paper proposes a novel variational approach to modeling visual attention, framing it as a problem of least action driven by curiosity and brightness invariance. The authors aim to generate scanpaths, not simply saliency maps, and present a mathematically grounded model with connections to physics. While the idea is intriguing and potentially valuable, the paper suffers from numerous, often minor, technical and presentational shortcomings that cumulatively detract from its overall impact.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The variational approach to modeling attention, linking it to principles of least action, is a fresh perspective.
* **Unified Framework:** The attempt to model both saccadic eye movements and object tracking within a single framework is commendable.
* **Mathematical Rigor:** The derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations, while somewhat dense, demonstrates a commitment to mathematical grounding.
* **Real-Time Capability:** The authors highlight the model's potential for real-time applications, a practical advantage.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Rigorous Justification for Principles:** The "driving principles" of boundedness, curiosity, and brightness invariance are stated without substantial justification rooted in neurobiology or perceptual psychology. Why these specific principles? What are the alternatives, and why are they less suitable?
* **Parameter Sensitivity & Simulated Annealing:** The reliance on simulated annealing for parameter estimation raises concerns. The search space is described vaguely (“parallelepiped-domain”), and the justification for the chosen batch size (100 images) is weak. The claim that it prevents “numerical issues” is insufficient without elaboration.  The authors acknowledge instability with high ηb/ηp, but don’t fully explore the implications for parameter selection.
* **Approximation & Justification:** The approximation in equation (6) is presented as simplifying the equations, but the justification – parallelogram law of Hilbert spaces – feels tacked on and doesn’t clearly explain *why* this approximation is valid or negligibly affects results.  A sensitivity analysis to the approximation would be valuable.
* **Experimental Setup – Input Preprocessing:** The introduction of a Gaussian blob for center bias is not well motivated. While common in saliency modeling, its purpose within *this* variational framework needs clearer explanation.
* **Evaluation Metrics:** While standard metrics are used, the presentation of results feels cursory.  The tables simply state "scores," lacking sufficient statistical detail.  What are the standard deviations? Are the differences statistically significant?
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison with other methods is superficial. The authors state the model "reaches best score in half of the cases," but this lacks nuance.  Which methods are consistently outperformed? Where does the proposed model fall short?
* **Clarity of Notation:** Several equations lack sufficient explanation of variables or assumptions. For example, the definition of ‘li’ in equation (3) is vague ("i-th dimension of the rectangle which represents the retina").
* **Typos & Formatting:** Numerous minor errors throughout the text detract from the professional impression. Examples include: inconsistent use of italics, "w.r.t." should be "with respect to," “Schwarz’s theorem” should be “Schwarz theorem,” and several instances of incorrect punctuation.

**Detailed Comments:**

* **Introduction:** The "egg-chicken dilemma" analogy is overwrought and doesn’t add substance. The discussion of related work feels descriptive rather than critical, simply listing papers without highlighting specific gaps or limitations.
* **Section 2.1:** The notation for the gradient (bx, px) is ambiguous.  Is this a vector or a scalar?  The role of ω in equation (4) is unexplained.
* **Section 2.2:** The rationale for assigning a negative sign to the curiosity term (C) is weak.
* **Appendix:** The derivations are dense and challenging to follow. More intermediate steps and explanations would be beneficial.  The discussion of the parameter space for λ feels incomplete. The condition D > 0 is not adequately explained in relation to the model's stability.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to visual attention modeling.
* The mathematical formulation is sophisticated and potentially opens new avenues for research.
* The real-time capability is a significant practical advantage.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of rigorous justification for the core principles weakens the theoretical foundation.
* The parameter estimation process and sensitivity analysis are insufficient.
* The experimental evaluation lacks depth and statistical rigor.
* The numerous minor errors and stylistic issues detract from the overall quality.
* The presentation, while mathematically detailed, is often unclear and difficult to follow.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially interesting ideas presented, the paper is currently not of sufficient quality for acceptance at NIPS. The numerous technical shortcomings, lack of rigorous validation, and presentational flaws outweigh the novelty of the approach. I recommend **rejection** with the suggestion that the authors address the concerns outlined above before resubmission. A significant overhaul is required to strengthen the theoretical justification, improve the experimental evaluation, and enhance the clarity and polish of the manuscript.
