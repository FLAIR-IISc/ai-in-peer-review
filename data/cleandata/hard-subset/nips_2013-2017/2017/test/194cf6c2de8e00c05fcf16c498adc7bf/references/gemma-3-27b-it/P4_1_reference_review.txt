This paper proposes a novel model of visual attention, termed EYMOL, based on a variational approach and unifying principles of curiosity and brightness invariance. The core idea is to model scanpaths as the solution to Euler-Lagrange equations derived from a generalized action principle, rather than relying on global saliency map computation. The authors compare the resulting, implicitly computed, saliency maps to state-of-the-art methods on image and video datasets.

This work builds upon the established concept of saliency-based visual attention (Itti et al., 2005; Koch & Ullman, 1985) but departs from the typical bottom-up, feature-integration approaches. Unlike methods that explicitly optimize for saliency map approximation (Judd et al., 2009; Vig et al., 2014), EYMOL derives saliency as a byproduct of simulating scanpath dynamics. The use of a least-action principle is a novel way to frame the problem, potentially linking it to broader principles of optimal control in biological systems.  The variational approach is reminiscent of work on optical flow estimation (Horn & Schunck, 1981), which is explicitly incorporated through the brightness invariance constraint.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The variational approach to modeling visual attention is a significant departure from existing methods. Framing the problem through an action principle and deriving scanpaths dynamically is a compelling idea.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The mathematical formulation using Euler-Lagrange equations provides a solid theoretical basis for the model. The inclusion of brightness invariance and curiosity as driving principles is well-motivated.
* **Unified Framework:** The model attempts to capture both saccadic eye movements and object tracking within a single framework, which is an ambitious goal.
* **Competitive Performance:** The model achieves competitive results on standard saliency detection benchmarks (MIT300, CAT2000, SFU), demonstrating its practical viability.
* **Clear Mathematical Description:** The paper provides a reasonably clear mathematical description of the model, including the derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Tuning:** The simulated annealing parameter estimation process seems complex and lacks detailed justification for the chosen parameter range. The sensitivity of the model to parameter choices isn’t thoroughly explored.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors claim real-time performance, the numerical integration of the differential equations may be computationally demanding for high-resolution images or videos. This is alluded to but not rigorously quantified.
* **Limited Biological Plausibility:** While inspired by biological principles, the model remains a simplified abstraction. It doesn't directly address the neural mechanisms underlying attention, or the role of specific brain areas.
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** The comparison between versions V1 and V2 (with and without the exact form of brightness invariance) is helpful, but more extensive ablation studies could reveal the relative importance of different terms in the Lagrangian.
* **Clarity of Appendix:** The appendix, containing the detailed derivations, could be more clearly presented. Some equations could benefit from further explanation.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a well-defined mathematical framework and experimental validation. However, the complexity of the model and the lack of detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity raise some concerns.  The work is complete in that it presents a full model and evaluates it on standard datasets. The authors are generally careful in their claims and acknowledge potential limitations.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, the mathematical notation can be dense at times. The description of the simulated annealing process could be more detailed. A more intuitive explanation of the action principle and its connection to biological motivation would be beneficial.

**Originality:** The variational approach and the unification of curiosity and brightness invariance are original contributions. While building upon existing work in saliency modeling, the paper offers a novel perspective. The related work section is comprehensive and adequately references relevant literature.

**Significance:** The results are significant in that they demonstrate a viable alternative to traditional saliency map-based approaches. The model has the potential to advance the understanding of visual attention mechanisms and could be applied to various applications, such as image/video understanding, robotics, and human-computer interaction. However, the practical impact will depend on its ability to scale to more complex scenarios and its integration with other cognitive processes.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel approach to modeling visual attention with a strong theoretical foundation.
* Competitive performance on standard benchmarks.
* Potential for a more biologically plausible model of attention.
* Unifies saccadic movements and object tracking.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Parameter tuning is complex and lacks detailed justification.
* Computational cost may be a limiting factor.
* Limited exploration of biological plausibility.
*  Need for more extensive ablation studies.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to modeling visual attention. While there are some weaknesses, the originality and potential significance of the work outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity, address the parameter tuning concerns, and provide a more detailed discussion of the computational cost. The authors should consider expanding the ablation studies to further analyze the contribution of individual components of the model.
