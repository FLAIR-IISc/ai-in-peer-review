- Proposes a novel variational (least-action) framework for modeling visual attention via scanpath dynamics and derives Euler-Lagrange equations.  
- Claims visual attention arises from boundedness (retina constraints), curiosity-driven detail gradients, and brightness invariance for fixation/tracking.  
- Derives saliency maps as a byproduct of simulated scanpaths and evaluates on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video datasets.  
- Strength: rigorous, well-structured mathematical formulation with clear derivations in the appendix.  
- Strength: conceptually elegant unified framework for static and dynamic attention.  
- Strength: comprehensive experimental setup covering images and videos.  
- Strength: parameter estimation via simulated annealing is reasonable though limited.  
- Weakness: theoretical justification for the brightness-invariance upper-bound approximation (Eq.6) is lacking.  
- Weakness: no sufficient evidence that the approximation does not degrade performance, especially for dynamic scenes.  
- Weakness: limited analysis and demonstration of actual object-tracking capabilities in videos.  
- Weakness: parameter estimation performed on only 100 training images, raising representativeness and sensitivity concerns.  
- Weakness: inadequate comparison to related work (e.g., Bayesian Surprise, optical flow, prior variational attention methods).  
- Clarity issue: ambiguous claim that saliency maps are merely a byproduct of scanpath modeling without distinguishing from prior scanpath models.  
- Clarity issue: confusing and insufficiently justified explanation of brightness invariance and its relation to human fixation behavior.  
- Clarity issue: superficial discussion of why the model excels on some metrics (e.g., NSS) but not others (e.g., AUC-Judd).  
- Originality: variational approach is novel in this context and offers a unified interpretation of principles.  
- Originality concern: insufficiently differentiated from prior “surprise” and variational attention work; "curiosity" resembles Bayesian surprise.  
- Originality concern: mischaracterizes developmental claims about early fixations and the eggs-or-chicken dilemma.  
- Significance: potential for real-time applications and unified static/dynamic attention modeling.  
- Significance concern: performance gains over state-of-the-art are marginal and possibly not statistically significant.  
- Significance concern: no analysis of failure cases or detailed tracking performance examples.  
- Overall assessment: theoretically interesting but experimental support and comparisons are insufficient; recommend weak rejection.  
- Suggested improvements: expand related work comparisons, justify brightness-invariance approximation theoretically/empirically, add failure-case analysis, run statistical significance tests, clarify biological relevance, and provide detailed video tracking examples.
