Summary
This paper proposes a physics-inspired, variational formulation of bottom-up visual attention. The authors define a Lagrangian whose stationary path (via Euler–Lagrange equations) drives a 2D “focus-of-attention” trajectory on the image/video: (i) a boundary potential keeps the trajectory on the retina; (ii) a curiosity term attracts the trajectory to high-gradient regions and alternates local vs peripheral (blurred) gradients via cos^2/sin^2 temporal modulation; (iii) a brightness-invariance term penalizes deviations from the optical-flow constraint to encourage fixation and, in videos, motion tracking. Saliency maps are obtained as a byproduct by counting trajectory visitations over multiple random initializations. Parameters are tuned by simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000, and the model is evaluated on MIT1003/300, CAT2000, and SFU video, showing competitive scores vs some classic and a few recent methods.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: A single variational framework yields saccadic-like explorations and (in principle) fixation/tracking behavior, bridging ideas from optical flow and saliency.
- Minimal features and training-light: Relies only on grayscale gradients and a blurred pyramid; no learned feature extractors.
- Generality: The same dynamical system is applied to static images and videos, with saliency maps emerging from scanpaths rather than being explicitly optimized.
- Empirical indications: The approximate and exact brightness-invariance formulations perform similarly on static images; the model is competitive on several metrics (details not fully shown in the text but claimed).

Weaknesses and detailed reasoning

Quality (technical soundness, support)
- Kinetic energy inconsistency: The paper defines K(ẋ) = (1/m) ẋ^2, but later uses EL equations consistent with K = (m/2)∥ẋ∥^2 (yielding mẍ). This dimensional and derivational inconsistency is critical and must be corrected; otherwise the equations of motion and parameter interpretations are unclear.
- Derivation issues: The determinant D and the bound λ < m/4 rely on |∇b| normalization assumptions that are not justified (brightness b ∈ [0,1] does not bound gradient magnitudes). There are also notational errors (e.g., repeated b^2x1 term) and unexplained steps (e.g., simplification of D).
- Missing dynamics details: The system appears undamped; without explicit friction/viscosity, trajectories may oscillate. The paper relies on the velocity-dependent “potential” term to regularize, but does not analyze stability or existence/uniqueness of solutions.
- Hyperparameters under-specified: The frequency ω, mass m, boundary stiffness k, numerical integrator/time step, and simulated annealing search domain are not fully specified. It is unclear how sensitive results are to these choices.
- Post hoc saliency tuning undermines the “byproduct” claim: The preprocessing multiplies images by a center Gaussian (center bias), initial conditions are near center, and the final maps are further tuned via grid search (blur and center parameters) to maximize metrics. These choices can markedly inflate AUC/NSS and confound the attribution of gains to the variational dynamics.

Clarity
- The manuscript contains numerous typos, incomplete references, and inserted boilerplate text; key equations have inconsistencies as noted. Important details (ω, m, k, parameter domains, integrator settings) are missing. A reader cannot reproduce results from the current description.

Originality
- Using a Lagrangian/least-action view for attentional dynamics is interesting and not common in recent saliency literature. However, the core ingredients (gradient-based “interest,” peripheral blur, optical-flow constraint) are individually standard; the novelty lies in their unification and the dynamic trajectory formulation. Related dynamical systems for scanpath generation (e.g., Le Meur et al.) and modern deep saliency approaches are only partially discussed.

Significance
- Potentially useful conceptual contribution that could inspire future work on model-based scanpath generation and unified fixation/tracking. However, empirical significance is limited by: (i) reliance on strong center bias and post-processing; (ii) absence of ablations (e.g., remove curiosity, remove brightness-invariance, no peripheral alternation); (iii) lack of scanpath-level evaluations; and (iv) incomplete comparisons to strong contemporary deep saliency models (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON-era CNNs). The SFU video dataset is small (12 clips) and the tracking claim is anecdotal.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct and carefully re-derive the Lagrangian, kinetic energy, and EL equations; provide a stability analysis or damping term, and clearly state all parameters (m, k, ω) with values and rationale.
- Provide full implementation details (integrator, time step, initial condition distributions) and release code.
- Include ablation studies quantifying the contribution of each term and the effect of cos^2/sin^2 alternation. Report sensitivity to ω and λ.
- Evaluate with center-bias–robust metrics (sAUC, IG), report a pure center-bias baseline, and measure against simple gradient-based baselines (e.g., GBVS, BMS) and recent deep models on standard benchmarks.
- Report scanpath metrics (e.g., MultiMatch, string edit) and quantitative tracking performance.
- Clarify fairness: whether blur/center tuning is also applied to baselines; avoid dataset-specific tuning that leaks into test metrics.

Pros
- Elegant, unified, and potentially general modeling framework.
- Simple features; moderate empirical performance on image/video saliency.
- Bridges fixation and tracking in one dynamical system.

Cons
- Critical derivational and notational errors; missing key parameter details.
- Heavy reliance on center bias and post hoc map tuning.
- Limited, outdated baselines; no ablations; no scanpath/tracking quantification.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues.

Recommendation
Reject in its current form. The central idea is appealing and could be impactful with a sound, transparent derivation and a more rigorous, bias-controlled evaluation (including ablations and modern baselines). I encourage the authors to address the technical inconsistencies, improve clarity, and strengthen the empirical analysis.
