### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel model of visual attention, named EYMOL (EYe MOvement Laws), grounded in a variational framework based on the principle of least action. The core idea is to model the scanpath of the eye's focus of attention as the trajectory of a particle that minimizes a generalized action functional. Unlike the majority of prior work in visual attention (e.g., Itti et al. [10], Bruce & Tsotsos [16], and recent deep learning models [18]), which primarily focuses on computing a static saliency map from which scanpaths are subsequently simulated, this work treats the scanpath itself as the primary output of a dynamic system.

The model is derived from three fundamental principles, each contributing a term to the system's Lagrangian:
1.  **Boundedness:** A harmonic potential confines the trajectory within the image boundaries.
2.  **Curiosity:** An attractive potential draws attention towards regions of high detail (large image gradients). This term novelly alternates between a local (original image) and a peripheral (blurred image) field to prevent the focus from becoming trapped.
3.  **Brightness Invariance:** A term based on the brightness constancy constraint (`ḃ ≈ 0`), widely used in optical flow estimation [20], is included to promote fixation on static object boundaries and the tracking of moving objects.

The stationary points of the action integral yield the Euler-Lagrange equations of motion, a set of second-order ordinary differential equations that govern the scanpath `x(t)`. Saliency maps, used for evaluation, are generated as a byproduct by aggregating the visitation frequencies from multiple simulated scanpaths. The model's parameters are tuned via simulated annealing. The authors evaluate their model on standard static image (MIT300, CAT2000) and dynamic video (SFU) saliency datasets, demonstrating competitive performance against several representative models from the literature.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Originality:** The primary strength of this paper is its highly original conceptualization of visual attention. Framing scanpath generation as a variational problem derived from first principles is a significant departure from the dominant "saliency-map-first" paradigm. This physics-inspired approach provides a unified and elegant mathematical framework that naturally integrates mechanisms for both exploratory saccades (curiosity) and fixation/tracking (brightness invariance).

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations from the defined Lagrangian is presented in detail in the appendix and appears correct. The model is well-defined, and the connection between the high-level principles and their mathematical instantiation is clear.

3.  **Significance:** The work has the potential to be significant. By modeling the dynamics of attention directly, it could inspire a new direction of research focused on the temporal aspects of visual exploration, rather than solely on predicting spatial saliency. The model is also computationally lightweight compared to modern deep learning architectures and is capable of real-time scanpath generation, which is valuable for practical applications.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The core principles are explained intuitively before their mathematical formulation, making the model easy to understand.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection / Areas for Improvement):**

1.  **Evaluation Mismatch:** The central claim of the paper is the development of a novel model for generating *scanpaths*. However, the entire quantitative evaluation is performed on *saliency maps*, which are a derived, time-aggregated representation. The paper misses a crucial opportunity to validate its primary contribution by comparing the dynamics of the generated scanpaths (e.g., saccade amplitude distributions, fixation durations, scanpath similarity) with human eye-tracking data. This evaluation would be far more convincing and directly relevant to the model's claims.

2.  **Justification of Model Components:** While the overall framework is principled, some specific modeling choices lack strong justification.
    *   The periodic alternation between local and peripheral fields in the curiosity term, using `cos^2(ωt)` and `sin^2(ωt)`, is an interesting heuristic to avoid local traps, but its specific functional form and the choice of frequency `ω` are not theoretically or empirically justified.
    *   For static images, the brightness invariance term `B` is replaced by an upper bound `B̄`. While experiments show this approximation works well, the theoretical justification for minimizing an upper bound within a least-action principle is not provided. It is not immediately obvious that this leads to the same dynamics.

3.  **Reproducibility:** Several key details required for reproducing the results are omitted. For instance, the values for the particle mass `m`, the elastic constant `k`, the curiosity frequency `ω`, the parameters of the Gaussian kernel for blurring `p(t, x)`, and the specific configuration of the simulated annealing algorithm are not specified. The parameters for the post-hoc Gaussian blur and center bias applied to the final saliency maps are also not detailed, making it difficult to assess their impact on the final scores.

4.  **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The selection of competing models for comparison is reasonable but could be stronger. The field of saliency prediction has advanced rapidly, and comparisons against more recent and powerful deep learning models (e.g., SALICON, ML-Net) would provide a more rigorous assessment of the model's performance on the saliency prediction sub-task.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   The paper's most significant contribution is a dynamic model of eye movements. I strongly recommend the authors include an analysis comparing the generated scanpath statistics against human data. This would directly support the central claims of the work and elevate its impact significantly.
*   Could the authors provide a more detailed motivation for the `cos^2(ωt)` modulation in the curiosity term? Are there alternative, perhaps non-periodic, mechanisms that could achieve the same goal of escaping local minima?
*   In Section 3.2, it is mentioned that a grid search over blur radius and center bias parameter `σ` was used to maximize scores. This suggests that a significant portion of the performance might come from this post-processing, which is detached from the core variational model. It would be valuable to report the scores both with and without this post-processing to clarify the contribution of the core EYMOL model.
*   The condition `λ < m/4` derived in the appendix is an interesting result of the formulation. Does the value of mass `m` have a conceptual interpretation beyond ensuring the well-posedness of the equations? It seems to control the "inertia" of attention; it would be interesting to discuss how this parameter relates to observable properties of human eye movements.
*   The simplified model V1 for static images is justified by its computational simplicity and empirically equivalent performance. A brief theoretical discussion on why minimizing the upper bound (Eq. 6) yields similar results to the original term would strengthen this section.
