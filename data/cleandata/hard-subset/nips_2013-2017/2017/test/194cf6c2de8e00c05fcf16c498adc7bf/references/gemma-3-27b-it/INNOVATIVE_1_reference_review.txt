This paper presents a novel, variational approach to modeling visual attention, framing it as a problem of finding trajectories driven by curiosity (detail) and brightness invariance. The authors cleverly connect these principles to the Euler-Lagrange equations, offering a potentially unifying framework for understanding saccadic eye movements and object tracking. This is a bold attempt to move beyond saliency map computation as the central problem, and instead model the underlying dynamics of attention itself. The connection to the least action principle and physics-inspired modeling is particularly intriguing.

The strengths of this work lie in its originality and conceptual elegance. The idea of deriving attention dynamics from fundamental principles, rather than explicitly engineering saliency maps, is a significant departure from the existing literature. The mathematical formulation, while potentially complex, provides a clear and concise framework. The results on both static images and videos, while not dramatically outperforming state-of-the-art, are competitive, particularly considering the model’s underlying simplicity and different approach. The use of simulated annealing for parameter estimation is a reasonable choice, and the analysis of the simplified form of the equations for static images is a good demonstration of mathematical rigor.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the model's theoretical foundations are strong, the experimental validation feels somewhat limited. The reliance on existing datasets (MIT, CAT2000, SFU) and comparison to established saliency models is standard, but doesn’t fully highlight the unique contribution of modeling *scanpaths* directly. The parameter sensitivity of the model, noted in the text, could be a significant issue. The description of the simulated annealing process is relatively brief, and it’s not entirely clear how robust the chosen parameters are. The paper mentions potential for tracking moving objects, but this aspect is not explored in significant detail. The claim of a "truly model of eye movements" seems overstated; it's a model *inspired* by eye movements, but doesn't incorporate many known neurophysiological constraints. While the authors acknowledge the limitation of not explicitly modeling feature extraction, this seems like a critical missing piece, and the reliance on brightness as the primary input feels restrictive. Further, the discussion of the numerical stability and the bounds on λ (parameter estimation) in the Appendix, while appreciated, feels a bit disconnected from the main narrative and could benefit from more explanation in the main body of the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **High Originality:** The variational approach to modeling attention is novel and represents a significant shift in perspective.
*   **Conceptual Elegance:** The connection to the least action principle and the unified treatment of curiosity and brightness invariance are compelling.
*   **Promising Results:** Despite being a fundamentally different approach, the model achieves competitive results on standard benchmarks.
*   **Potential for Future Work:** The framework offers a fertile ground for further research, particularly in integrating neurophysiological constraints and more sophisticated feature extraction methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Validation:** The experimental validation feels somewhat cursory and doesn’t fully exploit the potential of modeling scanpaths.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The reliance on simulated annealing and the potential for parameter sensitivity raise concerns about robustness.
*   **Simplifying Assumptions:** The reliance on brightness as the primary input and the lack of explicit feature extraction may limit the model’s generalizability.
*   **Overstated Claims:** The claim of a "truly model of eye movements" is likely an overstatement.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, I believe this paper presents a significant and potentially impactful contribution to the field of visual attention. The novelty of the approach and the conceptual elegance outweigh the limitations in validation. The work opens up a new avenue for research, shifting the focus from saliency map computation to modeling the underlying dynamics of attention. I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors address the reviewer comments regarding experimental validation, parameter sensitivity, and the limitations of the current model in future work. The willingness to overlook incomplete validations is justified given the boldness of the approach and the potential for significant future development.
