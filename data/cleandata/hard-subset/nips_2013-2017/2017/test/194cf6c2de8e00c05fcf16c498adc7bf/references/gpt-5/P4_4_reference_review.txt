Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a physics-inspired, variational framework for modeling human visual attention in dynamic scenes. The authors define a Lagrangian whose terms encode three driving principles: (i) boundedness within the retinal field via a border potential; (ii) a curiosity drive favoring regions with strong local gradients (and a temporally alternated peripheral, blurred gradient); and (iii) a brightness-invariance penalty derived from the optical flow constraint bt + bx ẋ = 0 to encourage fixation and tracking. The resulting Euler–Lagrange equations yield a second-order ODE (EYMOL) whose solution is interpreted as the scanpath. Saliency maps are obtained as a byproduct by counting visitation frequency over multiple simulated runs with randomized initial conditions. Parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) are tuned by simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a small subset of CAT2000-TRAIN; an additional grid search tunes blur and center bias. Experiments on static (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and dynamic data (SFU) report competitive saliency metrics against several baselines, and qualitative evidence of object tracking.

The work connects to bottom-up saliency models (e.g., Itti & Koch), optical-flow-based brightness constancy (Horn–Schunck), and saccade generation heuristics (winner-take-all with IOR). It differs by deriving a continuous-time dynamical system from a variational principle rather than specifying a saliency map and selection policy. Related broader literatures include optimal/active vision for gaze allocation (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler), dynamic neural field models of saccades, and physics/free-energy-inspired formulations of attention/active inference (Friston), which are not discussed in depth here.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: A single variational objective combines curiosity (local/peripheral detail) with brightness invariance, providing a coherent mechanism for both saccades and tracking.
- Dynamical formulation: Produces scanpaths directly from ODE integration, with saliency maps as a derived quantity, avoiding ad hoc winner-take-all and IOR rules.
- Simplicity and efficiency: The model is lightweight, can run in real time, and uses only grayscale inputs with simple pre-processing.
- Empirical results: Competitive saliency detection performance on standard image and video benchmarks, despite the model’s simplicity and minimal learned parameters.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness
- Inconsistency in kinetic term: Eq. (8) defines K(ẋ) = (1/m) ẋ^2, but subsequent derivations use d/dt ∂L/∂ẋ = m ẍ − … (Eq. (12)), which is only consistent with K = (m/2) ẋ^2. This discrepancy affects units and the subsequent bounds on λ; it needs correction.
- Appendix derivation issues: There are apparent typographical/notation errors (e.g., repeated b^2_{x1} terms where b^2_{x2} is expected; D simplification leading to λ < m/4 is not justified as written). The determinant condition and bound on λ hinge on these details; the current form is not reliable.
- Notation ambiguity: b_x and p_x are vectors, yet squared terms (b_x^2) are used without explicit norm notation. The gradient of squared gradient magnitude is computed as 2 Hessian·gradient, which is fine, but the notation should be precise.
- Exogenous oscillation: The curiosity term alternates local/peripheral gradients via cos^2(ωt)/sin^2(ωt). The physiological or computational basis for periodic alternation and the choice/sensitivity of ω are not justified or explored.
- Center bias and metric tuning: A multiplicative Gaussian center bias is applied to inputs and its parameter, along with post-hoc blur, is tuned to maximize AUC-Judd/NSS on training sets. This can inflate scores and complicates fair comparison, especially relative to models that do not explicitly tune center bias per dataset.
- Reproducibility: Key implementation details are missing (values for m, ω, λ, ηb, ηp; integrator settings; time step; initial condition distribution; integration horizon; number of steps; exact preprocessing). Tables with quantitative results are referenced but not included here, and confidence intervals/statistical tests are not detailed.

Clarity
- Numerous typos, incomplete references (missing years/venues), dangling footnotes, and overloaded notation reduce clarity.
- The presentation of the variational setup and the exact versus approximate brightness-invariance version would benefit from a clearer derivation, explicit assumptions (e.g., regularity of b), and a consolidated notation section.
- The experimental protocol (which data used for SA tuning per test set, cross-dataset generalization, and ablation details) is under-specified.

Originality
- The use of least-action/Euler–Lagrange to derive gaze dynamics is interesting but overlaps conceptually with existing optimal control/active vision and physics-inspired approaches. The curiosity term is closely related to gradient-magnitude saliency; the brightness constancy is standard. The novelty lies primarily in the particular combination and explicit variational derivation rather than new principles.
- The paper would benefit from a broader and more up-to-date related work discussion (e.g., deep saliency models like DeepGaze, SALICON, SAM/MLNet; dynamic field models; active inference), and a clear positioning.

Significance
- If technically sound and reproducible, a simple unsupervised dynamical model that is competitive on saliency benchmarks and yields scanpaths would be useful. However, the current experimental comparisons omit several state-of-the-art deep baselines and lack stronger analyses (ablations, sensitivity, scanpath metrics, tracking evaluation), making it difficult to assess impact.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Correct Eq. (8) and propagate the fix through the EL derivation; re-derive the determinant D and the bound on λ with clear assumptions. Define norms explicitly and fix typographical errors.
- Report full parameter settings (m, ω, λ, ηb, ηp), integrator details, and initial condition distributions. Provide code or sufficient pseudo-code for reproduction.
- Provide ablations: effect of curiosity vs brightness invariance; with/without center bias; effect of periodic alternation (ω). Include sensitivity analyses.
- Compare against stronger contemporary baselines (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON, MLNet/SAM) on MIT300/CAT2000; report all standard metrics with confidence intervals.
- Evaluate scanpath realism (e.g., MultiMatch, saccade amplitude/duration distributions) and tracking quantitatively on dynamic datasets.
- Clarify the training/validation protocol per dataset to avoid overfitting bias (especially for center bias/blur tuning).
- Expand the related work to include optimal gaze allocation and physics/free-energy-based accounts of attention/saccades.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, unified dynamical formulation for attention; intuitive principles.
- Lightweight, unsupervised model with promising empirical performance.
- Produces scanpaths directly, potentially linking to biological eye movement data.

Arguments against acceptance
- Technical inconsistencies in the derivations and parameter bounds.
- Insufficient experimental rigor (missing details, limited baselines, reliance on tuned center bias).
- Clarity and referencing issues impede verification and reproduction.
- Limited novelty relative to existing active vision/dynamical models without deeper theoretical or empirical advances.

Overall, while the idea is appealing and results are suggestive, the paper requires significant technical corrections, clearer exposition, and stronger experimental validation to meet NeurIPS standards.
