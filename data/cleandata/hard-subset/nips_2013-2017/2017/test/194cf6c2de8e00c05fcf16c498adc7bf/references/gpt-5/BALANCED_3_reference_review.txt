Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a dynamical, variational model of overt visual attention. A scanpath x(t) is generated as the trajectory of a particle in a generalized potential defined on the brightness field b(t, x). The action combines: (i) a bounding “retina” potential V(x) to keep the trajectory within the image; (ii) a “curiosity” drive C(t, x) favoring large spatial gradients of both the raw image and a blurred peripheral channel, alternated over time via cos2/sin2; and (iii) a brightness invariance term B(t, x, ẋ) encouraging trajectories that satisfy bt + bx ẋ ≈ 0 (the classic optical-flow constancy), argued to promote fixation on edges in static images and motion tracking in videos. The Euler–Lagrange equations yield second-order ODEs for x(t) (EYMOL), which are numerically integrated from randomized initial conditions. Saliency maps are obtained as visit-count histograms over many simulated runs. Parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) are tuned by simulated annealing on a small subset of CAT2000 training images using NSS; additional blur/center-bias postprocessing is grid-searched to optimize AUC-Judd/NSS. The model is evaluated on MIT1003/CAT2000-TRAIN (including an approximate vs exact brightness-invariance variant), and on MIT300/CAT2000-TEST via the MIT Benchmark, and on SFU videos, claiming competitive performance versus selected baselines (Itti-Koch, AIM, Judd, BMS, and a CNN/LSTM model).

This work bridges classical bottom-up saliency models (Itti & Koch; AIM; BMS) with dynamical scanpath generation, reminiscent of biologically inspired saccade models (e.g., Le Meur & Liu) and optimal/active vision perspectives (e.g., information maximization/surprise; optical flow). The proposed least-action formulation is a novel lens to unify “curiosity” and brightness invariance within one principled ODE.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: A compact variational principle yields a single set of motion equations that can produce saccade-like jumps and tracking behavior; saliency maps arise as a byproduct of simulated scanpaths.
- Analytical clarity (in spirit): The derivation highlights the role of brightness gradients, peripheral modulation, and the optical-flow constancy constraint; it provides explicit conditions for parameter stability (e.g., λ < m/4 under normalization).
- Simplicity and speed: The model uses only grayscale gradients and standard ODE solvers; real-time scanpath generation seems feasible (modulo the multiple-run aggregation for maps).
- Empirical reach: Evaluates on standard static datasets (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and a dynamic dataset (SFU), reporting competitive scores against diverse baselines.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Mathematical inconsistency: The kinetic energy is written as K(ẋ) = (1/m)‖ẋ‖2 (Eq. 8), yet subsequent derivations use d/dt ∂L/∂ẋ = m ẍ (Eq. 12), which is only consistent if K = (m/2)‖ẋ‖2. This is more than a typo; it affects units and the EL equations. Please correct and re-derive affected expressions.
- Ad hoc components: The curiosity alternation C(t, x) via cos2(ωt)/sin2(ωt) and the harmonic “retina” potential are introduced without empirical or theoretical justification (e.g., choice of ω, its timescale relative to fixation/saccade rates). Ablations comparing C with a simple convex combination (no alternation) are necessary to substantiate the claim that alternation “avoids trapping.”
- Postprocessing and metric tuning: Center bias and blur are tuned by grid search specifically to maximize AUC-Judd/NSS. This risks metric-gaming and obscures the contribution of the dynamical model. Shuffled AUC (sAUC) and Information Gain should be reported and emphasized; center bias artificially boosts AUC-Judd/NSS.
- Evaluation design: Parameters are tuned on a subset of CAT2000-TRAIN; it is unclear whether the same parameters are used across datasets (MIT300, SFU) or whether dataset-specific tuning occurs (the text suggests tuning blur/center on SFU itself). Avoid tuning on the test set; if tuning on SFU, use a train/val split or leave-one-video-out.
- Limited baselines and missing metrics: The comparisons omit several strong modern saliency models (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON/MLNet variants, SALICON-trained deep models), and do not report widely used metrics such as sAUC, IG, CC, KL, SIM across all datasets. Claiming “best in half the cases” is hard to assess without comprehensive tables.
- Scanpath validation: The paper focuses on saliency maps; no quantitative comparison of generated scanpaths to human scanpaths (e.g., MultiMatch, string edit distance, Jarodzka metrics, direction/amplitude distributions). This misses an opportunity to validate the core dynamical contribution.
- Reproducibility: Critical details are missing (values for m, k, ω, time step, ODE solver, initial-condition distribution, run time per image/video, exact preprocessing). Code availability is not stated.

Clarity
- The manuscript has numerous typos, equation mislabels, and reference inconsistencies, making it difficult to follow. Some equations (e.g., determinant D derivation) include typographical errors (repeated indices).
- The role and scaling of parameters (ηb, ηp, λ, m, k, ω) and their units are not explained. Provide intuition and ranges; include sensitivity analyses.

Originality
- The least-action framing and explicit EL derivation for attention dynamics are novel and intellectually appealing. However, the constituent terms (gradient-based saliency, optical flow brightness constancy, center bias) are standard; the alternation in C is new but under-motivated.

Significance
- If the model genuinely achieves competitive performance with minimal features and yields realistic scanpaths/tracking, it could be a lightweight alternative to feature-heavy or deep models and an interpretable framework. However, current evidence is insufficient: performance gains may stem from center bias/blur tuning, and scanpath realism is not evaluated.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, unified dynamical formulation with clear biological inspiration.
- Simple, interpretable model that plausibly generates saccades and tracking.
- Potentially competitive performance without heavy learning.

Cons for acceptance
- Mathematical inconsistency in the kinetic term and several derivational typos.
- Insufficient, possibly confounded empirical validation (metric-specific tuning, lack of sAUC/IG, limited baselines, unclear tuning protocol on videos).
- No scanpath-level evaluation.
- Clarity and completeness issues hinder reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify/correct the kinetic energy term and re-derive the EL equations accordingly.
- How are m, k, and ω chosen? Are they tuned per dataset? What is the sensitivity to ω?
- Are the same (ηb, ηp, λ) used across MIT300/CAT2000/SFU? If not, what is the tuning protocol to avoid test leakage?
- What ODE solver, step size, and horizon do you use? What fraction of time is spent per trajectory? How many trajectories are needed for stable saliency estimates?
- Can you report sAUC and IG, and ablations without added center bias/blur?

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the derivations; include a clean, dimensionally consistent formulation.
- Provide comprehensive evaluations: add sAUC/IG/CC/KL/SIM; include stronger baselines; perform ablations (no center bias; no blur; no alternation; remove B term; vary λ).
- Add scanpath metrics and qualitative/quantitative comparisons to human scanpaths; consider adding stochasticity (e.g., Langevin noise) rather than only random initial conditions.
- Justify and analyze the alternation frequency ω; connect to known oculomotor dynamics or show performance benefits.
- Release code and parameter settings for reproducibility.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The core idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the mathematical inconsistency, evaluation confounds, and missing scanpath validation prevent acceptance at this time. With corrected derivations, stronger and cleaner experiments (especially sAUC/IG and scanpath metrics), and clearer presentation, this work could become a valuable contribution. Confidence: medium.
