- Paper proposes a physics-inspired variational Lagrangian whose Euler–Lagrange stationary path produces 2D focus-of-attention trajectories and saliency maps from visit counts.  
- Model components: boundary potential, curiosity term (high-gradient attraction with cos^2/sin^2 alternation), and brightness-invariance term for fixation/tracking via optical-flow constraint.  
- Evaluated on MIT1003/300, CAT2000, SFU video with parameters tuned by simulated annealing and claims competitive scores versus some classic and recent methods.  

Strengths:
- Conceptual unification: single variational framework produces saccadic-like exploration and fixation/tracking behavior.  
- Minimal, training-light features: uses only grayscale gradients and a blurred pyramid (no learned features).  
- Generality: same dynamical system handles static images and videos; saliency emerges from scanpaths.  
- Empirical indication that approximate and exact brightness-invariance variants behave similarly on static images.  

Technical weaknesses:
- Critical kinetic-energy inconsistency: paper defines K = (1/m)ẋ^2 but uses EL consistent with K = (m/2)∥ẋ∥^2, invalidating equations and parameter interpretation.  
- Derivation errors: unjustified normalization assumptions for gradient bounds, incorrect determinant derivations, and notational mistakes.  
- Missing dynamics analysis: system appears undamped, with no stability/existence/uniqueness analysis and risk of oscillatory trajectories.  
- Hyperparameters under-specified: ω, m, k, integrator/time step, and annealing domains are not fully reported or justified.  
- Post-hoc tuning/center bias: preprocessing center Gaussian, central init conditions, and grid-search blur/center tuning likely inflate metrics and contradict “byproduct” saliency claim.  

Clarity and reproducibility:
- Manuscript contains typos, incomplete references, inconsistent equations, and lacks necessary implementation details to reproduce results.  

Originality:
- Lagrangian/least-action framing is novel in this context, but constituent ideas (gradient-based interest, peripheral blur, optical-flow constraint) are standard and related dynamical scanpath work is only partially discussed.  

Significance and empirical limitations:
- Conceptually promising but empirical impact limited by center bias reliance, absence of ablations, lack of scanpath-level metrics, incomplete comparisons to strong modern deep models, and small video dataset.  

Suggested improvements:
- Correct and fully re-derive the Lagrangian/EL equations, add damping/stability analysis, and justify parameter choices (m, k, ω).  
- Provide full implementation details (integrator, timestep, initial condition distributions) and release code.  
- Run ablations for each term (curiosity, brightness-invariance, alternation), sensitivity analyses over ω and λ, and report scanpath/tracking metrics.  
- Evaluate with center-bias–robust metrics (sAUC, IG), include pure center baseline, and ensure fair tuning across baselines including modern deep saliency models.  

Pros (concise):
- Elegant, unified, general framework that bridges fixation and tracking with simple features and moderate performance.  

Cons (concise):
- Fundamental derivational errors, heavy center-bias/post-processing, limited/outdated baselines, missing ablations, and poor reproducibility.  

Recommendation:
- Reject in current form; encourage resubmission after correcting derivations, improving clarity, and strengthening bias-controlled empirical evaluation.
