- Proposes a variational, physics-inspired framework modeling eye movements as particles in a potential field defined by boundedness, curiosity (gradient magnitude), and brightness invariance, yielding Euler–Lagrange motion equations.  
- Evaluated on standard saliency datasets (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, SFU video) with competitive performance using NSS and AUC-Judd metrics.

- Mathematical derivation is sound and implementation is efficient, but the saliency map claim is misleading because maps are produced by many runs (199 images, 100 videos), which is computationally intensive.  
- The approximation in Eq. (6) (upper bound for brightness invariance) is unjustified and its impact on performance is not evaluated.  
- The paper fails to discuss limitations or failure cases of the model.

- The connection between the "curiosity" principle and established gradient-based saliency models (e.g., Itti et al.) is insufficiently contrasted, overstating novelty.  
- The saliency-map generation process (multiple runs and visit counting) is buried and should be more prominently explained.

- The variational/unified approach is conceptually interesting, but the "curiosity" principle closely resembles existing gradient-based models.  
- The brightness invariance constraint is related to optical flow and previous attention models, reducing perceived novelty.  
- The paper does not adequately differentiate its contributions from prior work like Itti et al. and Bruce & Tsotsos.

- Real-time implementation and unified treatment of static and dynamic scenes are significant advantages.  
- Ability to generate scanpaths (not just saliency maps) is an important contribution.  
- Significance is limited by modest performance gains, lack of direct human-scanpath comparisons, and no clear theoretical advantage over simpler models.

- Strengths: principled variational foundation, unified static/dynamic framework, efficient real-time implementation, clear technical presentation.  
- Weaknesses: overstated novelty, misleading description of saliency generation, insufficient related-work comparison, lack of limitations discussion, modest empirical gains.

- Recommendation: weak acceptance contingent on revisions.  
- Required revisions: clarify novelty vs. gradient/optical-flow models; justify and compare the Eq.(6) approximation with the exact form; explicitly state and explain that saliency maps require multiple runs and are not mere byproducts; add a limitations/failure-cases section; include direct comparisons with human scanpaths.
