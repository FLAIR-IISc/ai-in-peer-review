### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel, principled model for visual attention, named EYMOL (EYe MOvement Laws), grounded in a variational framework analogous to the principle of least action in physics. The central idea is that the scanpath of the eye's focus of attention can be modeled as the trajectory of a particle moving according to Euler-Lagrange equations derived from a generalized action. The authors propose that this motion is governed by three fundamental principles: 1) a boundedness principle, which confines the trajectory within the visual field using a potential well; 2) a curiosity-driven principle, which attracts attention to regions with high detail (i.e., high image gradient magnitude); and 3) a brightness invariance principle, which encourages fixation on static objects and tracking of moving objects by penalizing changes in brightness at the point of focus. This latter principle is borrowed from the classic optical flow constraint.

Unlike most models in the literature, such as the classic work of Itti et al. [10] or recent deep learning approaches [18, 24], which directly compute a static saliency map, this model's primary output is a dynamic scanpath. The saliency map is then generated as a byproduct by aggregating the locations visited by the simulated scanpaths over time. The model is tested on standard image saliency benchmarks (MIT300, CAT2000) and a video saliency benchmark (SFU), where it demonstrates performance that is competitive with, and in some cases superior to, several state-of-the-art methods. The work positions itself as a departure from both complex, feature-engineered systems and black-box learning models, offering instead an elegant, generative model derived from first principles.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**
The paper is technically sound. The mathematical formulation, deriving the equations of motion from a Lagrangian via the principle of least action, is rigorous and clearly detailed in the appendix. The derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations and the analysis of the well-posedness conditions (e.g., the constraint on the parameter λ) are correct. The authors' claims about the model's performance are well-supported by extensive quantitative experiments on multiple standard benchmarks. The comparison between the full brightness invariance model (V2) and its approximation (V1) for static images is a thoughtful piece of analysis that validates the use of the simpler, more efficient version. The work represents a complete contribution, presenting a full theoretical model alongside empirical validation.

**Clarity:**
The paper is, for the most part, clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively situates the work within the existing literature and motivates the novel approach. The core principles of the model are explained intuitively before the mathematical formulation is presented. The separation of the detailed derivations into the appendix is appropriate and improves the readability of the main text. However, the clarity could be improved with respect to reproducibility. While the core equations are provided, crucial implementation details are missing. Specifically, the final values of the parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) found through simulated annealing are not reported. The values of fixed model parameters such as mass `m`, the harmonic oscillator constant `k`, and the curiosity oscillation frequency `ω` are also not stated. Furthermore, the parameters for pre-processing (center-bias blob) and post-processing (saliency map blur), which are known to significantly affect benchmark scores, are not provided. This lack of detail would make it difficult for another researcher to reproduce the results exactly.

**Originality:**
The originality of this work is its primary strength. The application of a variational, least-action framework to model visual scanpaths is a fundamentally new approach in the field of visual attention. While variational methods are used elsewhere in computer vision, their specific application to generate dynamic attentional trajectories based on principles of curiosity and brightness constancy is highly novel. The model provides a unified account of two key attentional behaviors: saccadic exploration (driven by the curiosity term) and fixation/smooth pursuit (driven by the brightness invariance term). Most existing models treat these as separate mechanisms or only model the static allocation of attention. This unified, dynamic perspective is a significant conceptual contribution that clearly distinguishes the paper from prior work.

**Significance:**
The significance of this paper is substantial. It presents a powerful alternative to the dominant paradigms of hand-engineered feature maps and end-to-end deep learning. The fact that a simple, elegant model with only a few free parameters can achieve state-of-the-art performance on challenging benchmarks is impressive and suggests that underlying principles, rather than complex feature learning alone, may govern much of early visual attention. The model is generative and operates in real-time, making it potentially useful for applications in robotics, active vision, and foveated rendering. The work is likely to inspire new research exploring principled, physics-inspired models of attention and other cognitive processes.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **High Novelty:** The paper introduces a genuinely new and elegant paradigm for modeling visual attention based on the principle of least action.
2.  **Conceptual Unification:** It provides a single, unified framework that can account for both exploratory saccades and fixation/tracking behaviors, a significant advance over existing models.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves highly competitive, and in some cases superior, performance compared to established and recent state-of-the-art methods on both image and video saliency datasets.
4.  **Inspirational Potential:** The principled, generative nature of the model is likely to open up new avenues of research in computational neuroscience and computer vision.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Reproducibility Issues:** Key parameters and implementation details are omitted, making it difficult to verify and build upon the work directly.
2.  **Model Simplicity as a Limitation:** The model relies exclusively on grayscale brightness gradients. It cannot capture attention driven by color, complex textures, or semantic information, which are known to be important factors in human vision. While the authors frame the work as a model of "early" vision, this limitation is significant and is not discussed in detail.
3.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The evaluation is entirely quantitative. The paper would be strengthened by a qualitative analysis showing examples of the generated scanpaths on different stimuli, especially in comparison to human scanpaths, to provide more intuitive insight into the model's behavior and its failure modes.
