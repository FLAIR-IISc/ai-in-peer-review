Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a dynamical model of visual attention (EYMOL) derived from a variational/least-action principle. A Lagrangian L = K − U is specified over the scanpath x(t) in image coordinates, with three terms in U: (i) a boundary potential that confines trajectories within the image; (ii) a curiosity term that attracts the focus toward spatial detail, implemented as the squared gradient magnitude of the brightness b and a peripheral/blurred version p that alternate over time via cos²(ωt)/sin²(ωt); and (iii) a brightness-invariance term B(t, x, ẋ) = (b_t + b_x ẋ)² that encourages fixation and, for videos, motion tracking. The Euler–Lagrange equations yield second-order ODEs for attention dynamics. Two variants are tested: V1 uses an upper bound approximation to B (notably, for static images it reduces to a velocity penalty weighted by |∇b|²), while V2 uses the exact term.

Parameters ηb, ηp, λ are selected by simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a 100-image subset of CAT2000-TRAIN; saliency maps are then obtained as visit-frequency maps over many stochastic runs with near-center initializations. The model is evaluated on MIT1003/CAT2000 (with ground-truth fixations), MIT300 (benchmark server), and the SFU video dataset, and compared against classical saliency methods (Itti-Koch, AIM, BMS) and some learning-based models (Judd et al., an LSTM model). The paper argues that EYMOL is competitive despite its simplicity and that saliency emerges as a byproduct of scanpath dynamics.

Relative to prior work, the model departs from the common “global saliency map + WTA scanpath” pipeline (e.g., Itti & Koch; Bruce & Tsotsos; BMS), and from recent CNN-based methods (e.g., Judd et al.; Vig et al.; Cornia et al.), by directly modeling attention as a physical system. The brightness-invariance term draws from the classical optical flow constraint (Horn–Schunck). Le Meur & Liu’s saccadic model is a closer precedent for dynamical scanpaths. The paper’s novelty is the unifying variational formulation and the explicit coupling of curiosity and invariance in a single Lagrangian.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: A clear, principled dynamical framework that ties together saccades, fixation, and tracking via a single Lagrangian, with interpretable terms.
- Analytical development: Derivation of the Euler–Lagrange equations and stability conditions (e.g., λ < m/4 under stated normalization) provides a concrete, testable model rather than a heuristic pipeline.
- Efficiency and byproduct saliency: Scanpaths are generated in real time via ODE integration, and saliency maps emerge from visitation counts without training a saliency predictor.
- Empirical scope: Evaluations on standard image datasets (MIT1003/MIT300/CAT2000) and a video dataset (SFU) demonstrate applicability to static and dynamic scenes.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Technical soundness
- Kinetic energy inconsistency: Eq. (8) sets K(ẋ) = (1/m) ẋ², but subsequent Euler–Lagrange equations use m ẍ as if K = (m/2) ẋ². This should be corrected and reconciled throughout.
- Notational/derivation ambiguities: The derivation of Bx in Eq. (16) omits the square in the first line and then applies chain rule; definitions of bx/px (vector) and “square” (norm vs component-wise) are unclear; Cx is written using bx · bxx but the shapes (Hessian times gradient) are not properly defined. A careful, dimensionally consistent tensor notation would improve rigor.
- Approximated B for static images: The bound used in V1 changes the geometry of the constraint from penalizing directional motion along ∇b to penalizing speed weighted by |∇b|². An ablation should quantify how this affects scanpaths and saliency, beyond “statistically equivalent” scores, and explain why equivalence holds.
- Parameterization gaps: The paper optimizes ηb, ηp, λ, but other critical parameters (m, k, ω, numerical step size, noise level in initial conditions) are not specified or studied. The stability condition links λ and m; the chosen m is not reported.
- Simulated annealing details: The acceptance probability P(·), neighborhood definition, and domain D are unspecified; this precludes reproducibility and makes it hard to assess overfitting risk.

Experimental design and fairness
- Center bias and blur tuning: The model multiplies the input by a Gaussian center bias and then performs a separate grid search on output blur and center parameters to maximize AUC-Judd and NSS on training data. As center bias is a strong driver of these metrics, ablations without these components are needed to isolate the contribution of the proposed dynamics.
- Initial condition bias: All runs are initialized near the center with small velocity, further injecting center bias. Please report results with diverse initializations and quantify sensitivity.
- Comparisons vs state-of-the-art: The paper compares to classical baselines and a single deep model. On MIT300 at the time, multiple CNN-based methods (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON variants) dominated; please include comparisons to contemporary SOTA and clarify training data allowances to ensure fairness.
- Missing tables/metrics: The manuscript refers to Tables 2–4 but they are not included here. For a complete assessment, report all metrics (AUC-Judd, AUC-Borji, NSS, CC, sAUC, KL, SIM) with standard deviations where applicable and specify whether center bias is applied to baselines.
- Video evaluation: Claims of object tracking are qualitative; consider adding tracking metrics or scanpath-temporal measures, and specify whether V2 (exact B) is used for videos.

Clarity
- The prose contains grammatical errors and formatting issues (e.g., footnotes inline, incomplete references), and several mathematical expressions are hard to parse. A thorough edit would aid readability. Define all symbols on first use, disambiguate vector/matrix operations, and provide an algorithm box with all hyperparameters.

Originality
- Moderate. The curiosity term and optical-flow constraint are standard, but their integration into a least-action dynamical system for attention is novel and interesting.

Significance
- Potentially meaningful if the model truly matches strong baselines without heavy biases and if the scanpath perspective yields insights not captured by static saliency maps. Current evidence is limited by the evaluation and ablation gaps noted above.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, interpretable dynamical formulation of attention.
- Unifies fixation, saccades, and tracking in one model.
- Competitive preliminary results claimed across image and video datasets.
- Low computational cost; saliency as a byproduct.

Cons for acceptance
- Mathematical inconsistencies and ambiguous notation (notably K, derivations of gradients/Hessians).
- Heavy reliance on center bias and blur tuning; lack of ablations and sensitivity analyses.
- Incomplete experimental details (parameter values, SA procedure) and missing comparisons to stronger contemporary baselines.
- Reproducibility concerns (no code link, incomplete algorithmic specifications).

Questions for authors
- Please fix and clarify the kinetic energy term and re-derive the EL equations accordingly.
- Specify m, k, ω, integration scheme/step, number of steps, noise level, and initialization distribution.
- Provide ablations: without center bias; without the curiosity term; without the brightness-invariance term; without time alternation (cos²/sin²); and sensitivity to ω and λ.
- Clarify P(·), neighborhood definition, and D in simulated annealing; report final parameter values.
- For MIT300, list exact leaderboard scores and contemporary baselines; state whether any training was done on MIT1003 or other fixation datasets.
- For videos, specify whether V1 or V2 was used and add quantitative tracking or scanpath metrics.

Overall assessment
The paper introduces a promising and interpretable variational framework for attention dynamics, which is a fresh angle relative to saliency-map-centric approaches. However, the current manuscript has technical inconsistencies, lacks crucial experimental details and ablations, and relies on strong center-bias tuning, making it difficult to assess the true contribution. Strengthening the mathematical clarity and experimental rigor would substantially improve the work. Based on the present version, the contribution does not yet meet the robustness and clarity expected for NIPS.
