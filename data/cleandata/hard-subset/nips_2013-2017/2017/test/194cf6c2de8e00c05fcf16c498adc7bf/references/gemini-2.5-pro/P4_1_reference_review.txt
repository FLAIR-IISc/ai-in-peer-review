This paper introduces a novel, principled model of visual attention, named EYMOL, that generates scanpaths for both static and dynamic scenes. The central idea is to frame the problem within a variational framework, where the trajectory of the focus of attention is determined by the principle of least action. This approach marks a significant departure from the dominant paradigm in visual attention modeling, which typically involves first computing a centralized saliency map and then simulating a scanpath using a winner-take-all mechanism. The authors propose that the scanpath dynamics are governed by three core principles encoded in a Lagrangian: 1) a bounding potential to confine the trajectory to the image plane, 2) a "curiosity" term that attracts attention to regions with high detail (image gradient), and 3) a "brightness invariance" term, identical to the classic optical flow constraint, which encourages fixation on static objects and tracking of moving ones. The scanpath is found by solving the Euler-Lagrange equations derived from this Lagrangian. A saliency map is then computed as a byproduct of the model by aggregating the locations visited over multiple simulated scanpath trajectories. This approach is contrasted with previous work, such as the foundational model of Itti et al. [10], information-theoretic models [16], and recent deep learning methods [18, 26], by emphasizing its "scanpath-first" and principled, physics-inspired formulation over a "saliency-map-first" or purely data-driven one.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality:** The primary strength of this paper is its originality. Formulating visual attention as a variational problem derived from the principle of least action is a highly novel and elegant approach in this field. It provides a new theoretical lens through which to view attention. The unification of saccadic movements (driven by curiosity) and fixation/tracking (driven by brightness invariance) within a single, coherent mathematical framework is a particularly insightful contribution.
*   **Quality (Technical Soundness):** The proposed model is technically sound. The derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations from the defined Lagrangian is carried out rigorously in the appendix. The model is a complete piece of work, providing a full description from principles to the final differential equations of motion. The claims of performance are well-supported by experiments on multiple standard benchmarks for both images (MIT300, CAT2000) and video (SFU), where the model demonstrates competitive, and in some cases superior, performance against a representative set of existing methods.
*   **Significance:** The paper offers a valuable contribution by presenting an interpretable, "white-box" model for a complex cognitive process. In a field increasingly dominated by opaque deep learning models, such principled approaches are important for advancing fundamental understanding. The model's real-time capability and strong performance suggest it could be practically useful in applications like robotics. Furthermore, the theoretical framework is extensible and could inspire future work incorporating additional principles (e.g., top-down biases) within the same variational structure.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The core principles are stated clearly, the mathematical model is developed systematically, and the experimental setup is described adequately. Separating the main ideas from the detailed derivations in the appendix enhances readability.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Model Justification and Interpretation:** While the overall framework is compelling, the motivation for some specific modeling choices could be strengthened. For instance, the curiosity term `C(t, x)` uses a sinusoidal modulation (`cos^2(ωt)`, `sin^2(ωt)`) to alternate between local and peripheral information. The paper states this is to avoid "trapping into regions with too many details," but the biological or cognitive justification for this specific periodic switching mechanism is not provided. Furthermore, the physical interpretation of model parameters like the "mass" `m` is not discussed; clarifying whether it represents a concept like attentional inertia would deepen the model's connection to the phenomenon it describes.
*   **Scope of the Model:** The model is presented as purely bottom-up and "completely data driven." While this is a valid simplification for modeling early vision (as argued in the introduction), it is a significant limitation. The paper acknowledges that top-down processes play a crucial role in attention, but does not address how they might be incorporated. This makes the model less plausible for explaining human scanpaths beyond the first few fixations.
*   **Evaluation:** The model's primary output is a scanpath, yet the quantitative evaluation relies exclusively on static saliency maps aggregated over time. A core claim of the paper is that it offers a model of *eye movements*, but there is no direct comparison of the model's scanpath dynamics (e.g., saccade lengths, fixation durations, transition probabilities) with human scanpath data. While acknowledged as future work, the absence of this analysis in the current paper weakens the validation of its central claim.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Presents a highly novel and elegant theoretical framework for visual attention based on the principle of least action.
2.  Unifies saccadic movements and fixation/tracking within a single, coherent set of equations.
3.  Offers a principled, interpretable "white-box" model that is competitive with state-of-the-art methods.
4.  The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The model is purely bottom-up, limiting its psychological plausibility for sustained viewing.
2.  The evaluation focuses on aggregate saliency maps, failing to directly validate the dynamics of the generated scanpaths, which is the model's primary output.
3.  The justification for some specific functional forms and physical parameters in the model is underdeveloped.

In summary, this paper presents a creative and thought-provoking new direction for modeling visual attention. Its primary contribution lies in its novel theoretical framework, which is both elegant and effective. While there are limitations regarding the model's scope and the depth of its evaluation, its originality and significance make it a strong candidate for acceptance.
