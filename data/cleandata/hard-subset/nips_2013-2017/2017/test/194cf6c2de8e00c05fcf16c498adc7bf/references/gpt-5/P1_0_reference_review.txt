Summary
The paper proposes a mechanistic, physics-inspired model of visual attention for dynamic scenes. The authors posit three principles: (i) boundedness (to keep the “particle” within the retinal field), (ii) curiosity (attraction to regions with high spatial detail, modeled via brightness gradient magnitude and a blurred peripheral term with time modulation), and (iii) brightness invariance (a soft constraint bt + bx·ẋ ≈ 0, akin to the optical flow constant-brightness assumption, to induce fixation/tracking). These are embedded in a variational framework: the scanpath is the trajectory x(t) that makes an action integral stationary, with a Lagrangian composed of kinetic minus a generalized potential including the above terms. The resulting Euler–Lagrange equations yield second-order ODEs for attention dynamics (EYMOL). Saliency maps are obtained as a byproduct by counting visit frequencies across multiple simulated trajectories. Parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) are tuned via simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a subset of CAT2000. The model is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video; the authors report competitive performance against several baselines and qualitative tracking behavior in videos.

Relation to prior work: The model connects to classic saliency (Itti & Koch), surprise/information-theoretic approaches (Bruce & Tsotsos; Itti & Baldi), scanpath modeling (Le Meur & Liu), and optical flow (Horn–Schunck). The variational least-action derivation and integration of a velocity-dependent brightness-invariance term provide a distinct angle relative to WTA and heuristic scanpath simulators. Recent deep saliency models (e.g., DeepGaze, SALICON, SAM) are acknowledged but not thoroughly compared.

Strengths
- A principled, interpretable formulation unifying saccades, fixation, and tracking within one variational framework. The modeling choices are grounded in vision science (edges and motion invariance).
- Derivation of explicit Euler–Lagrange dynamics and discussion of well-posedness (conditioning on λ) are valuable and novel for this topic.
- Saliency as a byproduct of simulated scanpaths is conceptually appealing and closer to behavior than static saliency maps.
- Empirical evaluation on images and videos suggests the approach is competitive despite simplicity, and it can run in real time.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Mathematical details contain inconsistencies/typos that undermine confidence. Examples: the kinetic energy K(ẋ)=1/m·||ẋ||^2 (unusual; typically K=(m/2)||ẋ||^2). The determinant simplification D and the condition λ < m/4 rely on assumptions about gradient magnitudes that are not stated; b∈[0,1] does not imply bounds on ||bx||. Please clarify the normalization and provide a correct, conservative bound on λ.
- The cosine/sine modulation frequency ω is introduced but not specified, justified, or analyzed. Sensitivity to ω and mass m, stiffness k is not shown.
- Simulated annealing procedure lacks essential details (acceptance function P, neighborhood definition, iteration budget, α). Reproducibility is limited.
- The evaluation mixes several sources of center bias: multiplying the input by a central Gaussian and a post-hoc grid-search over blur and center parameters to maximize AUC-Judd/NSS. This can inflate metrics sensitive to center bias; shuffled AUC (sAUC) and reporting of a center-bias baseline are necessary controls.
- No ablations isolating the contribution of each term (removing curiosity, brightness invariance, border potential) or the effect of the cos/sin alternation are reported. Similarly, the V1/V2 comparison is said to be statistically equivalent, but statistical tests, variances, and exact scores are missing in the text.
- Scanpath-level validation against human sequences (e.g., MultiMatch, string-edit distances, temporal correlation) is absent; this is central to the claim of modeling eye movements.

Clarity
- The paper contains grammatical errors and missing details (values or ranges for ω, m, k; numerical integrator step sizes; initialization noise; run-time). Footnotes are referenced but not provided; some equations have typographical errors (e.g., repeated indices in Eq. 42). The pseudo-code is incomplete. Reference entries are incomplete in places.
- Tables are referenced but not visible here; ensure all reported metrics (AUC-Judd, sAUC, NSS, CC, SIM, KL) and evaluation protocols are clearly described with train/test splits and no leakage.

Originality
- The least-action derivation with a velocity-dependent “potential” for brightness invariance is novel in this context, and the unification of saccades, fixation, and tracking within ODEs is interesting. However, there is a body of related work on dynamic attention/scanpath modeling (e.g., dynamic WTA circuits, optimal control/information gain, stochastic dynamical systems) that deserves deeper comparison. The paper would benefit from positioning relative to free-energy/active inference frameworks and to modern deep saliency methods.

Significance
- If robust, an interpretable, real-time model that yields competitive saliency without heavy learning is valuable. The significance would be strengthened by: (i) stronger and broader baselines (DeepGaze II, SALICON, MLNet/SAM, GBVS), (ii) center-bias–controlled metrics (sAUC), (iii) scanpath-level validation, and (iv) ablations and sensitivity analyses.

Questions for the authors
- What are the exact values/ranges used for ω, m, k, and how sensitive are results to them?
- Please clarify the gradient normalization and the derivation of the λ bound. Can you provide a rigorous bound or adaptive scheme ensuring D>0?
- What integrator, step size, and stopping criteria are used? How many steps correspond to “1 second”?
- How much do results degrade if the post-hoc center bias/blur tuning is removed? What is the performance of a center-bias-only baseline?
- Can you report sAUC and additional metrics (CC, SIM, KL) on all datasets?

Suggestions
- Add ablations (remove each term; switch off cos/sin alternation; exact vs approximate brightness invariance) and parameter sensitivity plots.
- Include sAUC and a center-bias baseline; avoid optimizing post-hoc blur/center on the test distribution.
- Expand comparisons to strong deep baselines and to a dynamical scanpath model (e.g., Le Meur & Liu).
- Provide scanpath metrics and qualitative examples aligning trajectories with human fixations.
- Clean up derivations, fix typos, and provide complete algorithmic details and, ideally, code.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually novel, interpretable variational formulation of attention dynamics.
- Unifies saccades, fixation, and tracking; saliency emerges as a byproduct.
- Competitive performance reported on multiple datasets with a lightweight model.

Cons against acceptance
- Mathematical and experimental details are insufficient/unclear; some derivations appear shaky.
- Evaluation does not control adequately for center bias; limited ablations and sensitivity analysis.
- Comparisons to modern strong baselines are incomplete; lack of scanpath-level validation.
- Reproducibility concerns (simulated annealing details, parameter choices).

Overall assessment
Borderline reject. The core idea is appealing and potentially impactful, but the current presentation and evaluation leave too many open questions. With clearer theory, stronger and more controlled experiments (including sAUC and scanpath metrics), and thorough ablations, this could be a solid contribution.
