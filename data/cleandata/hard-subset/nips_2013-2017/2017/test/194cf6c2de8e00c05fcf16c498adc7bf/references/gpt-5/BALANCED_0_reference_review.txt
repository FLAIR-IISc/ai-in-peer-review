Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a physics-inspired, variational formulation of bottom-up visual attention. The authors define an action functional over eye trajectories, with kinetic energy and a generalized potential that encodes: (i) boundedness within the retinal/image domain via a border penalty; (ii) “curiosity” attraction to detailed regions, modeled by the squared brightness gradient and its blurred counterpart, alternated through cos^2/sin^2 modulation; and (iii) a brightness constancy term (the classical optical-flow constraint) to encourage fixation and motion tracking. Stationarity of the action yields Euler–Lagrange equations whose numerical integration generates scanpaths. Saliency maps are obtained as a byproduct, by counting trajectory visits over time. Parameters are tuned via simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000 using the NSS metric. The model is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and the SFU video dataset, and is reported to be competitive with several classic and more recent saliency baselines.

The approach connects to long-standing bottom-up saliency work (e.g., Itti & Koch; information maximization; Bayesian Surprise), machine learning approaches (e.g., Judd et al.; CNN-based methods), and saccade/scanpath simulators (e.g., winner-take-all with inhibition of return). It also resonates with optical flow brightness constancy (Horn–Schunck) and with potential-field and dynamical systems views of attention/saccades. To my knowledge, a least-action/Euler–Lagrange derivation combining gradient attraction and brightness constancy to produce both saccades and tracking is less common in the NeurIPS literature and offers an interesting, interpretable alternative to data-driven models.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a compact, unified variational formulation that jointly induces exploratory movements and fixation/tracking behavior; interpretable terms and clear mechanistic hypotheses.
- Produces scanpaths directly; saliency maps arise as an emergent statistic rather than a primary objective.
- Lightweight and fast; minimal features (grayscale gradients and a blurred pyramid) still yield reasonable performance.
- Theoretical derivation is explicit; the Appendix details the Euler–Lagrange calculus.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Derivation contains inaccuracies/typos (e.g., kinetic energy K appears as “1 m ẋ^2” instead of 0.5 m||ẋ||^2; determinant expansion around Eqs. 41–42 drops the m^2 term and repeats indices). These raise concerns about correctness and stability guarantees; bounds on λ are not rigorously justified.
- The oscillatory alternation between local and peripheral gradients via cos^2(ωt)/sin^2(ωt) is ad hoc and not empirically validated; ω is not learned or justified.
- The dynamic is undamped; in realistic oculomotor systems one expects dissipative terms. Without damping, numerical stability and realism (e.g., saccade velocity profiles) are unclear.
- Evaluation protocol risks leakage/overfitting: parameters are selected using NSS on a subset, then “those configurations together with the average” are tested on the whole dataset to select the best one. Selection should be done on a held-out validation set only.
- Heavy center bias is injected by multiplying inputs with a Gaussian and further tuned via post-hoc blur/center-bias grid search. Metrics like AUC-Judd and NSS are sensitive to center bias; sAUC and shuffled baselines are required to assess true stimulus-driven signal. An ablation without center bias is needed.
- Limited metrics and missing quantitative scanpath evaluation (e.g., MultiMatch, ScanMatch, ST-D metrics). Tracking claims are qualitative; no comparisons to motion saliency or tracking baselines.
- Only grayscale is used; color/orientation channels are known to matter for bottom-up saliency.

Clarity
- Several footnotes are missing; many grammatical errors and notation inconsistencies; unclear hyperparameters (m, k, ω), integration details, step size, number of time steps, initial condition distribution, and computational cost. Tables referenced are missing in the submission text.

Originality
- Using EL to derive attention dynamics is refreshing; however, the ingredients (gradient magnitude, multi-scale blur, brightness constancy) are classical. The novelty is mainly in the unifying variational framing rather than in the underlying cues or the evaluation approach.

Significance
- Reported results are competitive with selected baselines, but comparisons omit recent top-performing deep saliency models (e.g., DeepGaze II-era models, modern LSTM/CNN benchmarks), and the video evaluation is on a small dataset without strong motion-aware baselines. Without stronger baselines and bias-controlled metrics, it is hard to assess impact on state of the art.

Constructive suggestions
- Fix derivation issues; provide a corrected, dimensionally consistent Lagrangian, a rigorous determinant analysis, and a stability/existence argument for solutions. Consider adding a Rayleigh dissipation term to model damping.
- Justify or learn the oscillation frequency ω; compare to a multi-scale blending ablation without time modulation.
- Provide thorough ablations: remove center bias; vary initialization away from center; report sAUC, shuffled-AUC, CC, KL, SIM; quantify sensitivity to runs and integration time.
- Evaluate scanpaths with established metrics and compare to scanpath simulators (e.g., Le Meur & Liu; models with inhibition of return).
- Expand baselines to include strong contemporary deep models; for video, add motion-saliency baselines and optical-flow-based methods.
- Use proper train/val/test splits; select hyperparameters on validation only. Report chosen values (ηb, ηp, λ, m, k, ω) and ranges.
- Release code and seeds to support reproducibility.
- Discuss biological plausibility (e.g., saccade kinematics) and how top-down task signals could modulate the variational terms.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, interpretable variational framework that unifies saccades and tracking; generates scanpaths and saliency as a byproduct.
- Competitive results despite simplicity; potential for real-time and robotics applications.

Arguments against acceptance
- Derivation errors and lack of stability analysis.
- Evaluation protocol and center-bias tuning confound conclusions; missing bias-robust metrics and strong baselines.
- Limited quantitative scanpath/tracking validation.
- Clarity issues and missing details hinder reproducibility.

Recommendation
Reject. The core idea is promising and potentially impactful, but the current version has technical inaccuracies, confounded evaluations, and clarity/reproducibility gaps. Addressing the above points could make this a strong submission in a future iteration.
