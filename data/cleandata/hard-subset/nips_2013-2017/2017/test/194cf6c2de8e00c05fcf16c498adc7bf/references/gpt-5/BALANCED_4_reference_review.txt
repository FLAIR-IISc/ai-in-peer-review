Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a physics-inspired, variational formulation of visual attention dynamics. The authors posit that scanpaths emerge from three principles encoded in a Lagrangian: (i) boundedness via a border “spring” potential, (ii) curiosity for regions with high spatial detail (gradient magnitude), modulated by an alternating local/peripheral drive, and (iii) brightness constancy to promote fixation and motion tracking (via the optical-flow constraint). The resulting Euler–Lagrange equations define a second-order dynamical system for the focus of attention. Saliency maps are then obtained as a byproduct by aggregating visited locations over multiple runs. Parameters are tuned via simulated annealing to match human data (NSS). The model is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000 (images) and SFU (videos), and is claimed to be competitive with several baselines, including deep models.

This connects to a long line of saliency work (Itti & Koch; Judd et al.; Bruce & Tsotsos; Itti & Baldi; modern CNN-based saliency) that largely posits a centralized saliency map and, often, a post-hoc WTA/inhibition-of-return for scanpaths. It also relates to dynamical/saccade-generation models (e.g., Le Meur & Liu) and to the optical flow literature (Horn–Schunck) through the brightness constancy term. The novelty here lies in formulating attention dynamics via least action with an explicit “kinetic energy” and combining curiosity and brightness invariance in a unified ODE framework.

Strengths:
- Conceptual unification: An elegant variational formulation that naturally produces scanpaths and, secondarily, saliency maps.
- Interpretable components: Each term has a clear function (confinement, edge/detail seeking, fixation/tracking).
- Simplicity: Requires only grayscale gradients and basic image processing; no supervised learning of complex feature towers.
- Potential for real-time scanpath simulation for a single trajectory; parameter λ constraints and derivations are explicit.
- Preliminary cross-domain applicability (images and videos) without task-specific engineering.

Weaknesses:
Quality:
- Empirical evidence is insufficiently documented. Tables with actual scores are not included here; claims like “best in half of the cases” are not verifiable. Dynamic-scene results are described qualitatively with minimal detail.
- Heavy metric-specific tuning and priors: The pipeline multiplies the input with a center Gaussian and performs post-hoc grid search over blur and center parameters to maximize AUC/NSS, which can inflate benchmark scores and confound attribution of gains to the dynamics.
- Saliency maps are aggregated from 100–199 randomized runs initialized near the center, further compounding center bias and deviating from the core “single-scanpath” model. Real-time claims are not aligned with this multi-run aggregation.
- Missing implementation details: how bt is computed from videos; choice and role of ω; initialization variance; integration scheme/step size; domain D and neighbor definition in SA; acceptance function P; run-time and complexity.
- Theory: No analysis of stability, existence/uniqueness, sensitivity to parameters, or robustness to noise. The curiosity modulation C(t, x) with cos^2/sin^2 is ad hoc and lacks justification (why alternating rather than a learned or state-dependent weighting?). The border potential uses piecewise indicators, which are non-smooth and could complicate ODE properties.
- Derivation concerns: There appear to be typos in the determinant expression for D and constraints on λ; care is needed to avoid algebraic errors.

Clarity:
- Writing is generally readable, but several definitions are incomplete (ω, m, k, precise normalization of b), and some footnotes and references are truncated or missing details. The experimental section omits critical numeric results and ablations. Organization around two model variants (exact vs approximate brightness constancy) is clear, but the approximation’s implications are not deeply analyzed.

Originality:
- Using least action for attention dynamics is interesting and moderately novel in this literature. However, the individual ingredients (gradient-based saliency, brightness constancy, border confinement) are standard, and the alternating local/peripheral drive feels heuristic. Prior dynamical attention models and active vision frameworks should be discussed more thoroughly.

Significance:
- If the model truly matches strong baselines without supervised training, it would be a noteworthy contribution. As presented, the evidence does not convincingly demonstrate a state-of-the-art advance; the reliance on center bias, blur tuning, and multi-run aggregation clouds the core scientific contribution. Scanpath realism (saccade amplitude distributions, fixation durations, temporal correlations) is not evaluated.

Pros:
- Elegant, interpretable dynamical formulation grounded in variational calculus.
- Unifies fixation and tracking behaviors via brightness constancy.
- Lightweight, potentially useful as a scanpath generator or prior.

Cons:
- Empirical validation is incomplete and confounded by metric-tuned priors and multi-run aggregation.
- Lack of ablations and sensitivity analysis (e.g., removing center bias/blur, curiosity term variants, brightness term off).
- Missing details and potential algebraic inconsistencies; unclear parameter choices (ω), stability, and reproducibility.
- Limited and partly qualitative evaluation on dynamic scenes; no scanpath-level metrics or behavioral comparisons.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide complete quantitative tables (MIT300, CAT2000-TEST, SFU) with all metrics, including standard deviations. Release code.
- Remove or rigorously control center bias and blur tuning; report with/without these additions to isolate the dynamical model’s contribution.
- Evaluate scanpaths directly (MultiMatch, ScanMatch, string-edit distance, saccade amplitude/angle distributions, fixation duration distributions).
- Ablations: curiosity on/off; brightness invariance on/off; local vs peripheral; different ω (and possibly learn ω); single-run vs multi-run aggregation.
- Analyze stability and parameter sensitivity; justify λ constraints; correct typos in derivations and double-check matrix expressions.
- Provide implementation details for bt estimation, integration, SA, and computational cost per frame.
- Compare against dedicated scanpath models and modern deep dynamic saliency methods; discuss biological plausibility of the alternating modulation.

Recommendation: Reject (weak). The conceptual framing is appealing, but the current empirical validation and clarity are insufficient for NeurIPS. With stronger experiments, ablations, clearer derivations, and scanpath-level analysis, this work could become a solid contribution. Confidence: High.
