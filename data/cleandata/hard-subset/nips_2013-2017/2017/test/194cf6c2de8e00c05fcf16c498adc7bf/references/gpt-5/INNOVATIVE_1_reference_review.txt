Summary
This paper proposes EYMOL, a physics-inspired, variational formulation of visual attention. The authors posit three primitives operating at early vision: (i) a bounding potential that constrains the focus of attention within the retinal image, (ii) a “curiosity” drive that alternates between local and peripheral detail via gradients of brightness and its blurred version, and (iii) a brightness-invariance (optical-flow) term that encourages fixation and motion tracking. They define a Lagrangian with kinetic energy and a generalized potential comprising these three terms, derive Euler–Lagrange equations for the 2D scanpath, and integrate the resulting ODEs. Saliency maps are obtained as a byproduct by counting pixel visits over multiple runs. Parameters are tuned via simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a subset of CAT2000, and a center bias and blur are post-tuned by grid search. The model is tested on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video; performance is competitive with classical baselines and some learning-based methods.

Relation to prior work: Unlike standard saliency work that computes a centralized saliency map (Itti & Koch; AIM; Bayesian surprise; SVM- and CNN-based models), this paper models attention dynamics directly and lets saliency emerge from visitation statistics. The use of brightness constancy links to optical flow literature; alternating local/peripheral cues echoes multi-scale saliency. Dynamical systems for saccade generation exist (e.g., Le Meur & Liu), but a least-action derivation that unifies curiosity, fixation, and tracking is comparatively uncommon. The paper would benefit from situating itself against active vision/optimal control and free-energy/active inference formulations.

Strengths
- Conceptually bold and original: a unified variational principle from which saccades, fixation, and tracking emerge, with saliency as a consequence rather than an objective.
- Derivation is explicit, including handling of velocity-dependent terms; they provide well-posedness constraints (e.g., λ < m/4).
- Practical: simple grayscale inputs, real-time integration, promising results on static and dynamic datasets without heavy features.
- The focus on scanpaths is valuable; few models produce trajectories with a physically interpretable law.

Weaknesses
- Technical clarity/consistency: the kinetic term is defined as K(ẋ) = (1/m)‖ẋ‖², but later derivations use d/dt ∂K/∂ẋ = m ẍ, which would require K = (m/2)‖ẋ‖². This should be corrected, as it affects dimensions and gradients.
- The time modulation between local and peripheral cues (cos² ωt / sin² ωt) lacks justification and analysis. ω is not specified nor learned; the behavioral consequences of frequency are unexplored.
- Heavy reliance on center bias and metric-tuned blur (applied to the input and via grid search to maximize AUC/NSS) undermines the claim that saliency “arises as a byproduct.” Also, initializing trajectories near the image center further injects center bias.
- Limited evaluation breadth: comparisons omit many state-of-the-art deep models on MIT300; reported “best in half the metrics” appears to be relative to a small subset of baselines. No scanpath-level metrics (e.g., string edit distance, saccade amplitude distributions) are provided. The tracking claim on videos is anecdotal; no quantitative object-tracking or motion-following assessment is shown.
- Ablation and sensitivity are missing: the roles of curiosity vs. brightness invariance, the effect of ω, and the necessity of the blurred term are not dissected. Numerical integration details (solver, step size, stability) and runtime are not quantified.
- Writing and references need polishing: several typos, incomplete citations, and missing parameter definitions (values for m, k, ω).

Quality
Technically plausible and mostly sound, but with a key inconsistency in the kinetic term and limited empirical rigor. The derivations are detailed; however, the model selection and metric-specific tuning introduce confounds. More thorough ablation and broader baselines are needed.

Clarity
Moderately clear but could be substantially improved. Tighten notation, fix the kinetic energy definition, define all parameters (m, k, ω) and their chosen values, describe the ODE solver and step sizes, and separate training vs. tuning vs. testing clearly. Reduce grammar issues and complete references.

Originality
High. The least-action framing of attention dynamics that unifies curiosity and brightness invariance, with saliency emerging from trajectories, is a fresh angle relative to both classical and deep saliency literature.

Significance
Potentially impactful as a conceptual shift and lightweight alternative that yields scanpaths and operates on dynamic scenes. Empirical significance is currently moderate due to limited comparisons and reliance on center bias; nonetheless, the idea could inspire follow-up work in variational/optimal-control models of attention.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Fix the kinetic energy definition and re-derive if needed; clarify dimensions and constants.
- Justify and analyze the ω modulation; consider learning or adapting ω, or replacing with a principled schedule.
- Provide ablations: remove center bias, remove one term at a time (V, C, B), vary ω, and report effects on metrics.
- Expand baselines on MIT300/CAT2000 with contemporary deep models; report scanpath metrics and dynamic tracking metrics.
- Quantify runtime, solver details, and stability; discuss the role of initial conditions beyond central starts.
- Consider connecting to active inference/free-energy and optimal control literatures.

Pros
- Novel, elegant variational formulation; unifies saccades, fixation, and tracking.
- Produces scanpaths; saliency is not explicitly optimized.
- Competitive results with very simple inputs; potentially real-time.

Cons
- Kinetic term inconsistency; several clarity/notation issues.
- Heavy reliance on center bias and metric tuning.
- Limited and somewhat outdated baseline comparisons; no scanpath or tracking metrics.
- Missing ablations/sensitivity analyses; undefined/untuned ω.

Recommendation
Weak accept (given novelty and a bold, unified modeling direction). With fixes to the kinetic term, clearer exposition, and stronger evaluation/ablations, this could be a compelling NeurIPS contribution.

Confidence
Moderate.
