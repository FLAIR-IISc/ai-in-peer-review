Summary
The paper proposes a dynamical systems/variational mechanics formulation of bottom-up visual attention for static and dynamic scenes. A scanpath x(t) is modeled as the trajectory of a particle evolving under an action S = ∫(K − U)dt. The potential U has three terms: (i) a boundary “spring” that keeps the trajectory within the retinal/image bounds; (ii) a curiosity term that attracts the particle to high-detail regions, combining local and peripheral gradient magnitudes via a time-modulated weighting; and (iii) a brightness invariance term derived from the optical flow constraint ḃ = bt + ∇b·ẋ, promoting fixation/tracking by penalizing brightness changes along the trajectory. The Euler-Lagrange equations yield second-order ODEs governing attention dynamics (EYMOL). For static images, an upper bound replaces B to simplify the equations. Parameters for the curiosity and brightness terms are fitted via simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a subset of CAT2000. Saliency maps are computed as visitation heat maps from multiple runs with random initializations near image center. Experiments on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video show the approach is competitive with several classical and some learning-based baselines according to standard saliency metrics.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: The least-action formalism provides a principled and compact way to couple curiosity-driven exploration with a fixation/tracking tendency (optical-flow constraint) in one dynamical model.
- From scanpaths to saliency: Saliency maps are not modeled directly but arise as a byproduct of simulated exploration, aligning with how human saliency maps aggregate fixations over time.
- Applicability to static and dynamic inputs within one framework is appealing. The optical flow constraint is a natural way to bias motion tracking behavior.
- Empirical results appear competitive with classic baselines, despite a relatively simple, hand-crafted model. The authors report real-time feasibility.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Mathematical inconsistencies: The kinetic energy is written as K = (1/m)‖ẋ‖², but later derivations require ∂K/∂ẋ = m ẋ to obtain the term mẍ. This mismatch suggests K should be (m/2)‖ẋ‖². In the appendix, the determinant D used to derive conditions on λ is simplified incorrectly (quadratic terms in λ are dropped), leading to an unjustified stability bound λ < m/4. There are repeated index/typo errors (e.g., b²x1 + b²x1), making parts of the derivation unreliable. These issues are serious and must be corrected.
- Many unspecified hyperparameters/details: ω (local/peripheral alternation rate), m, k, λ search ranges, SA acceptance function P(), neighbor definition, temperature schedule α, ODE solver and time-step, Gaussian blur σ, center-bias σ, number of steps per second, and initialization distributions. Reproducibility is currently not possible.
- Heavy, potentially confounding center bias: Inputs are multiplied by a central Gaussian and initial conditions are near the center. Saliency metrics such as AUC/Judd and NSS are sensitive to center bias. A strong center prior plus visit-count aggregation can inflate scores. This undermines the conclusion that the dynamics themselves explain performance.
- Parameter fitting/overfitting risk: Simulated annealing maximizes NSS on a subset of CAT2000-TRAIN, and additional grid search tunes blur/center bias. No cross-validation or ablation is shown to disentangle the contributions of the dynamics vs. center/blur priors and parameter tuning.
- Limited evaluation: Although the model purports to unify saccades and tracking, experiments evaluate only saliency maps (not scanpath similarity, saccade amplitude distributions, fixation durations, etc.) and only briefly mention moving-object tracking without quantitative analysis (e.g., pursuit gain, target lock). Comparisons with modern deep saliency models (static and video) are limited; details and exact scores are missing in the provided text.
- Clarity and presentation: Numerous typos, missing or malformed equations, incomplete references (years, venues), and cross-references to tables not included hinder readability. The overall writing needs significant editing.

Quality
- The central idea is intriguing, but technical correctness is compromised by derivation errors and missing details. Claims about stability bounds and explicit forms of the ODEs are not trustworthy as written. The empirical methodology lacks key controls (center bias removal; ablations of terms; sensitivity to ω and initial conditions).

Clarity
- The paper is not yet sufficiently clear or reproducible. Please precisely specify all hyperparameters, solver details, initial conditions, and training/validation splits; correct the mechanical derivations; and include ablation studies.

Originality
- Using a variational least-action framework for attention dynamics that blends curiosity (gradient energy) with the optical-flow constraint is a novel and elegant synthesis relative to classic saliency-map computation and to many dynamical models. That said, related lines (dynamic neural fields, energy-based active vision, information-maximizing attention, RL-based saccade control) should be discussed more comprehensively.

Significance
- If made technically sound and rigorously evaluated, the approach could provide a compact alternative to hand-designed saliency maps and open avenues for principled dynamic attention modeling. However, without stronger, center-bias-controlled benchmarks and scanpath analyses, the current empirical significance is limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the Lagrangian and derivations: correct K, re-derive ∂L/∂ẋ, and provide a rigorous, complete derivation of the determinant and stability conditions (include the λ² term).
- Specify all parameters and implementation details; release code.
- Provide ablations: remove center bias; vary initialization (uniform over image); remove curiosity or brightness terms; analyze effect of ω; compare exact vs. approximate B beyond static images.
- Evaluate scanpath statistics and dynamic tracking quantitatively (e.g., scanpath similarity to humans; pursuit metrics on moving targets).
- Compare against stronger contemporary models (e.g., recent deep static/video saliency models) and include center-bias baselines.
- Report computational costs and real-time benchmarks.
- Improve related work coverage (dynamic field theory, active vision, RL attention).

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually elegant unification via variational principles
- Potentially lightweight and real-time
- Promising initial performance on classic datasets

Cons for acceptance
- Mathematical errors and unclear derivations
- Heavy reliance on center bias and tuning; insufficient controls
- Missing details impede reproducibility
- Limited evaluation of scanpaths and tracking, modest comparison set

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is interesting and could be impactful, but the current submission has serious technical and empirical gaps that must be addressed.
