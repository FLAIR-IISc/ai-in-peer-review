- Proposes EYMOL, a variational, least-action model where attention emerges from boundedness, curiosity, and brightness invariance and is described by differential equations for scanpaths.  
- Claims saliency maps arise as a byproduct of scanpath dynamics rather than being optimized directly.  
- Evaluated on static (MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000) and dynamic (SFU) datasets using standard saliency metrics.  
- Situates work relative to classic and ML-based saliency models and emphasizes a dynamical, physics-inspired perspective distinct from most saliency-focused approaches.  
- Novelty: variational/least-action framing of attention is a genuinely new and refreshing contribution.  
- Unified framework: integrates curiosity, stability, and boundary constraints into a single tractable mathematical model.  
- Emphasis on dynamical scanpath modeling opens possibilities for modeling tracking and exploration beyond static saliency maps.  
- Experimental results are competitive with state-of-the-art despite model simplicity, notable because saliency is not directly optimized.  
- Paper provides a reasonably clear mathematical derivation (Euler–Lagrange) with details in the appendix.  
- Parameter sensitivity and reliance on simulated annealing raise robustness and computational-cost concerns; parameter search domain is not well justified.  
- Biological plausibility is underdeveloped: mathematical choices and weighting parameters lack clear neural interpretation.  
- Use of blurred input and center bias feels like tuning to human behavior, undermining claims of purely emergent attention.  
- Lacks detailed analysis of generated scanpath statistics (saccade amplitudes, fixation durations) and comparisons to human scanpaths.  
- Missing ablation studies to quantify each principle’s contribution and interactions.  
- Significance: offers a potentially impactful, unifying variational framework for attention, especially for dynamic scenes, if robustness and biological grounding improve.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel physical grounding, competitive benchmarks, clear math, and valuable focus on scanpath dynamics.  
- Arguments against acceptance: parameter sensitivity, limited biological/methodological justification for tweaks, and lack of scanpath/ablation analyses.  
- Overall assessment: strong submission with significant potential; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Suggested revisions: systematic parameter sensitivity analysis, discussion of neural mechanisms/biological justification, statistical analysis of scanpaths versus human data, ablation studies of each principle, and clearer discussion of limitations and future work.
