Summary
The paper proposes EYMOL, a variational/dynamical-systems model of visual attention. The authors posit three principles: (i) boundedness of eye trajectories via a border potential, (ii) “curiosity” that attracts the trajectory to regions with high local gradient magnitude and to peripheral structure via a blurred image, modulated over time by cos2/sin2 terms, and (iii) brightness invariance (optical flow constancy) as a soft constraint that encourages fixation along iso-brightness contours and motion tracking. These are combined into a Lagrangian (kinetic minus generalized potential), and the Euler–Lagrange equations yield a second-order ODE that governs the scanpath. Saliency maps are then computed as a byproduct by counting visitations over many simulated trajectories from random near-center initializations. Parameters are tuned by simulated annealing to maximize NSS on a subset of CAT2000-TRAIN; additional blur and center-bias post-processing are grid-searched to maximize AUC/NSS. The model is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000 (images), and SFU (videos), reporting competitive metrics against several classic models.

Relation to prior work
The setup connects to classic bottom-up saliency (Itti-Koch), information maximization (AIM), surprise, and recent CNN-based saliency. The novelty is the explicit least-action formulation and deriving scanpath dynamics rather than a saliency map, akin to some saccade simulators (e.g., Le Meur & Liu) and active-vision/dynamical models, but using a principled variational derivation. The brightness-constancy term is a standard optical-flow constraint used here as a driver of fixation/tracking. Relevant work on graph-based saliency (GBVS), strong modern deep baselines (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON and successors), and attention with learned saccades (e.g., Mnih et al., NIPS 2014) should be discussed and compared to better situate the contribution.

Quality
- Theory: The derivation is mostly sound and readable; using a velocity-dependent term in the “potential” is reasonable within Lagrangian mechanics. The matrix form and determinant condition for well-posedness are useful; however, some algebra is a bit sloppy (typos in indices in Eqs. 41–46), and important constants (m, k, ω) and their effects are not reported.
- Modeling choices: The curiosity field uses ||∇b||2 and ||∇p||2 with cos2/sin2 modulation; the rationale and choice of ω are not justified, and no ablations demonstrate their necessity. A damping/friction term is absent; stability is instead controlled by λ and the brightness term—this deserves discussion.
- Experiments: Saliency maps require multiple runs with random near-center initialization and explicit center bias/blur post-processing tuned on training sets. This can inflate AUC/NSS and confounds the contribution of the dynamical law versus priors. There are no ablations for: removing center bias; removing the brightness-invariance term; using only local vs. peripheral curiosity; varying ω; or sensitivity to initial conditions. For videos, there is no tracking metric; only saliency metrics are reported.
- Reproducibility: Key implementation details are missing (values/ranges for m, k, ω, integrator, step size, runtime, parameter ranges for SA, early stopping, initial condition distribution). Code/data are not promised.

Clarity
The paper is generally understandable, but there are formatting glitches, missing years/venues in references, and occasional typos. The appendix derivation is helpful. The experimental protocol needs clearer, self-contained description of hyperparameter tuning, cross-validation, and preprocessing decisions (especially center bias and Gaussian attenuation of peripheral contrast). Figures, qualitative examples, and scanpaths would help.

Originality
The least-action framing for attention that unifies curiosity with brightness-constancy in a single ODE is interesting and, to my knowledge, not widely explored in recent saliency literature. However, the operative terms are standard (gradient magnitude, optical-flow constraint), and the practical pipeline (aggregate visits, add center bias/blur) aligns with common saliency practices. The combination is novel but incremental without deeper empirical validation.

Significance
A simple, interpretable dynamical model that yields both scanpaths and saliency is appealing and could stimulate work at the intersection of vision and physics-inspired modeling. However, the empirical section does not convincingly advance the state of the art: strong modern deep baselines are missing, and heavy reliance on center bias/blur and near-center initializations clouds attribution. The video claims of tracking are not quantitatively demonstrated.

Pros
- Clear, principled variational formulation producing explicit equations of motion.
- Unifies fixation (brightness constancy) and exploration (curiosity) in one framework.
- Generates scanpaths in addition to saliency maps; real-time potential for single trajectories.
- Competitive metrics against several classic baselines on standard datasets.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on tuned center bias and blur; random starts near center; possible metric inflation.
- Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses; no quantitative scanpath comparison to human data.
- Lack of strong modern deep baselines (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON/variants, EML-NET).
- Insufficient details for reproducibility (hyperparameters, ω, integrator, runtime).
- Limited theoretical justification for time-modulated curiosity; no damping term discussion.
- Presentation issues (typos, incomplete references).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations: remove center bias; vary or remove brightness-invariance term; local vs. peripheral curiosity; different ω; with/without time modulation; effect of initial conditions.
- Compare against strong contemporary baselines from MIT/Tübingen benchmarks (DeepGaze II, SALICON, SAM-ResNet/MLNet, newer BMS/GBVS), and report multiple metrics (AUC-Judd, sAUC, NSS, CC, KL, SIM).
- For videos, evaluate tracking explicitly (e.g., overlap with moving-object masks/boxes) and include dynamic saliency baselines.
- Report full implementation details and release code. Include runtime for one scanpath and for saliency map generation.
- Analyze scanpaths vs human sequences (e.g., MultiMatch, string-edit distance, saccade amplitude/angle distributions).
- Justify the cos2/sin2 modulation and the choice of ω; consider adding a physically motivated damping term.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The conceptual contribution is interesting and potentially impactful, but the current empirical evaluation and analysis are not strong enough for NeurIPS. Strengthening comparisons, ablations, and reproducibility could make this a solid contribution. Confidence: medium.
