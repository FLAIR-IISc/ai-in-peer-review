Summary
The paper proposes a dynamical systems model of visual attention derived from a variational (least action) principle. The scanpath x(t) is modeled as the trajectory of a particle under a generalized Lagrangian L = K − U, with three main components: (i) a boundary potential V(x) that confines motion to the retinal/image bounds; (ii) a curiosity term C(t, x) encouraging motion toward high local gradient magnitude and a “peripheral” blurred gradient, alternated over time via cos^2/sin^2 modulation; (iii) a brightness invariance penalty B(t, x, ẋ) = (bt + bx ẋ)^2 encouraging fixation and motion along iso-brightness (Horn–Schunck optical flow constraint). The Euler–Lagrange equations yield second-order ODEs for the gaze location. For static images, an upper bound approximation B̄ ≈ 2|bx|^2|ẋ|^2 is proposed to simplify dynamics. Parameters (ηb, ηp, λ) are tuned via simulated annealing on a subset of CAT2000-TRAIN (NSS objective). Saliency maps are computed as visit counts over multiple simulated scanpaths initialized near the center. The approach is evaluated on MIT1003, MIT300, CAT2000, and SFU video, claiming competitive performance versus a few classical baselines, and anecdotal evidence of motion tracking.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a unified variational formulation that ties together curiosity-driven exploration and brightness constancy for fixation/tracking is elegant and interpretable.
- Derivation: the Euler–Lagrange machinery is worked out in detail, yielding explicit equations; the approach is computationally light and produces continuous scanpaths rather than only static saliency.
- Saliency as a byproduct: counting visits over simulated dynamics is a principled way to obtain saliency without a hand-crafted saliency map definition.
- Empirical scope: tested on standard image and video datasets, with metrics reported by the MIT Benchmark for some cases.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Technical inconsistencies. The kinetic energy is written as K(ẋ) = (1/m)|ẋ|^2 but later equations assume K = (1/2) m|ẋ|^2 (e.g., yielding mẍ). This should be corrected. The determinant D used to derive the λ < m/4 condition appears to drop terms (e.g., a −4λ^2(bx1 bx2)^2 contribution), and indices are inconsistent (b2x1 + b2x1). The well-posedness condition therefore is not convincingly established.
- Modeling choices are ad hoc and insufficiently justified: the periodic cos^2/sin^2 switching between local and peripheral gradients, the piecewise boundary potential, and applying a Gaussian center bias directly to the brightness field (which distorts gradients) rather than modeling priors in a more principled way.
- Empirical methodology raises concerns. Center bias and blur are explicitly tuned by grid search to optimize AUC-Judd/NSS; scanpaths are initialized near the center and aggregated over many runs. These choices can inflate metrics sensitive to center bias. No ablation reports the contribution of each component (C, B, boundary, center bias, ω), nor sensitivity to parameters.
- Evaluation is incomplete. Numerical results (Tables) are referenced but not presented in the text provided; it is hard to verify “best in half the cases.” Comparisons exclude many strong contemporary deep baselines (e.g., DeepGaze II, SALICON, MLNet/SAM/DeepFix for images; recent video saliency models). For video, claims of tracking are anecdotal without quantitative tracking metrics.
- Reproducibility: important details are missing (values/ranges of m, k, ω; integration method/step size; discrete bt estimation; stopping rules; exact train/val/test protocol). Code/data not provided.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable but contains typos, indexing errors, and some unclear notation. The derivation appendix is helpful, but the static-image approximation case is not derived to the same level of detail. The experimental section lacks crucial settings and complete result tables. Some references are incomplete or inconsistent.

Originality
- The variational formulation and explicit second-order dynamics for gaze are interesting and relatively uncommon in saliency modeling. However, the constituent principles (gradient-based “curiosity,” brightness constancy) are classical. There is related work in optimal control/infomax active vision (e.g., Najemnik & Geisler; Butko & Movellan), dynamical neural fields for attention, and saccade generation models that should be discussed to better position the contribution.

Significance
- As a conceptual and interpretable dynamical model of bottom-up attention and scanpaths, the work has potential to stimulate follow-up. However, without stronger and more comprehensive empirical evidence, careful ablations, and correction of technical issues, its impact on SOTA saliency prediction is limited. The need to aggregate many stochastic runs to form saliency maps also reduces practical efficiency.

Questions/suggestions for the authors
- Fix the kinetic energy expression and re-derive the motion equations and λ condition carefully. Provide a stability analysis and characterization of equilibria/attractors.
- Justify and analyze the time-modulated curiosity term; report results for constant weighting and different ω, and include ablations removing center bias and blur.
- Report complete benchmark results with modern baselines (image: DeepGaze II, SALICON, SAM/MLNet/DeepFix; video: recent deep spatiotemporal models). Include sAUC, CC, KL, SIM, and scanpath metrics (e.g., MultiMatch), and show performance without center bias.
- Provide parameter values, integration details, and sensitivity; release code.
- For video, detail how bt is estimated, smoothing/denoising, and provide quantitative tracking or dynamic saliency comparisons.
- Consider more principled priors (e.g., center bias as an additive prior over fixations rather than manipulating b), and biological oculomotor constraints (main sequence, refractory periods) if the goal is realistic scanpaths.

Pros
- Elegant, interpretable variational formulation with unified treatment of saccades/fixations/tracking.
- Produces continuous-time scanpaths; saliency emergent as a byproduct.
- Lightweight and potentially real-time.

Cons
- Technical inconsistencies in derivations; unclear well-posedness condition.
- Heavy reliance on center bias/blur and limited, possibly outdated comparisons.
- Missing ablations, stability analysis, and key experimental details; unclear reproducibility.
- Modest evidence of superiority on benchmarks; tracking claims not quantified.

Recommendation: Reject in its current form. The idea is promising, but the paper needs to correct technical errors, substantially strengthen the empirical evaluation and ablations, and improve clarity and positioning to meet the NeurIPS bar.
