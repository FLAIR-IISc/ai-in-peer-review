## Review of Pose Guided Person Image Generation

This paper proposes a novel Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG) for synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses, given a reference image and a target pose. The framework utilizes a two-stage approach: pose integration to generate a coarse initial image, and image refinement via an adversarial network to improve detail and sharpness.

The problem addressed is a valuable one, and the proposed approach represents a significant step towards controllable image generation, specifically for human figures. The two-stage architecture is a sensible strategy for tackling the complex task of simultaneously manipulating pose and maintaining identity. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ methodology and contributions. The extensive experimental results, including ablation studies on pose embedding methods and loss functions, demonstrate a thorough evaluation of the proposed approach. While the generated images aren't perfect and exhibit some artifacts, the visual results are promising and demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The task of conditioning image generation on both a reference image *and* a specified pose is novel and offers a significant degree of control over the generation process.
*   **Architectural Design:** The two-stage coarse-to-fine architecture is well-motivated and effectively breaks down the complex problem into more manageable steps. The use of U-Net-like networks in both stages is appropriate and leverages established architectures.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors present a thorough experimental evaluation, including ablation studies on different pose embedding methods, loss functions, and architectural choices.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making it easy to understand the proposed methodology and its contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** While the authors acknowledge the difficulty in establishing robust quantitative metrics for this type of generative task, the reported metrics (SSIM and Inception Score) offer a limited assessment of visual quality. The introduction of mask-SSIM is a step in the right direction, but the scores remain somewhat difficult to interpret.
*   **Related Work:** While the related work section covers relevant areas, it could benefit from including more recent work on cascaded or iterative refinement networks, particularly those used in image generation. For example, Carreira et al. (2016) and Newell et al. (2016) present relevant approaches to iterative refinement that could be discussed.
*   **Ablation Study Gap:** The authors mention a potential issue with the fully connected layer in Stage-I contributing to blurring, but don’t fully explore this in the ablation studies. A direct comparison of a version of Stage-I *without* the fully connected layer would strengthen the analysis.
*   **User Study Limitations:** The preliminary user study results are not particularly convincing and are appropriately downplayed by the authors themselves. The brief exposure time (1 second) may be insufficient for reliable judgment.

**Specific Comments:**

*   Line 110: "we find that using residual blocks as basic component improves the generation performance" – Please elaborate on *how* residual blocks improve performance. Is it due to improved gradient flow, feature reuse, or another factor?
*   Consider exploring the performance of a single-stage adversarial network trained directly on the Stage-I output, as suggested by the reviewer. This could clarify the benefit of the two-stage approach.
*   The failure cases shown in Figure 6 highlight potential limitations related to complex poses and data imbalances. Discussing these limitations more explicitly would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a novel and important problem in image generation.
*   The proposed approach demonstrates promising results, generating realistic-looking person images with controlled pose.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and includes a thorough experimental evaluation.
*   The authors acknowledge the limitations of their work and suggest directions for future research.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The quantitative evaluation is somewhat limited and doesn't fully capture the subjective quality of the generated images.
*   The user study is preliminary and doesn't provide strong evidence of user preference.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of controllable image generation. The novelty of the task, the well-designed architecture, and the thorough experimental evaluation outweigh the limitations. I confidently recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The work is a strong addition to NIPS and has the potential to inspire further research in this area.

I agree with the reviewer’s assessment that the preliminary nature of the user study renders it less impactful, and the authors should prioritize a more substantial human evaluation in any revisions. However, this does not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work.
