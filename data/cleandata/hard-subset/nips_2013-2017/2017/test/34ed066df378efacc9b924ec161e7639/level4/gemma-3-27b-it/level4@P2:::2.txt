This paper introduces a novel approach to generating person images in arbitrary poses, conditioned on a reference image and a target pose. The proposed Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) employs a two-stage adversarial training framework. The first stage uses a U-Net-like network to generate a coarse image integrating the reference appearance and target pose, while the second stage refines this initial result with another U-Net-like generator, effectively predicting a residual image to enhance detail. The method is evaluated on both a person re-identification dataset (Market-1501) and a fashion photo dataset (DeepFashion).

The core idea of explicitly guiding image generation with pose information is a valuable contribution. This task – manipulating a person’s pose in an image while preserving identity – has applications in areas like movie making and data augmentation for pose estimation. The proposed two-stage approach, dividing the problem into global structure and appearance detail refinement, is a reasonable strategy inspired by related work in image manipulation. The use of a difference map in the second stage to speed training and improve stability is also a noteworthy technique. The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the network architecture and experimental setup.

However, the paper has several areas for improvement. While the approach demonstrates a proof-of-concept, the visual quality of the generated images remains a significant concern. Despite the two-stage refinement process, artifacts are visible in the results (e.g., black dots in Figure 4, ID 346), indicating the model struggles to fully recover high-frequency details. This limits the practical applicability of the method.

The significance of the work could be strengthened. While the task is interesting, the proposed architecture builds upon existing conditional GAN techniques. The paper could more clearly articulate how it advances the state-of-the-art beyond simply applying established methods to a new problem. It’s important to differentiate this work from prior research on conditional GANs and stacked generative models (e.g., StackGAN [Zhang et al., 2016], Stacked GAN [Huang et al., 2016]).  The paper acknowledges these related works, but doesn’t fully contextualize its contribution relative to them.

Regarding clarity, the explanation of the pose embedding method is well-presented, and the justification for using heatmaps is reasonable. However, further elaboration on the design choices for the U-Net architectures (e.g., why residual blocks were chosen) would be helpful.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The task of pose-conditioned person image generation is a worthwhile exploration.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed two-stage architecture is logically sound and addresses the challenges of pose transfer.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the methodology effectively.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on two datasets demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Image Quality:** Generated images exhibit visible artifacts and lack high-frequency detail.
*   **Significance:** The work’s contribution feels incremental, building upon established conditional GAN techniques.
*   **Generalizability:** The specific focus on person image manipulation might limit broader applicability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a novel and potentially useful problem.
*   The proposed approach is technically sound and well-motivated.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the feasibility of the method, even with limitations.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The quality of the generated images is insufficient for practical applications.
*   The contribution, while interesting, may not be significant enough for NIPS.
*   The work is heavily reliant on existing GAN techniques without a major architectural innovation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a reasonable approach to pose-conditioned person image generation. While the image quality requires improvement and the novelty is somewhat incremental, the core idea and technical execution are sound.  I believe the paper has merit and warrants acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns regarding image quality and clearly articulating the unique contributions of their work relative to existing conditional GAN literature.

**Rating:** Accept (with minor revisions addressing image quality and contribution clarity)
