Summary:
This work presents a framework for synthesizing images of an individual, conditioned on an appearance from a source image and a target pose represented by heat maps.

Strengths:
+ The paper tackles a novel and challenging task.
+ The authors introduce a novel coarse-to-fine, two-stage architecture.
+ The manuscript is well-structured and clearly presented.
+ The generated results, while exhibiting some visible artifacts, demonstrate the promise and potential of the approach.
+ The authors provide a thorough ablation study to justify their architectural choices.

Weaknesses:
- The quantitative evaluation is somewhat limited. However, the reviewer acknowledges that this is not a significant drawback, as established metrics for this specific task are lacking.

Comments:
The proposed architecture utilizes an iterative refinement process. The related work section could be strengthened by discussing recent advancements in iterative refinement using cascaded networks, such as the work by Carreira et al. [a] and Newell et al. [b]:
[a] J. Carreira, P. Agrawal, K. Fragkiadaki, and J. Malik. Human pose estimation with iterative error feedback. In IEEE Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition (CVPR), 2016.
[b] A. Newell, K. Yang, and J. Deng. Stacked hourglass networks for human pose estimation. In European Conference on Computer Vision (ECCV), 2016.
- On line 110, the authors state that "using residual blocks as basic component improves the generation performance." The authors should elaborate on this point. In what specific ways is performance improved (e.g., visual quality, training stability)?
- The ablation studies are comprehensive, but one additional experiment could provide further insight. How would a model perform if the Stage 2 network were used directly with the Stage 1 inputs and trained adversarially? Furthermore, could the blurring observed in the Stage 1 + GAN results be attributed to the intermediate fully-connected layer?

Overall rating:
The novelty of the task and the proposed method are significant contributions. I therefore strongly recommend that this paper be accepted for the conference.

Rebuttal:
The reviewer acknowledges the authors' effort to conduct a user study. However, due to its preliminary nature, the study is not sufficiently convincing to impact the reviewer's assessment. For the final version of the paper, the authors are strongly encouraged to incorporate a more rigorous and substantive human evaluation. Nevertheless, this point does not change my overall positive evaluation of the work.
