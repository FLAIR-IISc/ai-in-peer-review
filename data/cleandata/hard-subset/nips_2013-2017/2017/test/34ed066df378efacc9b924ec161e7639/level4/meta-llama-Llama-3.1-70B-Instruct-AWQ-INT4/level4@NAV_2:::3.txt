This paper proposes the novel Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG) that allows synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses, based on an image of that person and a novel pose. The generation framework PG utilizes the pose information explicitly and consists of two key stages: pose integration and image refinement. The authors' approach is novel and the writing is easy to follow, and empirical experiments show that it is able to synthesize images that are realistic-looking at some level.

The paper relates to previous work on generative image modeling with deep learning techniques, including Variational Autoencoders (VAE), Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), and Autoregressive models. The authors also discuss related work on generating images conditioned on either category or attribute labels, texts, or images.

The proposed Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG) is a two-stage approach, with each stage focusing on one aspect. The first stage integrates a conditioning person image with a target pose to generate a coarse result that captures the global structure of the human body in the target image. The second stage refines the initial result by training a U-Net-like generator in an adversarial way. The authors also propose a novel pose mask loss to alleviate the influence of background changes.

The strengths of the paper include the proposal of a novel task of conditioning image generation on a reference image and an intended pose, the exploration of several ways to integrate pose information with a person image, and the division of the problem into two stages to address the challenging task of pose transfer.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. The authors do not provide quantitative evaluation of their method, such as user studies to evaluate how many times the generated images can trick human eyes. The empirical results are not visually appealing, with many noticeable visual errors when comparing the results to the targets. The authors should consider providing more convincing results to demonstrate the effectiveness of their method.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. However, the lack of quantitative evaluation and the visual errors in the results detract from the overall quality of the paper.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. However, the figures could be improved to better illustrate the results.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel task and approach to conditioning image generation on a reference image and an intended pose. However, the authors build on existing techniques, such as GANs and U-nets, and the contribution is mainly in the specific computer vision application.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a challenging task in computer vision, and the results have the potential to be useful in many applications, such as movie making and image retrieval based on synthesized images. However, the lack of quantitative evaluation and the visual errors in the results detract from the overall significance of the paper.
