This paper proposes a generative adversarial network (GAN) for synthesizing novel-view images of a person, conditioned on a target skeleton represented by keypoints. The system is primarily an assembly of existing components, such as DCGAN and U-Net architectures. Consequently, the work's main contribution lies in its application to this specific computer vision task rather than in novel methodology.

**Pros:**

*   The core idea of synthesizing person images from poses is compelling, and the paper is well-written and easy to follow.
*   The empirical results demonstrate a capacity to synthesize images that possess a degree of realism.

**Cons:**

*   Given that this work is application-oriented, the quality of the results is paramount. A major weakness is the complete lack of quantitative evaluation. For instance, a user study could have been organized to assess the realism of the generated images by measuring how frequently they are mistaken for real photographs.
*   Furthermore, the qualitative results are not visually convincing. In Figure 1, for example, the generated images are too small for detailed inspection, yet noticeable artifacts and discrepancies are still apparent when compared to the target images. This issue persists across nearly all results presented, including those in the supplementary material. While the research direction is promising, the current results are not yet mature enough to be considered high-quality or close to photorealistic. The work appears to be at an early stage, and significant improvements are needed before the method can be deemed successful.

**After Rebuttal:**

After reviewing the authors' rebuttal, my initial concerns remain. As noted by myself and other reviewers, the lack of rigorous quantitative and qualitative evaluation is a significant issue for an application-focused paper. While the authors attempted to address these points in their feedback, the response does not sufficiently resolve the core weaknesses. Therefore, I maintain my position that the paper, in its current state, does not meet the standard for publication at NIPS.
