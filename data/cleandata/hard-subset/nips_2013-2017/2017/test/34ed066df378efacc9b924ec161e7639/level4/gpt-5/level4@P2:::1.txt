Summary:
This paper introduces Pose Guided Person Generation (PG2), a conditional image generation framework that synthesizes images of a target person in arbitrary poses, given a reference image and a target pose. The method explicitly encodes pose as 18 keypoint heatmaps and adopts a two-stage, coarse-to-fine architecture. Stage I integrates the reference image and target pose using a U-Net–like generator with residual blocks to produce a coarse image that captures global body structure. A pose-aware masked L1 loss emphasizes the person over the background. Stage II refines high-frequency details using an adversarially trained U-Net to predict a residual (difference) map, paired with the same masked L1 for stability and a pairwise discriminator conditioned on the reference image. The approach is evaluated on Market-1501 (128×64) and DeepFashion (256×256), with extensive ablations on pose encodings, loss functions, and one-stage vs two-stage training. Results demonstrate convincing pose transfer with improved sharpness over the coarse outputs, though some artifacts remain. The work relates to conditional GANs, image-to-image translation, and pose-aware generation, and is positioned against prior efforts in multi-view generation and text/attribute conditioned GANs.

Strengths:
- Novel and well-scoped task: conditioning person image synthesis on both an identity image and an explicit pose representation.
- Clear, principled two-stage design (global structure first, then detail refinement) with a residual-map refinement that simplifies learning.
- Effective pose encoding via keypoint heatmaps and a simple, practical pose mask to downweight background mismatch.
- Thorough ablations on pose embeddings, losses, and training strategies; good qualitative results across two datasets.
- Writing is generally clear, and the method is easy to follow and reproduce at a high level.

Weaknesses:
- Quantitative evaluation is limited by imperfect metrics (SSIM/IS and their masked variants). While understandable, this reduces the strength of the empirical claims.
- The user study is preliminary and not sufficiently convincing in its current form.
- Some architectural choices (e.g., the impact of residual blocks and of fully connected layers) could be justified more concretely.

Evaluation against review criteria:
- Quality: The methodology is technically sound, and the staged design, masked loss, and pairwise discriminator are well motivated. The ablation studies are helpful, though stronger human evaluation and more robust metrics would enhance the claims.
- Clarity: The paper is well organized and readable. It would benefit from clarifying the concrete gains from residual blocks and from removing/retaining fully connected layers across stages.
- Originality: Conditioning on explicit pose heatmaps and refining via a learned residual in a two-stage framework is a novel combination that goes beyond prior conditional GAN/image-to-image setups for this task.
- Significance: The approach addresses an important, practical problem with many applications (content creation, data augmentation for pose estimation/re-ID) and appears likely to influence follow-up work.

Comments and suggestions:
- Please discuss related iterative refinement/cascaded architectures more explicitly, given the coarse-to-fine design. For example:
  [a] Carreira et al., Human pose estimation with iterative error feedback, CVPR 2016.
  [b] Newell et al., Stacked hourglass networks for human pose estimation, ECCV 2016.
- Section 3.1 (statement about residual blocks improving performance): in what way do residual blocks help? Please provide quantitative evidence (e.g., SSIM/mask-SSIM or a perceptual metric) or qualitative comparisons.
- Training/design question: How does a one-stage adversarial model using the Stage II architecture (i.e., fully convolutional U-Net without the bottleneck fully connected layer) perform when conditioned on the same inputs as Stage I? The current one-stage baseline uses the Stage I architecture; it would be useful to know whether the blurring seen with G1+GAN is due to the presence of the fully connected layer, rather than the one-stage setup per se.
- Metrics: While SSIM/IS and their masked variants are standard, consider complementing them with a human preference study with longer exposure and pairwise comparisons (A/B testing), and/or learned perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) to better capture visual fidelity.
- Minor: Clarify the role of λ in Stage II with a brief sensitivity analysis in the main text (currently deferred); readers would benefit from knowing the practical range that avoids artifacts vs. blur.

Overall rating:
Accept. The problem and approach are novel and well motivated, the method is carefully designed, and results are promising with solid ablations. Despite limitations in quantitative evaluation and a preliminary user study, the contribution is significant and timely.

Rebuttal:
I appreciate the attempt at a user study; however, in its current brief form it is not persuasive, and I discount it in my overall assessment. I encourage the authors to include a more substantive human evaluation (e.g., pairwise preference tests, longer exposure, and more participants) in the final version. My positive recommendation remains unchanged.
