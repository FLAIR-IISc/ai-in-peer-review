

This paper proposes the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), a novel two-stage framework for synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses based on a reference image and target pose. The approach addresses the challenging task of pose transfer by dividing it into two specialized stages: stage I integrates pose information to generate a coarse structural approximation, while stage II refines this result using adversarial training to produce high-quality, photo-realistic images. The authors introduce a pose mask loss to focus generation on human body regions rather than background, and a difference map approach that accelerates training by having the second generator predict appearance corrections rather than synthesizing images from scratch.

The paper makes several valuable contributions. First, it introduces a meaningful new task of conditioning image generation on both reference appearance and explicit pose information, which enables direct control over pose manipulation. Second, it explores multiple pose embedding strategies and proposes an effective pose mask loss that significantly improves generation quality by suppressing background interference. Third, the two-stage architecture effectively separates the challenges of pose structure generation and appearance detail refinement, leading to superior results compared to single-stage alternatives.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with comprehensive experiments on two datasets (DeepFashion and Market-1501), using both quantitative metrics (SSIM, Inception Score) and qualitative analysis. The ablation studies clearly validate the importance of the pose mask loss and the two-stage design. The authors appropriately compare against related work, including Zhao et al. (2017) on multi-view person image synthesis, showing that their method produces significantly more detailed and realistic results.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-structured organization and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The architecture diagrams and result visualizations effectively communicate the key innovations. The writing is generally clear, though some technical descriptions could be more precise.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of conditional image generation. While the two-stage approach draws inspiration from prior work like StackGAN (Zhang et al., 2016) and Stacked GANs (Huang et al., 2016), the specific application to pose-guided person generation with the pose mask loss and difference map refinement represents a novel combination tailored to the problem. The authors appropriately acknowledge related work while clearly articulating their distinct contributions.

In terms of significance, the work addresses a practical problem with applications in fashion, movie production, and pose estimation data augmentation. The ability to generate high-quality person images in arbitrary poses from a single reference image has tangible value for these domains. However, the significance is somewhat limited to vision and graphics applications, with less obvious broader impact across machine learning.

The paper's main weaknesses relate to the quality of the generated images. While the two-stage approach improves upon single-stage methods, the results still contain visible artifacts (e.g., black dots in ID 346 in Figure 4) and struggle to recover high-frequency details, particularly for complex poses or clothing patterns. The user study (Table 2) shows that generated images are still distinguishable from real ones by human raters, with only 14.9% of refined images being misclassified as real on DeepFashion. This suggests the model hasn't yet reached the level of quality required for practical applications.

The paper would benefit from more thorough discussion of failure cases (e.g., Figure 6 shows issues with rare poses and gender mismatches) and a clearer articulation of how the proposed method advances beyond the state of the art in terms of both quality and controllability. While the pose mask loss is effective, the paper could better quantify how much it contributes to the quality improvement.

Pro-acceptance arguments:
- Novel task formulation (pose-guided person generation)
- Effective two-stage architecture with clear rationale
- Strong experimental validation with ablation studies
- Practical applications in fashion and entertainment
- Well-executed evaluation on two datasets

Con-acceptance arguments:
- Generated images still contain visible artifacts
- Limited significance beyond vision/graphics applications
- Some techniques (two-stage GANs) have precedents in literature
- Quality not yet sufficient for practical applications
- User study shows significant gap between generated and real images

Overall, this is a solid technical contribution that effectively addresses a meaningful problem with a well-designed solution. While the generated images require further refinement to reach practical quality, the paper makes a clear and valuable contribution to the field of conditional image generation. The two-stage approach with pose mask loss represents a meaningful advancement over previous work, and the results demonstrate clear progress over single-stage alternatives. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that further work is needed to address the remaining quality limitations.
