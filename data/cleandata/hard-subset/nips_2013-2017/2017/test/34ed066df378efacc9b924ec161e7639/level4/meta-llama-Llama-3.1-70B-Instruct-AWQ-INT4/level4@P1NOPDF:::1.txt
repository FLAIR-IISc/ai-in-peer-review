Summary:
The paper proposes a novel architecture for generating images of a person that combines the appearance of a person in an input image with a specified input pose, represented using heat maps.

Strengths:
+ The proposed task is novel and contributes to the field of image generation.
+ The two-stage coarse-to-fine architecture is a unique approach to this problem.
+ The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
+ Although the results contain some discernible artifacts, they are promising and demonstrate the potential of the proposed method.
+ The authors conduct an extensive evaluation of their design decisions, providing valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

Weaknesses:
- The quantitative evaluation is generally weak, which is understandable given the lack of suitable evaluation metrics for this task. However, this does not significantly detract from the overall quality of the paper.

Comments:
Given the iterative refinement nature of the proposed architecture, it would be beneficial to include a discussion of recent work on iterative refinement using cascaded networks in the related work section. For example, [a] J. Carreira, P. Agrawal, K. Fragkiadaki, and J. Malik. Human pose estimation with iterative error feedback. In IEEE Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition (CVPR), 2016, and [b] A. Newell, K. Yang, and J. Deng. Stacked hourglass networks for human pose estimation. In European Conference on Computer Vision (ECCV), 2016, could be relevant additions.

On line 110, the authors state that "we find that using residual blocks as basic components improves the generation performance." However, it would be helpful to elaborate on how the residual blocks contribute to the improved performance. What specific benefits do they provide, and how do they impact the overall quality of the generated images?

While the evaluation is generally extensive, I was left wondering how the Stage 2 network would perform if it were trained with the inputs for Stage 1 in an adversarial manner. Would this approach alleviate the blurring observed with Stage 1 + GAN, and is the intermediate fully connected layer a contributing factor to this issue?

Overall rating:
Based on the novelty of the problem and approach, I confidently recommend that the paper be accepted.

Rebuttal:
Although I appreciate the authors' attempt to conduct a user study, I find the results unconvincing due to their preliminary nature. I am discounting the user study in my review rating, and I urge the authors to include a more substantive human evaluation in their revision. Nevertheless, my positive review of the work remains unchanged.
