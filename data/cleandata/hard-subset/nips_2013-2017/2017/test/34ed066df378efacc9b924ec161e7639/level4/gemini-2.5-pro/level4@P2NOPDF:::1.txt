### Summary

This paper introduces a novel two-stage, coarse-to-fine architecture for pose-guided person image generation. The model's objective is to synthesize a new image of a person that combines the visual appearance from a source image with a specified target pose, which is represented by a set of keypoint heat maps.

### Strengths

+   **Novelty of the Task:** The paper addresses the novel and challenging task of synthesizing person images conditioned on a target pose, which has significant potential applications.
+   **Novel Architecture:** The proposed two-stage generator, which progressively refines the output from a coarse initial estimate to a fine-grained result, is a well-designed and effective architecture for this problem.
+   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow.
+   **Promising Results:** The qualitative results are impressive and demonstrate the promise of the approach. While some generated images contain discernible artifacts, the overall quality is high.
+   **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors provide an extensive set of ablation studies that thoroughly evaluate their key design decisions, which strengthens the paper's contributions.

### Weaknesses

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its quantitative evaluation. However, this is understandable given the lack of established, objective evaluation metrics for this novel generative task. Therefore, this is considered a minor issue that does not significantly detract from the paper's overall contribution.

### Comments for the Authors

1.  **Related Work:** The proposed two-stage architecture employs a form of iterative refinement. It would strengthen the paper to discuss connections to other recent work on iterative refinement and cascaded networks in the related work section, such as the methods proposed for human pose estimation by Carreira et al. (2016) and Newell et al. (2016).

2.  **Clarification on Residual Blocks (Line 110):** On line 110, you state that "using residual blocks as basic component improves the generation performance." Could you please elaborate on this claim? For example, in what specific ways was performance improved—did it lead to faster convergence, higher qualitative results, or better scores on the user study?

3.  **Suggested Experiment:** The ablation studies are comprehensive. One additional experiment that would provide further insight is an evaluation of the Stage 2 generator when it is trained adversarially on the inputs intended for Stage 1. This could help clarify whether the blurring observed in the "Stage 1 + GAN" results is primarily a consequence of the architectural bottleneck from the intermediate fully connected layer.

4.  **User Study:** While the inclusion of a user study is appreciated, its current form is preliminary and lacks the detail needed for a convincing evaluation. To strengthen the paper's claims, I strongly urge the authors to conduct and include a more substantive and rigorously designed human evaluation in their final revision.

### Overall Recommendation

Based on the novelty of the problem, the well-designed architecture, and the promising results, I recommend that this paper be accepted. The work is a valuable contribution to the field of generative models and computer vision. Despite the need for a more rigorous human evaluation, the core technical contributions are solid and well-supported.
