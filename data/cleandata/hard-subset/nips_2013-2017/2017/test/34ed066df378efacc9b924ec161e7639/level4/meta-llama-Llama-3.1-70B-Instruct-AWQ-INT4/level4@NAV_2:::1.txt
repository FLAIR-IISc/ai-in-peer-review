The paper proposes a novel Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG) that synthesizes person images in arbitrary poses based on an image of that person and a novel pose. The generation framework PG utilizes pose information explicitly and consists of two key stages: pose integration and image refinement. The proposed task is novel, and the two-stage coarse-to-fine architecture is well-designed. The paper is well-written, and the results, although containing discernible artifacts, are promising.

The authors provide an extensive evaluation of their design decisions, including different pose embeddings, pose mask loss vs. standard L1 loss, and two-stage model vs. one-stage model. The quantitative evaluation is generally weak, but this is understandable given that no suitable evaluation metrics exist. The authors also perform a user study on Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT) for both datasets, which provides some insights into the quality of the generated images.

The paper relates to previous work on generative image modeling with deep learning techniques, including Variational Autoencoders (VAE), Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), and Autoregressive models. The authors also discuss related work on image generation conditioned on category or attribute labels, texts, or images.

The proposed PG network is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and adequately informs the reader.

The originality of the paper lies in the novel task of conditioning image generation on a reference image and an intended pose, as well as the proposed two-stage architecture. The work is significantly different from previous contributions, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the differences.

The significance of the paper is evident in its potential applications, such as face editing, movie making, and image retrieval based on synthesized images. The results are important, and other researchers and practitioners are likely to use these ideas or build on them. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

Arguments for acceptance:
- The proposed task is novel and well-motivated.
- The two-stage coarse-to-fine architecture is well-designed and effective.
- The paper is well-written, and the results are promising.
- The authors provide an extensive evaluation of their design decisions.

Arguments against acceptance:
- The quantitative evaluation is generally weak.
- The user study is preliminary and not convincing.

Overall, I recommend accepting the paper based on its novelty, technical soundness, and potential impact. However, I suggest that the authors include a more substantive human evaluation in their revision to strengthen their claims.
