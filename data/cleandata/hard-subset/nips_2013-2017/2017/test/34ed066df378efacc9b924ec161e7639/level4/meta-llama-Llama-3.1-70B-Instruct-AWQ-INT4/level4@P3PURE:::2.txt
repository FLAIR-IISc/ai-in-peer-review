This paper proposes a human image generator conditioned on appearance and human pose, utilizing an adversarial training architecture that consists of a two-step generative network to produce high-resolution images, which are then fed into a discriminator. In the generator, the first network employs a U-shape architecture to produce a coarse image given the appearance and pose map. The second network then takes the coarse input along with the original appearance to predict a residual that refines the coarse image. The DeepFashion dataset is used for evaluation.

The paper introduces several important ideas, including the novelty of the task, which involves conditioning on appearance and pose maps for human image generation. Additionally, the paper proposes a stacked architecture that predicts a difference map rather than directly upsampling, as well as a loss design.

However, there are several areas where the paper can improve to stand out. Firstly, the image quality still requires improvement. Secondly, the significance of the paper may be diminished by the fact that it can be seen as yet another GAN architecture. Lastly, the problem domain, while a good application of vision and graphics, may be difficult to generalize to other learning problems.

The paper is well-organized and effectively conveys the key aspects of the proposed architecture. The generator's coarse-to-fine strategy, conditioned on appearance and pose information, is clearly explained. The paper effectively utilizes the generation strategy in the dressing problem, and the proposed approach appears to be well-suited to the problem scenario. The difference map generation technique also shows promise in generating higher-resolution images.

However, one major concern with the paper is that the generated results contain visible artifacts and still require significant improvement for practical application. For example, the patterns in ID346 of Fig 4 result in black dots in the final image. Although the second generator mitigates the blurriness of the first generator's output, it appears that the model is still insufficient to recover high-frequency components in the target appearance.

Another potential concern, albeit not severe, is that some may view the proposed approach as an application of conditional GANs. Conditioning or stacking generators for adversarial training have been explored in the past, as seen in papers such as "StackGAN: Text to Photo-realistic Image Synthesis with Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks" by Han Zhang et al. and "Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks" by Xun Huang et al. While the paper addresses application-specific challenges, this may not appeal to a broad audience.

In overall, the paper successfully proposes a solution to the pose-conditioned image problem and conducts evaluation properly. The proposed approach demonstrates sufficient technical novelty, and the resulting images, although requiring quality improvement, are at least visually consistent. My initial rating is accept.
