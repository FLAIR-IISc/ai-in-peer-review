## Review: Generating Images with Controlled Pose

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel architecture for generating images of people, allowing control over both identity (derived from an input image) and pose (specified via heatmaps). The proposed approach effectively combines these inputs to create realistic images, representing a valuable contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The task of generating images with explicit pose control, while maintaining identity, is a significant and original contribution.
*   **Architecture:** The two-stage, coarse-to-fine architecture is a thoughtfully designed approach to tackle this challenging problem.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Promising Results:** Although the generated images exhibit some noticeable artifacts, the overall quality is encouraging and demonstrates the potential of the proposed method.
*   **Thorough Analysis:** The authors provide an extensive evaluation of key design choices, strengthening the validity of their findings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** The quantitative evaluation is limited, which is understandable given the current lack of established metrics for assessing the quality of images generated with this level of control. However, this remains a weakness that ideally should be addressed in future work. 

**Comments:**

The iterative refinement process employed by the proposed architecture shares similarities with recent advances in cascaded networks. Including related work such as:

*   Carreira et al. (2016) – "Human pose estimation with iterative error feedback" (CVPR)
*   Newell et al. (2016) – "Stacked hourglass networks for human pose estimation" (ECCV)

would provide valuable context and demonstrate awareness of the broader research landscape. 

Regarding implementation details, line 110 states: "we find that using residual blocks as basic component improves the generation performance." This statement would benefit from further elaboration. *How* does the inclusion of residual blocks lead to improved performance? A more detailed explanation is requested.

While the ablation studies are generally comprehensive, I am curious about the potential performance of using the Stage 2 network directly with Stage 1 inputs, trained with an adversarial loss. Investigating this configuration could provide further insight into the observed blurring in the Stage 1 + GAN results. Specifically, it would be helpful to understand if the fully connected layer within Stage 1 contributes to this blurring effect.

**Overall Rating:**

Despite the limitations in quantitative evaluation, the novelty of the problem addressed and the ingenuity of the proposed approach lead me to confidently recommend acceptance. The paper represents a significant step forward in image generation with pose control.

**Rebuttal Considerations:**

I acknowledge the authors’ attempt to conduct a user study. However, given its preliminary nature, I find the results inconclusive and have not factored them into my overall assessment. I strongly encourage the authors to incorporate a more substantial and rigorously designed human evaluation as part of their revision to provide more compelling evidence of the perceptual quality of the generated images. Regardless, my positive evaluation of the technical merits of this work remains unchanged.
