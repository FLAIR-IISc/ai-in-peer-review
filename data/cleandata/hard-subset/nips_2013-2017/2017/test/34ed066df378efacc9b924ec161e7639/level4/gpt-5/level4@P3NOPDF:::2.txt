Summary:
This paper addresses human image synthesis conditioned on clothing appearance and target pose. The authors propose a two-stage adversarial framework: (1) a U-Net-like generator produces a coarse image from an appearance reference and a pose map; (2) a second generator refines this result by predicting a residual (difference) map conditioned on the coarse output and the original appearance input. The method is evaluated on the DeepFashion dataset. The core ideas include conditioning on both appearance and pose, a stacked coarse-to-fine architecture, and a loss design tailored to improve image fidelity.

Strengths:
- Clear problem setup and an application of practical relevance in vision/graphics (e.g., virtual try-on, pose-guided person synthesis).
- Sensible two-stage coarse-to-fine design: using a residual refinement network to correct and sharpen the coarse output is a pragmatic choice that often stabilizes GAN training and improves detail.
- The decision to predict a difference map rather than directly upsample is a neat, lightweight technique that helps focus the second stage on high-frequency corrections.
- The paper is well organized and communicates the architecture and training setup clearly.
- Empirical results show visually consistent images that align with target poses and preserve key appearance cues, indicating that the approach effectively tackles the pose-conditioned generation problem.

Weaknesses and Concerns:
- Image quality is still limited. Visible artifacts remain, particularly in patterned clothing and fine textures. For example, in Fig. 4 (ID346), regular patterns translate into black dots and other artifacts, suggesting the model struggles with high-frequency detail and texture consistency.
- While the second stage mitigates blur from the first stage, the approach still appears insufficient for reliably reconstructing high-frequency components (e.g., small logos, repetitive patterns, stitching).
- Novelty may be perceived as incremental. The method could be viewed as an application-specific variant of conditional GANs with stacked generators, a well-explored direction. The contribution, while practically useful, may be framed as an engineering refinement rather than a conceptual leap.
- Generality is limited. The task is specific to human image generation under pose changes; insights may not transfer broadly to other domains without substantial adaptation.

Relation to Prior Work:
- The stacked refinement idea and multi-stage adversarial training have precedents, such as StackGAN (Zhang et al., 2016) and Stacked GANs (Huang et al., 2016). The paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison and discussion of differences (e.g., conditioning choices, residual refinement, loss design, and training dynamics).
- Positioning relative to contemporary pose-guided synthesis and virtual try-on works (if any are concurrent) would further clarify novelty and the unique challenges addressed here.

Suggestions for Improvement:
- Strengthen the refinement stage to better handle textures and high-frequency detail (e.g., multi-scale discriminators, patch-based or texture-consistency losses, style/perceptual losses, or attention mechanisms focusing on garment regions).
- Provide a deeper analysis of failure cases: when and why do artifacts occur (e.g., complex repetitive patterns, occlusions, extreme pose changes)?
- Expand quantitative evaluation beyond qualitative figures. Consider perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS), structural metrics (e.g., SSIM), and human preference studies. Where possible, compare against strong baselines, including single-stage cGANs and alternative refinement strategies.
- Include ablations that isolate the effect of (a) residual prediction vs. direct prediction in the second stage, (b) loss components, and (c) conditioning modalities (appearance-only, pose-only, both).
- Discuss computational considerations (inference time, memory) and any trade-offs introduced by the stacked design.

Clarity and Organization:
- The paper is generally clear and well structured. The architectural description and motivation for residual refinement are easy to follow.
- Ensure consistent terminology (e.g., “U-Net-like” for the first generator, “residual/difference map” for the second-stage output) and explicitly state inputs/outputs at each stage.
- If not already included, a concise overview diagram with annotations of conditioning signals and residual pathways would further aid comprehension.

Reproducibility:
- Please provide sufficient training details (dataset splits, preprocessing, pose estimation method, hyperparameters, loss weights, optimization schedule, and model capacity).
- If possible, release code and trained models, or at least pseudocode to facilitate replication.

Significance:
- The work offers a solid application-driven contribution with a straightforward and effective architectural choice. While it may be seen as incremental within the GAN literature, the method addresses a concrete and challenging problem and advances the practical state of pose-conditioned human image synthesis.

Overall Assessment:
- Despite visible artifacts and limited generality, the paper presents a coherent approach that produces visually consistent results and demonstrates meaningful, if incremental, technical novelty. The two-stage residual refinement is a simple, effective idea applied thoughtfully to the target problem. With stronger evaluation, clearer positioning relative to prior stacked GANs, and improved handling of high-frequency textures, the paper would be even more compelling.
- Rating: Accept (borderline to weak accept), conditional on clarifying novelty and strengthening the empirical evaluation.
