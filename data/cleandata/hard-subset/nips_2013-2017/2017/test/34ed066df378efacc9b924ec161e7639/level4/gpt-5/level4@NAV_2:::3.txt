This paper presents a GAN-based framework for synthesizing person images in new poses, driven by a target skeletal configuration represented by keypoints. The architecture primarily leverages established components (e.g., DCGANs and U-Nets), so the main contribution lies in the specific application and problem formulation rather than in introducing fundamentally new methods.

Pros:
- The problem is compelling, the manuscript is clearly written, and the empirical demonstrations show the system can produce images that look realistic to some extent.

Cons:
- Because the work is application-oriented, the strength of the results is crucial. My main concern is the lack of quantitative evaluation. For example, a user study assessing how often the generated images fool human observers would be very informative.
- The visual results are not particularly convincing. In Fig. 1, the generated images are too small to inspect, and even so there are many visible artifacts when comparing to the targets. This makes it hard to claim the outputs are high quality or close to real images. Similar issues appear across most figures, including the supplementary material. I believe the direction is promising, but the current results feel premature, and it seems too early to claim success.

After rebuttal:
I remain unconvinced after reading the authors’ response. As other reviewers also noted, there are still significant issues with both quantitative and qualitative evaluations—both are critical for a practically focused paper. The rebuttal includes some attempts, but these do not satisfactorily address the concerns. In my view, the work is not yet ready for a venue like NIPS.
