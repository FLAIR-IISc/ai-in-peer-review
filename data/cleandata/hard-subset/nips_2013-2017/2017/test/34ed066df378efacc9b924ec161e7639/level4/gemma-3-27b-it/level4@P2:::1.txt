This paper introduces a novel approach to person image generation, synthesizing images by transferring the appearance from a source image to a target pose, represented using heatmaps. The proposed Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) employs a two-stage architecture – pose integration followed by image refinement – to achieve this.

The work addresses a compelling and previously unexplored problem in image generation. The two-stage approach, dividing the task into global structure and detailed appearance, is a sensible strategy, reminiscent of approaches in other domains [35, 9, 3, 19]. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The presented results, while exhibiting some artifacts, demonstrate promising generation quality. The authors conduct an extensive analysis of design choices, including different pose embeddings and loss functions, showcasing a thorough understanding of the proposed system.

However, the quantitative evaluation is limited by the lack of established metrics for assessing the quality of generated images, particularly in tasks involving pose manipulation. While SSIM and Inception Score are used, their relevance is questioned given the inherent challenges of evaluating perceptual quality and the potential for high SSIM scores despite visual artifacts [10, 17]. The user study, while attempted, is preliminary and its conclusions are not particularly strong. 

Several related works could strengthen the context of the contribution. The iterative refinement aspect of the architecture aligns with recent advances in cascaded networks for tasks like pose estimation. Including citations such as [2, 3] would acknowledge this connection. Furthermore, the statement on the benefits of residual blocks (line 110) lacks sufficient detail. Specifying *how* residual blocks improve performance would be beneficial. 

An interesting experiment, not performed by the authors, would be to evaluate a network utilizing the Stage 2 architecture directly, taking the Stage 1 inputs and training it adversarially. Exploring whether the intermediate fully connected layer in Stage 1 contributes to the observed blurring would provide valuable insights.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed task and approach represent a significant contribution to the field of image generation.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The architecture is well-motivated and demonstrates promising results.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and easy to understand.
*   **Extensive Evaluation:** The authors thoroughly investigate and analyze various design choices.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Weak Quantitative Evaluation:** The lack of suitable metrics limits the objective assessment of the results.
*   **Limited User Study:** The preliminary nature of the user study doesn't strongly support the claims.



**Overall Rating:** Accept. 

Despite the limitations in quantitative evaluation, the novelty and potential of the proposed approach are substantial. The clear presentation and thorough analysis make this a valuable contribution to the field. While a more robust human evaluation would be beneficial, it is not a prerequisite for acceptance at this stage. I urge the authors to address the points regarding related work and the elaboration of residual block benefits in a revision.
