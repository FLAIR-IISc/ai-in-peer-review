

This paper proposes a GAN-based framework for synthesizing novel-view person images conditioned on skeleton keypoints. While the application—skeleton-guided image synthesis—is relevant, the technical approach primarily leverages established components (e.g., standard GAN architectures, U-Net encoders), resulting in a contribution that is application-focused rather than methodologically novel.  

The work’s strengths lie in its clear exposition and the demonstration of a functional pipeline. The methodology is well-structured and easy to follow, and the generated images exhibit *some* level of visual plausibility in specific cases, suggesting the core idea has merit.  

However, the paper falls short in critical areas essential for an application-oriented contribution at a venue like NeurIPS. First, the absence of quantitative evaluation is a major deficiency. For a task centered on realistic image synthesis, the authors provide no metrics (e.g., FID, LPIPS, or human subject studies) to objectively assess quality. Specifically, the lack of user studies to measure how frequently generated images fool human observers—crucial for evaluating "realism" in this context—is a significant oversight. Without such benchmarks, claims about the system’s effectiveness remain unsubstantiated.  

Second, the qualitative results are unconvincing. As noted in the draft, Figure 1 (and supplementary figures) present generated images at insufficient resolution for meaningful visual assessment, and even at full size, the outputs contain persistent artifacts: distorted limbs, unnatural textures, and misaligned body parts relative to the target skeletons. For instance, in Figure 1b, the generated arm appears disconnected from the body, and in Figure 4, the clothing lacks plausible geometry. These errors undermine the assertion that results are "realistic-looking" or "close to real images." The supplementary material does not address these issues, and the overall visual quality fails to meet expectations for a publication venue focused on high-impact technical contributions.  

The authors’ rebuttal acknowledges these concerns but fails to resolve them adequately. While they added limited quantitative metrics (e.g., FID scores for a subset of results), these do not correlate with the qualitative failures observed. The FID values remain high (e.g., 25.8 on the test set), and the rebuttal does not explain why such scores coexist with visible artifacts. More critically, no user studies were conducted to validate perceptual realism, leaving the core evaluation gap unaddressed. As noted by multiple reviewers, the persistent qualitative shortcomings—evident in all presented results—cannot be overlooked.  

Given NeurIPS’ emphasis on rigorous empirical validation and technical significance, the current work lacks the necessary depth to justify publication. The proposed system represents a promising *foundation* for skeleton-guided synthesis, but the results are not yet robust or compelling enough to claim practical viability. The authors would need to substantially improve both quantitative evaluation (including human studies) and visual quality (e.g., via architectural refinements or training adjustments) to meet the conference’s standards.  

In summary: The idea is interesting and the writing is clear, but the work is not sufficiently mature. The absence of rigorous evaluation and the persistent visual artifacts prevent a positive recommendation. The paper would benefit from significant revision—addressing quantitative gaps and demonstrating meaningful quality improvements—before reconsideration for NeurIPS. As presented, it does not meet the bar for acceptance.
