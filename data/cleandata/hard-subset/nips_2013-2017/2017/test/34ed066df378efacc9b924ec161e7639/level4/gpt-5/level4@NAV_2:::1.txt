Summary:
The paper presents a method for synthesizing person images by combining the appearance from a reference image with a specified target pose encoded as keypoint heatmaps. The approach uses a two-stage, coarse-to-fine pipeline: Stage I integrates the pose and appearance via a U-Net-like model trained with a masked L1 loss to emphasize the person over background, producing a structurally correct but blurry result; Stage II refines this output using an adversarially trained U-Net-like generator that predicts a residual (difference) map to enhance high-frequency details. Experiments on Market-1501 (128×64) and DeepFashion (256×256) demonstrate visually convincing results and a thorough ablation over pose embeddings, loss design, and the two-stage decomposition.

Strengths:
+ Novel problem setting: conditioning person image synthesis on both a reference image and an explicit pose representation.
+ Clear two-stage coarse-to-fine design with a residual/difference-map refinement in Stage II.
+ Well-written and easy to follow.
+ Results, while not artifact-free, are compelling and show strong qualitative improvements.
+ Extensive ablations on pose encoding, loss functions (including the pose mask), and one- vs two-stage training.

Weaknesses:
- The quantitative evaluation remains limited, which is understandable given the lack of robust metrics for this task, but nonetheless reduces the strength of the empirical claims.

Comments:
Given the iterative refinement nature of the pipeline, it would be helpful to cite related cascaded/iterative refinement works, e.g., [a,b]:
[a] J. Carreira, P. Agrawal, K. Fragkiadaki, and J. Malik. Human pose estimation with iterative error feedback. CVPR, 2016.
[b] A. Newell, K. Yang, and J. Deng. Stacked hourglass networks for human pose estimation. ECCV, 2016.
- Line 110: “we find that using residual blocks as basic component improves the generation performance.” In what respect (e.g., training stability, sharper details, better pose fidelity, convergence speed)? Please expand.
The ablations are generally thorough, but I am curious how using the Stage II architecture directly with Stage I inputs and adversarial training would fare. Is the blurring observed with “Stage I + GAN” primarily due to the fully connected layer in Stage I, or due to other factors (e.g., loss balance, lack of residual prediction)?

Overall rating:
On the basis of the novelty of the task and the proposed approach, I confidently recommend acceptance.

Rebuttal:
I appreciate the inclusion of a user study, but given its preliminary nature I do not find it persuasive and am discounting it in my score. I encourage the authors to include a more substantial human evaluation in a revision. My positive assessment of the contribution otherwise stands.
