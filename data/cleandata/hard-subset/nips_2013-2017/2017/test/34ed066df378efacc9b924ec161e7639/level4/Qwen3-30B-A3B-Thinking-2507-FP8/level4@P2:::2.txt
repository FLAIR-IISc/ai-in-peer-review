

This paper introduces a novel approach for synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses given a reference image of that person and a target pose. The proposed Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) employs a two-stage adversarial framework that effectively addresses the challenging task of pose transfer. In the first stage, a U-Net-like network integrates the reference image and target pose (represented as heatmaps) to produce a coarse image capturing global body structure. The second stage refines this coarse result using a conditional GAN that predicts a difference map between the initial output and target image, rather than generating the final image directly. The authors propose a pose mask loss to focus generation on human body regions rather than background, which significantly improves results.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) introducing the novel task of conditioning image generation on both reference image and target pose, (2) proposing effective pose embedding techniques and a pose mask loss to handle background interference, and (3) developing a two-stage framework that separates global structure learning from detail refinement.

Strengths of the paper include:
- Clear problem formulation and effective division of the pose transfer task into two manageable stages
- Novel application of the difference map approach, which enables faster convergence and better detail generation
- Thoughtful design of the pose mask loss, which effectively addresses background interference
- Comprehensive evaluation on two distinct datasets (DeepFashion and Market-1501) with both quantitative and qualitative results
- Detailed ablation studies that demonstrate the impact of different pose embeddings and loss functions

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a thorough comparison of alternative approaches. The two-stage framework is well-motivated and effectively addresses the challenge of simultaneously learning pose structure and appearance details.

However, the paper has several limitations that need to be addressed:

1. Quality limitations: While the generated images are visually consistent and pose-accurate, they still contain visible artifacts and lack sufficient high-frequency details. For instance, Figure 4 (ID 346) shows black dots in the generated clothing patterns, and the second generator fails to fully recover fine textures despite its refinement stage. The generated images remain noticeably less detailed than the target images, particularly in complex clothing patterns and facial details.

2. Originality and significance: The core approach of using conditional GANs with a two-stage architecture for image generation is not entirely novel. Similar stacked GAN approaches have been proposed in prior work, such as StackGAN (Zhang et al., 2016) and Stacked GANs (Huang et al., 2016). While the paper applies these concepts to a specific problem (pose-guided person generation), it doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how this application differs significantly from existing GAN frameworks. The significance of the work would be stronger if it could demonstrate broader applicability beyond human pose transfer.

3. Technical novelty: The pose embedding method using heatmaps is reasonable but not particularly innovative compared to existing techniques for incorporating pose information in image generation. The pose mask loss is useful but appears to be a straightforward adaptation of techniques used in other segmentation and image translation tasks.

4. Evaluation limitations: While the paper includes a user study, the results don't strongly demonstrate superiority over existing methods. The quantitative metrics (SSIM and Inception Score) don't fully capture the visual quality improvements observed in Figure 4. The comparison with Zhao et al. (2017) is limited, and the paper doesn't sufficiently address how the proposed method compares to more recent approaches in the literature.

The paper is well-written and addresses an interesting application in computer vision. The two-stage framework is logically sound and the pose mask loss is a practical contribution to the field. However, the technical novelty is somewhat limited, and the quality of the generated images requires further improvement to be truly compelling.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Provide more convincing evidence of the method's superiority over existing approaches, particularly in terms of visual quality
2. More clearly articulate how this work differs from and advances beyond previous GAN-based image generation methods
3. Address the quality limitations by exploring additional refinement techniques or architectural modifications
4. Consider comparing with more recent pose-guided generation methods beyond Zhao et al. (2017)

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the specific problem of pose-guided person image generation, but its broader significance to the machine learning community is somewhat limited by the incremental nature of the technical approach. While the results are promising for the specific application domain, the method doesn't clearly demonstrate how it would generalize to other vision or machine learning problems beyond this narrow task.

Overall, the paper is technically sound and well-executed for its specific task, but the novelty and significance are somewhat limited. The quality of the generated images needs further improvement to be compelling for real-world applications. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the novelty argument and address the quality limitations.
