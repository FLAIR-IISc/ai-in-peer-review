This paper introduces a novel approach to image generation, specifically creating images of individuals exhibiting a desired pose by transferring appearance from a source image and utilizing pose information encoded in heatmaps.

The work’s strengths lie in its novelty – the proposed task itself is a valuable contribution – and the innovative two-stage, coarse-to-fine architecture employed to address it. The paper is also exceptionally well-written and presents promising, albeit artifact-prone, results. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate a strong commitment to rigorous analysis through an extensive evaluation of their design choices.

A primary limitation is the challenge of quantitative evaluation, stemming from the lack of established metrics for this particular task. While this is acknowledged and understandable, it does present a difficulty in comprehensively assessing performance.

Regarding related work, I suggest the authors consider including recent advancements in iterative refinement via cascaded networks, such as [a] J. Carreira, P. Agrawal, K. Fragkiadaki, and J. Malik. H man pose estimation with iterative error feedback. In IEEE Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition (CVPR), 2016, and [b] A. Newell, K. Yang, and J. Deng. Stacked hourglass networks for human pose estimation. In European Conference on Computer Vision (ECCV), 2016. Additionally, line 110 states “we find that using residual blocks as basic component improves the generation performance,” but lacks specific detail regarding *how* performance is improved. Elaboration on this point would be beneficial. While the evaluation is thorough, I am curious about the potential performance of applying the Stage 2 network, trained adversarially, to the Stage 1 inputs. Investigating whether the blurring seen in the Stage 1 + GAN configuration is linked to the intermediate fully connected layer would also be insightful.

Overall, I recommend acceptance of this paper. The novelty of both the problem and the proposed solution are significant and outweigh the current limitations. 

Concerning the rebuttal and the included user study, while I appreciate the authors’ efforts, I find the preliminary nature of the study unconvincing and have therefore not factored it into my final assessment. I strongly encourage the authors to conduct a more substantive human evaluation as part of their revision. However, my positive evaluation of the core work remains unchanged.
