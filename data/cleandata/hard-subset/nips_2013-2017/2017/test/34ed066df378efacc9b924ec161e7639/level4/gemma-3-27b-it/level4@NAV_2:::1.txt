This paper proposes a novel Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG) for synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses, given a reference image and a target pose. The framework leverages pose information explicitly through a two-stage process: pose integration and image refinement. Stage-I uses a U-Net-like network to generate a coarse image based on the input image and target pose, while Stage-II refines this result with an adversarial U-Net-like generator. The authors demonstrate strong qualitative results on both re-identification and fashion photo datasets.

This work addresses an interesting and challenging problem in image generation – controlling pose in a realistic manner. The proposed two-stage approach, dividing the problem into global structure and appearance detail refinement, is a sensible strategy. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The extensive experimentation, including ablation studies of pose embedding methods and loss functions, demonstrates a thorough investigation of the design choices. The generated images, while not perfect, show promising results with convincing details, particularly in the refined outputs.

However, the quantitative evaluation lacks robustness. While the authors acknowledge the difficulty in defining appropriate metrics, the reported SSIM and Inception Score don't fully capture the perceptual quality of the generated images. It would be beneficial to explore alternative or complementary metrics, or perhaps more detailed perceptual studies. Additionally, the use of a pose mask to mitigate background influence is a clever idea, but its impact could be further substantiated with more detailed analysis.

Several related works could be considered for inclusion. For instance, iterative refinement techniques in pose estimation, such as those presented in Carreira et al. (2016) and Newell et al. (2016), share similarities with the two-stage approach and might offer valuable insights. Specifically, the authors mention a divide-and-conquer approach similar to others [35, 9, 3, 19], but a more detailed comparison with these would benefit the paper. On line 110, elaborating on *how* residual blocks improve performance would strengthen the explanation. Furthermore, exploring the performance of the Stage-II network directly applied to Stage-I inputs with adversarial training, and investigating the impact of the fully connected layer on blurring, would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the model’s behavior. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel task and approach to pose-guided person image generation.
*   Well-designed two-stage architecture with clear rationale.
*   Thorough experimentation with ablation studies.
*   Promising qualitative results demonstrating the potential of the method.
*   Clear and well-written paper.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak quantitative evaluation.
*   Limited discussion of related work in iterative refinement.
*   Lack of exploration of alternative architectural choices (e.g., directly applying Stage-II network to Stage-I inputs).

Despite the limitations in quantitative evaluation, the novelty and potential of the proposed approach outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance of this paper.

While the user study provides some additional insight, its preliminary nature and limited scope do not significantly impact my overall assessment. The authors should consider a more substantial human evaluation in future work. However, my positive evaluation of the paper remains unchanged.
