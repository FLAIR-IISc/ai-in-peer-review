This paper proposes a two-stage generative model for synthesizing images of a person in a novel pose. The model is conditioned on a source image of the person and a target pose, which is represented by a set of keypoints. The framework combines established techniques, including U-Net architectures and Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). As such, the primary contribution lies in the novel application and the specific architecture designed for this task, rather than in fundamental methodological advances.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses an interesting and challenging problem: synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses, which has clear applications in areas like data augmentation and content creation.
*   The manuscript is well-written and easy to follow.
*   The proposed method demonstrates the ability to generate images that capture the overall structure and appearance of the target, achieving a degree of realism.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weakness of the paper lies in the evaluation of the generated results, both quantitatively and qualitatively. Given that the work is application-focused, the quality of the output is a critical measure of its success.

*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** The paper lacks a rigorous quantitative evaluation of realism. While metrics like SSIM are included, they are known to correlate poorly with perceptual quality. For a task like this, a user study would be essential to assess how convincing the generated images are to human observers. Measuring the "fooling rate" is a standard practice for evaluating high-quality generative models and would provide a much stronger basis for the paper's claims of generating high-quality images.

*   **Qualitative Results:** The qualitative results, while demonstrating the basic functionality of the model, are not yet compelling. The images presented in the figures (e.g., Figure 1 and others) are often low-resolution, which makes a detailed assessment difficult. Even at this resolution, significant visual artifacts and discrepancies are apparent when compared to the ground truth targets. Details in clothing, faces, and limbs are often blurry or distorted. While the general pose and color are transferred correctly, the results do not yet reach a level of photorealism that would support the claim of generating "high-quality" or "convincing" images. Although the approach is promising, the current results suggest the work is still in a preliminary stage.

**Post-rebuttal comments:**
After reviewing the author rebuttal, my primary concerns remain. The authors' response attempts to address the issues raised regarding the evaluation, but the new evidence provided is not sufficient to alleviate the core weaknesses. As also noted by other reviewers, the lack of compelling quantitative and qualitative results is a significant issue for a paper of this nature. The rebuttal does not adequately resolve these concerns. Therefore, I maintain my assessment that the paper, in its current state, is not yet ready for publication at a top-tier conference like NIPS.
