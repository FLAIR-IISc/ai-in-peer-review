Summary
This paper addresses pose-guided person image generation: given a reference image of a person and a target pose (encoded as keypoint heatmaps), synthesize a new image of the same person in the target pose. The proposed PG2 framework proceeds in two stages: (1) a U-Net–like model integrates the reference image and the pose to produce a coarse image constrained by the target body structure and basic appearance, trained with a masked L1 loss that emphasizes the human foreground; (2) a conditional adversarial refinement network predicts a residual “difference map” to add high-frequency details and correct errors, using a pairwise discriminator that judges (generated image, condition image) versus (target image, condition image). The paper evaluates on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64), compares pose embeddings (coordinate vs heatmap vs direct concatenation), examines the proposed pose mask loss, contrasts two-stage versus one-stage cGAN training, and reports SSIM/IS (plus mask-SSIM/mask-IS) and an AMT user study. It also compares against a closely related multi-view person generation method [36].

Relation to prior work
The approach builds on established generative techniques: U-Nets with skip connections for conditional synthesis, adversarial training (DCGAN), and conditioning on side information (e.g., attributes, text, or pose). It is related to view synthesis and pose-conditioned generation (e.g., [22–24, 34–36]) and image-to-image translation with cGANs [9], but differs in using explicit pose keypoints as control and a two-stage strategy where the second stage predicts a residual map. The masked L1 loss is conceptually aligned with foreground-focused objectives used in segmentation/conditional synthesis.

Strengths
- Clear task formulation and a sensible two-stage “structure first, details later” design. Predicting residuals in stage II is a practical choice that simplifies refinement and appears to stabilize training.
- Effective use of pose heatmaps as input, and a foreground-weighted (masked) L1 loss to reduce background influence. The pairwise conditional discriminator is appropriate to prevent trivial copying of the condition image.
- Comprehensive ablations: pose embedding variants; standard L1 vs masked L1; one-stage vs two-stage training. Experiments on two datasets with different resolutions and scene complexity.
- Inclusion of a user study and comparisons to a related multi-view method [36].

Weaknesses
- Visual quality is inconsistent, especially on Market-1501; many generated images remain blurry, with background artifacts and noticeable pose/appearance errors. Figures are often small and make qualitative assessment difficult; zoomed-in crops of key regions (faces, hands, clothing details) would help.
- Evaluation does not directly assess critical aspects of this application:
  • Identity preservation: There is no quantitative test of whether the generated image retains the subject’s identity (e.g., using a re-identification network or face/ID classifier).
  • Pose fidelity: No metric to quantify adherence to the target pose (e.g., running a pose estimator on generated images and measuring PCK/OKS against the target keypoints).
  • Perceptual realism: SSIM/IS (even masked variants) are known to correlate weakly with human realism judgments; the reported user confusion rates are modest, and the protocol (1 s exposure) may understate visible artifacts.
- Baselines are limited. Beyond one-stage adversarial training and [36], stronger conditional baselines should be included, such as pix2pix-style U-Net cGANs conditioned on pose heatmaps and perceptual losses. A direct comparison to a single-stage cGAN with VGG/perceptual loss would clarify the added value of the two-stage residual refinement.
- The approach is deterministic and does not model uncertainty or generate diverse plausible appearances for ambiguous/occluded regions.
- Background handling remains ad hoc; while the pose mask helps, the method sometimes transfers or invents inconsistent background content, which affects realism and user judgments.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: Methodologically sound and carefully engineered; the two-stage design and residual prediction are reasonable. However, the empirical validation lacks key task-specific metrics (identity and pose accuracy) and stronger baselines, limiting the strength of the claims.
- Clarity: Generally well organized and readable. Architectural details and training procedures are described clearly. Figures should be larger, with detail crops; the failure case analysis is brief and could be more systematic.
- Originality: Moderate. The components (U-Net, conditional GAN, residual refinement, pose heatmaps) are known; the novelty lies in their combination for pose-guided person generation with a foreground-weighted loss and pairwise discriminator. The contribution is more application-focused than algorithmically novel.
- Significance: Potentially useful for content creation and data augmentation, but practical impact hinges on realism and identity/pose fidelity, which are not convincingly demonstrated. As a baseline for pose-guided synthesis, it is promising, but current results and evaluation fall short of a strong NIPS contribution.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative identity preservation: evaluate with a re-id model or face recognition on generated vs. target/condition, report verification/CMC metrics.
- Add pose fidelity metrics: run a pose estimator on generated images and compare to target keypoints (PCK/OKS).
- Report more perceptual metrics (e.g., FID) and a stronger user study (2AFC pairwise comparisons, unlimited or longer viewing times) with sufficient sample size and confidence intervals.
- Include stronger baselines: pix2pix-style single-stage cGAN conditioned on pose heatmaps, with and without perceptual loss; a feature-matching loss or VGG-based perceptual loss for stage II.
- Improve qualitative presentation: larger images, zoomed crops, systematic failure mode categorization (occlusions, rare poses, complex textures).
- Explore better background handling (explicit segmentation/foreground compositing) and, if feasible, stochasticity for plausible diversity in ambiguous regions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, timely problem setting with intuitive controllability via pose.
- Sensible architecture with thorough ablations; evidence of improvement over related view-synthesis baselines.
- Method likely to stimulate further work in pose-conditioned person synthesis.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty beyond a careful integration of known components.
- Visual quality still far from photorealistic on challenging data; figures do not convincingly support claims.
- Missing key task-specific quantitative evaluations and stronger baselines; current metrics are insufficient to substantiate realism and identity/pose fidelity.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents a promising and well-motivated approach, but the current experimental evidence and qualitative results do not yet meet the bar for NIPS. Strengthening the evaluation with identity and pose fidelity metrics, stronger baselines, improved qualitative analysis, and more robust human studies would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
