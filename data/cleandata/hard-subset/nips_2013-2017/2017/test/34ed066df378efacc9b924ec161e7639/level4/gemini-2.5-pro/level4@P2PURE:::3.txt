This paper proposes a GAN-based system for synthesizing novel-view images of a person, conditioned on a target skeleton represented as keypoints. The architecture is primarily composed of existing components, such as DCGANs and U-Nets, positioning the main contribution in its application to this specific computer vision task rather than in methodological novelty.

Pros:
*   The core idea is interesting, the paper is clearly written, and the experiments demonstrate the ability to synthesize images with a degree of realism.

Cons:
*   For an application-oriented paper, the quality of the results is paramount. My primary concern is that the work is lacking in this area. Specifically:
    *   **Lack of quantitative evaluation:** The paper does not provide any quantitative metrics to assess the quality of the generated images. For example, a user study could have been conducted to measure how frequently the synthesized images are mistaken for real ones.
    *   **Unconvincing qualitative results:** The generated images are not visually compelling. In Figure 1, the images are too small for detailed inspection, yet numerous visual errors are still noticeable when comparing them to the target images. This makes it difficult to conclude that the results are high-quality or close to photorealistic. This issue persists across nearly all results, including those in the supplementary material. While I believe the authors are on a promising research track, the work seems premature for publication in its current state.

After rebuttal:
Having read the authors' feedback, my concerns remain. As noted by other reviewers, significant issues persist with both the quantitative and qualitative results—both of which are critical for an application-focused paper. While the rebuttal shows some attempt to address these points, the new material does not resolve these core concerns to a satisfactory degree. I believe the work is not yet ready for presentation at a venue like NIPS.
