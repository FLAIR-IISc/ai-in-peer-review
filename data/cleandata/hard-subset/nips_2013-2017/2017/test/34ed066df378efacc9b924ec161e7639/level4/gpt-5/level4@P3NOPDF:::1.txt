Summary:
The paper introduces an architecture for synthesizing images of a person that preserve the appearance from a source image while matching a target pose specified via keypoint heatmaps. The method uses a novel two-stage, coarse-to-fine pipeline that first generates a rough result and then refines it.

Strengths:
- Novel and timely task: synthesizing person images conditioned on a target pose while maintaining identity and appearance.
- A well-motivated, novel two-stage coarse-to-fine architecture.
- Clear, well-written presentation.
- Results are promising despite visible artifacts.
- Extensive evaluation of design choices and ablations.

Weaknesses:
- Quantitative evaluation is limited. This is understandable given the lack of established metrics for this task, so I do not view it as a major weakness.

Comments and questions:
- Related work: Because the approach relies on iterative refinement, it would strengthen the paper to discuss connections to cascaded/iterative architectures, e.g.,
  [a] J. Carreira, P. Agrawal, K. Fragkiadaki, and J. Malik. Human pose estimation with iterative error feedback. CVPR 2016.
  [b] A. Newell, K. Yang, and J. Deng. Stacked hourglass networks for human pose estimation. ECCV 2016.
  Highlight similarities and differences in how errors are corrected across stages and how supervision is structured.

- Line 110: “we find that using residual blocks as the basic component improves the generation performance.” Please elaborate. In what specific ways does performance improve? It would be helpful to:
  - Provide a controlled ablation comparing residual blocks vs. plain convolutional blocks at matched depth/parameter count.
  - Report quantitative differences (e.g., PSNR/SSIM or perceptual metrics) and illustrate qualitative improvements (e.g., sharper textures, fewer artifacts).
  - Comment on training dynamics (stability, convergence speed) and whether residual connections mitigate vanishing gradients or facilitate deeper refiners.

- Architecture/evaluation suggestion: I am curious how using the Stage 2 (refinement) network directly with the inputs intended for Stage 1, trained adversarially, would perform. This could test whether the division of labor across stages is essential or whether a single, more expressive refiner suffices. Relatedly, is the blurring observed with Stage 1 + GAN attributable to the intermediate fully connected layer? An experiment that removes or replaces this layer (e.g., with fully convolutional features) could isolate its effect on sharpness.

- Evaluation: Although I am not penalizing the paper for limited metrics, consider adding proxy measures to complement the qualitative results:
  - Pose consistency: accuracy or PCK under a pretrained pose estimator applied to generated images.
  - Perceptual similarity (e.g., LPIPS) to capture alignment with the target appearance in a learned feature space.
  - A more substantial user study with a clear protocol, adequate sample size, and statistical analysis.

Overall assessment and rating:
This work addresses a novel and impactful problem with a thoughtfully designed two-stage architecture and promising results. The paper is well written and includes meaningful ablations. Despite limitations in quantitative evaluation, I find the contribution solid and recommend acceptance. Confidence: high.

Rebuttal/author response update:
I appreciate the attempt at a user study; however, given its preliminary nature, I do not find it convincing and therefore do not weigh it in my rating. I encourage the authors to include a more rigorous human evaluation in the final version. My positive assessment of the paper remains unchanged.
