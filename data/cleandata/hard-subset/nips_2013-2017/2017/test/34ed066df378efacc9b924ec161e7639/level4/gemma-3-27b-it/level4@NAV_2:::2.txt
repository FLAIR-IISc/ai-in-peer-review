The paper proposes a novel Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) for synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses, conditioned on a reference image and a target pose. The framework employs a two-stage adversarial training architecture: a U-Net-like generator initially creates a coarse image integrating pose information, followed by a second U-Net-like generator refining this result by predicting a residual image. The method is evaluated on both the 128x64 Market-1501 re-identification dataset and the 256x256 DeepFashion dataset.

This work builds upon the growing body of research in generative image modeling, particularly utilizing Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) [6] for sharp image generation, as demonstrated in prior work [21, 5, 1].  It extends conditional image generation, following lines of research that condition on category labels [32, 18], attributes [32], text [24, 22, 23], or other images [34, 33, 11, 8].  However, it distinguishes itself by explicitly leveraging pose information – represented as keypoints – to guide the generation process, offering more direct control than previous approaches utilizing less expressive conditioning signals. The two-stage approach, dividing the problem into global structure learning and detail refinement, echoes similar strategies seen in other generative modeling contexts [35, 9, 3, 19].

The strengths of the paper lie in its novel task formulation and the thoughtful design of the network architecture.  The proposed task of pose-conditioned person image generation has practical applications in areas like movie making and training data augmentation for pose estimation. The divide-and-conquer strategy, combined with the novel pose mask loss, demonstrates a clear understanding of the challenges involved in this task.  Generating a difference map in the second stage is a clever technique that facilitates faster convergence and detail refinement.  The empirical evaluation on two datasets shows promising results.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. While the results are promising, the generated images still exhibit visible artifacts, particularly in higher-resolution outputs, limiting their immediate practical applicability. The improvement gained by the two-stage approach, while apparent in qualitative results, could be more clearly demonstrated with a more comprehensive quantitative analysis.  The originality, while present in the specific combination of techniques and the task formulation, could be perceived as incremental, as the work builds heavily upon existing GAN architectures and conditioning techniques.  Similar approaches to conditional image generation and stacked GANs exist [36, 34, 33, 11, 8, 36, 36], though they don't specifically address pose-guided person image generation in the same way.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a novel and potentially useful task – pose-guided person image generation.
*   Presents a technically sound approach with a well-motivated two-stage architecture and a novel pose mask loss.
*   Demonstrates promising results on two datasets, suggesting the feasibility of the approach.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation of the method and experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Generated images still suffer from visible artifacts and require further quality improvement.
*   The originality, while present, may be viewed as incremental within the broader field of GAN research.
*   Quantitative results could be strengthened to provide more conclusive evidence of the method's effectiveness.



Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field. The paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated approach to a novel problem. While the generated image quality is not yet perfect, the results are promising and the proposed method has the potential to be further developed. I recommend acceptance.
