Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces PG2, a two-stage framework for pose-guided person image generation. Given a reference image of a person (appearance) and a target pose (18 keypoint heatmaps), Stage I uses a U-Net–like encoder-decoder with skip connections to synthesize a coarse image matching the target pose. A “pose mask” L1 loss upweights human pixels to reduce background mismatch between condition and target images. Stage II refines the coarse output using a conditional DCGAN that predicts a residual (difference) map, with a pairwise discriminator D(IA, I) and an additional masked L1 term to stabilize training. Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) show visually plausible pose transfer, and the paper reports ablations on pose embedding (coordinates vs. heatmaps), loss (L1 vs. masked), and training strategy (one-stage vs. two-stage). The authors also propose masked SSIM/IS and conduct a small user study.

The work is situated among conditional generation (e.g., conditional GANs, pix2pix, StackGAN), view synthesis and face rotation, and recent human pose estimation methods used here to obtain keypoints. The explicit use of keypoint heatmaps as a pose control signal aligns with prior conditional generation but provides more direct control than category/attribute/text conditioning. The coarse-to-fine design resonates with cascaded/refinement networks and multi-stage architectures that have been effective in pose estimation (e.g., iterative error feedback, stacked hourglass), super-resolution, and stacked GANs.

Strengths:
- Novel and useful task: explicit control of human pose conditioned on identity, with clear applications in content creation and data augmentation.
- Well-motivated two-stage, coarse-to-fine design; the residual/difference-map formulation in Stage II is elegant and practical, and the pairwise discriminator is appropriate for conditional refinement.
- Pose masked L1 is a simple, effective idea to mitigate background mismatch.
- Thorough ablations on pose embedding, loss, and training strategy; qualitative results are compelling on DeepFashion and reasonable given the difficulty of Market-1501.
- The paper is generally clear and well organized.

Weaknesses:
- Quantitative evaluation remains limited. SSIM/IS (even with masking) are imperfect for generation quality; the user study is small and not conclusive.
- Identity preservation is not directly measured, despite being central to the task.
- Some design choices are under-justified (e.g., the effect size of residual blocks; the impact of the fully connected bottleneck in Stage I).
- Background transfer and artifacts are visible in challenging cases; the method depends on pose detector quality but robustness is not analyzed.

Quality:
The methodology is technically sound and thoughtfully engineered. Stage II’s residual learning and pairwise discriminator are reasonable and likely contribute to stability and detail. The ablations on pose embedding convincingly support using spatial heatmaps. However, several analyses would strengthen the empirical claims:
- Identity preservation: evaluate with a re-identification network by measuring whether generated images remain close to the same identity (e.g., top-k retrieval, cosine similarity in a re-id embedding).
- Pose accuracy: run a pose estimator on generated images and compare to target keypoints (PCK or similar), quantifying pose transfer fidelity.
- Effect of architecture choices: report the impact of residual blocks and the fully connected layer in Stage I (e.g., removing the FC layer, as done in Stage II). Do residual blocks improve SSIM/IS or user preference? Does the FC layer correlate with blur?
- Metrics: consider reporting FID (on person crops) in addition to SSIM/IS; although imperfect, it is more aligned with human perception than IS/SSIM.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written. Suggested clarifications:
- “Using residual blocks improves performance”: please quantify on both datasets and specify which metrics improved.
- Discuss the rationale for including an FC layer in Stage I but not Stage II, and whether the FC layer contributes to blurring or background leakage.
- Provide sensitivity to λ with main-text plots (not only in the supplement) and guidance for setting it across datasets.

Originality:
The task formulation—controlling person generation via explicit pose keypoints given a reference appearance—is timely and novel relative to prior view synthesis and attribute-conditioned generation. The two-stage design with a residual refinement GAN and a pose-masked loss is a thoughtful combination of existing ideas adapted to a new problem. Related cascaded refinement work in other domains (e.g., stacked/cascaded networks in pose estimation, stacked GANs) is appropriately connected; adding a brief discussion to emphasize the broader “iterative refinement via cascades” perspective would be helpful.

Significance:
The ability to manipulate pose while preserving identity is important for content creation and could benefit pose estimation and re-id via data augmentation. The qualitative gains over single-stage baselines are substantial, especially on DeepFashion. With stronger quantitative and human evaluations, the impact would be higher. The approach should be a useful baseline for subsequent work on pose-conditioned generation.

Specific comments and questions:
- Please report: (1) G1 without the FC bottleneck; (2) G2 used as a single-stage generator with the same inputs as G1 (no FC), trained adversarially + masked L1. This isolates whether the Stage I FC contributes to blur and whether architectural differences (not just staging) explain the one-stage vs. two-stage gap.
- Robustness: How sensitive is PG2 to keypoint noise? Consider adding experiments perturbing keypoints or using alternative pose estimators.
- Background handling: The masked loss helps, but can you mitigate background bleeding via segmentations or by conditioning on a background mask? A brief analysis would be valuable.
- User study: A pairwise A/B test (two-alternative forced choice) comparing variants (e.g., G1 vs. G1+D vs. G1+G2+D) and a longer viewing time would be more informative. Also consider asking annotators to rate identity consistency and pose correctness separately.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and relevant problem; clear technical contribution tailored to it.
- Strong qualitative results; consistent ablations support design choices.
- Clear writing and reproducible architecture.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited, somewhat misaligned quantitative metrics and a preliminary user study.
- Missing analyses on identity preservation and pose accuracy.
- Some architectural choices insufficiently justified.

Overall recommendation:
Accept. The paper addresses a timely, well-scoped problem with a thoughtful, effective two-stage approach and provides strong qualitative evidence. With additional quantitative evaluations and minor clarifications, it would make a solid NIPS contribution.

Post-rebuttal comments:
I appreciate the attempt at a user study; however, in its current form it is too preliminary to be persuasive, and I largely discount it in my assessment. I encourage the authors to include a more substantial human evaluation (e.g., A/B tests with identity and pose judgments) and to add quantitative identity/pose metrics as suggested. My positive recommendation stands given the novelty and technical quality of the work.
