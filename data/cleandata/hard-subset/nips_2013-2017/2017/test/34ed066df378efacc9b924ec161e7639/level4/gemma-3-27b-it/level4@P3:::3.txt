This paper develops a GAN-based system for generating novel-view person images guided by a target pose, represented as keypoints. The system largely utilizes established techniques such as DCGANs and U-Nets, positioning the primary contribution within a specific computer vision application.

The core idea is interesting, and the paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The empirical experiments demonstrate a capacity to synthesize images that appear reasonably realistic at a cursory glance. However, several weaknesses prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance.

The most significant concern is the lack of rigorous quantitative evaluation. While visual assessment is valuable, it's subjective. A user study evaluating the realism of generated images – measuring how often they can fool human observers – would significantly strengthen the claims. Related work in image generation routinely includes such evaluations (e.g., [6, 21]). Furthermore, the provided empirical results are not compelling. Figure 1, and many subsequent figures including those in the supplementary material, present images that are too small for detailed inspection. Even at this scale, noticeable visual artifacts are present when comparing generated images to the targets. This makes it difficult to confidently assert that the results are of high quality or convincingly realistic. While the authors are clearly on a promising track, declaring a strong result at this stage feels premature.

The paper proposes a two-stage approach, motivated by the difficulty of jointly modeling pose and appearance. This is a reasonable strategy, and the exploration of different pose embedding techniques and the introduction of a pose mask loss are worthwhile contributions. However, the gains achieved by these modifications are not dramatically demonstrated, especially given the visual limitations mentioned above. 

Regarding related work, the authors adequately cover relevant literature on generative models and conditional image generation. However, a more detailed discussion of recent advances in pose-conditioned image manipulation, beyond those explicitly cited, would be beneficial. For example, the work of Kanazawa et al. on 3D face reconstruction and manipulation [1] uses similar pose-conditioning principles.

**Strengths:**

*   Clear and well-written paper.
*   Interesting and potentially impactful application of GANs.
*   Reasonable two-stage approach to address a challenging problem.
*   Exploration of different pose embedding methods and a novel pose mask loss.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of quantitative evaluation. A user study is strongly recommended.
*   Visual results are not sufficiently compelling due to small image size and noticeable artifacts.
*   The improvements offered by the proposed methods (pose mask loss, two-stage approach) are not dramatically demonstrated.
*   Related work could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of pose-conditioned image manipulation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a challenging and potentially useful problem – generating person images in arbitrary poses.
*   The proposed approach is well-motivated and clearly explained.
*   The authors explore several design choices and provide some empirical validation (though limited).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of quantitative evaluation is a significant weakness.
*   The visual results are not sufficiently convincing to demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
*   The contribution, while sound, is incremental and builds heavily on existing techniques.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the idea is interesting and the paper is well-written, the lack of quantitative evaluation and the limitations of the visual results prevent me from recommending acceptance to a highly competitive conference like NIPS. Addressing these issues – particularly by including a user study and presenting higher-resolution, more visually appealing results – would significantly strengthen the paper.
