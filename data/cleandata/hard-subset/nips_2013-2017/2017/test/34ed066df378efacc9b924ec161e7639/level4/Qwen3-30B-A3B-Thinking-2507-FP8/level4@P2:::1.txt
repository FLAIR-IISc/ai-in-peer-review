

This paper introduces a novel task of person image generation conditioned on both a reference image and a target pose, along with a two-stage architecture to address this challenging problem. The authors propose a Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) that first integrates pose information with the reference image to generate a coarse structural representation (Stage I), then refines this result with an adversarial network to produce high-quality details (Stage II). A key innovation is the pose mask loss that focuses generation on human body regions while suppressing background influence. The work is evaluated extensively on two datasets (Market-1501 and DeepFashion), with ablation studies examining pose embedding methods, loss functions, and the two-stage versus one-stage approach.

Strengths:
The paper makes a strong contribution by defining a novel task with clear applications in movie making, pose estimation, and image retrieval. The two-stage architecture is well-motivated and effectively addresses the difficulty of simultaneously generating correct pose and detailed appearance. The pose mask loss is a thoughtful solution to the background interference problem. The extensive ablation studies provide valuable insights into design choices, such as the superiority of heatmap-based pose embedding over coordinate-based methods and the effectiveness of the mask loss. The paper is clearly written with good organization, and the results demonstrate promising progress toward realistic person image generation.

Weaknesses:
While the qualitative results are compelling, the quantitative evaluation lacks depth. The paper relies primarily on SSIM and Inception Score, which are known to correlate poorly with human perception of image quality (as noted in the paper itself). The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not explore alternative metrics that better capture perceptual quality, such as Fréchet Inception Distance or human evaluation with larger-scale studies. The user study presented is preliminary and limited to 30 subjects with only 55 samples per condition, making its conclusions less convincing. A more robust human evaluation with multiple subjects, diverse samples, and standardized metrics would significantly strengthen the evaluation.

The paper would benefit from more detailed analysis of why the two-stage approach outperforms a one-stage model. Specifically, the authors note that Stage I with adversarial training (G1+D) performs comparably to the full model (G1+G2+D) in quantitative metrics despite producing visibly blurrier results. A deeper exploration of this disconnect between metrics and visual quality would be valuable.

Technical clarifications are needed in several places. The claim that "residual blocks as basic component improves generation performance" (line 110) requires specific evidence - how much does performance improve, and what metrics show this improvement? The role of the fully connected layer in Stage I (which is removed in Stage II) should be more thoroughly explained, as it appears to be a key factor in the two-stage design.

The related work section would be strengthened by including recent iterative refinement approaches in computer vision, such as Carreira et al. (CVPR 2016) on pose estimation with iterative error feedback and Newell et al. (ECCV 2016) on stacked hourglass networks. While the paper acknowledges the novelty of their approach, situating it within the broader context of iterative refinement techniques would better establish its contribution.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of conditional image generation. The proposed task is novel and well-motivated, the architecture is cleverly designed to address specific challenges, and the evaluation provides useful insights. While the quantitative evaluation could be strengthened, the qualitative results are compelling and the paper is clearly written. The authors should address the technical clarifications and expand their evaluation to better demonstrate the real-world impact of their approach.

The work aligns well with NIPS's focus on high-quality scientific contributions. It demonstrates technical soundness through careful ablation studies and a well-justified architecture. The significance lies in the novel task definition and the practical applications it enables. The clarity of presentation is excellent, with a well-structured paper that provides sufficient detail for reproduction.

I am confident that this paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS, and I recommend acceptance. The authors should address the suggested clarifications and evaluation improvements in their revision, which would further strengthen this already promising work.
