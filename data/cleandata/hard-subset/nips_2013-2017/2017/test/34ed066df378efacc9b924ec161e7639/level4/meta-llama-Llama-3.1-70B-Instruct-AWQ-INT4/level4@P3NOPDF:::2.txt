The paper proposes a novel approach to human image generation, conditioned on both appearance and human pose. This is achieved through an adversarial training architecture, employing a two-step generative network that produces high-resolution images to be evaluated by a discriminator. The generator consists of two components: the first produces a coarse image using a U-shape network, given the appearance and pose map as inputs, while the second generator takes the coarse input and the original appearance to predict a residual, refining the coarse image. The authors utilize the DeepFashion dataset for evaluation, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.

The paper contributes several important ideas, including the novelty of conditioning human image generation on both appearance and pose maps, as well as a stacked architecture that predicts a difference map rather than directly upsampling. Additionally, the authors propose a new loss design that aids in the generation of high-quality images.

While the paper presents a well-organized and coherent approach, there are areas that require improvement to make it stand out. Firstly, the quality of the generated images still needs to be improved. Although the proposed approach demonstrates technical novelty, it may be perceived as another variation of a GAN architecture, which could impact its significance. Furthermore, the problem domain, while relevant to vision and graphics applications, may be challenging to generalize to other learning problems.

The paper is well-structured, effectively conveying the key aspects of the proposed architecture. The authors' decision to condition the generator on appearance and pose information, and their use of a coarse-to-fine strategy, are both well-motivated and effectively implemented. The generation strategy is well-suited to the dressing problem, and the difference map generation technique is a valuable contribution to the field of image generation.

However, the generated results still contain visible artifacts, and significant improvement is needed for the approach to be viable for practical applications. For instance, the patterns in ID346 of Fig 4 result in black dots in the final image. Although the second generator mitigates the blurriness of the first generator, it appears that the model is still insufficient to recover high-frequency components in the target appearance.

Another potential concern is that some readers may view the proposed approach as an application of conditional GANs, as similar ideas have been explored in previous work, such as Han Zhang et al.'s "StackGAN: Text to Photo-realistic Image Synthesis with Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks" and Xun Huang et al.'s "Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks". While the paper addresses application-specific challenges, this may not be sufficient to appeal to a broad audience.

In conclusion, the paper successfully proposes a solution to the pose-conditioned image generation problem and conducts a thorough evaluation. The approach demonstrates technical novelty, and although the resulting images require quality improvement, they are at least visually consistent. Overall, I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance, with the understanding that further refinement is necessary to fully realize its potential.
