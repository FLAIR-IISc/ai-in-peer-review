This paper proposes a novel method for generating images of humans, conditioned on a target appearance and a desired pose. The approach is based on a generative adversarial network (GAN) with a two-stage generator designed to produce high-resolution images. The generator employs a coarse-to-fine strategy. The first stage, a U-Net-style network, generates a coarse image from the appearance and pose information. This coarse output, along with the original appearance encoding, is then fed into a second generator. This second network predicts a residual map, which is added to the coarse image to produce the final, refined result. The authors evaluate their method on the DeepFashion dataset.

The paper's main contributions are:
*   **Task Novelty:** It introduces and tackles the problem of human image generation conditioned on both a target appearance and a pose map.
*   **Technical Approach:** It proposes a stacked generator architecture that predicts a difference map for refinement, rather than relying on direct upsampling, and incorporates a well-designed loss function.

While the paper presents several interesting ideas, it could be strengthened in the following areas:
*   **Image Quality:** The generated results, while promising, still contain noticeable artifacts and require further improvement.
*   **Significance:** The work could be perceived as an incremental application of GANs, another entry in a growing list of GAN architectures.
*   **Problem Domain:** While this is a compelling application for computer vision and graphics, the proposed techniques may not be easily generalizable to other domains.

The paper is well-organized and clearly presents the proposed architecture. The coarse-to-fine strategy, implemented via a stacked generator, is a suitable and effective approach for this particular "virtual try-on" or dressing problem. The idea of predicting a residual map is a clever technique for improving the detail in the final high-resolution output.

The primary weakness of the paper lies in the quality of the generated images, which are not yet application-ready. The results contain visible artifacts; for instance, the clothing patterns for ID346 in Figure 4 manifest as black dots in the generated output. While the second generator successfully refines the blurry output of the first, the model still appears to struggle with recovering high-frequency details from the target appearance.

A secondary concern is the potential for the work to be viewed as an application-focused extension of existing conditional GAN frameworks. The core ideas of conditioning and stacking generators for adversarial training have been explored in recent work (e.g., the cited StackGAN papers, which, while on arXiv, are relevant prior art). While the authors tackle application-specific challenges effectively, the limited scope might reduce the paper's appeal to a broader machine learning audience.
*   Han Zhang, Tao Xu, Hongsheng Li, Shaoting Zhang, Xiaolei Huang, Xiaogang Wang, Dimitris Metaxas, "StackGAN: Text to Photo-realistic Image Synthesis with Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks", arXiv:1612.03242.
*   Xun Huang, Yixuan Li, Omid Poursaeed, John Hopcroft, Serge Belongie, Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks, arXiv:1612.04357.

In summary, the paper successfully proposes and evaluates a novel solution for pose-conditioned human image generation. The approach demonstrates sufficient technical novelty, particularly in its two-stage architecture and refinement strategy. Although the quality of the resulting images needs further improvement, the model is capable of generating visually consistent and plausible results. Therefore, I recommend acceptance of the paper.
