This paper presents a human image generator conditioned on both appearance and human pose. The proposed method employs an adversarial training architecture with a two-stage generative process to produce high-resolution images, feeding the output of the first stage into a discriminator. The generator comprises two networks: the first generates a coarse image from appearance and pose maps using a U-shaped network, and the second refines this coarse image by predicting a residual map conditioned on the original appearance. Evaluation is performed using the DeepFashion dataset.

The paper introduces several notable ideas. Specifically, it proposes a novel task – conditioning image generation on appearance and pose maps – and employs effective techniques including a stacked architecture that predicts residual maps instead of directly upsampling, alongside a carefully designed loss function. 

However, the paper could be strengthened to increase its impact. While the approach is promising, the generated image quality still requires improvement. Furthermore, the work could be perceived as yet another GAN architecture, diminishing its perceived significance. The application domain, while relevant to vision and graphics, may limit the generalizability of the proposed techniques to other learning problems.

The paper is well-organized and clearly conveys the key aspects of the proposed architecture. The two-stage generator, adopting a coarse-to-fine strategy conditioned on appearance and pose information, is effectively utilized within the context of the clothing/dressing problem. The use of a difference map for generating higher resolution images is a small but worthwhile technique, and the approach appears well-suited to the targeted problem scenario.

A primary concern is the presence of visible artifacts in the generated results, hindering their practical application. For example, in Figure 4, ID346 exhibits black dots in the final output. While the second generator mitigates blurriness from the first, the model struggles to fully recover high-frequency components present in the target appearance. 

A secondary, less critical concern is that the proposed approach can be seen as an application of conditional GANs. Prior work has explored conditioning and stacking generators within adversarial training frameworks (e.g., Zhang et al., arXiv:1612.03242; Huang et al., arXiv:1612.04357). Although the paper addresses application-specific challenges, this might limit its appeal to a broader audience.

Overall, this paper successfully proposes a solution to the problem of pose-conditioned image generation and includes a sound evaluation. The method demonstrates sufficient technical novelty. While image quality requires further refinement, the proposed model generates visually consistent images. My initial rating is accept.
