This paper proposes a novel method for generating images of humans, conditioned on a target pose and an image specifying the desired appearance (e.g., clothing). The core of the approach is a generative adversarial network (GAN) that employs a two-stage, coarse-to-fine generator. The first stage (G1) is a U-Net-like architecture that takes the target pose and appearance information to produce a coarse, low-resolution rendering. The second stage (G2) then takes this coarse output, along with the original appearance input, and predicts a residual map. This residual is added to the upsampled coarse image to produce the final, high-resolution output. The entire system is trained adversarially against a discriminator. The authors demonstrate their method on the DeepFashion dataset.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Task Novelty:** The paper addresses the challenging and interesting problem of pose-conditioned human image generation. This has clear applications in areas like virtual try-on, character animation, and image editing, making it a valuable contribution to the computer vision and graphics communities.

2.  **Coarse-to-Fine Architecture:** The two-stage generator is a well-motivated design choice. Decomposing the difficult generation task into first creating a coarse layout and then refining the details is an effective strategy. The specific choice to have the second generator predict a residual or difference map, rather than the full image, is a simple yet elegant technique for focusing the network's capacity on high-frequency details and correcting errors from the first stage.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors effectively explain the architecture, the loss functions, and the training procedure, making the method easy to understand and potentially reproduce. The experiments are appropriate for the task and provide a reasonable evaluation of the proposed model.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Output Quality and Artifacts:** The primary weakness of the paper is the quality of the generated images. While the results are often visually plausible, they contain noticeable artifacts and a lack of high-frequency detail. For instance, as seen in Figure 4 (ID346), complex patterns on clothing (e.g., small dots) are not rendered correctly and instead appear as black smudges or noise. Although the second generator is intended to mitigate blurriness from the first, the model still seems to struggle with faithfully transferring intricate textures from the source appearance image to the new pose. Significant improvements in quality would be needed for most practical applications.

2.  **Incremental Novelty in the Broader GAN Context:** While the application is novel, the core architectural ideas could be seen as an incremental advance. The concept of conditional GANs is well-established, and the use of stacked or multi-stage generators was an emerging idea at the time (e.g., StackGAN, arXiv:1612.03242 and arXiv:1612.04357, though these are concurrent arXiv preprints). The main contribution is therefore the skillful adaptation and engineering of these concepts for the specific problem of human image synthesis. While valuable, this may limit the paper's perceived significance to audiences outside of the direct application domain.

3.  **Limited Generalizability:** The proposed solution is highly specialized for the human synthesis task. While this is a worthy problem, the techniques and insights may not be easily generalizable to other domains of machine learning. This narrows the potential impact of the work for the broad NIPS audience.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this paper presents a solid and well-executed piece of research. It tackles a challenging problem with a thoughtfully designed model that combines several effective ideas into a coherent framework. The coarse-to-fine strategy with residual prediction is a sound approach, and the paper demonstrates its viability for this task. The primary concerns are the visible artifacts in the generated results, which suggest there is still a considerable gap to bridge for photorealism, and the fact that the technical contributions are more of an expert application of existing concepts than a fundamental breakthrough in generative modeling.

Despite these limitations, the paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing and providing a strong baseline for the novel problem of pose- and appearance-conditioned human image synthesis. The technical approach is sufficiently novel, and the evaluation is conducted properly. The generated images, while imperfect, are visually consistent and demonstrate the promise of the method. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
