This paper proposes a GAN-based system for generating novel-view person images guided by a target skeleton, represented as keypoints. The system primarily leverages known techniques such as DCGANs and U-nets, so its contribution lies in the specific application of these techniques to a computer vision problem.

The paper has several strengths. The proposed idea is interesting, and the writing is clear and easy to follow. The empirical experiments demonstrate the system's ability to synthesize images that are realistic-looking to some extent.

However, there are several weaknesses that detract from the overall quality of the paper. As this work is application-oriented, achieving good results is crucial. My main concern is the lack of quantitative evaluation. For instance, the authors could have conducted user studies to assess how often the generated images deceive human observers. Furthermore, the empirical results are not visually appealing. The generated images in Figure 1, for example, are too small to discern, and even at this scale, there are noticeable visual errors when compared to the target images. This makes it difficult to conclude that the results are of good quality or close to real images. Unfortunately, this issue persists in almost all the results, including the supplementary figures.

While I personally believe that the authors are on the right track and the work is promising, it is premature to declare victory at this stage. The paper requires further refinement to address the aforementioned concerns.

After reviewing the authors' rebuttal, I remain unconvinced. Other reviewers have also pointed out issues with both the quantitative and qualitative results, which are essential for a practical, application-oriented work like this. Although the rebuttal shows some attempts to address these concerns, they are not adequately resolved. In light of this, I feel that the paper may not be ready for presentation at a venue like NIPS.
