This paper introduces a human image generator conditioned on both appearance and human pose, utilizing an adversarial training framework with a two-stage generative process to produce high-resolution images. The generator employs a cascaded architecture: a U-Net initially generates a coarse image from appearance and pose maps, followed by a second generator that refines this output by predicting a residual map, incorporating the original appearance information. Evaluation is performed using the DeepFashion dataset.

The work presents several noteworthy contributions: a novel approach to conditioning image generation on appearance and pose, a stacked generator architecture that predicts residual detail instead of directly upsampling, and a carefully considered loss function. 

However, the paper could be strengthened in several areas. While technically sound, the generated image quality requires further improvement. The approach, while well-executed, risks being perceived as another incremental advance in GAN architectures. Furthermore, the problem domain, while relevant to vision and graphics applications, may limit broader appeal and generalization to other machine learning problems. 

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, effectively communicating the key aspects of the proposed architecture. The coarse-to-fine strategy, implemented through the stacked generators, is particularly well-suited to the dressing/image manipulation problem. The use of a difference map for high-resolution refinement is a subtle but effective technique.

A significant concern lies in the visible artifacts present in the generated results, hindering their practical application. For example, Fig 4, ID346 exhibits black dot artifacts. Although the second generator reduces blurriness, it appears insufficient to fully reconstruct high-frequency details from the target appearance.

A secondary consideration is the potential for the work to be viewed as an application of existing conditional GAN techniques. While the paper addresses application-specific challenges, the core concepts of conditioning and stacking generators within an adversarial framework have been explored previously (e.g., Zhang et al., 2016; Huang et al., 2016).

Overall, this paper presents a valid solution to the pose-conditioned image generation problem, supported by sound evaluation and demonstrating sufficient technical novelty. The generated images require further quality refinement, but the model consistently produces visually coherent results. I recommend acceptance.
