This paper presents a human image generator conditioned on both appearance and pose information. The proposed method utilizes an adversarial training framework with a two-stage generative process to produce high-resolution images. The generator consists of two networks: the first generates a coarse image from appearance and pose maps using a U-Net architecture, and the second refines this coarse output by predicting a residual image, incorporating the original appearance information. The system is evaluated on the DeepFashion dataset.

The paper introduces several noteworthy ideas. First, it tackles the novel task of conditioning image generation on both appearance *and* pose, allowing for control over both attributes. Second, the technical contributions include a stacked generator architecture that predicts residual maps instead of directly upsampling, and a carefully designed loss function. 

However, the paper could be strengthened to increase its impact. While the approach is sound, the generated image quality currently needs further improvement. Moreover, the work risks being perceived as yet another application of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs); the core architecture builds on existing GAN frameworks. Finally, while the application to human image generation and specifically to the dressing problem is a strong application area, the techniques may not readily generalize to a broader range of machine learning challenges.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the proposed architecture. The coarse-to-fine strategy, implemented through the stacked generator network, is effectively utilized for this task, and the approach seems well-suited to the problem of generating images based on appearance and pose. The use of a difference map for refinement is a small but valuable technique for achieving higher resolution.

The primary concern lies with the visual quality of the generated results. Visible artifacts persist, hindering the practical applicability of the method. For example, in Figure 4, the image corresponding to ID346 exhibits noticeable black dots. While the second generator does mitigate blurring present in the initial coarse output, it appears insufficient to fully recover high-frequency details in the target appearance. 

A secondary, though less critical, point is the potential for the work to be seen as a specific application of existing conditional GAN techniques. Prior work has explored conditioning GANs and stacking generators for adversarial training (e.g., Zhang et al., 2016; Huang et al., 2016), though these are primarily found in arXiv preprints. The paper addresses application-specific challenges related to human appearance and pose, but this narrow focus might limit its appeal to a wider audience.

References:
* Han Zhang, Tao Xu, Hongsheng Li, Shaoting Zhang, Xiaolei Huang, Xiaogang Wang, Dimitris Metaxas, "StackGAN: Text to Photo-realistic Image Synthesis with Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks", arXiv:1612.03242.
* Xun Huang, Yixuan Li, Omid Poursaeed, John Hopcroft, Serge Belongie, Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks, arXiv:1612.04357.

Overall, this paper successfully proposes a solution to the problem of pose-conditioned human image generation and provides a sound evaluation. The technical novelty is sufficient, and the model generates visually consistent images, albeit with room for improvement in image quality. 

I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that addressing the artifact issues should be a priority for future work.
