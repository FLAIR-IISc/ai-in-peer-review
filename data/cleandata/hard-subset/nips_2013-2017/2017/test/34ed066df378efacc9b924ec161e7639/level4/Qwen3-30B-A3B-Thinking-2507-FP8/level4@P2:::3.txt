

This paper introduces Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), a two-stage framework for synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses from a reference image and target pose. The approach divides the task into pose integration (Stage-I) using a U-Net-like generator with pose heatmaps and a novel pose mask loss, followed by image refinement (Stage-II) via adversarial training on a difference map. The work builds on prior image generation techniques (e.g., GANs, U-Nets) and related pose-based synthesis (e.g., Zhao et al. [36]), but introduces a more explicit pose conditioning mechanism using keypoints instead of viewpoint labels. The authors demonstrate results on Market-1501 and DeepFashion datasets, showing improvements over one-stage baselines and prior work.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Originality & Significance:** The paper addresses a novel application (pose-guided person generation) with clear practical relevance for video editing and pose estimation data augmentation. The two-stage strategy effectively decomposes the complex task, and the pose mask loss is a well-motivated technical contribution that explicitly addresses background interference. This work advances the state of the art in pose transfer by providing a more flexible and expressive conditioning mechanism compared to prior methods like [36].  
2. **Quality & Clarity:** The methodology is technically sound, with thorough ablation studies (pose embeddings, loss functions, two-stage vs. one-stage). The paper clearly articulates the problem, rationale for the two-stage design, and experimental setup. Figures 4–6 effectively illustrate qualitative improvements, and the supplementary materials provide sufficient implementation details.  
3. **Evaluation:** The paper includes comprehensive quantitative metrics (SSIM, IS, mask-SSIM, mask-IS) and a user study (AMT) to assess perceptual quality. The user study (Table 2) demonstrates that the two-stage model (G1+G2+D) is perceived as more realistic (14.9% "real" ratings vs. 9.3% for G1+D), directly addressing the significance criterion.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Presentation of Quantitative Results:** While metrics are provided, their interpretation requires clarification. The paper notes that mask-SSIM shows G1-poseMaskLoss > G1-L1, but Table 1 reports higher SSIM for G1-poseMaskLoss despite its blurrier appearance (Fig. 4). This disconnect needs explanation—e.g., the paper should discuss why SSIM may not align with perceptual quality (as noted in the text), and why the two-stage model achieves better user ratings despite similar SSIM.  
2. **Qualitative Analysis:** Some results (e.g., Fig. 4, Market-1501 samples) show noticeable artifacts (e.g., "blue blob" clothing in person ID 53, background inconsistencies). The failure analysis (Fig. 6) is helpful but could be expanded to discuss *why* these failures occur (e.g., rare pose data scarcity, gender imbalance) and how they might be mitigated. The claim that "results are not visually appealing" (draft) misrepresents the paper; Fig. 4 shows plausible results, though not perfect.  
3. **Comparison to [36]:** The comparison to Zhao et al. [36] (Fig. 5) is strong, but the paper should explicitly state *why* their method fails (e.g., "lacks explicit pose conditioning" vs. "uses viewpoint labels"). This clarifies the novelty gap.  

**Balanced Assessment:**  
The paper makes a solid contribution to pose-guided image synthesis with a well-structured approach, clear experiments, and meaningful results. The core technical innovations (pose mask loss, two-stage refinement) are sound and validated through both quantitative and qualitative analysis. While the results are not perfect (as acknowledged in failure cases), they represent a significant step forward over existing methods. The user study is a strong point that directly addresses significance.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Weak Accept.** The work is technically rigorous, addresses a meaningful problem, and demonstrates clear improvements over prior art. The weaknesses are presentational rather than substantive: the quantitative results need better contextualization (to reconcile SSIM with perceptual quality), and the failure analysis could be slightly expanded. However, the paper’s core contribution is novel, well-executed, and impactful for the field. It aligns with NIPS’ focus on high-quality scientific contributions and advances the state of the art in generative modeling for human-centric applications. The authors’ rebuttal adequately addresses concerns about evaluation, making the paper suitable for publication with minor revisions.
