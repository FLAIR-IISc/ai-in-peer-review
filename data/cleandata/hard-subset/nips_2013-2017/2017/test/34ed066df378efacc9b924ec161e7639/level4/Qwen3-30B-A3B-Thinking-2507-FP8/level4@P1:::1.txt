

Summary: This paper introduces a novel framework for synthesizing person images with arbitrary poses by conditioning on a reference image and target pose information. The authors propose a two-stage architecture called Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), which first integrates pose information with appearance through a coarse generation stage, followed by a refinement stage using adversarial training to add fine details. The approach builds on recent advances in conditional GANs while introducing several key innovations: a pose embedding method using heatmaps rather than raw coordinates, a pose mask loss to focus generation on human body regions, and a coarse-to-fine architecture that addresses the challenging nature of pose transfer.

Strengths: The paper makes a compelling contribution by introducing a novel task of pose-guided person image generation, which has clear applications in fields like movie making and pose estimation data augmentation. The two-stage architecture is well-motivated and effectively addresses the challenge of simultaneously generating correct pose and detailed appearance. The authors provide extensive ablation studies that thoroughly evaluate key design choices, including different pose embedding methods, the effect of the pose mask loss, and the comparative advantage of the two-stage approach over one-stage alternatives. The results demonstrate significant improvements over baseline approaches, with qualitative evidence showing the model's ability to generate photo-realistic images that preserve identity while transferring pose. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the technical contributions accessible.

Weaknesses: While the qualitative results are promising, the quantitative evaluation is limited. The authors use SSIM and Inception Score, but these metrics don't fully capture the perceptual quality of the generated images, as evidenced by cases where blurrier images achieve higher SSIM scores than more detailed ones. The user study on Amazon Mechanical Turk, while an attempt at human evaluation, is preliminary and lacks sufficient scale to be convincing. The authors should expand this into a more rigorous human evaluation in future work.

Comments: The paper would benefit from including recent work on iterative refinement using cascaded networks in the related work section, such as Carreira et al.'s "Human Pose Estimation with Iterative Error Feedback" (CVPR 2016) and Newell et al.'s "Stacked Hourglass Networks for Human Pose Estimation" (ECCV 2016), which share conceptual similarities with the authors' two-stage approach.

On line 110, the authors state "we find that using residual blocks as basic component improves the generation performance" but don't elaborate on how. It would be helpful to provide more specific details about the performance improvements observed when using residual blocks versus alternative architectures.

The authors should also consider addressing how the Stage 2 network would perform if trained adversarially with the Stage 1 inputs, rather than using the coarse output as input. It's unclear whether the blurring observed in Stage 1 is specifically due to the fully connected layer or a general limitation of L1-based generation.

Overall rating: The paper presents a novel and well-executed approach to an important problem with clear applications. The two-stage architecture is a thoughtful solution to the challenge of pose transfer, and the extensive evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed components. While the quantitative evaluation could be strengthened, the qualitative results and thorough ablation studies provide strong evidence for the method's effectiveness. The user study, though preliminary, aligns with the qualitative findings. Given the novelty of the problem formulation, the thoughtful architecture design, and the comprehensive evaluation, I recommend acceptance with confidence.
