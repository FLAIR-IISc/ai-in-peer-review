This paper introduces a novel architecture for image generation, specifically focusing on synthesizing images of a person exhibiting a desired pose, driven by both an input image defining appearance and a pose specified via heatmaps.

The work’s strengths lie in the novelty of the proposed task and the innovative two-stage, coarse-to-fine architecture employed to address it. The paper is exceptionally well-written and presents promising results, despite the presence of some noticeable artifacts. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate a thorough evaluation of key design choices.

A primary weakness is the limited strength of the quantitative evaluation. However, this is largely understandable given the current lack of established metrics suitable for assessing the quality of this type of generated imagery. 

Regarding related work, given the iterative refinement inherent in the proposed architecture, it would be beneficial to discuss recent advancements in cascaded networks for iterative refinement, such as the work by Carreira et al. (2016) [a] and Newell et al. (2016) [b]. 
[a] J. Carreira, P. Agrawal, K. Fragkiadaki, and J. Malik. Human pose estimation with iterative error feedback. In IEEE Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition (CVPR), 2016.
[b] A. Newell, K. Yang, and J. Deng. Stacked hourglass networks for human pose estimation. In European Conference on Computer Vision (ECCV), 2016.
Additionally, the statement on line 110 – “we find that using residual blocks as basic component improves the generation performance” – would benefit from further elaboration regarding *how* performance is improved. Finally, while the evaluation is comprehensive, I am curious about the potential performance of using the Stage 2 network directly with Stage 1 inputs, trained adversarially. It would also be insightful to investigate whether the blurring observed when combining Stage 1 with a GAN is attributable to the intermediate fully connected layer.

Overall, despite these minor points, I recommend acceptance of this paper. The novelty of the problem and the proposed approach are significant and compelling.

Concerning the rebuttal, while I acknowledge the authors’ attempt at a user study, I find the preliminary nature of the results unconvincing and therefore do not factor it into my overall assessment. I encourage the authors to conduct a more substantial human evaluation during the revision process. Nevertheless, my positive evaluation of the work remains unchanged.
