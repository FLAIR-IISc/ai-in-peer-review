

This paper introduces a two-stage GAN framework (PG2) for synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses using a reference image and target pose keypoints. The approach leverages U-Net architectures for pose integration (Stage-I) and adversarial refinement (Stage-II), with a novel pose mask loss to prioritize foreground details. The work is positioned as a practical application of existing generative techniques to a novel task: pose-guided person image generation.

**Strengths:**  
The paper clearly articulates the problem and proposes a well-motivated divide-and-conquer strategy. The pose embedding via heatmaps (instead of raw coordinates) and the pose mask loss are technically sound innovations that address key challenges in background interference. The writing is generally clear, and the two-stage design effectively separates global structure learning from detail refinement. Empirical results on Market-1501 and DeepFashion demonstrate the method’s ability to generate plausible pose transfers with reasonable visual quality, particularly in the DeepFashion dataset. The ablation studies (e.g., pose embedding variants, mask loss vs. L1) provide useful insights.

**Weaknesses:**  
The primary concern is insufficient evaluation for a NIPS submission. While the paper includes user studies (Table 2) and quantitative metrics (SSIM, IS), the evaluation lacks rigor expected at NIPS:  
- The user study is underpowered (only 30 users) and fails to address critical weaknesses in the results. For instance, Figure 4 reveals significant artifacts in Market-1501 samples (e.g., "blue blob" clothing in ID 53), yet the user study shows low "Real" scores (G2R=9.3% for G1+D, 14.9% for G1+G2+D), suggesting generated images are easily distinguishable.  
- Quantitative metrics (Table 1) are misleading: mask-SSIM and mask-IS are used, but the paper admits blurry images often achieve high SSIM (e.g., G1-poseMaskLoss has higher SSIM than G1+G2+D despite being less photo-realistic). The user study’s low G2R scores contradict the paper’s claim of "photo-realistic" results.  
- Visual results are inconsistent: DeepFashion samples show promise (e.g., ID 245, 346), but Market-1501 results (lower rows of Figure 4) exhibit severe artifacts (e.g., "stool in woman’s hands," incorrect gender, background bleed), undermining the paper’s core claim. The rebuttal does not adequately address these flaws.  

**Key Concerns:**  
1. **Evaluation depth:** The paper treats "photo-realism" as a given without rigorous comparison to state-of-the-art (e.g., [36] is only superficially compared). Ablations show G1+G2+D outperforms G1+D qualitatively, but user study scores (G2R) for Market-1501 are low (G1+G2+D: 14.9% vs. G1+D: 9.3%), suggesting limited real-world utility.  
2. **Technical limitations:** The pose mask loss fails on complex backgrounds (Market-1501), and the two-stage model inherits artifacts from Stage-I (e.g., ID 23 in Figure 4). The paper acknowledges these as "failure cases" (Figure 6) but does not quantify their frequency or propose solutions.  
3. **Overstated contribution:** The framework combines well-known components (U-Net, DCGAN, adversarial training). The novelty lies in the application and pose mask loss, but these are insufficient for NIPS without stronger evidence of impact.  

**Verdict:**  
While the problem is well-motivated and the approach is logically structured, the evaluation falls short of NIPS standards. The empirical results are inconsistent, and the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the generated images are "photo-realistic" or practically useful—especially for the challenging Market-1501 dataset. The rebuttal failed to address these gaps. The work shows promise but requires substantially stronger validation before being considered for publication. I recommend rejection.
