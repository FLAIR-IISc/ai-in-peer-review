Summary
The paper tackles pose-guided person image synthesis: given a reference image of a person and a desired target pose (represented by keypoint heatmaps), generate a new image of the same person in the target pose. The method, PG2, uses a two-stage pipeline. Stage I integrates the reference image with the pose using a U-Net–style encoder–decoder with skip connections and a pose mask–weighted L1 loss to produce a coarse result that matches global structure. Stage II refines this output with a conditional GAN that predicts a residual (difference map) to sharpen details and improve realism, trained with an adversarial loss plus the same mask-weighted L1 term. Experiments are presented on Market-1501 (128×64) and DeepFashion (256×256). The approach is grounded in widely used components (U-Nets, DCGANs, masked reconstruction losses, and conditional generation), applied to the specific task of pose-conditioned person synthesis.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on conditional image generation with VAEs/GANs and image-to-image translation, and is related to multi-view synthesis and pose-guided generation. The explicit conditioning on pose keypoints and the two-stage design echo recent trends in decomposing structure and appearance. While the architectural choices are mostly standard, the pose-mask loss and the residual refinement objective are tailored to this application.

Strengths
- Problem and formulation: Addressing explicit control via pose is interesting and practically relevant (e.g., content creation, data augmentation).
- Design: The two-stage decomposition (global structure first, then detail) is reasonable. Using a pose mask to de-emphasize background and a residual map in Stage II are sensible choices.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and easy to follow.
- Evidence: Qualitative examples show that the method can produce globally plausible poses and preserve some appearance cues.

Weaknesses
Quality
- The empirical validation lacks rigorous quantitative evaluation appropriate for perceptual fidelity. For such an application-driven paper, user studies and objective metrics that correlate with human judgment (e.g., human Turing tests, FID, LPIPS) would be important. It is unclear how often the outputs would fool human observers or whether they improve downstream tasks (e.g., re-ID).
- Qualitative results are not compelling enough. Figures (e.g., Fig. 1) are small, and even at that scale, many artifacts are visible when compared to targets (blurry textures, distorted limbs, facial artifacts, color bleeding). This weakens the claim of high-quality or near-photorealistic synthesis.
- Limited analysis of failure cases and training stability; it is hard to assess how robust the method is across diverse poses and occlusions.

Clarity
- Several figures are too small to inspect details. Larger crops or zoom-ins would help. Some architectural/training details could be expanded (e.g., effects of λ, discriminator conditioning variants) to improve reproducibility.

Originality
- The approach mainly combines known elements (U-Nets, conditional GANs, residual prediction, mask-weighted losses). The novelty lies primarily in the application and specific design choices rather than in new theory or architectures.

Significance
- The task is valuable, but the current evidence does not convincingly demonstrate state-of-the-art realism or utility. Without stronger quantitative and qualitative results, it is difficult to judge the impact on practitioners or subsequent research.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include a perceptual user study (e.g., real vs. fake discrimination rates) and complementary metrics (FID, LPIPS), and consider a downstream evaluation (e.g., augmenting re-ID training with generated images).
- Provide larger, high-resolution visualizations with side-by-side targets, zoomed regions, and systematic ablations (single-stage vs. two-stage, mask vs. no mask, residual vs. direct prediction).
- Compare against strong baselines (e.g., pix2pix-style conditional GANs, recent pose-guided methods) with controlled settings, and provide statistical significance where possible.
- Discuss limitations and failure modes in more depth, and, if feasible, release code/models.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated problem with potential applications.
- Coherent two-stage design with reasonable engineering choices.
- Promising qualitative indications that explicit pose conditioning helps.

Arguments against acceptance
- Primarily an application of existing techniques with limited novelty.
- Insufficient quantitative validation and qualitative evidence to claim high realism.
- Visual artifacts remain frequent; figures do not convincingly demonstrate robust, high-quality synthesis.

The additional results and metrics do not sufficiently address the need for rigorous quantitative evaluation (especially human perceptual studies) and stronger qualitative evidence at appropriate resolution.
