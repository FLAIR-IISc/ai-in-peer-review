This paper proposes a novel Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) to synthesize person images in arbitrary poses based on an image of that person and a novel pose. The generation framework PG utilizes the pose information explicitly and consists of two key stages: pose integration and image refinement. The authors' main contribution is the development of a two-stage approach to address the challenging task of pose transfer, with each stage focusing on one aspect.

The paper is well-written, and the idea of using a two-stage approach to generate person images in arbitrary poses is interesting. The authors provide extensive experimental results on two person datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method. The use of a pose mask loss to alleviate the influence of background changes and the employment of a variant of Deep Convolutional GAN (DCGAN) to refine the initial generation result are notable contributions.

However, there are some concerns regarding the quantitative and qualitative results. While the authors provide some quantitative evaluations, such as Structural Similarity (SSIM) and the Inception Score (IS), these metrics may not be sufficient to fully evaluate the quality of the generated images. Additionally, the empirical results, including the figures, show some noticeable visual errors when comparing the results to the targets. It is thus a bit discouraging to conclude that the results so far are of good quality or close to real images.

To strengthen the paper, the authors could consider organizing user studies to evaluate how many times the generated images can trick human eyes. This would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method. Furthermore, the authors could improve the visual quality of the generated images, as some of the results appear too small or contain noticeable errors.

In terms of related work, the authors provide a thorough review of existing methods for generative image modeling with deep learning techniques. However, it would be helpful to discuss how the proposed method differs from other approaches that use pose information for image generation.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the idea of using a two-stage approach to generate person images in arbitrary poses is interesting. However, the authors need to address the concerns regarding the quantitative and qualitative results to fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to generating person images in arbitrary poses.
* The authors provide extensive experimental results on two person datasets.
* The use of a pose mask loss and a variant of DCGAN are notable contributions.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The quantitative evaluations may not be sufficient to fully evaluate the quality of the generated images.
* The empirical results show some noticeable visual errors when comparing the results to the targets.
* The authors could improve the visual quality of the generated images.
