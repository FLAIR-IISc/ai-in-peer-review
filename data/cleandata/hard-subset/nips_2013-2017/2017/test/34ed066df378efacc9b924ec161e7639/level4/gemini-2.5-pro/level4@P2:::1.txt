This paper introduces a novel task and a corresponding framework, the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG²), for synthesizing an image of a specific person in an arbitrary target pose. The method takes a source image of the person and a target pose (represented as keypoint heatmaps) as input. The generation process is divided into a two-stage, coarse-to-fine architecture. Stage I employs a U-Net-like generator (G1) to integrate the source image and target pose, producing a coarse but structurally correct output. A key contribution here is a proposed "pose mask loss" to encourage the network to focus on the foreground person rather than background changes. Stage II refines this coarse output using another U-Net-like generator (G2) trained with a conditional GAN framework. This second stage focuses on adding realistic details and texture, correcting artifacts from the first stage. This approach is situated within the broader context of conditional image generation using GANs, but tackles the unique and challenging problem of explicit and fine-grained pose manipulation, differing from prior work that conditions on more abstract cues like text, attributes, or general viewpoint categories.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper proposes and tackles a novel and challenging problem: generating a person's image in an arbitrary, specified pose given a single source image. This is a significant step beyond existing conditional generation tasks. The problem is well-motivated, with clear potential applications in areas like data augmentation for pose estimation, virtual try-on for fashion, and content creation for media. The proposed two-stage architecture is a logical and effective way to decompose this difficult problem.

2.  **Technical Quality and Thoroughness:** The authors have conducted an extensive evaluation of their architectural and loss function design choices. The ablation studies comparing different pose embedding methods (coordinate vs. heatmap) and the impact of the pose mask loss are particularly valuable. These experiments provide strong evidence supporting the final model design and demonstrate a careful, scientific approach. The qualitative results are promising; while some artifacts are present, the model successfully transfers appearance to novel poses and generates plausible details, especially on the DeepFashion dataset.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The motivation for the two-stage approach—separating the challenges of global structure generation and local detail refinement—is well-articulated. The network diagrams and result figures are informative and support the paper's claims effectively.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Quantitative Evaluation:** The quantitative evaluation relies on SSIM and Inception Score. As the authors correctly note, these metrics often fail to correlate well with human perception of image quality, especially for generative tasks where blurry but structurally "correct" images can score highly. While this is a known challenge in the field and not a major flaw of the paper, the current quantitative results are not fully convincing on their own. The addition of the user study is a welcome step in this direction. However, in its current form, the study is preliminary. For the final version, a more substantive human evaluation (e.g., A/B testing comparing the two-stage model against the one-stage baseline, or rating realism on a Likert scale) would significantly strengthen the paper's conclusions.

2.  **Contextualization of the Architecture:** The iterative, coarse-to-fine refinement strategy is a powerful concept. The paper's discussion of related work could be strengthened by connecting this two-stage approach to other cascaded or stacked network architectures that employ iterative error correction, which have proven successful in related domains like human pose estimation. Including references such as Carreira et al. (2016) and Newell et al. (2016) would provide valuable context.
    *   [a] J. Carreira, et al. "Human pose estimation with iterative error feedback." CVPR 2016.
    *   [b] A. Newell, et al. "Stacked hourglass networks for human pose estimation." ECCV 2016.

3.  **Clarification of Design Choices:**
    *   On line 110, the authors state, "we find that using residual blocks as basic component improves the generation performance." This claim would be more impactful if elaborated. In what way was performance improved? Was it a quantitative improvement in metrics, a qualitative improvement in image sharpness, or enhanced training stability?
    *   The paper demonstrates that the two-stage model (G1+G2+D) outperforms the one-stage adversarial model (G1+D). A critical question is whether this improvement stems from the two-stage process itself, or from the architectural differences between G1 and G2 (specifically, the lack of a fully-connected bottleneck in G2). An interesting ablation study would be to train the more powerful G2 generator in a one-stage adversarial setup (i.e., using the Stage I inputs directly). This would help isolate the benefit of the coarse-to-fine strategy from the benefit of the G2 architecture itself.

**Overall Recommendation:**

Despite the limitations in quantitative evaluation, which are common for this type of generative work, this paper makes a strong contribution. The novelty and significance of the proposed task, combined with a well-designed and thoroughly evaluated coarse-to-fine approach, are highly compelling. The qualitative results are promising and demonstrate a clear step forward in controllable image synthesis. I am confidently recommending that the paper be **accepted**. The suggestions above are intended to help the authors further strengthen an already solid piece of work for the final version.
