This paper introduces a novel method for pose-guided person image generation, a task aimed at synthesizing a new image of a person from a source image, but in a new target pose. The core of the proposed method is a two-stage generative network, the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG²). The first stage integrates the source person's image with a heatmap representation of the target pose using a U-Net-like architecture (G1). This stage generates a coarse image that captures the global structure and color of the person in the new pose. To handle background changes, the authors introduce a "pose mask loss" that encourages the model to focus on the foreground person. The second stage refines this coarse output. A second U-Net-like generator (G2) takes the coarse image and the original source image as input and learns to generate a "difference map" to add finer details and correct artifacts. This refinement stage is trained adversarially with a discriminator (D), following the principles of conditional Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors evaluate their method on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness through quantitative metrics, a user study, and extensive qualitative examples. This work is related to the broader field of conditional image generation, but distinguishes itself by providing direct and fine-grained control over the output via an explicit pose representation, a task that has not been extensively explored.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty of the Task and Application:** The paper formulates a novel and challenging problem: generating a photorealistic image of a person conditioned on both an appearance template (the source image) and an arbitrary target pose (represented by keypoints). This is a compelling application with clear utility in areas like data augmentation for pose estimation, virtual try-on in fashion, and content creation for media. The explicit control via pose keypoints is a significant step beyond conditioning on more abstract information like text or class labels.

2.  **Sound Methodological Design:** The proposed two-stage, coarse-to-fine architecture is a logical and well-motivated approach to this difficult problem. Decomposing the task into first establishing the global structure (pose and rough appearance) and then refining the details (texture and high-frequency information) is an effective strategy. This divide-and-conquer approach helps stabilize the training and tackle the complexity of the transformation.

3.  **Effective Technical Contributions:** The paper introduces several thoughtful technical details. The use of a pose mask in the L1 loss is a clever solution to disentangle the foreground person generation from the unpredictable background, which is a common challenge in image-to-image translation tasks. Furthermore, having the second stage generator (G2) predict a residual or "difference map" rather than the full image is an elegant choice that helps the model focus specifically on refinement, often leading to faster convergence and better preservation of details from the first stage.

4.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of their method. The experiments on two distinct datasets (high-resolution fashion photos and low-resolution surveillance images) demonstrate the robustness of the approach. The paper includes important ablation studies that validate key design choices, such as the pose embedding method, the benefit of the pose mask loss, and the superiority of the two-stage model over a single-stage one. The inclusion of a user study provides a valuable measure of perceptual quality that complements standard metrics like SSIM and IS.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Image Quality and Artifacts:** While the results are promising and often visually convincing, the generated images still contain noticeable artifacts and can fail to capture high-frequency details. For instance, in Figure 4, complex patterns on clothing (ID 346) are not reproduced faithfully and instead appear as noisy dots. The model seems to struggle with fine textures. While the second stage improves upon the blurriness of the first, a gap in photorealism remains, which may limit immediate practical application.

2.  **Incremental Architectural Originality:** From an architectural perspective, the work builds heavily on established concepts. The use of stacked U-Net generators in a coarse-to-fine manner is reminiscent of other contemporary works such as StackGAN (Zhang et al., 2016) for text-to-image synthesis. The conditional adversarial training framework with a U-Net generator is also a core component of the influential pix2pix model (Isola et al., 2017). While the application of these techniques to the novel problem of pose guidance is a clear contribution, the paper could be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of its architectural relationship to these concurrent works and by better highlighting its own architectural novelties beyond the specific task-related loss functions.

3.  **Limited Scope of Contribution:** The proposed method is highly specialized for a particular computer vision and graphics task. While this is a valuable contribution to that domain, the direct methodological insights may be less transferable to the broader machine learning community compared to more fundamental research on generative models. This is not a major flaw but a point of consideration for a general-audience conference like NIPS.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

Overall, this paper presents a solid and valuable contribution. It successfully introduces and provides a strong baseline solution for the novel task of pose-guided person generation. The proposed two-stage method is well-designed and thoroughly evaluated. The main strengths are the novelty of the problem, the sensible coarse-to-fine approach, and the comprehensive experiments. The primary weaknesses are the remaining visual artifacts in the results and the fact that the network architecture itself is more of a novel combination of existing ideas rather than a fundamentally new one.

Despite these weaknesses, the strengths of the paper are significant. The authors have tackled a difficult problem and demonstrated impressive results that represent a clear step forward. The work is likely to inspire further research in controllable image synthesis. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
