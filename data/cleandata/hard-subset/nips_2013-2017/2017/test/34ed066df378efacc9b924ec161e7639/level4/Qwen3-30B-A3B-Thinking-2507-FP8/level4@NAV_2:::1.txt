

Summary:
The paper introduces a novel framework for synthesizing person images by combining the appearance of a person from a reference image with a specified target pose (represented as heatmaps). The proposed Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) employs a two-stage architecture where the first stage integrates pose information to generate a coarse image capturing global structure, and the second stage refines this result using adversarial training to produce higher-quality images with detailed appearance.

Strengths:
+ The task of person image generation conditioned on both reference image and target pose represents a significant novelty compared to previous NIPS publications.
+ The two-stage coarse-to-fine architecture effectively addresses the challenging pose transfer problem by dividing it into two manageable subproblems.
+ The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow that facilitates understanding of the methodology and results.
+ While the generated images occasionally contain artifacts, the overall quality is promising and demonstrates meaningful progress in the field.
+ The authors provide comprehensive evaluation of design decisions through extensive ablation studies on pose embedding methods, loss functions, and the two-stage versus one-stage architecture.

Weaknesses:
- The quantitative evaluation lacks strong metrics, though this limitation is understandable given the absence of established benchmarks for this specific task.

Comments:
- The related work section would be strengthened by incorporating recent work on iterative refinement using cascaded networks, such as:
  [a] J. Carreira, P. Agrawal, K. Fragkiadaki, and J. Malik. Human pose estimation with iterative error feedback. CVPR, 2016.
  [b] A. Newell, K. Yang, and J. Deng. Stacked hourglass networks for human pose estimation. ECCV, 2016.

- On line 110, the authors note that "using residual blocks as basic component improves the generation performance." It would be helpful to provide specific details on how these improvements manifest (e.g., quantitative metrics or qualitative observations).

- I was curious about the performance of using the Stage 2 network with Stage 1 inputs and trained adversarially. Would the blurring observed with Stage 1 + GAN be mitigated by removing the intermediate fully connected layer?

Overall rating:
Based on the novelty of the problem formulation and the innovative two-stage approach, I am confidently recommending that the paper be accepted.

Rebuttal:
While I appreciate the authors' effort to include a user study, its preliminary nature makes it unconvincing, and I am not incorporating it into my assessment. Nevertheless, my positive evaluation of the paper's scientific contribution remains unchanged, and I maintain my recommendation for acceptance.
