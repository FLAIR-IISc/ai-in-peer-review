Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles pose-guided person image generation: given a reference image of a person and a target pose, synthesize a new image of the same person in the new pose. The proposed PG2 framework is a two-stage, coarse-to-fine pipeline. Stage I integrates a conditioning image and a target pose, encoded as 18 keypoint heatmaps, using a U-Net-like generator with residual blocks and a fully connected bottleneck. A masked L1 loss down-weights background pixels to focus the training signal on the person. Stage II refines the coarse result via a conditional DCGAN: a second U-Net predicts a residual “difference map” that is added to the Stage I output, and a pairwise discriminator sees (condition image, generated image) vs (condition image, real target) to enforce pose-consistent realism. The method is evaluated on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64), with ablations on pose encoding, the mask loss, and one-stage vs two-stage training. Quantitative metrics (SSIM/IS and their masked variants), a user study, and a comparison to multi-view person synthesis [Zhao et al., 2017] are reported.

The paper builds on conditional generative modeling (cGANs, DCGANs) and coarse-to-fine training popularized by image-to-image translation and stacked GANs (e.g., pix2pix by Isola et al., 2017; StackGAN by Zhang et al., 2016; SGAN by Huang et al., 2016). It differs by conditioning generation on explicit human pose via keypoint heatmaps rather than on text, view labels, or coarse segmentations. The residual refinement and pairwise discriminator are sensible extensions for this task.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The two-stage design is well motivated: Stage I handles global structure (pose/layout), Stage II focuses on high-frequency details using adversarial training. The choice to predict a difference map in Stage II is pragmatic and helps convergence. The pairwise discriminator avoids trivial copying of the conditioning image. The pose mask loss is a simple but effective way to reduce background bias, and the ablations support these design choices.
- Empirical evaluation: Experiments cover two datasets with different resolutions and backgrounds, include ablations (coordinate vs heatmap pose encodings; L1 vs mask-L1; one-stage vs two-stage), and a user study. The introduction of mask-SSIM/mask-IS is reasonable for isolating foreground quality. The qualitative results show clear improvements from Stage II and from the mask loss.
- Limitations: Visible artifacts remain, especially on complex textures, cluttered scenes, and rare poses (e.g., ID 346 in Fig. 4 exhibits “black dots”/texture collapse). High-frequency details and fine garment patterns are often not faithfully recovered. Background transfer is ad hoc and can introduce artifacts, particularly on Market-1501. Identity preservation is not quantified; an identity/feature-consistency metric (e.g., VGG/face or re-id features) would strengthen claims. Comparisons to strong baselines such as pix2pix conditioned on pose heatmaps and the conditioning image are missing. The sensitivity to the mask weight λ is relegated to the supplement; bringing key findings into the main paper would aid reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and generally well organized. Architectural details, training procedures, and ablations are described with sufficient specificity to support reproduction. Figures are informative. Minor suggestions:
- Include the λ sensitivity study in the main text.
- Clarify how the residual/difference map is combined with Stage I (explicit equation).
- Discuss runtime and parameter counts for both stages.
- Provide more details on the pose mask creation and failure modes of the pose detector.

Originality
Conditioning on explicit pose keypoints and a reference appearance image for person synthesis is a timely and useful formulation. While the coarse-to-fine design and use of adversarial training are not new per se (cf. Isola et al., StackGAN/SGAN), the combination of: (i) pose heatmap concatenation for conditioning, (ii) a foreground mask loss tailored to pose transfer, (iii) residual refinement with a pairwise discriminator for pose-conditional realism, constitutes a coherent and novel approach for this specific problem.

Significance
The ability to control person pose while preserving appearance has applications in graphics, data augmentation for pose estimation/re-identification, and content creation. The method demonstrates convincing results on two public datasets and a clear improvement over a related multi-view approach [Zhao et al., 2017]. That said, artifacting and limited texture fidelity currently constrain practical deployment. Broader impact beyond people (other articulated categories) remains to be shown.

Pros
- Well-motivated, explicit pose-conditioned generation task with practical relevance.
- Clear, modular two-stage design; residual refinement and pairwise discriminator are effective.
- Simple, task-specific mask loss that measurably helps.
- Thorough ablations and user study; introduction of mask-SSIM/mask-IS acknowledges background confounds.
- Empirical gains over a related prior method.

Cons
- Noticeable artifacts and incomplete recovery of high-frequency details; background treatment remains brittle.
- No identity/appearance preservation metric; limited quantitative evaluation beyond SSIM/IS.
- Missing comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., pix2pix with pose heatmaps + appearance).
- Generalization to other categories and robustness to pose estimation errors are not explored.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a perceptual/feature-consistency loss (e.g., VGG-based) and/or an identity-preservation loss (re-id features) to better retain appearance details.
- Incorporate human parsing/segmentation or learned soft attention to separate foreground from background, reducing artifacts when backgrounds differ.
- Compare against pix2pix-style baselines and a one-stage residual cGAN conditioned on pose heatmaps and the conditioning image.
- Report identity similarity and pose accuracy metrics in addition to SSIM/IS; include λ sensitivity in the main paper.
- Analyze failure modes with respect to pose estimator errors, occlusions, and rare poses; consider data augmentation or part-wise warping for difficult poses.

Scope
The paper is squarely within NIPS/NeurIPS topics on deep generative modeling and conditional image synthesis.

Recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. Despite some remaining visual artifacts and room for stronger baselines/metrics, the paper presents a clear formulation, a technically sound and well-evaluated solution, and a useful contribution to controlled image generation via explicit pose guidance.
