Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper tackles pose-guided person image generation: given a reference image of a person and a target pose, synthesize a new image of the same person in the new pose. The authors propose PG2, a two-stage, coarse-to-fine framework. Stage I uses a U-Net-like generator to integrate the reference image and the target pose (encoded as 18 keypoint heatmaps) and produces a coarse image. A pose mask is used in the L1 loss to downweight background differences. Stage II refines the result via adversarial training with a conditional DCGAN variant: the generator predicts a difference map between the stage-I output and the target, and a pairwise discriminator receives (condition image, generated) versus (condition image, real) pairs. The authors perform extensive ablations: alternative pose embeddings (coordinate embedding, separate heatmap encoder, versus direct concatenation of heatmaps), standard L1 vs pose-masked L1, and one-stage vs two-stage adversarial training. They evaluate on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64), propose mask-SSIM and mask-IS to reduce background effects, and conduct a user study. They also provide qualitative comparisons to VariGAN (Zhao et al.), showing superior results.

This work is closely related to conditional GANs and stacked/coarse-to-fine generators (e.g., StackGAN and Stacked GAN), as well as image-to-image translation approaches that condition on structured signals. The paper’s contributions are the explicit conditioning on pose keypoints, the use of a pose-masked reconstruction loss, and the difference-map refinement stage.

Strengths:
- Clear formulation of a practically relevant task: pose-guided person image generation conditioned on an identity image and explicit keypoint pose.
- Solid, well-motivated two-stage design: coarse structural synthesis followed by adversarial refinement to recover details.
- Useful technical choices: concatenating pose heatmaps with the image, a pose-masked L1 loss to reduce background penalties, and predicting a difference map in Stage II to ease learning and speed convergence.
- Thorough empirical study: ablations on pose embeddings, loss functions, and one- vs two-stage training; evaluations on two datasets with differing difficulty; user study; and qualitative comparison to a related multi-view person synthesis method.
- The paper is well organized and easy to follow.

Weaknesses:
- Output quality still shows visible artifacts and loss of high-frequency details, limiting immediate applicability. For example, in Fig. 4, ID 346 exhibits black-dot artifacts; more generally, the refinement stage does not fully recover fine textures.
- Novelty may be perceived as incremental relative to existing conditional/stacked GAN architectures; the approach could be viewed as “yet another GAN variant” tailored to a specific application.
- The focus is domain-specific (fashion/person images), and it is unclear how easily the approach generalizes to other object categories or broader ML problems.
- On Market-1501, the method can transfer unwanted background from the condition image and introduce artifacts, which also negatively affects the user study outcome.

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: The method is technically sound and supported by ablations and comparisons. The pairwise discriminator and pose mask loss are reasonable design choices. However, the generated images still contain noticeable artifacts, especially in challenging poses or cluttered scenes.
- Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The methodology, loss functions, and architectural choices are explained, and the ablations are informative.
- Originality: Moderate. While the task formulation (explicit pose conditioning with keypoints) and the pose-masked loss/difference-map refinement are nice contributions, the overall approach builds on established conditional and stacked GAN paradigms.
- Significance: The work has value for vision/graphics applications (e.g., fashion imagery, data augmentation, pose manipulation), and the explicit pose control is compelling. That said, broader impact beyond this application domain may be limited, and current output quality constrains practical deployment.

Specific comments on experiments:
- Pose embedding: Directly concatenating pose heatmaps to the image input outperforms coordinate embedding and a separate heatmap-encoder, which struggle to map keypoints to correct image locations and yield blurrier, less accurate poses.
- Loss design: The pose-masked L1 loss improves focus on the human body and reduces background-induced penalties, producing sharper boundaries and more accurate colors (notably on Market-1501).
- Two-stage vs one-stage: The two-stage model yields more photo-realistic details than a single adversarial stage, even though traditional metrics (SSIM/IS and their masked variants) sometimes fail to reflect the perceptual improvements.
- User study: On DeepFashion, the refined two-stage model fools users more often than the one-stage model. On Market-1501, cluttered backgrounds and transferred artifacts reduce realism scores.
- Comparison to VariGAN: Qualitative examples show clearly superior person synthesis by PG2, especially for full-body generation conditioned on partial-body inputs.
- Failure cases: Complex poses and data imbalance (e.g., gender) cause failures such as fragmented limbs or ambiguous clothing appearance.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, well-motivated task and solution with explicit pose control.
- Sound two-stage architecture with thoughtful loss and conditioning design.
- Comprehensive ablations and user study; convincing qualitative improvements over a related baseline.

Cons against acceptance:
- Image quality remains inconsistent, with artifacts and missing high-frequency details.
- Incremental novelty relative to existing conditional/stacked GANs.
- Limited generalization beyond person/fashion imagery and sensitivity to background clutter.

Recommendation:
Despite remaining quality issues and the incremental nature of some components, the paper presents a coherent approach to a timely and practically useful problem, with solid experiments and clear insights. My initial rating is accept.
