The paper proposes a human image generator that conditions on both appearance and human pose, leveraging a two-step generative network within an adversarial training architecture to produce high-resolution images. The generator consists of two stages: the first stage uses a U-shaped network to generate a coarse image from the appearance and pose map, while the second stage takes the coarse input and the original appearance to predict a residual map that refines the coarse image. The authors evaluate their approach using the DeepFashion dataset.

The paper introduces several important ideas, including the novelty of conditioning on both appearance and pose maps for human image generation, a stacked architecture that predicts a difference map rather than directly upsampling, and a well-designed loss function.

To stand out, the paper could improve in several areas. Firstly, the generated images still require significant quality improvements. Secondly, the paper's significance might be diminished by its similarity to existing GAN architectures, which could lead to it being perceived as just another GAN variant. Lastly, the problem domain, while well-suited for vision and graphics applications, may not be easily generalizable to other learning problems.

The paper is well-organized and effectively conveys the key aspects of the proposed architecture. The use of a coarse-to-fine strategy, conditioned on appearance and pose information, is a suitable approach for the dressing problem. The difference map generation technique also shows promise for generating higher-resolution images.

However, the paper's major shortcoming is the presence of visible artifacts in the generated results, which still require substantial improvement for practical applications. For instance, the patterns in ID346 of Fig 4 result in black dots in the final image. Although the second generator mitigates the blurriness of the first generator, it appears that the model still struggles to recover high-frequency components in the target appearance.

Another potential concern, albeit less severe, is that some might view the proposed approach as an application of conditional GANs, as similar ideas have been explored in prior work, such as the Stacked GAN papers by Han Zhang et al. (arXiv:1612.03242) and Xun Huang et al. (arXiv:1612.04357). While the paper addresses application-specific challenges, this might limit its appeal to a broader audience.

Overall, the paper successfully proposes a solution to the pose-conditioned image generation problem and conducts a thorough evaluation. The approach demonstrates sufficient technical novelty, and although the resulting images require quality improvements, the proposed model generates visually consistent images. Based on this assessment, my initial rating is accept.
