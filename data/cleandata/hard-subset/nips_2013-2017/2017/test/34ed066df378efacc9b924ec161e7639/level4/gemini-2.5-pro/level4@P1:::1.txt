### Summary

This paper introduces a novel approach for generating images of a person by combining the appearance from a source image with a pose specified by a target set of keypoints. The proposed method, the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG²), employs a two-stage, coarse-to-fine architecture. In the first stage, a U-Net-like generator integrates the source image and a heatmap representation of the target pose to produce a coarse image that captures the global structure and pose. This stage is trained with a proposed masked L1 loss to focus learning on the foreground person. The second stage refines this coarse output using a conditional GAN, which learns to add finer details and textures, resulting in a more photorealistic image. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the Market-1501 and DeepFashion datasets, including extensive ablations of their design choices.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles the novel and challenging task of pose-guided person image generation. This is a significant problem with potential applications in data augmentation for tasks like pose estimation and person re-identification, as well as in content creation and editing. The proposed method provides a strong baseline for this new direction.
*   **Methodology:** The two-stage, coarse-to-fine architecture is a principled and logical way to address this difficult task. Decomposing the problem into first establishing the global structure (pose, color) and then refining the details (texture, sharpness) is an effective strategy. The use of a masked L1 loss to mitigate the influence of changing backgrounds is also a clever contribution.
*   **Thoroughness of Evaluation:** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of their architectural and loss function design choices. The qualitative comparisons between different pose embedding methods, loss functions, and the one-stage versus two-stage models clearly demonstrate the rationale behind the final proposed model.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The methodology is explained in sufficient detail, and the motivations for the two-stage design are well-argued.
*   **Qualitative Results:** While the generated images contain some discernible artifacts, the results are generally promising. The model is often successful at preserving the person's identity and clothing appearance while realistically transferring them to a new, and sometimes complex, pose.

### Weaknesses

*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** A primary weakness lies in the quantitative evaluation. As is common in generative modeling, standard metrics like SSIM and Inception Score do not always align with perceptual quality. The authors rightly note that blurrier images can achieve higher SSIM scores, which somewhat undermines the quantitative results.
*   **User Study:** The user study on Amazon Mechanical Turk is a good step towards perceptual evaluation, but it feels preliminary. The binary "real vs. fake" task is a very coarse measure of success and may not fully capture the nuances of generation quality (e.g., correct pose transfer vs. texture artifacts). A more substantive human evaluation, perhaps asking users to compare the outputs of different model variants directly, would significantly strengthen the paper's conclusions.

### Questions and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  The "coarse-to-fine" or iterative refinement strategy has strong parallels in other computer vision tasks, notably human pose estimation. The authors should consider discussing and citing related work on cascaded or stacked networks, such as Carreira et al. (2016) and Newell et al. (2016) [a, b], to better contextualize their architectural choice.
2.  On line 110, the authors state that "using residual blocks as basic component improves the generation performance." This claim should be substantiated. Could the authors please clarify in what specific way performance was improved (e.g., qualitatively, quantitatively, or in terms of training stability)?
3.  The paper compares the full two-stage model (G1+G2+D) against a one-stage model using the Stage 1 generator with adversarial training (G1+D). I am curious how a one-stage model using the more powerful, fully-convolutional Stage 2 architecture (G2) would perform when trained adversarially from scratch. Is the blurriness observed in the G1+D baseline primarily due to the difficulty of the one-stage task, or could the intermediate fully-connected layer in G1 be a contributing factor that limits detail preservation? An ablation on this would provide valuable insight into the necessity of the two-stage design versus simply using a better one-stage generator.

### Recommendation

Overall, this paper presents a novel and interesting problem, supported by a well-designed model and extensive experiments. Despite the inherent difficulties in quantitatively evaluating generative models, the qualitative results and thorough ablation studies demonstrate the merit of the proposed approach. The work is a solid contribution to the field of conditional image generation and provides a strong foundation for future research in controllable human image synthesis. Based on the novelty of the problem and the quality of the proposed solution, I confidently recommend that the paper be accepted.
