Summary:
This paper introduces the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), which synthesizes images of a person in arbitrary target poses given a reference image of that person. The approach is explicitly conditioned on pose, encoded as 18 keypoint heatmaps, and proceeds in two stages: (i) pose integration via a U-Net-like generator (G1) that produces a coarse result using a masked L1 loss to emphasize the human foreground; and (ii) image refinement via a conditional GAN (G2) that predicts a difference map to refine the stage-I output, trained with an adversarial loss and the same masked L1 regularizer. The discriminator operates on pairs (generated or real image, condition image) to enforce consistency with the conditioning input. Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) evaluate pose embeddings (coordinate, heatmap encoder, direct heatmap concatenation), the effect of the pose mask loss versus standard L1, and two-stage versus one-stage adversarial training. Quantitatively, the paper reports SSIM/IS and mask-SSIM/mask-IS to mitigate background effects; a small AMT study complements these results. Qualitatively, the method generates convincing images and improves over the most related prior work on multi-view person synthesis [36]. The paper is clearly situated in the literature on conditional generative models (e.g., CVAE, GANs) and pose estimation.

Strengths:
- Novel task formulation: conditioning on both a reference image and explicit target pose enables fine-grained, controllable person image synthesis.
- Clear, effective two-stage coarse-to-fine design that separates global structure (pose) from high-frequency detail.
- Good architectural choices: pose encoded as keypoint heatmaps; masked L1 to downweight background; GAN trained to produce a difference map; pairwise discriminator conditioning.
- Extensive ablations on pose embeddings, loss variants, and one-stage vs. two-stage training; comparisons on both low- and high-resolution datasets.
- Writing is clear and the figures convey the qualitative gains well.

Weaknesses:
- Quantitative evaluation remains limited, largely due to known shortcomings of SSIM/IS for perceptual realism; the proposed mask variants help but are still imperfect. This is understandable and not a major weakness.

Comments and suggestions:
- Related work: Given the iterative refinement nature of the approach, it would be useful to more explicitly relate PG2’s coarse-to-fine design to cascaded/iterative refinement architectures used in pose estimation, e.g.:
  [a] J. Carreira, P. Agrawal, K. Fragkiadaki, and J. Malik. Human pose estimation with iterative error feedback. CVPR, 2016.
  [b] A. Newell, K. Yang, and J. Deng. Stacked hourglass networks for human pose estimation. ECCV, 2016.
  While these works are cited, drawing the conceptual connection (and differences) would strengthen the positioning.
- Line 110: “we find that using residual blocks as basic component improves the generation performance.” Please elaborate. What aspects improve (e.g., SSIM/IS, AMT fooling rate, qualitative sharpness), and by how much? An ablation replacing residual blocks with plain conv layers would clarify the contribution.
- Architecture ablations:
  • One-stage adversarial training with G1 (G1+D) is evaluated. It would be informative to also report performance when using the G2 architecture (i.e., fully convolutional U-Net without the fully connected bottleneck) trained end-to-end from the stage-I inputs (IA and PB) in a single stage. This could isolate whether the blur observed with G1+GAN stems from the fully connected layer in G1 versus the benefits of two-stage optimization.
  • Similarly, quantify the training speed-up attributed to learning a difference map in G2 (e.g., convergence curves, epochs to reach a target metric).
- Masked loss: The masked L1 is a sensible choice given background mismatch. Please report how sensitive results are to mask quality and to the λ trade-off (even if this is partly covered in the supplement).
- Evaluation: The mask-SSIM/mask-IS adaptations are appreciated. Ultimately, human studies are crucial for perceptual realism. See rebuttal note below.

Assessment by review criteria:
- Quality: Technically sound with careful architectural design and ablations. Claims are supported by qualitative results and reasonable quantitative proxies; however, stronger human evaluation would further substantiate perceptual quality.
- Clarity: The paper is well organized and clearly written. Architectural details and training protocols are sufficiently described to support reproducibility.
- Originality: Conditioning person synthesis on explicit pose keypoints and decomposing the task into pose integration and adversarial refinement is a novel and compelling combination relative to prior view/attribute/text-conditioned generation.
- Significance: The method addresses a challenging and practically relevant problem (controllable human synthesis) and advances the state of the art qualitatively. The approach is likely to be used or extended in related tasks (e.g., data augmentation, graphics).

Overall rating:
Based on the novelty of the problem and approach, the soundness of the method, and the convincing qualitative results and ablations, I recommend acceptance.

Rebuttal:
I appreciate the attempt at a user study. However, given its very preliminary nature, I do not find it convincing and thus do not weigh it in my rating. I encourage the authors to include a more substantive human evaluation in the final version (e.g., larger sample, multiple exposure times, pairwise comparisons, and inter-rater consistency). My positive assessment of the core technical contribution remains unchanged.
