This paper proposes a method for generating images of humans conditioned on a target pose and an input image defining appearance. The core of the method is a two-stage generative adversarial network (GAN) designed to produce high-resolution results. The generator first creates a coarse, low-resolution image from the appearance and pose information using a U-Net-style architecture. A second generator then refines this coarse output by predicting a residual (or difference) map, which is added to an upsampled version of the coarse image to produce the final result. The entire model is trained and evaluated on the DeepFashion dataset.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper introduces a compelling and well-defined task: generating photorealistic images of a person by manipulating their pose while preserving their appearance. This is a challenging problem with clear applications in areas like virtual try-on, character animation, and image editing.

2.  **Coarse-to-Fine Architecture:** The proposed two-stage generator is a well-motivated design choice for this problem. The coarse-to-fine strategy is effective, and the specific technique of having the second stage predict a residual map to refine details is a clever way to handle the generation of high-frequency information, which is often a challenge for GANs.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The authors clearly explain the architecture, the loss functions, and the training procedure, making the method easy to understand and potentially reproduce. The experiments are appropriate for the task and provide a solid evaluation of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Quality of Generated Images:** A major concern is that the generated results, while often visually coherent, still contain noticeable artifacts that detract from their realism. As pointed out in the paper's own examples (e.g., Fig 4, ID346), complex patterns on clothing can be distorted into noisy textures like black dots. While the second generator improves upon the blurry output of the first, it still struggles to fully reconstruct fine, high-frequency details from the source appearance, leading to a loss of texture fidelity. For this method to be truly useful in practical applications, the quality and realism of the output images need significant improvement.

2.  **Novelty in the Context of GAN Architectures:** While the application is novel, the architectural contributions could be viewed as incremental. The use of conditional GANs, stacked generators, and coarse-to-fine refinement strategies has been explored in other contexts. For instance, recent works like StackGAN (Zhang et al., 2016) and the work by Huang et al. (2016) have proposed similar stacked generative architectures, albeit for different tasks. The primary novelty here lies in the successful application and adaptation of these ideas to the specific challenges of pose-conditioned human image generation. This may limit the paper's appeal to a broader machine learning audience focused on fundamental advances in generative models.

3.  **Limited Generality:** The work is presented as a strong vision and graphics application. However, the techniques are highly tailored to this specific domain, and it is not immediately clear how the architectural insights could generalize to other machine learning problems.

**Detailed Comments:**

The approach is well-suited to the target problem, and the use of a residual map in the refinement stage is a nice technical contribution for improving image resolution. The paper successfully demonstrates a complete system for a challenging generation task.

However, a potential concern regarding novelty is that the core approach can be framed as a sophisticated application of existing conditional GAN frameworks. The authors should consider positioning their work more explicitly in relation to other stacked and conditional GAN models to better highlight their unique contributions. While the cited related works are on arXiv, they represent concurrent ideas in the community that are important for context.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this paper presents a solid and interesting contribution to the field of generative image modeling. It successfully tackles the challenging problem of pose-conditioned human image synthesis with a well-designed coarse-to-fine architecture. The technical approach is sound, the evaluation is appropriate, and the paper is clearly presented. The main weaknesses are the visible artifacts in the generated images, which suggest that the model is not yet ready for real-world applications, and the somewhat limited novelty of the core generative architecture itself. Despite these limitations, the paper introduces a valuable problem and proposes a promising solution that achieves visually consistent results. It serves as a strong baseline for future work in this area. My recommendation is to accept the paper.
