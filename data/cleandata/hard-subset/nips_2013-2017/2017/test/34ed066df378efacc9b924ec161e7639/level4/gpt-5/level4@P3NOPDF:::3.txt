Summary:
This paper presents a GAN-based framework for novel-view human image synthesis conditioned on a target skeleton (keypoints). The system leverages established building blocks (e.g., DCGAN-style adversarial training and U-Net–like architectures) to generate person images matching a specified pose. The paper is clearly written and the task—pose-guided person image generation—is practically relevant. The primary contribution lies in applying and integrating known components to this specific problem.

Strengths:
- Clear problem setup and straightforward, readable exposition.
- The idea of pose-guided person synthesis is timely and useful for several applications (e.g., data augmentation, graphics).
- The method can produce images that are sometimes plausible at a glance, showing that the approach has potential.

Weaknesses:
- Lack of quantitative evaluation. For a practical, application-oriented work, rigorous metrics are essential. The paper does not provide user studies or quantitative measures of realism, identity preservation, or pose accuracy.
- Qualitative results are underwhelming. Many outputs contain noticeable artifacts and deviations from the target pose/appearance. Figures (e.g., Fig. 1) are too small to properly assess quality, and similar issues appear throughout the supplementary material.
- Limited novelty from a methodological standpoint. The design heavily relies on standard components (GAN + U-Net). Without strong empirical results, clear ablations, or new architectural insights, it is difficult to justify publication on novelty or technical depth.
- Insufficient comparisons. The paper does not include thorough baselines against closely related image-to-image and pose/appearance transfer methods (e.g., pix2pix, conditional GAN variants; pose-transfer or video/frame synthesis baselines available at the time). Without strong baselines, it is hard to attribute performance to the proposed design.
- Missing ablations. It is unclear which components (e.g., adversarial loss vs. L1/L2/perceptual losses, skip connections, multi-stage design) drive performance. An ablation study would strengthen the case for the chosen architecture.

Questions and suggestions for improvement:
- Quantitative metrics:
  - Human perceptual studies (pairwise preference or realism rating, with sufficient participants and images).
  - Fooling rate of a pretrained human/pose/action or person-classifier network (proxy for realism).
  - Identity preservation via re-identification similarity on benchmarks (e.g., Market-1501/DeepFashion if used), or face recognition when faces are visible.
  - Pose accuracy via a state-of-the-art keypoint detector applied to generated images (PCK/OKS relative to the target skeleton).
  - Distributional metrics (e.g., Inception Score, FID) computed carefully on a held-out set; if ground truth target images exist, also SSIM/PSNR/LPIPS for reference, while acknowledging their limitations for perceptual quality.
- Baselines:
  - Compare against pix2pix/conditional GANs trained directly on source-to-target pose pairs.
  - Include simpler alternatives (e.g., warping with optical flow or keypoint-driven deformation + refinement) to contextualize gains.
- Experimental details:
  - Report image resolution, training/inference speed, and memory footprint.
  - Provide dataset details, train/val/test splits, and identity overlap controls.
  - Show higher-resolution qualitative figures and zoomed-in crops on challenging regions (hands, faces, clothing details).
  - Discuss common failure modes (occlusions, extreme poses, rare clothing patterns) and the conditions under which the model succeeds.
- Analysis:
  - Conduct ablations on architectural choices and loss terms, and quantify their impact.
  - Evaluate generalization across identities and poses; measure performance as a function of pose displacement.
  - If feasible, test cross-dataset robustness.

After rebuttal:
I appreciate the additional materials, but I remain unconvinced. The quantitative results presented in the rebuttal are limited and do not adequately address the central concern: demonstrating convincing realism, pose fidelity, and identity preservation at scale. Qualitatively, the new examples still show prominent artifacts, and the figures remain too small or insufficiently curated for a fair assessment. Given the largely incremental methodology, the paper relies heavily on empirical strength to carry the contribution; at present, the evidence is not sufficient. I also note that other reviewers raised similar points regarding both quantitative and qualitative evaluations, and the rebuttal does not resolve them to a satisfactory degree.

Overall assessment:
The paper tackles a meaningful problem and presents a clear, well-motivated approach using established techniques. However, the current version falls short on the dimensions most critical for an application-driven contribution: strong, comprehensive evaluation and demonstrably high-quality results. I see promise in this direction and encourage the authors to strengthen the empirical study (robust metrics, solid baselines, ablations, and higher-resolution visuals) and refine the model to reduce artifacts. In its current form, I do not think it is ready for NIPS, but with improved evaluation and quality, it could become a compelling submission in a future iteration.
