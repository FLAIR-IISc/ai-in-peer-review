## Review of NIPS 2017 Submission

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel architecture for generating images of a person exhibiting a specified pose while retaining the appearance characteristics of an input image. The approach leverages a two-stage, coarse-to-fine generation process guided by pose heatmaps. The work addresses a challenging problem and presents a promising solution with visually compelling results, despite some noticeable artifacts.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The task of transferring appearance between images conditioned on a desired pose is a novel and interesting contribution. This work opens up possibilities for applications in virtual try-on, personalized avatar creation, and image manipulation.
* **Architecture:** The proposed two-stage coarse-to-fine architecture is well-motivated and effectively tackles the complexities of the problem. The separation of coarse pose generation and refinement allows for a more controlled and realistic output.
* **Clarity of Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly articulate their approach, design choices, and experimental setup.
* **Promising Results:** Despite the presence of some artifacts, the generated images demonstrate a significant degree of realism and convincingly portray the target pose while preserving the input person’s identity.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors provide an extensive ablation study, systematically evaluating the impact of different design decisions and components of their architecture. This demonstrates a strong understanding of their system and provides valuable insights.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Quantitative Evaluation:** The quantitative evaluation is understandably limited by the lack of established metrics for assessing the quality of this type of generative task. While the authors acknowledge this, it remains a weakness. Establishing even proxy metrics or exploring perceptual loss functions could strengthen the evaluation.
* **Missing Related Work:** Given the iterative refinement nature of the proposed architecture, it would benefit from a more thorough discussion of recent work employing cascaded networks for iterative refinement, such as Carreira et al. (2016) and Newell et al. (2016). These papers demonstrate effective strategies for progressively improving results through feedback loops.

**Comments:**

* **Line 110:** The statement "we find that using residual blocks as basic component improves the generation performance" lacks specific detail. Please elaborate on *how* residual blocks contribute to the performance improvement. Is it through gradient flow, improved representational capacity, or another mechanism?
* **Stage 2 Input & Adversarial Training:** I am curious about the potential performance of directly feeding the input of Stage 1 to Stage 2 and training the combined network adversarially. This configuration might mitigate the blurring observed when combining Stage 1 with a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN). Could this blurring be attributable to the intermediate fully connected layer in the Stage 1 architecture?
* **User Study:** While I appreciate the authors’ attempt to gather user feedback, the user study presented is preliminary and lacks sufficient rigor to be considered conclusive. I therefore do not factor it into my overall assessment. I strongly encourage the authors to conduct a more comprehensive human evaluation with a larger and more diverse participant pool, employing standardized evaluation protocols during the revision process.



**Overall Rating:**

Despite the limitations in quantitative evaluation and the preliminary nature of the user study, I confidently recommend acceptance of this paper. The novelty of the problem, the elegance of the proposed approach, and the promising results justify its inclusion in NIPS 2017. The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of image generation and has the potential to inspire further research.

**Rebuttal Considerations:**

The authors' attempt to address feedback with a user study was appreciated. However, as noted above, the initial user study was not sufficiently convincing. A more substantial and rigorously designed human evaluation would significantly strengthen the paper. Nevertheless, my positive assessment of the core contribution remains unchanged.
