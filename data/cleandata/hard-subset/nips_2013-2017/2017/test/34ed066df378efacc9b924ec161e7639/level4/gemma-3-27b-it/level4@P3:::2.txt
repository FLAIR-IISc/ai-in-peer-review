This paper proposes a novel approach to pose-guided person image generation, termed the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2). The method leverages a two-stage generative adversarial network (GAN) architecture. The first stage generates a coarse image from a reference image and a target pose, using a U-Net-like network. The second stage refines this initial result by predicting a difference map via another U-Net-like generator trained adversarially. The approach is evaluated on both a person re-identification dataset (Market-1501) and a fashion photo dataset (DeepFashion).

The core idea of conditioning image generation on both a reference image and a target pose is a valuable contribution, building on the recent surge of interest in controllable image synthesis. This work relates to earlier GAN-based image generation methods [6, 21, 5, 1] and extends them to a more specific and controlled manipulation – pose transfer. The proposed two-stage approach, mirroring strategies used in other image-to-image translation tasks [35, 9, 3, 19], is a reasonable strategy for tackling the complexity of this problem. The use of a difference map in the second stage is a clever technique for accelerating training and improving detail.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Task Formulation:** The problem of generating person images with specific poses, conditioned on a reference image, is a well-defined and potentially useful task with applications in areas like movie making and data augmentation for pose estimation.
* **Effective Architecture:** The two-stage approach with a U-Net-like generator followed by a refinement stage with a difference map appears well-motivated and provides a structured way to tackle the problem.
* **Pose Mask Loss:** The introduction of a pose mask loss is a thoughtful addition, effectively guiding the network to focus on the relevant parts of the image and mitigate the influence of background changes.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the proposed method, providing sufficient detail for reproduction.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Image Quality:** While the results are promising, the generated images still exhibit visible artifacts (e.g., the black dots mentioned in the original review, and generally blurry textures). The improvement in visual quality from stage one to stage two, while present, is not always substantial enough to produce truly photorealistic results.
* **Limited Significance:**  The work could be viewed as a specific application of conditional GANs. While the paper presents specific techniques to address this problem, the foundational elements are built upon established GAN principles. The novelty might not be sufficient to broadly impact the wider NIPS audience. Relevant works such as StackGAN [Zhang et al., 2016] and stacked GANs [Huang et al., 2016] demonstrate similar ideas in other domains.
* **Dataset Limitations:** While the use of two datasets is a positive, the evaluation is somewhat limited to datasets focusing on people.  It would be beneficial to explore the generalizability of the approach to other object categories.
* **Quantitative Evaluation:** The quantitative results, while reported, don't fully reflect the perceived visual quality issues. Relying solely on metrics like SSIM can be misleading, as noted in the review.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a novel and well-defined problem with a potentially impactful application.
* The proposed method is technically sound and demonstrates a reasonable approach to the problem.
* The paper includes insightful technical contributions, such as the pose mask loss and the difference map refinement strategy.
* The experimental results, while not perfect, demonstrate the feasibility of the approach and provide a solid foundation for future work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The generated images exhibit noticeable artifacts and lack the photorealism required for some applications.
* The paper builds heavily on established GAN techniques, potentially diminishing its overall novelty.
* The evaluation is limited to a specific domain (person images), raising questions about generalizability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a well-motivated and technically sound approach. While the image quality is not yet state-of-the-art, the proposed method demonstrates a promising direction for pose-guided image generation. The technical contributions, particularly the pose mask loss and the two-stage architecture, are valuable. Given the novelty of the task and the potential impact of the work, I lean towards acceptance, with the caveat that the authors should address the image quality concerns in future work.

**Rating:** Accept (Weak)
