

This paper proposes a two-stage framework for pose-guided person image generation, where the goal is to synthesize realistic images of a person in a target pose given a reference image and a target pose representation. The authors introduce a novel task of conditioning image generation on both a reference image and an explicit pose representation (keypoints), and propose a two-stage approach to address the challenge of simultaneously generating correct pose and detailed appearance. Stage I uses a U-Net-like architecture to integrate pose information with the reference image, generating a coarse pose-aligned image with a novel pose mask loss that focuses on human body regions rather than background. Stage II refines the initial result using a conditional GAN that generates a difference map between the initial result and target image, leading to sharper, more detailed outputs.

The paper makes a reasonable contribution to the field of conditional image generation by addressing a specific application domain with a well-structured approach. The work builds upon established techniques (U-Net, GANs) but applies them in a novel context with meaningful innovations like the pose mask loss. The authors relate their work to relevant NIPS literature on conditional image generation (e.g., [15, 22, 23, 24, 32, 36]), distinguishing their explicit pose guidance from previous work that used more abstract conditions like text descriptions or category labels.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness:** The two-stage approach is well-motivated and theoretically justified. The pose mask loss effectively addresses the challenge of background interference during pose transfer, and the difference map formulation in Stage II provides a clear technical contribution that speeds up training and improves quality.
2. **Experimental Rigor:** The paper evaluates on two relevant datasets (Market-1501 for re-identification, DeepFashion for fashion) with appropriate baselines. The ablation studies on pose embeddings and loss functions provide useful insights.
3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to experiments. The two-stage framework is easy to understand and well-illustrated.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Quality Assessment:** While the paper includes quantitative metrics (SSIM, Inception Score), these metrics do not fully capture perceptual quality. The qualitative results (Fig. 4) present significant challenges: the generated images are often too small to assess detail, and the visual comparisons show noticeable artifacts (e.g., incorrect body parts, color mismatches, blurry textures). The user study is limited to 30 participants and lacks statistical significance testing, making it difficult to assess the claimed "photo-realistic" quality.
2. **Significance Claims:** The paper overstates the quality of results. While the two-stage approach improves upon a one-stage model, the qualitative results (particularly for Market-1501) still contain numerous visible errors. The claim that results are "photo-realistic" is not convincingly supported by the presented evidence.
3. **Novelty Context:** While the specific application is novel, the core techniques (U-Net, GANs) are well-established. The paper could better highlight how this specific application advances the field beyond incremental improvements.

**Evaluation:**
- **Quality:** The technical approach is sound and well-executed, but the quality of results doesn't fully support the paper's claims. The lack of robust quantitative evaluation of perceptual quality is a significant weakness.
- **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized, though the presentation of results (particularly figure resolution) undermines the clarity of the qualitative assessment.
- **Originality:** The specific application of pose-guided person generation is novel, but the core techniques are not. The pose mask loss is a reasonable innovation but not groundbreaking.
- **Significance:** The problem has practical applications, but the current results don't convincingly demonstrate a significant advance over existing methods. The improvements over [36] are visible but not transformative.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**
*Pro:* The paper presents a well-structured approach to a meaningful application, with a technically sound two-stage framework and reasonable experimental validation. The pose mask loss is a useful contribution to the field of conditional image generation.
*Con:* The claimed quality of results is not sufficiently supported by the evidence. The qualitative results contain noticeable artifacts that undermine the "photo-realistic" claims, and the quantitative evaluation lacks sufficient depth to validate the claims.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper addresses an interesting application with a reasonable technical approach, the current results do not sufficiently demonstrate the quality claimed in the paper. The visual evidence presented does not convincingly support the "photo-realistic" quality claim, and the quantitative evaluation is limited. The paper would benefit from more rigorous perceptual evaluation (e.g., larger user studies with statistical significance testing) and better presentation of results (higher resolution images that allow for proper assessment of detail). As currently written, the paper overstates the quality of results relative to the evidence provided. Given the high standards of NIPS, I recommend rejection, though the authors should be encouraged to address these concerns in future work. The direction is promising, but the current evidence does not meet the threshold for acceptance at NIPS 2017.
