The paper proposes a human image generator that is conditioned on both appearance and human pose. This generation is based on an adversarial training architecture, utilizing a two-step generative network that produces high-resolution images to be fed into a discriminator. In the generator component, the first generator produces a coarse image using a U-shaped network, given an appearance and pose map. The second generator then takes this coarse input, along with the original appearance, to predict a residual that refines the coarse image. The DeepFashion dataset is used for evaluation.

The paper introduces several important ideas, including the novelty of the task, which involves conditioning on appearance and pose maps for human image generation. Additionally, the paper presents a stacked architecture that predicts a difference map rather than relying on direct upsampling, as well as a unique loss design.

However, there are several areas in which the paper can be improved to stand out. Firstly, the generated results still require significant quality improvement. Furthermore, the significance of the paper may be diminished by the fact that it can be seen as yet another GAN architecture. Lastly, the problem domain, while well-suited to vision and graphics applications, may be difficult to generalize to other learning problems.

The paper is well-organized and effectively conveys the key aspects of the proposed architecture. By conditioning on appearance and pose information, the proposed generator employs a coarse-to-fine strategy, stacking two networks to achieve this goal. This approach is well-suited to the dressing problem, and the generation strategy is effectively utilized. The use of difference map generation is also a notable technique for producing higher-resolution images.

One of the major concerns with the paper is that the generated results contain visible artifacts and still require significant improvement for practical application. For example, the patterns in ID346 of Fig 4 result in black dots in the final image. Although the second generator mitigates the blurriness of the image produced by the first generator, it appears that the model is still insufficient to recover high-frequency components in the target appearance.

Another potential concern, albeit less severe, is that some may view the proposed approach as an application of conditional GANs. While the paper does present application-specific challenges, the fact that conditioning or stacking of generators for adversarial training has been proposed in the past (e.g., Han Zhang et al., "StackGAN: Text to Photo-realistic Image Synthesis with Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks", arXiv:1612.03242, and Xun Huang et al., "Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks", arXiv:1612.04357) may reduce its appeal to a broader audience.

Overall, the paper successfully proposes a solution to the pose-conditioned image problem and conducts a thorough evaluation. The proposed approach demonstrates sufficient technical novelty, and while the resulting images still require quality improvement, the proposed model is at least able to generate visually consistent images. Based on this, my initial rating is to accept.
