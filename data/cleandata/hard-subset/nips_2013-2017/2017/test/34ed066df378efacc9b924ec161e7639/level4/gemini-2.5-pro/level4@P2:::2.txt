This paper introduces a method for generating images of a person in a novel, target pose, conditioned on a single source image of that person. The proposed model, the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG²), employs a two-stage, coarse-to-fine strategy. The first stage uses a U-Net-like generator (G1) to integrate the source image with a heatmap representation of the target pose, producing an initial, structurally correct but coarse output. A key contribution in this stage is a novel "pose mask loss," which encourages the model to focus on generating the person rather than replicating the background. The second stage refines this coarse output using another U-Net-like generator (G2) trained within a conditional GAN framework. Notably, G2 is trained to predict a residual or "difference map" to add high-frequency details to the Stage 1 result. The approach is evaluated on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets.

This work builds upon the extensive literature on conditional generative models, particularly conditional GANs. While prior work has conditioned generation on class labels, text, or coarse viewpoints, this paper's contribution is to use an explicit and detailed pose representation (keypoint heatmaps) for fine-grained control over the synthesized human figure. The two-stage architecture is conceptually similar to other stacked generative models, such as StackGAN, which also adopt a coarse-to-fine refinement process for a different task (text-to-image synthesis).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Significant Task:** The paper addresses the novel problem of pose-guided person image generation. This is a challenging task with clear applications in areas like data augmentation for pose estimation, virtual try-on for fashion, and creative tools for media production. The formulation of conditioning on both an appearance image and an explicit pose structure is a compelling direction for controllable image synthesis.

2.  **Well-Motivated Architecture:** The two-stage, coarse-to-fine strategy is a logical and effective way to tackle this complex problem. By decomposing the task into first establishing the global pose and structure (Stage 1) and then filling in appearance details (Stage 2), the model can handle the significant geometric and appearance transformations required.

3.  **Effective Technical Contributions:** The paper introduces several thoughtful technical details. The idea of training the second generator (G2) to predict a difference map is a nice touch, as it simplifies the learning problem from generating a full image to refining an already reasonable estimate. Furthermore, the proposed pose mask loss is a practical solution to the problem of background variation between source and target images, effectively focusing the learning process on the foreground subject.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors effectively walk the reader through their motivation, methodology, and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Image Quality and Artifacts:** The primary weakness is that the quality of the generated images, while promising, has significant room for improvement. The results often contain visible artifacts that betray their synthetic origin. For example, as seen in Figure 4 (ID 346), complex patterns on clothing can be distorted into noisy black dots in the final output. It appears the model, even with the refinement stage, struggles to fully recover high-frequency textures and details from the source appearance, leading to a loss of fidelity.

2.  **Incremental Novelty of Architecture:** While the application is novel, the core architectural components are based on well-established techniques (U-Nets, conditional GANs). The idea of stacking generators has also been explored in prior work (e.g., StackGAN by Zhang et al., 2016; SAGAN by Huang et al., 2016). Therefore, the work could be viewed as a successful and clever application of existing generative modeling techniques to a new domain, rather than a fundamental architectural breakthrough.

3.  **Limited Scope of Significance:** The problem domain is highly relevant to the computer vision and graphics communities. However, its direct impact or generalizability to other machine learning problems might be limited, which could narrow its appeal to the broader NIPS audience.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

Overall, this paper successfully proposes a solution for the novel and challenging task of pose-conditioned person image generation. The two-stage architecture is well-designed, and the technical contributions, such as the difference map refinement and the pose mask loss, are appropriate and effective for the problem at hand. The experimental evaluation is thorough and properly conducted.

The main drawback is the quality of the final images, which still contain noticeable artifacts and fail to preserve fine-grained details. However, the model consistently generates visually coherent images that correctly match the target pose while retaining the general appearance of the source person. Despite the architectural components being largely based on existing work, their combination and application to this new problem are sufficiently novel. The paper presents a complete piece of research that is likely to be of interest to the community and inspire future work in controllable image generation.

Therefore, my recommendation is to **Accept**.
