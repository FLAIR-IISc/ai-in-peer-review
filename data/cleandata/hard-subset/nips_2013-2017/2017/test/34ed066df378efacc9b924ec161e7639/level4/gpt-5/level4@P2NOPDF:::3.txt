This paper presents a GAN-based system for synthesizing person images in new viewpoints guided by a target skeleton defined by keypoints. The architecture relies largely on established components (e.g., DCGAN-style generators and U-Net skip connections), so the primary contribution is in applying these ideas to pose-guided human image synthesis.

Pros:
- The problem is interesting and relevant, and the paper is clearly written.
- The experiments indicate that the method can produce images that look realistic to some extent.

Cons:
- Given the application-driven nature of the work, strong empirical validation is essential. My main concern is the absence of quantitative evaluation. For instance, a user study could measure how often generated images fool human observers. Without such evidence, it is difficult to assess progress or compare to baselines.
- The qualitative results are not compelling. In Figure 1, the generated images are too small to inspect properly; even at that size, numerous visible artifacts appear when compared to the targets. This makes it hard to conclude that the outputs are of high quality or close to real images. Similar issues persist across most figures, including the supplementary material. While the direction is promising, the current results do not yet justify strong claims.

Overall assessment:
The idea is timely and the approach is promising, but the paper lacks the quantitative and qualitative evidence needed to support its claims. Improved visual quality and a rigorous evaluation—especially a user study or similar perceptual assessment—would significantly strengthen the work.

After rebuttal:
I remain unconvinced by the authors’ response. As other reviewers also noted, both quantitative and qualitative shortcomings persist, which is critical for a practically oriented contribution. The rebuttal offers some attempts at addressing these points, but they do not sufficiently resolve the concerns. In its current form, the work does not yet meet the standard for a venue like NIPS.
