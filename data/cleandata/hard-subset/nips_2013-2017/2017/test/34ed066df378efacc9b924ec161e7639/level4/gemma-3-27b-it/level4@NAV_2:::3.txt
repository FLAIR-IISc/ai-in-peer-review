This paper develops a GAN-based system for generating novel-view person images guided by a target pose, represented as keypoints. The system leverages established techniques like DCGANs and U-nets, positioning the primary contribution within a specific computer vision application.

**Summary of Ideas and Relation to Previous Work:** The authors address the problem of pose-guided person image generation, a task with applications in areas like movie making and data augmentation for pose estimation. This builds upon the recent surge in generative image modeling, particularly GANs [6], demonstrated in prior works for face generation [21, 5, 1] and scene synthesis. The approach specifically aims to control image generation through explicit pose conditioning, differentiating it from methods relying on abstract cues like text [24, 22, 23] or category labels [32, 18]. It’s related to multi-view synthesis [36, 4, 33, 34], but argues that pose information provides a more expressive conditioning signal than viewpoint. The two-stage architecture, separating pose integration and detail refinement, draws inspiration from earlier works [35, 9, 3, 19].

**Strengths:**
*   **Interesting Problem:** The task of pose-guided person image generation is well-motivated and has practical relevance.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, clearly articulating the proposed method and its components.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The authors demonstrate the feasibility of their approach with experiments on two datasets, showing some level of realistic image synthesis.
*   **Novel Combination:** The two-stage architecture combining pose integration with adversarial refinement represents a sensible strategy for tackling the complexity of the problem. The use of a difference map in the second stage is also a clever approach to accelerate training.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Lack of Comprehensive Quantitative Evaluation:** A significant weakness is the absence of robust quantitative metrics. While the authors present SSIM and Inception Score (Table 1), these are known to sometimes correlate poorly with perceptual quality [10, 17, 12, 27]. The inclusion of a user study would significantly strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Limited Visual Quality:** The presented results, even in supplementary materials, are not consistently visually compelling. Figures, including Fig. 1, often show small images making detailed assessment difficult. Noticeable artifacts and discrepancies between generated and target images are present, which raises concerns about the overall quality of the results.
*   **Limited Comparison:** While [36] is compared, the comparison is limited, and the advantages shown aren’t consistently compelling. A comparison against more recent or stronger baselines would be valuable.
*   **Dataset Bias/Limitations:** The discussion of failure cases (Section 4.4) hints at potential biases in the training data (e.g., gender imbalance, pose diversity), which could affect generalization.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem with a novel approach.
*   The method demonstrates a proof-of-concept for pose-guided image generation.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The lack of robust quantitative evaluation limits confidence in the reported results.
*   The visual quality of the generated images is not consistently high enough to demonstrate a significant advance.
*   The comparison to related work is limited and does not fully establish the novelty or superiority of the proposed approach.



**Overall Assessment:** While the idea presented in this paper is promising, the current experimental results and lack of comprehensive evaluation prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance to NIPS. Addressing the weaknesses, particularly through more robust quantitative metrics (e.g., user study) and improved visual quality of the generated images, would significantly strengthen the contribution.
