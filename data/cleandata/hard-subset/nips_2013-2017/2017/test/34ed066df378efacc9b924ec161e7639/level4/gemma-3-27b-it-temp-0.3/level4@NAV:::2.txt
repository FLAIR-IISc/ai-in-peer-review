This paper introduces a human image generator that leverages both appearance and pose information as conditioning inputs. The core of the method is an adversarial training framework employing a two-stage generative process to produce high-resolution images, evaluated on the DeepFashion dataset. The generator consists of an initial U-Net-based network that creates a coarse image from appearance and pose maps, followed by a second generator that refines this output by predicting a residual map, incorporating the original appearance information.

The work presents several noteworthy contributions: a novel approach to conditioning image generation on appearance and pose, a stacked architecture utilizing difference map prediction instead of direct upsampling, and a carefully designed loss function. 

However, the paper could be strengthened by addressing the following points: further improvements to the visual quality of the generated images are needed, the work risks being perceived as another incremental GAN architecture, and the problem domain, while relevant to vision and graphics, may limit its generalizability to other machine learning areas.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the proposed architecture. The coarse-to-fine strategy, implemented through the stacked generator network conditioned on appearance and pose, is effectively applied to the problem of human image generation, particularly in the context of dressing applications. The use of a difference map for high-resolution image generation is a subtle but valuable technique.

The primary concern is the presence of visible artifacts in the generated results, hindering their practical applicability. For instance, the example in ID346 of Fig 4 exhibits black dot artifacts. While the second generator reduces blurriness from the first, it appears insufficient to fully reconstruct high-frequency details in the target appearance. 

A secondary, though less critical, point is that the approach could be viewed as a specific application of conditional GANs. Prior work has explored conditioning and stacked generator architectures for adversarial training (e.g., Zhang et al., 2016; Huang et al., 2016), although this paper addresses application-specific challenges.

Overall, the paper successfully proposes and evaluates a solution to pose-conditioned human image generation, demonstrating sufficient technical novelty. While the image quality requires further refinement, the model produces visually coherent results. My initial assessment is to accept.
