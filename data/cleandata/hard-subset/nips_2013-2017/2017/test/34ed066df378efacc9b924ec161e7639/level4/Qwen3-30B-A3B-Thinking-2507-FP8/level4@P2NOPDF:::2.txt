

The paper introduces a human image generator conditioned on both appearance and pose information, employing a two-stage generative architecture trained adversarially. The first generator, implemented as a U-shape network, produces a coarse image from appearance and pose maps. The second generator then refines this output by predicting a residual map (using the original appearance) to enhance detail, rather than directly upsampling. Evaluation is conducted on the DeepFashion dataset.

The work demonstrates notable contributions in two key areas. First, it introduces a novel conditioning paradigm for human image generation—simultaneously leveraging appearance and pose maps to guide synthesis. Second, it proposes a stacked generator architecture that predicts residual maps for refinement, alongside a tailored loss design, which effectively addresses high-resolution output challenges.

Despite these strengths, the paper faces three critical limitations that hinder its impact. **Quality** remains a significant concern: generated images exhibit visible artifacts (e.g., black dots in ID346 of Fig. 4), and high-frequency details remain poorly reconstructed despite the residual refinement step. While the second generator mitigates blurriness from the first, the model struggles to recover fine textures, limiting practical applicability. **Significance** is also questionable; the approach resembles a specialized application of conditional GANs, with stacking and conditioning techniques previously explored in works like StackGAN (Zhang et al., 2016) and Stacked GANs (Huang et al., 2016). Although the paper addresses domain-specific challenges, this narrow scope may deter broader interest from the machine learning community. **Problem domain** further restricts generalizability; the solution is tightly coupled to human image synthesis, offering limited transferability to other vision or learning tasks.

The paper is well-structured and clearly communicates its core methodology. The coarse-to-fine strategy, leveraging appearance and pose conditioning, is logically presented and well-suited to the dressing problem. The residual-based refinement technique is a notable, albeit incremental, contribution to high-resolution image generation. The evaluation appropriately assesses the model’s performance on DeepFashion.

However, the persistent quality issues are a major drawback. Artifacts like those in Fig. 4 suggest the model has not yet achieved sufficient fidelity for real-world deployment, and the authors provide limited discussion on why high-frequency components remain elusive. Regarding the "yet-another-GAN" critique, the paper’s application-specific focus does not sufficiently distinguish it from prior work. While the cited arXiv papers (StackGAN, Huang et al.) demonstrate similar architectural principles, the current work’s novelty hinges on its specific conditioning setup and residual refinement—not on fundamentally new mechanisms. This weakens the technical contribution’s perceived novelty for a venue like NIPS.

In summary, the paper presents a technically sound solution to pose-conditioned human image synthesis with a clear methodology and adequate evaluation. The residual-based refinement is a thoughtful technique, and the approach is well-motivated for its target application. However, the unresolved quality limitations and lack of broader significance prevent it from standing out among the growing body of GAN-based image synthesis work. The model generates visually consistent outputs but falls short of producing artifact-free, high-fidelity results required for practical use.

Given the strong problem formulation and clear presentation, I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the quality limitations should be addressed in future work. The paper warrants publication as a solid, application-focused contribution, though its broader impact remains constrained by the identified weaknesses.
