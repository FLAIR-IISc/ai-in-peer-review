This paper introduces a method for pose-guided person image generation, a novel task that aims to synthesize an image of a person in a specified target pose, conditioned on a source image of that same person. The proposed system, the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG²), tackles this challenge using a two-stage approach. The first stage utilizes a U-Net-like architecture to generate a coarse image that captures the global structure and colors from the source image in the target pose. The second stage employs a conditional GAN to refine this coarse output, with the goal of adding realistic details and textures. The overall framework builds upon well-established deep generative models, such as U-Nets and GANs, combining them in a coarse-to-fine pipeline for this specific computer vision application.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a novel and interesting problem. The ability to realistically manipulate a person's pose in an image has significant potential applications in areas like data augmentation for human pose estimation, special effects, and e-commerce. The proposed task of conditioning generation on an explicit pose representation is a compelling direction for controllable image synthesis.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is well-written and easy to follow. The two-stage "divide-and-conquer" approach is logically motivated, clearly explained, and well-illustrated in the figures.
*   **Methodology:** The proposed architecture is technically sound. Decomposing the difficult end-to-end task into two stages—first capturing the global structure and then refining appearance details—is a sensible strategy. The introduction of a pose mask loss to focus the learning on the human body rather than the potentially changing background is a thoughtful contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Qualitative Results:** A primary concern for an application-oriented paper is the quality of the final results. While the method successfully transfers the general pose and appearance, the generated images often fall short of being photo-realistic. Many examples in Figure 4 and the supplement appear blurry, lack fine-grained texture detail, and contain noticeable visual artifacts when compared to the ground truth images. The figures themselves are presented at a small scale, which makes a detailed assessment of the quality difficult. The results are promising, but the claim of generating "high-quality person images with convincing details" seems premature at this stage.
*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** The quantitative support for the method's performance could be stronger. The authors commendably include both automated metrics (SSIM, IS) and a user study. However, as they rightly note, pixel-based metrics like SSIM are often poorly correlated with human perception of quality in generative tasks. Furthermore, the user study results, while a valuable addition, indicate that the generated images can fool a human observer in only a minority of cases (e.g., 14.9% on the DeepFashion dataset). This suggests a significant perceptual gap still exists between the synthesized results and real images.

In summary, the paper introduces a promising research direction and presents a logical first-step architecture. However, the work in its current form feels preliminary. The visual quality of the generated images needs substantial improvement to be considered compelling, and the evaluation does not yet convincingly demonstrate state-of-the-art performance in realistic image synthesis. While the direction is valuable, the execution and results may not yet meet the high bar for a top-tier conference like NIPS.
