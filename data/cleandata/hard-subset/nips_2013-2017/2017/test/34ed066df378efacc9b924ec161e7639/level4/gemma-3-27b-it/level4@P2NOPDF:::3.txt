This paper presents a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN)-based system for generating novel views of a person given a target skeleton, represented as keypoints. The architecture largely utilizes established techniques – DCGANs and U-nets – positioning the primary contribution as a specific application of these methods within computer vision.

**Strengths:**

* The core idea is compelling and the paper is well-written and easy to understand.
* The empirical results demonstrate the potential to synthesize visually plausible images, albeit to a limited degree.

**Weaknesses:**

* Given the application-oriented nature of this work, strong results are crucial. A significant shortcoming is the complete lack of quantitative evaluation. A user study, for example, could assess the realism of the generated images by measuring their ability to convincingly deceive human observers.
* The visual quality of the empirical results is underwhelming. Figure 1, and indeed most of the presented images (including those in the supplementary material), are too small for detailed inspection. Even at this size, noticeable visual artifacts are apparent when comparing the generated images to the corresponding target skeletons. Consequently, the claim of “good quality” or near-realistic results feels unsupported. While the authors are clearly pursuing a promising direction, the current results do not yet warrant a strong conclusion.

**Overall Assessment:**

This work shows initial promise, but requires significant refinement before it would be suitable for presentation at a venue like NIPS. The lack of quantitative assessment is a major concern, particularly given the practical focus of the application. The current visual results, while suggestive, are not compelling enough to demonstrate a substantial advancement. More robust evaluation and improved visual fidelity are necessary to establish the effectiveness of the proposed system.

**Post-Rebuttal Assessment:**

I remain unconvinced following the authors’ response. Several reviewers, including myself, have highlighted the continuing deficiencies in both quantitative and qualitative evaluation. These are critical aspects for a practically-oriented contribution. The rebuttal demonstrates some attempt to address these concerns, but the responses do not fully alleviate them. I believe the work is not yet mature enough for presentation at a highly competitive conference such as NIPS.
