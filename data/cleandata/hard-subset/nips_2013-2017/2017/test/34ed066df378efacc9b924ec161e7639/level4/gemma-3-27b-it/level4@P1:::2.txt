The paper proposes a novel approach to person image generation conditioned on both appearance and pose. The proposed method, Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), employs a two-stage adversarial training architecture to synthesize high-resolution images. The first generator produces a coarse image from an input image and a pose map, utilizing a U-Net-like network. The second generator then refines this coarse output by predicting a residual, effectively enhancing the image quality. The method is evaluated on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets.

The paper introduces several noteworthy ideas:

* **Task Novelty:** The concept of conditioning image generation on both appearance and pose is a valuable contribution.
* **Technical Contributions:** The stacked architecture predicting a difference map, rather than directly upsampling, and the design of the pose mask loss are significant technical elements.

However, the paper could be strengthened in the following areas:

* **Generation Quality:** While the results are promising, the generated images still exhibit visible artifacts and require further improvement for practical applications.
* **Significance:** The work could be viewed as another instance of a GAN architecture, potentially limiting its broader impact.
* **Generalizability:** The problem domain, while relevant to vision and graphics, may not readily generalize to other machine learning problems.

The paper is well-organized and clearly conveys the key aspects of the proposed architecture. The conditioning on appearance and pose information, combined with the coarse-to-fine strategy implemented through stacked networks, is a sensible approach for this problem. The use of a difference map for refinement is a clever technique to improve high-resolution image generation.

A primary concern is the presence of visible artifacts in the generated results, hindering their practical applicability. For example, the images for ID 346 in Figure 4 exhibit black dots. While the second generator mitigates the blurriness from the first, the model appears insufficient to fully recover high-frequency details in the target appearance.

Another, less critical, point is that the approach could be perceived as an application of conditional GANs. Conditioning and stacking generators within an adversarial training framework have been explored previously (e.g., Zhang et al., StackGAN; Huang et al., Stacked GANs). Although the paper addresses application-specific challenges, this may limit its appeal to a wider audience.

* Han Zhang, Tao Xu, Hongsheng Li, Shaoting Zhang, Xiaolei Huang, Xiaogang Wang, Dimitris Metaxas, "StackGAN: Text to Photo-realistic Image Synthesis with Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks", arXiv:1612.03242.
* Xun Huang, Yixuan Li, Omid Poursaeed, John Hopcroft, Serge Belongie, Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks, arXiv:1612.04357.

Overall, the paper successfully proposes a solution to the pose-conditioned image generation problem and presents a thorough evaluation. The approach demonstrates sufficient technical novelty, and the generated images exhibit visual consistency, though quality improvements are needed. My initial rating is accept.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel task formulation: conditioning image generation on appearance and pose.
*   Effective two-stage architecture for addressing the complexity of the problem.
*   Well-structured presentation and clear explanation of the method.
*   Demonstrated improvement over a single-stage approach.
*   Quantitative and qualitative results support the claims.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Generated images still contain visible artifacts.
*   Potential for being viewed as a specific application of existing GAN techniques.
*   Limited generalizability to other learning domains.
