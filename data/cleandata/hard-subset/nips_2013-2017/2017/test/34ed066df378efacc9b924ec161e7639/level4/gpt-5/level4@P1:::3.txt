This paper presents a GAN-based system for generating images of a person in a specified target pose (represented by keypoint skeletons), given a reference image of that person. The approach relies on largely standard components, including U-Net-style generators and DCGAN-style adversarial training, so the primary contribution is the specific application to pose-guided person image synthesis rather than novel methodology.

Pros:
- The idea is interesting and practically relevant. Conditioning generation on pose is a natural and useful direction.
- The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.
- Empirical demonstrations show that the method can produce images that look realistic to some extent.

Cons:
- Given the application-driven nature of the work, empirical quality and rigorous evaluation are crucial. My main concerns are:
  - There is no substantive quantitative evaluation. A user study would be particularly appropriate here to assess how often generated images fool human observers. Objective metrics (e.g., SSIM, Inception Score, FID) would also help, with careful handling of background differences.
  - The visual quality of the results is not compelling. For example, in Figure 1 the generated images are too small to judge, and even at that scale there are many noticeable artifacts when compared to the targets. Similar issues appear throughout the main paper and supplementary material. As a result, it is difficult to conclude that the current system produces images that are close to real. The direction is promising, but the results do not yet justify a strong claim of success.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a thorough quantitative evaluation, including:
  - A user study (e.g., two-alternative forced choice with brief exposure) to measure fooling rates.
  - Objective image-quality metrics tailored to the person region (e.g., mask-based SSIM/IS/FID) to reduce confounding by background changes.
- Improve the presentation of qualitative results:
  - Use higher-resolution figures and include zoomed-in crops of key regions (faces, hands, clothing textures).
  - Provide systematic side-by-side comparisons with targets, as well as with strong baselines, and include a clear failure-case analysis.
- Clarify the scope and limitations (e.g., pose complexity, occlusion, viewpoint, and background shift), and quantify performance across these factors if possible.

After rebuttal:
I remain unconvinced by the response. As also noted by other reviewers, both the quantitative and qualitative evidence is still insufficient for a practically oriented paper. While the rebuttal includes some attempts at additional evaluation, they do not satisfactorily address the core concerns above. In its current form, I do not think the paper is ready for a strong presentation at NIPS.
