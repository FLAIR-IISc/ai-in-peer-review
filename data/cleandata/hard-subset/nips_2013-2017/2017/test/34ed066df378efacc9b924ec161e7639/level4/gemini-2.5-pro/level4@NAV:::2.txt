This paper introduces a method for generating human images, conditioned on a target pose and a source appearance. The methodology is built upon a generative adversarial network (GAN) framework, featuring a two-stage generator designed to produce high-resolution outputs for the discriminator. Specifically, the generator consists of two stages: the first employs a U-Net architecture to produce a coarse rendering from the appearance and pose maps. The second stage then refines this initial output by taking the coarse image and the original appearance as input to predict a residual map, thereby adding finer details. The authors validate their approach using the DeepFashion dataset.

The primary contributions of this work are:
*   **Task Formulation:** The paper presents a novel application by conditioning human image synthesis on both appearance and a target pose.
*   **Architectural Design:** The work introduces a stacked generative architecture that refines images by predicting a residual map, a notable alternative to direct upsampling. The design of the loss function is also a key contribution.

To strengthen the paper further and enhance its impact, the authors could address the following points:
*   **Output Quality:** The quality of the generated images, while promising, has noticeable room for improvement.
*   **Perceived Novelty:** The framework might be viewed as an incremental improvement or another variation within the extensive literature on GAN architectures.
*   **Generalizability:** While the method is effective for its specific vision/graphics application, its applicability to broader machine learning problems is not immediately apparent.

The manuscript is well-structured and clearly communicates the core components of the proposed architecture. The generator's coarse-to-fine strategy, implemented via two stacked networks conditioned on appearance and pose, is explained effectively. This generation strategy is aptly applied to the virtual try-on problem, and the overall approach is well-suited to the target problem domain. Furthermore, the concept of generating a residual map for refinement is a subtle but effective technique for improving image resolution.

The most significant limitation of the current work lies in the quality of the generated images, which exhibit visible artifacts and would require substantial enhancement for practical applications. For instance, as shown with ID346 in Figure 4, clothing patterns can be distorted into artifacts like black dots in the final synthesis. Although the second generator successfully sharpens the blurry output from the first, the model still appears to struggle with reconstructing high-frequency details from the source appearance.

A secondary concern, though less critical, is the potential perception of this work as a direct application of conditional GANs. The core ideas of conditioning and stacking generators for adversarial training have been explored previously in works such as StackGAN (Zhang et al., arXiv:1612.03242; Huang et al., arXiv:1612.04357), even if these are primarily available as pre-prints. While the paper does tackle challenges specific to its application domain, this narrow focus might limit its appeal to the broader machine learning community.

In summary, the paper presents a successful solution for pose-conditioned human image generation and supports it with a proper experimental evaluation. The proposed method demonstrates sufficient technical novelty. While the quality of the synthesized images requires further improvement, the model is capable of producing visually coherent results. Therefore, my initial recommendation is to accept the paper.
