The paper presents a person image synthesis approach conditioned on both appearance and human pose. The method adopts a two-stage generation pipeline with adversarial refinement: a first U-shaped network produces a coarse image given a reference appearance and pose representation, and a second generator refines this result by predicting a residual (difference) map conditioned on the coarse output and the original appearance. The evaluation uses datasets such as DeepFashion.

The paper contributes several notable ideas.
* Task novelty: explicitly conditioning generation on a reference appearance and a pose map for human image synthesis.
* Techniques: a stacked, coarse-to-fine architecture that predicts a difference map rather than directly synthesizing the final image, along with a tailored loss design.

The work could be strengthened in the following respects.
* Output fidelity still needs improvement.
* Significance: the method risks being viewed as another variant within the conditional GAN design space.
* Scope: while compelling for vision/graphics, it may be hard to generalize to broader learning problems.

The manuscript is well structured and clearly conveys the key components of the architecture. By conditioning on appearance and pose, the proposed generator employs a two-stage, coarse-to-fine strategy that is well matched to the task. The approach is a reasonable fit for the “dressing”/pose-transfer scenario. The residual/difference-map prediction is a modest but effective technique for sharpening and adding detail at higher resolutions.

The main concern is that synthesized images still show visible artifacts and fall short of application-level quality. For instance, in Fig. 4 (ID 346), texture transfer introduces black speckling in the final output. Although the second generator reduces the blur from stage I, the model often fails to recover fine-grained, high-frequency details in the target appearance.

A secondary, less critical concern is that some may view this as an application of conditional GANs with stacking, which has prior art. Related ideas on conditioning and multi-stage adversarial training have appeared, e.g.:
* Han Zhang, Tao Xu, Hongsheng Li, Shaoting Zhang, Xiaolei Huang, Xiaogang Wang, Dimitris Metaxas, “StackGAN: Text to Photo-realistic Image Synthesis with Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks,” arXiv:1612.03242.
* Xun Huang, Yixuan Li, Omid Poursaeed, John Hopcroft, Serge Belongie, “Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks,” arXiv:1612.04357.

Overall, the paper presents a sound solution to pose-conditioned person image generation with appropriate evaluation. The approach offers sufficient technical novelty and delivers visually coherent results despite remaining quality issues. My preliminary rating is accept.
