

This paper introduces a novel two-stage framework, Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), for synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses based on a reference image and target pose. The approach explicitly leverages pose information through a pose mask loss and a divide-and-conquer strategy, addressing a gap in previous work that typically used less expressive conditioning (e.g., [36] used viewpoint descriptions rather than explicit pose keypoints). The method builds upon established techniques like U-Nets and GANs but makes significant contributions to the specific application of pose-guided person image generation.

Strengths:
* The paper clearly articulates a novel application domain for pose-guided image generation, with well-motivated technical choices including the two-stage architecture and pose mask loss.
* The writing is clear and well-organized, with detailed methodology and thorough ablation studies comparing pose embedding methods, loss functions, and the two-stage versus one-stage approaches.
* The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines and alternative pose embedding techniques, with the two-stage model producing visibly sharper results than single-stage approaches.
* The method is well-suited for practical applications such as movie making and pose estimation data generation, as claimed in the introduction.

Weaknesses:
* The paper lacks sufficient quantitative evaluation to support its claim of generating "high-quality" images. While SSIM and Inception Score are reported, these metrics do not correlate well with human perception of image quality (as noted in [12, 27]), and no user studies were conducted to assess how often generated images fool human observers.
* The qualitative results are underwhelming; as shown in Figure 4, the generated images contain numerous visual artifacts (e.g., incorrect body parts, color mismatches, and background inconsistencies) that make the results appear far from "photo-realistic" as claimed in the abstract.
* The paper fails to adequately address the limitations of the approach, particularly for challenging cases like complex poses or gender inconsistencies, which are evident in the failure examples (Figure 6).
* The comparison with [36] is insufficient; while the paper claims better results, Figure 5 shows only a few examples without sufficient context to demonstrate clear superiority.

Pro: Novel application of pose-guided person generation with a well-motivated two-stage architecture and pose mask loss
Pro: Clear writing and thorough ablation studies demonstrating technical improvements
Pro: Applicable to practical domains like movie making and pose estimation
Con: Lack of human evaluation to validate image quality claims
Con: Qualitative results contain significant visual artifacts that undermine the "high-quality" claim
Con: Insufficient discussion of limitations and failure cases
Con: Comparison with prior work is limited to a single related paper [36]

While the paper presents an interesting application of GANs to pose-guided person image generation, the current results are not compelling enough to demonstrate the claimed quality. The authors have not adequately addressed concerns about both quantitative evaluation and the visual quality of results, which are crucial for a practical application like this. The paper would benefit significantly from additional user studies and more detailed analysis of failure cases before it could be considered suitable for presentation at NIPS.
