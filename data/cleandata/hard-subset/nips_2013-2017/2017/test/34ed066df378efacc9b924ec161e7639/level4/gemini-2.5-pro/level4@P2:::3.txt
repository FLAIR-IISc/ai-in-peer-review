**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG²), a novel framework for synthesizing an image of a person in a target pose, conditioned on a single source image of that person. The core contribution is a two-stage generative process designed to tackle this complex task. Stage I utilizes a U-Net-like architecture to generate a coarse initial image. This stage takes the source image and a heatmap representation of the target pose as input, focusing on correctly transferring the overall body structure, shape, and color. A key component here is a proposed "pose mask loss" (a masked L1 loss) designed to encourage the network to focus on the person's body rather than the background. Stage II then refines the coarse output from the first stage. It employs another U-Net-like generator within a conditional GAN framework (DCGAN) to add finer details and textures, aiming for a more photo-realistic result. The discriminator is trained on pairs of images (condition and generated/real) to enforce consistency. The method is evaluated on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets, demonstrating its ability to generate plausible images in new poses. This work is situated within the broader context of conditional image generation but specifically addresses the novel problem of explicit pose transfer for full-body person images, distinguishing itself from prior work that conditioned on more abstract cues like viewpoints or attributes.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a novel and challenging problem: generating a full-body image of a specific person in an arbitrary new pose. This has significant potential applications in areas like data augmentation for pose estimation, virtual try-on systems in fashion, and content creation for media. The explicit conditioning on a pose skeleton provides a more direct and flexible control mechanism than previous methods based on general viewpoints or text descriptions.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The two-stage "coarse-to-fine" approach is well-motivated, breaking down a difficult problem into two more manageable sub-tasks: generating the correct global structure and then refining local details. The methodology and network architecture are explained with sufficient detail.

3.  **Sound Technical Approach:** The proposed two-stage architecture is a logical and sound approach. Using a U-Net with a masked L1 loss to establish the basic structure in Stage I, followed by a conditional GAN for refinement in Stage II, is a sensible combination of established techniques. The ablation studies on different pose embeddings and the use of the pose mask loss effectively justify the architectural choices made.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Quality of Generated Results:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in the qualitative results. While the method successfully transfers the pose, the generated images often fall short of the "high-quality" and "photo-realistic" claims made by the authors. In many examples across both datasets (Figure 4 and supplementary material), the results suffer from noticeable artifacts, blurriness, and a lack of high-frequency detail. Specific issues include:
    *   **Loss of Identity-Preserving Details:** Fine textures on clothing (e.g., the stripes on the T-shirt for ID 53 in Fig. 4), facial features, and accessories are often blurred or lost entirely. The refined results are sharper than the coarse ones but still lack the fidelity of real images.
    *   **Background Artifacts:** The model struggles with background consistency, often copying and distorting parts of the source image's background into the new scene, as acknowledged in the user study analysis for the Market-1501 dataset.
    *   **Unnatural Blurring:** The final images often have an overly smooth, "airbrushed" appearance, particularly on skin and clothing, which makes them easily identifiable as synthetic.

2.  **Evaluation and Mismatch with Claims:** Although the paper includes quantitative metrics (SSIM, IS) and a user study, they do not fully support the strength of the paper's claims.
    *   The authors rightly note that SSIM and IS are imperfect metrics, as the blurry Stage I results can score better than the sharper Stage II results. This highlights a disconnect between the chosen metrics and the goal of photo-realism.
    *   The user study results in Table 2 are not particularly strong. On the more challenging Market-1501 dataset, the model (G1+G2+D) only fools users 8.2% of the time. Even on the cleaner DeepFashion dataset, the 14.9% "fool rate" indicates that the generated images are still readily distinguishable from real photographs. This suggests the work is a promising step but has not yet achieved a high degree of realism.

**Overall Recommendation**

This paper presents a promising and well-designed framework for a novel and interesting computer vision task. The two-stage approach is logical, and the writing is clear. However, the work is let down by the quality of the final results, which do not fully live up to the claims of being "photo-realistic." The generated images contain noticeable artifacts and lack the fine details necessary to be truly convincing, a conclusion supported by the modest results of the user study.

While the direction is excellent and the methodology is sound, the current results seem preliminary for a top-tier conference like NIPS, where the bar for generative model quality is exceptionally high. The work feels like an important proof-of-concept that requires further refinement to achieve its stated goals. For these reasons, I lean towards rejection at this stage, but I would strongly encourage the authors to continue this line of research, as it holds significant promise.
