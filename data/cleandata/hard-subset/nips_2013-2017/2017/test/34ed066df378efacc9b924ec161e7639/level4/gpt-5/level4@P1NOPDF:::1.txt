Summary:
The paper proposes an architecture for generating images of a person that combine the appearance of a person from an input image with a specified target pose (encoded as heatmaps).

Strengths:
+ The task is novel.
+ The two-stage, coarse-to-fine architecture is also novel.
+ The paper is clearly written.
+ Although the results exhibit visible artifacts, they are promising.
+ The paper provides an extensive evaluation of design choices.

Weaknesses:
- The quantitative evaluation is generally weak; this is understandable (and not a major weakness) given the lack of suitable metrics for this task.

Comments:
Given the iterative refinement nature of the proposed architecture, I suggest including recent related work on iterative refinement with cascaded networks, e.g., [a, b]:
[a] J. Carreira, P. Agrawal, K. Fragkiadaki, and J. Malik. Human pose estimation with iterative error feedback. In CVPR, 2016.
[b] A. Newell, K. Yang, and J. Deng. Stacked hourglass networks for human pose estimation. In ECCV, 2016.

- Line 110: “we find that using residual blocks as basic component improves the generation performance.” In what way? Please elaborate. Does this improvement refer to sharper textures, better structural consistency with the target pose, faster convergence, or training stability? Some quantitative or qualitative substantiation would help.

While the evaluation is generally extensive, I remain curious how a variant would perform in which the Stage 2 network is trained adversarially but receives the same inputs as Stage 1 (i.e., bypassing the intermediate representation). Is the blurring observed with Stage 1 + GAN attributable to the intermediate fully connected layer, or to the objective/architecture mismatch at that stage? An ablation that removes the fully connected bottleneck or that feeds Stage 1 inputs directly to a Stage 2-style generator trained with a GAN loss could clarify this.

Overall rating:
Based on the novelty of the problem and the proposed approach, I confidently recommend acceptance.

Rebuttal:
While I appreciate the attempt at a user study, in its current preliminary form I do not find it convincing and therefore discount it in my rating. I urge the authors to include a more substantive human evaluation in their revision. Nonetheless, my positive assessment of the work still stands.
