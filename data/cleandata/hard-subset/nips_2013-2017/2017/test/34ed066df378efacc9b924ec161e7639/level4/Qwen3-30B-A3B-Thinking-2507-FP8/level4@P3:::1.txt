

**Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"**

**Summary:** This paper introduces a novel task of generating person images conditioned on a reference image and a target pose, which has significant applications in content creation and data augmentation. The authors propose a two-stage architecture (PG2) that first integrates pose information with the reference image to generate a coarse pose-aligned image (Stage I), then refines this result using adversarial training to produce high-quality, detailed images (Stage II). This work builds upon recent advances in conditional image generation (e.g., [22, 23, 32, 36]) but differs significantly by explicitly using pose keypoints as conditioning information rather than relying on abstract attributes or category labels. Unlike [36] which synthesizes multi-view images using view descriptions, this work directly conditions on pose keypoint heatmaps, providing more precise control over body structure. The approach also relates to pose estimation literature [2, 19, 20] by leveraging state-of-the-art pose estimation to avoid manual annotation.

**Strengths:**  
*Originality (High):* The paper identifies a meaningful gap in pose-conditioned image generation and proposes a novel task with clear applications. The two-stage coarse-to-fine architecture is well-motivated and distinct from prior work. The pose mask loss that focuses generation on human body regions (rather than background) represents a significant technical contribution.

*Quality (High):* The paper demonstrates strong technical execution. The architecture is carefully designed with appropriate ablation studies on pose embedding methods, loss functions, and stage-wise comparison. The implementation details are thorough, and the model architecture is clearly described. The qualitative results (Figures 4-5) convincingly show the superiority of the two-stage approach over one-stage alternatives.

*Clarity (High):* The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear organization and logical flow. The technical explanations are precise, and the figures effectively illustrate the methodology and results. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility (e.g., network architecture, training parameters).

*Significance (Moderate-High):* The work addresses a challenging problem with practical applications in film production and pose estimation data augmentation. While the direct applications may be somewhat narrow, the two-stage framework and pose mask loss could be adapted to other image generation tasks, making the contribution broadly valuable to the generative modeling community.

**Weaknesses:**  
*Quantitative Evaluation (Moderate):* The paper acknowledges the lack of established metrics for this specific task, which is understandable. However, the evaluation relies heavily on qualitative results and user studies with limited scale (30 users). The authors could have included more rigorous quantitative comparisons with alternative metrics (e.g., Fréchet Inception Distance) or additional baselines to strengthen the evaluation. The user study (Section 4.4) shows some promise but is preliminary and could be expanded.

*Technical Details (Moderate):* The claim that residual blocks improve generation performance (Line 110) lacks specific evidence. The authors should quantify how much residual blocks improve performance compared to standard U-Net blocks. Additionally, the paper doesn't fully explain why the Stage I + GAN combination (G1+D) produces blurry results despite using adversarial training—this could be due to the fully connected layer in G1 or the coarse initial result.

*Related Work (Moderate):* The paper could better contextualize its approach within the growing literature on iterative refinement techniques in vision. Recent works like [a, b] on iterative pose estimation (Carreira et al., CVPR 2016; Newell et al., ECCV 2016) demonstrate similar coarse-to-fine strategies that would strengthen the paper's connection to existing methodologies.

**Recommendation:**  
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of conditional image generation by introducing a novel task and a well-designed two-stage architecture. The technical execution is strong, the writing is clear, and the results are promising. While the quantitative evaluation could be strengthened, the paper's novelty, technical quality, and significance outweigh this limitation. I recommend acceptance.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**  
1. Include a more detailed comparison with recent iterative refinement approaches in the related work section.
2. Provide quantitative evidence for the claim about residual blocks improving performance (e.g., SSIM/IS scores comparing residual vs. non-residual blocks).
3. Expand the user study to include more participants and potentially include a comparison with additional baselines.
4. Address the potential causes of blurriness when combining Stage I with adversarial training (G1+D).

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**
*Pro:* Novel task, strong technical execution, clear writing, well-motivated two-stage architecture, promising qualitative results.
*Con:* Quantitative evaluation could be more rigorous, some technical claims lack supporting evidence, user study is preliminary.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that advances the state of the art in pose-conditioned image generation. The contributions are substantial and well-executed, making it a strong candidate for NIPS 2017.
