

The paper introduces a human image generator conditioned on both appearance and pose information. The approach employs an adversarial framework featuring a two-stage generative architecture, where high-resolution outputs are produced for discriminator input. Specifically, the first generator (a U-shaped network) creates a coarse image from appearance and pose maps, while the second generator refines this output by predicting a residual map using the original appearance input. Evaluation is conducted on the DeepFashion dataset.

The paper advances several key contributions:
* **Task novelty:** Pioneering the conditioning of human image generation on both appearance and pose maps.
* **Technical innovation:** Implementing a stacked architecture that predicts residual maps rather than direct upsampling, alongside novel loss design.

To enhance its impact, the paper could address the following limitations:
* **Quality improvement:** Current results exhibit visible artifacts and require significant refinement for practical applications.
* **Significance:** The work may be perceived as another GAN architecture without sufficient differentiation.
* **Problem domain:** While well-suited for vision/graphics tasks like dressing, the approach lacks broad applicability to other learning domains.

The paper is well-structured, clearly conveying the coarse-to-fine generator strategy conditioned on appearance and pose. The application to dressing problems is appropriately justified, and the residual-based refinement technique offers a meaningful improvement for high-resolution generation.

The primary concern remains the persistent artifacts in generated images, exemplified by black dots in patterned regions (e.g., ID346 in Fig. 4). Although the second generator reduces blurriness from the first stage, the model still fails to adequately reconstruct high-frequency appearance details.

A secondary concern is that the methodology builds upon established conditional GAN and stacked generator concepts (e.g., Zhang et al. and Huang et al. cited below), despite addressing application-specific challenges that may limit broader appeal:
* Han Zhang et al., "StackGAN: Text to Photo-realistic Image Synthesis with Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks," arXiv:1612.03242.
* Xun Huang et al., "Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks," arXiv:1612.04357.

Overall, the paper successfully addresses pose-conditioned human image generation with a technically sound solution and appropriate evaluation. While the generated images require quality improvements, they remain visually consistent. The approach demonstrates sufficient technical novelty for acceptance. My initial recommendation is **accept**.
