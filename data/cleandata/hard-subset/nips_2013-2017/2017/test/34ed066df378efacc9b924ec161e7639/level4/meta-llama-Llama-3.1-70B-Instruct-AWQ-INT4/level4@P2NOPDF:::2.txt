The paper proposes a novel human image generator that is conditioned on both appearance and human pose, leveraging a two-step adversarial training architecture to produce high-resolution images. The generator consists of two networks: the first generates a coarse image using a U-shape network given appearance and pose maps, while the second refines the coarse image by predicting a residual map based on the coarse input and the original appearance. The DeepFashion dataset is utilized for evaluation.

The paper introduces several important ideas, including task novelty by conditioning human image generation on appearance and pose maps, as well as technical innovations such as a stacked architecture that predicts difference maps rather than direct upsampling, and a well-designed loss function.

However, to stand out, the paper could improve in several areas. Firstly, the quality of the generated images still requires significant improvement. Additionally, the significance of the paper may be diminished by its similarity to existing GAN architectures, which could lead to it being perceived as yet another incremental contribution. Furthermore, the problem domain, while well-suited to vision and graphics applications, may be challenging to generalize to other learning problems.

The paper is well-organized and effectively conveys the key aspects of the proposed architecture. The use of a coarse-to-fine strategy, conditioned on appearance and pose information, is a suitable approach for the dressing problem. The difference map generation technique is also a useful innovation for producing higher-resolution images.

One major concern with the paper is the presence of visible artifacts in the generated results, which still require significant improvement for practical applications. For instance, the patterns in ID346 of Fig 4 result in black dots in the final image. Although the second generator mitigates the blurriness of the first generator, it appears that the model is still insufficient to recover high-frequency components in the target appearance.

Another concern, albeit less severe, is that some readers may view the proposed approach as a straightforward application of conditional GANs, as conditioning and stacking generators for adversarial training have been explored in previous work (e.g., Han Zhang et al.'s "StackGAN: Text to Photo-realistic Image Synthesis with Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks" and Xun Huang et al.'s "Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks"). While the paper addresses application-specific challenges, this may not appeal to a broad audience.

In conclusion, the paper successfully proposes a solution to the pose-conditioned image problem and conducts a thorough evaluation. The approach demonstrates sufficient technical novelty, and the resulting images, although requiring quality improvement, are at least visually consistent. Overall, my initial rating is accept.
