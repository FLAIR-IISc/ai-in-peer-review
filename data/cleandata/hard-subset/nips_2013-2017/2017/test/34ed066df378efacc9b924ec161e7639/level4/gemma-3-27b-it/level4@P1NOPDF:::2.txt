The paper proposes a human image generator conditioned on appearance and human pose. The proposed generation framework utilizes an adversarial training architecture with two-stage generative networks to produce high-resolution images for a discriminator. The generator consists of two networks: the first generates a coarse image from appearance and pose maps using a U-shaped network, and the second takes this coarse image, along with the original appearance information, to predict a residual map for refining the initial output. The DeepFashion dataset is used for evaluation.

The paper presents several noteworthy ideas:

* **Task Novelty:** The introduction of conditioning on both appearance and pose maps for human image generation.
* **Technical Contributions:** A stacked architecture that predicts residual maps instead of directly upsampling, and a carefully designed loss function.

However, the paper could be strengthened in the following areas to enhance its impact:

* **Image Quality:** The generated images still require improvement in terms of visual fidelity.
* **Significance:** The work could be perceived as another iteration of a GAN architecture, lacking a substantial departure from existing approaches.
* **Generalizability:** While a good application for vision and graphics, it’s unclear how readily the techniques could generalize to other learning problems.

The paper is well-organized and effectively conveys the key aspects of the proposed architecture. By conditioning on appearance and pose information, the generator employs a coarse-to-fine strategy implemented through a stack of two networks. This approach is well-suited to the problem of clothing/appearance transfer. The use of residual map generation is a small but valuable technique for achieving higher resolution outputs.

The primary concern with the paper is the visible artifacts present in the generated results, indicating a need for further refinement before practical application. For example, the patterns in ID346 of Figure 4 result in black dots in the final output. While the second generator mitigates the blurriness of the first, the model appears insufficient to fully recover high-frequency components from the target appearance. 

A secondary, though less severe, concern is that the approach could be viewed as an application of conditional GANs. Stacking generators and conditioning for adversarial training have been explored previously (e.g., see below), although these are currently arXiv preprints. The paper addresses application-specific challenges, but this focus might limit its appeal to a broader audience.

* Han Zhang, Tao Xu, Hongsheng Li, Shaoting Zhang, Xiaolei Huang, Xiaogang Wang, Dimitris Metaxas, "StackGAN: Text to Photo-realistic Image Synthesis with Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks", arXiv:1612.03242.
* Xun Huang, Yixuan Li, Omid Poursaeed, John Hopcroft, Serge Belongie, Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks, arXiv:1612.04357.

Overall, the paper successfully proposes a solution to the pose-conditioned image generation problem and presents a sound evaluation. The proposed approach demonstrates sufficient technical novelty. While the resulting images still require quality improvements, the model generates visually consistent results. My initial rating is accept.
