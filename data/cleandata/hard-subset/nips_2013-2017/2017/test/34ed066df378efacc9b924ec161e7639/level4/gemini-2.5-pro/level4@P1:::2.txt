This paper introduces a method for generating an image of a person in a specified target pose, conditioned on a source image of that person's appearance. The proposed framework is a two-stage generative adversarial network (GAN). In the first stage, a U-Net-like generator takes the source image and a heatmap representation of the target pose to produce a coarse initial image. This stage focuses on correctly mapping the overall structure and color. The second stage aims to refine this coarse output by using another generator, which takes the coarse image and the original source image as input, to predict a residual map. This refinement step adds finer details and textures, resulting in a higher-quality final image. The authors evaluate their method on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Task Formulation:** The paper addresses the interesting and challenging problem of pose-guided person image generation. This is a novel task with significant potential for applications in computer graphics, data augmentation for tasks like pose estimation, and virtual try-on systems.
*   **Effective Two-Stage Architecture:** The proposed coarse-to-fine strategy is a logical and effective way to decompose this complex problem. The first stage successfully handles the global structure transfer (i.e., changing the pose), while the second stage focuses on rendering high-frequency appearance details.
*   **Methodological Contributions:** The idea for the second generator to learn a difference map, rather than the full image, is a smart technique for focusing the model on refinement and potentially stabilizing training. Furthermore, the proposed pose mask loss is a useful contribution for handling variable backgrounds and encouraging the generator to concentrate on the foreground person.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Image Quality and Artifacts:** The primary weakness is that the generated results, while promising, still contain visible artifacts and could be improved for practical applications. The model seems to struggle with preserving high-frequency details, such as complex clothing patterns. For instance, in Figure 4, the pattern on the clothes of person ID 346 is rendered as black dots in the final result. This suggests the model is not yet sufficient for faithfully recovering all appearance details from the source image.
*   **Incremental Originality:** The architectural design, while well-suited for the task, could be viewed as a skillful application of existing ideas rather than a fundamental innovation. The core components—U-Nets, conditional GANs, and coarse-to-fine refinement strategies—are well-established in the literature. The concept of stacking generators for adversarial training has been explored in other domains, such as text-to-image synthesis in concurrent works [1, 2]. While the paper addresses application-specific challenges effectively, this may limit its perceived novelty to some readers.
*   **Limited Generalizability:** The proposed solution is highly tailored to the specific application of human image generation. While this is a valuable domain, the paper does not discuss how the core ideas might generalize to other object categories or machine learning problems, which may narrow its appeal to a broader audience at NIPS.

**Detailed Comments:**

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, effectively conveying the key aspects of the proposed architecture. The two-stage approach is appropriate for the problem, and the ablation studies in Section 4 convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed pose embedding and the masked loss function.

My main concern is the visual quality of the final outputs. Although the second generator mitigates the blurriness from the first stage, it appears insufficient for fully recovering high-frequency components from the source appearance. Addressing this limitation would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

Regarding originality, while the problem formulation itself is novel, the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of its relationship to concurrent works on stacked generative models. For example, the following papers, though pre-prints at the time, also explore stacked architectures for high-quality image generation and would be relevant to position this work:

[1] Zhang et al., "StackGAN: Text to Photo-realistic Image Synthesis with Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks", arXiv:1612.03242.
[2] Huang et al., "Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks", arXiv:1612.04357.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this paper presents a solid and well-executed contribution to conditional image generation. It successfully proposes a solution to the novel and practical problem of pose-conditioned person synthesis. The proposed model is thoughtfully designed, and the evaluation is conducted properly. Although the quality of the generated images still has room for improvement and the architectural novelty could be seen as incremental, the model produces visually consistent images and represents a complete and functional system for a challenging task. Therefore, I lean towards acceptance.
