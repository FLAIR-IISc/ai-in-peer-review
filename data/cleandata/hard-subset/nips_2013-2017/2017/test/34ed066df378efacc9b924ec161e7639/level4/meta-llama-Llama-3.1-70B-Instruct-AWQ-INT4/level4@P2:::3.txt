This paper proposes a novel Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) that synthesizes person images in arbitrary poses based on a reference image and a target pose. The framework consists of two stages: pose integration and image refinement. The first stage uses a U-Net-like network to generate an initial coarse image, while the second stage refines the result using a variant of Deep Convolutional GAN (DCGAN) through adversarial training.

The authors' contribution is three-fold: (i) proposing a novel task of conditioning image generation on a reference image and an intended pose, (ii) exploring several ways to integrate pose information with a person image, and (iii) dividing the problem into two stages to address the challenging task of pose transfer.

The paper is well-written, and the idea is interesting. The empirical experiments show promising results, and the authors have made an effort to evaluate their method on two datasets: Market-1501 and DeepFashion.

However, there are some concerns regarding the quality of the results. While the generated images are realistic-looking at some level, they are not visually appealing, and there are noticeable visual errors when comparing the results to the targets. For example, the generated images in Figure 4 are too small to see, and even at this size, there are many errors.

To further evaluate the quality of the results, I would like to see more quantitative evaluations, such as user studies to assess how many times the generated images can trick human eyes. Additionally, the authors could provide more detailed analysis of the results, such as comparing the performance of their method to other state-of-the-art approaches.

In terms of the NIPS evaluation criteria, I would rate this paper as follows:

* Quality: 6/10 (the paper is well-written, but the results are not yet convincing)
* Clarity: 8/10 (the paper is easy to follow, but some figures are too small to see)
* Originality: 7/10 (the idea is interesting, but the techniques used are mostly known)
* Significance: 6/10 (the results are promising, but not yet visually appealing)

Overall, I think the authors are on the right track, but the work is not yet ready to be presented at NIPS. With more quantitative evaluations and detailed analysis, I believe this paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel task and approach to person image generation.
* The empirical experiments show promising results.
* The authors have made an effort to evaluate their method on two datasets.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The results are not yet visually appealing, and there are noticeable visual errors.
* There is a lack of quantitative evaluations, such as user studies.
* The techniques used are mostly known, and the paper does not provide enough new insights.
