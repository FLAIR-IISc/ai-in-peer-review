This paper presents a GAN-based framework for generating novel-view images of a person, conditioned on a target pose represented by skeletal keypoints. The system architecture primarily leverages established techniques, such as DCGANs and U-Nets. Consequently, the main contribution lies not in novel methodology but in the specific application of these components to the challenging computer vision task of pose-guided person synthesis.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses an interesting and relevant problem in computer vision. The core idea of synthesizing person images from a target pose is a compelling application.
*   The manuscript is well-written and the proposed approach is described clearly, making it easy for the reader to follow.
*   The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed method is capable of generating images that achieve a degree of realism and correspond to the target pose.

**Weaknesses:**
My primary concerns relate to the evaluation of the proposed method. As this is an application-oriented paper, the quality and verification of the results are paramount. The current evaluation is lacking in two critical aspects:

1.  **Absence of Quantitative Evaluation:** The paper provides no quantitative metrics to assess the quality of the generated images. For a task centered on realism, this is a significant omission. The authors could have conducted user studies to measure perceptual realism, for instance, by evaluating the rate at which human observers mistake the synthesized images for real ones. Objective metrics that correlate with image quality could also strengthen the evaluation. Without any quantitative analysis, it is difficult to objectively gauge the method's performance or compare it to potential future work.

2.  **Unconvincing Qualitative Results:** The visual quality of the generated images is not compelling enough to substantiate the paper's claims. For example, in Figure 1, the output images are presented at a very low resolution, which hinders detailed inspection. Even at this small scale, significant visual artifacts and noticeable discrepancies are apparent when comparing the generated outputs to the ground truth targets. This issue of visual flaws and low resolution persists across most of the presented results, including those in the supplementary material. This makes it difficult to conclude that the results are of high quality or approaching photorealism.

In summary, while the research direction is valuable and the work shows promise, the results presented in the paper seem premature. The method is not yet advanced enough to declare success, and the lack of rigorous evaluation makes its current contribution difficult to assess.

**After Rebuttal:**
I have read the authors' feedback and remain unconvinced. My primary concerns regarding the lack of robust quantitative and qualitative evaluation, which were also raised by other reviewers, have not been sufficiently addressed. For an application-oriented paper such as this, strong evidence of performance through both objective metrics and high-quality visual results is critical. The authors' rebuttal shows some attempts to mitigate these issues, but the provided additions do not resolve the core concerns to a satisfactory degree. Therefore, I believe the work in its current state may not meet the high standards for publication at a premier conference like NIPS.
