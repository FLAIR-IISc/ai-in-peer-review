The paper proposes a novel architecture for generating images of people, combining the appearance from an input image with a specified pose (represented using heatmaps).

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed task – conditioning image generation on both a reference image and a desired pose – is novel and addresses a practical problem with applications in areas like movie making and training data generation for pose estimation.
*   The two-stage coarse-to-fine architecture is a sensible approach to tackle the complexity of the problem, breaking it down into global structure learning and detailed appearance refinement.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method.
*   The generated results, while containing some artifacts, demonstrate promising visual quality and pose accuracy.
*   The authors provide an extensive evaluation of different design choices, including pose embedding methods and loss functions, showcasing a thorough understanding of the challenges.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Quantitative evaluation is limited by the lack of established metrics for assessing the quality of pose-conditioned image generation. While the authors acknowledge this, relying solely on SSIM and Inception Score can be misleading, as these metrics don't always correlate with perceived visual quality (as noted in the paper itself).
*   The connection to related work on iterative refinement techniques, such as cascaded networks, could be more explicit. 

**Comments:**

Given the iterative refinement nature of the proposed architecture, including discussion of recent work on iterative refinement using cascaded networks would strengthen the related work section. For example:

*   J. Carreira, P. Agrawal, K. Fragkiadaki, and J. Malik. Human pose estimation with iterative error feedback. In *IEEE Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition (CVPR)*, 2016.
*   A. Newell, K. Yang, and J. Deng. Stacked hourglass networks for human pose estimation. In *European Conference on Computer Vision (ECCV)*, 2016.

*   Line 110: "we find that using residual blocks as basic component improves the generation performance." This statement would benefit from further elaboration. Specifically, *how* do residual blocks improve performance in this context?
*   The paper briefly mentions exploring the performance of a single-stage adversarial network (trained directly on the input image and target pose). A more detailed analysis of the results, and a discussion of why the blurring observed in this setup might occur (e.g., due to the intermediate fully connected layer), would be valuable. 

**Overall Rating:**

Based on the novelty of the problem and the proposed approach, I confidently recommend that this paper be accepted. The work represents a significant contribution to the field of image generation and opens up new possibilities for controlling and manipulating human appearance in images.

**Rebuttal Consideration:**

While the authors' attempt at a user study is appreciated, its preliminary nature and limited scope make it less persuasive. I do not weigh the user study results heavily in my review rating. The authors are encouraged to conduct a more comprehensive human evaluation in their revision. However, my positive assessment of the paper remains unchanged.
