Summary
The paper introduces PG2, a pose-guided person image generation framework that synthesizes an image of a given person in an arbitrary target pose. The method conditions on a reference image and a pose representation and proceeds in two stages. Stage I uses a U-Net-like encoder–decoder with residual blocks to integrate the appearance image with pose heatmaps (18 keypoint maps) and produce a coarse image that matches the target pose; a masked L1 loss emphasizes the person over the background. Stage II refines this result using a conditional GAN: a second U-Net predicts a difference (residual) map that, when added to the coarse output, recovers higher-frequency details. The discriminator operates on image pairs (generated or real paired with the condition image) to enforce pose- and identity-consistent realism. Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) include ablations (pose embeddings, loss variants, one- vs two-stage), new masked SSIM/IS metrics, and a user study. The approach generally produces plausible pose transfers with sharper details than a single-stage baseline.

Relation to prior work
The task relates to conditional image generation with VAEs, PixelRNN/PixelCNN, and GANs. Compared to prior conditional GANs that use category/attribute/text inputs or weak pose cues, the paper conditions directly on pose keypoint heatmaps and a reference image. The coarse-to-fine strategy echoes stacked/iterative generators (e.g., StackGAN), but here is tailored to pose transfer with a difference-map refinement and a pairwise discriminator. Prior multi-view generation methods (e.g., Zhao et al.) use viewpoint labels; PG2 provides stronger, more explicit control via keypoint poses.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation: generating a specific person under a target pose using pose heatmaps and a reference image.
- Two-stage design is well motivated: coarse pose/structure first, then detail refinement via adversarial learning.
- Useful technical elements: pose heatmap concatenation, masked L1 loss to down-weight background, difference-map refinement that accelerates convergence, and pairwise conditioning for the discriminator.
- Solid experimental protocol: two diverse datasets; ablations on pose embedding and loss; comparisons of one- vs two-stage training; qualitative and quantitative results; user study.
- Results demonstrate noticeable gains in sharpness and pose accuracy over single-stage baselines and prior multi-view generation.

Weaknesses
- Visual quality still exhibits artifacts and loss of high-frequency detail; some results (e.g., Fig. 4, ID 346) contain dotting/texture breakdown and background artifacts, especially on Market-1501.
- Novelty is incremental relative to conditional/stacked GAN literature; the contributions are primarily in problem setup and engineering choices rather than new theory.
- Generality beyond human pose transfer is not demonstrated; the method is tailored to people and may not directly translate to other object classes or broader ML tasks.
- Evaluation metrics (SSIM/IS and masked variants) do not fully align with perceptual quality; the paper notes cases where sharper outputs score lower.

Quality
The method is technically sound and carefully implemented. Losses and architectural choices are justified, and ablations support key design claims (heatmap embedding > coordinate or separate HME; masked L1 > plain L1; two-stage > one-stage). The user study is a helpful complement, though the absolute fooling rates remain modest. Additional quantitative measures of identity preservation and pose accuracy would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The model, training losses, and architectural differences between stages are described concretely. Figures effectively illustrate the pipeline and qualitative outcomes. A brief schematic of the mask construction and more explicit training/inference runtimes would further improve clarity.

Originality
Moderate. The task formulation (pose-guided person image generation) and combination of pose heatmaps, masked losses, and residual refinement are well integrated, but draw on known components. The difference-map refinement and pairwise conditioning are practical and useful twists in this context.

Significance
The problem is relevant to vision/graphics applications (content creation, data augmentation for pose/re-id). Within this domain, the work is a meaningful step toward controllable person synthesis. Broader methodological impact is more limited, as the approach leverages established generative techniques.

Suggestions for improvement
- Incorporate perceptual losses (e.g., VGG-based feature loss) to better reconstruct fine textures and mitigate dot artifacts; report their effect alongside adversarial loss.
- Evaluate identity preservation using a pretrained re-identification network (feature similarity or re-id accuracy) and pose correctness via a pose estimator (PCK or OKS).
- Add baselines such as pix2pix-style cGAN with U-Net and perceptual loss, and a single-stage residual-prediction variant, to isolate the benefit of two-stage training vs. residual learning.
- Analyze failure modes (e.g., rare poses, gender imbalance) and report data balancing or augmentation strategies; consider multi-modal outputs or stochasticity to model uncertainty.
- Provide quantitative breakdowns for foreground vs background artifacts and include timing/memory costs.

Pros for acceptance
- Well-motivated task with explicit, controllable conditioning.
- Effective two-stage design with practical innovations (masked loss, residual refinement, pairwise discriminator).
- Comprehensive ablations and cross-dataset evaluation; observable qualitative improvements over one-stage and prior work.

Cons for acceptance
- Remaining artifacts and limited high-frequency detail; quality not yet production-ready.
- Incremental novelty relative to existing conditional/stacked GAN methods.
- Limited evidence of generalization beyond human pose transfer; evaluation metrics only partially reflect perceptual quality.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper addresses an important, well-scoped problem with a thoughtfully engineered solution, provides solid empirical evidence, and is likely to be of interest to the NIPS community working on conditional generative modeling and controllable image synthesis.
