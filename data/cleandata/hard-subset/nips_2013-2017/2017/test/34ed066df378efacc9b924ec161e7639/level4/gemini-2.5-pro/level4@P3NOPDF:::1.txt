### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel, two-stage generative architecture for human image synthesis. The model is designed to generate an image of a person by combining the appearance from a source image with a target pose, specified via pose heatmaps. The coarse-to-fine approach first generates a blurred, low-resolution image that captures the overall structure and then refines this output in a second stage to produce a higher-fidelity result.

### **Strengths**

*   **Novelty of the Task:** The paper addresses the novel and challenging task of pose-guided person image generation, which has significant potential applications in areas like computer graphics, animation, and data augmentation.
*   **Novel Architecture:** The proposed two-stage, coarse-to-fine generator architecture is a well-motivated and effective contribution for tackling this complex generation problem.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors effectively communicate their method and contributions.
*   **Promising Results:** The qualitative results, while containing some discernible artifacts, are promising and effectively demonstrate the model's capabilities in transferring appearance to new poses.
*   **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The authors provide an extensive set of experiments that thoroughly evaluate their key design decisions, lending strong support to their proposed architecture.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The primary weakness lies in the quantitative evaluation. However, this is understandable given the novelty of the task and the lack of established, suitable evaluation metrics. Therefore, this is not a major flaw. The paper includes a user study, which is a commendable step. However, the study is presented in a preliminary form and lacks the necessary detail and scale to be fully convincing. While I have not factored this heavily into my overall rating, I strongly urge the authors to conduct and include a more substantive human evaluation in their final revision to better support their claims.

I also have a few specific suggestions and questions for the authors:

1.  **Related Work:** Given the iterative, refinement-based nature of the proposed architecture, the related work section could be strengthened by discussing recent work on cascaded or stacked networks for iterative refinement, particularly in the context of human pose estimation. These methods share a conceptual similarity with the proposed two-stage approach. Relevant examples include:
    *   [a] J. Carreira, P. Agrawal, K. Fragkiadaki, and J. Malik. Human pose estimation with iterative error feedback. In IEEE Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition (CVPR), 2016.
    *   [b] A. Newell, K. Yang, and J. Deng. Stacked hourglass networks for human pose estimation. In European Conference on Computer Vision (ECCV), 2016.

2.  **Clarification on Residual Blocks:** On line 110, the authors state, "we find that using residual blocks as basic component improves the generation performance." Could you please elaborate on the specific nature of this improvement? For instance, did it lead to better qualitative results, improved quantitative scores on a specific metric, or more stable training?

3.  **Ablation Study:** While the evaluation of design decisions is extensive, I am curious about one additional experiment. How would the Stage 2 network perform if it were trained adversarially on the inputs intended for Stage 1 (i.e., without the coarse output from Stage 1 as an input)? This could help clarify whether the blurring observed with the "Stage 1 + GAN" baseline is primarily due to architectural limitations in Stage 1, such as the intermediate fully connected layer.

### **Recommendation**

Despite the minor weaknesses in the quantitative evaluation, the novelty of the problem formulation and the proposed architectural solution are significant contributions to the field of generative models. The approach is sound, the results are compelling, and the paper is well-executed. I am confident in my recommendation for acceptance.
