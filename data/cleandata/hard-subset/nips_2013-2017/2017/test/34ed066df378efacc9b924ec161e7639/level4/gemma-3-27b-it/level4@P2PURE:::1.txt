Summary:
This paper introduces a novel architecture for generating images of people, effectively combining the appearance of an individual from a source image with a desired pose specified using heatmaps.

Strengths:
+ The proposed task is a novel contribution to the field.
+ The authors present a novel two-stage, coarse-to-fine architecture to address this task.
+ The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
+ While the generated results exhibit some noticeable artifacts, the outcomes are nonetheless promising.
+ The design choices are supported by an extensive evaluation.

Weaknesses:
- The quantitative evaluation is relatively weak; however, this is understandable given the current lack of suitable evaluation metrics for this type of task and does not represent a critical flaw.

Comments:
Considering the iterative refinement inherent in the proposed architecture, it would be beneficial to discuss related work on iterative refinement techniques employing cascaded networks, such as [a,b]:
[a] J. Carreira, P. Agrawal, K. Fragkiadaki, and J. Malik. Human pose estimation with iterative error feedback. In IEEE Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition (CVPR), 2016.
[b] A. Newell, K. Yang, and J. Deng. Stacked hourglass networks for human pose estimation. In European Conference on Computer Vision (ECCV), 2016.
- Line 110 states: “we find that using residual blocks as basic component improves the generation performance.” This statement lacks detail; please elaborate on *how* the performance is improved.
- Despite the generally comprehensive evaluation, I am curious about the potential performance of using the Stage 2 network with Stage 1 inputs, trained adversarially. Could the blurring observed in the Stage 1 + GAN results be attributable to the intermediate fully connected layer?

Overall rating:
Based on the novelty of both the problem and the proposed approach, I confidently recommend accepting this paper.

Rebuttal:
I appreciate the authors’ attempt at a user study; however, due to its preliminary nature, I find the results unconvincing and therefore do not factor them into my review rating. I encourage the authors to include a more substantial human evaluation during the revision process. Regardless, my positive assessment of the work remains unchanged.
