The paper presents a human image generator conditioned on appearance and human pose. The model is trained adversarially and uses a two-stage generator that produces a high-resolution image for the discriminator. In the generator, the first stage uses a U-shaped network to create a coarse image from the appearance and pose map; the second stage takes this coarse output together with the original appearance and predicts a residual (difference map) to refine the result. Evaluation is conducted on the DeepFashion dataset.

The paper introduces a few noteworthy ideas:
- Task novelty: conditioning human image generation on both appearance and a pose map.
- Techniques: a stacked architecture that predicts a difference map rather than directly upsampling, along with a specific loss design.

Areas that could be improved to help the paper stand out:
- Image quality still needs improvement.
- Significance: the work may be perceived as yet another GAN variant.
- Problem domain: it addresses a compelling vision/graphics application but may be hard to generalize to other learning problems.

The paper is well organized and clearly presents the proposed architecture. Given appearance and pose inputs, the generator employs a coarse-to-fine strategy by stacking two networks. This strategy is effectively applied to the dressing problem, and the approach appears appropriate for the target scenario. The use of a difference map is a small but effective technique for producing higher-resolution images.

The main concern is that the generated images exhibit visible artifacts and still require substantial improvement for practical applications. For instance, in ID346 of Fig. 4, the pattern leads to black dots in the final image. While the second generator reduces the blurriness introduced by the first, the model still seems unable to recover high-frequency details in the target appearance.

A secondary, less severe concern is that some may view the approach as an application of conditional GANs. Conditioning and stacking generators for adversarial training have been explored previously (albeit in arXiv work), for example:
- Han Zhang, Tao Xu, Hongsheng Li, Shaoting Zhang, Xiaolei Huang, Xiaogang Wang, Dimitris Metaxas, "StackGAN: Text to Photo-realistic Image Synthesis with Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks", arXiv:1612.03242.
- Xun Huang, Yixuan Li, Omid Poursaeed, John Hopcroft, Serge Belongie, "Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks", arXiv:1612.04357.
The paper addresses application-specific challenges, but these may not resonate with a broad audience.

Overall, the paper provides a solid solution to pose-conditioned image generation and conducts appropriate evaluations. The work presents sufficient technical novelty. Although image quality still needs improvement, the model produces visually consistent results. My initial rating is accept.
