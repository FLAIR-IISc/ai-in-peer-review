This work presents a generative framework, based on a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN), to synthesize novel views of a person conditioned on a target pose, which is represented by skeletal keypoints. The architecture primarily leverages established components like DCGANs and U-Nets, positioning the paper's main contribution in its novel application within computer vision rather than in methodological innovation.

Pros:
*   The core idea is compelling and well-motivated.
*   The manuscript is clearly written and easy to understand.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the capacity to generate images that achieve a certain degree of visual plausibility.

Cons:
*   Given that this is an application-driven work, the quality of the final output is paramount. My primary concerns are:
    *   The paper lacks any form of quantitative evaluation. For instance, perceptual studies could have been conducted to assess the realism of the synthesized images and determine how frequently they are indistinguishable from real photographs to a human observer.
    *   The qualitative results are not sufficiently convincing. The generated images in Figure 1, for example, are presented at a low resolution that hinders detailed inspection. Even at this size, significant visual artifacts are apparent when comparing the outputs to the ground-truth targets. It is therefore challenging to affirm that the results are of high fidelity or approach photorealism. This limitation extends to most of the results, including those in the supplementary material. While I believe the authors are pursuing a promising research direction, the work in its current form feels premature and requires further refinement.

After Rebuttal:
After reviewing the authors' rebuttal, my assessment remains unchanged. As other reviewers have also highlighted, significant concerns persist regarding both the quantitative and qualitative results—two aspects that are critical for an empirically focused paper. While the rebuttal attempts to address these points, the provided responses and additional results do not adequately resolve these fundamental weaknesses. Therefore, I maintain that the paper is not yet mature enough for publication at a premier venue such as NeurIPS.
