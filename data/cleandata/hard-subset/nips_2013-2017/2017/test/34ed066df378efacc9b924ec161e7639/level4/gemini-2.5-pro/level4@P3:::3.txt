This paper introduces the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG²), a two-stage framework for synthesizing an image of a person in a novel target pose, given a single source image of that person. The core idea is to disentangle the challenging task of pose transfer into two manageable steps. The first stage uses a U-Net-like architecture to integrate the source image with a heatmap representation of the target pose, generating a coarse image that captures the global structure and colors. A novel "pose mask loss" is introduced here to focus the L1 loss on the foreground person, mitigating issues with background changes. The second stage refines this coarse output using a conditional GAN, which is trained to add finer details and textures, resulting in a sharper, more realistic image. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets.

This work builds upon the extensive literature of conditional generative models, particularly conditional GANs (e.g., Isola et al., 2017) and techniques for view synthesis (e.g., Zhao et al., 2017). Unlike previous works that might condition on more abstract information like viewpoints or text, this paper proposes a more direct and explicit form of control by using skeletal keypoints. The two-stage, coarse-to-fine refinement strategy is a common and effective technique in image generation (e.g., StackGAN by Zhang et al., 2017), and this paper successfully adapts it to the novel problem of person-specific pose transfer.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem:** The paper tackles the novel and challenging task of generating person images in arbitrary poses from a single example. This is a problem with significant potential applications in areas like data augmentation for pose estimation and person re-identification, as well as creative applications in entertainment and fashion. The proposed method of conditioning on an explicit pose representation offers a high degree of control.

2.  **Sound Methodological Design:** The proposed two-stage "divide-and-conquer" strategy is logical and well-motivated. Decomposing the complex generation process into first establishing the global structure (Stage I) and then adding high-frequency details (Stage II) is a sensible approach that appears to work effectively. The introduction of the pose mask loss is a thoughtful addition to handle the practical challenge of inconsistent backgrounds.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors clearly articulate the problem, their proposed solution, and the motivation behind their architectural choices. The experiments are structured to validate specific components of their model, such as the pose embedding method and the two-stage design.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Qualitative Performance:** My primary concern lies with the quality of the generated results. While the method successfully transfers the general appearance to the new pose, the final images presented in the paper (e.g., Figure 4) are not consistently photo-realistic. Upon close inspection, the results, especially on the more challenging Market-1501 dataset, often appear blurry, lack fine-grained textures (e.g., fabric patterns on clothing), and contain noticeable artifacts and anatomical distortions. For instance, in Figure 4, the stripes on the T-shirt for ID 53 are lost, and the face of the woman in ID 23 is poorly rendered. While the two-stage model is an improvement, the final quality does not fully support the claim of generating "high-quality person images with convincing details."

2.  **Underwhelming Quantitative Evaluation:** The quantitative evaluation, while present, is not fully convincing. The user study in Section 4.4 is a good inclusion, but the results show that the generated images are still easily distinguished from real ones. For example, on the DeepFashion dataset, users correctly identified generated images as "fake" over 85% of the time. While fooling humans is a high bar, this result tempers the claims of photo-realism. Furthermore, the reliance on SSIM is problematic, as the authors themselves note that it often favors blurry images over sharper, more realistic ones produced by GANs.

3.  **Incremental Technical Originality:** While the application is novel, the technical components of the framework are primarily based on existing and well-established architectures, namely U-Nets and conditional DCGANs. The main contributions are the specific formulation of the two-stage pipeline for this task and the pose mask loss. The overall contribution feels more like a strong application paper than one introducing fundamental new techniques in generative modeling.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper presents a well-designed framework for the interesting and important problem of pose-guided person image generation. The two-stage approach is sensible, and the paper is clearly written.

However, as an application-focused paper, the quality of the final output is paramount. The presented results, both qualitative and quantitative, are not yet strong enough to be considered a definitive success. The generated images often contain visible flaws and are not consistently realistic, and the user study confirms that they are not yet able to deceive human observers reliably. While the work is promising and the research direction is valuable, the current execution and results feel premature for a top-tier conference like NIPS. The paper demonstrates a proof-of-concept, but it has not yet reached a stage where it can "declare victory" on this challenging task.

For these reasons, I lean towards rejection. I believe the work could be a strong contribution with substantial improvements to the generation quality and more rigorous evaluation.
