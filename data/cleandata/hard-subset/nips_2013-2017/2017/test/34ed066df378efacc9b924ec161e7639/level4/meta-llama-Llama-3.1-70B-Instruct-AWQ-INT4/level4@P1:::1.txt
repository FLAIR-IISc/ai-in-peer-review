The paper proposes an architecture for generating images containing a person that combines the appearance of a person in an input image and a specified input pose using heat maps.

Strengths:
+ The proposed task is novel and addresses a challenging problem in image generation.
+ The two-stage coarse-to-fine architecture is well-designed and effectively leverages the strengths of both stages to produce high-quality results.
+ The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its components.
+ Although the results contain discernible artifacts, they are promising and demonstrate the potential of the proposed approach.
+ The authors provide an extensive evaluation of their design decisions, which helps to build confidence in the effectiveness of their method.

Weaknesses:
- The quantitative evaluation is generally weak, which is understandable given that no suitable evaluation metrics exist for this task. However, the authors could consider providing more qualitative analysis or user studies to demonstrate the effectiveness of their method.

Comments:
Given the iterative refinement nature of the proposed architecture, it would be beneficial to include some recent work on iterative refinement using cascaded networks in the related work section. For example, the authors could cite [a] J. Carreira, P. Agrawal, K. Fragkiadaki, and J. Malik. Human pose estimation with iterative error feedback. In IEEE Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition (CVPR), 2016, and [b] A. Newell, K. Yang, and J. Deng. Stacked hourglass networks for human pose estimation. In European Conference on Computer Vision (ECCV), 2016.

On line 110, the authors state that using residual blocks as basic components improves the generation performance. However, they do not elaborate on how this improvement is achieved. Providing more details on this point would strengthen the paper.

While the evaluation is generally extensive, it would be interesting to see how using the Stage 2 network with the inputs for Stage 1 and trained adversarially would perform. Is the blurring observed with Stage 1 + GAN due to the intermediate fully connected layer? Answering this question could provide valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of the proposed method.

Overall rating:
Based on the novelty of the problem and approach, I am confidently recommending that the paper be accepted. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field of image generation, and their work has the potential to inspire new research directions.

Rebuttal:
While I appreciate the attempt at a user study, I do not find it convincing due to its preliminary nature. I urge the authors to include a more substantive human evaluation in their revision. Nonetheless, my positive review of the work still stands.
