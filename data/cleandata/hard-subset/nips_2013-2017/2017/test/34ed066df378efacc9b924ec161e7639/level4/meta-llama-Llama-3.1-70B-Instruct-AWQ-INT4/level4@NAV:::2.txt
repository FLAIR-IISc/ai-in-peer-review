The authors propose a novel human image generator that leverages both appearance and pose information. This approach employs a two-stage adversarial training architecture, where a pair of generative networks collaborate to produce high-resolution images that are subsequently evaluated by a discriminator. Specifically, the generator component utilizes a U-shape network to generate a coarse image from appearance and pose maps, which is then refined by a second generator that predicts residual values to enhance the initial image. The DeepFashion dataset serves as the basis for evaluation.

This paper introduces several key concepts, including:

* A novel task formulation, where human image generation is conditioned on both appearance and pose maps.
* A stacked architecture that predicts difference maps rather than relying on direct upsampling, as well as a tailored loss function design.

To further enhance its impact, the paper could address the following areas:

* Image quality improvement: the generated results still exhibit visible artifacts and require refinement for practical applications.
* Significance: some readers might view the paper as yet another GAN architecture, rather than a groundbreaking contribution.
* Generalizability: while the approach is well-suited for vision and graphics applications, its applicability to other domains is limited.

The paper is well-organized and effectively conveys the core aspects of the proposed architecture. By stacking two networks, the generator adopts a coarse-to-fine strategy that is conditioned on appearance and pose information. This approach is particularly effective in addressing the dressing problem. The technique of generating difference maps is also noteworthy, as it facilitates the creation of higher-resolution images.

However, the paper's main limitation lies in the quality of the generated results, which still contain visible artifacts and require significant improvement for real-world applications. For instance, the patterns in ID346 of Fig 4 result in black dots in the final image. Although the second generator mitigates the blurriness introduced by the first generator, it appears that the model still struggles to recover high-frequency components in the target appearance.

Another potential concern is that some readers might view the proposed approach as an application of conditional GANs, rather than a novel contribution. While the paper addresses application-specific challenges, this might not resonate with a broader audience. Similar ideas have been explored in prior work, such as:

* Han Zhang et al., "StackGAN: Text to Photo-realistic Image Synthesis with Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks," arXiv:1612.03242.
* Xun Huang et al., "Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks," arXiv:1612.04357.

In conclusion, the paper successfully proposes a solution to the pose-conditioned image generation problem and conducts a thorough evaluation. The approach demonstrates technical novelty, although the resulting images require further quality improvement. Nevertheless, the proposed model generates visually consistent images. Based on these strengths, my initial rating is accept.
