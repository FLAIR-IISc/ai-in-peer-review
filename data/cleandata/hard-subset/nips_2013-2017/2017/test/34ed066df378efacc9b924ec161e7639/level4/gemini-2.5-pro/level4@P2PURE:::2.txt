This paper proposes a method for generating images of humans, conditioned on a target pose and an appearance encoding. The method employs a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) architecture featuring a two-stage generator that produces high-resolution images. The generator consists of two stages: the first, a U-Net-like network, takes the appearance information and a target pose map to produce a coarse image. The second stage then refines this coarse output by taking it, along with the original appearance encoding, and predicting a residual map. The authors evaluate their model on the DeepFashion dataset.

The paper's main contributions include the novelty of the task—conditioning human image generation on both appearance and pose—and its technical approach, which features a stacked, coarse-to-fine architecture that predicts a difference map, combined with a well-designed loss function.

The paper is well-organized and clearly presents the key aspects of the proposed architecture. The authors effectively apply this generation strategy to the problem of virtual try-on. The proposed approach seems well-suited to this problem, and the idea of predicting a residual map is an elegant technique for refining the output to generate higher-resolution images.

However, the paper has weaknesses that should be addressed. A major concern is the quality of the generated results, which contain visible artifacts and would require significant improvement for practical applications. For instance, in Figure 4, the patterns on the clothing of ID346 are rendered as black dots in the final output. While the second refinement network helps mitigate the blurriness from the first stage, the model still appears insufficient at recovering high-frequency details from the target appearance.

A further, though less severe, concern is that the work could be viewed as an incremental application of conditional GANs. The core ideas of conditioning and stacking generators within an adversarial framework have been explored in prior work, such as the following (though they are currently on arXiv):
* Han Zhang, et al., "StackGAN: Text to Photo-realistic Image Synthesis with Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks", arXiv:1612.03242.
* Xun Huang, et al., "Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks", arXiv:1612.04357.
While the paper effectively tackles application-specific challenges, this narrow focus on a vision/graphics problem might limit its appeal to the broader NIPS audience.

Overall, the paper successfully proposes a solution for pose-conditioned human image generation and provides a proper evaluation. The proposed approach demonstrates sufficient technical novelty, particularly in its two-stage, residual-based generator. Although the quality of the generated images needs improvement, the model is able to produce visually consistent results. My initial recommendation is to accept.
