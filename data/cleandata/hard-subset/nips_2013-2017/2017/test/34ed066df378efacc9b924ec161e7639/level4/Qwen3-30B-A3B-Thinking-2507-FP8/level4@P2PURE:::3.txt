

Review:  
This paper proposes a GAN-based system for synthesizing novel-view person images guided by a target skeleton (represented via keypoints). While the architecture primarily leverages established techniques (e.g., DCGANs, U-Nets), the contribution lies in its specific application to person image synthesis.  

Pros:  
* The core idea is novel and well-motivated, the exposition is clear, and empirical results demonstrate the system’s capacity to generate visually plausible images under certain conditions.  

Cons:  
* As an application-focused work, achieving robust results is critical. My primary concern is the absence of quantitative evaluation. For instance, conducting user studies to assess human perception of generated images (e.g., how often they deceive observers) would strengthen claims of realism.  
* The empirical results lack visual appeal. Figure 1, for example, displays generated images at insufficient resolution for clear inspection, and visible artifacts persist when comparing outputs to targets. This undermines confidence in the quality of the results, which are not convincingly realistic. Similar shortcomings persist across all presented results, including supplementary figures. While I acknowledge the work is on the right track and holds promise, it is premature to declare success at this stage.  

After Rebuttal:  
I remain unconvinced following the authors’ response. Other reviewers have also highlighted unresolved issues regarding both quantitative metrics and qualitative results—critical for a practical application. The rebuttal attempts to address these but fails to resolve them adequately. Consequently, the work does not meet the standard required for publication at a venue like NeurIPS.
