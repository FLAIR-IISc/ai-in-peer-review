- Task: Pose-guided person image synthesis — generate a person image in a target pose from a reference image and keypoint heatmaps.
- Method: PG2 uses a two-stage pipeline — Stage I U-Net encoder–decoder with pose-mask weighted L1 for a coarse result; Stage II a conditional GAN predicting a residual map with adversarial + mask-weighted L1 to refine details.
- Datasets: Evaluated on Market-1501 (128×64) and DeepFashion (256×256).
- Relation to prior work: Builds on conditional image generation, image-to-image translation, and pose/multi-view synthesis; mainly combines standard components.
- Novelty: Limited — main contributions are application-specific choices (pose-mask loss, residual refinement) rather than new theory or architectures.
- Strength — problem importance: Explicit pose control is interesting and practically relevant (content creation, data augmentation).
- Strength — design: Two-stage decomposition and use of pose mask and residual prediction are sensible engineering choices.
- Strength — clarity: Paper is generally well written and easy to follow.
- Strength — qualitative evidence: Shows globally plausible poses and some preserved appearance cues.
- Weakness — quantitative evaluation: Lacks rigorous perceptual metrics and human studies (e.g., FID, LPIPS, human Turing tests) and downstream task evaluation (e.g., re-ID).
- Weakness — visual quality: Qualitative results show artifacts (blurry textures, distorted limbs, facial artifacts, color bleeding) and figures are too small to assess.
- Weakness — analysis: Limited discussion of failure modes, robustness across diverse poses/occlusions, and training stability.
- Clarity issues: Need larger, high-resolution visualizations, zoomed crops, and more architectural/training detail (e.g., effect of λ, discriminator conditioning).
- Significance concern: Task is valuable but current evidence doesn’t convincingly demonstrate state-of-the-art realism or practical impact.
- Suggestion — evaluation: Add perceptual user studies, FID/LPIPS, and downstream evaluations (e.g., augmenting re-ID training).
- Suggestion — visuals & ablations: Provide high-res side-by-side targets, zoom-ins, and systematic ablation studies (single vs two-stage, mask vs no mask, residual vs direct).
- Suggestion — baselines & reproducibility: Compare to strong baselines (pix2pix-style, recent pose-guided methods), report statistical significance, and release code/models.
- Arguments for acceptance: Clear, well-motivated problem; coherent two-stage design; promising qualitative indication that pose conditioning helps.
- Arguments against acceptance: Mostly application of existing techniques, insufficient quantitative/qualitative evidence, persistent visual artifacts, and not ready for NeurIPS.
- After rebuttal: Authors’ response did not alleviate main concerns; additional results/metrics remain insufficient, reviewer still leans against acceptance.
