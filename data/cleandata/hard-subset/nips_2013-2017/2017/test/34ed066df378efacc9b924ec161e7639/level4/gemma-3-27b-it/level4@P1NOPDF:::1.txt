Summary:
This paper proposes an architecture for generating images of people, combining the appearance of an individual from an input image with a specified pose defined using heatmaps.

Strengths:
+ The proposed task is novel.
+ The two-stage, coarse-to-fine architecture is a novel contribution.
+ The paper is well-written and clear.
+ While the results exhibit some discernible artifacts, the overall outcome is promising.
+ The authors provide an extensive evaluation of the design choices made in their approach.

Weaknesses:
- The quantitative evaluation is relatively weak; however, this is understandable – and not a significant flaw – given the current lack of suitable evaluation metrics for this type of task. 

Comments:
Given the iterative refinement inherent in the proposed architecture, it would be beneficial to include recent work on iterative refinement using cascaded networks in the related work section, for example:
[a] J. Carreira, P. Agrawal, K. Fragkiadaki, and J. Malik. Human pose estimation with iterative error feedback. In IEEE Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition (CVPR), 2016.
[b] A. Newell, K. Yang, and J. Deng. Stacked hourglass networks for human pose estimation. In European Conference on Computer Vision (ECCV), 2016.
- Line 110: “we find that using residual blocks as basic components improves the generation performance.” Could you please elaborate on *how* the performance improves? More detail would be helpful.
- The evaluation is generally thorough, but I am curious about the potential performance of using the Stage 2 network with the inputs intended for Stage 1, trained adversarially. Is the blurring observed with Stage 1 + GAN related to the intermediate fully connected layer?

Overall rating:
Based on the novelty of the problem and the proposed approach, I confidently recommend accepting this paper.

Rebuttal:
While I appreciate the authors’ attempt to conduct a user study, its preliminary nature renders it unconvincing. Therefore, I have discounted it in my review rating. I encourage the authors to include a more substantive human evaluation in their revision. Nonetheless, my positive assessment of the work remains unchanged.
