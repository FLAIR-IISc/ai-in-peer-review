This paper presents a human image generator conditioned on explicit appearance and pose inputs. The model is trained adversarially with a two-stage, coarse-to-fine generator that produces high-resolution outputs for a discriminator. The first stage is a U-shaped network that synthesizes a coarse image from an appearance representation and a pose map. The second stage refines this result by predicting a residual (difference) map from the coarse image and the original appearance, which is then added to improve detail. Evaluation is conducted on the DeepFashion dataset.

Key contributions:
- Task novelty: conditioning generation jointly on appearance and pose for human image synthesis.
- Technical ideas: a stacked generator that predicts a residual rather than directly upsampling, together with a tailored loss.

Strengths:
- The paper is clearly written and the architecture is well explained.
- The coarse-to-fine residual refinement is a sensible design that effectively targets missing details and mitigates blur from the first stage.
- The method is well aligned with the “dressing” or virtual try-on scenario, where preserving appearance while adapting to a target pose is crucial.
- The difference-map refinement is a simple yet useful technique for producing sharper, higher-resolution images.
- The experimental setup on DeepFashion is appropriate and the evaluation appears properly conducted.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Output quality remains a major issue. Generated images often contain visible artifacts and insufficient high-frequency detail. For instance, in Fig. 4 (ID346), texture patterns degenerate into black dots in the final image. Although the second stage reduces blur, it does not reliably recover fine textures in the target appearance, which limits practical applicability.
- Significance and positioning: the approach may be viewed as a variant of conditional GANs with stacked generators. Similar stacking strategies have appeared previously, which could diminish perceived novelty even though the application focus here is different. Relevant examples include:
  - Han Zhang et al., “StackGAN: Text to Photo-realistic Image Synthesis with Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks,” arXiv:1612.03242.
  - Xun Huang et al., “Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks,” arXiv:1612.04357.
- Generality: while the application is compelling for vision/graphics, the method appears tailored to human pose and appearance and may be difficult to generalize to broader learning problems.

Overall assessment:
The paper addresses pose-conditioned human image generation with a coherent, well-motivated architecture and demonstrates solid execution on a relevant dataset. The residual-based, two-stage refinement offers a modest but meaningful technical contribution. However, the visual quality still needs improvement to handle complex textures and remove artifacts. Despite these limitations and the proximity to prior stacked GAN ideas, the work provides a useful solution for a timely application and shows sufficient technical novelty and empirical validation. My preliminary rating is accept.
