The paper proposes a human image generator conditioned on both appearance and human pose. The generation process utilizes an adversarial training architecture with a two-stage generative network to produce high-resolution images, which are then fed into a discriminator. The generator consists of two networks: the first generates a coarse image from appearance and pose maps using a U-shaped network; the second takes this coarse image along with the original appearance information to predict a residual map for refining the initial output. The authors evaluate their approach on the DeepFashion dataset.

The paper presents several noteworthy ideas:
* **Task Novelty:** The conditioning of image generation on both appearance and pose maps is a valuable contribution.
* **Techniques:** The stacked architecture, which predicts a difference map rather than performing direct upsampling, and the loss function design are both positive aspects.

However, the paper could be strengthened in the following areas to increase its impact:
* **Image Quality:** The generated results still exhibit visible artifacts and require further refinement for practical applications.
* **Significance:** The work could be viewed as another instance of a GAN architecture, potentially diminishing its overall impact.
* **Generalizability:** While the application to human image generation is relevant, the approach may be difficult to generalize to other learning problems.

The paper is well-organized and effectively conveys the key aspects of the proposed architecture. The use of a coarse-to-fine strategy, implemented through stacking two networks conditioned on appearance and pose, is a sensible approach. The method appears well-suited to the target problem of dressing and image manipulation. The residual map generation is a small but valuable technique for improving the resolution of generated images.

A major concern is the presence of visible artifacts in the generated results, limiting their applicability. For example, the patterns in ID346 of Figure 4 result in noticeable black dots in the final output. While the second generator mitigates the blurriness of the initial coarse image, the model appears insufficient to fully recover high-frequency components present in the target appearance. 

A secondary, though not severe, concern is that the proposed approach could be considered an application of conditional GANs. Prior work has explored conditioning and stacking generators for adversarial training (e.g., Zhang et al., 2016; Huang et al., 2016). The paper does address application-specific challenges, but this may not resonate with a broad audience. 

* Han Zhang, Tao Xu, Hongsheng Li, Shaoting Zhang, Xiaolei Huang, Xiaogang Wang, Dimitris Metaxas, "StackGAN: Text to Photo-realistic Image Synthesis with Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks", arXiv:1612.03242.
* Xun Huang, Yixuan Li, Omid Poursaeed, John Hopcroft, Serge Belongie, Stacked Generative Adversarial Networks, arXiv:1612.04357.

Overall, the paper successfully proposes a solution to the pose-conditioned image generation problem and presents a thorough evaluation. The approach demonstrates sufficient technical novelty. While the image quality still requires improvement, the model generates visually consistent results. My initial rating is accept.
