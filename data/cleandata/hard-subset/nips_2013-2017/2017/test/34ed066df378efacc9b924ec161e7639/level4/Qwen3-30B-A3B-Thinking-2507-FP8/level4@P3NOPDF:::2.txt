

The paper introduces a pose- and appearance-conditioned human image generation framework using a two-stage generative adversarial network (GAN). The approach employs a coarse-to-fine strategy: the first generator (U-shaped) produces a low-resolution image from appearance and pose maps, while the second generator refines this output by predicting a residual map, which is added to the coarse image. The method is evaluated on the DeepFashion dataset for virtual try-on applications.  

**Strengths:**  
The work demonstrates clear task novelty by conditioning generation on both appearance and pose, a significant departure from pose-only or appearance-only approaches. The technical contributions are well-motivated: the stacked architecture with residual prediction (rather than direct upsampling) effectively mitigates blurriness from coarse outputs, and the loss design appropriately balances perceptual quality and fidelity. The organization is logical, clearly articulating the coarse-to-fine pipeline and its suitability for dressing tasks. The residual-based refinement is a concise yet effective technique for enhancing high-frequency details, aligning well with the application’s demands.  

**Key Concerns:**  
1. **Quality Limitations:** The generated images exhibit persistent artifacts (e.g., black dots in ID 346 of Fig. 4), indicating insufficient recovery of high-frequency appearance details. While the second generator reduces blurriness from the first stage, the model struggles with complex textures and fine patterns, limiting practical utility. The authors acknowledge this but do not sufficiently address *why* high-frequency components remain unresolved (e.g., insufficient training data, architectural constraints).  

2. **Novelty and Significance:** The core architecture—stacked generators with residual refinement—is closely aligned with prior work. Zhang et al. (2016) introduced StackGAN for text-to-image synthesis using stacked generators and residual prediction, while Huang et al. (2016) explored stacked GANs for image generation. Although this paper applies the framework to a novel domain (human image generation with pose + appearance), the technical novelty is incremental. The contribution may be perceived as a specialized application of existing GAN paradigms rather than a foundational advancement. The significance is thus limited to vision/graphics communities, with minimal broader impact for machine learning audiences.  

3. **Evaluation Scope:** The evaluation relies solely on DeepFashion, without comparisons to recent pose-conditioned baselines (e.g., DressNet, Pose-Driven GANs) or quantitative metrics beyond qualitative results. This hinders assessment of whether the method outperforms state-of-the-art in *both* fidelity and pose alignment.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper presents a technically sound solution to a well-defined problem (pose-conditioned human image generation), with a clear pipeline and reasonable evaluation. The coarse-to-fine strategy with residual refinement is a practical adaptation to the task, though not transformative. The most significant weakness is the unresolved quality gap—artifacts persist despite the two-stage refinement, suggesting the approach may not yet meet the standards for real-world deployment. While the method generates visually plausible outputs, the artifacts (e.g., pattern distortions, texture inconsistencies) undermine confidence in its robustness.  

The novelty is somewhat constrained by existing stacked GAN literature (Zhang et al., 2016; Huang et al., 2016), though the application-specific challenges (e.g., handling garment textures under pose variations) add contextual value. For a venue like NIPS, the work’s appeal is narrower than ideal, as it primarily advances a niche application rather than a generalizable technique.  

**Recommendation:**  
I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper is well-executed and addresses a meaningful problem, but the authors must:  
- Quantify quality limitations (e.g., FID scores, user studies) to clarify the gap versus baselines.  
- Strengthen the novelty argument by explicitly contrasting with StackGAN and stacked GANs, emphasizing *why* the pose+appearance conditioning enables better results for human images than prior stacked GANs.  
- Include comparisons to recent pose-conditioned methods (e.g., DressNet, Pose-Driven GANs) in the evaluation.  

The current work is a solid contribution to the virtual try-on literature, and with these clarifications, it would better justify its place in a top-tier ML venue. The technical approach is appropriate, and the results, while imperfect, demonstrate feasibility. The paper successfully bridges pose estimation and image synthesis for a practical task, but must better articulate its distinctiveness and limitations.
