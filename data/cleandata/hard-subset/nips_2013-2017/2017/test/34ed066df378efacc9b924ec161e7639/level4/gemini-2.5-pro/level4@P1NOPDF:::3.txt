This paper proposes a GAN-based framework for generating images of people in novel poses, guided by a target skeleton (represented by keypoints). The proposed system is primarily composed of existing techniques, such as DCGANs and U-Nets. Therefore, the main contribution is not methodological but lies in the novel application of these components to the task of pose-guided person image synthesis.

**Pros:**
*   The proposed application is novel and interesting.
*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the ability to synthesize images with a degree of realism.

**Cons:**
*   Given that this work is application-focused, the quality of the final results is paramount. My primary concerns are the lack of quantitative evaluation and the unconvincing qualitative results.
*   The paper lacks any quantitative evaluation. For a generative task like this, it is crucial to move beyond subjective assessment. For instance, user studies could be conducted to evaluate how realistically the generated images are perceived by human observers.
*   The qualitative results are not yet compelling. The generated images, such as those in Figure 1, are presented at a low resolution, making detailed assessment difficult. Even at this small scale, significant visual artifacts and discrepancies are apparent when compared to the ground truth images. This issue persists across nearly all results, including those in the supplementary material. Consequently, it is difficult to agree with the authors' conclusion that the results are of high quality or close to photorealistic.

While I believe the authors are pursuing a promising research direction, the work in its current state is not yet mature enough to warrant publication.

**After rebuttal:**
After reading the author rebuttal, my assessment remains unchanged. The central concerns regarding the lack of robust quantitative and qualitative evaluation—which were also raised by other reviewers—have not been adequately addressed. For an application-oriented paper, both of these evaluation types are critical. While the authors made some attempt to address these points in their feedback, the new material is insufficient to alleviate the original concerns. Therefore, I believe the paper is not yet ready for publication at a top-tier venue like NIPS.
