Summary:
The paper presents an architecture for synthesizing person images that combine the appearance of a source image with a specified target pose represented as heatmaps. The method follows a two-stage, coarse-to-fine pipeline.

Strengths:
- Novel and well-motivated task.
- Effective two-stage coarse-to-fine architecture.
- Clear, well-structured writing.
- Results are promising despite visible artifacts.
- Extensive analysis and ablations of design choices.

Weaknesses:
- Quantitative evaluation is limited. This is understandable given the lack of standard metrics, but it remains a gap.

Comments:
- Because the method relies on iterative refinement, the related work should discuss recent cascaded/iterative refinement approaches, e.g., [a, b]:
[a] J. Carreira, P. Agrawal, K. Fragkiadaki, and J. Malik. Human pose estimation with iterative error feedback. CVPR, 2016.
[b] A. Newell, K. Yang, and J. Deng. Stacked Hourglass Networks for Human Pose Estimation. ECCV, 2016.
- Line 110: “we find that using residual blocks as a basic component improves the generation performance.” Please clarify the nature of the improvement: Does it yield sharper textures, better structural fidelity, fewer artifacts, faster convergence, or improved quantitative scores? Including specific qualitative examples and any available metrics would help.
- I am curious how a single Stage-2 network, trained adversarially but fed the same inputs as Stage 1, would perform. Would this collapse of the pipeline retain quality, or is the explicit coarse-to-fine decomposition essential?
- Relatedly, is the blurring observed with Stage 1 + GAN due to the intermediate fully connected layer? Consider an ablation that removes or replaces it with a fully convolutional alternative and report the effect on sharpness and detail.

Overall rating:
Accept. The problem and approach are novel, and the results and analysis are sufficiently compelling to warrant publication.

Rebuttal:
I appreciate the attempt at a user study, but in its current, very preliminary form it is not convincing; I therefore give it little weight in my assessment. I encourage the authors to include a more substantive human evaluation in the revision. Nonetheless, my positive recommendation stands.
