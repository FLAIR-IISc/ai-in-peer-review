Summary
The paper tackles pose-guided person image generation: given a source image of a person and a target pose (keypoints), synthesize a realistic image of the same person in the target pose. The method (PG2) uses a two-stage pipeline: Stage-I is a U-Net-like generator G1 that takes the reference image concatenated with 18-channel pose heatmaps and produces a coarse image aligned to the target pose, trained with a masked L1 loss that emphasizes human foreground. Stage-II is a conditional DCGAN whose generator G2 refines Stage-I by predicting a residual “difference map” from the coarse image and the condition image; the discriminator sees pairs (IA, IB) vs (IA, ÎB2). Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) include ablations on pose representation, loss, and one- vs two-stage training; quantitative metrics include SSIM/IS and mask-SSIM/IS; a small AMT user study is reported; and a qualitative comparison with VariGAN [36] is provided.

Strengths
- Well-motivated, practical task with clear applications; explicit control via keypoint heatmaps is intuitive and flexible.
- The two-stage “structure-then-detail” decomposition is reasonable and empirically effective, echoing stacked generation paradigms while tailored to pose transfer.
- Stage-II residual generation is a sound design choice, akin to residual learning, and likely stabilizes training and accelerates convergence.
- The masked L1 loss is simple and appropriate to reduce background confounds when condition and target backgrounds differ.
- Good ablations: (i) pose encoding (coordinates vs heatmaps concatenated at input vs separate encoder) (ii) loss with/without mask (iii) one-stage adversarial vs two-stage adversarial. Qualitative gains are convincing, especially on DeepFashion.
- Pairwise conditioning of D on (IA, ·) is important for preventing degenerate copying of IA and encourages identity consistency.
- Implementation details and datasets are stated clearly.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Baselines are limited. The “one-stage adversarial” baseline uses the same G1+D. Missing are stronger, widely used alternatives such as pix2pix-style cGAN with U-Net and PatchGAN trained directly on {IA, PB}→IB (with and without perceptual losses), or a single-stage model taking both IA and pose heatmaps with the same mask loss. The qualitative comparison to [36] is useful but that method is not strictly comparable (coarse view labels instead of keypoints).
- Evaluation metrics: SSIM and IS are known to correlate poorly with perceptual realism; IS on person datasets is problematic. The proposed mask-SSIM/IS removes background influence but uses estimated masks derived from keypoints; this could bias evaluation and does not resolve the core shortcomings of SSIM/IS. No FID or human-perceptual metric beyond a small AMT study is reported. The AMT protocol (1 s exposure) is very strict; more informative would be longer exposures and pairwise preference tests.
- No objective pose-accuracy measure: since pose is central, it would be natural to run a pose detector on generated images and compare to the target pose (PCK/OKS), quantifying how well pose is matched across variants.
- Identity preservation is not measured. As the identity/appearance should be retained, a downstream metric such as cosine similarity in a re-identification or person embedding space would be appropriate. Downstream utility (e.g., augment re-ID training with generated samples) is not explored.
- Stage-II ignores the target pose input; it only sees IA and ÎB1. A discussion or ablation showing that G2 does not drift from the intended pose (and whether including PB helps) would strengthen the claim.
- Background handling: while the mask loss deemphasizes background, the method often copies or artifacts the condition background (acknowledged by the authors). A stronger treatment (segmentation-guided training, explicit compositing, or inpainting a new background) is not attempted.
- Sensitivity to pose estimation noise is not analyzed. An experiment with synthetic noise on keypoints, or with ground-truth keypoints when available, would be informative.
- Some design choices are under-justified: removal of batch norm/dropout in G1/G2, choice of weighting (1+MB) rather than a tunable α·MB, and claims about faster convergence with residual generation are not quantified.
- Reproducibility: architecture is outlined, but exact channel widths, skip-connection details, and λ selection process are partly deferred to the supplement; code availability is not discussed. Runtime and memory footprint are not reported.

Quality
The method is technically sound and the ablations support key claims (pose embedding choice, benefit of mask loss, and two-stage refinement). However, the evaluation lacks stronger baselines and objective measures of pose/identity fidelity, and some claims (e.g., convergence speed, artifact reduction trade-offs with λ) would benefit from quantitative backing.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. Figures (especially the pipeline) help. The masked L1 definition uses (1+MB), which effectively doubles foreground weight; consider clarifying the rationale and whether other weights were explored. Some typos and minor grammar issues remain. Explicitly stating whether PatchGAN is used and the discriminator’s receptive field would help.

Originality
The components (U-Net with heatmaps, masked reconstruction loss, two-stage training, residual generation, conditional GAN) are individually known, but their combination for explicit pose-conditioned person image generation is novel for this setting and contrasts with contemporaneous view-conditioned generation. The residual-in-GAN refinement is a neat twist.

Significance
Pose-guided person generation is important for graphics, data augmentation, and controllable synthesis. The qualitative results are strong on DeepFashion, and the approach is likely to influence subsequent work. That said, the lack of rigorous comparisons and task-relevant metrics limits the strength of the empirical claims.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please add a pix2pix-style baseline taking [IA, PB]→IB with PatchGAN and perceptual loss, and report both quantitative and qualitative comparisons.
- Report a pose-accuracy metric (e.g., PCK) by running a pose estimator on generated images.
- Report an identity similarity metric using a person re-ID network; optionally test if augmenting re-ID training with generated images improves accuracy.
- Provide quantitative evidence that the residual formulation accelerates convergence, and add an ablation including PB as input to G2 to check pose drift.
- Analyze sensitivity to keypoint noise; if possible, compare estimated vs (semi-)ground-truth keypoints.
- Clarify discriminator design (PatchGAN vs global) and include λ ablations in the main paper. Consider a tunable foreground weight α instead of (1+MB).
- Discuss or mitigate background artifacts (e.g., segmentation-aware training, compositing).
- Include runtime and parameter counts; release code if possible.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper addresses a timely, impactful problem with a sensible, well-executed method and solid qualitative improvements. Strengthening the baselines and adding pose/identity metrics would significantly bolster the empirical case. Confidence: high.
