**Summary**

This paper introduces the novel task of pose-guided person image generation: synthesizing a photorealistic image of a person from a source image, guided by a target pose represented by keypoints. To tackle this challenging problem, the authors propose a two-stage generative network, PG2. The first stage employs a U-Net-like architecture to generate a coarse image that captures the global structure and color of the person in the target pose. A key contribution here is a "pose mask loss" designed to focus the L1 loss on the foreground person, mitigating issues with background variation. The second stage uses a conditional GAN to refine the coarse output, adding high-frequency details and improving realism. The generator in this stage is cleverly designed to learn a residual map, and the discriminator is conditioned on the source image to enforce identity consistency. The method is evaluated on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets, demonstrating compelling qualitative results and validating the design choices through ablation studies.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its novelty and ambition. The authors are not merely proposing an incremental improvement on an existing task; they are defining a new, challenging, and highly relevant problem for the community. The ability to directly manipulate a person's pose via an explicit and intuitive representation like keypoints is a significant conceptual leap beyond prior work in view synthesis, which often relies on more abstract conditioning like viewpoint labels. This work opens up a compelling new direction for controllable generative models with clear applications in data augmentation, content creation, and virtual reality. This boldness and originality should be highly valued.

The proposed two-stage method is a logical and well-motivated approach to this difficult problem. Decomposing the task into first establishing the global structure (Stage I) and then adding fine details (Stage II) is an effective strategy that makes the problem more tractable. Several technical contributions are noteworthy:
1.  The exploration of different pose embeddings clearly justifies the chosen heatmap concatenation approach.
2.  The pose mask loss is a simple but effective solution to the inherent ambiguity of background generation, elegantly focusing the model's capacity on the subject of interest.
3.  The design of the Stage II refinement network, particularly the residual generation and the conditional discriminator, is well-conceived for improving image quality while preserving identity.

The experimental evaluation is solid. The qualitative results are impressive, especially on the DeepFashion dataset, showing that the model can generate coherent textures and details on clothing and even faces while successfully transferring the pose. The ablation studies systematically demonstrate the value of the key components of the proposed architecture (pose embedding, pose mask loss, two-stage design). The inclusion of a user study is commendable, as it provides a more meaningful measure of perceptual quality than standard metrics like SSIM, which, as the authors rightly note, often fail to correlate with human judgment for GAN-generated images.

The paper does have some weaknesses, though they are largely excusable given the novelty of the task. The network architecture itself is primarily a thoughtful combination of existing components (U-Net, residual blocks, DCGAN) rather than a fundamental architectural innovation. However, the novelty here lies in the problem formulation and the specific way these components are integrated to solve it, which is a perfectly valid form of contribution. The quantitative results using SSIM are not entirely convincing, as the refined GAN-based model sometimes scores lower than the blurry Stage-I output. This is a known artifact of perception-based generative models, and the authors are transparent about it. The user study results are more compelling and should be weighed more heavily. Finally, the failure cases highlight the current limitations of the method, such as handling rare, complex poses or occasional identity/attribute confusion. I view these not as fatal flaws, but as honest reporting and clear avenues for the future work that this paper is sure to inspire.

**Conclusion and Recommendation**

This paper presents a bold new direction for conditional image generation. It introduces a significant and challenging task, proposes a strong and well-engineered baseline solution, and validates its approach with compelling results. While it is not without minor imperfections, its conceptual contribution far outweighs them. The work is likely to have a substantial impact, inspiring follow-up research on controllable human image synthesis. It prioritizes a novel and important problem over a methodologically perfect but incremental solution, which is precisely the kind of forward-looking research that NIPS should champion.

I strongly recommend acceptance.
