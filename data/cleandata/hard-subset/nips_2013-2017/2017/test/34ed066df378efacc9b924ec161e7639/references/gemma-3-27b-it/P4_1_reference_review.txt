This paper proposes a two-stage generative adversarial network (GAN) for pose-guided person image generation. The core idea is to first generate a coarse image respecting the target pose, then refine it using a second GAN to add details. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a good overview of related work.

**Summary of Related Work:** The paper appropriately positions itself within the landscape of image generation, covering VAEs, GANs, and autoregressive models. It highlights recent works in conditional image generation, both attribute-conditioned and image-conditioned, and identifies the gap in explicit pose control. The comparison to existing multi-view synthesis methods, such as [36], is relevant and demonstrates the novelty of conditioning on keypoints rather than viewpoint.

**Quality:** The technical soundness is generally good. The two-stage approach is a reasonable strategy for tackling the complex task of pose transfer. The use of a U-Net for initial pose integration and a DCGAN for refinement is standard practice. The introduction of the pose mask loss is a sensible addition to focus training on the relevant body parts. However, the motivation for some design choices, particularly the removal of the fully connected layer in the second-stage generator, feels somewhat weak. The paper would benefit from a more detailed ablation study examining the impact of different components. The quantitative results, while presenting improvements, are somewhat limited in their interpretability, as noted by the authors themselves. The connection between SSIM scores and perceptual quality is acknowledged, but a broader set of metrics (e.g., FID) would be helpful. The experiments appear to be well-designed, employing two datasets with varying resolutions, and the comparisons to the related work [36] are useful.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The figures are informative and effectively illustrate the method. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are concise and easy to follow. The architecture diagrams are particularly helpful. The implementation details provide sufficient information for reproducing the results, although the exact hyperparameter settings could be more explicitly stated.

**Originality:** The task of pose-guided person image generation, conditioning on both a reference image *and* a specified pose, is a novel contribution. While image-conditioned generation is not new, the explicit pose control using keypoints is a significant step forward. The pose mask loss is a relatively minor but useful addition. The two-stage approach, while not entirely novel in image generation, is effectively applied to this specific problem. The combination of these elements constitutes a worthwhile contribution.

**Significance:** The potential applications of this work are significant, including movie making, human pose estimation data augmentation, and virtual try-on systems. The ability to manipulate a person's pose in an image opens up interesting possibilities for content creation and editing. While the current results are not flawless, they demonstrate a promising direction for future research. The method could potentially be extended to other object categories beyond humans.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel task and approach to pose-guided image generation.
*   Clear and well-written paper with informative illustrations.
*   Solid technical foundation with a reasonable two-stage strategy.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness on two datasets.
*   Potential for significant real-world applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The motivation for some design choices (e.g., removing the fully connected layer) is unclear.
*   Limited scope of quantitative evaluation; the inclusion of FID score would be helpful.
*   The ablation study could be more extensive.
*   The qualitative results, while good, are not consistently high-quality; some generated images exhibit artifacts.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper that addresses a novel and challenging problem. The method is technically sound, clearly explained, and demonstrates promising results. While there are some minor areas for improvement, the originality and potential significance of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
