Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles conditional person image synthesis: given a reference person image and a target pose (keypoints), generate the person in the new pose while preserving identity/appearance. The proposed PG2 system is two-stage. Stage I integrates the input image with a pose representation using a U-Net-like generator with skip connections and a fully-connected bottleneck, producing a coarse image trained with a masked L1 loss that emphasizes foreground (body) over background. Stage II refines details via a conditional GAN: the generator predicts a residual “difference map” added to Stage I’s output, and the discriminator receives image pairs (condition image, generated/real) to enforce consistency with the condition. The paper explores pose embeddings (coordinates vs. heatmaps), compares L1 vs. the proposed pose mask loss, and one- vs. two-stage training. Experiments on DeepFashion and Market-1501 show qualitative improvements, ablation gains, SSIM/IS and user study results, and a comparison to VariGAN for multi-view synthesis.

This work connects to conditional image generation (CVAE, conditional GANs), image-to-image translation (e.g., pix2pix), and pose/view-conditioned synthesis for faces and people. The explicit pose-as-keypoints conditioning and two-stage “coarse-to-fine” strategy relate to stacked/refinement GANs, and the residual/difference learning is aligned with residual learning (ResNets) and refinement ideas. Compared to prior view-conditioned generation that uses coarse view labels or text, this paper uses fine-grained 2D keypoints and emphasizes preserving identity.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and motivation (explicit, controllable person pose transfer from a single reference image).
- Sensible two-stage design that separates structure (pose) from details; the “difference map” refinement is simple and effective.
- Thoughtful pose representation as multi-channel heatmaps directly compatible with convolution; ablation convincingly shows its advantage over coordinate-based embeddings.
- Masked L1 loss is a practical idea to mitigate background mismatch and focus learning on the person.
- Comprehensive ablations (pose embeddings; loss variants; one- vs. two-stage), qualitative results on two datasets, and a user study.
- Pairwise conditional discriminator aligns adversarial training with the conditioning signal, reducing trivial copying of the input.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation metrics are limited: SSIM and (mask-)IS are known to correlate poorly with perceptual quality for conditional generation; trends sometimes contradict visual quality. No FID or perceptual similarity (e.g., LPIPS) reported.
- Identity preservation is central but not quantitatively evaluated (e.g., using a re-identification model to check identity consistency or retrieval from generated images).
- Background modeling is largely sidestepped; the model either ignores or imperfectly copies/introduces artifacts. Generating a foreground plus alpha matte, or explicit background handling, would be stronger.
- Reliance on 2D keypoints; no analysis of robustness to keypoint errors. Failure cases show sensitivity to rare poses and data imbalance, but no systematic study or mitigation.
- Limited comparisons: VariGAN is not directly comparable in conditioning and task; missing comparisons to appearance flow/view synthesis approaches (e.g., Zhou et al., ECCV’16) and contemporary image-to-image methods conditioned on structure (even if adapted).
- Deterministic outputs: no mechanism for sampling diverse plausible textures/details; noise is removed in Stage II.
- Some architectural/design choices are under-motivated or lightly analyzed (e.g., the FC bottleneck in Stage I vs. fully-convolutional in Stage II; lambda sensitivity relegated to supplement).

Quality
Overall technically sound and well-executed. The masked L1 and pairwise conditional discriminator are appropriate. The residual refinement is a reasonable design that accelerates training. Experiments cover key ablations and include a user study. However, the evaluation does not directly measure identity preservation or pose accuracy, lacks stronger perceptual metrics, and comparisons are sparse. The method does not demonstrate downstream utility (e.g., data augmentation for re-ID/pose estimation).

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with intuitive figures and sensible descriptions. Some references appear incomplete or inconsistent; a few figures could benefit from higher resolution and clearer legends. The construction of the foreground mask from keypoints could be described more precisely (algorithmic details, failure handling). Hyperparameters (e.g., λ) and architectural sizes are partly deferred to the supplement.

Originality
Moderate. The problem setting (pose-guided person generation from a single image) is timely and interesting. Using pose heatmaps as conditioning and a two-stage coarse-to-fine refinement with residual learning are combinations of known ideas adapted to this task. The masked L1 is a practical but incremental contribution. The work’s novelty mainly lies in assembling an effective pipeline for this specific application.

Significance
Promising for graphics, data augmentation, and controllable content creation. The qualitative results are compelling on DeepFashion. Broader impact would be strengthened by showing gains in a downstream task (e.g., augmenting re-ID training) and more robust handling of backgrounds and rare poses. As-is, it advances the state of practice on this task but leaves open issues in evaluation and robustness.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add identity-preservation evaluation: retrieval with a pretrained re-ID model (mAP/Rank-1), face/body feature similarity, or human AB tests focused on identity.
- Report stronger perceptual metrics (FID; user pairwise preference tests). Consider LPIPS for perceptual similarity.
- Evaluate pose accuracy on generated images (e.g., PCK with a pose estimator) to quantify structural correctness.
- Provide sensitivity analyses: keypoint noise/occlusions; λ ablation in the main text; effect of the Stage I FC bottleneck vs. fully convolutional alternatives.
- Improve background handling: predict foreground + alpha matte; train with segmentation supervision when available; composite with estimated backgrounds to avoid artifacts.
- Introduce stochasticity to allow diverse outputs, e.g., latent noise injection or dropout at inference, possibly with a diversity loss.
- Broaden comparisons and discuss relations to appearance flow/view-synthesis and other structure-conditioned generation approaches.
- Address dataset imbalance (e.g., reweighting/augmentation) and show improved performance on rare poses/genders.

Pros for acceptance
- Well-motivated, practical task with clear contributions.
- Effective two-stage architecture with solid ablations.
- Strong qualitative results on high-resolution data.
- Pairwise conditional discriminator and residual refinement are sensible and useful.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited quantitative evaluation (no identity/pose metrics; weak perceptual metrics).
- Background modeling and robustness issues remain.
- Comparisons are limited; novelty is incremental.
- No demonstration of downstream utility.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a coherent, effective approach to an important conditional generation problem, with convincing qualitative gains and thoughtful ablations. Strengthening the evaluation (identity, pose, perceptual metrics) and comparisons would substantially improve the work. Even so, the method is likely to be of interest to the NeurIPS community and could spur follow-up research on controllable, pose-guided synthesis.
