## Review of Pose Guided Person Image Generation

This paper proposes a two-stage GAN-based framework for generating person images conditioned on both a reference image and a target pose. The core idea is to decouple pose transfer into a global structure learning phase (Stage-I) followed by a detail refinement phase (Stage-II). The authors explore various pose embedding techniques and introduce a pose mask loss to focus on foreground transfer, addressing a challenging image manipulation problem with potential applications in areas like movie making and data augmentation.

While the problem addressed is interesting and potentially valuable, I find the presented work to be incremental and lacking the rigor expected for NIPS acceptance. The methodology, while reasonably sound, relies heavily on established techniques (U-Net, DCGAN, adversarial training) and the novelty lies primarily in the combination of these techniques and the specific application to pose-guided person image generation. Crucially, the paper lacks sufficient comparative evaluation against strong baselines, particularly those employing more recent and sophisticated generative modeling approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Statement:** The task of pose-guided image generation is well-defined and has practical relevance.
* **Decoupled Approach:** The two-stage strategy of separating global structure and detail refinement is a sensible approach, and the motivation is clear.
* **Pose Mask Loss:** The introduction of a pose mask loss is a reasonable attempt to address the issue of background changes and focus on relevant image regions.
* **Qualitative Results:** The visual examples presented demonstrate a degree of success in transferring poses and maintaining identity.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Strong Baselines:** The comparison with Zhao et al. [36] is insufficient. That work addresses multi-view *synthesis*, a different problem.  A thorough evaluation requires comparison with state-of-the-art image-to-image translation methods (e.g., Pix2Pix, CycleGAN) adapted to this specific task, as well as more recent GAN architectures (e.g., StyleGAN). Simply stating that existing methods struggle with explicit pose control is not a substitute for empirical demonstration.
* **Limited Novelty:** The core building blocks (U-Net, DCGAN) are well-established. While the combination is reasonable, it doesn't represent a significant methodological breakthrough. The pose embedding, while potentially effective, is not a fundamentally new contribution.
* **Insufficient Ablation Study:** The ablation study (different pose embeddings, loss functions) is somewhat limited. The impact of each component could be more rigorously assessed. For example, the justification for removing the fully connected layer in G2 is not fully convincing and warrants further investigation.
* **Quantitative Evaluation Concerns:** While quantitative metrics are provided, the reliance on SSIM, especially in the presence of generated artifacts and background differences, is questionable. The introduction of mask-SSIM is a step in the right direction, but further metrics capturing perceptual quality (e.g., FID) would be more informative.  The user study results are mixed and suggest issues with the generated images (specifically, artifacts impacting user perception).
* **Failure Cases:** The discussion of failure cases, while present, lacks depth. A more detailed analysis of the limitations of the approach and potential future directions would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and potentially useful problem.
* The proposed approach demonstrates a reasonable degree of success in the task of pose-guided person image generation.
* The introduction of the pose mask loss is a sensible addition.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of comparison with strong baselines significantly weakens the claims of novelty and effectiveness.
* The methodological contribution is incremental, relying heavily on existing techniques.
* The quantitative evaluation is limited and potentially misleading.
* The user study results raise concerns about the perceptual quality of the generated images.




**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the work is not fundamentally flawed, it does not meet the high standards of originality, significance, and rigorous evaluation expected for NIPS. The authors need to significantly strengthen the experimental evaluation, providing a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods. Furthermore, a more detailed analysis of the limitations and potential future directions would improve the paper’s overall contribution.  The current submission reads more like a proof-of-concept than a substantial research contribution.
