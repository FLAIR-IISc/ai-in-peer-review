Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Pose Guided Person Generation (PG2), a conditional image synthesis framework that, given a reference person image and an explicit target pose (18 keypoint heatmaps), produces a new image of the same person in the desired pose. The system is explicitly designed in two stages: (i) a U-Net-like generator (G1) integrates the reference image and target pose to produce a coarse image capturing global body structure; a pose-mask-weighted L1 loss de-emphasizes background mismatch. (ii) A refinement stage (G2) uses a conditional DCGAN to predict a difference map that refines high-frequency appearance; the discriminator operates on pairs (generated-or-real, condition) to discourage trivial copying of the condition image. The same pose-mask is used in an L1 regularizer to stabilize adversarial training.

The paper investigates several pose embeddings (coordinates, separate heatmap encoder, and direct concatenation of pose heatmaps with the image), shows that direct per-keypoint heatmap concatenation is most effective, and demonstrates the value of the pose-mask loss. Experiments on DeepFashion and Market-1501 include qualitative results, ablations (pose embedding, mask loss, one- vs two-stage), a user study, and masked SSIM/IS metrics to reduce background effects. The method outperforms a closely related multi-view synthesis baseline and qualitatively improves over a one-stage cGAN baseline.

Relative to prior conditional generation (CVAE/C-GAN, pix2pix-style translation, view-conditioned face/body work, text-to-image, and multi-view clothing), this work adds an explicit and flexible pose control signal via keypoint heatmaps and a two-stage, difference-map refinement scheme tailored to pose transfer.

Strengths
- Novel and useful problem setting: conditioning on both a reference image (appearance) and an explicit target pose offers fine-grained, user-controllable generation beyond category-, text-, or viewpoint-only conditioning.
- Clear and simple architectural choices with good intuition: (a) per-keypoint heatmap concatenation; (b) a two-stage divide-and-conquer that separates structure from detail; (c) predicting a difference map in stage II to speed convergence; (d) pairwise discriminator input to enforce consistency with the conditioning image.
- Thoughtful loss design: pose-mask-weighted L1 focuses learning on the person, addressing unavoidable background ambiguity.
- Comprehensive ablations that isolate the contribution of pose embedding, mask loss, and two-stage refinement; inclusion of a user study is appreciated.
- Practical impact: the approach could serve content creation, data augmentation for pose estimation/re-ID, and controllable human image editing.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Reliance on an external pose estimator is a hidden dependency; failures or inaccuracies in keypoints are likely failure modes. A robustness analysis to pose noise or missing joints is missing.
- Evaluation gaps: identity preservation is central to the task but is not quantified. For example, re-ID matching accuracy between generated and real images or perceptual identity metrics would be informative. Similarly, pose accuracy of the outputs could be measured by running a pose detector on generated images.
- Metrics: SSIM/IS (even masked) correlate weakly with perceptual quality; more perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) and diversity/realism metrics would strengthen claims. Qualitative results suggest sharper images sometimes score worse on SSIM.
- Background handling: although the mask loss mitigates background mismatch, the method can transfer or hallucinate background fragments from the reference, introducing artifacts (as noted by the authors for Market-1501). A simple background inpainting or explicit foreground compositing strategy could help.
- Limited comparisons: while a one-stage adversarial baseline (G1+D) is included, a stronger pix2pix-style baseline that concatenates [IA, pose] with multi-scale discriminator and perceptual loss would better contextualize gains.
- Some clarity/formatting issues: missing details for mask construction (exact morphology pipeline), sensitivity to λ relegated to the supplement, and several references are incomplete. A few architectural choices (e.g., no normalization) merit brief justification.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The two-stage design, difference-map refinement, and pairwise discriminator are reasonable and well-motivated. Experiments are substantial on two datasets with ablations and a user study. The main shortcomings are in evaluation breadth (identity/pose metrics, stronger baselines) and analysis of failure modes.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized, with useful figures and ablations. The paper would benefit from tightening reference formatting, providing precise mask-construction details, reporting λ sensitivity in the main text, and briefly explaining the choice of omitting normalization/dropout.

Originality
High. The explicit conditioning on target keypoint heatmaps for person pose transfer, combined with a two-stage, difference-map GAN refinement and pose-masked losses, represents a novel formulation at the time relative to view/text/attribute conditioning and prior face/body synthesis works.

Significance
High. This is a compelling, controllable generation paradigm with clear downstream uses. The approach is simple enough to be adopted and extended, and the empirical gains are visible even if some quantitative metrics are imperfect.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- How robust is PG2 to noisy/missing keypoints? Please report performance vs. synthetic keypoint noise or ablate joints.
- Can you quantify identity preservation (e.g., re-ID rank-1 between generated and target of the same identity) and pose accuracy (e.g., PCK on generated images)?
- How does PG2 compare to a pix2pix-like baseline with VGG perceptual loss and multi-scale discriminator?
- Did you try foreground compositing (segmenting the person before/after generation) to reduce background artifacts?
- Please include details for pose mask computation and λ sensitivity in the main paper, and consider releasing code/models.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, bold problem formulation with explicit pose control.
- Simple, well-motivated architecture and loss design.
- Strong qualitative results and useful ablations.
- Clear potential impact and extensibility.

Cons against acceptance
- Evaluation misses identity and pose fidelity metrics; baselines could be stronger.
- Background artifacts and dependency on pose estimator not deeply analyzed.
- Some clarity/formatting issues.

Recommendation
Accept (7/10). The conceptual advance—explicit pose-guided person image generation with a pragmatic two-stage refinement—is significant and likely to influence subsequent work. While the evaluation can be strengthened, the novelty and demonstrated promise outweigh the limitations.

Reviewer confidence: 3.5/5.
