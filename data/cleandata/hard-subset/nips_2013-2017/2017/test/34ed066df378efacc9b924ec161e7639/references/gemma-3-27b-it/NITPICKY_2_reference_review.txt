## Review of Pose Guided Person Image Generation

This paper proposes a two-stage GAN-based framework, PG2, for generating person images conditioned on both a reference image and a target pose. The core idea of decomposing the problem into global structure estimation (Stage-I) and detail refinement (Stage-II) is reasonable, and the proposed pose mask loss aims to focus generation on the person’s appearance rather than the background. The authors evaluate the approach on two datasets, DeepFashion and Market-1501, presenting both qualitative and quantitative results.

While the paper addresses a potentially useful and interesting task, and demonstrates some initial success, a multitude of minor flaws and inconsistencies, alongside questionable experimental practices, detract significantly from its overall quality. The presentation is often imprecise, and the justification for certain design choices is lacking.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Task:** Conditioning image generation on both reference image and pose is a plausible and potentially useful task, setting it apart from existing work focusing on single conditions.
*   **Divide-and-Conquer Strategy:** Decomposing the problem into global structure and detail refinement is a logical approach, potentially simplifying the learning process.
*   **Pose Mask Loss:** The proposed pose mask loss is a sensible attempt to address background inconsistencies, a common issue in image translation tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigorous Evaluation:** The quantitative evaluation is weak. Reporting SSIM *and* Inception Score is standard, but the validity of these metrics is questionable given the task. The proposed *mask*-SSIM/IS, while attempting to address a real issue, is not well-motivated. A more robust evaluation, perhaps involving user studies assessing photorealism *and* pose accuracy, is needed. The provided user study has flaws (see below).
*   **Imprecise Writing & Lack of Detail:** The paper suffers from a pervasive lack of precision. For example, “simplified residual block” lacks a clear definition – what exactly was simplified? What are the filter sizes used in various layers? What are the precise dimensions of heatmaps? The lack of detail hinders reproducibility.
*   **Questionable Experimental Setup:** The hyperparameter selection (λ) is alluded to but not thoroughly reported. The influence of λ is delegated to supplementary materials, which is unacceptable for a crucial parameter affecting image quality. Data augmentation details are vague (“left-right flip”) - were any other augmentations applied?
*   **Inconsistent Reporting:** The text refers to “two datasets” (DeepFashion and Market-1501) yet experiments are not equally weighted. The impact of the datasets' differing resolutions (256x256 vs. 128x64) is not discussed.
*   **User Study Issues:** The user study methodology is problematic. The extremely short exposure time (1 second) raises concerns about the reliability of human judgment. The reported metrics (R2G and G2R) require more explanation of their interpretation. The fact that Market-1501 consistently shows higher R2G (Real images misclassified as generated) suggests the low resolution and noise inherent in surveillance footage are confounding the results.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to Zhao et al. [36] is superficial and focuses on a single visual example. A more comprehensive comparison, including quantitative metrics and ablation studies, is required.
* **Formatting and minor errors:** Eq. 1 is not properly formatted, and the sentence following it is incomplete. There are numerous minor grammatical and stylistic issues throughout the paper.
* **Unnecessary Acronyms:** Repeated use of overly-long acronyms (e.g., PG2) without sufficient context.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Abstract/Introduction:** The motivation is reasonable but lacks a concise problem statement.
*   **Related Work:** While covering relevant areas, the related work section is somewhat disorganized and lacks critical analysis.
*   **Method (Pose Embedding):** The justification for using heatmaps instead of direct coordinate embedding is weak. While the authors claim direct mapping is difficult, this assertion is not supported by any experimentation.
*   **Method (Stage-I):** The description of the U-Net architecture is high-level and lacks critical details.
*   **Method (Pose Mask Loss):** The method for creating the pose mask “connecting human body parts and applying a set of morphological operations” is overly vague.
*   **Method (Stage-II):** The explanation for pairing the G2 output with the condition image in the discriminator is convoluted and poorly explained.
*   **Experiments:** The lack of ablation studies is a significant omission. Were both stages truly necessary? What is the impact of the pose mask loss?
*   **Results:** The qualitative results are not compelling. Many generated images exhibit artifacts and inconsistencies. The conclusion that Stage-II improves image quality is not convincingly demonstrated.
*   **Table 1:** The table is poorly formatted. The column headings are too long.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Against Acceptance:**

*   Numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies in writing, methodology, and presentation.
*   Weak quantitative evaluation and questionable experimental practices.
*   Lack of ablation studies to justify design choices.
*   Superficial comparison to related work.
*   Problematic user study methodology.

**For Acceptance:**

*   The task is novel and potentially interesting.
*   The divide-and-conquer strategy is a reasonable approach.
*   The proposed pose mask loss is a sensible idea.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially interesting ideas presented, the paper suffers from a multitude of flaws that undermine its contribution. The lack of rigor in evaluation, imprecise writing, and numerous minor inconsistencies make it difficult to assess the true value of the proposed approach. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should address the issues raised in this review and significantly revise the paper before resubmission. A more thorough evaluation, clearer presentation, and stronger justification for design choices are essential.
