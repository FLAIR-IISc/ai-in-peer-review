Summary
The paper tackles pose-guided person image generation: given a reference image of a person and a target pose (18 keypoints), synthesize the person in the new pose. The method (PG2) uses a two-stage, coarse-to-fine pipeline. Stage-I is a U-Net-like generator G1 that takes the reference image concatenated with pose heatmaps and is trained with an L1 loss weighted by a pose-derived foreground mask (“pose mask loss”) to prioritize the person over background. Stage-II is a conditional DCGAN-style refinement network G2 that takes the coarse output and the reference image, and learns a residual/difference map to add details; training combines an adversarial loss with the same masked L1. The discriminator sees image pairs (generated or real, each paired with the reference) to enforce consistency with the condition. Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) include qualitative results, ablations on pose embeddings (coordinates vs heatmaps), loss variants (L1 vs pose mask loss), and 1-stage vs 2-stage adversarial training. Quantitative evaluation uses SSIM and a “masked” SSIM/IS intended to discount backgrounds, plus a brief AMT user study. The method outperforms a multi-view baseline [36] qualitatively.

Strengths
- Clear and sensible task formulation; conditioning on explicit 2D keypoints offers fine-grained control.
- Two-stage design (coarse structural synthesis, then detail refinement) is simple and effective; the “difference map” refinement is a practical twist that can ease optimization.
- The pose heatmap concatenation is empirically better than coordinate embeddings.
- Pose mask weighting is intuitive for handling background mismatch and yields visibly crisper people.
- Ablations (pose embedding, loss, 1-stage vs 2-stage) are informative; user study is a useful addition.
- Results on two diverse datasets show promise; qualitative improvements over [36] are convincing.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/methodological
- Baselines: Missing a strong, relevant baseline such as pix2pix/cGAN (Isola et al. 2017) using pose heatmaps (and optionally foreground masking) as input. Given the architecture similarities, this is an important reference point. Other coarse-to-fine/residual GAN baselines (LapGAN/StackGAN) are discussed but not instantiated.
- Identity preservation is central to the task but not evaluated quantitatively. A re-ID classifier-based metric (e.g., rank-1/mean AP) or face-ID similarity for upper-body/face crops would directly test whether identity is preserved.
- Pose correctness is not quantified. A detector applied to generated images (PCK/PCKh or OKS between generated and target pose) would measure structural fidelity and complement SSIM/IS.
- “Mask-IS” is ill-defined and potentially invalid: IS relies on an ImageNet classifier; masking/corrupting backgrounds changes the data distribution and renders IS harder to interpret. Precise details (mask creation, normalization, effect on the classifier) are missing.
- Key details are deferred to the supplement or omitted: the λ value(s), difference map combination formula (is output clipped? applied additively? in what range?), discriminator/gen update ratio, learning rates for G vs D, weight initialization, image normalization, and exact number of channels per block. As written, the work is not fully reproducible.
- Stage-II discriminator conditions only on the reference image, not the target pose. This seems inconsistent with the goal of enforcing pose faithfulness at refinement. Why not condition D on pose as well?
- The decision to remove batch normalization and to include a fully connected layer at Stage-I’s bottleneck is unusual and unexplained. The FC layer breaks full-convolutionality, increases parameters, and may hinder generalization across resolutions.
- No robustness analysis to pose estimator errors (noise/missing keypoints). Given reliance on an external estimator, sensitivity analysis is important.
- The proposed foreground weighting (1+MB) is arbitrary; no ablation on the foreground-to-background weight nor on mask quality.
- Background handling: Stage-II appears to transfer background from the condition image, yet the target background is unknown; this can yield artifacts and confound evaluation. A background-agnostic or compositing strategy could be discussed.

Experimental/analysis
- Quantitative metrics are weak proxies for perceptual quality; FID or LPIPS (or perceptual studies with pairwise comparisons) would strengthen claims. The paper acknowledges SSIM’s limitations but still leans on it.
- Dataset protocols: For DeepFashion, clarify identity/clothing disjointness between train/test. For Market-1501, large numbers of training pairs are created—ensure there is no pair leakage across splits.
- Comparison to [36] is not strictly comparable (they condition on view words, not keypoints). A fair apples-to-apples pose-conditioned baseline is missing.
- No speed, memory, or parameter counts; no training stability discussion; convergence claims for residual learning are qualitative.

Clarity/formatting
- Numerous formatting and citation issues (many references incomplete; missing venue/pages; inconsistent styles; obvious LaTeX artifacts, e.g., “st Conference...”, stray footnotes, grammar/typos: duplicated words, etc.). Figures/captions are hard to follow without the supplemental visuals.
- Some notational inconsistencies (e.g., ÎB, ÎB1, ÎB2 usage) and missing equations (how the difference map composes with ÎB1).

Broader impact
- No discussion of potential misuse/ethics (identity manipulation, deepfakes), dataset biases (gender imbalance mentioned only as a failure cause), or safeguards.

Questions for the authors
1) What λ values were used on each dataset, and how sensitive are results to λ? Please report these in the main paper.  
2) Precisely define mask-SSIM and mask-IS (mask construction; preprocessing; whether scores are computed on masked crops or zeroed backgrounds).  
3) How is the difference map combined with ÎB1 (formula, intensity range, clipping)?  
4) Why not condition D on the target pose as well? Did you try it?  
5) Provide a pix2pix-style baseline with pose heatmaps and foreground mask.  
6) Provide identity preservation metrics via a re-ID model and pose accuracy via PCK/OKS on generated images.  
7) Report parameter counts, training stability, and ablate the FC bottleneck vs fully convolutional.  
8) Analyze robustness to noisy/missing keypoints.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical and timely task; intuitive two-stage design with residual refinement.  
- Clear qualitative gains over simple L1 and over view-conditioned baseline.  
- Useful ablations; user study supports perceptual improvement.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing strong baselines and key quantitative metrics tied to identity and pose.  
- Several methodological ambiguities prevent reproduction.  
- Weaknesses in evaluation protocol (mask-IS/SSIM), lack of robustness analysis.  
- Significant presentation/formatting issues.

Recommendation: Reject (confidence: high). The paper presents a promising approach, but the accumulation of missing baselines, incomplete quantitative evaluation, unclear details, and substantial presentation issues outweigh the contributions. With stronger baselines, clearer methodology, and rigorous metrics (identity and pose fidelity), this could become a solid contribution.
