Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Pose Guided Person Generation (PG2), a two-stage conditional image generation framework that synthesizes a target image of a person given a reference image of the same person and an explicit target pose. Pose is represented as 18 keypoint heatmaps obtained from an off-the-shelf pose estimator. Stage I (G1) is a U-Net-like encoder-decoder with skip connections and residual blocks that integrates the reference image and pose to produce a coarse image capturing global structure. A foreground-weighted “pose mask” L1 loss emphasizes person pixels to mitigate background mismatch across views. Stage II (G2) is a conditional DCGAN that refines the coarse image by predicting a difference map between the Stage I output and the target, trained with an adversarial loss using a pairwise discriminator on (condition image, output) vs (condition image, ground truth) and a masked L1 regularizer.

The paper relates to conditional generative models (CVAE, GANs, autoregressive) and image-to-image translation (e.g., pix2pix). Compared to prior conditional generation with labels, text, or view tokens, this work uses explicit pose keypoints for fine-grained control. The coarse-to-fine design echoes stacked/ staged GANs and residual learning, but is adapted here to person pose transfer. The approach is evaluated on DeepFashion and Market-1501, with ablations on pose embeddings (coordinates, separate heatmap encoder, vs direct concatenation), loss functions (standard vs mask L1), and training strategy (one-stage adversarial vs proposed two-stage). Quantitative metrics include SSIM/IS and mask-variants to discount background; a brief user study is reported. A qualitative comparison to VariGAN [36] is shown.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation: conditioning on a reference image and explicit target pose enables precise control over person pose transfer, a practically relevant task.
- Sound architecture design: two-stage coarse-to-fine decomposition, residual U-Net backbone, pairwise conditional discriminator, and a difference-map refinement are coherent and technically sensible.
- Effective pose representation: concatenating keypoint heatmaps to the image input outperforms coordinate/bottleneck embeddings in ablations.
- Foreground-aware objective: the pose mask loss is a simple, effective heuristic to reduce background mismatch across camera views and datasets.
- Comprehensive qualitative evidence: results on two datasets with wide pose variation; ablations (pose embedding, loss, stages) show consistent improvements; user study indicates realism gains on DeepFashion.
- Practical details: training regimes, optimizers, and architecture sizes are provided; failure cases are discussed.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative rigor: reliance on SSIM/IS and custom mask-SSIM/mask-IS is not fully convincing; standard perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) or distributional distances (FID/KID) are absent. Identity preservation is not quantitatively evaluated (e.g., re-ID retrieval accuracy between generated and real images), despite being central to the task.
- Comparisons: only a qualitative comparison to VariGAN [36]; no comparisons to contemporary conditional image-to-image baselines (e.g., pix2pix trained for this setup) or other human image synthesis approaches available at the time.
- Evaluation design: user study is small and uses brief exposures; conclusions are limited, and Market-1501 results show lower G2R and some background artifacts.
- Method details: the construction of the pose mask (MB) is not fully specified (exact morphological operations, sensitivity to keypoint errors), and the weighting λ for the masked L1 is deferred to the supplement. The use of a fully connected layer in G1 may degrade spatial fidelity and contributes to blur; the motivation is given, but ablation for FC vs fully convolutional at Stage I would help.
- Generalization and robustness: sensitivity to pose detector noise, occlusions, and rare poses is only qualitatively discussed; failure modes (gender ambiguity, unusual poses) suggest dataset biases.
- Metrics vs claims: the two-stage adversarial model improves realism, but quantitative gains over one-stage are modest or ambiguous on SSIM/IS; the paper notes SSIM’s bias toward blur, but stronger evaluation would substantiate the claimed advantages.

Quality
Technically sound and well-motivated. The architecture, loss functions, and training are standard but appropriately adapted. Empirical validation includes ablations and two datasets. However, quantitative evaluation is limited and does not directly measure key desiderata (identity preservation, pose accuracy), and comparisons to strong baselines are sparse.

Clarity
Overall clearly written, with a logical structure and informative figures. Ablation studies are helpful. Some implementation specifics (pose mask generation, exact λ, per-layer filter counts) are deferred or omitted. The description of the discriminator pairing and difference-map generation is clear.

Originality
The combination of an explicit pose-conditioning via keypoint heatmaps with a two-stage coarse-to-fine GAN and a difference-map refinement is a reasonable and somewhat novel integration. Individual components (U-Net, conditional GAN, residual learning, foreground weighting) are known. The problem setup—pose-guided person image generation from a reference image—is timely and distinct from view-token conditioning.

Significance
The task is important for controllable human image synthesis and data augmentation, and the approach appears practical and effective, especially on DeepFashion. The work is likely to be used or extended. That said, the evidence on robustness and identity preservation is incomplete, and stronger quantitative baselines would bolster impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add identity-preservation metrics (e.g., re-ID accuracy using embeddings on generated vs real images), and pose-accuracy metrics (e.g., PCK between target pose and pose of generated image).
- Include FID/KID and LPIPS; report results with and without mask preprocessing to clarify background effects.
- Compare against a pix2pix-style baseline trained for the same task and a fully convolutional one-stage U-Net+GAN, and ablate the difference-map strategy vs direct refinement.
- Provide full details for pose mask construction, λ values, and sensitivity analyses to pose estimator errors.
- Analyze failure cases quantitatively (e.g., by pose rarity) and report results under occlusions or extreme poses.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, useful problem formulation enabling explicit pose control.
- Thoughtful, effective model design with two stages and pose-aware loss.
- Strong qualitative results and ablations demonstrating benefits of key components.
- Relevance to NeurIPS topics in generative modeling and controllable synthesis.

Arguments against acceptance
- Quantitative evaluation is limited and uses nonstandard metrics; lack of identity and pose accuracy measures.
- Sparse comparisons to strong baselines; improvements over a one-stage adversarial model are not clearly quantified.
- Some methodological details are under-specified, limiting reproducibility and interpretability of the mask loss impact.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents a coherent and practically meaningful approach with convincing qualitative gains and solid ablations, but would benefit from stronger quantitative evaluation and broader baselines.
