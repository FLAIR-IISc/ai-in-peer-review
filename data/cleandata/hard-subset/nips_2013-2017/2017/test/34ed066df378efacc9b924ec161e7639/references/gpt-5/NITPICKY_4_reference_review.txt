Summary
This paper tackles pose-guided person image generation: given a reference image IA and a target pose PB (as keypoint heatmaps), generate an image IB̂ of the same person under PB. The method, PG2, is a two-stage pipeline: Stage-I (G1) is a U-Net-like encoder-decoder (with residual blocks and a fully connected bottleneck) trained with a masked L1 loss to produce a coarse image that matches the target pose and low-frequency appearance; Stage-II (G2) is a conditional DCGAN trained adversarially (plus the same masked L1) to refine high-frequency details. The discriminator receives paired inputs (IA, output) vs (IA, IB). The authors also propose a “difference map” concept at Stage-II (G2 is said to predict the residual relative to G1’s output). They evaluate on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) with SSIM/mask-SSIM and Inception Score/mask-IS, plus a small AMT user study; ablations consider alternate pose embeddings and losses. They compare qualitatively to VariGAN [36].

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on conditional GAN/image-to-image translation paradigms (e.g., pix2pix [Isola et al.], DCGAN [Radford et al.]) and pose-conditioned generation (e.g., Reed et al. on controllable structure, Zhao et al. on multi-view clothing). The two-stage design echoes stacked/patch-level GANs (e.g., StackGAN; cascaded refinement; image-to-image pipelines that first get structure then texture). Using pose heatmaps as conditioning is standard (e.g., in pose estimation/pose-guided generation contemporaries), and masked/foreground-weighted losses are common in segmentation/refinement settings. The claimed novelty primarily lies in (i) conditioning on both a reference image and explicit target pose for full-body person transfer and (ii) training G2 to refine Stage-I output (purportedly as a residual).

Strengths
- Well-motivated task with practical applications; explicit pose conditioning offers precise control.
- Simple, decomposed architecture that isolates structure and texture; empirically effective.
- Sensible use of pose heatmaps vs coordinate embeddings; ablations support the choice.
- Masked L1 is a practical engineering trick to reduce background mismatch issues.
- Pairwise conditional discriminator (with IA) is appropriate to avoid trivial copying.
- Demonstrates results on both a high-resolution fashion dataset and a more challenging surveillance dataset; includes AMT study and ablations.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Inconsistency regarding the “difference map”: the text says G2 predicts a difference map added to ÎB1, but the loss/equations treat G2(IA, ÎB1) as the final image without addition. The paper must specify ÎB2 = ÎB1 + Δ (and use Δ = G2(...)) or remove the difference-claim. As written, the method description is self-contradictory.
- Baselines are insufficient. A strong one-stage pix2pix-style baseline using IA+pose-heatmaps→IB with a PatchGAN discriminator and perceptual/feature-matching losses is missing. The “G1+D” baseline is not clearly aligned with standard image-to-image cGAN practice. No comparison to using G2 alone conditioned on (IA, PB) is provided. VariGAN comparison is not apples-to-apples (view label vs explicit pose), is only qualitative, and lacks metrics.
- Quantitative metrics are weakly justified. Inception Score (and especially mask-IS) on these domains is questionable and not standard for conditional generation; the Inception network/class labels are not specified. Mask-SSIM/IS definitions need precise details (how masks are derived for evaluation; whether the same masking biases the feature distribution fed to Inception). No FID (acknowledging its timing, but at least precision/recall-like measures could be considered); no pose-accuracy metric (e.g., running a pose estimator on outputs to quantify pose fidelity).
- AMT study design is under-specified: number of unique images per method, randomization, inter-annotator agreement, confidence intervals, and statistical significance are missing. The 1s exposure protocol is reasonable but the aggregation lacks rigor.
- Architecture details are incomplete for reproducibility: exact channel counts per block, spatial resolutions, placement/dimensions of the fully connected layer in G1 (especially for 256×256), initialization, training schedules (D:G update ratio), λ value(s) and selection procedure (reported only “in supplement”), whether Stage-I is frozen during Stage-II, data normalization, and preprocessing. Not using BN/dropout needs justification given DCGAN practices; stability measures are not discussed.
- The “pose mask” construction is vague (which morphological ops, connectivity rules, how failure of pose detection is handled, sensitivity to keypoint errors).
- The claim “converges faster” due to difference maps is not substantiated with training curves or wall-clock/epoch comparisons.
- Background handling: the method often copies background from IA or introduces artifacts (acknowledged qualitatively), but no quantitative or architectural mitigation (e.g., segmentation guidance, background inpainting) is explored.
- Writing/formatting issues: numerous typos and grammatical errors; broken figure captions and cross-references; stray footnote markers; incomplete references (missing venues, years, titles truncated); inconsistent notation; some sentences are hard to parse. This significantly hampers clarity.
- Scope/novelty: the two-stage decomposition and masked L1 are incremental; conditioning on pose heatmaps plus reference image aligns with contemporaneous work. Novelty claims should be toned down and differences to closely related pose-guided generation works clarified.

Quality
The experimental evidence suggests the approach works, but the evaluation is not rigorous enough and key method details are missing/ambiguous (difference-map inconsistency, hyperparameters). Reproducibility is questionable.

Clarity
The paper is difficult to follow in places due to formatting and grammar, and lacks critical implementation specifics. Figures referenced are not sufficiently described in-text for a reader to independently assess.

Originality
Moderate. The problem setup is compelling, but the technical contributions (two-stage with masked L1 and pairwise cGAN) are modest extensions of known ideas. The “difference map” would be a small but nice idea if clearly and correctly implemented.

Significance
Potentially useful for pose-guided editing and data augmentation, but the paper in its current form does not convincingly establish state of the art due to baseline gaps and metric concerns.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, impactful task with visible qualitative improvements.
- Simple method that practitioners could adopt.
- Ablations lend some insight into design choices (heatmaps vs coordinates; masked L1).

Cons for acceptance
- Method description inconsistency (difference map) and missing key details.
- Inadequate baselines and questionable metrics undermine claims.
- Poorly polished writing and references; clarity issues.
- Limited quantitative rigor (no error bars, significance, or robust metrics).

Questions for authors
1) Clarify whether G2 outputs a residual added to ÎB1; fix equations and training losses accordingly. Report convergence comparisons. 2) Provide λ values and selection protocol; is G1 frozen during Stage-II? 3) Add strong pix2pix-style baselines with PatchGAN and perceptual/feature-matching losses; report FID (if feasible) and pose-accuracy of outputs. 4) Detail architecture (channels, resolutions, FC size), mask construction, handling of missing keypoints, and training schedules. 5) Strengthen AMT with confidence intervals and significance tests; expand VariGAN comparisons fairly.

Recommendation
Reject (due to cumulative technical/detail omissions, evaluation shortcomings, and clarity/formatting issues). The idea is promising; with clearer method specification, stronger baselines/metrics, and improved writing, this work could be much more compelling. Confidence: high.
