### Summary

This paper introduces a novel task: generating a realistic image of a person in a specified target pose, given a single source image of that person. The authors propose a two-stage generative network, termed PG2, to tackle this problem. The first stage consists of a U-Net-like architecture that takes the source image and a heatmap representation of the target pose to generate a coarse output. This stage focuses on capturing the global structure and uses a novel "pose mask L1 loss" to prioritize the foreground person over potentially changing backgrounds. The second stage refines this coarse output using a conditional GAN. A second U-Net-like generator produces a refinement/difference map, and a conditional discriminator judges pairs of (source image, generated image) to enforce identity preservation and realism. The method is evaluated on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets, showing qualitative and quantitative improvements over single-stage baselines and demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed components.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Interesting Problem:** The task of pose-guided person image generation is well-motivated and has clear applications in areas like data augmentation, virtual try-on, and content creation. It presents a significant technical challenge that is a valuable direction for the generative modeling community.
2.  **Logical Two-Stage Approach:** The divide-and-conquer strategy is sensible. Decomposing the complex generation task into a coarse structural generation stage followed by a fine-detail refinement stage is a logical design choice that appears effective, as suggested by the qualitative results.
3.  **Thoughtful Component Design:** The paper explores several design choices, such as different pose embeddings. The proposed pose mask loss is a clever idea to mitigate the ambiguity of background generation, which is a key difficulty in this task. Conditioning the discriminator on the source image is also a standard but appropriate choice for this problem.

### Weaknesses

Despite the novel contributions, the paper suffers from a substantial number of minor flaws in its methodology, evaluation, and presentation. These issues, in aggregate, undermine the confidence in the work's technical soundness and completeness.

**1. Presentation and Clarity:**

*   **Formatting Issues:** The manuscript contains several formatting errors that suggest a lack of careful preparation. The footnote `∗Equal contribution.` and the NIPS conference line appear as floating text at the bottom of the first page. The citation style is frequently broken, with multiple extraneous commas (e.g., "[2] arXiv,, , ,").
*   **Missing/Incomplete Captions:** The text refers to Figures 1 and 2, but these figures and their captions are entirely absent from the provided paper. Figure 5 and 6 have captions that read like author's personal notes ("few man samples and complex texture -->failure details in G2"), which is highly unprofessional for a formal submission.
*   **Vague Methodological Descriptions:** Key details of the methodology are underspecified, hindering reproducibility.
    *   The generation of the `pose mask MB` is described as "connecting human body parts and applying a set of morphological operations". This is critically vague. The exact procedure, including the type of connection and the specific morphological operations and their parameters, is not provided. Since this mask is central to the proposed loss function and evaluation metrics, its heuristic and opaque nature is a significant weakness.
    *   The pose heatmap generation uses a "radius of 4 pixels". It is unclear if this radius is adapted for the different resolutions of the two datasets (256x256 vs. 128x64). A fixed pixel radius would have a different semantic meaning across resolutions.
    *   Crucial architectural details and hyperparameters are omitted from the main text (e.g., the value of `λ` in Eq. 4, precise filter counts, kernel sizes in residual blocks), being relegated to supplementary materials.

**2. Methodological Concerns:**

*   **G1 Architecture:** The use of a fully-connected layer in G1 to "exchange information" between distant body parts is a dated approach that creates a severe information bottleneck and discards all spatial structure. The authors even note its removal in G2 is beneficial, which calls into question its inclusion in G1 in the first place. More modern alternatives like attention mechanisms or dilated convolutions would be more appropriate.
*   **G2 Inputs:** The generator G2 takes both the coarse result `ÎB1` and the original condition image `IA` as input. The need to re-introduce `IA` suggests that G1 fails to adequately transfer identity-preserving appearance information to `ÎB1`. A more effective G1 would render `IA` redundant for the refinement stage.
*   **Discriminator Conditioning:** The discriminator `D` sees the pair `(IA, ÎB2)` but not the target pose `PB`. While the L1 loss on the generator enforces the pose, the discriminator itself cannot penalize pose inaccuracies. This might limit the network's ability to learn fine-grained pose correctness from the adversarial signal.

**3. Evaluation and Analysis:**

*   **Non-Standard Metrics:** The paper introduces "mask-SSIM" and "mask-IS". While the motivation to exclude backgrounds is sound, these metrics are non-standard and their implementation is not detailed. How is IS computed on a masked image distribution? This is not a trivial modification and requires thorough justification, which is absent. The dependency of these metrics on the poorly described mask generation process is also concerning.
*   **Contradictory Quantitative Results:** The authors explicitly state that quantitative metrics like SSIM do not align with perceptual quality, noting that blurrier images from G1 achieve higher scores than the sharper results from the full model. This admission significantly devalues the quantitative results presented in Table 1 and suggests that the chosen metrics are not suitable for this task. The paper would be stronger if it relied on more perceptually-aligned metrics (e.g., LPIPS, FID) or focused more heavily on the user study.
*   **User Study Flaws:** Showing each image for only 1 second seems insufficient for users to make a reliable judgment about photorealism, especially for a complex task involving human figures and clothing textures. This short duration could introduce significant noise into the results.
*   **Weak Comparison:** The comparison with VariGAN [36] is limited to two qualitative examples on one dataset. The tasks are also not identical, as [36] uses categorical view labels rather than explicit keypoint poses, making a direct comparison difficult to interpret.

### Recommendation: Reject

While the paper addresses a novel and significant problem with a promising high-level approach, the execution is marred by a large number of small-to-medium-sized flaws. The lack of clarity in key methodological components, the questionable evaluation metrics, the contradictory quantitative results, and the numerous presentation errors collectively suggest that the work is premature for publication at a top-tier venue like NIPS. The paper requires a major revision to address the methodological ambiguities, strengthen the experimental evaluation with appropriate and well-justified metrics, and correct the significant presentation and formatting issues.
