**Summary:**
This paper introduces a novel task: generating an image of a person in a specified target pose, conditioned on a single source image of that person. The authors propose a two-stage generative model, called the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), to tackle this problem. The first stage uses a U-Net-like architecture to generate a coarse image that matches the target pose's global structure, integrating the source image with a heatmap representation of the target pose. A key contribution here is a "pose mask loss" which weights the L1 loss to focus on the person's body rather than the background. The second stage refines this coarse output using a conditional GAN. This refinement generator (G2) learns to predict a residual (or difference map) to add high-frequency details, and the discriminator (D) is conditioned on both the generated image and the source image to ensure identity preservation. The method is evaluated on two datasets, DeepFashion and Market-1501, showing convincing qualitative results and supported by quantitative metrics and a user study.

**Review:**

This is a well-executed paper that introduces a novel and interesting problem with a sensible and effective solution. The two-stage, coarse-to-fine approach is a strong design choice for this complex conditional generation task. The experimental validation is thorough and largely supports the authors' claims.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed two-stage architecture is logical: Stage 1 handles the large geometric transformation (re-posing), while Stage 2 focuses on texture and detail refinement, a common and effective strategy in generation tasks. The design choices within each stage are well-justified.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The ablation studies in Section 4.2 are excellent. They clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed heatmap concatenation for pose embedding over simpler coordinate-based methods.
    *   The introduction of the pose mask loss is a simple but clever solution to the practical problem of changing backgrounds, and the qualitative results (Fig. 4, col 6 vs 7) show its effectiveness in reducing background noise and improving focus on the subject.
    *   The design of the Stage-II conditional GAN, where the discriminator sees pairs of (generated, source) images, is a good way to enforce that the generated person resembles the source identity, not just any realistic person.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The quantitative evaluation relies on SSIM and Inception Score (IS). The authors rightly note that these metrics often don't correlate well with perceptual quality for GAN-generated images, especially SSIM which can favor blurry results. While the inclusion of "mask-SSIM" and "mask-IS" is a good idea to mitigate background effects, the fundamental limitations of these metrics remain. The user study provides stronger evidence, but the results on the more challenging Market-1501 dataset are less conclusive (G1+G2+D performs worse than G1+D in fooling users), and the explanation provided feels somewhat post-hoc.
    *   The analysis of failure cases in Section 4.5 is brief. Given the complexity of the task, a more in-depth discussion of limitations would strengthen the paper. For instance, how does the model handle significant self-occlusion in the target pose? How does it deal with loose, complex clothing (e.g., skirts, long coats) whose shape is not fully determined by the skeleton?

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly defined, the proposed method is explained logically, and the figures (especially the main architecture diagram in Figure 2) are illustrative and helpful. The structure of the paper follows a standard, coherent path from introduction to conclusion. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly. The writing is precise, and the authors provide enough detail for an expert to grasp the core concepts of the architecture and training procedure.

**Originality:** The primary originality of this work lies in the formulation of the problem itself: pose-guided person image generation. While conditional image generation is a well-studied field, conditioning on an explicit and detailed skeletal pose to transform a specific person's appearance is a novel contribution.

The components used (U-Nets, residual blocks, conditional GANs, L1+adversarial loss) are standard building blocks in the generative modeling literature. However, the novelty here comes from their thoughtful combination and application to solve this new, challenging task. The pose mask loss and the specific two-stage refinement process are also novel contributions within this problem context. The paper does a good job of situating its work within the related literature, clearly distinguishing it from attribute/text-conditioned generation and viewpoint synthesis.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a problem with significant practical implications. The ability to realistically re-pose individuals in images has clear applications in areas like virtual try-on for e-commerce, data augmentation for pose estimation and action recognition systems (as mentioned by the authors), and special effects in media production. The results are of high enough quality to suggest that this is a promising research direction. The proposed PG2 network serves as a strong baseline for future work in this area. The clear problem definition and solid technical execution make it likely that other researchers will build upon these ideas.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Introduces a novel and well-motivated problem of pose-guided person generation.
2.  Proposes a sensible and effective two-stage solution that decomposes the problem into global structure generation and local detail refinement.
3.  Includes strong and thorough experimental validation, including well-designed ablation studies that justify the model's key components (pose embedding, mask loss).
4.  The qualitative results are compelling, especially on the high-resolution DeepFashion dataset.
5.  The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and easy to understand.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The novelty is primarily in the problem formulation and combination of existing techniques, rather than a fundamental advance in generative modeling itself.
2.  The quantitative metrics (SSIM, IS) are not fully convincing, and the user study results are somewhat mixed on the more difficult dataset.
3.  The analysis of failure modes and limitations could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Recommendation:**
I recommend **Accept**. The strengths of this paper—particularly its novel problem formulation, well-designed solution, and thorough empirical validation—significantly outweigh its weaknesses. It is a complete and high-quality piece of research that opens up a new and interesting direction in conditional image generation. It is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community and to inspire follow-up work.
