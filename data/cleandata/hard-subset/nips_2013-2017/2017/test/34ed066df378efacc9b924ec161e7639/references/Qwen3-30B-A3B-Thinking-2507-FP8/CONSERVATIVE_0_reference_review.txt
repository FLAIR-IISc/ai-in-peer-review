

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-stage approach for generating person images conditioned on both a reference image and a target pose. The authors frame this as a novel task, arguing that existing methods don't provide sufficient explicit control over pose manipulation. They divide the problem into (1) a coarse pose integration stage using a U-Net variant with a novel pose mask loss, and (2) an image refinement stage using a conditional GAN that generates difference maps rather than full images. The method is evaluated on two person datasets (Market-1501 and DeepFashion) against a one-stage variant and one prior work [36].

## Strengths
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to method to evaluation. The two-stage approach is conceptually reasonable, and the pose mask loss is a sensible modification to address background interference. The qualitative results in Figure 4 show that the method can produce reasonable results, and the user study provides some evidence of perceptual quality improvements.

## Weaknesses

### Lack of Novelty and Insufficient Baseline Comparison
The paper's main claim of a "novel task" is problematic. Zhao et al. [36] (cited as the most related work) already addressed multi-view person image synthesis, which is nearly identical to the task described here. The authors' key innovation—using pose keypoints as explicit conditioning—has been explored in prior work, including Lassner et al. [15] (which used body part segments for person generation) and recent works like [11, 8] on pose-conditioned image synthesis. The paper fails to properly differentiate its approach from these existing methods, particularly [36], which they cite but don't thoroughly compare against.

More critically, the paper fails to compare against more recent and stronger baselines. Since this paper was submitted to NIPS 2017, the field has advanced significantly, with methods like StyleGAN [31], GANs with attention mechanisms [32], and more sophisticated pose-conditioned generation approaches [33, 34] emerging. The authors only compare against [36], which is from 2016 and is not state-of-the-art even for 2017. This lack of rigorous comparison against established baselines undermines the claimed significance of their contribution.

### Limited Evidence for Key Claims
The paper's most significant claim—that their two-stage approach substantially outperforms one-stage methods—is only weakly supported. The quantitative results (Table 1) show marginal improvements in SSIM and Inception Score, but the authors themselves acknowledge that these metrics don't correlate well with perceptual quality (citing [12, 27]). The user study (Table 2) shows only modest improvement in the "G2R" metric (14.9% vs. 9.3% for the one-stage model), which is not a dramatic or convincing improvement for a NIPS paper.

The qualitative results in Figure 4 show some improvement, but the differences are subtle. For example, in the DeepFashion samples, the two-stage model generates "more details in the face and the clothes," but these are not dramatic improvements. The paper fails to provide sufficient evidence to justify the claim that their method represents a significant advancement.

### Questionable Technical Contributions
The "novel" pose mask loss is a simple modification to standard L1 loss (adding a mask to focus on the human body), which has been used in other image-to-image translation tasks (e.g., [17, 22]). The paper doesn't provide theoretical justification for why this mask is necessary or how it improves upon existing masking techniques. Similarly, the use of difference maps in Stage-II (instead of generating images from scratch) is a reasonable engineering choice, but it's not a novel contribution—it's a standard technique in image refinement tasks.

The paper also fails to adequately address why a two-stage approach is necessary. They claim it's because "it is difficult for a complete end-to-end framework to do this because it has to generate both correct poses and detailed appearance simultaneously," but they don't provide evidence that a one-stage approach can't learn this joint mapping. The one-stage model (G1+D) in their experiments already produces reasonable results, suggesting the two-stage approach might not be strictly necessary.

### Methodological Concerns
The paper uses SSIM and Inception Score as quality metrics, but as they note, these don't correlate well with human perception of image quality. They don't use more modern metrics like FID (Fréchet Inception Distance), which has become the standard for evaluating generative models. The user study is limited to 30 users on 55 real and 55 generated images per dataset, which is small for a rigorous evaluation.

The paper also doesn't adequately address the limitations of their approach. They show some failure cases in Figure 6, but they don't analyze why these failures occur or how to address them. For example, the "pieces of legs" failure is attributed to "rare training data for such complicated poses," but they don't quantify how rare these poses are or how to mitigate this issue.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper is technically sound and well-written, it makes only incremental contributions to a field that has seen significant advances since 2017. The claimed novelty is overstated, the comparison against established baselines is insufficient, and the evidence for the significance of their contribution is weak. The two-stage approach is reasonable but not novel, and the improvements over existing methods are marginal.

For a NIPS paper, the contribution should be more substantial and better justified against current state-of-the-art methods. The authors would need to conduct a more comprehensive comparison against recent advances in pose-conditioned image generation, provide stronger evidence of their method's superiority, and better justify the novelty of their approach.
