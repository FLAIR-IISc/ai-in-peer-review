### Summary

This paper introduces a novel task: generating a realistic image of a specific person in a target pose, conditioned on a single source image of that person and a set of target pose keypoints. To tackle this challenging problem, the authors propose a two-stage generative framework, the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2). The first stage consists of a U-Net-like generator (G1) that takes the source image and a heatmap representation of the target pose as input. Its goal is to generate a coarse image that correctly captures the global structure, pose, and low-frequency appearance details of the person. A key contribution here is a "pose mask loss" which encourages the model to focus on the foreground person rather than the background, which may differ between the source and target contexts. The second stage employs a conditional GAN (G2 and D) to refine the coarse output from Stage 1. The generator G2, also a U-Net variant, learns to generate a "difference map" to add high-frequency details and improve photorealism. The discriminator is conditioned on the source image to ensure the refined output preserves the identity of the person. The proposed method is evaluated on a high-resolution fashion dataset (DeepFashion) and a low-resolution person re-identification dataset (Market-1501), demonstrating strong qualitative and quantitative results.

This work fits well within the broader context of conditional image generation, particularly methods that use a coarse-to-fine or stacked generation process like StackGAN [35] and image-to-image translation frameworks like pix2pix [10]. While prior work has explored view synthesis for faces [34] or objects [36], this paper is among the first to tackle the generation of full-body humans with explicit and flexible control over articulated pose using keypoints, which is a significant and more complex problem.

### Strengths

1.  **Problem Formulation and Significance:** The paper frames a novel and challenging problem of person generation guided by an explicit pose. This is a significant task with clear applications in data augmentation for tasks like pose estimation and person re-identification, as well as in creative domains like film and photo editing. The ability to directly manipulate a person's pose while preserving their identity and appearance is a valuable contribution to the field of controllable generative models.

2.  **Methodology:** The proposed two-stage, coarse-to-fine approach is well-motivated and effective. Decomposing the difficult end-to-end generation problem into two sub-problems—global structure synthesis (Stage I) and detail refinement (Stage II)—is a logical and powerful strategy. The introduction of the pose mask L1 loss is a simple but clever way to handle the ambiguity of background generation and focus the model's capacity on the primary task of person synthesis.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of their model.
    *   **Multiple Datasets:** Testing on both the high-quality DeepFashion and the more challenging, low-resolution Market-1501 datasets demonstrates the method's robustness.
    *   **Ablation Studies:** The paper includes valuable ablation studies that justify key design choices. The comparison between different pose embedding methods clearly shows the superiority of concatenating pose heatmaps. The comparison between the standard L1 loss and the proposed pose mask loss effectively validates its benefit.
    *   **Qualitative and Quantitative Analysis:** The paper presents compelling visual results alongside quantitative metrics (SSIM, IS) and a user study. The authors are commendably honest about the limitations of automated metrics like SSIM, which often favor blurry but structurally correct images, and use a human evaluation to provide a more meaningful assessment of perceptual quality.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Architectural Novelty:** While the overall framework is effective, the core architectural components (U-Net with skip connections, DCGAN discriminator) are largely standard building blocks from prior work. The main novelty lies in the problem definition and the specific two-stage configuration, rather than in fundamental network design.

2.  **Background Handling:** The method explicitly sidesteps the challenge of background generation by using the mask loss and essentially copying/hallucinating background details from the source image. While this is a pragmatic choice, it is also a limitation. In many generated examples (e.g., Market-1501 results in Fig. 4), the background appears distorted or inconsistent with the new pose, which detracts from the overall realism. A more complete solution would need to address background inpainting or synthesis.

3.  **Dependence on Pose Estimation:** The entire pipeline relies on an external, pre-trained pose estimator to provide the conditioning information. The performance is therefore implicitly upper-bounded by the quality of this estimator. The paper does not analyze the model's robustness or sensitivity to errors or noise in the input pose keypoints, which would be a realistic failure mode in practice.

### Constructive Feedback and Questions

1.  **Visualizing the Difference Map:** In Section 3.2, it is stated that G2 generates a "difference map". It would be highly insightful to visualize this map. This could help readers understand what specific high-frequency details (e.g., texture, wrinkles, facial features) the refinement network is learning to add or correct, further elucidating the benefit of the two-stage approach.

2.  **Comparison to a Stronger Baseline:** The one-stage model (G1+D) serves as a good internal baseline. However, comparing it to a state-of-the-art general-purpose image-to-image translation method like pix2pix [10] could provide a stronger external baseline and better contextualize the performance gains from the specialized two-stage design. The input to pix2pix could be the same as PG2's: the source image concatenated with the target pose heatmaps.

3.  **Background Artifacts:** As noted in the user study, the model can sometimes introduce artifacts by transferring background elements from the source image that are inconsistent with the target pose. Have the authors considered incorporating an explicit background modeling or inpainting module to create a more coherent scene? For instance, one could try to predict a foreground mask for the source image and use that to inpaint the background before transferring the person.

### Recommendation: Accept

This paper presents a well-executed and valuable contribution to the field of conditional image generation. It introduces a novel and significant task—pose-guided person generation—and proposes a solid, effective, and well-motivated two-stage solution. The experiments are thorough, including strong ablation studies and a user study that validates the method's ability to produce photorealistic results. While the architectural components are not entirely novel and the background handling is simplified, the overall framework is sound, the results are convincing, and the work opens up a promising direction for future research in controllable human synthesis. The paper is well-written, clear, and represents a complete piece of research that meets the high standards of NIPS.
