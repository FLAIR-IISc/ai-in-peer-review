This paper proposes a two-stage generative model for transferring a person's pose in an image, conditioned on both a reference image and a target pose. The method addresses a novel task and utilizes a U-Net for initial pose integration and a conditional DCGAN to refine the result, focusing on appearance details. A novel pose mask loss is introduced to emphasize the human body during training. The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the problem, proposed solution, and experimental results.

**Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of pose-guided person image generation, which hasn't been explicitly addressed in prior work. The authors correctly situate their work within the broader context of generative modeling (VAEs, GANs, autoregressive models) and conditional image generation. They highlight existing works on attribute-conditioned generation, text-to-image synthesis, and single-view to multi-view synthesis, carefully distinguishing their approach, which utilizes explicit pose information via keypoints. The related work section adequately references relevant publications, including those on GANs for face and scene generation, and explicitly states where their approach differs (e.g., from Zhao et al. [36] in terms of expressiveness of pose conditioning).

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The task of generating a person in a new pose given a reference image and target pose is a novel contribution. This is a challenging problem that has potential applications in areas such as movie making and data augmentation for pose estimation.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The two-stage approach is well-motivated, dividing the problem into global structure and appearance refinement. The use of U-Net and DCGAN are appropriate choices, and the proposed pose mask loss seems effective in focusing the model on the relevant parts of the image.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand. The architecture diagrams (Figure 2) are helpful, and the explanations of the different components are clear. The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the contribution of each component.
*   **Experiments:** The experiments are performed on two datasets (DeepFashion and Market-1501), demonstrating the generalizability of the approach. The quantitative and qualitative results are well-presented. User studies provide subjective evaluation.
*   **Addressing Limitations:** The authors acknowledge and discuss the limitations of their approach, like failure cases and potential artifacts.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparison:** While the authors compare to Zhao et al. [36], a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art image manipulation and generative models would strengthen the paper. Specifically, methods that perform image editing based on semantic segmentation or keypoints could serve as useful baselines.
*   **Dependency on Pose Estimation:** The method relies on an external pose estimator. Errors in pose estimation could negatively impact the quality of the generated images. This dependency isn’t deeply analyzed. Robustness to pose estimation errors would be beneficial to address.
*   **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** The paper lacks theoretical analysis of the proposed method. While the empirical results are convincing, a theoretical justification for the effectiveness of the pose mask loss or the two-stage approach would be valuable.
*   **Qualitative Results - Limited Scope**: Some qualitative results are only shown cropped. Showing full generated images provides a better assessment of how well the backgrounds are handled and the overall realism.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a novel and challenging problem with a well-motivated approach.
*   The proposed method achieves promising results on two datasets.
*   The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow.
*   The ablation studies and user studies provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of a comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
*   The reliance on an external pose estimator without a thorough analysis of its impact.
*   The absence of theoretical justification for the proposed method.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that presents a novel and technically sound approach to pose-guided person image generation. While there are some weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of generative modeling and has the potential to impact a range of applications.

**Recommendation:**

Accept. However, I would encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by adding a more comprehensive comparison to relevant baselines and discussing the robustness of their method to pose estimation errors.
