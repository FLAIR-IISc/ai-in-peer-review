- Paper addresses pose-guided person image generation via a two-stage conditional approach (coarse U-Net then adversarial residual refinement).
- Pose encoded as 18 keypoint heatmaps; masked L1 loss used to focus on person foreground.
- Evaluated on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) with ablations and a user study.
- Builds on cGANs, U-Nets, stacked GANs, and structure-conditioned generation; discriminator conditioned on image pairs similar to pix2pix.
- Two-stage design (global supervised stage + adversarial residual stage) stabilizes training and speeds convergence.
- Heatmap concatenation is a simple, effective pose embedding that outperforms coordinate/encoder alternatives in ablations.
- Masked L1 pragmaticly mitigates background mismatch and shows benefits, especially on cluttered data.
- Comprehensive experimental setup with datasets of differing difficulty, ablations, pairwise discriminator study, and AMT evaluation.
- Missing stronger baselines: pix2pix-style cGAN, warping/appearance-flow approaches, and perceptual-loss (L1+VGG) baselines.
- Evaluation metrics are suboptimal: SSIM and IS (even masked) are inadequate; recommend FID/KID, LPIPS, and identity re-ID metrics with statistics/confidence intervals.
- Method lacks explicit identity-preservation constraint, leading to failure cases (gender/face changes); suggest VGG/re-ID perceptual loss.
- Background artifacts persist and Stage II sometimes copies reference background; suggest explicit foreground segmentation or PatchGAN/local discriminators.
- Robustness/generalization not analyzed: sensitivity to pose estimation errors, larger pose/appearance gaps, and lack of modeled diversity (deterministic outputs).
- Pose mask construction, lambda values, training time, and architectural specifics are under-specified hindering reproducibility.
- Some architectural choices (e.g., batch-norm removal, FC in Stage I) lack quantitative justification or ablations.
- Novelty is moderate: combination of known components applied to pose-guided person transfer with careful engineering.
- Task is important and method is a useful baseline, but stronger quantitative evidence would increase impact.
- Pros for acceptance: clear problem, sensible two-stage solution, convincing qualitative results, and practical contributions (heatmaps, residuals, masked loss).
- Cons for acceptance: limited baselines, suboptimal metrics, background artifacts, no explicit identity constraints, and reliance on paired data without robustness analysis.
- Questions for authors: report FID/LPIPS/re-ID metrics, sensitivity to pose errors, lambda choices, PatchGAN/multi-scale discriminator comparisons, and code/mask release.
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — useful, well-structured contribution but needs stronger quantitative evaluation and baselines.
