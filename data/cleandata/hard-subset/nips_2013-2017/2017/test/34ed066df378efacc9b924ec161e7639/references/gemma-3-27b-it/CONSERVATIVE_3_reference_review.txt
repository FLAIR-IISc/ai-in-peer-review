This paper proposes a two-stage GAN-based approach for transferring a person’s pose in an image, conditioned on a reference image and a target pose. The method divides the problem into generating a coarse structure (Stage-I) and refining the details (Stage-II). While the problem is framed as a useful application, and the proposed divide-and-conquer strategy is intuitively reasonable, the paper lacks the rigor and thorough evaluation expected at NIPS.

The core idea of conditioning image generation on pose is not entirely novel; several works (cited in the paper) already explore conditioning generation on various forms of input, including keypoints and images. However, the authors position their work as explicitly integrating pose information in a “flexible” way using heatmaps. The advantages of this approach over existing methods aren’t convincingly demonstrated. The ablation studies regarding different pose embeddings (heatmap vs. coordinate embedding) are helpful, but the clear superiority of the heatmap embedding isn’t adequately explained beyond a vague statement about the difficulty of mapping coordinates. 

The proposed mask loss, intended to focus on the human body and reduce the influence of background changes, is a sensible addition. However, its impact isn't sufficiently quantified. While the qualitative results suggest improvement, the quantitative results (SSIM and Inception Score) aren't significantly different with and without the mask loss, leaving the reader skeptical about its true contribution. The explanation for this discrepancy – that these metrics don’t always correlate with perceptual quality – is acknowledged, but doesn't excuse the lack of a more robust assessment.

The two-stage approach itself feels somewhat ad-hoc. The justification for splitting the problem hinges on the difficulty of generating both pose *and* appearance simultaneously. However, end-to-end trainable GANs have achieved remarkable results in complex image generation tasks. The paper doesn’t present a compelling argument why a direct end-to-end approach wouldn’t be feasible, nor does it explore potential difficulties encountered in such an attempt. The comparison to a one-stage model is provided, but the difference in quality seems minor and the quantitative results don’t clearly favor the two-stage approach.

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation is limited. The datasets used (Market-1501 and DeepFashion) are reasonable, but the experiments are largely confined to demonstrating the effect of the proposed components. A more comprehensive evaluation should include comparisons to strong baseline methods for image-to-image translation and pose transfer, even if they don't explicitly condition on pose in the same manner. The comparison to Zhao et al. [36] is weak, as the authors acknowledge that work uses different conditioning information. The presented qualitative results are not compelling enough to justify the complexity of the proposed system. More examples, including failure cases, should have been included to get a better sense of where the system struggles.

The writing is generally clear, but some sections lack detail. For example, the network architectures are described at a high level, and details regarding hyperparameter tuning are missing. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The problem of pose-guided person image generation is potentially valuable.
* The two-stage approach is a reasonable starting point for addressing the problem.
* The mask loss is a sensible idea.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The paper lacks rigorous evaluation and comparison to relevant baselines.
* The benefits of the proposed heatmap embedding and mask loss are not convincingly demonstrated.
* The justification for the two-stage approach is not compelling.
* The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
* The work does not present a significant advance over existing techniques.



Overall, the paper presents an interesting idea, but falls short of the quality and rigor expected at NIPS. The lack of thorough evaluation, weak baseline comparisons, and somewhat ad-hoc nature of the proposed method raise significant concerns. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should consider significantly expanding the experimental evaluation, including comparisons to state-of-the-art image-to-image translation methods, and providing a more rigorous justification for their design choices.
