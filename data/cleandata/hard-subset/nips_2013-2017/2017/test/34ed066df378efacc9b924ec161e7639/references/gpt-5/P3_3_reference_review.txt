Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses pose-guided person image synthesis: given a reference image IA of a person and a target pose PB (as 2D keypoints), generate an image IB that preserves identity/appearance while matching PB. The method (PG2) adopts a two-stage, coarse-to-fine pipeline. Stage-I (G1) is a U-Net-like encoder-decoder (with residual blocks and a fully connected bottleneck) that takes the concatenation of IA and pose heatmaps to produce a coarse image ÎB1 using an L1 loss weighted by a pose-derived foreground mask (pose mask loss). Stage-II (G2) is a conditional GAN that refines the coarse result by “predicting a difference map” and is trained adversarially with a pairwise discriminator D that distinguishes (IA, ÎB2) from (IA, IB), with an additional masked L1 term. They investigate several pose embeddings, validate the pose mask loss, and compare a one-stage adversarial baseline vs. the proposed two-stage approach. Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) include SSIM/IS and masked variants, ablations, a small AMT user study, and qualitative comparisons to VariGAN [36].

The work builds on conditional image generation (cVAE, GANs, pixelCNN), image-to-image translation (pix2pix), and coarse-to-fine GANs (e.g., StackGAN). Using pose heatmaps as structural conditioning is related to prior keypoint/segmentation-conditioned image synthesis, but here applied explicitly to person pose transfer with identity preservation.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation with practical applications (data augmentation for pose estimation, content creation).
- Sensible two-stage design that separates structure (pose) from detail (appearance), improving stability and quality.
- Empirical investigation of pose embeddings; the spatial pose-heatmap concatenation is a reasonable and effective design.
- Pose mask loss is well motivated for re-id datasets where backgrounds differ; the masked evaluation metrics are consistent with this.
- Pairwise conditioning of the discriminator on the reference image reduces trivial solutions and encourages identity preservation.
- Comprehensive experiments across two datasets, with ablations (embeddings, loss, one vs. two stage) and a user study; qualitative improvement over a relevant baseline [36].

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Technical soundness and clarity
- Inconsistency around “difference map.” The text claims G2 predicts a difference map to be added to ÎB1, but the equations for LDadv and LG2 use G2(IA, ÎB1) directly as the refined image without an explicit residual connection (ÎB2 = ÎB1 + Δ). Please clarify whether G2 outputs the residual or the final image, and adjust loss definitions accordingly. If it is residual, the discriminator should see IA and ÎB1 + G2(IA, ÎB1).
- Discriminator conditioning does not include pose PB. Without PB, D cannot enforce pose-consistency beyond what the L1 term encourages. Conditioning D on both IA and PB (e.g., concatenating heatmaps to its input) would more directly penalize pose mismatch and could reduce reliance on L1.
- The fairness of the pose embedding comparison is debatable. The HME baseline injects pose only at the bottleneck; this handicaps it relative to early fusion of spatial heatmaps. A stronger control would inject HME features at multiple decoder scales (or early concatenation after a shared shallow conv) to disentangle representation choice from injection location.
- The Stage-I architecture includes a fully connected bottleneck. For 256×256, this is unconventional/expensive and may harm translation equivariance. The choice should be motivated and ablated (vs. fully convolutional with larger receptive fields via downsampling/dilation).
- “No batch normalization or dropout” is unusual for GAN training. Please justify and report any stability measures; consider alternatives such as instance normalization or feature matching to reduce mode collapse/artifacts.
- Pose mask construction from keypoints via morphology is heuristic and may fail under occlusions/missing joints; how sensitive are results to mask quality? Some quantitative robustness analysis would strengthen the claim.

Experimental evaluation
- Baselines: The one-stage “G1+D” baseline is essentially pix2pix; ensure D is conditioned on both IA and PB, as in pix2pix, for a fair comparison. Including a warping baseline (e.g., appearance flow from pose/keypoints, or STN-based warping) would contextualize gains over geometric alignment methods.
- Metrics: IS and SSIM (even masked) are weak for conditional human synthesis. FID (known in 2017), LPIPS (later but aligns with human judgment), or re-identification similarity (distance in a re-id embedding between generated and target) would better capture realism and identity preservation. Given the application, a re-id retrieval evaluation using generated images as queries would be compelling.
- User study: 1-second exposure is reasonable, but report variance and statistical significance; 14.9% fool rate is modest. It would help to stratify by pose difficulty and background complexity.
- Market-1501 backgrounds: While the masked loss and metrics de-emphasize background, some results show background leakage/artifacts from IA. Consider training with explicit human parsing/segmentation to decouple background synthesis (e.g., compose generated person with target background or a neutral background).
- Hyperparameters: The paper references λ analysis in the supplement; a brief summary in the main text and guidance for practitioners (typical λ range, qualitative trade-offs) would improve reproducibility.

Originality and significance
- The overall approach is a thoughtful combination of known components (pose heatmaps, U-Net with skip connections, coarse-to-fine GAN refinement, masked L1). The novelty lies in the specific task setting (pose-guided person image generation) and a set of practical design choices (foreground-weighted L1, pairwise D with reference image, purported difference-map refinement). This is incremental but meaningful, and the problem is timely and of interest to the community.
- The comparison to [36] is qualitative and not strictly comparable (view labels vs. keypoint poses). Nonetheless, the method appears stronger on full-body synthesis. Stronger quantitative or user-study comparisons would bolster the claim.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and ablate the “difference map” vs. direct refinement; explicitly enforce residual learning if claimed.
- Condition D on PB; consider PatchGAN/multi-scale discriminators and perceptual/feature-matching losses to improve local texture fidelity.
- Replace or complement IS/SSIM with FID and identity preservation metrics (e.g., cosine similarity in a re-id or face embedding).
- Fairer pose embedding ablations that control the injection point; analyze sensitivity to pose estimation noise and missing joints.
- Remove the fully connected layer or justify its benefit with ablations; explore fully convolutional, larger receptive fields (dilated convs).
- Consider explicit human parsing to disentangle person/background, reducing background artifacts and improving Market-1501 performance.

Pros
- Clear, practical problem; method produces convincing results on two datasets.
- Well-structured two-stage pipeline with ablations and reasonable engineering insights (pose heatmaps, masked loss).
- Pairwise discriminator with reference image helps preserve identity.

Cons
- Some methodological ambiguities (difference map) and missing discriminator conditioning on pose.
- Baseline fairness and metric choices limit the strength of quantitative claims.
- Limited analysis of robustness to pose estimation errors and of identity preservation quality.

Recommendation
Lean accept. While the novelty is moderate, the paper addresses an important and challenging task with a solid, empirically validated approach. With clarifications and stronger baselines/metrics, this could be a valuable contribution to NeurIPS.
