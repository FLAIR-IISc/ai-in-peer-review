This paper introduces a novel task: generating an image of a specific person in a target pose, conditioned on a source image of that person and a representation of the target pose. To tackle this challenging problem, the authors propose a two-stage generative network, termed PG2. The first stage employs a U-Net-like architecture (G1) to generate a coarse image that matches the target pose while retaining the person's overall appearance. A key contribution here is a masked L1 loss, designed to focus learning on the foreground person rather than background changes. The second stage uses another U-Net-like generator (G2) within a conditional GAN framework to refine the coarse output from Stage I, adding finer details and improving realism. The work is evaluated on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets, showing qualitative and quantitative results, including ablations and a user study. The approach relates to the broader field of conditional image synthesis, particularly image-to-image translation methods like pix2pix, but defines a new and specific conditioning mechanism based on human pose keypoints.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty of the Task:** The problem of transferring a person's appearance to an arbitrary new pose is both interesting and practically relevant for applications in media, data augmentation, and e-commerce. It is a more explicit and controllable form of generation than conditioning on abstract attributes or text.
2.  **Logical Two-Stage Design:** The proposed "divide-and-conquer" strategy is well-motivated. Decomposing the problem into first establishing the global structure (pose) and then refining local details (texture, sharpness) is an intuitive and effective approach for complex generation tasks.
3.  **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The paper provides a good set of experiments analyzing different components of the proposed model. The comparisons between different pose embedding methods, the standard L1 loss versus the proposed pose mask loss, and the one-stage versus two-stage models provide valuable insight into the design choices.
4.  **Honest Evaluation:** The authors commendably include a discussion of failure cases and acknowledge the known limitations of quantitative metrics like SSIM, where blurry images can score deceptively high.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the novelty of the idea, the paper suffers from a significant number of issues related to clarity, methodological rigor, and presentation, which collectively undermine the contribution.

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Reproducibility:** Many crucial details of the method are either vaguely described or omitted entirely.
    *   **Pose Mask Generation:** The mask `MB` is central to the proposed loss function, yet its generation is opaquely described as "connecting human body parts and applying a set of morphological operations". This is insufficient for reproduction. Which parts are connected? What are the specific operations and their parameters (e.g., kernel shapes, sizes, iterations)?
    *   **Network Architectures:** Section 3.3 is too high-level. Stating "N depends on the size of input" or "the number of filters are increased linearly" is not adequate. The exact number of layers, filter counts per layer, and kernel sizes for each dataset must be specified. The activation function for the final output layer (e.g., Tanh) is also missing.
    *   **Stage-II Generator (G2):** The description of G2 is contradictory. The text claims it generates a "difference map," but the loss function in Eq. 4, `‖(G2(IA, ÎB1)− IB)...‖1`, implies G2 directly outputs the final refined image `ÎB2`. These are two fundamentally different approaches, and this ambiguity makes the core mechanism of Stage-II unclear.
    *   **Mask-IS Metric:** The paper introduces a "mask-IS" metric but fails to explain how it is implemented. The Inception Score is computed on class probabilities from an Inception network. How is a binary mask applied to an input image in a way that is meaningful for this computation?

2.  **Methodological Inconsistencies and Unsubstantiated Claims:**
    *   **Fully Connected Layer:** The FC layer in G1 is justified as enabling information exchange between distant body parts. However, it is removed in G2 to "preserve more details." This is a design contradiction. If the FC layer is crucial for global reasoning in G1, why is it detrimental in G2, which also operates on the full image? This requires a more rigorous explanation or ablation.
    *   **Training Speed:** The claim that learning a "difference map" makes the task "easier" and that "training converges faster" is presented without any empirical evidence, such as convergence plots comparing this approach to direct generation.
    *   **Hyperparameter Justification:** Key design choices, such as the 4-pixel radius for pose heatmaps or the `(1+MB)` weighting in the mask loss, are presented as given, with no justification, sensitivity analysis, or ablation.

3.  **Weak Experimental Support:**
    *   The user study results on the more challenging Market-1501 dataset are problematic. The one-stage model (G1+D) is perceived as more realistic than the full two-stage model (G1+G2+D). The post-hoc explanation that the two-stage model creates more background artifacts is an admission that a key part of the proposed method fails on this dataset, which weakens the paper's overall claim of superiority.

4.  **Sloppy Presentation and Formatting:** The manuscript is replete with formatting errors that suggest a lack of care.
    *   The footer "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)..." appears to be a template remnant.
    *   Figure 1 is mentioned in the introduction but does not appear in the provided text.
    *   The reference list is highly inconsistent and incomplete. For instance, reference [2] ends with "fields. arXiv,, , ,", and [8], [10], and [13] are also incomplete or improperly formatted. This is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   Figure 4's caption notes that "images are cut for the sake of display," which hinders proper evaluation of the results.

**Recommendation**

While the paper addresses a compelling and novel problem with a conceptually sound two-stage approach, the execution and presentation are critically flawed. The lack of clarity and reproducibility in the methodology, combined with methodological inconsistencies and weak empirical support on the more difficult dataset, are major concerns. Furthermore, the numerous formatting and referencing errors detract significantly from the paper's quality and professionalism.

The accumulation of these issues, from minor formatting details to major questions about the core method, prevents me from recommending acceptance in its current form. The work shows promise, but it requires a substantial revision to address the clarity, rigor, and presentation issues before it meets the high standards of NIPS.

**Decision: Reject.**
