Summary
The paper addresses pose-guided person image generation: given a reference person image and a target pose (as keypoints), synthesize the person in the new pose while preserving identity and appearance. The method (PG2) is a two-stage pipeline: Stage-I is a U-Net-like encoder-decoder that takes the reference image concatenated with pose heatmaps and produces a coarse image. A pose mask–weighted L1 loss emphasizes the person over background. Stage-II is a conditional GAN that refines Stage-I’s output by predicting a residual (difference map) conditioned on the Stage-I image and the reference image; the discriminator sees pairs (generated or real) with the reference to enforce consistency. Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) include ablations (pose embedding variants, mask loss vs L1, one-stage vs two-stage), SSIM/IS variants that ignore background, and a small AMT study. The method outperforms simple variants qualitatively and compares favorably to VariGAN [36].

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and practical conditioning signal (keypoint heatmaps) obtainable from off-the-shelf pose estimators.
- Sensible, modular design: a coarse stage for global structure and an adversarial refinement stage for high-frequency details; residual learning in Stage-II is well-motivated.
- Ablation studies on pose embedding and mask loss, and a comparison of one- vs two-stage training provide some insight.
- Results on two datasets; high-resolution DeepFashion samples look convincing; inclusion of failure cases is appreciated.
- Pairwise discriminator input (generated/real with the same reference) is a reasonable design to discourage trivial copying of the reference.

Weaknesses
- Baselines are weak. The most relevant baseline—pix2pix-style conditional GAN (reference + pose heatmaps → target) with PatchGAN and perceptual loss—was not included. The “G1+D” baseline is an internal variant and may not reflect a tuned, strong one-stage baseline.
- Limited quantitative rigor. SSIM/IS (even masked) are weak proxies for perceptual quality; no FID, no pose-accuracy metric (e.g., PCK/OKS on generated images using a pose estimator), and no identity preservation metric (e.g., a re-ID model verifying identity consistency). Claims about faster convergence with residual learning are not quantified.
- The “pose mask loss” is essentially a spatial weighting of L1; the choice of weight (1+MB), mask construction, and sensitivity to parameters are not analyzed. An ablation on different weights or learned masks would strengthen the claim.
- The refinement stage is not conditioned on the target pose explicitly (only via Stage-I output). This may limit robustness; a discriminator also conditioned on the target pose could be more principled.
- No comparison to simple warping/bilinear transfer based on estimated dense correspondences or TPS keyed by pose (a common, strong baseline for pose transfer).
- User study is small and coarse (1s exposure), with modest fooling rates; no statistical significance reported.
- Writing/clarity issues: several typos, inconsistent naming, and reliance on supplementary for crucial architectural details; rationale for some choices (e.g., removing batchnorm, exact λ values) is thin.

Quality
The approach is technically sound and leverages established components (U-Net, residual prediction, cGAN). However, the empirical evaluation is not as rigorous as expected: missing strong baselines (pix2pix + perceptual losses, deformation-based baselines), missing standard modern metrics (FID), and missing task-relevant metrics (pose accuracy, identity preservation). The masking loss is reasonable but not novel and under-analyzed. The reported ablations are helpful but limited.

Clarity
Overall readable, with a clear high-level pipeline. Important specifics (architectural hyperparameters, λ selection, discriminator details such as PatchGAN vs global, training schedules) are partly deferred to the supplement. Some figures/references are difficult to follow in isolation. The paper would benefit from tighter exposition and fuller reproducibility details.

Originality
The task setup—pose-guided person generation from a reference—is interesting, but the method combines well-known ingredients: U-Net with heatmap concatenation, spatially weighted L1, residual prediction, and adversarial refinement. Two-stage generation is also established (e.g., LapGAN/StackGAN). The pairwise discriminator and difference-map framing are reasonable but not particularly novel. Novelty lies more in the specific combination for this application than in core methodological advances.

Significance
Potentially useful for applications (editing, data augmentation). The qualitative results are promising, especially on DeepFashion. That said, without stronger baselines and more rigorous metrics (including identity and pose fidelity), it is hard to conclude that this substantially advances the state of the art. Demonstrating downstream gains (e.g., improved re-ID via augmentation) would strengthen impact.

Questions for authors
- How does PG2 compare to a strong pix2pix baseline (reference+pose heatmaps input, PatchGAN, perceptual/VGG loss) and to deformation-based warping baselines?
- Can you report FID, pose accuracy on generated images (via an off-the-shelf pose estimator), and identity preservation using a re-ID network?
- What is the effect of conditioning Stage-II’s discriminator (and generator) explicitly on the target pose?
- Sensitivity to λ and to the mask weighting form (1+MB vs αMB) and to heatmap radius?
- Does the method generalize to occlusions and rare poses quantitatively (not just failure visuals)?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, practical problem; coherent, effective architecture with reasonable ablations.
- Strong qualitative results and demonstration on two datasets.
- Method uses established, stable techniques likely to be reproducible and extendable.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing strong baselines and key quantitative evaluations (FID, pose/identity metrics).
- Limited novelty beyond a specific integration of known components.
- Some design choices under-justified; user study and metrics are weak.

Recommendation
Borderline/weak accept. The problem is relevant and the system is competent with convincing visuals, but I would like to see stronger baselines and quantitative evidence (pose and identity fidelity, FID) to justify publication at NIPS. Confidence: medium.
