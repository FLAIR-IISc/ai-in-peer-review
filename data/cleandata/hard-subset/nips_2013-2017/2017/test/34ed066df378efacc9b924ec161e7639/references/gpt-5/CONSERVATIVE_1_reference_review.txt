Summary
This paper tackles pose-guided person image generation: given a reference image of a person and a target pose (as 2D keypoint heatmaps), synthesize the person in the new pose while preserving identity/appearance. The method (PG2) uses a two-stage pipeline: Stage-I is a U-Net-like generator (with a fully connected bottleneck) trained with a masked L1 loss to produce a coarse image matching global structure; Stage-II is a conditional DCGAN that refines the Stage-I output by predicting a residual “difference map,” trained with an adversarial loss plus the same masked L1. The discriminator is conditioned on the reference image to discourage trivial copying. Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) include ablations on pose embeddings (coordinate vs heatmap vs concatenation), loss (L1 vs masked L1), and one-stage vs two-stage. Quantitative evaluation uses SSIM and Inception Score, along with “mask-SSIM/IS” to discount background. A small AMT study is reported.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on conditional generative modeling (CVAE, conditional GAN), and especially image-to-image translation with conditional GANs (e.g., pix2pix), and coarse-to-fine/staged GANs. Encoding pose as keypoint heatmaps is standard in pose-conditional tasks. The two-stage “coarse-to-fine” idea and residual/difference-map learning align with prior residual learning and stacked GAN architectures. The most direct prior baseline would be a strong pix2pix-like model conditioned on concatenated reference image and pose heatmaps with a PatchGAN discriminator and perceptual losses, which is not presented. The comparison to VariGAN [36] is only tangential (view rather than pose) and not compelling as a state-of-the-art baseline.

Strengths
- Clear problem setup and sensible decomposition into structure (Stage-I) and details (Stage-II).
- Pose representation with heatmaps and masked L1 targeting foreground are reasonable design choices; ablations within the authors’ variants are informative.
- The pairwise conditioning of the discriminator on the reference image is a useful detail to avoid copying.
- Predicting a difference map at Stage-II (residual refinement) is a practical training heuristic; removing the FC layer in Stage-II to preserve spatial detail is reasonable.
- Results are shown on two datasets with different resolutions; qualitative examples indicate the method can produce plausible pose changes and sometimes preserve texture.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Baselines are insufficient. There is no comparison to a strong image-to-image baseline (e.g., pix2pix-style U-Net + PatchGAN, multi-scale discriminators, and perceptual/VGG loss) conditioned on pose heatmaps, which is the most natural and established alternative. The “one-stage” baseline (G1+D) is an in-house variant and not necessarily competitive.
- Metrics are weak. SSIM/IS (and masked variants) are not well-aligned with human perception or conditional fidelity; no FID is reported; no evaluation of pose accuracy (e.g., PCK on generated images) or identity preservation (e.g., using a pre-trained re-ID model or face/ID similarity). The AMT study is limited (1s exposure, small sample) and shows mixed results (on Market-1501, the two-stage model is rated worse).
- Reliance on pose estimator and mask generation is under-evaluated. There is no robustness study to pose estimation errors, nor clarity on how masks are constructed and how sensitive the method is to keypoint noise or missing joints.
- Background handling is ad hoc. The masked loss de-emphasizes background, but qualitative results suggest background leakage from the conditioning image; the objective may unintentionally encourage copying background instead of learning to synthesize or ignore it.

Clarity
- Many architectural and training details are deferred to supplementary (e.g., λ for L1, exact mask construction). References are incomplete/inconsistent. The role and size of the fully connected bottleneck in Stage-I for 256×256 inputs is unusual and risks discarding spatial information; the rationale could be better justified.

Originality
- The core components are known: heatmap-based pose conditioning, U-Net with skip connections, residual refinement, masked L1, and adversarial loss. The proposed “pose mask loss” is a straightforward weighted L1; the two-stage coarse-to-fine setup and difference-map refinement are incremental. Novelty is therefore modest.

Significance
- The task is interesting and potentially useful. However, given the incremental nature of the method, the significance depends on rigorous validation against strong baselines and comprehensive metrics. As presented, the empirical evidence is not strong enough to clearly advance the state of the art.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Add strong baselines: a pix2pix-style model conditioned on [reference image + pose heatmaps], with PatchGAN (possibly multi-scale) and perceptual/VGG loss; also try a single-stage residual U-Net with these losses.
- Report FID and user preference tests with sufficient viewing time; evaluate pose accuracy (PCK) on generated images and identity preservation using a re-id/ID recognition model.
- Analyze robustness to pose estimation errors and missing keypoints; report performance when the target pose comes from noisy detectors vs annotated or hand-specified poses.
- Clarify mask generation procedure and its failure modes; consider using a learned or off-the-shelf person segmentation model for masks.
- Justify the fully connected bottleneck in Stage-I or provide ablations removing it (as done for Stage-II).
- Discuss generalization beyond the specific datasets and to more diverse backgrounds.

Pros
- Sensible two-stage design with residual refinement.
- Practical pose encoding and foreground-weighted loss.
- Clear ablations among authors’ variants and decent qualitative results on two datasets.

Cons
- Missing strong, established baselines; limited and misaligned metrics.
- Mixed human study; no identity/pose quantitative validation.
- Incremental novelty; several design choices not well justified or analyzed.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The problem is relevant and the approach is reasonable, but the empirical evaluation falls short of NeurIPS standards without comparisons to strong baselines and more rigorous, aligned metrics. Strengthening the baselines and evaluations could move this to acceptability.

Confidence
High.
