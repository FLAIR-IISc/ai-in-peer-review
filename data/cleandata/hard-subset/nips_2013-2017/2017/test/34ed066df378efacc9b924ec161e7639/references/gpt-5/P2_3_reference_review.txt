Summary and contributions
The paper tackles pose-guided person image generation: given a reference image of a person and a target pose (as 2D keypoints), synthesize an image of the same person in the target pose. The authors propose a two-stage network (PG2). Stage I is a U-Net-like generator G1 that takes the reference image concatenated with 18 pose heatmaps and produces a coarse image matching the target pose; training uses an L1 loss weighted by a foreground pose mask to emphasize the person over background. Stage II is a conditional GAN (G2+D) that refines the Stage I output by predicting a difference map (residual) wrt the coarse image; the discriminator takes image pairs (generated/real, both paired with the reference image) to enforce conditional realism. The paper evaluates on DeepFashion and Market-1501, includes ablations (pose embeddings, masked L1 vs L1, two-stage vs one-stage), user studies, and limited comparison to the closest prior work (VariGAN for multi-view clothing images).

Relation to prior work
The problem sits at the intersection of conditional image generation and controllable/structured generation. The use of pose keypoints parallels cGANs with structured conditioning (e.g., pix2pix, text+keypoints generation). The Stage I U-Net with concatenated pose heatmaps is consistent with recent image-to-image translation works; the pose mask loss is a heuristic to handle background mismatch. The Stage II refinement via adversarial training, particularly predicting a residual/difference map instead of a full image, aligns with residual learning ideas and multi-stage GANs (e.g., stacked/RefineNet-style generators). The pairwise discriminator input (reference image + generated/real) is standard for conditional GANs and helps avoid degenerate copying of the condition image. The paper distinguishes itself from multi-view synthesis works that condition on coarse viewpoint labels rather than fine-grained keypoints, and from face rotation methods by addressing full-body pose transfer.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The overall design is sensible and components are implemented correctly. The ablations are useful and largely support the design choices: (i) pose heatmaps concatenated to the image outperform coordinate embeddings; (ii) masked L1 improves robustness to background differences; (iii) two-stage refinement improves perceptual quality over a one-stage adversarial model.
- Empirical support: The qualitative results are convincing on DeepFashion and reasonable on the more challenging Market-1501. Quantitative metrics (SSIM, IS and their masked variants) are reported, along with a small user study. However, there are notable gaps:
  - Metrics emphasize similarity (SSIM) and IS; both are known to be weak indicators of perceptual quality for this task. The paper acknowledges SSIM’s bias toward blur. There is no FID/KID, and IS on person images is questionable.
  - No quantitative identity preservation metric is reported (e.g., retrieval accuracy or feature distance using a re-identification network), which is central to the task.
  - The “mask-SSIM” and “mask-IS” introduce evaluation bias via heuristically constructed masks; they mitigate background effects but also make comparisons less standardized.
  - Baselines are limited. A strong cGAN baseline (e.g., pix2pix-like with pose heatmaps) trained end-to-end on this task is not directly evaluated beyond “G1+D” using the Stage I architecture. A refinement-only baseline that directly predicts the final image (not a difference map) from (IA, ÎB1) would isolate the benefit of residual prediction.
- Methodological clarity: The two-stage training is straightforward. Some choices merit more justification (e.g., fully connected bottleneck in Stage I vs fully convolutional Stage II; no batch norm; λ selection), but the supplementary presumably contains details. Failure cases are shown and discussed.

Clarity
The paper is well-organized and easy to follow. Figures clearly illustrate the pipeline and qualitative results. Loss definitions and training procedures are explicitly stated. A few notational/formatting issues and typos aside, the exposition suffices for reproduction by an expert, though full hyperparameters and code availability would improve reproducibility.

Originality
The overall problem formulation (pose-guided person image synthesis using keypoints) and the specific two-stage decomposition with masked L1 and residual GAN refinement represent a pragmatic combination of known techniques. The most distinctive elements are:
- Using pose keypoints as dense heatmaps concatenated to the image for effective conditioning.
- Residual/difference-map prediction in Stage II to ease GAN training.
- Pose-mask-weighted L1 to de-emphasize background discrepancies.
These are incremental but coherent contributions to the conditional generation literature.

Significance
The task is practically relevant (content creation, data augmentation, pose manipulation). The method advances the state of the art qualitatively on DeepFashion and outperforms a related method [36]. The ablations provide actionable insights (how to encode pose, benefits of two-stage refinement). However, broader impact is limited by:
- Lack of rigorous identity preservation metrics and stronger baselines.
- Deterministic outputs without modeling pose/appearance uncertainty (no diversity).
- Background handling remains brittle; artifacts can be copied from the condition image.

Strengths
- Clear problem setup with an intuitive and effective two-stage design.
- Strong qualitative results, especially on high-resolution DeepFashion.
- Careful ablation studies isolating key design choices.
- Sensible engineering choices (pose heatmaps, residual refinement, pairwise discriminator).
- Foreground-focused masking addresses a real evaluation/training challenge.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental; many components are adaptations of standard cGAN/U-Net practices.
- Quantitative evaluation is limited; lacks FID/KID and identity preservation metrics.
- Baseline comparisons could be stronger (e.g., pix2pix-like end-to-end with pose heatmaps, refinement without residual).
- Deterministic generation; no modeling of multi-modality or uncertainty.
- Reliance on heuristic pose masks for both loss and evaluation; potential bias.
- Some architectural choices (FC bottleneck in Stage I, no normalization) are under-motivated.

Questions and suggestions
- Report identity preservation using a pretrained re-ID network (e.g., feature distance or retrieval accuracy), and FID/KID on masked and unmasked regions.
- Compare to a direct refinement generator (predicting the final image) to quantify the gain from difference-map prediction.
- Include a stronger end-to-end cGAN baseline that conditions on pose heatmaps with adversarial + L1 losses (akin to pix2pix).
- Analyze sensitivity to pose estimation errors; possibly train end-to-end with pose uncertainty or part affinity maps.
- Consider simple diversity mechanisms (e.g., noise injection or dropout at test) and evaluate multi-modal outputs.

Overall assessment
The paper is well-executed, clearly written, and addresses a relevant problem with a practical, effective two-stage design. While the novelty is moderate and quantitative evaluation could be stronger, the ablations and qualitative gains are compelling. With improved baselines and identity-aware metrics, this would be a solid contribution.

Recommendation: Weak accept.
