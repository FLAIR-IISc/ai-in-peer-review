This paper tackles the interesting and challenging problem of pose-guided person image generation. The core idea – conditioning generation on both a reference image *and* a target pose – is a compelling and potentially powerful approach for controllable image manipulation, with applications in areas like movie-making and data augmentation. This is a genuinely novel framing of the problem, moving beyond simple attribute or viewpoint conditioning. The authors’ decision to adopt a divide-and-conquer strategy, separating global structure from fine-grained details, is reasonable and well-motivated. The proposed masked L1 loss is a sensible addition to address background inconsistencies.

The paper's strengths lie primarily in its novelty. The explicit pose control and the two-stage generation pipeline represent a worthwhile exploration. The qualitative results, especially those demonstrating the impact of the pose mask loss and the refinement stage, are promising and visually convincing. The experimental setup, employing two datasets with varying resolutions, is good.

However, the paper is not without weaknesses. While the two-stage approach is conceptually sound, the implementation feels somewhat pragmatic rather than theoretically driven. The justification for the specific U-Net and DCGAN architectures feels lacking – why these choices, and how do they specifically contribute to the task?  The ablation studies, while present, could be more thorough. For example, a more detailed analysis of the λ parameter in the second stage loss would be beneficial.  The quantitative results, specifically the SSIM and IS scores, don’t fully support the visual improvements observed, raising questions about the appropriateness of these metrics for evaluating this particular task. The user study results, while providing *some* support, are nuanced and require careful interpretation (as acknowledged by the authors). Finally, the failure cases highlight potential limitations regarding complex poses and dataset imbalances, and these could be addressed with more discussion.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The problem framing and the proposed approach are genuinely novel and represent a significant departure from existing work.
* **Potential Impact:**  Successful pose-guided generation has clear practical applications.
* **Sound Conceptual Framework:** The divide-and-conquer strategy is well-reasoned.
* **Promising Qualitative Results:** The visual examples suggest the method is capable of generating plausible results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Limited Theoretical Justification:** Architectural choices lack strong theoretical grounding.
* **Weak Quantitative Evaluation:** Standard metrics don’t fully align with visual improvements.
* **Incomplete Ablation Studies:**  Some parameters lack a thorough analysis.
* **Known Failure Cases:** Limited robustness in certain scenarios is acknowledged, but not fully addressed.



Despite the weaknesses, I lean towards **accepting** this paper. The novelty and potential impact of the work are significant enough to outweigh the methodological shortcomings. While the paper isn't a polished, perfectly validated contribution, it introduces a compelling new direction for research in image generation. The authors have identified a challenging problem and proposed a reasonable initial solution. The paper sparks further research directions – exploring more sophisticated architectures, improving quantitative evaluation metrics, and addressing the identified failure cases – which are hallmarks of a valuable NIPS contribution. I would encourage the authors to address the reviewer comments in a revised version, focusing on strengthening the theoretical justification and providing a more comprehensive evaluation.
