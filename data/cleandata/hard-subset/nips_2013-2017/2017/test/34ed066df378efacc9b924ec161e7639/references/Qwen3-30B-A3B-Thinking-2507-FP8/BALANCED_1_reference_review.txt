

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-stage framework (PG2) for generating person images conditioned on both a reference image and a target pose. The authors address the challenging task of pose transfer by dividing it into two stages: Stage-I generates a coarse image capturing global body structure using pose heatmaps and a novel pose mask loss to focus on human body regions; Stage-II refines the coarse result by generating a difference map using a conditional GAN. The approach is evaluated on DeepFashion (high-resolution fashion images) and Market-1501 (low-resolution person re-identification) datasets. The paper demonstrates that their pose-guided approach outperforms one-stage alternatives and the most related work [36], with both quantitative metrics and user studies.

## Strengths
1. **Problem Definition and Motivation**: The paper clearly articulates a meaningful problem (pose-guided person image generation) with practical applications in movie making, human pose estimation, and data augmentation. The distinction from previous work that conditions on less explicit pose representations is well-motivated.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The two-stage approach is well-justified as a divide-and-conquer strategy for the complex pose transfer task. The ablation studies thoroughly validate key design choices: the pose heatmap embedding outperforms coordinate and heatmap embeddings; the pose mask loss improves results over standard L1 loss; and the two-stage refinement significantly enhances quality over one-stage models.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper provides thorough quantitative results (SSIM, Inception Score, mask variants) and a user study on both datasets. The qualitative results in Figure 4 effectively demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method.

4. **Novel Contributions**: The pose mask loss is a clever and effective solution to the background interference problem, and the two-stage refinement approach (generating difference maps rather than full images) is well-motivated and effective.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Theoretical Justification**: While the two-stage approach is empirically justified, the paper lacks a theoretical argument for why this division is superior to alternative single-stage approaches with better loss functions. The authors note that "it is difficult for a complete end-to-end framework to do this," but don't provide concrete evidence or analysis to support this claim.

2. **Comparison with Related Work**: The comparison with [36] is limited to qualitative examples (Figure 5) without detailed quantitative analysis. The paper doesn't explain why their method outperforms [36] in terms of the underlying technical differences (e.g., how pose representation or conditioning differs).

3. **Pose Mask Implementation**: The paper doesn't clarify why the pose mask is computed from the target pose rather than the condition image. Since the condition image contains the actual human body, using its mask would be more natural and avoid potential misalignment between the target pose and the actual body in the condition image.

4. **Failure Analysis**: The failure cases (Figure 6) are noted but not deeply analyzed. For instance, the gender mismatch could be linked to dataset imbalance, but the paper doesn't explore whether this is a fundamental limitation of the approach or could be addressed through better data curation or model modifications.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. Expand the comparison with [36] with additional quantitative metrics to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method beyond visual examples.

2. Provide a more detailed explanation of the pose mask computation, including how it's aligned with the actual human body in the condition image rather than the target pose.

3. Include a brief analysis of computational efficiency (training time, memory requirements) compared to alternative approaches to better assess practical utility.

4. Deepen the failure analysis by exploring potential solutions for the identified limitations (e.g., how to address gender mismatch or rare pose generation).

5. Consider adding a discussion of how the approach might generalize to other object categories beyond humans, which would strengthen the significance claim.

## Overall Assessment
This is a well-executed, technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of conditional image generation. The pose-guided approach with the two-stage refinement strategy is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrated to be effective through comprehensive experiments. While the paper has some limitations in theoretical justification and comparative analysis, these are relatively minor given the solid empirical results and clear contributions. The work aligns well with NIPS' focus on high-quality, impactful research in machine learning.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of generative models, particularly for person image synthesis with pose control. The two-stage framework and pose mask loss are likely to be influential for future work in this area. With the suggested improvements, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS.

**Recommendation**: Accept (with minor revisions)
