

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

This paper proposes a two-stage approach for person image generation conditioned on a reference image and a target pose, aiming to transfer a person from one pose to another. The authors introduce a novel pose mask loss to focus generation on the human body rather than background, and employ a divide-and-conquer strategy (stage-I for global structure, stage-II for detail refinement). While the problem statement is relevant, the paper suffers from significant methodological and validation shortcomings.

The main contribution appears to be the two-stage framework and pose mask loss. However, the paper's claims about novelty are overstated. The pose mask loss bears strong resemblance to techniques used in image inpainting and foreground-background separation tasks, and the two-stage approach is a common strategy in image generation (e.g., [35, 9, 3, 19] cited by the authors). The paper fails to adequately differentiate its approach from these established methods.

The most concerning issue is the lack of rigorous comparison with established baselines. The authors only compare with one previous method ([36]), which is a weak benchmark for a paper claiming significant advances. The paper does not compare with standard image-to-image translation methods (e.g., Pix2Pix, CycleGAN), standard GAN architectures (e.g., StyleGAN), or recent person image generation approaches. The quantitative results in Table 1 show marginal improvements over baselines (e.g., only 0.02-0.05 improvement in SSIM), which is insufficient to justify the novelty claim.

The evaluation methodology is problematic. The authors acknowledge that SSIM and Inception Score correlate poorly with perceptual quality (as do most image quality metrics), yet they rely heavily on these metrics. The user study is underpowered (30 users) and does not provide statistical significance testing. The paper's claim that their method "manages to confuse users" (G2R score of 14.9% on DeepFashion) is weak evidence of quality, especially when compared to other recent image generation works that achieve much higher scores.

The paper's experimental design has several flaws. The Market-1501 dataset is not designed for image generation (it's a person re-identification dataset with surveillance camera images), making it an inappropriate testbed for a generation task. The DeepFashion dataset has simpler backgrounds than real-world applications, limiting the practical relevance of the results. The failure cases (Figure 6) reveal significant issues with the method's robustness, particularly with complex poses and gender mismatches, but these limitations are not thoroughly analyzed.

The paper's theoretical justification for the two-stage approach is weak. While the authors claim that "it is difficult for a complete end-to-end framework to do this because it has to generate both correct poses and detailed appearance simultaneously," they provide no theoretical analysis or empirical evidence to support this claim. The marginal qualitative improvements shown in Figure 4 (e.g., slightly better clothing texture) do not justify the added complexity of the two-stage framework.

The paper also lacks sufficient ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of each component. For instance, it's unclear how much the pose mask loss contributes versus the two-stage architecture. The paper claims the pose mask loss "effectively alleviates the influence of noisy backgrounds," but provides no quantitative analysis of this effect.

The paper's claims about applications (movie making, pose estimation data generation) are overstated. The method's limitations (e.g., inability to handle complex backgrounds, gender mismatches, rare poses) make it unsuitable for most practical applications without significant additional development.

While the paper is well-written and methodologically sound in its implementation of established techniques, it fails to meet NIPS standards for rigor and novelty. The contributions are incremental rather than transformative, and the validation is insufficient to support the claims. The paper would benefit from extensive comparison with standard image generation baselines, more comprehensive evaluation on diverse datasets, and deeper analysis of limitations.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting application of established techniques to a specific problem, but falls short of the novelty and rigor expected for NIPS. The marginal improvements over existing methods, limited validation, and lack of thorough comparison with standard baselines make it difficult to justify acceptance. The authors should substantially strengthen the evaluation and better contextualize their contributions relative to established image generation methods before considering publication at NIPS.

Strengths: Clear problem statement, well-organized presentation, reasonable use of established techniques, useful failure analysis.

Weaknesses: Insufficient comparison with standard baselines, weak evaluation methodology, overstatement of novelty, limited datasets, marginal quantitative improvements, lack of theoretical justification for key design choices.
