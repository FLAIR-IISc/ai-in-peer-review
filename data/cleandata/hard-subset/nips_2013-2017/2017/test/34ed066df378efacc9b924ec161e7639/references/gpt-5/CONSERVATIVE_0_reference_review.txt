Summary
This paper tackles pose-guided person image generation: given a source image of a person and a target pose (as 2D keypoints), synthesize the same person in the new pose. The method (PG2) is a two-stage pipeline. Stage-I is a U-Net-like generator (with a fully connected bottleneck and residual blocks) that takes the source image concatenated with 18 pose heatmaps and outputs a coarse result optimized with a masked L1 loss that emphasizes foreground pixels. Stage-II is a conditional DCGAN trained to predict a residual “difference map” that refines the Stage-I output; the discriminator receives pairs (source image, generated/real target) and the generator is regularized by the same masked L1 loss. Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) include ablations on pose embeddings (coordinates vs heatmaps vs concatenation), loss (standard L1 vs masked L1), and one-stage vs two-stage training. Quantitative evaluation uses SSIM and Inception Score (and masked variants) plus a brief 1s-per-image AMT study; there is a qualitative comparison to VariGAN [36].

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on conditional image generation (e.g., pix2pix) and stacked/ two-stage GANs (StackGAN, Stacked GANs), and prior view/pose-conditional synthesis (Reed et al., VariGAN). Using pose heatmaps as conditioning is consistent with contemporaneous keypoint-conditional GANs, and the foreground-weighted loss echoes attention/masking ideas. The “difference map” is a residual formulation widely used in super-resolution and refinement networks; conditioning the discriminator on paired inputs is standard in conditional GANs.

Strengths
- Problem focus and motivation are clear: explicit pose control using a reference image is practical and potentially useful.
- The two-stage, coarse-to-fine structure is simple and sensible; the residual refinement is an effective engineering choice and likely stabilizes training.
- Pose heatmap concatenation is an appropriate design; the ablation convincingly shows its advantage over coordinate embeddings.
- Masked L1 loss is a reasonable way to mitigate background mismatch; qualitative gains on Market-1501 reflect that.
- Presentation includes architecture summaries, training details, and multiple ablations; qualitative results are often convincing, especially on DeepFashion.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality (soundness and evidence)
- Limited and weak baselines. Most comparisons are to ablations of the proposed method. A strong baseline such as pix2pix conditioned on pose heatmaps (source + pose-to-target) is missing. The single external comparison (VariGAN) is not tightly matched to this setting.
- Metrics are not well-justified. IS on person crops (and the ad hoc mask-IS) is of questionable validity; SSIM is known to correlate poorly with perceptual quality (acknowledged by authors). FID (or perceptual metrics) would be more informative; identity preservation should be measured with a pretrained re-identification network, and pose correctness could be assessed using a pose estimator on generated images (PCK-like).
- User study is very limited (1s exposures, ~15% fool rate on DeepFashion, lower on Market), suggesting realism is still far from convincing. No comparative human study against strong baselines.
- Claims such as “difference map speeds convergence” are not supported by quantitative training curves or ablations (e.g., time-to-metric threshold).
- Robustness to pose estimation noise, missing keypoints, and rare/occluded poses is not analyzed.

Clarity
- Overall readable and organized. However, some choices are insufficiently motivated:
  - Why include a fully connected bottleneck in Stage-I but remove it in Stage-II? Any ablation showing its necessity/harm?
  - The weighting λ for the masked L1 is relegated to supplementary; main text lacks sensitivity analysis and chosen values.
  - Details for the mask construction from keypoints (parameters of morphological ops) are brief; reproducibility would benefit from more specifics.

Originality
- The components are largely known (U-Net with pose heatmaps, foreground-weighted L1, conditional discriminator, residual refinement). The contribution lies in assembling them for pose-guided person image generation with a two-stage training. Given the state of the art, the novelty is incremental.

Significance
- The task is relevant and within scope. However, without strong baselines and more rigorous evaluation, it is hard to conclude that the method advances the state of the art. The gains over a reasonable pix2pix-like baseline or other pose-conditioned generators are not demonstrated. No downstream utility (e.g., augmenting re-ID or pose estimators) is shown.

Questions for authors
1. How does PG2 compare to a pix2pix baseline that takes [IA, PB] as input and predicts IB directly, trained with adversarial + L1 (with and without masked L1)?
2. What are the chosen λ values on both datasets? Please provide sensitivity curves for λ and training curves showing the claimed faster convergence of the difference-map formulation.
3. Can you report FID (on masked crops and on full images) and an identity preservation metric using a standard re-ID network? Also, report a pose accuracy metric by running a pose estimator on generated images.
4. What is the effect of the fully connected bottleneck in Stage-I? Please include an ablation.
5. How robust is the method to missing/noisy keypoints? Any failure analysis beyond two examples?

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear task and practical conditioning signal (pose heatmaps).
- Simple, coherent two-stage design with well-motivated ablations.
- Qualitative improvements on challenging pose changes; masked loss is an effective detail.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Incremental novelty; many components are standard.
- Lack of strong, established baselines and rigorous metrics (FID, identity/pose preservation).
- Weak user study; limited evidence that images are realistic under scrutiny.
- Limited analysis of robustness and design choices (FC bottleneck, λ).
- Conclusions (e.g., faster convergence) not backed by quantitative evidence.

Overall evaluation
While the problem is interesting and the engineering choices are sensible, the empirical evidence falls short of NeurIPS standards for establishing clear improvements over established methods. In particular, the lack of strong baselines and rigorous, task-relevant metrics makes it difficult to assess the contribution’s significance. I recommend rejection at this time, with the suggestion to strengthen baselines (e.g., pix2pix with pose), add identity and pose accuracy metrics, include FID, and provide robustness and ablations.
