## NIPS 2017 Review: Pose Guided Person Image Generation

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and interesting problem – pose-guided person image generation – and proposes a reasonable two-stage approach to tackle it. The method combines existing techniques (U-Net, DCGAN) in a clever way, leveraging a mask-based loss and a difference map strategy to improve training. The experimental results, while not groundbreaking, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach on two datasets. However, the paper suffers from a lack of detailed ablation studies, a somewhat superficial comparison to related work, and a slightly over-optimistic tone. I recommend **weak accept** with major revisions, contingent on addressing the points detailed below.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The authors address the problem of generating person images conditioned on a reference image and a desired pose. This is a valuable goal with applications in areas like film-making and data augmentation.  They correctly identify that direct end-to-end generation is challenging and propose a divide-and-conquer strategy, separating global structure learning (Stage-I) from detailed appearance refinement (Stage-II). The paper positions itself well within the landscape of generative models (VAEs, GANs, autoregressive models) and acknowledges the recent advancements in conditional image generation. 

The related work section covers the key areas adequately, including conditional GANs and image-to-image translation techniques. However, the discussion feels somewhat cursory.  While the authors acknowledge related work on view synthesis and multi-view generation (e.g., [36]), they don't fully explore the distinctions in the *expressiveness* of pose conditioning versus viewpoint conditioning, which is a central claim of their work.  More depth in the related work would strengthen the paper’s position. 

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novel Problem Formulation:** The task of pose-guided person image generation is a well-defined and potentially impactful problem.
* **Reasonable Approach:** The two-stage approach is a logical way to decompose the problem. Dividing it into global structure and detail refinement is a sound strategy.
* **Masked Loss:**  The proposed mask loss is a clever addition that helps focus the model on the relevant regions of the image (the person) and mitigate the influence of background variations.
* **Difference Map Strategy:** Training the Stage-II GAN to generate a difference map is a sensible idea that likely improves training stability and speed.
* **Qualitative Results:**  The qualitative results presented in Figure 4 demonstrate that the proposed method can generate plausible images with the desired pose.
* **Experimental Validation:** The authors evaluate their method on two datasets, providing some evidence of its generalizability.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Ablation Studies:** The paper presents some ablation results (different pose embeddings, losses, one- vs. two-stage), but these are insufficient.  Specifically, the impact of the lambda parameter in the Stage-II loss is only mentioned as being explored in the supplementary materials. A more thorough investigation of its effect on image quality would be beneficial.  Further ablation on different network architectures within each stage (e.g., different U-Net depths) would also strengthen the analysis.
* **Superficial Comparison to Related Work:** The comparison to [36] is limited. It would be valuable to include quantitative comparisons and a more detailed discussion of the differences in their approaches, datasets, and results.
* **Over-Optimistic Tone:** The authors use strong language at times (e.g., "explicit and flexible", "directly manipulate") that isn’t fully justified by the experimental results. The current results are promising, but there is still room for improvement.
* **Lack of Failure Case Analysis:** While the authors mention a few failure cases at the end, a more systematic analysis of the limitations of the method would be helpful.  Understanding *why* the method fails in certain scenarios would provide valuable insights for future research.
* **Quantitative Evaluation Caveats:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of SSIM and IS, particularly in the context of generative models. Relying heavily on these metrics is problematic. The user study results show a discrepancy with quantitative results, pointing to this issue. 
* **Pose Estimation Dependency:** The method relies on a separate pose estimator.  While reasonable, the performance of the overall system is inherently limited by the accuracy of the pose estimation. The authors should acknowledge this more explicitly and potentially explore ways to mitigate this dependency (e.g., by incorporating pose estimation uncertainty).



**4. Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**Pro Acceptance:**

* Addresses a novel and interesting problem.
* Proposes a technically sound and well-motivated approach.
* Demonstrates promising results on two datasets.
* Includes a clever mask-based loss function.

**Con Acceptance:**

* Limited ablation studies and analysis.
* Superficial comparison to related work.
* Overly optimistic tone.
* Dependence on external pose estimator.
* Quantitative results don’t fully reflect the perceived quality from qualitative results.

**5. Recommendation:**

**Weak Accept** with major revisions. The paper presents a promising approach to a challenging problem. However, the authors need to address the weaknesses outlined above to strengthen the paper's technical contribution and increase its impact. Specifically, they should:

* Conduct more comprehensive ablation studies to understand the influence of different hyperparameters and network configurations.
* Provide a more in-depth comparison to related work, including quantitative results when possible.
* Tone down the language and present a more balanced assessment of the method's strengths and limitations.
*  Expand the failure case analysis.
*  Investigate the potential for incorporating pose estimation uncertainty.



I believe that addressing these points would significantly improve the quality of the paper and make it a valuable contribution to the NIPS conference.
