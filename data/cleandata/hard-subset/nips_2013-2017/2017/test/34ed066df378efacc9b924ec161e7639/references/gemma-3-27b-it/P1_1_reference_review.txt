## Peer Review for "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and technically sound approach to pose-guided person image generation. The core idea of decoupling pose control from appearance details via a two-stage GAN framework is well-motivated and effective, as demonstrated through experiments on two datasets. The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology. However, while the results are promising, the significance of the contribution, in the broader landscape of image manipulation and generation, could be strengthened. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper addresses the task of generating a person image with a specified pose, conditioned on a reference image. Recognizing the difficulty of end-to-end pose and appearance generation, the authors propose a two-stage pipeline. Stage-I uses a U-Net to generate a coarse image reflecting the target pose, and Stage-II employs a conditional DCGAN to refine the image details.  Key contributions include the exploration of pose embedding methods, a novel pose mask loss to focus on body transfer, and the staged generation approach.

The related work section provides a good overview of image generation techniques (VAEs, GANs, autoregressive models) and conditioned image generation (attribute, text, image conditioning). The discussion of related work on pose-conditioned generation ([36] and others) is relevant and accurately contextualizes the novelty of the proposed approach, particularly the explicit pose control through keypoints.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Conditioning image generation on both a reference image *and* a specific pose, represented by keypoints, is a novel combination.  This allows for a more direct and controllable manipulation of human pose compared to methods relying solely on viewpoint or abstract cues.
* **Technical Soundness:** The two-stage approach is well-justified and logically structured. The use of U-Net for initial pose transfer and DCGAN for refinement is a reasonable choice. The proposed pose mask loss is a clever way to alleviate background interference.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on both DeepFashion and Market-1501 demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.  The ablation studies (different pose embeddings, loss functions, one-stage vs. two-stage) are well-conducted and provide insightful analysis.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The diagrams (Figures 2 & 3) are helpful in understanding the architecture and the pose mask concept.
* **Implementation Details:** Providing details on network architecture, optimizer, and training iterations improves reproducibility.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Significance:** While the results are good, the improvement over existing methods isn’t dramatically shown.  The comparison with [36] is limited to a few visual examples, and a more comprehensive quantitative comparison with other state-of-the-art methods (even if they don’t directly address the same task) would strengthen the paper's impact.
* **Failure Cases:** The paper briefly mentions failure cases, but a more thorough analysis of these limitations would be valuable.  For example, the issues with complex poses and gender imbalance deserve further discussion.
* **User Study Interpretation:** While the user study provides some support for the visual quality of the generated images, the interpretation of the results (especially for Market-1501) could be more nuanced. The authors attribute lower scores to artifacts, but other factors (e.g., low quality of source images in Market-1501) may contribute.
* **Limited Generalization:** The task is very specific to *person* image generation. While the authors mention potential applications (movie making, pose estimation data generation), the method’s applicability to other object categories is not explored.



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*  Novel and well-motivated approach to pose-guided image generation.
*  Technically sound methodology with clear justification.
*  Strong experimental validation on two datasets.
*  Clear and well-organized writing.

**Against Acceptance:**

*  Significance of the contribution could be more strongly demonstrated.
*  Limited analysis of failure cases.
*  User study interpretation could be more nuanced.
*  Lack of generalization discussion to other object categories.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* Can you provide a more comprehensive quantitative comparison with existing methods, even if they address slightly different tasks? (e.g., image-to-image translation, viewpoint manipulation).
* Could you provide more details on the failure cases?  Are there specific poses or body types where the method consistently struggles?
*  What modifications would be required to adapt this method to generate images of other object categories (e.g., cars, furniture)?
*  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the proposed method compared to existing techniques?

**6. Confidence:**

8/10. I am reasonably confident in my assessment. The paper is a solid contribution with a clear methodology and thorough experimental evaluation. Addressing the minor weaknesses identified above would significantly strengthen the paper.
