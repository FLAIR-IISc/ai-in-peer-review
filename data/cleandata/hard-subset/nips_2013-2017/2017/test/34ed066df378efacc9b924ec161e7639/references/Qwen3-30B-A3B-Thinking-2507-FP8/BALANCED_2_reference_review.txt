

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel two-stage approach called Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) for generating person images conditioned on a reference image and a target pose. The authors propose a divide-and-conquer strategy where Stage-I generates a coarse image capturing global human body structure using a U-Net-like architecture with a novel pose mask loss, and Stage-II refines this result using a conditional DCGAN variant that generates a difference map. The work is positioned as a significant extension of previous person image synthesis approaches, particularly compared to Zhao et al. [36] which uses a different conditioning approach.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation**: The paper effectively identifies a meaningful application gap in pose-guided person image generation and clearly articulates why explicit pose conditioning is valuable for applications like movie making and human pose estimation.

2. **Novel Technical Contributions**: The pose mask loss is a well-motivated technical contribution that effectively addresses the challenge of background interference during pose transfer. The two-stage framework is well-justified and demonstrates clear advantages over single-stage approaches.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper provides thorough quantitative results (SSIM, Inception Score), qualitative comparisons, and a user study across two datasets (Market-1501 and DeepFashion) with appropriate ablation studies on pose embeddings and loss functions.

4. **Strong Baseline Comparison**: The comparison with Zhao et al. [36] is particularly convincing, with clear visual evidence showing superior results from the proposed approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Incremental Nature**: While the two-stage approach is well-executed, the overall methodology builds heavily on existing GAN and U-Net architectures without introducing fundamentally new theoretical insights. The novelty is primarily in the application and specific loss function design, which may not be sufficient for NIPS acceptance.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient theoretical reasoning for why the two-stage approach is necessary. A more thorough discussion of why a single-stage model with a more complex architecture wouldn't work as well would strengthen the contribution.

3. **Limited Ablation Study**: The ablation studies focus primarily on pose embeddings and loss functions, but don't thoroughly explore the impact of the two-stage architecture itself. More systematic comparison of the two-stage vs. one-stage approach with varying complexity would be valuable.

4. **Computational Cost Analysis**: The paper doesn't address the computational cost of the two-stage approach compared to single-stage alternatives, which is important for practical adoption.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Novel application of pose conditioning for person image generation with clear practical value
- Pose mask loss is a well-motivated technical contribution addressing a real problem
- Strong experimental results with comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets
- Clear comparison with the most relevant prior work (Zhao et al. [36])

**Con:**
- Incremental technical contribution that builds on existing architectures without sufficient theoretical novelty
- Limited theoretical justification for the two-stage approach
- Incomplete ablation study of the core architectural innovation (two-stage vs. one-stage)
- Lack of discussion about computational efficiency and scalability

## Recommendations for Improvement

1. **Strengthen the Theoretical Foundation**: Add a section discussing why the two-stage approach is theoretically advantageous over single-stage alternatives. Consider providing theoretical analysis of the optimization landscape for both approaches.

2. **Expand Ablation Studies**: Include more comprehensive ablation studies specifically focused on the two-stage architecture. For example, compare the performance of a single-stage model with a more complex architecture (e.g., deeper U-Net) to determine if the two-stage approach is fundamentally better or just a practical implementation choice.

3. **Address Computational Cost**: Include analysis of training time and memory requirements for the two-stage approach compared to single-stage alternatives. This would help demonstrate practical viability.

4. **Clarify Contribution Statement**: The paper's contribution statement should more precisely differentiate this work from existing GAN-based image translation approaches. The current statement doesn't sufficiently emphasize how this work differs from general image-to-image translation methods.

5. **Improve Failure Analysis**: The failure cases (Figure 6) are interesting but could be expanded to include more systematic analysis of failure modes and potential solutions.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the task of pose-guided person image generation. While the technical novelty is somewhat incremental, the quality of the experiments, clarity of presentation, and practical significance of the application make it a strong candidate for NIPS. However, the lack of sufficient theoretical justification for the two-stage approach and the incremental nature of the contribution make it borderline for acceptance at NIPS.

With the suggested improvements—particularly strengthening the theoretical foundation and expanding the ablation studies to focus on the core architectural innovation—this paper would be a strong fit for NIPS. As it stands, it demonstrates good quality and significance but falls slightly short of the threshold for a top-tier acceptance due to the incremental nature of the technical contribution. I would recommend rejection with the understanding that with substantial revisions addressing the theoretical justification and ablation studies, this work could be a strong candidate for future NIPS submissions.
