This paper tackles the challenging problem of pose-guided person image generation, a task with clear potential applications in areas like movie making and data augmentation for pose estimation. The core idea – conditioning generation on both a reference image *and* a target pose – is a strong one, offering more explicit control than existing approaches. The two-stage approach, dividing the problem into global structure and detailed appearance, feels intuitively sensible and addresses a key difficulty of end-to-end generation. While the presentation isn't flawless and some experimental details feel cursory, the novelty of the task formulation and the reasonable approach taken are compelling.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** This is the paper’s biggest strength. Conditioning image generation on *both* a reference image and a specific pose is a novel contribution. While related work exists on conditional generation, explicitly controlling pose in this manner is a valuable step forward.
*   **Intuitive Approach:** Dividing the generation into two stages – coarse structure followed by detail refinement – is a sensible strategy for tackling this complex problem. It mirrors how one might approach the task manually.
*   **Pose Mask Loss:** The proposed mask loss is a clever idea for focusing the generation on the person’s appearance rather than irrelevant background changes, which is particularly important given the potential for varying backgrounds.
*   **Qualitative Results:** The visual examples presented are convincing, demonstrating the ability to transfer pose while preserving identity. The comparisons with the one-stage model highlight the benefit of the two-stage approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Quantitative Analysis:** The quantitative results, while present, are not particularly strong. SSIM, even with the mask, is known to have limitations, and the Inception Score doesn’t always align with perceptual quality (as noted by the authors themselves). More robust and targeted evaluation metrics are needed.
*   **Lack of Ablation Study Depth:** While ablation studies are *mentioned* (different pose embeddings, loss functions), the analysis feels somewhat superficial. The impact of each component isn’t fully dissected.
*   **Dataset Choice & Generalization:** Experiments on two datasets are good, but the datasets differ significantly in resolution. The results on Market-1501, a more challenging dataset, are less compelling and suggest potential limitations in generalization.
*   **Comparison to Related Work:** The comparison to Zhao et al. [36] is a good start, but the field of conditional image generation is broad. A more thorough comparison to other relevant techniques would strengthen the paper.
*   **Implementation Details:** The description of network architecture is somewhat sparse. More details on layer sizes, activation functions, and hyperparameter settings would aid reproducibility.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a genuinely novel and interesting problem.
*   The proposed approach is intuitive and reasonably well-motivated.
*   The qualitative results are promising and demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.
*   The pose mask loss is a thoughtful addition that improves performance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The quantitative evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous.
*   The ablation studies lack depth and thoroughness.
*   The connection to a broader landscape of conditional generation work is somewhat weak.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its flaws, I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. The novelty of the proposed task and the sensible approach taken outweigh the weaknesses. The paper introduces a new direction for research in pose-guided image manipulation. I am willing to overlook the somewhat limited quantitative analysis and the lack of extensive ablation studies, given the innovative nature of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

Accept. While further refinement and more rigorous evaluation would be beneficial, the core ideas presented are strong enough to warrant acceptance to NIPS. I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses mentioned above in future work.
