

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-stage framework (PG2) for person image generation conditioned on a reference image and a target pose. The authors claim novelty in explicitly conditioning on pose information (via keypoints) for person image synthesis. Stage-I integrates pose information using a U-Net variant with a novel pose mask loss to focus on human body regions. Stage-II refines the coarse output using a conditional GAN that generates a difference map. The authors evaluate on Market-1501 and DeepFashion datasets, showing qualitative and quantitative improvements over one-stage alternatives and a prior method [36].

## Strengths
The paper addresses an interesting problem in person image synthesis with practical applications. The two-stage approach is logically motivated by the difficulty of simultaneously generating pose and appearance. The pose mask loss is a reasonable attempt to handle background inconsistencies. The qualitative results in Figure 4 show reasonable pose transfer with some detail preservation.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Technical Inconsistencies
The paper contains multiple formatting errors that undermine its credibility. The header states "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)" - the "st" is incorrect and should be "2017" or "the 2017". The reference list contains poorly formatted entries (e.g., "Realtime multi-person 2d pose estimation using part affinity, Zhe Cao, Tomas Simon, Shih-En Wei, Yaser Sheikh, fields. arXiv,, , , ") with missing information. The paper inconsistently uses "L1" and "L1" notation for the loss function. Figure 4 caption contains inconsistent ID formatting ("ID. 49" vs "ID. 53").

### Technical Misunderstandings
The paper states "it would generate blurry result due to the use of L1" - this is fundamentally incorrect. L1 loss does not inherently cause blurriness; it's the combination of L1 loss with the generator architecture that can cause blurriness. The paper fails to clarify this basic concept. The pose mask loss equation (Eq. 1) is written as "‖(G1(IA, PB)− IB) (1 +MB)‖1" which is confusingly formatted (the parentheses are misplaced and it's unclear if this is the L1 norm or element-wise multiplication).

### Methodological Issues
The claim of novelty is questionable. The paper states "we make use of pose information in a more explicit and flexible way" but uses keypoints in the same way as prior work [36]. The pose embedding (using 18 heatmaps) is not novel - similar approaches appear in [22, 23, 36]. The two-stage framework is also not novel; multi-stage approaches are common in generative modeling (e.g., StyleGAN2).

The paper claims "our method is not able to imagine what the new background looks like" but doesn't explain how this limitation affects their evaluation. The use of mask-SSIM and mask-IS in 4.3 is a reasonable attempt to handle background differences, but the paper fails to justify why these metrics are better than standard metrics for their specific task.

### Experimental Weaknesses
The evaluation is insufficient for making strong claims. The comparison with [36] (Figure 5) shows only two examples, which is inadequate to support the claim of "much better" results. The user study in Section 4.4 is poorly described - it doesn't explain how the 55 real images were selected or how the generated images were chosen for the study.

The paper claims "our full model generates sharper and more photo-realistic results" but the quantitative results (Table 1) show that the one-stage model (G1+D) achieves comparable or better SSIM scores. The paper acknowledges this discrepancy ("blurry images often get good SSIM despite being less convincing") but fails to address why their method is better despite this metric.

### Originality Concerns
The paper's three contributions are not sufficiently novel. The first contribution ("conditioning on reference image and intended pose") is not novel - similar approaches exist in [36] and [22, 23]. The second contribution ("pose mask loss") is a minor variation of standard techniques. The third contribution ("two-stage approach") is methodologically common in generative models.

## Recommendation
While the paper addresses an interesting problem and shows reasonable qualitative results, it suffers from multiple critical flaws that undermine its scientific contribution. The technical misunderstandings, formatting inconsistencies, questionable novelty claims, and insufficient experimental evaluation collectively prevent this paper from meeting NIPS's standards for publication.

The paper would benefit from substantial revision to clarify technical concepts, properly evaluate the novelty of its contributions, and provide more rigorous experimental validation. As written, it contains too many fundamental errors and inconsistencies to be accepted at NIPS.

**Recommendation: Reject**
