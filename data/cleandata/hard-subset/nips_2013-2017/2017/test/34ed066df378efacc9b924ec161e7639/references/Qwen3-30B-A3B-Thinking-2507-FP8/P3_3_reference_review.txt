

# Peer Review: Pose Guided Person Image Generation

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-stage framework for generating person images conditioned on a reference image and a target pose, addressing a novel task in image synthesis. The authors decompose the challenging pose transfer problem into two stages: Stage-I generates a coarse image capturing global structure using a U-Net variant with pose heatmaps and a novel pose mask loss, while Stage-II refines the result using a conditional GAN that generates a difference map. The approach is evaluated on Market-1501 and DeepFashion datasets, showing improvements over single-stage baselines and the most similar work [36].

The paper builds on existing work in conditional image generation (e.g., [17, 22, 24]) but introduces a more explicit pose condition using keypoint heatmaps rather than abstract pose representations. Unlike [36] which uses textual descriptions of views, this work directly conditions on pose keypoint information.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Task Definition**: The paper successfully identifies and formalizes a meaningful task—pose-guided person image generation—which has clear applications in fashion, entertainment, and pose estimation data augmentation.

2. **Well-Motivated Two-Stage Approach**: The decomposition of the problem into global structure generation followed by detail refinement is theoretically sound and well-justified by the difficulty of end-to-end pose transfer. The qualitative results clearly demonstrate the advantage of this decomposition.

3. **Effective Pose Representation**: The pose heatmap embedding is a clever solution that avoids expensive pose annotation while providing an effective input for the network. The ablation study (Figure 4) convincingly shows this representation outperforms coordinate-based alternatives.

4. **Pose Mask Loss**: The mask loss is well-designed to focus generation on the human body rather than background, significantly improving results as shown in Figure 4 and Table 1. This is a valuable contribution to the field of conditional image generation.

5. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper provides thorough quantitative results (SSIM, Inception Score), a user study, and detailed qualitative comparisons. The evaluation on two distinct datasets (Market-1501 for challenging re-identification, DeepFashion for high-resolution fashion) demonstrates robustness.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Justification Lacking**: While the two-stage approach is empirically effective, the paper lacks a theoretical explanation for why decomposition improves performance. The authors note the approach "divides the problem into two stages which focus on learning global human body structure and appearance details respectively," but don't explain *why* this decomposition is necessary or optimal for the pose transfer task.

2. **Pose Mask Computation Ambiguity**: The paper states the pose mask "is computed by connecting human body parts and applying a set of morphological operations" but provides no details on this process. A clearer explanation or diagram would be valuable, as this mask is critical to the approach's success.

3. **Limited Failure Analysis**: While the paper shows some failure cases (Figure 6), it doesn't thoroughly analyze *why* these failures occur. For instance, the "pieces of legs" failure is attributed to "rare training data for such complicated poses," but the paper doesn't quantify how common these poses are in the training data.

4. **Identity Preservation Mechanism Unclear**: The paper states the goal is "transferring the appearance of a person from a given pose to a desired pose," but doesn't adequately explain how identity is preserved across pose changes. How does the model ensure the generated person matches the reference person's identity rather than generating a generic person?

5. **Comparison with StyleGAN/Other Conditioning Methods**: The paper compares with [36] but doesn't discuss how this approach relates to or improves upon more recent conditional GANs like StyleGAN that can control attributes. The paper would benefit from a comparison with state-of-the-art conditional image synthesis methods.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of conditional image generation, particularly for person images. The two-stage approach is well-executed, and the pose mask loss is a valuable technique that could be applied to other conditional generation tasks. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate improvements over the baseline and the most similar work.

However, the paper's main limitation is its lack of deeper analysis into *why* the approach works as well as it does. The empirical results are strong, but without a theoretical justification for the two-stage decomposition, it's difficult to assess whether this is the optimal approach or merely a practical solution. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more detailed analysis of the decomposition's benefits.

The pose mask loss is particularly valuable, but the paper doesn't explore how this technique might generalize to other conditional image generation tasks. This could be a significant contribution beyond person image generation.

## Recommendation

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of conditional image generation with a novel task definition, effective methodology, and thorough evaluation. While the theoretical justification could be strengthened, the empirical results are convincing and the work is clearly relevant to NIPS.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments**:
- Novel task definition with clear applications
- Well-designed two-stage approach with strong empirical results
- Pose mask loss is a valuable technical contribution
- Comprehensive evaluation on multiple datasets
- Clear, well-written paper with good figures

**Con Acceptance Arguments**:
- Theoretical justification for two-stage approach is lacking
- Pose mask computation details are insufficient
- Identity preservation mechanism is not clearly explained
- Comparison with more recent conditional GANs (e.g., StyleGAN) would strengthen the contribution

The authors should address the weaknesses above, particularly by providing more detailed explanation of the pose mask computation and clarifying the identity preservation mechanism. A brief theoretical discussion of why the two-stage approach works better than single-stage would also be valuable. With these clarifications, this paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS.
