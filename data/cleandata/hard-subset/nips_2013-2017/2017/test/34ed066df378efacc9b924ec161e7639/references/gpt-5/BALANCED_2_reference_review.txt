Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Pose Guided Person Image Generation (PG2), a conditional image synthesis framework that, given a reference person image IA and a target pose PB (as keypoints), generates an image of the same person in the desired pose. The method proceeds in two stages: Stage-I (G1) fuses IA with a pose embedding to produce a coarse but pose-consistent image using a U-Net–like encoder-decoder (with residual blocks and a bottleneck fully connected layer). A pose mask L1 loss reweights the loss towards foreground pixels to mitigate background mismatch. Stage-II (G2) refines the result via a conditional DCGAN by predicting a residual/difference map between the coarse output and the ground truth; the discriminator takes pairs (IA, I) to encourage consistency with the conditioning image, and a masked L1 term is retained to stabilize training. Experiments on DeepFashion and Market-1501 include ablations on pose embeddings (coordinates vs heatmaps), loss (L1 vs masked), and one- vs two-stage training, along with SSIM/IS (and masked variants) and a user study. The method compares qualitatively to VariGAN [36].

The work builds on conditional generation (cVAE [32], cGANs [18, 21]), image-to-image translation with adversarial losses [9], and coarse-to-fine adversarial refinement (e.g., StackGAN [33]). Compared to view-conditioned generation of faces or clothing [34, 36], the pose conditioning here is more explicit via keypoint heatmaps, enabling finer control.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and an intuitive, effective two-stage “structure-then-detail” design. The residual/difference-map refinement is a sensible choice that simplifies learning and speeds convergence.
- Careful engineering choices: pose embedding via per-joint heatmaps integrated by convolutions; pairwise discriminator (IA, I) to prevent trivial copying; foreground-weighted (masked) L1 to handle background mismatch.
- Solid ablations: comparing pose embeddings, losses, and training regimes; consistent qualitative gains from heatmap embedding and masked loss; two-stage outperforms one-stage on perceptual quality.
- Practical value: applicable to data augmentation and content creation. Uses off-the-shelf pose estimation to avoid manual annotations.
- Reproducibility: many architectural and training details are provided; experiments on two datasets.

Weaknesses and assessment by criteria
Quality (technical soundness)
- The novelty of several components is incremental: residual prediction and foreground-weighted L1 are well-known ideas adapted to this task; the pairwise discriminator is a reasonable but standard conditional-GAN design. The paper claims faster convergence from residual learning but provides no quantitative training curves.
- Evaluation metrics are limited. SSIM/IS (even masked variants) are known to correlate weakly with perceptual quality, and perceptual metrics such as LPIPS or FID are missing. Identity preservation is central to the task, yet no identity-similarity metric (e.g., re-id/face embedding similarity) is reported.
- Baselines are thin. The “one-stage adversarial” baseline (G1+D) is helpful, but a stronger pix2pix-style baseline directly conditioned on IA and pose heatmaps (and potentially with perceptual/feature-matching losses) would be informative. The comparison to VariGAN [36] is not apples-to-apples (their conditioning and objective differ).
- The Stage-I fully connected bottleneck may harm spatial fidelity; the motivation for including it (and removing it in Stage-II) could be better justified or ablated.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. Figures are helpful. Some reference entries are incomplete. A more explicit description of the pose mask construction and its robustness to pose-estimation noise would help. Minor typographical issues exist.

Originality
- The overall task—explicitly pose-guided person image generation from a single reference—is timely and interesting (less explored than view- or attribute-conditioned generation at the time). However, the core building blocks (heatmap embedding, coarse-to-fine GAN, residual prediction, masked L1) are adaptations of existing techniques rather than fundamentally novel algorithms.

Significance
- The approach is practically useful and extends controllability of conditional image synthesis. Results on two datasets and the user study show promise. That said, the absence of identity-preservation metrics and stronger baselines makes it hard to quantify the advance over competent alternatives. Broader impact beyond persons (other articulated objects) is not investigated.

Constructive suggestions
- Add stronger baselines: e.g., a pix2pix-like U-Net conditioned on [IA; PB] with (i) standard adversarial + L1, (ii) feature-matching and/or perceptual (VGG) losses [11], and (iii) triplet input to D: (IA, PB, I).
- Report perceptual metrics (FID, LPIPS) and identity preservation (similarity via a re-id or face embedding network). A retrieval-based evaluation (does generated image retrieve the correct identity?) would be compelling on Market-1501.
- Provide convergence plots to substantiate claims about residual learning accelerating training; ablate the pairwise discriminator vs an unconditional discriminator, and ablate the Stage-I fully connected layer.
- Explore structure-aware appearance transfer (warp/flow/TPS) to copy fine textures from IA to the target pose before adversarial refinement, which may reduce artifacts and improve high-frequency details.
- Improve robustness analysis: quantify sensitivity to pose-estimation errors; test on occluded or rare poses; expand failure case analysis.
- Consider giving D access to pose PB (triplets (IA, PB, I)) to enforce pose consistency more directly.
- Clarify and complete citations; proofread.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear and relevant problem with explicit controllability via pose.
- Simple, well-motivated two-stage design with consistent qualitative improvements.
- Thorough ablations on key design choices; practical masked loss addresses a real nuisance (background mismatch).
- Demonstrated on two datasets with user study support.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental technical novelty; components largely adapted from prior work.
- Limited quantitative evaluation (no identity metric, FID/LPIPS), and relatively weak baseline comparisons.
- Claims about training efficiency and certain design choices (FC bottleneck) are not rigorously validated.
- Some artifacts and background leakage remain; limited analysis of failure modes.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper tackles a useful and underexplored conditional generation setting with a clean and effective two-stage approach and provides meaningful ablations. Strengthening evaluation (especially identity preservation and perceptual metrics) and adding stronger baselines would solidify the contribution.
