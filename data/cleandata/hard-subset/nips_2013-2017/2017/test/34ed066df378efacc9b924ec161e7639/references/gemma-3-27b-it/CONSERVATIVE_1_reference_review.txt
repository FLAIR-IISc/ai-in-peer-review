This paper proposes a two-stage GAN-based approach for pose-guided person image generation, aiming to transfer a person’s appearance from one pose to another given a reference image. The core idea of dividing the problem into global structure and appearance refinement is reasonable, and the proposed pose mask loss is a sensible addition to address background inconsistencies. However, while the idea is intuitively appealing, the presentation lacks sufficient rigor and the empirical validation is not compelling enough to justify acceptance to NIPS.

The primary weakness lies in the novelty and justification of the proposed method. While the task itself – pose-guided image generation – is presented as novel, the core techniques employed (U-Net, DCGAN, adversarial loss, L1 loss) are all well-established. The contribution isn’t a fundamentally new technique, but rather an application of existing methods to a specific problem. The authors don’t convincingly demonstrate why their approach is superior to simply training a single, more complex GAN directly on the task, or adapting existing image-to-image translation methods (e.g., Pix2Pix) with pose conditioning. The rationale for the two-stage approach feels somewhat ad-hoc without a deeper analysis of its benefits.

Furthermore, the experimental setup is insufficient. The comparison to Zhao et al. [36] is valuable, but only a single related work is compared against, and the chosen dataset (DeepFashion) appears to be where that work also concentrated. A more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art image manipulation techniques, especially those employing GANs, is essential. The quantitative results (SSIM, IS) are reported, but their interpretation is hampered by the acknowledgment that they don’t fully capture perceptual quality, and the user study results are mixed. The chosen metrics feel insufficient to fully demonstrate the impact of the proposed technique. The ablation study, while present, is limited to the pose embedding and loss function – a more thorough ablation investigating the necessity of the two-stage approach would be valuable. 

The writing quality is adequate but could be improved. The introduction lacks a precise statement of the problem and contributions. The related work section is descriptive but doesn’t critically analyze the existing literature or clearly position the authors’ work within the broader field. The discussion of the network architecture is detailed but somewhat verbose.

**Strengths:**

*   The two-stage approach provides a structured way to tackle a complex problem.
*   The pose mask loss is a reasonable heuristic for focusing the generation on the human body.
*   Qualitative results demonstrate the ability to transfer pose, at least in some cases.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty – the method relies heavily on established techniques.
*   Insufficient experimental validation – lacks comprehensive comparison with relevant baselines.
*   Weak justification for the two-stage approach.
*   Quantitative results are difficult to interpret.
*   Writing quality could be improved for clarity and conciseness.
*   The failure cases highlight limitations that are not adequately addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The task is potentially valuable for applications like movie making and data augmentation.
*   The pose mask loss demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of the problem's challenges.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of substantial novelty and the limited experimental validation outweigh the potential benefits.
*   The method does not significantly advance the state of the art in image generation.
*   The two-stage approach is not convincingly justified.



**Overall Assessment:**

Reject. This paper presents a reasonable but ultimately incremental contribution. The lack of substantial novelty and the insufficient experimental validation prevent it from meeting the standards for acceptance to NIPS. The authors should consider significantly expanding the experimental evaluation, providing a more compelling justification for their approach, and more clearly positioning their work within the broader field of image generation.
