- Paper proposes PG2: a two-stage pose-guided person image generation with stage-I U-Net conditioned on keypoint heatmaps and stage-II residual DCGAN refinement.
- Introduces pose heatmap concatenation as the preferred pose embedding over coordinate-based alternatives and heatmap encoders.
- Uses a foreground-weighted (“pose mask”) L1 loss to downweight background mismatch between conditioning and target images.
- Stage-II learns a residual (difference) map with a pairwise conditional discriminator observing (condition image, output) vs (condition image, target).
- Ablations support key claims: heatmap concatenation and pose-mask loss improve results; two-stage residual refinement yields sharper images.
- Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) show strong qualitative improvements and AMT study results.
- Quantitative evaluation relies on SSIM/IS (and masked variants), which are limited and can penalize sharper images.
- No quantitative evaluation of identity preservation (re-ID or face/ID classifier) or pose correctness (pose estimator on generated images).
- Robustness to pose-estimation noise and large/missing keypoints is not analyzed, though keypoints are obtained automatically.
- Comparison to prior work is limited (brief [36] comparison); stronger baselines (e.g., pix2pix-style with identical conditioning) are not systematically reported.
- Reproducibility is fairly good with many implementation details provided, but some specifics (λ selection, mask morphology, exact mask construction) are deferred or vague.
- Clarity is generally good; motivation, architecture choices, and figures effectively support the arguments.
- Some implementation choices lack justification (e.g., fully connected layer in stage-I and its impact at higher resolutions).
- Originality is moderate: components are known but their specific combination and ablation-driven insights are a meaningful contribution to pose-guided synthesis.
- Significance is solid for controllable synthesis and data augmentation, but would be stronger with quantitative identity/pose metrics and downstream task demonstrations.
- Pros for acceptance: clear task formulation, well-motivated two-stage system, thorough ablations, and strong qualitative results.
- Cons for acceptance: weak quantitative metrics, limited baseline comparisons, incomplete justification of some design choices, and missing robustness analysis.
- Suggested improvements: add identity and pose accuracy metrics, report FID and pairwise perceptual studies, include stronger baselines, analyze robustness to noisy/missing keypoints and large pose changes, and consider perceptual/feature-matching losses.
- Overall assessment: well-executed and clearly presented with convincing ablations and qualitative gains; leans toward acceptance contingent on stronger evaluation and clarification of implementation details.
