

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

This paper introduces a novel task of generating person images conditioned on both a reference image and a target pose, proposing a two-stage approach called Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2). The authors frame this as a meaningful extension beyond existing work that conditions image generation on attributes, text, or other image conditions. The key insight is that explicit pose information (via keypoints) provides more flexible and direct control over the generation process than previous approaches that used abstract conditions. The method divides the challenging pose transfer task into two stages: stage-I integrates pose information with the reference image to capture global structure using a novel pose mask loss that focuses on the human body rather than background; stage-II refines the result using a conditional GAN that generates a difference map rather than the full image, enabling faster convergence and sharper results.

The paper relates well to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. It acknowledges the foundational work on GANs [6, 21] and image-to-image translation [11, 22], while clearly differentiating their approach from related work on pose-based generation [36]. The authors correctly note that [36] conditions on viewpoint labels rather than explicit pose information, making their approach more expressive for diverse pose transfer.

Strengths: The paper is technically sound with a well-motivated approach to a challenging problem. The pose mask loss is a significant contribution that effectively addresses the background interference issue in pose transfer. The two-stage framework is well-justified and demonstrates clear advantages over one-stage approaches in both qualitative and quantitative results. The experimental evaluation is thorough, including comparisons across different pose embedding methods, loss functions, and model architectures. The user study on Amazon Mechanical Turk provides additional validation beyond standard metrics. The implementation details are sufficiently clear for reproducibility, and the ablation studies effectively isolate the contributions of different components.

Weaknesses: While the specific application is novel, the overall architecture (U-Net with skip connections + conditional GAN) builds upon existing image-to-image translation techniques rather than introducing fundamentally new architectural innovations. The comparison with [36] is limited to DeepFashion, while the authors also evaluate on Market-1501, a more challenging dataset. The paper could better contextualize how this work relates to the rapidly growing literature on human pose transfer from 2017-2018. The failure cases in Figure 6 highlight limitations (e.g., rare poses, gender errors) that could be more thoroughly addressed.

Novelty assessment: The core contribution is the specific formulation of conditioning image generation on reference image + target pose, which has not been previously explored in the literature. The pose mask loss is a novel technical contribution that addresses a specific challenge in this domain. While the two-stage framework is inspired by prior work, its application to pose transfer with the specific architectural adaptations (difference map generation, pose mask loss) represents a meaningful contribution to the field.

Significance: The task has clear applications in movie making, human pose estimation, and image retrieval. The results demonstrate the ability to generate photo-realistic person images with correct poses, which could be valuable for generating training data for pose estimation systems. The method shows clear improvements over the most relevant prior work [36] and establishes a new benchmark for pose-guided person image generation.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient detail to reproduce the experiments. The figures effectively illustrate the approach and results, and the supplementary materials provide additional implementation details. The quality of the experiments is strong, with appropriate metrics (SSIM, Inception Score) and a user study that complements the quantitative results.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper addresses a meaningful problem with a well-motivated solution, provides clear evidence of improvement over prior approaches, and makes a specific technical contribution with the pose mask loss. The two-stage framework is soundly justified and demonstrates significant practical improvements.

Arguments against acceptance: The overall approach is somewhat incremental, building on established techniques rather than introducing fundamentally new methodology. The novelty is primarily in the specific application and the pose mask loss, rather than in the core architecture.

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution that advances the state of the art in pose-guided image generation. While the architectural components are not entirely novel, their specific application to this problem with the pose mask loss represents a meaningful contribution. The paper is technically sound, well-evaluated, and addresses an important application area. The work is clearly differentiated from prior NIPS publications and makes a valuable contribution to the field.
