Summary
The paper tackles pose-guided person image generation: given a reference image of a person and a target pose (as keypoints), synthesize the person in the new pose while preserving appearance. The method (PG2) is a two-stage pipeline. Stage-I uses a U-Net-like encoder–decoder with skip connections to fuse the input image with pose heatmaps and trains with a masked L1 loss to emphasize foreground. Stage-II uses a conditional DCGAN variant to refine the coarse Stage-I output by predicting a residual (difference) map, mixing adversarial loss with the same masked L1. The authors claim that the two-stage design improves global pose correctness (Stage-I) and sharpness/details (Stage-II). They evaluate on DeepFashion and Market-1501 with qualitative examples, SSIM/IS and masked variants thereof, and a brief AMT study. Ablations include different pose embeddings, loss variants, and one-stage vs two-stage training.

Strengths
- Clear task formulation and practical motivation; conditioning on explicit pose is more controllable than view labels.
- Sensible use of established components: pose heatmaps, U-Net with skip connections, adversarial refinement; predicting a residual is a practical design for stabilization and faster convergence.
- The masked L1 is a simple heuristic to emphasize the person, addressing background mismatch across camera views.
- Good qualitative improvements from Stage-II; ablations on pose embedding (coordinates vs heatmaps vs concatenated heatmaps) are informative.
- Experiments on two datasets of different difficulty; some user study evidence.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Baselines are limited and mostly self-comparisons. There is no strong comparison to established image-to-image models conditioned on pose, e.g., a pix2pix-style baseline that takes IA concatenated with pose heatmaps (and, optionally, segmentation masks) trained end-to-end. The one-stage “G1+D” is not clearly a best-effort baseline (e.g., architecture, training schedule, and loss tuning might handicap it).
- No quantitative measure of identity preservation, which is central to the task. Using a standard person re-ID network to compute feature similarity/retrieval accuracy between generated images and ground truth (or same identity across views) would be far more meaningful than SSIM/IS. As is, there is no evidence that identity is preserved beyond qualitative anecdotes.
- The proposed mask-SSIM and mask-IS are ad hoc and may favor the method; their reliability is not validated (e.g., correlation with human judgments). FID (Fréchet Inception Distance) or retrieval-based measures would be stronger.
- Claims about “faster convergence” of residual prediction are not substantiated with training curves or wall-clock comparisons.
- Sensitivity to pose estimation noise is not analyzed. Since predicted keypoints are used, robustness to errors (e.g., quantitative degradation vs GT/noisy keypoints) is important.
- Stage-I uses a fully connected bottleneck at 256×256, which is atypical and heavy; rationale and parameterization are not justified. Later Stage-II removes it, implicitly acknowledging drawbacks.
- The masked L1 uses (1+MB), effectively weighting foreground twice and still penalizing background, which seems misaligned with the stated goal of suppressing background influence. A stricter masking or a learned foreground weighting (e.g., with segmentation) would be more principled.
- Limited failure analysis and no discussion of mode collapse or artifacts beyond a couple of examples; no evaluation of diversity (the model is deterministic, no sampling).

Clarity
- Overall readable with useful figures, but important implementation details are deferred to the supplement (e.g., λ selection, channels per block). For reproducibility, the main paper should include λ, exact architectures (filters per stage), and training schedules.
- Reference formatting is inconsistent/incomplete; several entries are missing venues/years and have arXiv placeholders without identifiers.

Originality
- The components are largely standard: heatmap pose conditioning, U-Net with skip connections, residual refinement with a conditional GAN, and foreground-weighted pixel loss. The two-stage design resembles existing staged GAN pipelines (e.g., StackGAN, Laplacian pyramid GANs) applied to this task. The novelty is primarily in the specific problem instantiation and some engineering choices (pose mask loss, residual refinement). This is a reasonable but modest level of originality.

Significance
- The problem is interesting and potentially useful (content creation, data augmentation for pose/re-ID). However, without strong baselines and identity-preservation metrics, it is hard to conclude that the approach advances the state of the art beyond plausible alternatives. The user study shows moderate realism but is small and does not address identity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add strong baselines:
  - pix2pix-style conditional GAN taking IA and pose heatmaps as input, with identical data and tuning.
  - A simple pose-guided warping baseline (e.g., TPS/part-based affine warps from keypoints) followed by refinement.
- Provide identity-preservation metrics using a pretrained re-ID network: feature distance, top-k retrieval, or verification accuracy between generated and ground-truth target images of the same identity.
- Report FID (and masked FID if desired) and training curves to support convergence claims.
- Analyze robustness to pose estimation noise and occlusions; if possible, compare with ground-truth keypoints or controlled perturbations.
- Clarify and justify the architecture (especially the FC bottleneck in Stage-I), the λ values used, and sensitivity to λ.
- Consider a stricter or learned foreground mask (segmentation) and report the impact of mask quality.
- Release code and trained models.

Pros
- Clear task; sensible architecture; convincing qualitative improvements from the two-stage design.
- Practical masked loss; residual refinement is effective.
- Evaluation on two datasets; ablations on pose embedding and loss.

Cons
- Missing strong baselines; limited and ad hoc quantitative evaluation.
- No identity preservation metric; limited robustness analysis.
- Modest novelty; several claims insufficiently supported.
- Incomplete implementation details; reference list issues.

Recommendation
Score: 5 (weak reject)
Confidence: 0.7

Rationale: The paper addresses a worthwhile problem with a reasonable and mostly standard two-stage approach, and the qualitative results are promising. However, the empirical evidence is not yet rigorous enough: key baselines are missing, identity preservation is not quantified, and some claims (e.g., faster convergence) are unsupported. Strengthening the evaluation along the lines above would make the contribution much more compelling.
