

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

This paper proposes a two-stage framework for generating person images conditioned on a reference image and a target pose. The authors claim to address a novel task of manipulating a person in an image to an arbitrary pose. While the task is interesting, the paper contains several methodological inconsistencies, technical oversights, and formatting issues that significantly undermine its quality.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper introduces a two-stage approach: Stage-I integrates pose information (represented as heatmaps) with a reference image to generate a coarse result, and Stage-II refines this result using a conditional GAN that generates a difference map. The authors compare this to related work, primarily Zhao et al. [36], which they claim is less expressive. However, they fail to adequately differentiate their approach from prior pose-based image generation methods.

## Major Technical Issues

1. **Inconsistent Notation and Formulation**: Equation (1) uses inconsistent notation for the pose mask loss. The paper writes "‖(G1(IA, PB)− IB) (1 +MB)‖1" but should use element-wise multiplication (⊙) instead of implicit multiplication. This is a serious technical oversight that would confuse readers and reviewers.

2. **Vague Methodological Details**: The pose mask MB is described as "computed by connecting human body parts and applying a set of morphological operations" but provides no specific details about which morphological operations or how the mask is generated. This is critical for reproducibility.

3. **Inadequate Justification for Key Design Choices**: The paper claims "G1 does capture the global structural information specified by the target pose" but provides no quantitative evidence beyond visual inspection. The choice of 18 keypoints (standard for human pose estimation) is not justified, and the paper doesn't explore how this choice affects performance.

4. **Contradictory User Study Results**: The paper states that "G1+G2+D gets a lower score than G1+D" on Market-1501 because "G1+G2+D transfers more backgrounds from the condition image," which directly contradicts the paper's core premise that background should be ignored during pose transfer. This undermines the main claim of the paper.

5. **Inconsistent Loss Function Notation**: The paper uses both "L1" and "L1 loss" interchangeably without standardization, creating confusion. The use of "Lbce" for binary cross-entropy loss without definition is also problematic.

## Minor but Significant Issues

1. **Implementation Details**: The paper uses Adam with β1 = 0.5, which is highly unusual (typically β1 = 0.9). No justification is provided for this choice, which could significantly impact convergence.

2. **Pose Estimation**: The paper claims to use "a state-of-the-art pose estimator" but doesn't specify which one, making reproducibility impossible.

3. **Output Layer Activation**: The paper states "We apply rectified linear unit (ReLU) to each layer except the fully connected layer and the output convolution layer" but doesn't explain why the output layer doesn't use a sigmoid or tanh activation, which is standard for image generation.

4. **Formatting Inconsistencies**: The paper references "Figure 1" but doesn't include it in the provided text. The conference name formatting is inconsistent ("st Conference" should be "2nd Conference").

5. **Quantitative Evidence**: The paper claims the pose mask loss "effectively alleviates the influence of noisy backgrounds" but the quantitative results in Table 1 show minimal improvement for Market-1501 (the dataset with more complex backgrounds), weakening this claim.

## Strengths

- The two-stage approach is reasonable for this task.
- The pose mask loss is a simple but potentially useful modification to focus on human body regions.
- The paper provides a clear comparison with related work, particularly Zhao et al. [36].

## Weaknesses

- The paper's main claim of "novel task" is overstated, as pose transfer has been explored in related works.
- The justification for the two-stage approach is primarily empirical rather than theoretical.
- The paper fails to adequately address the limitations of their approach, particularly the background transfer issue.
- The user study results contradict the paper's main claim about background handling.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound but has significant methodological inconsistencies that weaken its technical contribution. The experimental results don't fully support the claims, particularly regarding the pose mask loss's effectiveness.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written but suffers from inconsistent notation and missing methodological details that would prevent full reproducibility.

**Originality**: The two-stage approach is somewhat novel but not groundbreaking. The pose mask loss is a minor variation of existing techniques. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate from related work.

**Significance**: The improvements over prior work are incremental. The applications mentioned (movie making, human pose estimation) are plausible but not compelling.

## Recommendation

While the paper addresses an interesting problem, the numerous methodological inconsistencies, technical oversights, and formatting issues significantly undermine its quality. The contradictions in the user study results and the lack of clear justification for key design choices make it difficult to evaluate the paper's true contribution. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these issues, particularly the inconsistent notation, vague methodological details, and contradictory results.

I recommend rejection due to these substantial flaws, despite the paper's potentially interesting application. The paper's claims are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided, and the technical inconsistencies would be confusing to readers and reviewers alike.
