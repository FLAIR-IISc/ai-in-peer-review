

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

This paper introduces a novel task of generating person images by conditioning on both a reference image and an intended pose, rather than solely on a reference image or pose alone. The authors propose a two-stage approach to address the challenging problem of pose transfer: Stage-I integrates the reference image with target pose to generate a coarse result capturing global structure (using a U-Net variant with a novel pose mask loss), while Stage-II refines the result using a conditional GAN that generates a difference map rather than the full image. This approach is both conceptually fresh and practically effective.

The core novelty of this work lies in the explicit conditioning on both a reference image and an intended pose - a significant departure from previous work that either conditions on labels/text or uses reference images without pose guidance. The authors convincingly argue that their pose-guided approach provides more direct and flexible control over the generation process. The two-stage strategy is particularly bold: rather than attempting to solve the entire problem end-to-end (which would require generating both correct poses and detailed appearance simultaneously), they separate the problem into two more manageable components, with each stage focusing on a specific aspect of the task.

The pose mask loss is a particularly clever innovation that effectively addresses the challenge of background interference during pose transfer. By weighting the loss to focus on the human body rather than background, the model can better concentrate on transferring the person's appearance rather than generating plausible but incorrect backgrounds. This is a simple yet effective solution to a non-trivial problem.

The use of difference maps in Stage-II is another bold and novel contribution. Instead of training a GAN to generate the full image from scratch, they train it to generate the difference between the coarse result and the target image. This not only speeds up convergence but also makes the task more tractable, as the model only needs to learn the missing details rather than synthesize the entire image. The authors provide a clear explanation for why this approach works better than traditional GAN training for this specific task.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The experimental results are thorough and convincing, with both quantitative (SSIM, Inception Score, user study) and qualitative evaluations across two different datasets. The comparisons with different pose embeddings, loss functions, and the two-stage vs. one-stage approaches provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of their proposed method. The comparison with the most similar work [36] is particularly compelling, with the authors' method clearly outperforming the baseline in both qualitative and quantitative terms.

The paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The main limitations are that the paper doesn't deeply analyze the error propagation from the pose estimation step (which is used to obtain the target pose keypoints), and the comparison with [36] could be strengthened with more quantitative metrics. However, these are relatively minor issues that don't undermine the paper's core contributions.

In terms of significance, this work opens up a new direction in person image generation with clear applications in movie making, human pose estimation, and virtual try-on systems. The two-stage approach could potentially be generalized to other image generation tasks with similar challenges, making this work broadly impactful. The pose mask loss could also find applications in other pose-related image generation tasks.

From a novelty perspective, this paper stands out as a bold and conceptually fresh contribution. It's not merely an incremental improvement on existing methods, but rather proposes a new task formulation and a fundamentally different approach to solving it. The authors have successfully demonstrated that conditioning on both reference image and intended pose is a viable and effective paradigm for person image generation.

Compared to the vast literature on conditional image generation, this paper offers a significantly different perspective that moves beyond simply conditioning on labels or text. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from previous work, particularly in terms of the explicit and flexible control they provide over the generation process.

Given the reviewer's emphasis on novelty over methodological perfection, this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance. The core idea of pose-guided person generation is both novel and significant, and the two-stage approach with the pose mask loss and difference map representation represents a bold and innovative solution to a challenging problem.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides thorough experimental validation. While there are some minor limitations, they don't detract from the paper's overall contribution. This work clearly advances the field by introducing a new task and a novel approach to solving it, with potential for significant impact in both research and applications.

**Recommendation: Accept**
