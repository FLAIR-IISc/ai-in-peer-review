Summary
This paper addresses conditional person image generation guided by a reference image (appearance) and a target pose (structure). The key idea is to disentangle global pose transfer from fine-grained appearance synthesis via a two-stage framework (PG2). Stage-I (G1) is a U-Net-like encoder–decoder that integrates the reference image with pose keypoints encoded as 18 heatmaps, trained with a masked L1 loss that upweights person pixels to mitigate background discrepancies. Stage-II (G2) refines the coarse result by predicting a residual “difference map” using a conditional GAN; the discriminator judges pairs (generated image, condition image) vs (real target, condition), and the generator is regularized by the masked L1 term. The method is evaluated on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64), with ablations on pose embedding (coordinates vs heatmaps), loss (L1 vs masked L1), and one-stage vs two-stage setups. Quantitative metrics include SSIM/IS and their masked variants; a user study on AMT is also reported. The approach is positioned relative to conditional GANs (e.g., pix2pix), stacked GANs, and multi-view generation; the closest baseline [36] uses view labels rather than explicit pose keypoints.

Quality
- Soundness: The two-stage decomposition is reasonable for this task: Stage-I enforces pose-consistent structure, Stage-II adds high-frequency details. Using pose heatmaps for spatially aligned conditioning is appropriate and supported by ablation (coordinates/FC underperform). The residual formulation at Stage-II is standard and accelerates training. Pairwise discrimination with the condition image reduces the risk of mode collapse to the reference.
- Experimental support: Qualitative results show clear pose adherence and sharper details with the two-stage pipeline. Ablations are informative (pose embeddings, masked loss, one- vs two-stage). The user study supports perceptual improvements on DeepFashion. However, some evaluation choices limit rigor: SSIM/IS (and their masked variants) are imperfect proxies for perceptual quality; the claim that SSIM favors blur is correct but alternative metrics (e.g., FID, LPIPS) would strengthen conclusions. The method’s effect on identity preservation is not quantified; a re-ID feature similarity metric would be appropriate given the intended applications. No downstream task evaluation (e.g., data augmentation benefits) is provided.
- Methodological details: Architecture and training hyperparameters are described with reasonable specificity (blocks, learning rates, batch sizes), though some choices warrant justification (e.g., a fully connected bottleneck in G1 may harm scalability; no batch norm/dropout in GAN training). The masked loss uses a heuristic mask from keypoints; robustness to pose estimation errors and mask generation details (e.g., morphology parameters) are not systematically analyzed.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-structured. The stage-wise design, losses, and discriminator pairing are explained with intuition. Figures support understanding. Some areas for improvement:
- Clarify whether the discriminator sees the target pose PB; currently D conditions on IA only, which may reduce its ability to enforce pose correctness at Stage-II.
- Provide more mask construction details and sensitivity to mask generation errors.
- Report the number of filters per stage and exact skip-connection topology for full reproducibility.
- Minor typos/inconsistencies can be polished.

Originality
The problem setting—conditioning on both a reference image and explicit pose keypoints for person image generation—fills a gap between label/text-conditioned generation and multi-view synthesis conditioned on coarse view labels. The two-stage decomposition and residual refinement are inspired by prior work (e.g., stacked GANs, residual learning), but their application to pose-guided person generation with a simple pose mask loss is a meaningful and practical combination. Ablations on pose encodings strengthen the contribution by demonstrating that heatmap concatenation is effective for this task.

Significance
The task is relevant to controllable image synthesis and has potential impact in data augmentation (pose estimation, re-ID), content creation, and editing. Qualitative improvements are noticeable, especially on DeepFashion; Market-1501 results are more challenging due to clutter and low resolution. The paper would be stronger with:
- Quantitative identity-preservation measurement (e.g., using a re-ID or face-recognition model).
- Perceptual metrics less sensitive to blur and background (e.g., LPIPS, FID/KID computed on person crops/masks).
- Downstream task evaluation to demonstrate practical utility.

Pros
- Clear, modular two-stage design that separates structure and detail.
- Effective and simple pose encoding via keypoint heatmaps; ablation shows clear benefit over coordinate embeddings.
- Masked L1 loss is a practical improvement for handling background mismatch.
- Residual refinement accelerates convergence and improves detail; qualitative gains are evident.
- Comprehensive ablations and a user study; comparison to the closest prior [36] favors this approach.

Cons
- Evaluation relies on SSIM/IS and ad-hoc masked variants; lacks modern perceptual metrics and identity preservation analysis.
- Discriminator does not condition on the target pose, potentially limiting enforcement of pose correctness at Stage-II.
- No downstream validation of claimed applications (pose estimation/re-ID augmentation).
- Limited analysis of robustness to pose estimation errors and mask quality.
- Use of a fully connected bottleneck in Stage-I may impede scalability to higher resolutions and fine details.

Questions for the authors
- Why is the discriminator not conditioned on PB (pose heatmaps) in addition to IA? Would D(IA, PB, I) better enforce pose fidelity?
- How sensitive is performance to pose estimation noise and mask construction parameters?
- Can you report identity similarity using a re-ID network on Market-1501 and DeepFashion?
- What is the effect of removing the fully connected layer in G1, or replacing it with dilated convolutions/self-attention?

Suggestions
- Add pose-conditioned discrimination (or a pose-consistency loss) at Stage-II.
- Report LPIPS and FID/KID on foreground-masked images; include a re-ID similarity metric.
- Consider multi-scale discriminators and feature-matching/perceptual losses to stabilize GAN training and reduce artifacts.
- Provide robustness analysis to pose estimation errors and ablations on the mask weighting (1+MB) and λ.
- Explore warping-based modules (e.g., STN/flow) to better preserve local textures across large pose changes.

Decision lean: Weak accept. The paper presents a clear and practically useful two-stage method for pose-guided person image generation with solid qualitative results and informative ablations. Addressing evaluation limitations and pose-conditioning in the discriminator would further strengthen the contribution. The topic, approach, and results are within NeurIPS scope and of interest to the generative modeling and vision communities.
