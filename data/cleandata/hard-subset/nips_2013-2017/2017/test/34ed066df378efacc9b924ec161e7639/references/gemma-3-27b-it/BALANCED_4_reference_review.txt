This paper proposes a two-stage generative model for pose-guided person image generation. The core idea is to decouple the problem into generating a coarse structure based on the target pose in the first stage and refining the details with a GAN in the second stage. The authors also introduce a pose mask loss to focus the model on transferring human body appearance.

This is a relevant and interesting problem with connections to several existing areas in generative modeling and computer vision, including image-to-image translation, pose estimation, and controllable image generation. The authors correctly position their work within the context of GANs, VAEs, and autoregressive models, and specifically highlight relevant work on conditional image generation and pose transfer. The two-stage approach is a reasonable strategy for tackling this complex task, and the proposed pose mask loss is a sensible addition.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The specific combination of pose-guided generation with a two-stage approach and pose mask loss is a novel contribution. While conditional GANs are prevalent, conditioning on pose *and* a reference image simultaneously adds complexity and control.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed architecture appears sound and leverages well-established techniques like U-Net and DCGAN. The motivation for the two-stage approach is well-justified, and the mask loss is a logical extension of L1 loss for this task.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their method on two datasets (Market-1501 and DeepFashion) and provide both quantitative (SSIM, IS) and qualitative results. The ablation studies comparing different pose embeddings and loss functions are helpful. The user study provides some evidence of perceived realism.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The figures are helpful in illustrating the pipeline and results. The motivations behind design choices are clearly explained.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparison:** While the authors compare to one related work [36], the comparisons are not extensive. More thorough comparisons to existing image-to-image translation methods (e.g., CycleGAN, Pix2Pix) or recent pose transfer techniques would strengthen the evaluation. The difference in input (viewpoint *vs.* pose) is acknowledged but a more direct comparison of performance, even with caveats, is desired.
*   **Quantitative Results Interpretation:** The quantitative results (SSIM, IS) are not always convincing. As noted by the authors, these metrics can be misleading.  A more focused evaluation on pose accuracy (e.g., using a pose estimator on the generated images) would be valuable.  Furthermore, the authors should discuss the limitations of these metrics in the context of their task.
*   **Failure Cases:** The discussion of failure cases is limited. While a few examples are provided, a more systematic analysis of the types of poses or identities where the method struggles would be helpful.  The stated causes (rare data, imbalance) are plausible but require more evidence.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The influence of the λ parameter (balancing adversarial and L1 loss) is mentioned briefly but not thoroughly explored. A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would be beneficial. The supplementary material refers to this, but it’s essential to have more detail in the main body.
*   **Pose Estimation Dependency:** The method relies on an external pose estimator. Errors in pose estimation will directly impact the quality of the generated images. This dependency should be discussed more explicitly, and potential strategies for mitigating these errors (e.g., incorporating uncertainty estimates from the pose estimator) could be considered.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel problem formulation and a reasonable approach.
*   Solid technical foundation and well-executed experiments.
*   Clear and well-written paper.
*   The two-stage approach with pose mask loss demonstrates a promising direction for pose-guided image generation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited comparison to related work.
*   Quantitative evaluation is not entirely convincing and lacks a pose-specific metric.
*   Insufficient analysis of failure cases and hyperparameter sensitivity.
*   Reliance on an external pose estimator introduces a potential source of error.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good paper with a novel contribution and solid technical work. However, the limited comparison to related methods and the somewhat unconvincing quantitative results prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. The authors address a challenging problem and propose a promising approach, but further refinement and more comprehensive evaluation are needed.

**Recommendation:** Reject with encouragement to revise and resubmit.

**Suggestions for Revision:**

*   **Expand the related work comparison:** Include more comparisons to relevant image-to-image translation and pose transfer methods.
*   **Introduce a pose accuracy metric:** Evaluate the generated images using a pose estimator to quantify the accuracy of the transferred pose.
*   **Analyze failure cases more thoroughly:** Provide a systematic analysis of the types of poses or identities where the method struggles.
*   **Investigate hyperparameter sensitivity:** Conduct a more detailed analysis of the influence of key hyperparameters, such as λ.
*   **Discuss the limitations of the pose estimator dependency:** Acknowledge the potential impact of pose estimation errors and explore possible mitigation strategies.
*   **Clarify the user study results:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the user study data and address the discrepancy between subjective evaluation and quantitative metrics.
