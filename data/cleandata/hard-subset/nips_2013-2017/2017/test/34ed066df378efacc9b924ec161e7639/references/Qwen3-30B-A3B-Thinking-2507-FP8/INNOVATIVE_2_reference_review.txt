

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

This paper introduces a novel task of generating person images by conditioning on both a reference image and a target pose, rather than on abstract attributes or categories as in previous work. The authors propose a two-stage approach (PG2) where Stage-I integrates pose information to capture global structure (using pose heatmaps and a novel pose mask loss), and Stage-II refines the result by generating a difference map instead of the full image from scratch. This is a bold, novel direction that addresses a meaningful gap in image generation literature.

The core novelty lies in explicitly conditioning generation on pose information in a flexible, expressive manner using keypoints as input. Previous work (e.g., [36]) conditions on abstract view descriptions ("side," "front") rather than explicit pose information, making their conditioning less precise. The authors' pose heatmap representation and pose mask loss are clever innovations that effectively focus the model on human body transfer rather than background, as demonstrated in their ablation studies.

The two-stage approach is particularly innovative. By first generating a coarse pose-aligned image (Stage-I) and then refining it with a difference map generator (Stage-II), the authors solve the challenging problem of simultaneously handling pose and appearance. This divide-and-conquer strategy is more effective than single-stage alternatives, as shown by both qualitative results (Figure 4) and user studies (Table 2). The difference map approach also speeds up training, as the generator doesn't have to synthesize the entire image from scratch.

The paper makes a strong case for its novelty by thoroughly comparing with related work, particularly [36], which represents the closest prior work. The comparison in Figure 5 clearly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed method, especially for generating high-quality whole-body images from partial inputs.

While the methodology is sound, there are some minor limitations that don't detract from the paper's novelty. The paper uses pose estimation instead of ground-truth poses, which might introduce errors, though the authors acknowledge this limitation. The failure cases analysis is limited to just two examples, but this is a minor issue for a paper of such high novelty. The paper could have included more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art image generation methods, but the focus on a novel task justifies this limited scope.

The experimental results are thorough, with ablation studies on pose embeddings, loss functions, and one-stage vs. two-stage approaches. The quantitative metrics (SSIM, IS) and user studies provide convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness. The qualitative results in Figure 4 clearly demonstrate the advantage of the proposed pose mask loss and two-stage approach.

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by introducing a novel generation task with clear applications in movie making, human pose estimation, and image retrieval. The two-stage approach with difference map generation is a clever solution to a challenging problem, and the pose mask loss is a simple yet effective innovation. While the methodological details are sound, the true strength of this paper lies in its bold conceptual contribution rather than technical perfection.

I appreciate how the authors clearly articulate the novelty of their work in the introduction and related work sections. They properly position themselves against previous work while demonstrating how their approach is fundamentally different and more expressive. The paper avoids incremental improvements and instead proposes a new direction for image generation research.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, I find this paper compelling. The task of pose-guided person image generation is genuinely new, and the two-stage approach offers a fresh perspective on solving this challenging problem. The paper's contributions are significant and could inspire a new line of research in conditional image generation.

The paper meets all the key criteria for NIPS: it is original (novel task and approach), significant (addresses a meaningful problem with practical applications), and well-written (clear methodology and results). While there are minor weaknesses in the experimental comparisons and failure analysis, these don't diminish the paper's core novelty.

**Recommendation**: Accept

The paper introduces a genuinely novel concept that pushes the boundaries of conditional image generation. The pose-guided approach with two-stage refinement is bold, well-motivated, and effectively solves a challenging problem. The authors have made a clear contribution to the field that will likely inspire future research, which is exactly what NIPS seeks to publish.
