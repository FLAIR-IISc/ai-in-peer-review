Summary
This paper tackles pose-guided person image generation: given a reference image of a person and a target human pose (as 2D keypoints), synthesize a new image of the same person in the target pose. The core idea is a two-stage, conditional approach (PG2). Stage I integrates the reference image and pose via a U-Net–style encoder-decoder with skip connections to produce a coarse image that captures global structure; it uses a masked L1 loss to focus on the person rather than background. Stage II refines details with a conditional DCGAN trained to predict a residual (“difference map”) over the Stage I output, with a pairwise discriminator on (reference, generated) vs (reference, real) and an additional masked L1 term for stability. The pose is encoded as 18 heatmaps (one per keypoint). The method is evaluated on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64), with ablations on pose embeddings, losses, and one- vs two-stage training, plus a user study. The approach improves visual realism over a one-stage cGAN baseline and outperforms a related multi-view work [36] qualitatively.

Relation to prior work
The paper is situated within conditional generative modeling, building on cGANs and U-Nets (e.g., Isola et al. “pix2pix”), stacked/iterative GANs (e.g., StackGAN, Laplacian refinement), and image generation conditioned on structure (keypoints, segmentation). The conditioning of the discriminator on image pairs follows pix2pix. The residual/difference prediction is akin to residual learning and multi-stage refinement. Conditioning on pose keypoints as heatmaps connects to prior keypoint/structure-conditioned generation works, but here the explicit person pose transfer with an appearance reference is the focus. Compared to [36] (multi-view person from a single view + viewpoint token), this method uses richer, more explicit pose conditioning via keypoints.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and practical relevance (pose-conditioned appearance-preserving synthesis; applications to content creation, data augmentation).
- Thoughtful decomposition into two stages: global structure via supervised loss, followed by adversarial refinement of details. Predicting a difference map is a sensible design that stabilizes training and speeds convergence.
- Simple, effective pose embedding via heatmap concatenation; ablations convincingly show it outperforms coordinate or independent-encoder embeddings.
- Masked L1 is a pragmatic contribution to mitigate background mismatch; ablations indicate benefits, especially on cluttered Market-1501.
- Comprehensive experimental design: two datasets of different difficulty/resolution; ablations (embeddings, losses, one vs two stage); pairwise discriminator; qualitative comparison to the closest prior [36]; AMT user study.
- The paper is generally clear and well structured.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Baselines: While a one-stage adversarial baseline is included, stronger and more direct baselines are missing. In particular: (i) pix2pix-style cGAN trained to map (reference image + keypoint heatmaps) to target, with PatchGAN discriminator; (ii) a warping/appearance flow baseline (pose-guided warping followed by refinement), which is standard in pose transfer; (iii) perceptual-loss–based L1+VGG baseline.
- Metrics: SSIM and Inception Score (IS), especially their masked variants, are not ideal or standard for this task; IS is known to be uninformative for conditional, single-domain generation. More appropriate measures would include FID/KID, LPIPS (perceptual distance), and identity preservation via a pre-trained person re-ID network (e.g., top-1/top-5 retrieval on generated images). Report statistics (mean/std, significance) and confidence intervals for the user study.
- Identity preservation: The method lacks an explicit identity/appearance constraint. Failure cases (gender swap, face changes) suggest adding an identity/perceptual loss (VGG or re-ID features) could help retain person-specific appearance.
- Background handling: The masked L1 is helpful, but background artifacts persist and Stage II sometimes copies background from the reference image. Consider an explicit foreground segmentation or using DensePose/body-part segmentations to decouple person/background, or training with a PatchGAN (local) discriminator to reduce background copying.
- Robustness and generalization: The approach relies on accurate keypoints and paired images of the same identity. An analysis of sensitivity to pose estimation errors and to larger pose/appearance gaps would strengthen the claims. Diversity is not modeled (deterministic outputs); discussion or experiments on stochasticity (e.g., noise injection or dropout) would be interesting.

Clarity
- Pose mask construction is only briefly described; more details (exact morphological operations, thresholds) would aid reproducibility. Similarly, provide lambda values, training time, and architecture specifics (channels, feature map sizes) in the main text or a public supplement.
- Some architectural choices (e.g., removal of batch norm, use of FC in Stage I but not II) are justified only qualitatively; brief ablations or rationale would improve clarity.

Originality
- The overall design (two-stage, residual refinement, pairwise discriminator) combines known components. The contributions are primarily in the specific problem instantiation (pose-guided person transfer with a reference) and careful engineering (heatmap concatenation, masked loss). Novelty is moderate but reasonable given the application was underexplored at the time.

Significance
- The task is important and the method provides a solid baseline with practical value. The qualitative improvements over a naive one-stage baseline and over [36] are convincing, especially on DeepFashion. Stronger quantitative evidence (FID/LPIPS, identity metrics) would make the case more compelling and help future work build on this.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, relevant problem; simple, effective two-stage solution with meaningful ablations.
- Strong qualitative results and user study evidence on two datasets.
- Practical contributions (pose heatmap embedding, residual refinement, masked loss) that others can adopt.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited baselines and suboptimal metrics; missing standard modern measures (FID/LPIPS) and identity preservation evaluation.
- Some artifacts and background leakage; no explicit identity/perceptual constraints.
- Dependence on paired data and pose estimator not analyzed for robustness.

Questions for authors
- Can you report FID and LPIPS, and a re-ID–based identity preservation metric? How sensitive are results to pose estimation errors?
- What lambda values were used at both stages, and how were they selected?
- How does a PatchGAN discriminator compare to your current D? Any benefit from multi-scale discriminators?
- Could you release code and masks to aid reproducibility?

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a well-motivated, practical approach with clear ablations and solid qualitative results on a meaningful, underexplored task. Strengthening quantitative evaluation and baselines would further improve the paper, but the current contribution is likely to be useful to the community. Confidence: medium.
