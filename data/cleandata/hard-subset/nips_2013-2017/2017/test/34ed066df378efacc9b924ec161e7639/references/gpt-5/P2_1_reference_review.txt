Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles pose-guided person image generation: given a reference image (appearance) and a target pose (keypoints), synthesize the person in the new pose. The method (PG2) is two-stage. Stage I is a U-Net-like encoder–decoder with residual blocks that takes the input image concatenated with 18-channel pose heatmaps and outputs a coarse image. A masked L1 loss, computed with a foreground human-body mask derived from keypoints, emphasizes the person over the background. Stage II is a conditional DCGAN that refines the coarse result by predicting a difference map; it uses a pairwise discriminator on (condition image, generated/refined image) vs (condition image, real target), and mixes adversarial and masked L1 losses. Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) include ablations on pose embeddings (coordinate vs heatmap encoders vs simple heatmap concatenation), loss (masked vs vanilla L1), and one-stage vs two-stage adversarial training; quantitative metrics (SSIM/IS and masked variants), AMT user studies, and a qualitative comparison to VariGAN [36] are reported.

This situates well within conditional generative modeling (VAEs, autoregressive, GANs), especially conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero), image-to-image translation (e.g., pix2pix), text/attribute-conditioned synthesis (Reed et al., StackGAN), multi-view and face-view synthesis. The contribution is to explicitly condition on target pose keypoints, use a foreground-weighted loss, and adopt a staged coarse-to-fine pipeline with a difference-map GAN to sharpen details.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architecture and losses are reasonable and align with established practice. Encoding pose as per-joint heatmaps concatenated with the image is a sound design that leverages convolutional locality; the results and ablations convincingly show it outperforms coordinate-based embeddings in this setting. The masked L1 loss is a simple, sensible way to de-emphasize background mismatch, especially on Market-1501. The Stage II GAN trained to predict a residual/difference map, with a pairwise discriminator, is well-motivated and leads to visibly sharper details.
- Empirical support: Ablations are informative (pose embedding, loss, one vs two stage). The qualitative results match the claims: Stage I captures structure, Stage II improves high-frequency details. The AMT study corroborates improved realism on DeepFashion (G1+G2+D confuses users more than G1+D). The masked SSIM/IS attempt to reduce background confounds on Market-1501 is thoughtful.
- Weaknesses in evaluation: Quantitative evaluation relies on SSIM/IS (and masked variants). These metrics are known to correlate poorly with perceptual realism; no modern perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) or distributional metrics (e.g., FID) are reported. Identity preservation—a central goal—is not quantitatively assessed (e.g., via re-ID feature similarity or human identity recognition). Baselines are limited: aside from an internal one-stage adversarial variant, comparisons to standard image-to-image baselines (e.g., pix2pix conditioned on pose heatmaps) are missing; only a qualitative comparison to VariGAN is provided. The claim that difference maps speed convergence is plausible but not measured.
- Assumptions and limitations: The method relies on a pretrained pose estimator to obtain target keypoints for training and inference (reasonable), and uses target-pose-derived masks only for training losses. Background transfer artifacts and failure cases for rare poses or imbalanced gender are acknowledged. Generalization beyond the two datasets or to unconstrained images is not explored. The model is deterministic; diversity under pose ambiguity is not addressed.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The staged pipeline, losses, and architectures are described with sufficient detail (layer types, block counts, training settings) to support reproduction. Figures (pose heatmaps, qualitative grids) help. Some aspects could be clarified: precise construction of the pose mask (the morphological operations and connectivity assumptions), rationale for the fully connected layer in G1 but not G2, and more explicit baselines. The references list contains numerous formatting issues and missing bibliographic details; fixing these would improve polish.

Originality
The problem formulation—explicitly conditioning on a target pose specified by keypoints, given a reference image—is a meaningful departure from viewpoint- or text-conditioned synthesis common at the time. The components themselves are mostly known (U-Net with skip connections, residual blocks, conditional GANs, pairwise discriminator), but their combination—pose heatmap concatenation, foreground-weighted L1, and a staged residual-refinement GAN—is a thoughtful and nontrivial synthesis. The staged approach relates to stacked GANs; using a difference map for refinement is an incremental but useful twist.

Significance
Pose-guided person synthesis is practically relevant (graphics, data augmentation) and scientifically useful as a controllable generative benchmark. The method appears to substantially improve qualitative fidelity over a one-stage baseline and over VariGAN on DeepFashion. While the lack of stronger baselines and identity metrics tempers the strength of the claims, the approach is likely to influence subsequent work and be adopted as a baseline. The contribution is incremental in methodology but significant in demonstrating a viable, controllable pipeline and in highlighting design choices (pose encoding, foreground weighting, staged refinement).

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear, well-motivated problem with practical importance; explicit pose control via keypoints.
- Effective staged design with strong, ablated evidence that each component helps (pose heatmaps > coordinates; masked L1 > L1; two-stage > one-stage).
- Sensible GAN refinement via difference maps and pairwise conditioning; improved realism in qualitative and AMT results.
- Thorough experimental details and acknowledgment of limitations.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Limited quantitative rigor: reliance on SSIM/IS (even masked) without perceptual or identity-preservation metrics; no FID/LPIPS.
- Missing strong baselines (e.g., pix2pix-like models with the same pose conditioning) and limited comparative evaluation.
- Incremental novelty in methodology; components are mostly established.
- Background artifacts on Market-1501 and no demonstration of downstream utility (e.g., improving re-ID or pose estimation via generated data).

Overall assessment
This is a solid, well-executed application paper with a clear formulation, sensible architectural choices, and convincing qualitative improvements for a meaningful task. Despite some shortcomings in baseline comparisons and metrics, the work advances controllable person image generation and would be of interest to the community. I lean toward acceptance, with suggestions to strengthen evaluation (add identity metrics, perceptual/distributional scores, and stronger baselines) and to clarify mask construction and reference formatting.
