This paper proposes a two-stage GAN-based approach for transferring a person’s pose in an image, conditioned on both a reference image and a target pose. The method first generates a coarse image representing the target pose and then refines it with a second GAN to add details. While the idea of pose-guided image generation is interesting, the paper presents incremental work with limited novelty and insufficient validation, raising concerns about its suitability for NIPS.

The primary weakness lies in the lack of thorough comparison against established methods. While the related work section mentions several relevant GAN-based image generation techniques, there’s no quantitative or qualitative comparison with these baselines, particularly for pose transfer or image manipulation tasks. Simply stating that existing methods lack “explicit” pose control isn't sufficient justification. The comparison to [36] is limited and doesn’t demonstrate a significant advantage beyond handling a slightly different conditioning input. A critical comparison with existing image manipulation techniques, even those not explicitly pose-based (e.g., methods for attribute editing using GANs), is necessary.

The methodological choices also require stronger justification. The two-stage approach, while potentially simplifying the problem, feels ad-hoc. The reasoning for dividing the problem into global structure and appearance details isn’t convincingly presented. Why is a U-Net suitable for stage-I and a DCGAN for stage-II? What analysis was done to determine the optimal architecture for each stage? The introduction of a “pose mask loss” is a reasonable attempt to focus on the human body, but its effectiveness seems largely demonstrated through visual examples, with quantitative benefits appearing minor (Table 1). The dependence on a pre-trained pose estimator is a potential limitation, as the accuracy of the estimator directly impacts the quality of the generated images. A discussion of the estimator’s potential errors and their impact is missing.

The experiments lack rigor. The ablation studies examining different pose embeddings and losses are helpful, but the conclusions drawn are somewhat weak. The gains from using the proposed pose mask are not substantial, and the qualitative results, while demonstrating some improvement, aren't overwhelmingly convincing. The user study results are mixed, showing that the generated images are sometimes confused with real images but also frequently identified as fake, particularly on the more challenging Market-1501 dataset. This suggests that the generated images still suffer from artifacts or inconsistencies. The analysis of failure cases is appreciated, but further investigation into *why* these failures occur is needed. 

The paper’s clarity is generally good, but the presentation could be improved. The figures are somewhat cluttered, and the descriptions of the network architectures could be more concise. The notation is consistent, but a clearer explanation of the loss functions would be beneficial.

**Strengths:**

*   The idea of explicitly conditioning image generation on pose is potentially useful for applications like animation and data augmentation.
*   The two-stage approach provides a structured way to tackle the complex problem of pose transfer.
*   The introduction of the pose mask loss is a reasonable attempt to improve generation quality.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of thorough comparison to established methods.
*   Ad-hoc nature of the two-stage approach without sufficient justification.
*   Limited quantitative gains and mixed results from user studies.
*   Dependence on a pre-trained pose estimator without detailed error analysis.
*   Incremental contribution with limited novelty.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant problem in image generation.
*   The proposed method demonstrates some promising results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of strong baselines and rigorous evaluation makes it difficult to assess the significance of the contribution.
*   The methodological choices are not fully justified.
*   The paper lacks substantial novelty compared to existing work.
*   The results are not consistently convincing across datasets and evaluation metrics.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. The paper presents an incremental contribution to the field of image generation. While the idea is interesting, the lack of thorough comparison, the ad-hoc nature of the method, and the limited quantitative results raise significant concerns about its suitability for NIPS. The authors need to provide a much more compelling demonstration of the method’s advantages over existing techniques and address the methodological weaknesses before this work would be considered a significant contribution to the field.
