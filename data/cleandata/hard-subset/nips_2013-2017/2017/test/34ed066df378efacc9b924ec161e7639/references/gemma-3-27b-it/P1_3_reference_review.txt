## Peer Review: Pose Guided Person Image Generation

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of generating person images conditioned on both a reference image and a target pose. The authors propose a two-stage approach – Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) – where the first stage generates a coarse image capturing the global structure based on the target pose, and the second stage refines the initial result with a GAN to generate sharper, more detailed images. They introduce a pose mask loss to focus learning on the human body and alleviate background influence. The paper presents results on two datasets (Market-1501 and DeepFashion) and provides ablation studies exploring different components of their approach.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the growing body of work in image generation using GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014), VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2013), and autoregressive models (van den Oord et al., 2016). It also relates to conditional image generation (Mirza & Osindero, 2014; Reed et al., 2016) and specifically, works that combine image and pose information (Chen et al., 2017; Zhao et al., 2017). The authors clearly position their work within this context, highlighting how their approach differs by explicitly conditioning on pose keypoints for more flexible control compared to other methods.  The comparison to StackGAN++ (Zhang et al., 2017) and Zhao et al. (2017) is relevant and well-articulated.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The task of explicitly conditioning image generation on both a reference image *and* a specified pose is a worthwhile contribution. This provides a degree of control over image generation that is less common in existing methods.
* **Divide-and-Conquer Strategy:** The two-stage approach is a sensible way to break down a complex problem. Learning global structure first and then refining details seems like a reasonable strategy, especially given the difficulty of generating both pose and appearance simultaneously.
* **Pose Mask Loss:** The introduction of the pose mask loss is a clever way to address the issue of background inconsistency between the reference and target images, allowing the model to focus on the human body.
* **Thorough Experiments:**  The authors perform several ablation studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of different components of their method (pose embeddings, loss functions, and the two-stage approach).  Experiments on two datasets of varying difficulty are also a plus.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and experimental setup. The figures are helpful in illustrating the process.
* **User Study:** Inclusion of a user study strengthens the evaluation of the generated images and adds to the credibility of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Quantitative Improvement:** While ablation studies show component-wise improvements, the quantitative gains (SSIM, IS) are often modest.  The authors acknowledge this and note that these metrics don’t always align with perceptual quality, but it’s still a point to consider.
* **Blurriness in Stage-I:** The authors acknowledge that Stage-I produces blurry results despite the use of residual blocks. This suggests that further exploration of network architectures or loss functions for the initial stage might be beneficial.
* **Dataset Limitations:** The datasets used, while helpful for demonstration, have limitations. Market-1501 is challenging due to the low resolution and complex backgrounds, but the results there appear less convincing.  DeepFashion is a more controlled dataset, and the model performs better, potentially obscuring the challenges of the task.
* **GAN Training Instability:** As with many GAN-based approaches, potential issues with training instability aren’t explicitly addressed beyond mentioning the use of adversarial loss. A discussion on the stability of the GAN training process would be helpful.
* **Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The comparison with related work (Zhao et al., 2017) is limited.  A more comprehensive comparison against stronger baselines, or a detailed explanation as to *why* a more direct comparison wasn't possible, would be valuable.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a novel and interesting problem with a well-motivated approach.
* The two-stage strategy and pose mask loss are valuable contributions.
* The experimental results, while not overwhelmingly large, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The quantitative improvements are somewhat modest.
*  The user study results on Market-1501 show lower scores compared to DeepFashion.
*  The comparison to related work could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of image generation. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and supported by experimental results. While the quantitative gains are not dramatic, the qualitative results are promising, and the insights gained from the ablation studies are valuable.  The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggestions for Revision:**

* **Expand Comparison with Related Work:** Include a more thorough comparison to existing methods, particularly those that address similar tasks, and justify why a direct comparison was not possible if applicable.
* **Address GAN Training Stability:** Briefly discuss the techniques used to stabilize GAN training, or acknowledge the potential for instability and its impact on results.
* **Investigate Stage-I Bluriness:** Explore alternative architectures or loss functions to reduce the blurriness observed in the initial stage.
* **Expand Dataset Discussion:** Acknowledge the limitations of the datasets used and discuss how the results might generalize to more challenging scenarios.



**Confidence:** 7/10
