

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

This paper proposes a two-stage approach for generating person images conditioned on a reference image and target pose. The authors divide the problem into: 1) a pose integration stage using a U-Net variant to generate a coarse result with a novel "pose mask loss," and 2) an image refinement stage using a conditional GAN to generate higher quality results. The work builds on established techniques (U-Net, GANs) rather than introducing fundamentally new methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written
- The two-stage approach is logically motivated by the challenge of simultaneously generating pose and appearance details
- The pose mask loss is a reasonable adaptation of standard techniques to address background interference
- The qualitative results in Figure 4 demonstrate clear improvements over one-stage approaches
- The method is evaluated on two diverse datasets (Market-1501 and DeepFashion)

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weakness is that the paper presents a combination of established techniques rather than a novel contribution. The core components (U-Net architecture, conditional GAN, masked loss) are all well-established in the image generation literature. The "novelty" claimed in the paper is largely in the specific application of these techniques to pose-guided person generation, not in the techniques themselves.

The paper fails to adequately demonstrate that their approach represents a significant advancement over existing methods. While the qualitative results show improvements, the quantitative results (Table 1) show only marginal gains in SSIM and IS metrics. The user study (Table 2) shows a modest 5.6% improvement in "real" ratings for the two-stage model over the one-stage model, which is not statistically compelling for a NIPS paper.

The comparison with the most related work [36] is insufficient. The authors acknowledge that [36] is not a direct comparison since it uses different input conditions (a word vector for target view vs. pose keypoints), yet they don't provide a more rigorous comparison with other conditional image generation methods. The paper doesn't demonstrate how their pose mask loss or two-stage approach outperforms standard conditional GAN approaches on this task.

The paper also doesn't sufficiently address limitations. The failure cases in Figure 6 reveal significant problems (e.g., generating "pieces of legs" for rare poses, gender mismatches), yet the authors don't discuss how to address these issues or how common they are in practice. The Market-1501 results (more challenging dataset) show significantly degraded performance compared to DeepFashion, which suggests the method's limitations are substantial.

**Overall assessment:**
While the paper presents a technically sound approach to pose-guided person image generation, the contribution is incremental rather than transformative. The method builds on well-established techniques without providing sufficient evidence of significant improvement over existing methods. The novelty is limited to the specific application of these techniques rather than the techniques themselves.

The paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art conditional image generation methods (e.g., Pix2Pix, CycleGAN, StyleGAN variants) on the same task. It should also provide more detailed analysis of failure cases and discuss how the method's limitations might be addressed in future work.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. The paper represents an incremental combination of established techniques with limited evidence of significant improvement over existing methods. While the problem is interesting, the contribution does not meet NIPS standards for novelty and significance. The authors should either demonstrate substantially stronger improvements over baselines or develop a genuinely novel approach to justify publication at NIPS. The marginal gains shown in the user study (14.9% vs 9.3% "real" ratings) are insufficient to justify the paper's claims of novelty.
