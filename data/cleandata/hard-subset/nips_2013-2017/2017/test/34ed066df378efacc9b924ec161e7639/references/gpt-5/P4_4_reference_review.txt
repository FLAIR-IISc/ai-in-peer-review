Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses pose-guided person image generation: synthesize an image of a person in a target pose given a reference image of the same person. The method (PG2) is a two-stage conditional image generation pipeline. Stage I uses a U-Net-like encoder-decoder with skip connections to integrate the reference image with target pose heatmaps (built from 18 keypoints) to produce a coarse image capturing global structure. A masked L1 loss (foreground-weighted using a pose-derived mask) de-emphasizes background mismatch. Stage II refines details via a conditional GAN: a U-Net generator predicts a residual (difference map) to add to the Stage I result, and a discriminator judges real-vs-fake using pairs (condition image, generated/real image). A masked L1 term regularizes the adversarial objective. Experiments on DeepFashion and Market-1501 include ablations on pose embedding (coordinate vs heatmaps vs direct concatenation), loss (L1 vs masked), and one-stage vs two-stage. Quantitative evaluation uses SSIM/IS and masked variants; a small AMT study is included. A qualitative comparison is provided to VariGAN [36].

This work builds on conditional generative models (CVAE [14], conditional GANs [18, 21], autoregressive decoders [28–30]), and image-to-image translation with cGANs [9]. It relates to staged generation frameworks (e.g., Laplacian GANs, StackGAN) and residual learning in image restoration. It differs from text-conditioned and view-conditioned synthesis [22–24, 34, 36] by using explicit pose keypoints, similar in spirit to conditioning on structure (e.g., segmentation maps [15]) but with keypoint heatmaps as a lightweight structural prior.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation with practical utility; explicit pose conditioning via heatmaps is effective and simple.
- Sensible two-stage decomposition: coarse pose/appearance followed by adversarial refinement; predicting a residual simplifies Stage II learning.
- Foreground-weighted (masked) L1 is a targeted, low-cost way to mitigate background variation.
- The discriminator conditions on (reference, target/generated) pairs, encouraging pose- and identity-consistent refinement.
- Thorough ablations on pose embeddings and loss in Stage I; qualitative improvements from Stage II are evident on DeepFashion; AMT results support perceptual gains.
- Implementation details (optimizers, training schedule, datasets) are reasonably described.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is moderate: most components (U-Net with pose heatmaps, foreground-weighted L1, residual prediction, pairwise conditional discriminator) are known ideas adapted to this task. The two-stage refinement resembles prior coarse-to-fine/staged GANs; the masked L1 is a straightforward attention weighting.
- Quantitative evaluation is limited. SSIM/IS (even masked) are known to correlate poorly with perceptual quality; FID or human preference tests stratified by variant would be stronger. Stage II offers clear qualitative gains, but quantitative improvements are weak or inconsistent, especially on Market-1501.
- No evaluation of identity preservation or pose fidelity. For a person synthesis task, reporting re-ID feature similarity/match rates, or keypoint accuracy on generated images, would directly assess the core goals.
- Sensitivity to pose estimation errors is not analyzed; the pipeline’s robustness to noisy/missing keypoints (common in the wild) is unclear.
- Background handling is ad hoc. The pose-derived mask may be imprecise; artifacts and background transfer are noted by the authors on Market-1501. Using segmentation (even predicted) could improve masking and evaluation.
- Missing comparisons with strong baselines such as pix2pix-style cGANs conditioned on (reference image + pose heatmaps) in a single stage with multi-scale discriminators; or LapGAN-style coarse-to-fine baselines. Claims about faster convergence of residual prediction are not supported by training curves.
- Some architectural choices are unusual or under-motivated (e.g., fully connected bottleneck in G1, no normalization). Ablations on FC vs fully convolutional in Stage I, normalization effects, and λ values are deferred to the supplement.

Quality
Overall technically sound and well-engineered. Losses and architectures are standard; training details are adequate. However, several claims (e.g., faster convergence with residual prediction, foreground masking benefits beyond qualitative examples) would benefit from stronger, task-relevant quantitative evidence. The evaluation omits identity/pose fidelity metrics and robustness analyses.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The problem, architecture, and losses are described with equations. Some details could be clarified:
- Exact λ used in Stage II, and sensitivity analysis in the main text.
- How the foreground mask is constructed from keypoints (algorithmic steps, failure modes).
- Architectural specifics (channel counts per block, skip-connection details, image normalization) to aid reproducibility.
- Rationale for the FC bottleneck in G1 (and its impact) versus the fully convolutional design in G2.

Originality
Moderate. The task setting (pose-guided person generation with a reference image) was relatively new and underexplored at the time. The combination of components is reasonable but largely incremental relative to existing conditional and staged generation literature.

Significance
Potentially useful for controllable synthesis and data augmentation. The qualitative results on DeepFashion are compelling. However, the limited quantitative gains, lack of identity/pose fidelity metrics, and sensitivity to background clutter temper the demonstrated impact. Broader comparisons and stronger metrics would increase confidence in adoption.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide identity preservation metrics (e.g., cosine similarity in a re-ID embedding, retrieval accuracy) and pose fidelity (keypoint detector on generated images vs target pose).
- Include FID and/or human two-alternative forced-choice comparisons between variants, and report training curves to substantiate convergence claims for residual prediction.
- Add robustness analysis to pose noise and missing keypoints; report failure cases quantitatively.
- Compare to a strong pix2pix baseline (reference+pose heatmaps → target) with multi-scale discriminators and perceptual losses.
- Clarify the mask construction; consider using predicted person segmentation to build masks for both loss and evaluation.
- Justify/remove the FC bottleneck in Stage I; provide ablations showing its effect on detail retention.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated task with explicit controllability via pose.
- Effective, simple design that produces strong qualitative results on a challenging, high-resolution dataset.
- Useful ablations elucidating design choices (pose embedding, masking, two-stage refinement).
- Potentially impactful for applications (editing, data augmentation).

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental novelty; components largely known.
- Limited and partially misaligned quantitative evaluation; core properties (identity, pose correctness) not measured.
- Mixed results on cluttered data; background artifacts and limited robustness analysis.
- Missing comparisons to strong, closely related baselines.

Overall, the work is solid and practically relevant, but the empirical evaluation should be strengthened with task-appropriate metrics, robustness analyses, and stronger baselines to fully support its claims.
