Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses pose-guided person image generation: given a reference image of a person and a target pose (as 2D keypoints), synthesize a new image of the same person in the target pose. The method (PG2) is a two-stage pipeline. Stage I integrates the reference image and target pose via a U-Net-like encoder–decoder with skip connections to produce a coarse image that captures global structure (pose, colors). Pose is encoded as 18 binary/blurred keypoint heatmaps concatenated with the image input. A “pose mask” L1 loss upweights the person region to reduce penalties from background mismatch between the reference and target. Stage II refines details using a conditional GAN: a U-Net-like generator predicts a residual “difference map” to add to the stage-I output, and a discriminator judges pairs (reference image, generated image) versus (reference image, ground truth) to enforce pose-consistent realism. Training uses adversarial loss plus the same pose-masked L1.

The paper situates itself among GANs (DCGAN), conditional GANs, CVAE, and autoregressive models, and among conditional generation with labels/text and image-conditioned view synthesis. It is closest to image-to-image translation and pose/view-conditioned generation, including concurrent works that use heatmaps, masks, and stacked/cascaded generators to improve fidelity. Compared to view-conditioned methods (e.g., VariGAN) and segmentation-guided person generation, the paper claims more explicit and flexible control via keypoint poses and focuses on preserving identity.

Strengths
- Clear task definition with practical motivation (controllable editing, data augmentation).
- Well-structured, two-stage design that cleanly separates global pose/structure (Stage I) from appearance refinement (Stage II).
- Effective pose conditioning via heatmap concatenation; the ablation convincingly shows it is superior to coordinate-level embeddings in this setting.
- Pose-masked L1 is a simple, reasonable way to mitigate background ambiguity and focus learning on the person.
- The residual/difference-map formulation in Stage II is a sensible design choice that likely eases optimization; the pairwise discriminator is appropriate to avoid trivial copying of the condition image.
- Extensive qualitative results on two datasets (DeepFashion, Market-1501), with ablations (pose embeddings, losses, one- vs two-stage) and AMT user study.

Weaknesses
- Quantitative evaluation is limited. SSIM/IS (and masked variants) have known shortcomings for perceptual quality and identity; they do not clearly reflect the qualitative gains. No FID or LPIPS; no identity preservation metrics (e.g., re-ID feature distance); no pose accuracy metrics (e.g., PCK on generated images).
- Comparison to prior work is predominantly qualitative and limited (only to VariGAN). No quantitative baselines (e.g., pix2pix with pose heatmaps, a one-stage cGAN with PatchGAN, segmentation/pose-guided person generation).
- The benefit of predicting a difference map is argued but not quantified (e.g., training speed, convergence, or ablation on with/without residual learning).
- Stage I includes a fully connected bottleneck, which can harm spatial fidelity and generalization to larger images; design justifications are limited. No batch normalization/normalization may affect stability; rationale is not discussed.
- The method sometimes transfers background from the condition image, producing artifacts (as noted by the authors). There is no explicit mechanism to model or synthesize the background consistent with the target frame.
- Details missing or deferred to supplement (e.g., exact λ values, architectural hyperparameters), which may hinder reproducibility.

Quality
The approach is technically sound and builds on established components (U-Net, conditional GANs, residual learning), used appropriately for this problem. The pose mask loss is well motivated. The pairwise discriminator setup is standard for conditional generation. However, empirical support for some design choices (difference map impact, FC layer necessity, no-BN choice) is largely qualitative. Evaluation lacks strong, task-aligned metrics (identity and pose correctness). The user study is helpful but short exposure may limit discriminative power.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The task, architecture, and losses are explained at a high level. Figures aid understanding. Some reference formatting and numbering are inconsistent; a few typos persist. Important implementation details (λ selection, exact architectures) are partly relegated to the supplement. A brief discussion of how missing or inaccurate keypoints are handled would improve clarity.

Originality
Conditioning on a reference image and explicit keypoint poses for person synthesis is a meaningful refinement of contemporaneous conditional generation work. The two-stage decomposition and pose mask loss are incremental but practical. The use of a learned residual “difference map” in Stage II is a modest but plausible novelty in this context. Overall, the paper is more about an effective combination of known techniques than a fundamentally new algorithm.

Significance
If robust, the method could be useful for controlled content creation and data augmentation. The experiments show clear qualitative improvements over simpler baselines and a related multi-view method. However, the lack of strong, quantitative, task-aligned metrics and broader baselines limits the demonstrated impact. Demonstrating identity preservation (e.g., via re-ID performance) and pose accuracy would strengthen significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative measures aligned with goals:
  - Identity preservation: distance in a re-ID embedding; downstream re-ID performance when augmenting with generated images.
  - Pose accuracy: run a pose estimator on generated images and compare to target poses (PCK/OKS).
  - Perceptual quality: FID and LPIPS, ideally on person crops.
- Provide quantitative ablations on: difference-map vs direct prediction; with/without FC layer in Stage I; effect of λ; normalization choices; PatchGAN/multi-scale discriminators.
- Compare against stronger baselines: pix2pix-style cGAN conditioned on pose heatmaps; segmentation/part-based conditioning; warping-based baselines.
- Mitigate background artifacts (e.g., learned person parsing to restrict refinement to foreground; or a separate background synthesis/transfer module).
- Discuss handling of missing/noisy keypoints; analyze robustness to pose estimation errors.
- Consider a perceptual/feature loss to improve identity and texture fidelity.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear problem and practical importance.
- Simple, well-motivated two-stage design with effective conditioning on explicit poses.
- Strong qualitative results and useful ablations (pose embedding, mask loss, two-stage).
- Pairwise conditioning in the discriminator and residual refinement are appropriate and likely broadly applicable.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited quantitative evaluation; lack of identity and pose accuracy metrics.
- Sparse comparisons to prior/stronger baselines; improvements not rigorously benchmarked.
- Some design choices insufficiently justified by experiments.
- Notable artifacts and background transfer issues; no explicit background modeling.
- Reproducibility details partially missing.

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed application paper with incremental methodological contributions. Strengthening the quantitative evaluation and baselines would substantially improve its case.
