

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-stage framework (PG2) for generating person images by conditioning on a reference image and a target pose. The first stage integrates pose information with a reference image using a U-Net variant with a novel pose mask loss, while the second stage refines the coarse output using a conditional GAN that generates a difference map. The authors evaluate their approach on two person datasets (Market-1501 and DeepFashion) and compare with related work.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in person image synthesis and presents a structured two-stage approach that effectively separates global structure learning from detail refinement. The pose mask loss is a novel contribution that helps focus generation on the human body rather than background. The qualitative results show reasonable improvements over single-stage baselines.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Technical Inconsistencies
1. The paper contains multiple formatting errors: "st Conference" should be "The Conference" (p. 1), "the the generator" (p. 8), inconsistent ID formatting (e.g., "ID. 49" vs. "ID 245" in Figure 4), and inconsistent mathematical notation (e.g., "1 +MB" vs. "1 + M_B" in equation 1).

2. Equation (4) contains a typo: "when λ is big, the the generator with a basic L1 loss dominates the training" should be "when λ is big, the L1 loss dominates the training."

3. The paper states "We apply rectified linear unit (ReLU) to each layer except the fully connected layer and the output convolution layer" (p. 8) but provides no justification for excluding ReLU from these layers, which contradicts standard practice.

### Methodological Issues

1. The pose mask MB is computed from the target image (p. 5), which is not available during inference. The paper fails to address how this mask would be generated for real-world applications where the target image is unknown.

2. The paper claims "a state-of-the-art pose estimator [2] is used" but doesn't specify which estimator, why it was chosen, or how its errors impact the overall pipeline. The reference [2] (Zhe Cao et al.) is a pose estimation paper, but the paper doesn't evaluate different pose estimators or their error rates.

3. The paper states "we use a variant of conditional DCGAN" (p. 7) but doesn't clearly explain how it differs from standard conditional DCGAN, making it difficult to assess the novelty.

4. The pose embedding method (using 18 heatmaps) is described, but the paper doesn't provide quantitative comparisons between heatmap embedding and other representations (e.g., coordinate-based or heatmap + encoder approaches) beyond qualitative results.

5. The user study (Section 4.4) uses only 30 users and 55 images per dataset, which is insufficient for statistical significance. The paper should provide confidence intervals or more rigorous statistical analysis.

### Experimental Design

1. The paper uses Market-1501 as a "more challenging re-identification dataset" but Market-1501 is primarily a person re-identification dataset, not a pose transfer dataset. This mischaracterization undermines the experimental setup.

2. The paper claims "the pose mask MB is computed by connecting human body parts and applying a set of morphological operations" (p. 5) but provides no details about these operations or how they're implemented. Without this information, the method cannot be replicated.

3. The paper states "the use of difference maps speeds up convergence" (p. 7) but provides no evidence for this claim. A comparison of training curves for the two-stage vs. one-stage model would be necessary to support this assertion.

4. The paper compares with [36] but fails to provide a detailed analysis of how their approach differs from [36] beyond visual comparisons. The paper should explicitly state the technical differences in their methods.

### Theoretical and Conceptual Issues

1. The paper claims "a wide range of poses is consistent with any given viewpoint making the conditioning less expressive than in our work" (p. 3), but this statement lacks supporting evidence. The paper doesn't quantify how pose keypoints provide more expressive conditioning than viewpoint labels.

2. The paper titles the task "Pose Guided Person Image Generation" but the method is specifically about pose transfer, not general person image generation. This misalignment between title and content is confusing.

3. The paper states "the generator G2 at the second stage aims to generate an appearance difference map that brings the initial result closer to the target image" (p. 7) but doesn't define what constitutes an "appearance difference map" or how it's different from standard GAN outputs.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents a novel two-stage approach for pose-guided person image generation with some promising results, the methodological inconsistencies, missing implementation details, and experimental shortcomings significantly undermine the contribution. The paper's failure to address the pose mask's dependency on target images, the lack of justification for architectural choices, and the numerous formatting errors make it difficult to assess the true novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.

The paper's claim of "novelty" is weakened by the lack of clear technical differentiation from related work and the absence of rigorous ablation studies. The small user study sample size and inconsistent presentation of results further diminish the paper's scientific contribution.

## Recommendation
**Reject**

The paper's technical flaws and presentation issues are too numerous and significant to overlook. Despite the potentially interesting problem and some promising qualitative results, the paper fails to provide sufficient detail to assess the method's validity or novelty. The numerous inconsistencies in the methodology and presentation, combined with the lack of rigorous experimental validation, prevent me from recommending acceptance. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to address these issues before it could be considered for publication.
