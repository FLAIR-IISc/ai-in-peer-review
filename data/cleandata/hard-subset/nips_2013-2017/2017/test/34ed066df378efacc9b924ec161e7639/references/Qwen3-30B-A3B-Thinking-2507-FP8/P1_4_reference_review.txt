

# Peer Review: Pose Guided Person Image Generation

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel task of "pose guided person image generation," where the goal is to synthesize a person image in a specified pose given a reference image and target pose. The authors propose a two-stage framework (PG2) that first generates a coarse pose-aligned image (Stage I) using a U-Net variant with a novel pose mask loss, followed by refinement of appearance details (Stage II) using a conditional GAN that generates a difference map. The approach effectively separates global pose structure generation from fine appearance detail synthesis, addressing a challenging problem that cannot be well solved by end-to-end approaches. The work builds upon but significantly extends prior work on conditional image generation (e.g., [36]) and pose estimation.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound approach with strong experimental validation. The two-stage design is well-motivated by the difficulty of simultaneously generating correct poses and detailed appearance in a single network. The pose mask loss (Eq. 1) is a clever solution to the background interference problem that plagues pose transfer tasks. The use of difference maps in Stage II (instead of generating images from scratch) is particularly well-justified and leads to faster convergence. The experiments on two distinct datasets (Market-1501 and DeepFashion) with both quantitative (SSIM, Inception Score) and qualitative evaluations demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines. The authors also provide a thorough user study (AMT) showing that their refined results are more convincing to human raters. The paper acknowledges limitations, including failure cases with rare poses and gender imbalance, which shows good scholarly integrity.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear from the beginning, and the two-stage framework is logically presented with appropriate diagrams (Figures 2-4). The method section provides sufficient technical detail for reproducibility, though some network architecture specifics are relegated to supplementary materials. The ablation studies (Figures 4-5) effectively demonstrate the contributions of each component (pose embedding, mask loss, two-stage design). The writing is concise and avoids unnecessary jargon, making the paper accessible to a broad NIPS audience.

## Originality
This work makes several meaningful contributions to the field. The core idea of pose-guided person image generation is novel—previous work (e.g., [36]) focused on multi-view synthesis without explicit pose control. The pose mask loss is a novel technical contribution that directly addresses a key challenge in this task. The two-stage design (with Stage I focusing on pose structure and Stage II on appearance details) is a thoughtful solution to the problem's complexity. While the use of GANs for image generation is not new, the specific adaptation to this pose-guided task with the difference map approach is original. The paper properly situates itself within the literature, with comprehensive related work discussion.

## Significance
The problem addressed has clear practical applications in areas like movie making, virtual try-on, and generating training data for pose estimation. The paper demonstrates that their approach produces photo-realistic results that are both pose-correct and visually convincing, which is an important advance over previous work. The two-stage framework provides a principled way to handle the complex interplay between pose structure and appearance details. While the task is specific, the approach could inspire similar strategies for other image generation tasks requiring explicit control over multiple aspects of the output.

## Strengths and Weaknesses
**Strengths:**
- Clear problem formulation and well-motivated two-stage solution
- Novel pose mask loss effectively handles background interference
- Difference map approach in Stage II leads to faster convergence
- Comprehensive experiments on two datasets with multiple evaluation metrics
- Strong qualitative results showing realistic detail generation
- Thorough ablation studies demonstrating each component's contribution

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is from 2017, so it doesn't compare with more recent GAN architectures (e.g., StyleGAN) that might better handle pose transfer
- Limited discussion of how the pose estimation step affects the overall pipeline (e.g., what happens when pose estimation is inaccurate)
- The failure cases (Figure 6) are well-identified but not thoroughly analyzed for potential fixes

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance. This is a strong, well-executed paper that makes meaningful contributions to conditional image generation. The pose-guided person generation task is novel, the two-stage approach is elegant and well-justified, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the approach's superiority over relevant baselines. While some technical details could be expanded (particularly regarding pose estimation robustness), the paper is complete and well-supported. The work has significant potential for practical applications and could inspire further research in controlled image generation.

The paper addresses an important challenge in image synthesis with a clear and effective solution, advancing the state of the art in pose-controlled person image generation. The two-stage framework provides a valuable paradigm for solving complex image generation tasks that require multiple aspects of control.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Novel task formulation with clear practical applications
- Well-designed two-stage approach with strong theoretical justification
- Comprehensive experiments demonstrating clear improvements
- Novel technical contributions (pose mask loss, difference map approach)
- Well-written and clearly organized

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- Limited comparison with more recent GAN architectures
- Some details on pose estimation robustness could be expanded
- The paper is from 2017, so it doesn't incorporate more recent advances

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of generative modeling, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
