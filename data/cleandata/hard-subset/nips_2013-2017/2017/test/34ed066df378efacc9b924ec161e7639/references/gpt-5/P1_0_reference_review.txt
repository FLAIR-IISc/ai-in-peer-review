Summary
The paper addresses pose-guided person image generation: given a reference image of a person and a target pose (as 2D keypoints), synthesize a new image of the same person in the desired pose. The method, PG2, is a two-stage pipeline. Stage I integrates the conditioning image and pose using a U-Net-like encoder-decoder with skip connections and residual blocks; pose is represented as 18 binary heatmaps concatenated to the image. Training uses a masked L1 loss that upweights the person foreground (derived from pose via simple morphology) to de-emphasize background mismatch. This yields a coarse, pose-correct but blurry output. Stage II refines details using a conditional DCGAN whose generator predicts a residual (difference) map between Stage I output and the target; the discriminator receives pairs (condition image, generated/real image). A masked L1 term regularizes the adversarial objective. The approach is evaluated on DeepFashion (256x256) and Market-1501 (128x64), with ablations on pose embeddings (coordinates vs heatmaps), loss (L1 vs masked L1), and one-stage vs two-stage training. Quantitative measures include SSIM/IS and proposed mask-SSIM/mask-IS; a small AMT user study is also reported. Qualitative results are strong; the method outperforms the most related prior work on view-conditioned person synthesis.

Relation to prior work
The task is related to conditional generation (cGANs, CVAEs), view synthesis of people, and pose-conditioned image modeling. The use of pose heatmaps is in line with common pose estimation representations. The two-stage, coarse-to-fine strategy aligns with stacked/iterative refinement GANs and residual learning. Conditioning the discriminator on the input pair follows standard practice in pix2pix-like frameworks. The novelty lies in combining these choices for explicit pose control with a reference appearance, and in simple yet effective engineering choices (mask loss, residual prediction).

Strengths
- Problem importance: explicit control of human pose given appearance is practically useful (editing, data augmentation) and scientifically relevant.
- Clear architecture decomposition: Stage I for geometry, Stage II for details; predicting a residual/difference map is a sensible, efficient refinement strategy.
- Pose representation: simple heatmap concatenation proves effective vs alternatives; well-justified empirical choice.
- Masked L1: foreground-focused loss is intuitive and improves results, especially with background changes.
- Solid qualitative results at 256x256; two datasets with different challenges.
- Ablations: comparisons of pose embeddings, loss types, and two-stage vs one-stage show clear trends.
- Pairwise conditioning in the discriminator helps prevent trivial copying of the condition image.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental: most components (U-Net + pose heatmaps, foreground-weighted L1, pairwise cGAN, coarse-to-fine residual refinement) are known; contributions are primarily in integration and application.
- Quantitative evaluation is limited: SSIM/IS (even mask variants) are weak correlates of perceptual quality for this task. There is no FID/kID or LPIPS, and no metrics for identity preservation, which is central here.
- Baselines: a strong pix2pix-like baseline that directly consumes (reference image, pose heatmaps) with adversarial + L1/perceptual loss is not thoroughly compared; the one-stage G1+D is close but details on parity with standard pix2pix (e.g., multiscale discriminator, perceptual/VGG loss) are missing.
- Lack of analysis on the “difference map” claim (faster convergence, better stability) beyond qualitative statements; no training curves or quantitative convergence evidence.
- Identity preservation is not measured (e.g., via a re-ID or face recognition network). The user study is small and the protocol (1s exposure) is coarse.
- Background handling remains problematic; the model sometimes copies or hallucinates background from the conditioning image, which can mislead the discriminator and users.
- Architectural choices: Stage I uses a fully connected bottleneck which limits scalability and may harm detail preservation; rationale and ablation (with/without FC) could be stronger. No normalization is used; implications and sensitivity are not discussed.

Quality
Technically sound with clear, reproducible training details (optimizers, schedules, architectures summarized). Claims about qualitative improvements are supported, and ablations are informative. However, several key claims (difference-map benefits, identity preservation, robustness to pose estimation errors) lack quantitative support. The proposed mask-SSIM/IS are reasonable but not thoroughly validated.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized; figures (as described) appear helpful. Some reference formatting issues and minor typos. The construction of the pose mask and its role (train-time only) could be emphasized more clearly. More explicit comparison to standard pix2pix configurations would aid readers.

Originality
Moderate. The task formulation (pose-guided person generation with appearance conditioning) is timely and useful. The components individually are established; the integration is coherent but not conceptually groundbreaking.

Significance
Practical impact is good; the method appears to advance the state of the art for this specific task at the time, and is likely to be used or extended by others. Stronger baselines and identity metrics would bolster significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include stronger baselines: pix2pix with (image + pose) input, multiscale discriminator, and VGG perceptual loss; report FID/kID and LPIPS.
- Measure identity preservation using a re-ID network or face recognition (cosine similarity and verification accuracy).
- Provide convergence plots and a controlled ablation comparing direct prediction vs residual prediction.
- Analyze robustness to noisy/missing keypoints and to pose outliers; consider a learned or segmentation-based foreground mask rather than morphological heuristics.
- Replace the FC bottleneck with fully convolutional bottlenecks; report effect on detail/scale.
- Discuss and, if possible, mitigate background leakage (e.g., by compositing on a neutral background or using a background discriminator).
- Release code/models for reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, well-scoped problem with a simple, effective solution.
- Strong qualitative results on two datasets; sensible ablations.
- Clear, reproducible method; contributions likely to be useful to the community.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty; many components are standard.
- Limited quantitative evaluation and missing identity preservation metrics.
- Baselines could be stronger; some claims (residual training benefit) under-evidenced.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a solid engineering contribution with convincing qualitative improvements on a relevant problem, though novelty and quantitative rigor could be stronger. A stronger baseline and identity-focused evaluation would elevate the work.
