## Review of Pose Guided Person Image Generation

This paper proposes a two-stage generative model, PG2, for transferring a person's pose in an image, conditioning on a reference image and a target pose. Stage-I generates a coarse image reflecting the target pose, and Stage-II refines this result using a GAN to produce a more detailed image. The authors explore different pose embedding methods and introduce a pose mask loss to focus generation on the human body. Experiments are conducted on two datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

While the problem addressed – controllable pose transfer – is interesting and potentially useful, and the overall idea of a two-stage approach is reasonable, the paper suffers from numerous shortcomings in its technical detail, presentation, and experimental rigor. The numerous minor flaws accumulate to a degree that outweighs the novelty of the approach, leading me to recommend rejection.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Formulation:** The task of pose-guided person image generation is well-defined and potentially impactful for applications like movie making and data augmentation.
*   **Two-Stage Approach:** Dividing the problem into global structure and detail refinement is a sensible strategy given the complexity of the task.
*   **Pose Mask Loss:** The motivation behind the pose mask loss is clear and addresses a potential issue with background interference.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Technical Depth & Justification:** The architectural choices, particularly regarding the U-Net and DCGAN variants, are largely unexplained. Why a U-Net specifically? Why simplify residual blocks? The rationale behind these design decisions is missing. The removal of the fully connected layer in G2 is stated to help preserve details but lacks any supporting argument or analysis.
*   **Inconsistent Loss Weighting:** The lambda (λ) parameter controlling the L1 loss in Stage-II is mentioned as requiring careful tuning but the details of this tuning, and the chosen value, are absent from the main text (referred to supplementary materials which are inaccessible during review). This makes reproducing and assessing the results difficult.
*   **Ambiguous Pose Embedding:** The description of the heatmap embedding lacks clarity. It is stated that a radius of 4 pixels is used around keypoints, but the choice of radius is arbitrary and not justified. How does this radius affect performance? The comparison to CE and HME is underwhelming as the advantages aren't clearly demonstrated.
*   **Limited Baselines:** Comparing to only one related work ([36]) is insufficient. Many other relevant works on image-to-image translation and pose-conditioned generation (beyond those cited in the Related Work section) should be considered for a more thorough evaluation.
*   **Evaluation Metrics & Analysis:** The reliance on SSIM and IS, alongside acknowledgment of their shortcomings, raises concerns. The introduction of 'mask-SSIM' is a good attempt to address the background issue, but the rationale behind its formulation could be improved. The observed discrepancy between SSIM and visual quality suggests the metrics aren't adequately capturing the perceptual quality of the generated images. The user study has questionable design and reported scores are presented without sufficient context.
*   **Formatting & Presentation:** Throughout the paper, there are minor inconsistencies in formatting and phrasing. For example, "Eq. 1" appears without a preceding clear statement of the equation it references. The descriptions of figures are often vague and don't clearly explain what the reader should be looking for.
*   **Minor Errors:** The use of “G2R” and “R2G” in the user study results section are defined without explanation, making it difficult to interpret the numbers reported. Furthermore, reference [37] is for a dataset, Market-1501, which does not, in itself, constitute a "work" needing citation.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The proposed task is interesting and has potential applications.
*   The two-stage approach offers a reasonable strategy for tackling a complex problem.

**Con:**

*   Lack of technical justification for architectural choices.
*   Insufficient experimental comparison with state-of-the-art methods.
*   Weak evaluation metrics and analysis.
*   Numerous minor flaws in presentation and formatting.
*   The described improvements, while present, aren’t substantial enough to overcome the identified deficiencies.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite a promising idea, the paper lacks the technical depth, experimental rigor, and clarity expected of a NIPS publication. The numerous minor flaws and omissions detract from the overall quality of the work and leave me unconvinced of its significant contribution to the field. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors are encouraged to address the identified weaknesses and resubmit to a future conference. A focus on providing strong justifications for design choices, conducting a thorough experimental evaluation, and improving the clarity of presentation would significantly strengthen the paper.
