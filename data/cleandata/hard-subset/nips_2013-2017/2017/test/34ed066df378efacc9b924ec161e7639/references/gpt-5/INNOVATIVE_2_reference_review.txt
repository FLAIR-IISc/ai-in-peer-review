Summary
This paper tackles a compelling and underexplored task at submission time: generating a person image conditioned on a reference image and an explicit target pose. The proposed PG2 framework decomposes the problem into two stages: (I) a U-Net-like generator integrates the source image with pose keypoint heatmaps to produce a coarse synthesis trained with a pose-masked L1 loss to emphasize the person over background; (II) a conditional DCGAN refines details by predicting a difference map relative to the coarse output, using a pairwise discriminator on (generated,target) vs (real,target) paired with the same masked L1. The authors explore multiple pose embeddings, show the benefit of the mask loss, and demonstrate that the two-stage design yields sharper, more plausible results than a one-stage adversarial model. Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) include ablations, masked SSIM/IS metrics, a small AMT study, qualitative comparisons to VariGAN, and some failure analysis.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned between conditional GANs (e.g., pix2pix) and multiview/pose-conditioned synthesis. Unlike methods that condition on coarse view labels (e.g., “front/side”), PG2 uses explicit body keypoints (18-channel heatmaps), yielding finer control. Compared to contemporaneous multiview clothing/person generation, the explicit target pose and two-stage decomposition are the core differentiators. The idea of pairwise discrimination is in line with conditional GANs, but using a difference-map generator and the pose-masked L1 are notable design choices. The work is well situated in the literature, though stronger comparisons to generic image-to-image baselines using the same inputs would further clarify gains.

Strengths
- Novel problem definition and control signal: Conditioning on explicit pose keypoints offers precise control and a clear path to practical applications (editing, data augmentation).
- Clean, effective two-stage design: Coarse structure first, then adversarial refinement via difference maps. This decomposition is intuitive, stable, and empirically justified.
- Pose embedding via heatmaps: Simple yet effective compared to coordinate embeddings; ablations substantiate the choice.
- Pose-masked L1: Focuses learning on the person and mitigates background mismatch; ablations show consistent improvements.
- Thoughtful evaluation: Ablations, two datasets with different difficulty, masked similarity metrics to isolate person quality, AMT study, and qualitative comparison to the most related prior work.
- Practicality: Uses off-the-shelf pose estimation; shows robustness across typical dataset noise.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation gaps: No quantitative measure of identity preservation (e.g., re-ID feature consistency) or pose accuracy (e.g., PCK on generated images). IS/SSIM (even masked) have known limitations; FID or retrieval-based metrics would strengthen claims.
- Baselines: The strongest generic baseline—pix2pix or similar—taking source image + pose heatmaps as input is not reported. The one staged adversarial variant helps, but a widely used, tuned baseline would be fairer.
- Diversity and stochasticity: Stage-II omits noise; the method appears deterministic given a pose, limiting multimodality in ambiguous regions (occlusions, clothing details).
- Dependence on pose estimator: The paper does not quantify sensitivity to missing/incorrect keypoints, which likely drive some failure modes.
- Minor clarity issues: Some reference formatting and figure integration are rough, and λ selection and sensitivity are relegated to the supplement.

Quality
Technically sound with coherent design choices and ablations supporting key components (pose heatmaps, mask loss, two-stage vs one-stage). The difference-map idea is pragmatic and appears to stabilize training. However, the quantitative evaluation could be more convincing with identity- and pose-aware metrics and stronger baselines. Limitations (e.g., background artifacts, failure on rare poses) are acknowledged.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The method, losses, and architectures are described with sufficient detail to reproduce. Minor editorial issues (reference formatting, figure captions) can be cleaned up.

Originality
High. Using explicit pose keypoint heatmaps as a control signal for person image generation, combined with a two-stage “coarse-to-fine via difference map” refinement and pose-masked loss, represents a meaningful step beyond prior multiview and conditional GAN work at the time.

Significance
High. This work opens a practical and controllable direction for pose-conditioned image synthesis with clear downstream relevance (editing, data augmentation for re-ID/pose estimation). The approach is simple enough to be adopted and extended.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is PG2 to pose estimator errors or missing keypoints? Can you report performance vs PCK noise?
- Can you report identity preservation using a re-ID network or face/body identity features?
- How was λ chosen, and how robust are results to its value?
- How would a pix2pix-style baseline with the same inputs (source + pose heatmaps) compare?
- Could adding a perceptual/feature loss further improve identity fidelity without sacrificing sharpness?

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a timely, well-motivated new task with explicit control.
- Simple, effective two-stage architecture with clear ablations.
- Strong qualitative results across datasets; practical contributions (pose mask, difference map).
- Likely to catalyze follow-up work.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing stronger quantitative baselines and identity/pose-aware metrics.
- Limited analysis of robustness to noisy pose inputs and lack of diversity.

Recommendation
Accept (clear contribution and promising direction despite some evaluation gaps).

Confidence
High.
