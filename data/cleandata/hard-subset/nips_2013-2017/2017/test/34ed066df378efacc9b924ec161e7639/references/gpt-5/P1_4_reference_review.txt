Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles pose-guided person image generation: given a reference image and a target pose (as keypoints), synthesize a new image of the same person in the target pose. The proposed PG2 framework is two-stage. Stage-I uses a U-Net-like encoder-decoder (with residual blocks and a bottleneck FC) to fuse the reference image with pose heatmaps and outputs a coarse image. A pose-mask-weighted L1 loss down-weights background discrepancies. Stage-II refines details using a conditional DCGAN variant: the generator predicts a residual (difference map) between Stage-I output and the ground truth; the discriminator operates on pairs (condition image, generated/real image). Training also includes the pose-mask L1. Experiments on DeepFashion and Market-1501 include ablations on pose embedding (coordinates vs heatmap encoder vs direct heatmap concatenation), losses (L1 vs pose mask), and one-stage vs two-stage. Quantitative metrics include SSIM, Inception Score and their masked variants; an AMT user study is provided. The method is compared to VariGAN [36] and shows superior qualitative results.

This work builds on conditional image generation (CVAE, cGAN), image-to-image translation (pix2pix), text/attribute-conditioned generation, and pose/geometry-conditioned synthesis. Its key differences are explicit keypoint conditioning, a two-stage residual refinement, and a simple pose-mask loss to deemphasize backgrounds.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and practical task (pose-controlled person synthesis) with relevant applications.
- Simple, well-motivated two-stage design that separates global structure from high-frequency refinement; residual learning at Stage-II is a good design choice for stability and convergence.
- Using pose heatmaps concatenated to the image as conditioning is effective and outperforms alternative embeddings in ablations.
- Pose-mask L1 is a simple, effective heuristic to reduce background mismatch and focus learning on the person.
- Pairwise discriminator conditioned on the reference image discourages trivial copying and enforces consistency with the target pose.
- Extensive qualitative results on two datasets; ablations isolate the contributions; user study adds human perceptual evidence.
- Comparison to the closest prior method [36] favors the proposed approach.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation metrics: SSIM/IS (even with masking) are weak proxies for perceptual/identity quality; no FID, no feature-based identity preservation metric (e.g., re-ID embedding similarity), no keypoint accuracy on generated outputs.
- Baselines could be stronger: a direct pix2pix-like model using pose heatmaps and the condition image (with perceptual/feature matching losses) would be a more competitive one-stage baseline; limited comparisons beyond [36].
- Reliance on off-the-shelf keypoints; no analysis of robustness to pose estimation noise or missing joints, and no end-to-end joint training with pose.
- Background handling remains ad hoc; transferring or hallucinating backgrounds is not addressed beyond the mask loss, and artifacts persist on Market-1501.
- Some architectural/training details are underspecified (e.g., exact filter counts, skip connection specifics, mask construction details), affecting reproducibility.
- Deterministic generation with no modeling of multi-modality (e.g., occlusions, ambiguous regions) or diversity.

Quality
- The approach is technically sound and components are coherent. The residual refinement and pairwise discriminator are appropriate choices and supported by ablations.
- Claims are largely supported by experiments; however, stronger quantitative evidence (identity preservation, FID, landmark accuracy) would better substantiate “photo-realistic and identity-preserving” claims.
- The proposed masked SSIM/IS help control for background but are author-defined; reporting standard metrics in addition would aid comparability.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized; the motivation for two stages and the design choices are clear. Figures (as described) are informative.
- Reproducibility would benefit from more implementation specifics (channel sizes, exact mask generation algorithm, lambda selection, training schedules beyond iteration counts). Code availability is not discussed.

Originality
- The task framing (explicit pose control conditioned on a reference image) and the specific combination of techniques (pose heatmaps + mask loss + residual refinement + pairwise D) is a thoughtful and practical contribution. Individually, components are known, but their integration for this task is novel for the time.
- Related work is broadly covered; positioning relative to image-to-image translation and prior pose/view-conditioned works is adequate.

Significance
- The problem is important and the approach is likely to be useful in practice. The two-stage design and pose conditioning are likely to influence subsequent work.
- Demonstrated improvements on two datasets, but generalization to unconstrained scenes remains limited; lack of stronger quantitative identity/perceptual metrics tempers the strength of the claims.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to errors in the pose estimator (missing or inaccurate joints)? Any robustness experiments or qualitative analysis?
- How is λ chosen for the Stage-II loss? Any guidance or sensitivity plots?
- Could you report identity preservation using a re-ID network similarity between generated and target images?
- What are the exact architectural hyperparameters (filters per block) and the mask generation pipeline (morphology parameters)?
- How does Stage-II perform if trained with feature matching or perceptual loss in addition to adversarial and L1?

Suggestions
- Add evaluations with feature-level identity metrics, FID, and optionally keypoint accuracy on generated images.
- Include a stronger one-stage baseline (pix2pix with pose heatmaps and perceptual loss) and/or multi-scale discriminators.
- Explore learned attention/segmentation for foreground emphasis instead of hand-crafted mask, and analyze robustness to pose noise.
- Consider modeling uncertainty/diversity (e.g., stochastic refinement or multimodal outputs).

Pro and con arguments
Pros:
- Clear, practically relevant task with a well-motivated, effective two-stage design.
- Solid ablations demonstrating the value of pose heatmaps, mask loss, and residual refinement.
- Good qualitative results and user study; improvement over closest prior work.

Cons:
- Quantitative evaluation is limited; no identity/perceptual metrics; custom mask-SSIM/IS.
- Baselines could be stronger; limited comparisons.
- Background handling and robustness to pose noise not thoroughly addressed; some reproducibility gaps.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper presents a coherent and useful approach with clear empirical gains and ablations, despite shortcomings in quantitative evaluation and baselines. It should be of interest to the NeurIPS community working on conditional generation and controllable image synthesis.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
