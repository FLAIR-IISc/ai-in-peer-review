## Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of generating person images conditioned on both a reference image and a desired pose. The authors propose a two-stage approach: a first stage leveraging a U-Net to establish a coarse generation reflecting the target pose, and a second stage employing a conditional DCGAN to refine the image and add details. They introduce a pose mask loss to focus generation on the human body and avoid background artifacts.  The work is evaluated on both a low-resolution re-identification dataset (Market-1501) and a high-resolution fashion photo dataset (DeepFashion).

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Task Formulation:** Conditioning image generation on both reference image *and* pose is a novel and potentially useful contribution. It offers more control than existing methods relying on just attributes or text.
* **Divide-and-Conquer Strategy:** The two-stage approach is a sensible way to break down a complex problem. Generating structure first, then details, is a good design choice.
* **Pose Mask Loss:** The proposed pose mask loss is a valuable addition, demonstrably improving results by focusing generation on the foreground and reducing background artifacts.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors evaluate their method on two datasets with different resolutions and complexities, performing ablation studies for key components.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the method and experimental setup. Figures are helpful and well-labeled.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Quantitative Gains:** While the qualitative results are promising, the quantitative improvements reported in Table 1 are modest, and often not statistically significant. SSIM, in particular, isn't a great metric for evaluating generative models and is often misleading.
* **Lack of Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The comparison to Zhao et al. [36] is a good starting point, but a stronger comparison to more recent and relevant GAN-based image manipulation techniques (e.g., StyleGAN-based approaches) would strengthen the paper.
* **Failure Cases:** The authors acknowledge some failure cases (e.g., incomplete limbs, inaccurate gender).  A more in-depth analysis of these failures and potential causes would be valuable.  The discussion of the failures feels somewhat cursory.
* **Limited Scope:**  The application is restricted to person images. While focusing is reasonable, a discussion of the generalizability to other object categories would be useful.
* **User Study Interpretation:** The user study results are somewhat ambiguous.  The G2R score is relatively low on Market-1501, indicating the generated images are often distinguishable from real ones. The explanation provided (background artifacts) feels incomplete.



**Review of Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed method is logically structured and the experiments provide support for the claims. However, the quantitative results are not overwhelmingly convincing. The analysis of the limitations could be more in-depth. (Score: 7/10)
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the method. The organization is logical, and the figures are helpful. (Score: 8/10)
* **Originality:** The combination of reference image and pose conditioning is novel, and the pose mask loss is a useful contribution.  However, the underlying building blocks (U-Net, DCGAN) are standard. (Score: 7/10)
* **Significance:** The potential applications (movie making, data augmentation for pose estimation) are promising, but the current results don’t yet demonstrate a significant breakthrough. A more robust demonstration of image quality and control would be needed to establish greater significance. (Score: 6/10)

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Addresses a novel and interesting problem.
* Proposes a reasonable and well-motivated approach.
* Demonstrates promising qualitative results.
* Includes a helpful pose mask loss.

**Against:**

* Quantitative results are modest.
* Limited comparison to strong baselines.
* Analysis of failure cases could be more thorough.
* Significance isn't fully established.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Weak Accept.**  I believe this paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of image generation. The novelty of the task and the reasonable approach make it a suitable addition to NIPS. However, the limited quantitative gains and the lack of comparison to stronger baselines prevent me from giving a strong acceptance.  I recommend acceptance with the caveat that the authors address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly by including a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art GAN-based methods and providing a more detailed analysis of the failure cases.
