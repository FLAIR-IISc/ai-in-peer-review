Summary and relation to prior work
The paper targets pose-guided person image generation: given a reference image of a person and a target pose (as keypoints), synthesize the person under the new pose. The method (PG2) is two-stage. Stage I is a U-Net-like encoder–decoder that takes the reference image concatenated with 18 pose heatmaps and is trained with an L1 loss weighted by a binary “pose mask” to de-emphasize background. Stage II refines the Stage-I output with a conditional DCGAN: the generator predicts a difference map between the coarse output and the target; the discriminator receives image–condition pairs (generated or real paired with the reference image). Experiments on DeepFashion and Market-1501 include ablations on pose embeddings, mask vs plain L1, and single- vs two-stage training. Quantitative evaluation uses SSIM and Inception Score, both in a masked variant; a small AMT user study is also reported. The paper compares qualitatively to VariGAN.

The general setup relates to conditional GANs and image-to-image translation (e.g., pix2pix), stacked/coarse-to-fine GANs (e.g., LapGAN/StackGAN), and keypoint/structure-conditioned image generation (e.g., Reed et al.). Using pose heatmaps is standard in pose-conditioned tasks; training a second-stage refinement network to predict residuals is also common in refinement networks and super-resolution. Pairwise discriminators are widely used in conditional GANs. The contribution here is a particular instantiation for the person pose-transfer task, plus a simple mask-weighted L1.

Strengths
- Clear, practical task definition and a straightforward conditional formulation using pose heatmaps.
- Coarse-to-fine decomposition is sensible; using a residual/difference map in Stage II is a reasonable engineering choice that appears to stabilize training.
- The pairwise discriminator setup is appropriate for conditioning on a reference image.
- Ablations on pose encoding and the mask vs vanilla L1 provide some insight; qualitative results on DeepFashion are visually plausible.
- Uses an off-the-shelf pose estimator to avoid manual annotations.

Weaknesses and evaluation against criteria

Quality
- Baselines are insufficient. The most relevant baseline—a strong pix2pix-style cGAN that takes the reference image concatenated with pose heatmaps, trained with adversarial + L1 and/or perceptual loss—is missing. Warping/appearance-flow baselines (e.g., learned flow from reference to target pose) are also absent. Comparing only to VariGAN (conditioned on view words) is not compelling for this task.
- Metrics are weak and partly tailored. SSIM/IS (even masked) are known to be poorly correlated with perceptual fidelity; the paper acknowledges that better-looking images can get worse SSIM. There is no FID or LPIPS (foreground-masked), nor any quantitative evaluation of pose accuracy (e.g., PCK by running a pose estimator on generated images and comparing to the target pose).
- No identity preservation metric. Given the stated motivation (e.g., re-ID augmentation), one should evaluate similarity of re-ID embeddings between generated and real images and/or retrieval performance using generated images.
- Robustness is not assessed: no analysis of sensitivity to pose-estimation noise, occlusions, or rare poses beyond a couple of anecdotal failures. No study of generalization to unseen identities with substantially different clothing/textures.
- Background handling remains ad hoc. The mask-weighted L1 is a heuristic; Stage II introduces artifacts and sometimes transfers background from the reference image. There is no quantitative evaluation on foreground-only generation/compositing, nor a strategy to handle background mismatch.
- Experimental design choices (e.g., removing batch norm and all fully connected layers in Stage II, using an FC layer in Stage I) are not justified empirically beyond light ablations. The role of the FC layer in Stage I (which breaks translation equivariance and may overfit) is unclear.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly readable, but important details are deferred to the supplement: channel widths, exact λ for the L1 term, mask construction from keypoints, and training schedules. Figures in the main paper carry heavy qualitative weight but are small and hard to assess without high-resolution.
- References are inconsistently formatted; several citations are incomplete.
- Some architectural choices (no batch norm/dropout) need justification given common practice in GAN training.

Originality
- The two-stage coarse-to-fine design, pose-heatmap conditioning, pairwise discriminators, residual refinement, and mask-weighted L1 are all known ideas. The novelty is primarily in the particular combination for this task; the mask loss is a simple heuristic. As such, the methodological contribution is incremental.

Significance
- The task is interesting and potentially useful, but the empirical evidence does not convincingly advance the state of the art. Gains over a plausible single-stage cGAN baseline are not demonstrated quantitatively, and performance on the more challenging Market-1501 is limited. There is no downstream validation (e.g., does augmenting re-ID training with generated images improve re-ID?).

Questions for the authors
- How does PG2 compare to a strong pix2pix baseline conditioned on reference+pose heatmaps with perceptual loss and a multi-scale discriminator?
- What is the exact λ used in Stage II? How sensitive are results to λ?
- How is the pose mask computed from keypoints precisely, and how robust is it to missing/erroneous keypoints?
- Can you report FID/LPIPS (foreground-masked) and a pose-accuracy metric on generated images?
- Does the method preserve identity under a re-ID embedding? Please report rank-1/CMC changes when using generated images.
- What happens if you inject noise into Stage II to allow multiple plausible solutions for ambiguous regions?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add strong baselines (pix2pix-style, perceptual losses, appearance-flow/warping).
- Evaluate with foreground-masked FID/LPIPS, pose accuracy (PCK), and identity preservation (re-ID features).
- Provide robustness studies to pose noise and occlusion; ablate FC and normalization choices.
- Consider generating only the person with explicit foreground compositing, or conditioning on a background mask.
- Release code and masks to aid reproducibility.

Overall recommendation
- Recommendation: Reject (borderline)
- Justification: The paper addresses a relevant problem and shows promising qualitative results, but the methodological novelty is limited and the evaluation lacks strong baselines and rigorous, task-relevant metrics. As submitted, it does not meet the bar for NeurIPS.

Pros
- Clear task, straightforward approach, reasonable qualitative results on DeepFashion
- Useful ablations (pose encoding; mask vs L1)
- Sensible conditional/discriminator design; residual refinement stabilizes training

Cons
- Missing strong baselines; limited metrics; no identity/p>

- Weak robustness and background handling; limited novelty
- Clarity/reproducibility gaps; incomplete references
