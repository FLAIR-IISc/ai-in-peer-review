**Summary:**
This paper introduces the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), a two-stage generative model for synthesizing an image of a specific person in a novel target pose. The model is conditioned on a source image of the person and a set of target pose keypoints. The first stage employs a U-Net-like generator (G1) to produce a coarse rendering of the person in the target pose, using pose heatmaps as spatial guidance. A key component at this stage is a proposed "pose mask loss," a masked L1 loss designed to focus learning on the foreground person and mitigate the influence of background changes. The second stage uses another U-Net-like generator (G2) within a conditional GAN framework to refine the coarse output from stage one, adding finer details and improving photorealism. The authors evaluate their method on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets, showing qualitative and quantitative results, including ablation studies and a user study. The work is situated within the context of conditional image generation, relating to prior work on attribute/text-conditioned synthesis and view synthesis, but claims novelty in using explicit pose keypoints for fine-grained control over person generation.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a novel and challenging task: generating a person's image in an arbitrary, specified pose given a single source image. This has significant potential applications in areas like data augmentation, virtual try-on, and content creation.
2.  **Methodological Approach:** The proposed two-stage, coarse-to-fine strategy is a logical and effective way to decompose this difficult problem. Separating the generation of global structure (Stage I) from the refinement of high-frequency details (Stage II) is a sensible design choice.
3.  **Qualitative Results:** The qualitative results, particularly on the DeepFashion dataset, are visually impressive. The model appears capable of preserving a person's identity and clothing while successfully transferring them to a new pose, including generating plausible details like facial features and clothing textures.
4.  **Ablation Studies:** The authors provide several useful ablation studies that validate specific design choices, such as the effectiveness of their concatenated pose heatmap embedding over alternative coordinate-based methods and the benefits of the pose mask loss.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, despite its interesting premise, is marred by a substantial number of flaws in its technical description, experimental evaluation, and overall presentation.

1.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:**
    *   The description of the "pose mask loss," claimed as a contribution, is critically lacking in detail. The mask `MB` is described as being computed by "connecting human body parts and applying a set of morphological operations." This is far too vague for reproduction. What specific operations were used? What were their kernel sizes and parameters? Without this information, a core component of the method is not reproducible.
    *   The architectural justification is inconsistent. In G1, a fully-connected layer is included to allow information exchange between distant body parts. In G2, this layer is explicitly removed to "preserve more details." This raises questions: why is detail preservation not a priority in G1? Why is the information exchange bottleneck necessary in G1 but detrimental in G2? This inconsistent reasoning undermines the reader's confidence in the architectural design.
    *   The input to G2 is `(IA, ÎB1)`, meaning the original source image is fed back into the refinement network. This is an important detail, but its implication is not discussed. It suggests that G1 loses significant identity information that G2 must recover from the source, casting doubt on the efficacy of G1's representation.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The quantitative metrics (SSIM, IS) are poorly chosen. The authors themselves correctly note that these metrics often do not correlate with perceptual quality and that blurry images can achieve high SSIM scores. This admission significantly weakens the credibility of the quantitative results presented in Table 1. The use of FID, a more standard and perceptually-aligned metric, would have been more appropriate.
    *   The introduction of "mask-SSIM" and "mask-IS" feels ad-hoc and self-serving. While the motivation to ignore unpredictable backgrounds is understandable, creating custom evaluation metrics that conveniently circumvent a model's primary weakness is not rigorous scientific practice.
    *   The interpretation of the user study is not entirely objective. For the Market-1501 dataset, the one-stage model (G1+D) fools users more often than the full two-stage model (G1+G2+D). The paper attributes this to the full model generating more background artifacts, but this is ultimately a failure of the proposed model. This negative result is downplayed rather than being honestly presented as a limitation.
    *   The comparison to the most related work [36] is insufficient, consisting of only two qualitative examples and lacking any quantitative comparison.

3.  **Presentation and Formatting:** The manuscript exhibits a notable lack of care in its preparation, which obstructs clarity and reflects poorly on the work's polish.
    *   The document contains leftover template text on the first page ("∗Equal contribution.", "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems...").
    *   The bibliography is incomplete and improperly formatted. Multiple references (e.g., [2], [8], [14], [21]) are malformed, ending in "arXiv,, , ,". This is a major oversight.
    *   There are numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "the the generator" in Sec 3.2, footnote `6` in Sec 4.5).
    *   The caption for Figure 4 is excessively long and difficult to parse, while the figure itself is poorly labeled and confusing to interpret.
    *   The footnotes in Table 2 are incorrectly numbered (5G2R instead of G2R).

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper addresses an interesting problem with a promising high-level approach, the execution is severely flawed. The lack of methodological detail for a key contribution (pose mask), the weak and potentially misleading quantitative evaluation, and the sheer volume of formatting errors and typos prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper feels premature and rushed. A strong NIPS submission requires not only a novel idea but also a rigorous, reproducible, and carefully presented execution. This work, in its current state, does not meet that bar. I would encourage the authors to significantly revise the manuscript to address the issues of reproducibility, conduct a more robust evaluation with appropriate metrics, and thoroughly proofread the document before resubmission.
