Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses conditional person image synthesis: given a reference image IA and a target pose PB (as 2D keypoints), synthesize an image of the same person in the target pose. The approach (PG2) is two-stage: Stage-I (G1) is a U-Net-like encoder–decoder (with residual blocks and a fully connected bottleneck) trained with a masked L1 loss to produce a coarse image capturing global structure; Stage-II (G2) is a conditional DCGAN refinement network that allegedly predicts a “difference map” to sharpen details, trained with an adversarial loss plus the same masked L1. Poses are encoded as 18 heatmaps. The discriminator takes pairs (IA, Î) vs (IA, IB). Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) show qualitative improvements and user-study results; quantitative metrics include SSIM, IS, and the authors’ masked variants.

The paper builds on cGANs/pix2pix-like conditioning, U-Net, pose heatmap conditioning, and staged/stacked GANs. Related efforts in multi-view/pose-conditioned synthesis (e.g., pix2pix, StackGAN, pose-guided works) are discussed, and the authors argue for explicit keypoint conditioning and a two-stage coarse-to-fine scheme.

Strengths
- Clear, practically relevant task: explicit pose control with appearance preserved from a reference image.
- Sensible decomposition: coarse pose alignment (Stage-I) + high-frequency refinement (Stage-II).
- Pose encoding via heatmaps is effective and simple; the ablation vs coordinate embeddings is useful.
- Masked L1 is a practical heuristic to reduce background mismatch sensitivity.
- Pairwise discriminator conditioning on (IA, ⋅) is a reasonable design to enforce consistency with the reference identity.
- Qualitative results on two datasets are often compelling; the user study supports improvements on DeepFashion.
- The idea to refine via an additive residual/difference (if correctly implemented) is a good way to stabilize and focus Stage-II.

Weaknesses (technical, methodological, clarity)
- Inconsistency about the “difference map” in Stage-II: The text states G2 outputs a difference to be added to ÎB1, but Eqs. (2–4) and prose use G2(IA, ÎB1) directly as the generated image. If the output is a residual, this should be explicit (ÎB2 = ÎB1 + Δ) in losses and architecture. As written, the formulation is contradictory.
- Mask construction is underspecified: how foreground masks MB are produced (“connecting body parts” and “morphological operations”) lacks algorithmic detail (which joints are connected, kernel sizes/iterations, thresholds, handling of missing keypoints), making reproducibility difficult; mask quality is critical for both loss and evaluation (mask-SSIM/IS).
- Choice of evaluation metrics: SSIM/IS (and masked variants) are known to be poorly aligned with human perceptual quality for conditional image synthesis; no LPIPS/perceptual similarity or human pairwise comparative tests. Mask-IS is nonstandard and may be unstable; details of the masking pipeline for IS are not given.
- Limited and potentially weak baselines: The most direct baseline is a strong pix2pix/cGAN conditioned on [IA; pose heatmaps] with identical capacity and training, but results are primarily compared to internal variants and a less related multi-view method [36]. Quantitative comparisons to a tuned one-stage cGAN (beyond a single G1+D) are thin.
- Adversarial setup may encourage background leakage from IA: D sees (IA, ⋅), but there is no mechanism to discourage copying IA’s background (authors note artifacts/transfer of background in the user study). A foreground-aware discriminator or masking within D could help; this is neither attempted nor analyzed.
- Stage-I architecture includes a fully-connected bottleneck; for 256×256 this can explode parameter count and may harm spatial fidelity. No parameter counts, memory considerations, or ablation on the FC layer’s necessity are provided (Stage-II removes it).
- Hyperparameters are under-specified: the value of λ (L1/adversarial tradeoff) is deferred to the supplement; the main paper lacks its numeric value(s) and selection protocol. Learning rate schedules, number of epochs (iterations alone are given), and data sampling details are omitted.
- Pose heatmap radius is fixed at 4 pixels, but image resolutions differ (256×256 vs 128×64). No justification or scaling analysis; this can materially affect the difficulty of the pose integration.
- Data protocol ambiguity: For DeepFashion, are identities disjoint across train/test? For Market-1501, pair sampling strategy (all pairs per identity?) is only loosely described; potential distributional bias is not analyzed.
- User study design leaves confounders: Are generated images from different methods mixed in the same session? Were workers exposed to both datasets in the same HIT? No inter-rater reliability, confidence intervals, or statistical tests reported.
- Numerous clarity/formatting issues that impede readability and reproducibility:
  - Inline footnotes unresolved (e.g., “blurry results2”, “flip for both datasets3”), dangling “∗Equal contribution.”, malformed conference header (“st Conference …”).
  - Typographical errors (“the the generator”), inconsistent symbol accents (ÎB vs ÎB1), inconsistent capitalization.
  - Missing details on normalization/output activations (Are images in [−1,1]? tanh at output?), padding, and exact filter/channel schedules.
  - Discriminator conditioning mechanism not specified (concatenate along channels? spatial tiling of IA?).
  - References are severely incomplete/malformed (missing years/venues/pages; multiple “arXiv,, , ,”; inconsistent titles).
- Threat to problem validity: At training/evaluation, target poses are inferred from the target images; thus the test setup assumes access to target keypoints extracted from the ground-truth target. Practical use-cases (user-specified keypoints, off-the-shelf mocap) are not demonstrated, nor is robustness to noisy/missing keypoints quantified.

Quality
Technically plausible with solid qualitative evidence, but the Stage-II residual inconsistency, missing details (masks, λ, activations), and limited baselines weaken the empirical rigor and reproducibility.

Clarity
Readable at a high level but marred by many formatting/typo issues and insufficient implementation specifics.

Originality
Moderate. Combines known components (pose heatmaps, U-Net, cGANs, coarse-to-fine). Masked L1 and residual refinement are incremental.

Significance
The task is significant and the results promising, but the evaluation and baselines are not strong enough to convincingly establish SOTA or broad impact.

Questions for the authors
1) Clarify Stage-II: Is G2 output added to ÎB1 (residual)? If so, please correct Eqs. (2–4) and describe the skip.
2) Provide full mask-generation algorithm and code-level details; how do you handle missing/low-confidence keypoints?
3) What λ values were used on both datasets? How sensitive are results to λ?
4) How is D conditioned on IA exactly (concatenation, projection, or other)? Is any foreground mask used in D?
5) Do you scale the pose heatmap radius with resolution? Did you try Gaussian heatmaps?
6) Provide an ablation of the FC bottleneck vs fully-convolutional Stage-I.
7) Compare against a strong pix2pix baseline with identical capacity and training, and report LPIPS or a perceptual metric, plus a pairwise user study.

Minor corrections and formatting
- Fix unresolved footnotes and malformed conference header.
- Clean and complete the reference list (venues, years, pages).
- Fix typos (e.g., “the the”, capitalization), and ensure consistent notation (ÎB, ÎB1, IB).
- Specify image normalization, output activation, padding/stride details, and parameter counts.

Pros
- Clear, important problem; intuitive two-stage design
- Effective pose heatmap conditioning
- Useful masked loss to reduce background confounds
- Strong qualitative improvements, especially on DeepFashion

Cons
- Stage-II residual inconsistency; missing crucial implementation details
- Weak/limited baselines and questionable metrics
- D encourages background copying; no mitigation
- Numerous clarity/formatting/reference issues

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea and results are promising, but the paper in its current form has too many methodological ambiguities, incomplete evaluations, and presentation issues for NeurIPS standards. Addressing the inconsistencies, strengthening baselines/metrics, and improving clarity could make it competitive. Confidence: high.
