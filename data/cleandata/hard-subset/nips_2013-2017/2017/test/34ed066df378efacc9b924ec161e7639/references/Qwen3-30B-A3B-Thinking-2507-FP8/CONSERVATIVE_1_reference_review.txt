

**Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"**

This paper proposes a two-stage framework for person image generation conditioned on a reference image and target pose. The authors argue that end-to-end pose transfer is challenging due to simultaneous requirements for pose structure and appearance details, and propose a "divide-and-conquer" strategy with Stage-I generating coarse pose-structured images and Stage-II refining details.

**Strengths**
The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the two-stage approach. The pose mask loss is a reasonable adaptation to focus generation on the human body rather than background. The method is evaluated on two relevant datasets (Market-1501 and DeepFashion) with qualitative results showing reasonable pose transfer. The user study provides additional validation of the method's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses**
The paper's most significant weakness is the lack of strong baselines for comparison. The authors compare primarily against Zhao et al. (2017), but omit established image-to-image translation methods like Pix2Pix (Isola et al., 2017), CycleGAN (Zhu et al., 2017), and other recent GAN-based approaches. Without these comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether the two-stage approach truly advances the field beyond standard image translation techniques.

The claimed novelty is questionable. The pose mask loss is a minor modification to standard L1 loss (used in many image translation papers), and the two-stage approach resembles other multi-stage generation frameworks (e.g., progressive GANs). The paper fails to clearly articulate how their method differs from established techniques in the field.

The quantitative results are inconsistent with the qualitative claims. While the two-stage model (G1+G2+D) produces sharper images qualitatively, Table 1 shows comparable or even worse SSIM and IS scores than the one-stage model (G1+D). The authors acknowledge this discrepancy but provide insufficient explanation, undermining their core claim.

The failure analysis is limited to only two examples, without systematic investigation of failure modes (e.g., rare poses, gender imbalance). The paper states that "the condition person wears a long-sleeve jacket of similar color to his inner short-sleeve, making the generated cloth look like a mixture of both," but doesn't analyze why this occurs or how to prevent it.

Implementation details are insufficient for reproducibility. The paper mentions "N residual blocks" but doesn't specify N for different image sizes, and the exact architecture of the U-Net variants isn't fully described. The pose estimation pipeline is mentioned but not detailed.

**Quality Assessment**
The technical approach is sound but not innovative. The experimental evaluation is incomplete without strong baselines, and the inconsistent quantitative results weaken the paper's contribution. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their metrics (SSIM/IS not correlating with photo-realism) but fail to provide more meaningful evaluation.

**Originality and Significance**
The task of pose-guided person image generation is novel, but the solution is a combination of established techniques with minimal innovation. The paper doesn't demonstrate that their approach significantly outperforms existing methods, which is required for meaningful contribution at NIPS. The significance is limited to a specific application domain without broader implications for generative modeling.

**Recommendation**
Reject. The paper presents a reasonable approach to a specific problem but lacks sufficient evidence of significant advancement over existing image translation techniques. The contribution appears incremental rather than transformative, and the evaluation is incomplete without strong baselines.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**
*Pro:*
- Well-motivated problem statement
- Clear two-stage framework
- Thorough qualitative results
- Reasonable pose mask loss adaptation

*Con:*
- Lack of strong baselines (Pix2Pix, CycleGAN)
- Inconsistent quantitative results
- Questionable novelty (minor modifications to established methods)
- Insufficient implementation details
- Limited failure analysis

**Suggestions for Improvement**
1. Add comparisons to established image-to-image translation methods (Pix2Pix, CycleGAN)
2. Provide thorough explanation of qualitative-quantitative discrepancy
3. Clarify how the method differs from existing approaches
4. Include more detailed implementation specifications
5. Conduct systematic failure analysis to identify limitations

The paper addresses a useful application but fails to demonstrate that its approach represents a meaningful advancement over the state of the art. Without stronger evidence of significant contribution, this work does not meet NIPS standards for acceptance.
