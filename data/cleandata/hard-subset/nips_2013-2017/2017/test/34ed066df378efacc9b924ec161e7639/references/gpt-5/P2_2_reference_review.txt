Summary
The paper tackles pose-guided person image generation: given a reference image of a person and a target pose (keypoints), synthesize the person in the new pose while preserving appearance. The approach, PG2, is two-stage. Stage I is a U-Net-like encoder-decoder that takes the reference image concatenated with 18-channel pose heatmaps and produces a coarse result optimized with a masked L1 loss that emphasizes foreground human pixels. Stage II is a conditional GAN that refines the coarse result by predicting a residual (“difference map”) conditioned on the Stage I output and the reference image; the discriminator sees pairs (condition image, output) vs (condition image, ground truth). The method is evaluated on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64), with ablations on pose embedding, loss (L1 vs masked L1), and one-stage vs two-stage models, plus a user study and qualitative comparison to Zhao et al. (2017).

Relation to prior work
The work builds on GANs and image-to-image translation (e.g., U-Nets and conditional GANs such as pix2pix), and on conditioning by structural cues (keypoints). The idea of using pose keypoints as explicit conditioning is close to concurrent efforts in controllable generation, but here it is applied to full-body person synthesis with a simple, practical pipeline. The pairwise conditional discriminator is standard in pix2pix. The residual/difference-map refinement stage echoes multi-stage GANs and residual learning from super-resolution/video prediction. The authors cite relevant works, though a direct quantitative baseline against pix2pix-like one-stage conditional GANs is missing.

Quality
- Soundness: The method is technically reasonable and internally consistent. The masked L1 loss is a simple and effective heuristic to downweight unknown background; the two-stage decomposition mitigates the difficulty of jointly solving for pose and appearance details; and using pose heatmaps as spatial channels is a strong design choice.
- Empirical support: The ablations are informative: heatmap concatenation outperforms coordinate embeddings; masked L1 improves results on cluttered backgrounds; two-stage modeling gives sharper details than one-stage adversarial training. User studies corroborate qualitative claims on DeepFashion. However, several aspects limit the strength of evidence:
  - Baselines: No quantitative comparison to a strong one-stage pix2pix baseline conditioned on pose heatmaps and the reference image with the same loss; the “G1+D” baseline differs architecturally and may not be competitive.
  - Metrics: SSIM/IS (and their masked variants) are imperfect for perceptual realism; FID or LPIPS would be more appropriate. Identity preservation is not quantitatively assessed (e.g., via re-id embeddings or recognition scores).
  - Robustness: No analysis of sensitivity to pose estimator errors, and failure cases suggest issues with rare poses and gender imbalance but without systematic quantification.
  - Generalization: The approach is deterministic and trained on paired images of the same identity; diversity/uncertainty is not modeled.
- Reproducibility: Many architectural and training details are provided (blocks, learning rates, iterations, batch sizes). Pose mask construction is described qualitatively; exact morphological operations or code availability are not specified.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The motivation for two-stage training and masked loss is well explained; figures help. There are minor typos and some redundancy. A more explicit description of the discriminator input (it appears identical in spirit to pix2pix’s concatenation) and the exact mask-generation pipeline would further aid reproducibility.

Originality
- Task framing (explicit pose-guided person synthesis from a reference image) was relatively new and practically compelling at the time.
- Technical novelty is moderate: pose heatmap concatenation and conditional pairwise discriminators are established; two-stage refinement and residual prediction have precedents. The masked L1 weighting is a straightforward heuristic. The combination tailored to this task is sensible engineering rather than a fundamentally new theory.
- The difference-map GAN for refinement is a neat and well-justified design choice, though conceptually incremental.

Significance
The problem is important for controllable generation and has downstream uses (content creation, data augmentation). The qualitative gains are clear, especially on DeepFashion; on Market-1501 the task is harder but improvements remain visible. Despite limitations in baselines and metrics, the paper is likely to influence subsequent work on pose-conditioned synthesis and multi-stage refinement, and provides a practical recipe for the community.

Pros
- Clear problem definition; practical and impactful task.
- Simple, effective architecture: pose heatmaps, two-stage training, residual refinement.
- Thoughtful ablations isolating contributions of embedding, loss, and staging.
- User study and masked evaluation to account for background differences.

Cons
- Missing strong quantitative baseline against a canonical pix2pix-style one-stage model with identical conditioning.
- Weak metrics for realism and identity preservation (no FID/LPIPS/re-id scoring).
- No analysis of pose estimation noise or mask quality sensitivity.
- Deterministic model; no diversity or uncertainty quantification.
- Limited quantitative comparison to related work; comparison to [36] is purely qualitative.

Questions for authors
- How does PG2 compare quantitatively to a pix2pix baseline using the same inputs (reference image + pose heatmaps) and masked L1 + adversarial loss?
- Can you report FID and LPIPS (masked and unmasked), and an identity-preservation metric (e.g., re-id Rank-1 or cosine similarity in a re-id embedding)?
- How sensitive are results to pose estimator errors and to the mask generation procedure?
- Does adding a perceptual/content loss help Stage II without reintroducing blur?
- Can modest stochasticity (noise injection or dropout) yield diverse but plausible outputs for ambiguous poses?

Recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. While the components are mostly known, the paper addresses a timely, useful task with a coherent design and convincing qualitative results, and provides clear ablations and insights (pose heatmaps, masked loss, residual refinement). Strengthening baselines and metrics would further solidify the contribution, but overall the paper meets the NeurIPS criteria reasonably well: soundness and clarity are good, originality is moderate, and significance is moderate to high.
