

# Peer Review: "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces a novel two-stage framework called Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) for generating person images conditioned on a reference image and a target pose. The authors address the challenging problem of pose transfer—transforming a person from one pose to another while preserving identity and appearance details. The key insight is that direct end-to-end pose transfer is difficult due to the need to simultaneously generate correct pose and detailed appearance, so they divide the problem into two stages: Stage-I generates a coarse result capturing global structure, and Stage-II refines this with high-frequency details.

The work relates well to recent advances in conditional image generation (e.g., [15, 22, 23, 32]) but makes a significant contribution by using pose keypoints as a direct conditioning signal rather than abstract attributes or text. The authors compare with Zhao et al. [36], the most similar work on multi-view person synthesis, and demonstrate clear improvements.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and Well-Motivated Problem**: The paper identifies a meaningful task with practical applications in fashion, entertainment, and pose estimation data augmentation. The motivation is compelling and well-justified.

2. **Effective Two-Stage Approach**: The division into pose structure generation followed by detail refinement is well-justified and empirically validated. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate that this approach outperforms a one-stage alternative.

3. **Innovative Pose Representation**: The use of pose heatmaps as input (rather than raw coordinates) is simple yet effective, avoiding expensive pose annotation while providing a natural representation for convolutional networks.

4. **Pose Mask Loss**: This clever solution to focus generation on the human body rather than background is well-motivated and significantly improves results, as shown in both qualitative and quantitative evaluations.

5. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation**: The paper evaluates on two diverse datasets (DeepFashion and Market-1501) with comprehensive qualitative, quantitative, and user study results. The user study on Amazon Mechanical Turk provides valuable human perception validation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison to Recent Work**: While the paper compares to Zhao et al. [36], it misses several more recent works in pose-guided image generation (e.g., works from CVPR/ICCV 2017-2018). The authors should clarify how their work differs from similar approaches like [19, 35] that also use two-stage frameworks for pose transfer.

2. **Theoretical Justification**: The paper makes strong empirical claims but lacks theoretical justification for why the two-stage approach is superior. A more principled argument about learning dynamics or computational complexity would strengthen the contribution.

3. **Failure Analysis**: The two failure cases shown in Figure 6 are not thoroughly analyzed. The authors could discuss how these failures relate to data limitations (e.g., rare poses, gender imbalance) and whether these issues are inherent to the problem or solvable with better data or architecture.

4. **Metric Limitations**: While the paper acknowledges that SSIM/IS don't perfectly correlate with human perception (noting their method has lower SSIM but better visual quality), they rely heavily on these metrics. Including more perceptual metrics like FID would provide a more complete evaluation.

5. **Reproducibility Details**: Some implementation specifics are missing, such as exact architecture dimensions for G1 and G2 beyond the general description, and details about the pose estimator used for generating keypoint heatmaps.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a clear contribution to conditional image generation with a novel and effective approach to pose-guided person image synthesis. The two-stage framework with pose mask loss is well-designed and empirically validated. The problem is significant for both practical applications and academic research in image generation.

While the paper has some weaknesses in comparison to recent work and theoretical justification, these do not undermine the core contributions. The experimental evaluation is robust, and the method demonstrates clear improvements over existing approaches.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses an important problem with a well-executed solution that advances the state of the art in conditional image generation. The work has clear practical applications and makes a meaningful contribution to the field.

## Pro/Con Acceptance Summary

*Pro:*
- Novel problem formulation with compelling practical applications
- Effective two-stage approach with strong empirical validation
- Simple yet effective pose representation and mask loss
- Comprehensive evaluation on two diverse datasets with human evaluation
- Clear and well-structured presentation

*Con:*
- Limited comparison to more recent work in pose-guided generation
- Insufficient theoretical justification for the two-stage approach
- Limited failure analysis and discussion of limitations
- Overreliance on SSIM/IS metrics that don't fully capture perceptual quality
- Some missing implementation details for reproducibility

The paper is of high quality and makes a significant contribution to the field of conditional image generation, making it appropriate for NeurIPS.
