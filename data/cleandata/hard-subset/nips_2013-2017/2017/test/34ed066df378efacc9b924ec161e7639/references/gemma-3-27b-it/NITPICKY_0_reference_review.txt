## Review of Pose Guided Person Image Generation

This paper proposes a two-stage GAN-based framework, PG2, for transferring a person’s pose in an image, conditioned on a reference image and a desired pose represented by keypoints. The approach divides the problem into a coarse structure generation stage (Stage-I) and a refinement stage (Stage-II) utilizing U-Nets and DCGANs, respectively. A novel pose mask loss is introduced to focus the generation on the human body. The paper is generally well-motivated, aiming to address a practical problem with potential applications in movie making and data augmentation. However, despite the novelty of the task framing and the detailed methodology, numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies detract from the overall quality and warrant a rejection recommendation.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Novel Task Formulation:** Conditioning image generation on both a reference image *and* a pose is a reasonable and potentially useful problem setup.
* **Divide-and-Conquer Strategy:** The two-stage approach is conceptually sound, addressing the difficulty of simultaneous pose and appearance generation.
* **Pose Mask Loss:** The proposed mask loss is a sensible addition, aiming to improve focus on the person’s appearance and mitigate background artifacts.
* **Detailed Methodology:** The paper provides a fairly comprehensive description of the network architectures and training procedures.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Rigorous Ablation:** While several variants are explored (different pose embeddings, loss functions, stages), the ablation studies lack sufficient detail and statistical significance. Results are often presented qualitatively without supporting quantitative data. For example, the qualitative results in Figure 4 do not consistently demonstrate clear advantages of one method over another, and the claims of improvement are not substantiated with numerical scores within the figure caption.
* **Inconsistent Reporting & Minor Errors:** The paper suffers from several inconsistencies and minor errors. The claim that L1 loss generates blurry results is a generalization, and not universally true; its impact depends on the network architecture and data.  The description of the residual block simplification is unclear – what exactly was simplified?  The use of "G2" as a shorthand for the Stage-II generator is inconsistent - sometimes used in text and other times simply referred to as "the generator."  There's a typo: "few man samples" in the failure analysis (page 13).
* **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** While a comparison to [36] is included, it’s limited. A more thorough comparison to other image-to-image translation techniques, even if not specifically pose-guided, would strengthen the evaluation.  The choice of [36] as the primary baseline appears somewhat arbitrary.
* **Limited Quantitative Evaluation:** The quantitative results (Table 1) are presented without sufficient context.  The relative performance difference between methods is often small, and the statistical significance is not addressed.  The use of mask-SSIM and mask-IS is a reasonable attempt to address the issue of differing backgrounds, but the impact of this masking is not sufficiently analyzed.
* **User Study Issues:** The user study results are confusing and partially contradict the qualitative and quantitative results. The low G2R scores on Market-1501, despite visually apparent improvements, raise concerns. The interpretation of R2G scores is also unclear.
* **Failure Case Analysis:** The failure case analysis feels superficial and doesn’t offer concrete insights into the limitations of the approach or potential avenues for improvement. Simply stating “rare training data” or “imbalance of training data” is insufficient.
* **Formatting & Writing Quality:** The writing is sometimes awkward and verbose. The organization could be improved for better flow.  For instance, the description of the pose embedding methods feels disjointed. The reference list formatting is not consistent with NIPS guidelines.

**Specific Concerns & Questions:**

* **Pose Embedding:** Why was heatmap embedding ultimately chosen over coordinate embedding?  The qualitative results in Figure 4 show little difference, yet the paper states CE is clearly worse.
* **Loss Weight (λ):** The sensitivity analysis of λ is relegated to the supplementary materials, making it difficult to assess its impact.
* **Network Architecture Details:** The number of filters increasing linearly "with each block" is vague. Specific numbers would be helpful.
* **Stage-I vs. One-Stage:** While qualitatively demonstrating the two-stage approach’s benefit, the quantitative metrics don’t reflect this advantage, raising questions about the validity of the observed improvement.
* **Dataset Split:** The random selection of 12,800 pairs for testing – is this consistent across all experiments and compared methods?

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Against Acceptance:**

* Numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies in reporting.
* Insufficient rigor in ablation studies and quantitative evaluation.
* Limited baseline comparison.
* Questionable user study results.
* Formatting and writing quality issues.

**For Acceptance:**

* Novel task formulation.
* Sensible two-stage approach.
* Introduction of a potentially useful pose mask loss.

**Overall Recommendation:**

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting idea and a relatively well-developed methodology, the numerous minor flaws, inconsistencies, and lack of rigorous evaluation ultimately undermine its contribution. The weaknesses outweigh the strengths, and the paper, in its current form, does not meet the quality standards expected for NIPS publication.  Significant revisions, addressing the concerns outlined above, would be necessary for reconsideration. The attention to detail required for a strong NIPS submission is currently lacking.
