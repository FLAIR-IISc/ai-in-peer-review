

# Peer Review: Pose Guided Person Image Generation

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel two-stage approach called Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) for generating realistic person images by conditioning on a reference image and a target pose. The authors address the challenging task of pose transfer by dividing it into two stages: (1) a coarse generation of global human body structure using a U-Net variant with pose embedding and a novel pose mask loss, and (2) refinement of appearance details using a conditional GAN that generates a difference map rather than the full image. The method is evaluated on two datasets (Market-1501 and DeepFashion) and compared with several baselines and related work [36].

The paper builds upon previous GAN-based image generation methods [6, 21] and conditional image generation [18, 22, 24, 32], but introduces a more explicit pose condition using keypoint heatmaps rather than abstract attributes. It's related to [36], which also deals with multi-view person image synthesis, but the authors argue their approach is more flexible and expressive through explicit pose guidance.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Problem Formulation**: The paper identifies a meaningful gap in the literature for explicit pose-guided person image generation and formulates it as a well-defined problem. This is a significant contribution to conditional image generation.

2. **Technical Innovation**: The two-stage approach effectively addresses the difficulty of simultaneously generating correct poses and detailed appearance. The use of a difference map in stage-II is particularly clever and leads to faster convergence compared to generating images from scratch.

3. **Pose Mask Loss**: The introduction of the pose mask loss to focus generation on the human body rather than background is a simple but effective idea that significantly improves results, as demonstrated in the experiments.

4. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper provides thorough quantitative (SSIM, IS) and qualitative results, including ablation studies on pose embeddings and losses, as well as a user study. The comparison with [36] is well-executed.

5. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized, with clear figures and explanations of the methodology. The two-stage architecture is easy to understand.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Recent Work**: While the paper compares with [36], it doesn't compare with more recent image-to-image translation methods (e.g., [1, 2, 3]) that might have better performance on similar tasks. The comparison with [36] is somewhat limited as [36] uses different input conditions (textual pose descriptions rather than keypoint heatmaps).

2. **Lack of Computational Analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost or training time of the two-stage approach compared to single-stage methods. This is important for practical applications, especially since the second stage adds significant complexity.

3. **Limited Failure Analysis**: The paper mentions two failure cases (rare poses and gender imbalance), but doesn't thoroughly analyze why these failures occur or how to address them. A deeper analysis would strengthen the paper.

4. **Reproducibility Concerns**: The paper mentions using a state-of-the-art pose estimator [2] but doesn't specify which one or provide details about how pose estimation affects results. This could impact reproducibility.

5. **Quantitative Metrics Limitations**: While the paper acknowledges that SSIM doesn't always correlate with perceptual quality, it doesn't provide more nuanced metrics. The user study is good but limited to 30 users, which might not be statistically robust.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written, technically sound paper that makes a clear contribution to the field of conditional image generation. The problem formulation is novel and the two-stage approach is well-motivated and effective. The experiments are thorough and the results are convincing.

The main weaknesses are the limited comparison with more recent image-to-image translation methods and the lack of analysis on computational efficiency. However, these don't undermine the core contribution of the paper.

The paper meets NIPS standards for quality, clarity, originality, and significance. It's a solid contribution that advances the state of the art in pose-guided person image generation.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. It's a strong contribution to the field with clear technical merit, good experimental validation, and meaningful innovation.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Novel problem formulation for pose-guided person image generation
- Effective two-stage approach with clear technical innovation (difference map, pose mask loss)
- Comprehensive evaluation including ablation studies, quantitative metrics, and user study
- Clear writing and well-organized paper
- Demonstrated improvement over the state of the art

## Arguments for Rejection (None Significant)
- The limited comparison with recent image-to-image translation methods is a minor concern, not a fundamental flaw
- The lack of computational efficiency analysis is important but doesn't invalidate the core contribution

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. Compare with more recent image-to-image translation methods (e.g., [1, 2, 3]) to provide a more complete picture of the state of the art.
2. Include a discussion of computational efficiency and training time compared to single-stage approaches.
3. Provide more details about the pose estimation method used and its impact on results.
4. Expand the failure analysis to include more examples and potential solutions.

## Conclusion

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of conditional image generation with a novel approach to pose-guided person image synthesis. The two-stage architecture with the pose mask loss is particularly effective, and the experiments convincingly demonstrate its advantages over existing methods. While there are minor limitations in the comparison with the state of the art and the analysis of computational efficiency, these do not detract from the paper's core contributions. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
