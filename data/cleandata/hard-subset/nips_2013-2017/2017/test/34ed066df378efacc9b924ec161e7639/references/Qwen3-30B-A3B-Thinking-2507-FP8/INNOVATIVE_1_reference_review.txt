

# Review for "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

This paper introduces a novel task of generating person images conditioned on both a reference image and a target pose, rather than just one or the other. The authors propose a two-stage framework called Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) that first generates a coarse image capturing the global structure of the target pose (Stage I), then refines it to add appearance details (Stage II). The key innovations include: (1) using pose heatmaps instead of raw keypoint coordinates for pose embedding, (2) introducing a pose mask loss that weights human body regions more heavily during training, and (3) using a difference map approach in Stage II to refine the coarse result rather than generating the entire image from scratch.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of image generation by identifying a meaningful gap in the literature: most pose transfer works either condition on a single image (with limited pose control) or require extensive pose annotations. By using a state-of-the-art pose estimator to obtain keypoints and encoding them as heatmaps, the authors enable more flexible and explicit pose control without expensive manual annotations. The pose mask loss is particularly clever, addressing the challenge of background interference during pose transfer by focusing the model's attention on the human body.

The work is well-positioned in the literature. While there are related works on conditional image generation (e.g., [32, 36]), the authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from prior work by using pose as a direct condition rather than as an implicit attribute. The comparison with Zhao et al. [36] in Figure 5 is compelling, showing that their method generates significantly more realistic and detailed person images, especially for complex poses.

The paper's strengths are substantial:
1. **Novelty**: The core task and two-stage approach represent a genuinely new direction in image generation research. The pose-guided conditioning is a bold and well-motivated extension of existing conditional generation techniques.
2. **Technical Contribution**: The pose mask loss and difference map refinement are elegant solutions to specific challenges in this task.
3. **Experimental Rigor**: The ablation studies on pose embedding methods and loss functions are thorough, and the results on two distinct datasets (Market-1501 and DeepFashion) demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines.
4. **Applications**: The paper clearly articulates meaningful applications in movie making, human pose estimation data augmentation, and image retrieval.

The paper has minor weaknesses that don't undermine its core contribution:
- The user study on Market-1501 shows lower scores than on DeepFashion, which is understandable given the more challenging dataset, but the authors could more explicitly discuss this limitation.
- The failure cases shown in Figure 6 (incomplete body generation, gender errors) highlight areas for future work, but don't diminish the paper's current contribution.
- The pose mask generation process could be slightly better explained, though it's clear from the examples.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper highly compelling. The core idea of conditioning image generation on both a reference image and a target pose is genuinely novel and opens up new research directions in image synthesis. While the methodology is sound, it's the conceptual novelty that makes this paper stand out. The two-stage framework is well-justified, the pose mask loss is clever, and the difference map approach in Stage II is a thoughtful innovation that improves training efficiency and result quality.

The paper does not represent an incremental improvement but rather a significant new direction in the field. The authors have clearly identified a gap in the literature and proposed a novel solution with strong experimental validation. The results consistently outperform the most relevant baseline [36], with clear qualitative and quantitative improvements.

Compared to many NIPS submissions that offer incremental improvements to existing frameworks, this paper makes a bold conceptual leap that could inspire future work in conditional image generation. The problem statement is well-motivated, the solution is novel, and the results convincingly demonstrate the value of the approach.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The novelty of the core task and the elegance of the proposed solution outweigh the minor methodological limitations. This work represents a meaningful step forward in the field of conditional image generation with clear applications in real-world domains. The authors have made a significant contribution that will likely influence future research in this area.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction. The experiments are comprehensive, and the ablation studies effectively validate the key components of the proposed approach. While the methodology is not perfect, the novelty and significance of the contribution make it a strong candidate for NIPS.
