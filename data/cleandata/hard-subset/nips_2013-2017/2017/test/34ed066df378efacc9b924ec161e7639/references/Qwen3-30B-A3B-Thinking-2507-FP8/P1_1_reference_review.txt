

# Peer Review: Pose Guided Person Image Generation

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel task of generating person images by conditioning on both a reference image and a target pose, which they term "Pose Guided Person Image Generation." The authors propose a two-stage approach (PG2) to address this challenging problem: Stage-I integrates the reference image with the target pose to generate a coarse image capturing global structure, while Stage-II refines this coarse result using adversarial training to add appearance details. The key innovations include a novel pose mask loss that focuses generation on the human body rather than background, and a two-stage architecture that divides the complex generation task into manageable subproblems. The approach is evaluated on two person datasets (Market-1501 and DeepFashion) and shows clear improvements over single-stage alternatives and the most similar prior work [36].

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-justified methodology. The two-stage approach is particularly well-motivated by the difficulty of simultaneously generating correct pose and detailed appearance in a single step. The pose mask loss (Eq. 1) is a clever solution to the background interference problem, and the ablation studies on pose embedding methods (coordinate vs. heatmap) and loss functions (standard L1 vs. pose mask loss) provide strong evidence for their design choices.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with both quantitative metrics (SSIM, Inception Score) and qualitative results. The user study on AMT provides valuable human evaluation. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, including failure cases related to rare poses and gender imbalance in training data.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The problem statement is well-motivated with compelling application examples (movie making, pose estimation). The methodology is explained with precise technical details, supported by clear architecture diagrams (Fig. 2) and qualitative results (Fig. 4). The writing is concise and accessible to researchers in computer vision and generative modeling.

## Originality

The core contribution of pose-guided person image generation is novel. While there are related works on image-to-image translation (Isola et al., 2017) and pose estimation (Cao et al., 2017), the specific problem of conditioning image generation on both a reference person image and target pose hasn't been addressed. The pose mask loss and two-stage architecture represent significant conceptual advances. The paper adequately distinguishes its contribution from related work, particularly from Zhao et al. [36] (Multi-view image generation from a single-view), showing clear qualitative and quantitative improvements.

## Significance

This work addresses a valuable problem with practical applications in movie making, human pose estimation, and image retrieval. The ability to generate realistic person images with specified poses is particularly useful for creating training data for pose estimation systems. The paper demonstrates clear improvements over the most similar prior work [36], with qualitative examples showing significantly better results. The two-stage architecture could potentially inspire similar approaches for other complex image generation tasks.

## Strengths

1. Novel problem formulation of pose-guided person image generation
2. Clever two-stage architecture that addresses the challenge of simultaneous pose and appearance generation
3. Pose mask loss effectively focuses generation on the human body
4. Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets and metrics
5. Well-written with clear explanations and helpful visualizations

## Weaknesses

1. Limited quantitative comparison with Zhao et al. [36] (only qualitative in Fig. 5)
2. The paper claims their method can "manipulate any object to an arbitrary pose" but only demonstrates results for persons
3. The pose estimator details are minimal (just "state-of-the-art pose estimator [2]"), which is insufficient for reproducibility
4. The failure analysis (Fig. 6) is good but could be expanded to better understand the limitations

## Specific Comments

The pose embedding method using 18 heatmaps is well-justified and superior to coordinate-based approaches. The explanation for why coordinate embedding leads to "wrong poses" (due to additional difficulty mapping coordinates to image locations) is particularly insightful.

I appreciate the careful attention to the background interference problem through the pose mask loss. The qualitative results in Fig. 4 clearly show the improvement over standard L1 loss.

The user study (Table 2) is well-designed but would benefit from more detail about the rating criteria. What specifically made users rate images as "real" or "fake"?

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the pose estimator (reference [2]) affects the quality of the results. Is the pose estimation accuracy the bottleneck?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field of generative image modeling. The problem is well-motivated, the approach is novel and well-executed, and the results are convincing. The paper meets all the quality criteria for NIPS: it's technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the field.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a novel and well-executed approach to pose-guided person image generation that significantly advances the state of the art. The two-stage architecture with the pose mask loss is a clever solution to a challenging problem, and the experimental results are compelling.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add quantitative comparison with Zhao et al. [36] (e.g., SSIM, IS scores)
2. Provide more details about the pose estimation method used
3. Clarify the scope of the method (currently claims "any object" but only demonstrates for persons)
4. Expand the failure analysis to better understand limitations

The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field of generative image modeling with clear applications. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and would be a strong addition to the proceedings.
