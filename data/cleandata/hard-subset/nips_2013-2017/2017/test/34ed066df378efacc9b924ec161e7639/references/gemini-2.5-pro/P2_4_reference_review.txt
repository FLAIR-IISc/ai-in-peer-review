**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel task: pose-guided person image generation. The goal is to synthesize a new image of a person from a source image, but in a new target pose specified by a set of keypoints. To tackle this challenging problem, the authors propose a two-stage generative network called PG2. Stage I employs a U-Net-like architecture (G1) to generate a coarse image that captures the global structure and color of the person in the target pose. A key contribution here is a "pose mask loss," a weighted L1 loss that encourages the model to focus on the foreground person rather than the background, which may differ between the source and target images. Stage II uses a conditional GAN (G2 and a discriminator D) to refine the coarse output from Stage I, adding high-frequency details and improving photorealism. The authors conduct extensive experiments on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets, including ablation studies on pose embedding, loss functions, and the two-stage design, as well as a user study to evaluate the realism of the generated images.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed two-stage approach is well-justified as a "divide-and-conquer" strategy for a complex generation task, separating the problem of global structure generation from fine-grained texture synthesis. The experimental evaluation is a major strength. The authors provide a thorough set of ablation studies that convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of their design choices:
1.  The concatenation of pose heatmaps is shown to be superior to alternative coordinate-based or feature-level embeddings.
2.  The pose mask loss is shown to improve results, particularly on the challenging Market-1501 dataset with cluttered backgrounds.
3.  The two-stage model is shown qualitatively and through a user study to produce more detailed and realistic images than a one-stage adversarial model.

The authors are also commendably honest about the limitations of their work and evaluation. They acknowledge that standard quantitative metrics like SSIM and IS do not fully align with perceptual quality, a known issue in generative modeling, and supplement their analysis with a user study. The inclusion of failure cases provides valuable insight into the model's limitations. The overall work is complete and the claims are well-supported by the provided evidence.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for the problem is well-articulated in the introduction. The proposed method is described in a logical flow, from the high-level two-stage framework down to the details of each component (G1, G2, D) and the loss functions. Figure 2 provides an excellent architectural overview that greatly aids in understanding the model. The experiments are presented logically, and the qualitative results in Figure 4 are well-chosen to illustrate the key comparisons and the effectiveness of the final model. The paper is easy to follow and provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work.

**Originality:** The primary originality of this paper lies in its problem formulation. While conditional image generation is a well-studied area, conditioning simultaneously on a source image identity and an explicit, fine-grained target pose for full-body human synthesis is a novel and challenging task. This work effectively establishes this problem and provides a strong first baseline.

The components of the solution are largely combinations of existing techniques (U-Nets, conditional GANs, residual blocks). However, the way they are assembled into a two-stage, coarse-to-fine pipeline is effective and well-suited for this problem. The "pose mask loss" is a simple but novel and effective contribution that directly addresses a key challenge of disentangling the subject from the background. The refinement stage, where the GAN is conditioned on the coarse output of the first stage, is a familiar strategy (e.g., in StackGAN), but its application to this specific pose-transfer task is new and demonstrated to be effective.

**Significance:** This work is significant for several reasons. It opens up a new and interesting direction in controllable, conditional image generation with clear applications in areas like data augmentation for pose estimation, virtual try-on for e-commerce, and special effects in media. The problem is difficult, and the proposed PG2 network demonstrates a promising path forward, advancing the state of the art in controllable human image synthesis. The thorough analysis and strong results on challenging datasets make this paper a valuable contribution that future work in this area will likely build upon and compare against. The code and models, if released, would be a valuable resource for the community.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

*   The description of G2 learning a "difference map" (Section 3.2) is slightly misleading. The text suggests the model generates a residual which is then added to the input. However, the architecture diagram and description imply that G2 is a standard U-Net that takes the coarse image `I_B1` (and `I_A`) as input and directly outputs the refined image `I_B2`. While it effectively learns to *refine* the image, it is not explicitly learning a residual/difference map. This phrasing could be clarified for greater technical precision.
*   The quantitative results in Table 1 are the weakest part of the evaluation, as they show little-to-no improvement (and sometimes degradation) for the full model (G1+G2+D) over the intermediate steps. While the authors correctly point out this is a known artifact of metrics like SSIM favoring blurrier images, it does weaken the quantitative argument for the second stage. The user study is therefore crucial and rightly included to support the claim of improved realism.
*   The comparison to VariGAN [36] is useful as it is the most related work, but the conditioning signals are different (a categorical view label vs. explicit keypoints). This makes it an indirect comparison. The authors' strongest baseline is their own one-stage variant (G1+D), which is a fair and insightful comparison.

**Conclusion:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel and important problem, proposes a well-designed and effective solution, and supports its claims with a thorough and convincing set of experiments. The strengths—a novel problem formulation, a solid and well-motivated model, and an extensive evaluation—far outweigh the minor weaknesses related to the limitations of quantitative metrics and the incremental novelty of individual components. The work is a clear advancement for the field of generative models and will be of high interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
