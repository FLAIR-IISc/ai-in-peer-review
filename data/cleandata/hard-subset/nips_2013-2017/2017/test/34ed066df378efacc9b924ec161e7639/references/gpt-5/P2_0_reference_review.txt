Summary
This paper tackles pose-guided person image generation: given a reference image of a person and a target pose (keypoints), synthesize the same person in the new pose. The method (PG2) is two-stage. Stage-I uses a U-Net variant conditioned on an 18-channel keypoint-heatmap representation concatenated with the input image to generate a coarse result that captures global structure. A foreground-weighted (“pose mask”) L1 loss downweights background differences between the conditioning and target images. Stage-II refines details via a conditional DCGAN trained to predict a residual (difference map) that, when added to the stage-I output, sharpens high-frequency appearance; the discriminator observes pairs (condition image, output) vs (condition image, target). The paper ablates pose embeddings (coordinates vs heatmap encoder vs direct heatmap concatenation), loss (standard L1 vs pose mask), and training regime (one-stage GAN vs two-stage). Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) show qualitative improvements, SSIM/IS (and masked variants) trends, and an AMT study. A brief comparison with a multi-view baseline [36] is provided. Failure cases are discussed.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on conditional generation (VAE/GAN/PixelRNN), pix2pix-style image-to-image, staged GANs (e.g., StackGAN), and view/pose-conditioned generation. Its key differences are (i) explicit keypoint heatmaps as pose control, (ii) a coarse-to-fine decomposition where stage-II learns a residual map, (iii) a foreground-weighted L1 to mitigate background mismatch, and (iv) a pairwise conditional discriminator. These are reasonable adaptations of known techniques to the specific problem of pose-guided person synthesis.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architecture is sensible, and the design choices are generally justified. The ablations convincingly support two main claims: (1) explicit heatmap concatenation is a more effective pose embedding than coordinate-based alternatives; (2) the pose mask improves results when backgrounds differ. The two-stage residual refinement is also supported qualitatively, and there is a clear rationale for learning a difference map.
- Experimental support: The qualitative results are compelling on both datasets. The ablations are informative and directly tied to the contributions. However:
  - The quantitative metrics (SSIM/IS and masked variants) are limited and known to correlate poorly with perceptual quality; the paper acknowledges that sharper images can score lower on SSIM. Stronger metrics (e.g., FID) and/or 2AFC perceptual studies would strengthen claims.
  - There is no quantitative evaluation of identity preservation (e.g., via a re-ID model or face/ID classifier) or of pose correctness (e.g., running a pose estimator on generated images and comparing to the target keypoints). These would directly measure the paper’s stated goals.
  - Robustness to pose estimation noise is not analyzed, even though keypoints are obtained automatically; results might depend heavily on pose estimator accuracy.
  - The comparison to prior work is limited to [36] with a few examples; more systematic comparisons or at least to a strong pix2pix baseline (the one-stage G1+D is close, but reporting numbers and qualitative side-by-sides as a baseline section would help) would increase confidence.
- Reproducibility: Many implementation details are given (optimizers, learning rates, blocks, training schedules, dataset splits). Some important details are deferred to the supplement (e.g., λ selection and sensitivity). The mask construction is described at a high level but could be more precise (structuring elements, thresholds).

Clarity
The manuscript is generally clear and well-organized. The motivation for a two-stage approach, the pose embedding choice, and the discriminator’s pairwise conditioning are easy to follow. Figures (as described) appear to support the arguments. Minor areas for improvement:
- Specify λ and provide its sensitivity in the main text (not only supplement), since it balances sharpness vs artifacts.
- Describe the exact morphology used to build the pose mask.
- Clarify why a fully connected layer is used in stage-I (and removed in stage-II), and whether it materially affects results at higher resolutions.

Originality
The problem setting—explicitly controlling person pose via keypoint heatmaps conditioned on a reference image—was not widely explored, and the decomposition into a coarse stage plus a residual-refinement GAN is a thoughtful combination of known ideas. The mask-weighted L1 is simple but effective. While no single component is radically novel, the overall system and ablation-driven insights constitute a meaningful contribution for this task.

Significance
The task is important for controllable synthesis and has potential downstream impacts (e.g., content creation, data augmentation). The proposed method moves the needle with clear qualitative improvements and lessons (pose embedding, foreground weighting, residual refinement). Broader impact would be stronger with:
- Quantitative identity/pose metrics to verify controllability and identity preservation.
- Demonstration of utility in downstream tasks (e.g., augmentation for re-ID or pose estimation).
- Robustness analyses to pose noise and out-of-distribution poses.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear formulation of an impactful, controllable generation task.
- Solid engineering of a two-stage system with a well-motivated residual refinement and pairwise conditional discriminator.
- Thorough ablations supporting design choices (pose embedding, mask loss, two-stage vs one-stage).
- Strong qualitative results on two challenging datasets; informative user study.

Cons for acceptance
- Quantitative evaluation relies on weak metrics; lacks identity and pose accuracy measures.
- Limited baseline comparisons beyond an internal one-stage model and a brief comparison to [36].
- Some implementation choices (e.g., stage-I fully connected layer) are not fully justified; key hyperparameter sensitivity relegated to supplement.
- No robustness analysis to pose estimator errors; limited failure analysis.

Suggestions
- Add identity preservation and pose accuracy metrics.
- Report FID (or similar) and conduct pairwise perceptual studies.
- Provide a stronger baseline section (e.g., pix2pix with identical conditioning) with quantitative and qualitative comparisons.
- Analyze robustness to noisy/missing keypoints and to large pose changes.
- Consider perceptual or feature-matching losses to complement L1.

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed, clearly presented paper with a practical and timely contribution. While the novelty is moderate and quantitative evidence could be stronger, the ablations and qualitative improvements are convincing. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening evaluation and clarifying a few implementation details.
