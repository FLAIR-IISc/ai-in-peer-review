### Summary

This paper introduces a novel task: generating an image of a person by conditioning on a source image of that person and a target pose. The proposed model, named Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), employs a two-stage "divide-and-conquer" strategy. Stage I uses a U-Net-like generator (G1) to produce a coarse image that captures the global body structure of the target pose, guided by a novel pose mask L1 loss designed to focus on the foreground. Stage II refines this coarse output using a second generator (G2) and a conditional discriminator, trained adversarially to add finer details and improve photorealism. The approach is evaluated on the high-resolution DeepFashion dataset and the more challenging low-resolution Market-1501 dataset, showing visually compelling results and outperforming a related view-synthesis method.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses a novel and practical problem of explicit pose-guided person image generation. This task has clear applications in areas like data augmentation, virtual try-on, and media production. The proposed conditioning mechanism, using an explicit pose representation, offers more direct control than related view-synthesis methods (e.g., [36]) that rely on more abstract labels.

2.  **Methodological Design:** The two-stage framework is logical and well-motivated. Decomposing the difficult end-to-end task into generating global structure first and then refining details is a sound strategy that appears effective, as demonstrated by the qualitative results. The introduction of masked versions of SSIM and IS for evaluation is also a thoughtful contribution that accounts for background variation.

3.  **Extensive Experimental Validation:** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation. This includes:
    *   Experiments on two datasets with different characteristics (high-res/clean vs. low-res/cluttered).
    *   Ablation studies on key design choices, such as the pose embedding method and the effect of the pose mask loss.
    *   Qualitative and quantitative comparisons against a one-stage baseline.
    *   A user study to assess the perceived realism of the generated images.
    *   A comparison with the most relevant prior work [36].
    *   An honest inclusion of failure cases.

### Weaknesses

Despite the promising direction, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws in its technical description, presentation, and experimental details, which undermine its quality and reproducibility.

**1. Critical Lack of Clarity in Methodology:**

*   **Ambiguity of Stage-II Generator (G2):** Section 3.2 states that G2 "aims to generate an appearance difference map". However, the overall framework (Figure 2) and the loss function (Eq. 4), `LG2 = ...‖(G2(...)− IB)...‖1`, suggest that G2 directly outputs the final refined image `ÎB2`. A difference map would typically be added to the input, i.e., `ÎB2 = ÎB1 + G2(...)`. This is a fundamental contradiction in the description of the core refinement mechanism. The paper needs to be precise about whether G2 is a residual generator or not.
*   **Pose Mask Loss Formulation:** The proposed mask loss in Eq. 1 is given as `‖(G1(IA, PB)− IB) (1 +MB)‖1`. This effectively applies a weight of 2 to foreground pixels (`MB=1`) and 1 to background pixels (`MB=0`). This formulation is non-standard and lacks justification. Why this specific weighting scheme? A more conventional approach would be `‖... ⊙ (1 + λMB)‖1` where `λ` is a hyperparameter, or simply `‖... ⊙ MB‖1` to ignore the background entirely. The paper should motivate this specific choice.
*   **Vague Details for Reproducibility:** The construction of the pose mask `MB` is vaguely described as being computed by "connecting human body parts and applying a set of morphological operations". This is insufficient for reproduction. What are the specific operations, kernel sizes, and connection logic? Similarly, architectural details like "the number of filters are increased linearly with each block" are too imprecise.

**2. Pervasive Formatting and Presentation Issues:**

*   **Incomplete and Inconsistent References:** The bibliography is in a state of disarray. Numerous entries are incomplete, missing venues, years, or page numbers (e.g., [2], [4], [6], [8], [10], [14], [21], etc.). The formatting is inconsistent throughout. This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a NIPS submission and suggests a rushed preparation.
*   **Unprofessional Artifacts:** The paper contains several unprofessional elements. The caption for Figure 6 (Failure Cases) includes what appears to be a placeholder note: "2 few man samples and complex texture -->failure details in G2". The footer on the first page, "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017), Long Beach, CA, USA," is a template artifact that was not removed.
*   **Minor Presentation Flaws:** Footnote 2 is not a footnote but a descriptive sentence that should be in the main text. The use of "randomly assigned" IDs in Figure 4 is unusual and should be clarified. The caption for Figure 4 is overly dense and hard to parse.

**3. Methodological and Experimental Gaps:**

*   **Architectural Choices:** The paper notes that "no batch normalization or dropout are applied" but provides no justification for this decision, which deviates from common practices in GAN literature (e.g., [10, 21]). The inclusion of a fully-connected layer in G1 is an interesting idea for long-range spatial reasoning, but its impact is not ablated or analyzed.
*   **Mixed User Study Results:** The user study reveals a key weakness: the full two-stage model (G1+G2+D) is perceived as *less* realistic than the one-stage model (G1+D) on the challenging Market-1501 dataset. The authors' explanation—that G2 transfers background details from the condition image, which can create artifacts—is plausible but highlights a significant limitation of the proposed refinement strategy. This suggests the refinement process is not robust to cluttered scenes.

### Conclusion

The paper presents a novel and interesting approach to a challenging generative task. The two-stage design is intuitive, and the qualitative results are promising. However, the work in its current form is not ready for publication at NIPS. The manuscript suffers from a critical lack of clarity on a core component of its method (the Stage-II generator), coupled with unsubstantiated methodological choices and a pervasive lack of professionalism in its presentation, most notably in the bibliography and figure captions. While the core idea has merit, the accumulation of these significant flaws makes the paper difficult to trust, impossible to reproduce, and ultimately falls short of the high standards of the conference.

**Recommendation: Reject**
