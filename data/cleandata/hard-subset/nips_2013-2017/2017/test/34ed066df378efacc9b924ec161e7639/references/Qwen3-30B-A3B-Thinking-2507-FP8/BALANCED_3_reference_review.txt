

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel task of generating person images conditioned on a reference image and a target pose (represented as keypoints), with applications in movie making and human pose estimation. The authors introduce a two-stage framework: Stage-I integrates pose information with a reference image using a U-Net variant with a novel pose mask loss to generate a coarse global structure, and Stage-II refines this result using a conditional GAN that generates a difference map. The paper evaluates on Market-1501 and DeepFashion datasets, comparing different pose embeddings, losses, and two-stage vs. one-stage approaches. The authors position their work as distinct from previous conditional image generation methods by using explicit pose information rather than abstract attributes.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Task Definition**: The paper correctly identifies a gap in the literature by defining a specific task of conditioning image generation on explicit pose keypoints rather than abstract attributes. This is a meaningful contribution with practical applications in fields like movie making and pose estimation.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The two-stage approach is well-motivated, and the ablation studies (comparing pose embeddings, losses, and model architectures) provide strong evidence for the authors' design choices. The pose mask loss is particularly clever and well-justified for focusing on human body appearance rather than background.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper evaluates on two distinct datasets with different characteristics (high-resolution fashion images and low-resolution re-identification images), providing a more robust assessment of the method's generalizability. The user study adds valuable human evaluation metrics beyond standard image quality metrics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty Compared to Existing Work**: While the pose-guided generation is novel, the overall framework (U-Net with skip connections + conditional GAN) is very similar to established image-to-image translation methods like Pix2Pix. The paper fails to adequately differentiate its approach from these existing frameworks, weakening the originality claim. The authors should explicitly state how pose-specific conditioning differs from general image-to-image translation.

2. **Inconsistent Qualitative-Quantitative Results**: The paper claims the two-stage model produces sharper images (Figure 4), but quantitative results (Table 1) show the one-stage model (G1+D) has comparable or better SSIM scores than the two-stage model (G1+G2+D). The authors acknowledge this discrepancy but don't adequately explain why qualitative improvements don't translate to quantitative metrics. This undermines the main contribution of the two-stage approach.

3. **Insufficient Failure Analysis**: Only two failure examples are provided (Figure 6), which is insufficient to understand the limitations of the approach. More detailed failure analysis would help readers understand the conditions under which the method works well and where it struggles.

4. **Outdated Related Work Comparisons**: The paper only compares to Zhao et al. [36] for multi-view person synthesis but fails to include more recent works in pose-guided image generation (e.g., Wang et al. 2018, Chen et al. 2018), which weakens the significance of the contribution.

## Constructive Suggestions

1. **Clarify Novelty**: Better distinguish the pose-guided approach from existing image-to-image translation frameworks. Explicitly state how pose-specific conditioning differs from general image-to-image translation and why the pose mask loss is particularly beneficial for pose transfer.

2. **Address Quality-Quantitative Discrepancy**: Provide a thorough explanation for why the two-stage model produces sharper images qualitatively but has similar or worse quantitative scores. Consider adding perceptual quality metrics (e.g., LPIPS) that better align with human perception.

3. **Expand Failure Analysis**: Include more failure examples with detailed analysis of why they occur (e.g., rare poses, gender imbalance, clothing complexity) and discuss potential solutions.

4. **Update Related Work**: Add comparisons to more recent pose-guided image generation methods to better position the contribution within the current literature.

## Overall Assessment
The paper presents a well-executed study of a meaningful problem with a clear methodology and comprehensive evaluation. However, the limited novelty compared to existing image-to-image translation work and the inconsistent results between qualitative and quantitative evaluations weaken the paper's contribution. The authors could significantly strengthen their paper by better differentiating their approach from existing work, addressing the quality-quantitative discrepancy, and providing more thorough failure analysis.

## Decision
I recommend **weak rejection**. The paper has good technical execution and addresses an interesting problem, but the novelty claim is not sufficiently supported, and the inconsistent results undermine the main contribution. With significant revisions addressing the points above, this paper could become a strong contribution to NIPS.

## Pro/Con Acceptance
*Pro*: Novel task definition, strong ablation studies, comprehensive evaluation on multiple datasets.
*Con*: Limited novelty compared to existing image-to-image translation work, inconsistent qualitative/quantitative results, limited failure analysis, outdated related work comparisons.
