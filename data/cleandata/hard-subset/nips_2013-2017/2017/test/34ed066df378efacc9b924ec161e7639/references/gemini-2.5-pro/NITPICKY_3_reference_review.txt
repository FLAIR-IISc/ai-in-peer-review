**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel task: generating a person's image in a desired pose, conditioned on a source image of that person and the target pose's keypoints. To tackle this challenging problem, the authors propose a two-stage framework called the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2). In the first stage, a U-Net-like generator produces a coarse rendering of the person in the target pose. This stage focuses on correctly transferring the global structure and appearance. A key contribution here is a "pose mask loss," which up-weights the L1 loss on the foreground region to mitigate the influence of background changes. In the second stage, a conditional GAN is used to refine the coarse output from stage one, adding finer details and improving photorealism. The authors conduct experiments on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets, evaluating different pose embedding strategies, the effect of their proposed loss function, and comparing their two-stage model to a one-stage alternative.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Significant Task:** The problem of explicit pose-guided person image generation is well-motivated and has clear applications in areas like data augmentation, content creation, and virtual try-on. It represents a more direct and flexible form of control compared to previous conditional generation work based on attributes or view categories.
2.  **Sound Methodological Approach:** The proposed two-stage, coarse-to-fine strategy is intuitive and effective for a complex generation task. Decomposing the problem into first getting the structure right and then refining the texture is a logical design choice that appears to yield strong qualitative results.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a comprehensive set of experiments. The ablation studies on pose embedding methods and the comparison between standard L1 loss and the proposed pose mask loss effectively justify their design choices. The inclusion of a user study and failure case analysis adds to the paper's completeness.
4.  **Compelling Qualitative Results:** The images generated by the full PG2 model, particularly on the DeepFashion dataset, are visually impressive. The model appears to successfully preserve the person's identity and clothing while realistically warping them to the new pose.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising direction, the paper is marred by a significant number of flaws, ranging from critical ambiguities in the methodology to a general lack of rigor in the presentation.

1.  **Technical Clarity and Reproducibility:**
    *   There is a major contradiction in the description of the Stage-II generator (G2). Section 3.2 states that G2 "aims to generate an appearance difference map". However, the loss function in Eq. (4) compares the direct output of G2 with the target image `IB` (i.e., `‖(G2(...) - IB) ...‖1`). If G2 outputs a difference map, the loss should be applied to the sum of the coarse input and the difference map (e.g., `‖(ÎB1 + G2(...)) - IB) ...‖1`). If it outputs the final image, the description of it learning a "difference map" is misleading. This ambiguity makes the refinement stage, a core part of the contribution, difficult to understand and reproduce.
    *   The "pose mask loss" in Eq. (1) uses a weighting factor of `(1 + MB)`. This effectively weights the foreground pixels twice as much as the background pixels. This specific `1:2` weighting scheme is presented without justification. It is unclear why this particular weighting was chosen over other possibilities, making it seem ad-hoc.
    *   The network architecture details in Section 3.3 are too vague for reproducibility. Statements like "N depends on the size of input" are insufficient. The claim that "no batch normalization or dropout are applied" is a non-standard design choice, especially for GAN training, and warrants a clear explanation which is not provided.

2.  **Experimental Rigor:**
    *   While the qualitative results for the two-stage model are strong, the quantitative results in Table 1 are not. The SSIM and IS scores for the two-stage model (G1+G2+D) are comparable or even slightly worse than the one-stage adversarial model (G1+D). The authors acknowledge that these metrics are flawed, but this still weakens the central claim that the two-stage approach is superior.
    *   The comparison to VariGAN [36] in Section 4.5 is not entirely fair. The proposed method is provided with precise 18-point keypoint locations for the target pose, which is a very strong and explicit conditioning signal. In contrast, [36] uses only a high-level categorical view label (e.g., "side"). The superior performance of the proposed method is therefore expected, and this significant difference in input information should be acknowledged more explicitly.

3.  **Presentation and Formatting:**
    *   The manuscript appears to be a hasty draft. The NIPS 2017 conference header is still present on the first page, which is a glaring oversight.
    *   The bibliography is in an unacceptable state for a top-tier conference submission. A large number of references are incomplete, missing publication venues, years, and page numbers, often just pointing to arXiv preprints for papers that have long been published (e.g., [2], [5], [9], [14], [21]). This demonstrates a severe lack of diligence.
    *   There are numerous minor formatting errors and typos. For example, "the the generator" in Section 3.2; a reference to a non-existent footnote 3 in Section 4.1; and a mislabeled footnote 5 in the caption of Table 2.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses a novel and interesting problem.
*   The core idea of a two-stage generation process is sound.
*   The qualitative results are visually impressive and demonstrate the potential of the approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   A critical ambiguity in the methodology of the refinement stage (G2) hinders understanding and reproducibility.
*   Key design choices (e.g., mask loss weighting, lack of BN) are not sufficiently justified.
*   Quantitative results fail to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed two-stage model over a simpler one-stage baseline.
*   The paper suffers from an exceptionally high number of formatting errors and an incomplete, poorly formatted bibliography, falling well below the expected standard of presentation for NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Reject

While the paper presents a promising direction for conditional image generation, the combination of a critical methodological ambiguity, weak quantitative support for a central claim, and an unacceptable number of presentation and formatting flaws leads me to recommend rejection. The work has potential, but the manuscript in its current form is not ready for publication at NIPS. The authors are encouraged to thoroughly revise the paper to address the technical clarity issues, strengthen their experimental validation, and meticulously proofread and format the manuscript and its references before resubmission.
