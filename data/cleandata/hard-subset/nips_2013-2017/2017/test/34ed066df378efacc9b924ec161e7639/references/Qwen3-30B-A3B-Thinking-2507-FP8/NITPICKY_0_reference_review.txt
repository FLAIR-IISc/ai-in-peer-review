

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

This paper proposes a two-stage approach for generating person images conditioned on a reference image and a target pose. The authors address the challenging problem of pose transfer by first generating a coarse image capturing global structure (Stage I) and then refining it to add details (Stage II). The main contributions include a novel pose-guided generation task, a pose mask loss to focus on human body rather than background, and a two-stage architecture.

## Strengths
The paper presents a well-motivated approach to an important problem in image synthesis. The two-stage decomposition is logical and addresses a key challenge in pose transfer (simultaneous generation of correct pose and appearance details). The pose mask loss is a thoughtful addition that effectively addresses background interference. The experimental results, including both quantitative metrics and user studies, provide reasonable evidence of the method's effectiveness. The comparison with Zhao et al. [36] is fair and demonstrates the superiority of the proposed approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Technical Inconsistencies and Errors**: The paper contains several technical inconsistencies that undermine confidence in the method. The pose mask loss formulation in Equation (1) is unconventional: LG1 = ‖(G1(IA, PB)− IB) (1 +MB)‖1. If MB is 1 for foreground (human body) and 0 for background, this gives foreground a weight of 2 and background a weight of 1. While this technically gives more weight to foreground, standard practice would use MB alone (not 1+MB) to achieve this weighting. The authors should clarify why this formulation is used and provide justification.

2. **Missing Figures**: The paper references multiple figures (Figure 1, Figure 3, Figure 4, Figure 5) that are not included in the provided text. This is a critical omission that makes it impossible to verify the qualitative results or understand the pose mask implementation.

3. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper inconsistently uses "stage-I" (with hyphen) and "stage I" (without hyphen) throughout the text. This is a minor but distracting formatting issue that suggests lack of attention to detail.

4. **Reference List Incompleteness**: The reference list contains numerous incomplete entries (e.g., "Realtime multi-person 2d pose estimation using part affinity, Zhe Cao, Tomas Simon, Shih-En Wei, Yaser Sheikh, fields. arXiv,, , ," and "Unsupervised representation learning with deep convolutional generative adversarial, Alec Radford, Luke Metz, Soumith Chintala, networks. arXiv,, , ,"). This suggests the paper was not properly proofread before submission.

5. **Unjustified Hyperparameter Choice**: The paper uses Adam with β1 = 0.5, which is highly unusual (standard is β1 = 0.9). The authors provide no justification for this choice, which could significantly impact convergence and performance. This should be addressed or justified.

6. **Confusing User Study Description**: The description of R2G ("#Real images rated as generated / #Real images") is confusing as it would produce a value between 0 and 1, but the paper reports it as percentages (e.g., 9.3%). The metric should be clearly defined as either a percentage or a proportion.

7. **Inconsistent Citation**: The introduction cites "[21, 5, 1]" but the reference list does not contain entries 5 or 1 (the first reference is [14]). This suggests a citation error.

8. **Ambiguous Comparison with [36]**: The paper compares with "VariGAN [36]" but the reference [36] is listed as "Multi-view image generation from a single-view" by Zhao et al., which appears to be a different method than "VariGAN." The authors should clarify this discrepancy.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a novel task and a reasonable approach to pose-guided person image generation, the technical inconsistencies, missing figures, and formatting errors are substantial. The pose mask loss formulation is unconventional without justification, the reference list is incomplete, and the paper contains multiple instances of inconsistent notation and unclear descriptions. These minor but significant issues collectively undermine the paper's technical rigor and presentation quality.

The paper's contributions are interesting but the execution lacks the precision expected at NIPS. The experimental results are promising, but the technical details provided are insufficient to fully evaluate the method's strengths. The reviewers' time would be better spent on papers with more meticulous attention to detail.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial number of minor but significant technical and presentation flaws that prevent a thorough evaluation of the work. These issues, while individually small, collectively indicate a lack of careful preparation that would be problematic for a high-quality NIPS submission.
