Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles pose-guided person image generation: given a reference image of a person and a target 2D pose (18 keypoints), synthesize the same person in the new pose. The central idea is a two-stage, coarse-to-fine pipeline (PG2). Stage I is a U-Net–like encoder-decoder that takes the reference image concatenated with pose heatmaps and produces a coarse image capturing global structure; training uses an L1 loss weighted by a foreground “pose mask” to deemphasize background mismatch. Stage II refines details via a conditional DCGAN that predicts a residual/difference map between Stage I output and the target; the discriminator operates on pairs (condition, generated) versus (condition, real), and an additional masked L1 term regularizes the adversarial training. The method is evaluated on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64), with ablations (pose embedding strategies, mask vs. L1, one-stage vs. two-stage), SSIM/IS and “mask”-variants, and an AMT user study. A qualitative comparison to VariGAN [36] is included.

Relative to prior work, the paper situates itself among conditional VAEs/GANs and image-to-image translation (e.g., pix2pix), multi-view synthesis, and text-to-image works, and argues that explicit pose conditioning via keypoint heatmaps offers more precise control than view labels. The two-stage design is reminiscent of stacked/Coarse-to-fine GANs (e.g., StackGAN) and pix2pix’s conditional adversarial loss; the novel elements are the explicit person-pose transfer setting, the simple but effective pose heatmap concatenation, and the pose-masked reconstruction loss.

Strengths:
- Clear task formulation with practical relevance (content-preserving pose transfer) and explicit control via pose keypoints.
- Sensible two-stage decomposition that separates structure from appearance detail; residual refinement is well-motivated and appears to stabilize training.
- Strong qualitative results on two datasets; ablations are informative (pose heatmaps vs. coordinate embeddings; mask vs. vanilla L1; one-stage vs. two-stage).
- The pose-masked L1 is a simple and effective idea for mitigating background change, especially important on Market-1501.
- Pairwise conditioning for the discriminator is appropriate for avoiding trivial copying of the condition image.
- User study substantiates perceptual gains of the two-stage model over the one-stage baseline.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty is incremental: two-stage GANs, U-Net with skip connections, and conditional pairwise discriminators are well-established; the main novelty lies in the application and a few training tricks (mask loss, residual prediction).
- Quantitative evaluation is limited and somewhat misaligned with perceptual quality; SSIM/mask-SSIM and IS/mask-IS are known to be weak proxies for realism on person images. The two-stage model does not clearly improve SSIM/IS over one-stage despite qualitative gains.
- No identity-preservation metric: since the core claim is keeping the person’s identity/appearance, evaluation should include identity similarity (e.g., via a re-ID network or face/body embedding) and retrieval-based tests.
- Limited comparisons: only to VariGAN [36], which targets a different conditioning (view label). Missing baselines include pix2pix-style models with pose-conditioning, feature warping/appearance flow, or pose-guided image-to-image methods.
- The pose mask creation and its impact are under-specified (details delegated to supp): how is MB derived from keypoints; robustness to pose estimator errors; sensitivity to mask quality and λ.
- Deterministic outputs: the system does not model multimodality (e.g., plausible variations in texture/occlusions given the same pose), though the problem is inherently one-to-many.
- Background handling remains weak; artifacts are noted in the user study for Market-1501.

Quality:
The methodology is technically sound and the design choices are reasonable. Ablations support key components. However, the empirical support for identity preservation and state-of-the-art standing is incomplete. Claims about faster convergence with residual prediction are plausible but not quantified. More rigorous quantitative evaluation would strengthen the paper substantially.

Clarity:
Generally clear and well-organized. Architectural descriptions are understandable; however, some important details are light or deferred: exact mask generation procedure, λ selection and sensitivity, training stability without BN, and pose estimator accuracy/impact.

Originality:
Moderate. The application is timely and interesting, but the building blocks (U-Net + heatmaps, masked L1, conditional GAN with pairwise input, residual prediction) are adaptations of known techniques.

Significance:
Moderate. The task is valuable and likely to inspire follow-up work in pose-guided synthesis and data augmentation for pose/re-ID. The practical gains are more qualitative than quantitative at present. With stronger evaluation and comparisons, the impact could be higher.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses a compelling, well-scoped problem with explicit control via pose.
- Two-stage design yields clear qualitative gains; simple ideas (pose mask, residual refinement) are effective.
- Ablation studies and user study provide evidence of improvements.
- Solid engineering and clear presentation; likely to be of interest to the community.

Cons for acceptance:
- Incremental novelty relative to prior GAN frameworks.
- Quantitative evaluation is limited; no identity-preservation metric; mixed SSIM/IS results.
- Sparse comparisons to strong baselines; unclear SOTA advancement.
- Limited analysis of robustness to pose estimation noise and mask quality; background artifacts remain.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add identity-preservation evaluation: (i) rank-1 re-ID accuracy of generated images when queried against galleries, (ii) cosine similarity in a re-ID/face embedding, or verification ROC.
- Include stronger baselines: pix2pix conditioned on source image + pose heatmaps; feature warping/appearance flow baselines; a single-stage residual+GAN baseline to isolate the benefit of the two-stage schedule.
- Report perceptual metrics better aligned with human judgment (e.g., human A/B preference tests with longer viewing time and pairwise comparisons); if feasible, include modern distributional metrics (e.g., FID) and discuss their limitations for person datasets.
- Analyze sensitivity: pose estimator noise (compare to GT keypoints on a subset), heatmap radius, λ, and mask generation procedure; provide failure mode statistics by pose difficulty/occlusion.
- Consider explicit background modeling or segmentation-guided compositing to reduce background artifacts (or augment with background inpainting/harmonization).
- Discuss and, if possible, support multimodality (e.g., stochastic noise input at Stage II) to capture diverse but plausible details.
- Release code and pre-trained models to aid reproducibility.

Recommendation: Weak Accept. While the novelty is incremental, the problem is important, the approach is well-executed, and the qualitative results and ablations are convincing. With stronger quantitative evidence (identity preservation, broader comparisons), this work would be a clear accept.
