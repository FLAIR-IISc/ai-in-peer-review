## Peer Review: Pose Guided Person Image Generation

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an interesting and timely approach to controllable image generation, specifically focusing on pose transfer for person images. The idea of conditioning generation on both a reference image *and* a target pose is novel and has potential applications in areas like movie making and data augmentation for pose estimation. The two-stage approach is well-motivated, and the proposed pose mask loss is a sensible addition. While the technical execution is solid and the results are promising, the paper suffers from a lack of thorough comparison to related work and a somewhat limited evaluation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary of Ideas & Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of generating a person image in a desired pose, given a reference image and the target pose itself.  This builds upon the recent surge in GAN-based image generation methods (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Radford et al., 2015), which have demonstrated impressive results in generating realistic images. However, most existing GAN approaches lack precise control over specific attributes. The authors address this by explicitly conditioning the generation process on pose, drawing inspiration from earlier work on conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and image-to-image translation (Isola et al., 2017).  They differentiate their work from existing methods that condition on attributes or text (Yan et al., 2015; Reed et al., 2016) by using explicit pose information via keypoints. The two-stage approach of first generating a coarse structure and then refining it with a GAN is reminiscent of coarse-to-fine generation strategies used in other contexts (e.g., Lassner et al., 2017).  The paper appropriately cites relevant work, but the connection to Zhao et al. (2017) appears understated – their multi-view image generation is a close relative, and a more detailed discussion of the differences in expressiveness (as the authors claim) would be beneficial.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  The explicit conditioning on *both* a reference image and a target pose is a novel contribution. It provides a more direct and controllable method for pose manipulation compared to previous approaches.
* **Well-Motivated Approach:** Dividing the problem into two stages (global structure then appearance details) is a reasonable and effective strategy, especially given the complexity of the task.
* **Pose Mask Loss:** The proposed pose mask loss is a clever way to focus the generation process on the human body and mitigate the impact of background changes.
* **Solid Technical Execution:** The use of U-Nets and conditional DCGANs are appropriate choices, and the implementation appears sound.
* **Promising Results:** The qualitative results are visually appealing, demonstrating the ability to transfer poses while preserving identity. The quantitative results, while needing further scrutiny (see weaknesses), suggest the method performs competitively.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The comparison to only one related work (Zhao et al., 2017) is insufficient.  The authors should compare against more recent and state-of-the-art image manipulation techniques, including those based on StyleGAN or other advanced GAN architectures.  Simply mentioning that those methods are not directly applicable isn’t enough; an attempt to adapt them or a more detailed justification is required.
* **Quantitative Evaluation Concerns:**  While the quantitative results are reported, the interpretation is somewhat misleading. The authors acknowledge that SSIM may not correlate well with perceptual quality. Relying heavily on SSIM, particularly when it conflicts with the visual evidence, weakens the quantitative evaluation.  Consider including more perceptually relevant metrics, like FID or LPIPS. Furthermore, the improvement provided by the two-stage method is not as significant in quantitative results as suggested by the qualitative results.
* **Dataset Bias:** The experiments predominantly use datasets where the background is relatively simple (DeepFashion) or the images are low-resolution (Market-1501). This limits the generalizability of the results.  Experiments on more complex scenes with diverse backgrounds would be valuable.
* **Ablation Study Depth:** While ablation studies are performed (pose embedding, loss function), they are not as exhaustive as they could be. For example, the impact of the fully-connected layer removal in Stage-II G2 could be investigated more thoroughly.
* **Failure Case Analysis:** While failure cases are discussed, the analysis is brief.  A more systematic investigation of the failure modes would be beneficial for understanding the limitations of the approach.

**4. Originality:**

The combination of reference-image conditioning and explicit pose control is original. While individual components (GANs, U-Nets, conditional generation) are well-established, their application to this specific problem with the proposed two-stage approach and pose mask loss represents a novel contribution.

**5. Significance:**

The proposed approach has the potential to be impactful. Controllable image generation is a rapidly growing field with applications in various domains, including content creation, virtual reality, and data augmentation. The ability to manipulate human pose in a realistic manner is particularly valuable.  The potential for generating training data for pose estimation (as mentioned by the authors) is also significant.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and well-motivated approach to pose-guided image generation.
* Technically sound and well-executed.
* Promising qualitative results demonstrating the feasibility of the approach.
* The proposed pose mask loss is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited comparison to strong baselines.
* Concerns about the reliability of the quantitative evaluation metrics.
* Limited dataset diversity.




**Overall Recommendation:** **Accept with minor revisions.**  

The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, specifically:

1.  **Expand the comparison to related work**, including more recent and relevant image manipulation techniques.
2.  **Improve the quantitative evaluation** by incorporating more perceptually relevant metrics.
3.  **Perform experiments on more diverse datasets** to demonstrate generalizability.
4.  **Provide a more detailed analysis of failure cases.**
