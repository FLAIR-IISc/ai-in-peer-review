

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

This paper introduces a novel task in image generation: conditioning person image synthesis on both a reference image and a target pose. The authors propose a two-stage framework (PG2) that first integrates pose information with the reference image to generate a coarse result with a novel pose mask loss (to focus on human body rather than background), then refines this result using a conditional GAN that generates a difference map rather than a full image. This is a significant departure from previous work that conditions on labels or text, instead using explicit pose information in the form of keypoints.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) proposing the pose-guided person image generation task, (2) exploring different pose integration methods and introducing a pose mask loss, and (3) developing a two-stage framework that separates global structure learning from detail refinement. The authors effectively demonstrate that their pose mask loss significantly improves results by focusing generation on the human body rather than background, and that the two-stage approach outperforms one-stage alternatives.

What makes this paper stand out is the boldness of the core idea: using pose as an explicit, flexible condition for person image generation. While previous work has explored conditional generation (e.g., [22, 23, 32, 36]), this is the first to use pose keypoints as the primary condition for person image synthesis. The pose mask loss is particularly clever—it addresses a real challenge in pose transfer (background interference) with a simple but effective modification to the loss function.

The paper's experimental validation is thorough, with ablation studies on pose embeddings, loss functions, and the two-stage vs. one-stage approach. The quantitative results (SSIM, IS) and qualitative comparisons (especially against [36]) convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach. The failure cases they identify (complex poses, gender imbalance) are reasonable and expected for this challenging task.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a strong case for the novelty of its approach. The connection to applications in movie making and pose estimation adds practical significance. The two-stage architecture is a thoughtful solution to the difficulty of simultaneously learning pose and appearance.

Minor weaknesses include:
- The user study on Market-1501 shows G1+G2+D sometimes gets lower scores than G1+D, though the authors reasonably explain this as a side effect of the two-stage approach preserving more background from the reference image.
- The paper doesn't fully address how to handle cases where the reference image and target pose have very different body types (e.g., transferring a slender person to a muscular pose), though this is a challenging edge case for any pose transfer method.
- The comparison with [36] is limited to DeepFashion; a more thorough comparison on Market-1501 would have been ideal.

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, these are minor concerns. The core contribution—posing person image generation as a pose-guided task—is genuinely novel and opens up new research directions. The two-stage framework with pose mask loss is a clean, elegant solution to a challenging problem, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over previous work.

This paper represents a significant step forward in conditional image generation, moving beyond simple label conditioning to explicit pose control. The approach could be extended to other domains (e.g., object generation) and has clear potential for practical applications. The authors have identified a meaningful gap in the literature and provided a compelling, well-executed solution.

I believe this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of generative modeling. It introduces a new problem space, proposes a novel solution with clear technical innovation, and provides strong empirical validation. While not perfect, the paper's novelty and significance far outweigh its minor methodological shortcomings.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Strengths:**
- Novel task definition (pose-guided person image generation)
- Clever pose mask loss addressing background interference
- Two-stage architecture that cleanly separates structure and detail learning
- Strong empirical validation with ablation studies and comparisons
- Clear connection to practical applications

**Weaknesses:**
- Minor performance drop in user study on Market-1501 (reasonably explained)
- Limited discussion of edge cases like body type mismatch
- Comparison with [36] could be more comprehensive on Market-1501

This paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel work that advances the field. The pose-guided generation concept is compelling and likely to inspire future research, making it a strong candidate for NIPS.
