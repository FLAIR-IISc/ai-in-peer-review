**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel and challenging task: pose-guided person image generation. The goal is to synthesize a realistic image of a specific person in a target pose, given a single source image of that person and the target pose keypoints. The authors propose a two-stage generative network, termed PG2, to tackle this problem. The first stage employs a U-Net-like architecture (G1) to generate a coarse image that captures the global structure and pose from the input source image and target pose heatmaps. A key contribution here is a "pose mask loss" (a weighted L1 loss) designed to focus learning on the foreground person, mitigating issues with changing backgrounds. The second stage uses a conditional GAN (G2 and D) to refine the coarse output from stage one, adding finer details and improving photo-realism. This stage's generator learns to predict a residual or difference map, and the discriminator is conditioned on the source image to ensure the generated person is consistent with the source identity. The method is evaluated on the high-resolution DeepFashion dataset and the more challenging, low-resolution Market-1501 re-identification dataset, demonstrating convincing qualitative and quantitative results through ablations and a user study.

The proposed method builds upon established concepts in generative modeling, particularly U-Net architectures for image-to-image translation [10] and the coarse-to-fine strategy seen in works like StackGAN [35]. However, it applies them to a new, well-defined problem with a more structured and complex conditioning signal (pose keypoints) than typical image translation tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** The paper defines a clear, practical, and challenging problem. The ability to control a person's pose in an image has significant applications in areas like data augmentation for pose estimation, virtual try-on, and content creation for media. This work represents an important early step in this direction.

2.  **Sound and Effective Methodology:** The two-stage, coarse-to-fine approach is a very sensible design choice for this complex task. Decomposing the problem into generating the correct global structure first (Stage I) and then adding high-frequency details (Stage II) is logical and empirically shown to be effective. The refinement stage's focus on generating a difference map is also a smart detail, as it simplifies the learning problem for the second generator.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation and Ablation Studies:** The authors perform a comprehensive set of experiments that validate their key design choices. The comparison of different pose embedding strategies convincingly shows the superiority of concatenating pose heatmaps. The ablation study on the pose mask loss demonstrates its benefit, particularly on the cluttered Market-1501 dataset. Furthermore, the qualitative comparison between the one-stage (G1+D) and two-stage (G1+G2+D) models clearly highlights the value of the refinement stage.

4.  **Strong Qualitative Results and User Study:** The visual results, especially on the DeepFashion dataset, are impressive for the time. The model successfully transfers detailed clothing textures and appearance to novel and complex poses while maintaining identity. The inclusion of a user study on Amazon Mechanical Turk provides a stronger basis for claims of photo-realism than relying solely on automated metrics, which the authors rightly point out can be misleading.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Lack of Clarity on Pose Mask Generation:** The paper describes the pose mask `MB` as being computed by "connecting human body parts and applying a set of morphological operations." This description is too vague for reproducibility. What specific morphological operations (e.g., dilation, closing) are used? What are their parameters (e.g., kernel size)? How are the body parts "connected" to form an initial mask? Given that the proposed pose mask loss is a key contribution, this part of the methodology requires more detail, perhaps in an appendix.

2.  **Architectural Justification in Stage II:** The generator G2 in the refinement stage takes both the coarse image `I_hat_B1` and the original source image `IA` as input. While `I_hat_B1` is necessary, the re-introduction of `IA` is not fully justified. All appearance information from `IA` should ideally have been encoded into `I_hat_B1` by Stage I. Including `IA` again in Stage II risks the network learning to "copy-paste" features or background elements from the source image, rather than learning a true refinement of the posed structure. The user study results on Market-1501, where the two-stage model was rated as more fake due to transferring background artifacts, seem to support this concern. An analysis of the effect of removing `IA` from G2's input would have been insightful.

3.  **Quantitative Metrics:** While the authors are commended for acknowledging the limitations of SSIM for evaluating GANs, the choice of Inception Score (IS) is also suboptimal for a conditional generation task. IS primarily measures the quality and diversity of a generated set of images, not their fidelity to the conditioning input. A more standard and appropriate metric for this type of task would be the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID), which has been shown to correlate better with human perception of image quality. The introduction of "mask-SSIM" and "mask-IS" is an interesting idea, but their properties are not analyzed, leaving their interpretation somewhat ambiguous.

4.  **Limited Baselines:** Given the novelty of the task, the lack of direct comparison works is understandable. However, the one-stage baseline (G1+D) is an internal comparison. A stronger baseline could have been constructed by adapting a general-purpose image-to-image translation network like pix2pix [10] to this task directly (i.e., mapping [source image + pose heatmaps] -> target image in one go). This would provide a more robust benchmark against a well-established external method.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution by introducing a novel problem and a strong, well-evaluated baseline solution. The methodology is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results are convincing. The paper is clearly written and well-organized. While there are some minor weaknesses concerning the clarity of certain methodological details and the choice of quantitative metrics, the strengths of the paper—particularly its originality and significance—far outweigh them. This work laid important groundwork for subsequent research in human synthesis and controllable generative models. I strongly recommend acceptance.
