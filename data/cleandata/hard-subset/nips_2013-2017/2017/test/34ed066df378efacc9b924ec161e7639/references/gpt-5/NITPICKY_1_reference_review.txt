Summary
The paper proposes PG2, a two-stage conditional image generation framework for pose-guided person image synthesis. Given a reference image IA and target pose PB (from a keypoint detector), Stage I (G1) is a U-Net-like generator that outputs a coarse image using an L1 loss weighted by a “pose mask” to emphasize the person over background. Stage II (G2) refines the output via a conditional DCGAN—purportedly predicting a “difference map”—with a pairwise discriminator D that judges (IA, generated) versus (IA, target). The method is evaluated qualitatively and with SSIM/IS (and masked variants) on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64), and with a user study. Ablations examine pose embeddings and the mask loss; a one-stage adversarial baseline (G1+D) is included. The paper reports improved qualitative realism and some metric gains, and a qualitative advantage over VariGAN [36].

Strengths
- Clear, practical problem formulation: pose-conditioned person image synthesis with explicit control via keypoints.
- Reasonable architecture design and training strategy: a staged, coarse-to-fine refinement with a conditional adversarial loss; pairwise conditioning in D is sound.
- Useful pose embedding via heatmap concatenation; the ablation (CE vs HME vs concatenation) is informative.
- Mask-weighted L1 is simple and effective in practice to de-emphasize background mismatch.
- Extensive qualitative visualization on two datasets and a user study; ablations cover stage-I embedding and loss and two-stage vs one-stage.

Weaknesses (technical and methodological)
1) Inconsistency in “difference map” claim: The text states G2 learns a difference map, but the equations and discriminator inputs treat G2’s output as a full image (Eq. (4): G2(IA, ÎB1) is compared directly with IB; no residual addition ÎB1+Δ is shown). The symbol ÎB2 is introduced but not defined rigorously. Please clarify whether G2 predicts an image or an additive residual, and fix equations and notation accordingly.

2) Pose mask loss underspecified and somewhat ad hoc:
- MB is binary, and the weighting is (1+MB), i.e., foreground weight 2, background weight 1, with no justification or tuning discussion. Why not use a scalar α∈[0,∞) and ablate?
- MB is generated via morphological ops; details are missing (structuring element, connectivity, handling missing keypoints), affecting reproducibility and performance.
- The keypoint disk radius is fixed at 4 px for both 256×256 and 128×64, which is scale-mismatched. A scale-normalized radius (e.g., relative to person height) seems more appropriate. This is not analyzed.
- For evaluation, mask-SSIM and mask-IS use a pose mask but the procedure is not precisely defined (e.g., whether masked pixels are zeroed, inpainted, cropped). Masking for IS (a classifier-based metric) is especially dubious; zeroing background may hurt IS in non-standard ways.

3) Architecture choices are not well justified:
- Stage I uses a fully connected bottleneck in a U-Net, which destroys spatial equivariance, inflates parameters, and likely harms generalization. Later you remove FC in Stage II. Why is FC beneficial in Stage I? Parameter counts and memory footprints are not provided.
- The paper explicitly disables batch normalization and dropout in both generators; this contradicts standard DCGAN practice and may slow or destabilize training. No rationale or stability analysis is given.
- No discussion of alternative stabilizers (e.g., one-sided label smoothing, gradient penalty, spectral normalization). Training details (epochs vs. iterations, convergence diagnostics, compute budget) are sparse.

4) Evaluation gaps:
- IS/SSIM are known to be weak proxies for conditional image quality. No FID (introduced in 2017), no LPIPS (later), no identity preservation metric (e.g., re-ID feature similarity), and no pose accuracy metric (e.g., PCK/OKS by running a pose estimator on outputs). These are crucial for this task (pose correctness and identity preservation).
- User study lacks statistical rigor: no confidence intervals, inter-annotator agreement, quality control, or significance tests; the design (1 s exposure) may bias results and is not justified for this domain.
- Comparison to [36] is only qualitative and not strictly comparable (conditioning differs). Stronger baselines are needed: e.g., pix2pix/cGAN with keypoint heatmaps, a residual refinement trained end-to-end, or modern image-to-image methods under the same protocol.

5) Experimental design details missing/unclear:
- λ for Eq. (4) is relegated to supplementary; the main text should report the chosen value(s) and sensitivity. Similarly for the mask weighting and morphological parameters.
- Data construction: Market-1501 pairing scheme is not fully described (per-ID pairing vs. all pairs, handling camera overlap, imbalance). Reporting the distribution of pose changes would help.
- No robustness analysis to pose detector errors; the system’s sensitivity to noisy/missing keypoints is not quantified.

6) Over-claiming:
- “In principle … arbitrary pose” is asserted without systematic stress tests (rare/extreme poses are only anecdotal; failure cases are not analyzed quantitatively).
- Training “converges faster” with difference maps is claimed but not substantiated with learning curves or wall-clock comparisons.

Clarity/Presentation issues
- Numerous typos and formatting problems: “st Conference…”, “the the generator”, stray figure labels (“Our generation sequence”, “FConv-ResUAE”), inconsistent notation (ÎB, ÎB1, ÎB2), and missing figure references hurt readability.
- References are incomplete/incorrectly formatted (missing venues/years for several entries). Some key related works on pose-guided synthesis and human image generation around 2016–2017 could be better contextualized.
- Important choices are deferred to supplementary; the main text should be self-contained enough to reproduce results.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a timely, practical task with a reasonable decomposition and simple, effective components.
- Clear benefit of pose heatmap embedding over coordinate vectors.
- Qualitative results on DeepFashion are convincing; the two-stage approach helps perceptual quality.

Cons for acceptance
- Technical inconsistencies (difference map vs full image) and underspecified losses/architectural choices.
- Weak and partially inappropriate quantitative evaluation; lack of identity/pose correctness metrics.
- Limited, non-rigorous baselines and user study; claims not strongly supported.
- Clarity and formatting issues throughout, hindering reproducibility.

Suggestions
- Correct and unify equations/notation; explicitly define residual vs direct prediction and reflect in D’s inputs/targets.
- Provide full details for pose mask construction and evaluate alternatives (α weighting, scale-adaptive radii).
- Add identity similarity, pose accuracy, and FID metrics; include stronger baselines; report learning curves and stability.
- Justify/remove the FC bottleneck; consider BN or other stabilizers; report parameter counts.
- Improve writing, figures, and references; release code or a detailed appendix with hyperparameters.

Overall recommendation: Reject (promising idea and solid qualitative results, but too many technical, evaluative, and presentation shortcomings to meet NeurIPS standards in current form).

Confidence: High.
