

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

This paper proposes a two-stage approach for generating person images conditioned on a reference image and a target pose. The authors argue that explicitly conditioning on pose information (represented as keypoints) enables more direct control over the generation process compared to previous methods. The approach divides the task into: (1) a stage-I model that integrates pose information with the reference image to generate a coarse result capturing global structure, and (2) a stage-II model that refines the coarse result using a conditional GAN to generate sharper images.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a clear and well-motivated task that is relevant to applications like movie making and pose estimation.
- The two-stage approach is a reasonable decomposition of a challenging problem.
- The pose mask loss is a thoughtful modification that addresses background interference.
- The experimental results on two datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach compared to ablation studies.

**Weaknesses:**

**Methodological and Technical Issues:**
1. The paper claims to use "a state-of-the-art pose estimator" without proper citation or specification. The reference [2] is listed in the references as "Realtime multi-person 2d pose estimation using part affinity fields," but this citation appears incomplete and lacks the author names. This is a significant omission that undermines the reproducibility of their method.

2. The pose mask loss (Equation 1) contains inconsistent notation: "LG1 = ‖(G1(IA, PB)− IB) (1 +MB)‖1." The parentheses should be consistent (either "(1 + MB)" or "(1 + MB)"), and the spacing around the multiplication is unclear.

3. The paper claims that "the use of difference maps speeds up the convergence of model training," but provides no empirical evidence for this claim (e.g., training time comparisons or convergence curves). This is a significant claim that should be supported by data.

4. The paper states "No batch normalization or dropout are applied" in the network architecture, but provides no justification for this design choice. Without empirical evidence demonstrating that these techniques would degrade performance, this is an unusual and unsupported architectural decision.

**Experimental and Evaluation Issues:**
1. The paper acknowledges that "image quality does not always correspond to such image similarity metrics" (SSIM and Inception Score), but fails to provide additional metrics that better capture perceptual quality (e.g., FID, human evaluation beyond the user study).

2. In the user study, the authors note that their full model "G1+G2+D gets a lower score than G1+D on Market-1501 because it transfers more backgrounds from the condition image, which brings extra artifacts." This is a significant weakness that they acknowledge but don't address in their method, yet they don't provide a solution or even discuss how to mitigate this issue.

3. The comparison with Zhao et al. [36] is misleading. [36] addresses multi-view person image synthesis from a single view, while the paper's task is pose-guided image generation. The comparison in Figure 5 is not apples-to-apples, and the paper fails to acknowledge this fundamental difference in the problem formulation.

**Writing and Clarity Issues:**
1. The paper uses inconsistent terminology: "pose" and "keypoints" are used interchangeably without clear distinction.

2. The paper claims to be the first to "use poses in the format of keypoints to model diverse human body appearance," but Lassner et al. [15] already used body part segments (which are similar to keypoints) for generating people in clothing. This claim of novelty is overstated.

3. The paper states "a wide range of poses is consistent with any given viewpoint making the conditioning less expressive than in our work," but doesn't provide evidence or explanation for why this is the case.

4. There are grammatical errors and awkward phrasing throughout the paper (e.g., "It is difficult for a complete end-to-end framework to do this because it has to generate both correct poses and detailed appearance simultaneously").

**Overall Assessment:**
While the paper presents a reasonable approach to pose-guided person image generation, the technical and methodological shortcomings are significant. The paper lacks proper citations for key components, contains inconsistent notation, makes unsupported claims about training speed, and provides an unfair comparison with prior work. The acknowledgment of their method's weakness in background transfer (which degrades user study results) without a solution is particularly concerning.

The paper's contribution is incremental at best. The two-stage approach is similar to prior work (e.g., [35, 9, 3, 19]), and the pose mask loss is a minor modification. The claimed novelty is overstated, and the paper fails to adequately address the limitations of their approach.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. While the paper presents a coherent approach to a relevant problem, the technical flaws, unsupported claims, and lack of proper comparison with related work undermine its scientific contribution. The paper would benefit from significant revision to address these issues before consideration for publication. The minor flaws identified in this review collectively exceed the threshold for acceptance at NIPS.
