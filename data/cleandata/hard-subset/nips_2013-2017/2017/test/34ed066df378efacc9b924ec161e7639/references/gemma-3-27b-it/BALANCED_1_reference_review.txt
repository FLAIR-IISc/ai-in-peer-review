This paper proposes a two-stage GAN-based approach for pose-guided person image generation. The core idea is to transfer the appearance of a person from a source pose to a target pose, conditioned on both a reference image and the target pose keypoints. The method divides the problem into a coarse structure generation stage (Stage-I) followed by a refinement stage (Stage-II) employing a novel difference map approach within the GAN framework.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical and interesting problem with potential applications in movie making and data augmentation for pose estimation. The problem setup is clearly defined, and the proposed approach seems reasonable, leveraging existing techniques (U-Net, DCGAN) in a novel combination. The related work section is comprehensive, covering relevant literature in generative modeling and conditional image generation, and appropriately positions the contribution within the field.  The two-stage approach with the difference map in Stage-II is a clever idea to tackle the difficulty of generating complex images directly.  The experimental results on two datasets, DeepFashion and Market-1501, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, both qualitatively and quantitatively.  The ablation studies exploring different pose embeddings and losses provide valuable insights. The inclusion of a user study provides further validation.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. While the idea of a difference map is good, its theoretical justification is limited. Why does learning a difference map make the problem "easier"? A more detailed explanation of this would be beneficial.  The ablation studies, while present, are somewhat limited in scope. For example, the impact of different network architectures within each stage is not explored. The choice of U-Net and DCGAN is presented as almost default without justification. The user study, while appreciated, has a relatively small sample size (30 users) and lacks details about the participant demographics, which could affect the results.  The discussion of failure cases is brief and lacks a deeper analysis of the underlying reasons for the failures beyond data imbalance.  The quantitative results, especially the SSIM scores, seem counterintuitive, showing sometimes lower scores for the refined images despite their visual superiority, highlighting a reliance on metrics that don't always correlate well with perceptual quality.  The paper lacks a clear discussion of computational cost and training time for the proposed method.  Finally, while the related work section is extensive, the connection to the burgeoning field of image manipulation and editing (e.g., StyleGAN-based methods) could be strengthened.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel task formulation: Conditioning image generation on both a reference image and an intended pose is a new approach.
*   Effective two-stage framework: The divide-and-conquer strategy addresses the complexity of the pose transfer problem effectively.
*   Difference map approach: Learning a difference map in Stage-II is a clever idea that accelerates training and improves image quality.
*   Solid experimental validation:  The quantitative and qualitative results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on two datasets.
*   Ablation studies: The inclusion of ablation studies provides insights into the contribution of different components.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited theoretical justification:  The rationale behind the difference map approach could be more thoroughly explained.
*   Limited ablation studies: The exploration of different network architectures and hyperparameters is insufficient.
*   Small user study: The user study has a small sample size and lacks detailed information about the participants.
*   Insufficient failure analysis: The discussion of failure cases is brief and lacks a deeper analysis.
*   Dependence on potentially misleading metrics: Reliance on SSIM alongside perceptual evaluation.
*   Lack of discussion about computational cost.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of generative modeling and pose-guided image manipulation.  The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates promising results. However, the weaknesses related to theoretical justification, limited ablation studies, and the user study prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form.

**Recommendation:**

Reject with encouragement to revise.

**Suggestions for improvement:**

1.  **Provide a more thorough theoretical justification for the difference map approach.**  Explain why learning a difference map simplifies the problem and leads to faster convergence.
2.  **Conduct more extensive ablation studies.** Explore different network architectures (e.g., different U-Net variants, alternative GAN architectures) within each stage and investigate the impact of different hyperparameters.
3.  **Expand the user study.** Increase the sample size and provide more details about the participant demographics. Consider using more robust evaluation metrics beyond simple preference ratings.
4.  **Provide a more in-depth analysis of failure cases.** Identify the common failure modes and discuss potential solutions.
5.  **Discuss the computational cost and training time of the proposed method.**
6.  **Strengthen the connection to related work in image manipulation and editing.**  Discuss how the proposed method compares to recent advances in this field (e.g., StyleGAN-based methods).
7.  **Consider adding a comparison to other relevant baselines beyond the one mentioned.** This will strengthen the assessment of the proposed approach.
8.  **Investigate using other metrics in addition to SSIM to assess image quality.** Consider LPIPS, FID, or other perceptual metrics that are known to correlate better with human perception.
