

# Peer Review: Pose Guided Person Image Generation

## Summary and Context

This paper introduces a novel two-stage framework for person image generation conditioned on a reference image and a target pose. The authors propose a meaningful extension to conditional image generation by explicitly incorporating pose information as a conditioning factor, addressing a challenging task where both pose transfer and appearance preservation are required. The method divides the problem into (1) a global structure stage using a U-Net with pose heatmaps and a novel pose mask loss, and (2) a detail refinement stage using a conditional GAN that generates a difference map. The work is relevant to NeurIPS as it advances conditional image generation with practical applications in movie making and human pose estimation.

## Strengths

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, the two-stage approach is well-motivated and addresses a genuine challenge in pose transfer: the difficulty of simultaneously generating correct poses and detailed appearance. The pose embedding using heatmaps (rather than raw coordinates) is practical and avoids expensive pose annotation. The pose mask loss is particularly clever, effectively focusing the generator on the human body while suppressing background interference - a significant technical contribution that addresses a real pain point in this domain.

The experimental evaluation is thorough. The authors test on two distinct datasets (Market-1501 and DeepFashion) with both quantitative (SSIM, Inception Score) and qualitative (user study) metrics. The ablation studies on pose embeddings and losses provide valuable insights into the design choices. The user study on AMT is well-conducted and shows the two-stage model generates more convincing results than one-stage alternatives.

## Weaknesses

While the paper is well-written and technically sound, several concerns need addressing:

1. **Novelty claim**: The paper claims novelty for "conditioning image generation on a reference image and an intended pose," but this overlaps significantly with recent work like Zhao et al. [36] on multi-view image synthesis. The comparison with [36] in Figure 5 is limited to qualitative examples without quantitative metrics. The authors should more clearly articulate how their pose-guided approach differs from previous conditional generation methods, particularly in terms of pose representation and control.

2. **Pose estimation dependency**: The method relies on a pose estimator to generate keypoints, but the paper doesn't analyze how errors in pose estimation propagate through the pipeline. This is critical as pose estimation errors could lead to incorrect pose transfer, which would be a fundamental limitation.

3. **Market-1501 user study contradiction**: The user study shows G1+G2+D (two-stage) gets *lower* scores than G1+D (one-stage) on Market-1501. The authors note this is because "G1+G2+D transfers more backgrounds from the condition image," but this explanation feels insufficient. A more thorough analysis of why the two-stage model performs worse in this specific dataset would strengthen the paper.

4. **Lack of computational analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss inference time or computational requirements, which is important for practical applications. A comparison with [36] on inference speed would help contextualize the trade-offs.

5. **Limited failure analysis**: The failure cases (Figure 6) are well-identified but could be more comprehensive. The paper should discuss how common these failures are and whether they're specific to the pose estimation step or the generation framework.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a technically sound approach to a meaningful problem in conditional image generation. The two-stage framework is well-justified, and the pose mask loss is a valuable technical contribution. The experimental evaluation is strong, though the novelty claims need refinement, and the Market-1501 user study contradiction requires better explanation.

**Pro arguments for acceptance**:
- Clear problem statement addressing a meaningful gap in conditional image generation
- Well-motivated two-stage approach with strong theoretical justification
- Novel pose mask loss that effectively addresses background interference
- Comprehensive evaluation with multiple datasets and metrics
- Practical applications with clear potential impact

**Con arguments against acceptance**:
- Novelty claim overstated relative to related work [36]
- Insufficient analysis of pose estimation error propagation
- Contradictory user study results on Market-1501 without adequate explanation
- Missing computational efficiency analysis

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper makes a solid contribution to conditional image generation with a well-executed approach to pose-guided person image generation. The main issue is the need for more precise articulation of novelty and addressing the Market-1501 user study contradiction. The authors should strengthen the related work section to better distinguish their approach from [36], provide more thorough analysis of the Market-1501 results, and discuss the computational aspects of their method.

The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how pose estimation errors affect the final results and whether the method can handle extreme pose variations. A brief analysis of the failure cases in terms of pose estimation accuracy would be valuable.

This work advances the state of the art in conditional image generation with a practical approach to pose manipulation, and with the requested revisions, it would make a strong contribution to NeurIPS.
