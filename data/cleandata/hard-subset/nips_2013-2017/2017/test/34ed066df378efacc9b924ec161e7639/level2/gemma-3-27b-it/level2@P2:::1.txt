This paper proposes a novel Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG) for synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses given a reference image and a target pose. The approach utilizes a two-stage framework: pose integration via a U-Net-like network followed by image refinement using an adversarial GAN. A masked L1 loss is introduced to focus on the human body during pose integration. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on both low-resolution re-identification images and high-resolution fashion photos.

The paper addresses an interesting and challenging problem with potential applications in areas like movie making and training data generation for pose estimation. The idea of explicitly conditioning image generation on pose is a valuable contribution, and the two-stage approach appears reasonable given the complexity of the task. The proposed mask loss is a sensible addition to guide the network to focus on the relevant body parts. The experiments on two datasets, with different resolutions, strengthens the evaluation.

However, several aspects of the paper require improvement.

**Quality:** While the overall approach seems sound, the technical details and justifications for certain design choices are somewhat lacking. The motivation for simplifying the residual block in Stage-I is not clearly explained. The choice of 18 keypoints for pose representation is arbitrary and not justified – a comparison with different numbers of keypoints, or different pose encoding schemes (e.g., pose vectors) would be beneficial. The paper claims faster convergence with the difference map approach in Stage-II, but doesn’t provide quantitative evidence to support this claim (e.g., training curves). The ablation study comparing different pose embedding methods (heatmap, coordinate) is somewhat unclear regarding *which* loss function was used alongside each embedding (the text says “G1-CE-L1”, but doesn't explicitly state if L1 loss was used for all). Further, while the paper mentions qualitative improvements with the mask loss, a quantitative comparison showing improvements in metrics like SSIM or IS with and without the mask loss would strengthen the argument. The comparison to the related work [36] appears limited; only a single qualitative example is shown, and the paper doesn’t delve into the differences in the datasets, training procedures, or network architectures that might explain the performance gap.

**Clarity:** The writing is generally clear, but there are instances where details are missing or could be more explicit. For example, the details about the hyperparameters (learning rate, batch size) are not provided in the main text, requiring referring to the experimental setup section. The description of the discriminator loss in Stage-II is a little convoluted. The explanation of *why* the generator learns a difference map is better than the initial presentation, but could be more directly stated earlier. The phrase “few man samples and complex texture” used in the failure case analysis is vague and unhelpful. A more detailed explanation would be needed for someone unfamiliar with the problem domain.

**Originality:** The use of GANs and U-Nets is not novel in itself. The contribution lies in the specific application to pose-guided person generation and the two-stage framework with the mask loss. While this combination is new, the paper could better highlight how it differs from existing image translation or pose transfer methods, particularly those that might use similar underlying architectures. A more thorough discussion of related work in pose space image manipulation or 3D human mesh reconstruction with texture mapping would be helpful to better position the contribution.

**Significance:** The proposed method has the potential to be significant, especially if it can be extended to more complex scenarios or applied to other object categories. However, the current evaluation focuses primarily on image quality and does not demonstrate any downstream task performance gains. For instance, showing that generated images can improve the performance of a pose estimation model or be used for data augmentation in a re-identification task would significantly strengthen the significance of the work. The user study, while providing some subjective evaluation, is limited and doesn’t provide a strong indicator of real-world impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
* Addresses an interesting and challenging problem with potential applications.
* Novel combination of techniques (two-stage GAN with mask loss) for pose-guided person generation.
* Demonstrates promising qualitative results on two datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
* Technical details and justifications are lacking.
* Limited quantitative evaluation and ablation studies.
* Comparison to related work is superficial.
* Lack of demonstration of downstream task performance.
* Clarity can be improved in certain sections.



I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a reasonable approach to a challenging problem, the lack of thorough evaluation, detailed technical explanations, and a strong connection to real-world applications weakens the overall contribution. Addressing the concerns raised above, particularly the quantitative evaluation, comparison to related work, and justification for design choices, could significantly improve the paper.
