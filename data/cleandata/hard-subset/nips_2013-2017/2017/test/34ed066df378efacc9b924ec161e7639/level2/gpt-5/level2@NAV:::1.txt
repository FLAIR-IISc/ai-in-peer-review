Summary and relation to prior work: The paper introduces PG2, a two-stage pose-guided person image generator conditioned on a reference image and explicit target pose keypoints. Stage I is a U-Net variant that integrates pose heatmaps and uses a pose-mask L1 to produce a coarse result emphasizing the foreground. Stage II is a conditional GAN trained to predict a residual “difference map” that refines details, with a pairwise discriminator D(I_A, Î_B) to enforce consistency with the conditioning image. Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) include ablations (pose embeddings, mask loss, one- vs two-stage) and user studies. The work builds on conditional generative modeling (GANs, DCGAN, pix2pix-like setups), pose estimation, and multi-view synthesis (e.g., StackGAN/InfoGAN/Reed et al.; DeepFashion/VariGAN), and positions itself by using explicit 2D keypoints rather than coarse view labels or part segments.

Quality: The claims are largely supported by thorough ablations and convincing qualitative results; the residual refinement and pairwise conditioning are well-motivated and effective. Quantitative evaluation (SSIM/IS and their masked variants) is informative but imperfect for perceptual fidelity; the AMT test is small-scale and lacks statistical analysis. The paper details architectures and training hyperparameters, aiding reproducibility, though code is not referenced.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized and readable, with clear figures illustrating the pipeline and ablations. A few references are inconsistently formatted; minor editorial issues do not impede comprehension.

Originality: Conditioning on explicit keypoint heatmaps for controllable person pose transfer and refining via a learned difference map is a novel and coherent combination. The pose-mask loss is a simple but useful idea. Relation to prior NIPS work is adequate, though a direct pix2pix-style baseline would strengthen positioning.

Significance and usefulness: The method addresses a relevant problem with practical utility (content creation, data augmentation, pose-conditioned synthesis). Results are state-of-the-art relative to closest prior (VariGAN) and likely to be adopted.

Limitations and suggestions: Background mismatch remains a challenge; consider leveraging a learned person segmentation or background inpainting. Identity preservation is not quantitatively assessed; evaluate with re-id features or face/body embeddings. Add perceptual/feature-matching losses and report FID/LPIPS with person masks. Provide statistical tests for AMT and more diverse baselines (e.g., pix2pix with keypoint channels). Discuss robustness to pose estimator errors and occlusions.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, well-motivated two-stage design with strong qualitative gains
- Thorough ablations; practical and impactful problem
- Simple, reproducible components likely to influence follow-up work

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited quantitative perceptual evaluation and identity metrics
- Background artifacts; incomplete analysis of failure modes and estimator noise
- Missing strong baselines and statistical rigor in user study
