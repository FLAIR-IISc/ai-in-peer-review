

This paper proposes a novel two-stage framework for pose-guided person image generation, where the goal is to synthesize person images in arbitrary poses given a reference image of that person and a target pose. The authors introduce a Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) that divides the task into pose integration (stage-I) and image refinement (stage-II). Stage-I uses a U-Net-like architecture to integrate pose information (represented as 18 heatmaps) with the reference image, while stage-II employs adversarial training to refine the coarse output. A key contribution is the pose mask loss that focuses generation on human body regions rather than background.

The paper demonstrates strong experimental support for its claims. On both Market-1501 (128×64 re-identification images) and DeepFashion (256×256 fashion photos), the authors show significant improvements over baselines, including variants of their own model (e.g., different pose embeddings, loss functions) and the most related work [36]. The qualitative results (Figure 4) clearly show their two-stage approach generates more photo-realistic images with better pose alignment and detail preservation. Quantitative results using SSIM and Inception Score, along with user studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk, further validate the effectiveness of their approach.

The work is well-positioned within the field, building upon GAN-based image generation literature (e.g., [6, 21]) while introducing novel contributions to pose-guided synthesis. The pose mask loss and two-stage strategy represent meaningful innovations over previous work that used less explicit pose conditioning [36].

Strengths include clear problem formulation, comprehensive experiments across two datasets, and a well-structured two-stage approach that effectively addresses the challenges of pose transfer. The paper also appropriately acknowledges limitations, such as difficulties with rare poses and background artifacts.

Weaknesses are minimal but include some lack of discussion about computational efficiency and limited exploration of how the model handles extreme pose variations. The user study, while helpful, could have included more detailed comparisons with additional baselines.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in pose-guided image generation. The work is technically sound, well-written, and has clear practical applications in areas like movie making and pose estimation. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.
