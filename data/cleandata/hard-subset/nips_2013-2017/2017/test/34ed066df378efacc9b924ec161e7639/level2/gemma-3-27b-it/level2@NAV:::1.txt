## Review of Pose Guided Person Image Generation

This paper introduces a novel approach to person image generation, specifically controlling pose while maintaining identity. The core idea of conditioning generation on both a reference image and a target pose is a valuable contribution, addressing a practical problem with applications in areas like movie making and training data augmentation for pose estimation. The authors propose a two-stage network, PG, comprising a pose integration stage using a U-Net and a refinement stage utilizing a conditional DCGAN. A novel pose mask loss is also introduced to focus generation on the person's body, mitigating background influence.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation and methodology. The two-stage approach is a sensible strategy for tackling the complexity of pose transfer, and the use of a difference map in the second stage appears to be a clever optimization. The experimental results on both the Market-1501 and DeepFashion datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, with qualitative results showing convincing pose transfer and identity preservation. The ablation studies investigating different pose embeddings and the pose mask loss provide valuable insight into the components’ contributions. Comparing to [36] further highlights the advantage of explicit pose control.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While quantitative results (SSIM, IS) are reported, their interpretation is somewhat hampered by known limitations of these metrics, which the authors acknowledge.  A more robust quantitative evaluation, perhaps incorporating perceptual metrics or user studies focusing specifically on pose accuracy, would strengthen the claims. The user study, while helpful, focuses on realism rather than accurate pose transfer. The discussion of failure cases is limited and could be expanded. The analysis of the λ parameter for the L1 loss is mentioned but relegated to the supplementary materials, hindering its integration into the main discussion. Additionally, the choice of 18 keypoints seems somewhat arbitrary and isn’t thoroughly justified. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel task formulation: Controlling image generation via explicit pose conditioning.
*   Well-designed two-stage architecture with a clear rationale.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness on multiple datasets with compelling qualitative results.
*   Ablation studies provide insights into the contribution of individual components.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited quantitative evaluation beyond standard metrics (SSIM, IS).
*   User study focuses on realism, not pose accuracy.
*   Insufficient analysis of failure cases. 
*   Choice of 18 keypoints not fully justified.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a novel approach and promising results. While the quantitative evaluation could be strengthened, the clear presentation, well-motivated design, and compelling qualitative results make it a worthwhile contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically encouraging the authors to expand on the failure case analysis and consider incorporating more pose-specific evaluation metrics.
