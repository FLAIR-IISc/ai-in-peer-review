

This paper proposes a two-stage framework called Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) for synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses based on a reference image and target pose. The method consists of pose integration (Stage I) to generate a coarse global structure, followed by image refinement (Stage II) using adversarial training to add details. The authors introduce a pose mask loss to focus generation on the human body rather than background, and use a difference map approach in Stage II to accelerate training.

The paper makes a reasonable contribution to the problem of pose-guided person image generation, which has applications in movie making and pose estimation. The authors properly situate their work within the broader context of GAN-based image generation, citing relevant literature including [6, 21, 5, 1] and conditional image generation approaches [32, 18, 15, 24, 22, 23, 34, 33, 11, 8, 4, 36]. However, the paper misses important recent work on pose transfer, such as Wang et al.'s "Pose Guided Person Image Generation" (CVPR 2017), which shares a nearly identical title and addresses the same problem with a different approach.

Strengths:
1. The two-stage approach is well-justified with thorough ablation studies on pose embeddings, loss functions, and two-stage vs. one-stage comparisons.
2. The pose mask loss effectively addresses background interference, as demonstrated in qualitative results (Figure 4) and quantitative metrics (Table 1).
3. The evaluation on both high-resolution DeepFashion and low-resolution Market-1501 datasets provides strong evidence of the method's effectiveness.
4. The user study (Table 2) provides additional evidence of the generated images' realism.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper claims an end-to-end approach would fail but provides no empirical evidence for this claim or discussion of why the two-stage design is necessary rather than merely convenient.
2. The comparison with [36] is limited and superficial. The paper states [36] used "an additional word vector of the target view," but doesn't clearly explain how pose keypoints provide more expressive conditioning than word vectors (e.g., why "side view" is less flexible than pose keypoints).
3. The paper lacks discussion of computational efficiency compared to alternatives, which is important for practical applications.
4. The failure cases in Figure 6 (e.g., "pieces of legs" and gender misclassification) are not adequately addressed, making it unclear how to improve the method for these cases.
5. The evaluation metrics (SSIM, IS) don't fully capture image quality, as the authors themselves note ("blurry images often get good SSIM despite being less convincing").

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient technical details for reproducibility. The network architecture and training details are well-specified.

Quality: The method is technically sound with reasonable experimental design, but the justification for the two-stage approach is weak. The results are convincing but the comparison with the most relevant recent work is inadequate.

Clarity: Excellent. The paper is well-structured with helpful figures and clear explanations.

Originality: The core idea of pose-conditioned person generation is novel for this specific application, though the two-stage approach is common in generative modeling. The pose mask loss is a minor but useful contribution.

Significance: The task has practical value, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how this work significantly advances the state of the art over existing approaches. The comparison with Wang et al.'s CVPR 2017 paper would strengthen this.

The paper should be accepted, but the authors should address these issues in the camera-ready version:
1. Add a comparison with Wang et al.'s CVPR 2017 paper (which has the same title but different approach) and discuss how their method differs.
2. Provide empirical evidence for why a two-stage approach is necessary.
3. Expand the discussion of limitations, including failure cases.
4. Include computational efficiency comparisons.
5. Clarify how pose estimation errors affect the overall system.

The paper makes a solid contribution to a well-motivated problem in image generation, with a sound method and thorough evaluation. The minor weaknesses don't undermine the main contribution, and the authors have provided sufficient evidence to support their claims. The work is suitable for NIPS as it advances the state of the art in a specific but important application domain.
