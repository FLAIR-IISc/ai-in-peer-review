Summary
The paper addresses pose-guided person image synthesis: given a reference image of a person and a target pose (keypoints), generate a realistic image of the same person in the target pose. The proposed PG2 framework is two-stage: (i) a U-Net-like encoder–decoder (with residual blocks and a fully-connected bottleneck) integrates the target pose (as 18 heatmaps) with the input image to produce a coarse result optimized with a masked L1 loss that prioritizes foreground over background; (ii) a conditional adversarial refinement network (also U-Net-like, without an FC layer) generates a residual “difference map” to sharpen and add details, trained with a pairwise discriminator D that sees (condition image, output) vs (condition image, ground-truth). Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) include ablations on pose embeddings, the masked L1 loss, and one-stage vs two-stage training, plus user studies and masked SSIM/IS metrics. Qualitative results are compelling; quantitative gains are modest but consistent.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on VAEs, GANs, and image-to-image translation. Conditioning on poses via keypoint heatmaps is a reasonable design used in related tasks (e.g., pose/segmentation-guided generation). The two-stage design echoes stacked generation (e.g., StackGAN), and the “difference map” is akin to residual learning. The pairwise conditional discriminator is standard in conditional GANs (e.g., pix2pix). Compared with view-conditioned generation (e.g., Zhao et al. 2016), the explicit keypoint conditioning affords more fine-grained control. Concurrent/related works on pose-based person image synthesis and deformable warping are not fully discussed; nevertheless, the combination of design choices tailored to full-body person synthesis is valuable.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation with practical relevance (data augmentation, content creation).
- Sensible two-stage “structure then details” approach; decomposition improves stability and quality.
- Effective pose embedding via keypoint heatmaps; ablation against alternative embeddings is convincing.
- Masked L1 loss is simple and effective at de-emphasizing background mismatch; ablation supports the claim.
- Residual (difference-map) refinement is a well-motivated engineering choice that speeds convergence.
- Qualitative results on two datasets are strong, with interpretable failure cases and insightful analysis.
- User study provides complementary perceptual evidence; the paper is generally well-organized.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty is moderate. Many components (U-Net, pose heatmaps, conditional D, residual learning, two-stage GANs) are known; the main contribution is their integration for this task and a masked loss. The methodological deltas over a strong pix2pix-style baseline are incremental.
- Quantitative evaluation is limited. SSIM/IS (and masked variants) are weak proxies; masked-IS is especially questionable because masking disrupts natural image statistics for the Inception network. More appropriate metrics (e.g., FID, LPIPS) or human preference comparisons (A/B with longer exposure) would strengthen the case.
- Identity preservation is not quantitatively evaluated. Since preserving a person’s appearance is central, measuring similarity with a re-id network (feature distance, or re-id retrieval performance when augmenting training with generated images) would be informative.
- Background handling remains problematic, especially on Market-1501; the model appears to copy background via skip connections despite the masked L1. A stronger mechanism (explicit segmentation/foreground compositing, patch discriminators focused on the person, or background randomization during training) could mitigate artifacts and reduce AMT penalties.
- The rationale for the stage-I fully connected bottleneck is not fully justified; it is parameter-heavy and may hurt scalability vs alternatives (dilated convs, attention).
- Missing or incomplete comparisons. A direct comparison with a tuned pix2pix baseline that takes (reference image + pose heatmaps) and uses a PatchGAN/perceptual loss would be more compelling than the reported one-stage adversarial setting. Recent related pose-guided/deformable-GAN works should be cited and contrasted.
- Diversity/uncertainty is not addressed; the deterministic pipeline tends to average plausible details, especially for occluded regions.

Quality
The approach is technically sound, and the ablations are thoughtful. Claims about masked losses and two-stage refinement are supported by experiments. However, the evaluation lacks stronger, task-relevant metrics and baselines (identity preservation, re-id augmentation), which limits the strength of the significance claim.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well-structured; figures effectively illustrate improvements and failure modes. Some references are incomplete, and certain design choices (FC layer in stage-I, discriminator configuration, mask construction) would benefit from more detail in the main text (currently deferred to supplementary).

Originality
Moderate. The task emphasis and careful integration are useful, but individual components are largely established. The residual “difference-map” framing and the masked L1 foreground emphasis are incremental.

Significance
Promising for controllable person synthesis, with practical potential in data augmentation and content creation. Demonstrated qualitative advances are notable; significance would be much stronger with quantitative identity preservation and downstream utility (e.g., improved re-id with synthetic training data).

Questions for the authors
- Can you report identity preservation via a trained re-id network (feature distances or retrieval accuracy) and/or re-id performance gains when augmenting training with generated images?
- How is masked-IS computed exactly, and how sensitive is it to the masking procedure? Have you considered FID or LPIPS?
- What is the impact of replacing the FC bottleneck in stage-I with dilated/atrous convolutions or attention?
- Can you stabilize background by training with random background replacement or by using an explicit person segmentation and a foreground-only patch discriminator?
- How robust is the method to pose-estimation errors or missing keypoints?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, useful problem; solid engineering; convincing qualitative results; thorough ablations within the proposed framework.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty; limited quantitative evaluation; no identity preservation or downstream-task validation; background artifacts on challenging data.

Recommendation
Weak accept.

Reviewer confidence
Medium.
