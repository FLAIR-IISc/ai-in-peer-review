This paper introduces a novel two-stage framework, Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), for generating person images in arbitrary poses given a reference image and a target pose. The approach divides the problem into pose integration (Stage-I) and image refinement (Stage-II), employing U-Net-like architectures and GANs respectively. The paper proposes a pose mask loss to focus on transferring body appearance and demonstrates results on both low-resolution re-identification and high-resolution fashion datasets.

The core idea of conditioning image generation on both a reference image *and* pose is a logical extension of previous work in conditional image generation (e.g., [18, 24, 32]) and multi-view synthesis [36]. However, directly controlling pose through keypoints, as opposed to view angles or textual descriptions, is a novel contribution. The two-stage approach, borrowing from ideas in image editing [35, 9, 3, 19], is a sensible strategy for tackling the complex problem of pose transfer. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The task of explicitly conditioning generation on both a reference image and a specified pose is novel.  The explicit use of pose keypoints, as opposed to implicit conditioning through other modalities, is a strong point.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed architecture, combining U-Nets and GANs, is well-motivated and builds on established techniques. The pose mask loss seems like a reasonable approach to improve the focus on body appearance.
* **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide extensive experimental results on two datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach through both quantitative metrics (SSIM, IS) and qualitative visual comparisons. The ablation studies (different pose embeddings, loss functions, one vs. two-stage) are helpful in understanding the contribution of each component.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The two-stage process is clearly explained, and the diagrams (Figures 2 and 3) are helpful.
* **Thorough Comparison:** The comparison to [36] is appropriate and demonstrates a clear improvement.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison:** While the comparison to [36] is good, a more comprehensive comparison with other relevant works would strengthen the paper. For example, comparing against methods that perform image-to-image translation or pose transfer (even if not specifically for people) could provide valuable context.
* **Qualitative Limitations:** While visual results are provided, the comparisons are sometimes difficult to interpret.  A more systematic evaluation (e.g., user studies with clearer instructions and scoring criteria) would be beneficial, and the presented user study has some limitations (discussed in the paper itself).
* **Failure Cases:** The discussion of failure cases is a good start, but it could be expanded. Analyzing *why* the model fails in certain scenarios (e.g., complex poses, clothing interactions) would provide valuable insights and suggest directions for future work.
* **Dependency on Pose Estimator:**  The reliance on a pre-trained pose estimator [2] introduces a potential source of error. The quality of the generated images is directly tied to the accuracy of the estimated pose. This dependency should be more thoroughly discussed.
* **Metric Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of SSIM and IS, but the results still rely heavily on these metrics.  Exploring alternative evaluation metrics that better capture perceptual quality could be beneficial.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a novel and challenging problem with a well-motivated approach.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The paper is well-written and clear, making it easy to understand the contributions.
* The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the design choices.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The comparison to related work is somewhat limited.
* The reliance on a pre-trained pose estimator introduces a potential weakness.
* The subjective nature of image quality makes it difficult to fully assess the performance based on quantitative metrics alone.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution and deserves to be accepted. The novelty of the task, the sound technical approach, and the thorough experimental evaluation outweigh the minor weaknesses. While more extensive comparisons and a more in-depth analysis of failure cases would be beneficial, the paper presents a significant advancement in the field of conditional image generation.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
