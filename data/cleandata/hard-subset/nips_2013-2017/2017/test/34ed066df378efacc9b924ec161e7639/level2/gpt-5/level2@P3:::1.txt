Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses pose-guided person image generation: given a reference image of a person and a target pose (as 2D keypoints), synthesize that person under the new pose. The proposed PG2 framework is two-stage: (i) a U-Net-like encoder-decoder integrates the input image with target pose heatmaps to produce a coarse image aligned to the target pose, trained with an L1 reconstruction augmented by a pose mask that emphasizes the human foreground; (ii) a refinement stage trains a U-Net-like conditional GAN to predict a residual “difference map” that sharpens details, with a pairwise discriminator that takes (condition image, generated image) versus (condition image, target) and a masked L1 regularizer. Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) provide qualitative results, ablations on pose embeddings, loss functions, and single- vs two-stage training, plus quantitative SSIM/IS and masked variants and a user study. A comparison to VariGAN [Zhao et al., 2017] is included.

The work builds on conditional generation with VAEs/GANs [Kingma & Welling; Mirza & Osindero], image-to-image translation with conditional GANs [Isola et al.], view synthesis [Yang et al.; Chen & Grauman; Zhao et al.], and people/clothing generation [Lassner et al.]. Relative to view-conditioned generation (e.g., VariGAN, which uses view labels), conditioning on explicit pose keypoint heatmaps affords finer control. The two-stage design echoes stacked or coarse-to-fine generators [e.g., StackGAN; Johnson et al. in perceptual losses], but here tailored to pose transfer with a residual refinement objective.

Quality
- Methodological soundness: The two-stage decomposition is sensible for disentangling structure (pose) and high-frequency appearance. Using keypoint heatmaps as pose input and a foreground-weighted mask L1 are appropriate choices. The pairwise discriminator matches standard practice in conditional GANs (as in pix2pix), and the residual learning objective is a reasonable design that likely eases training.
- Empirical support: The paper includes informative ablations (pose embeddings: coordinates vs heatmaps; L1 vs mask-L1; one- vs two-stage). Qualitative results are compelling, especially on DeepFashion. Quantitative metrics (SSIM/IS and masked variants) are provided along with a user study.
- Gaps: (a) No quantitative assessment of identity preservation (e.g., re-ID similarity or face/appearance embeddings), which is critical for this task. (b) No quantitative measure of pose accuracy on generated images (e.g., running a pose estimator and comparing to the target). (c) The claim that residual prediction accelerates convergence is plausible but not substantiated with training curves or time-to-target metrics. (d) Background handling remains ad hoc; artifacts are visible on Market-1501, and masked metrics partly sidestep this challenge.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a useful high-level figure and thorough description of stages and losses. Architectural details (number of blocks, stride, use of residual blocks) and training hyperparameters are summarized. The pose mask construction is briefly described; a more explicit procedure would improve reproducibility. Some references appear incomplete in the provided text, but this may be a formatting artifact. The distinction between the discriminator used and standard conditional GAN discriminators could be phrased more carefully, as the pairwise setup is standard.

Originality
The problem formulation—pose-guided person image generation conditioned on a specific reference image plus explicit 2D keypoint pose—is timely and distinctly more controllable than view labels alone. The two-stage pipeline with a residual difference map and a pose-masked L1 is a thoughtful combination; none of the components individually are entirely novel, but the integration for this task is novel for NIPS 2017. The ablation on pose embeddings highlights a practical and effective representation choice.

Significance
The task is important for controllable image synthesis, data augmentation for pose/re-ID, content creation, and human-centric manipulation. The results on both low- and higher-resolution datasets suggest the approach could become a baseline for future work. The lack of identity and pose metrics limits the immediate impact from a measurement standpoint, but the qualitative advance is notable. The framework is likely to spur follow-up improvements (e.g., better warping, attention, or 3D reasoning).

Strengths
- Clear and practical decomposition into pose integration and refinement, with strong qualitative results.
- Effective and well-justified pose representation via keypoint heatmaps; ablation supports the choice.
- Foreground-focused mask L1 improves robustness to background differences.
- Residual refinement with adversarial training yields sharper details and stabilizes the second stage.
- Comprehensive ablations and a user study; evaluation on two datasets with different resolutions and challenges.

Weaknesses
- No identity preservation evaluation; for person-centric generation this is crucial.
- No pose accuracy evaluation of outputs; only visual inspection.
- Quantitative gains are modest on SSIM/IS; masked variants help but are non-standard and may obscure background issues.
- Comparison to other strong conditional baselines (e.g., pix2pix conditioned on both input image and pose heatmaps with a perceptual loss) is missing.
- Limited discussion of failure modes and of the sensitivity to pose detection errors.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Add identity preservation metrics: compute similarity using a re-ID network or a face/body embedding between the reference and generated images under the target pose.
- Add pose accuracy metrics: run a pose estimator on generated images and report PCK/OKS relative to the target pose.
- Include a baseline using a single-stage pix2pix-style conditional GAN with input image concatenated with pose heatmaps, trained with adversarial + L1 and optionally a VGG perceptual loss; compare to the two-stage method.
- Provide evidence for faster convergence of residual refinement (e.g., training curves, number of iterations to a quality threshold).

Nice-to-have (optional):
- Explore a perceptual/feature-matching loss in stage II to reduce artifacts while keeping sharpness.
- Clarify and release the pose mask generation procedure; consider learning the foreground attention map.
- Report robustness to pose estimator noise (e.g., perturb keypoints).
- Discuss potential downstream utility (e.g., data augmentation for re-ID) with a small-scale study.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and well-scoped problem with a clear, effective design.
- Strong qualitative improvements and convincing ablations on pose representation and two-stage refinement.
- Demonstrates controllable generation at 256×256 resolution with realistic details.
- Likely to be influential for subsequent pose-guided synthesis work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing key evaluations (identity and pose correctness), leaving a central claim under-quantified.
- Limited baseline comparisons; unclear how much the two-stage design surpasses a carefully tuned single-stage conditional GAN with perceptual loss.
- Quantitative metrics used are limited and partly non-standard; background artifacts remain.

Overall assessment
A solid and timely contribution that presents a practical and effective approach to pose-guided person image generation, with good clarity and convincing qualitative results. Strengthening the evaluation along identity and pose metrics and adding stronger baselines would significantly improve the scientific rigor. With those additions, the work would be a strong fit for NIPS.
