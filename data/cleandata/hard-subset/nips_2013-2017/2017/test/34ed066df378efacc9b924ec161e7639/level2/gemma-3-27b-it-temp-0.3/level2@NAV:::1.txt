## Review of Pose Guided Person Image Generation

This paper proposes a novel two-stage framework, Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), for generating person images in arbitrary poses given a reference image and a target pose. The core idea is to decouple the problem into pose integration (Stage-I) and image refinement (Stage-II), leveraging U-Net-like architectures and adversarial training. The authors address a challenging problem with potential applications in areas like movie making and data augmentation for pose estimation.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation and approach. The proposed method builds upon existing GAN-based image generation techniques, specifically incorporating pose information through heatmap embeddings and a novel pose mask loss to focus on transferring body appearance. The two-stage approach is justified as a way to handle the complexity of simultaneously generating correct pose and detailed appearance. The experiments on both low-resolution (Market-1501) and high-resolution (DeepFashion) datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, with qualitative and quantitative results presented. The ablation studies, comparing different pose embeddings and loss functions, are particularly valuable.

A key strength lies in the explicit use of pose information, which allows for more controlled image generation compared to methods relying solely on visual cues. The pose mask loss is a clever addition, improving the quality of generated images by focusing on the relevant regions. The comparison with related work [36] shows a clear advantage of the proposed method in generating more realistic and detailed images.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the quantitative results show improvements, the differences in SSIM and IS are often small. The user study results are somewhat mixed, with the two-stage model performing better on DeepFashion but worse on Market-1501, suggesting potential issues with background transfer. The discussion of failure cases is limited, and a more in-depth analysis of the limitations of the method would be beneficial. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent advancements in pose-guided image manipulation, beyond the single related work cited.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel task and approach to pose-guided person image generation.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation of the method.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness through quantitative and qualitative experiments.
*   Useful ablation studies and insightful analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited improvement in quantitative metrics.
*   Mixed results in the user study.
*   Lack of a comprehensive comparison with recent related work.
*   Limited discussion of failure cases and limitations.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of image generation. While there are some areas for improvement, the novelty of the approach, the clear presentation, and the promising experimental results make it a worthwhile contribution. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, specifically addressing the limitations and expanding the comparison with related work. The authors should also provide a more detailed analysis of the user study results and discuss potential solutions to the observed issues with background transfer.
