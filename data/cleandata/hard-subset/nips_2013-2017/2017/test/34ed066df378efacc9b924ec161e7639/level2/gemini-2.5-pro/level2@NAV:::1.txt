This paper introduces the novel task of pose-guided person image generation: synthesizing a realistic image of a person from a source image, conditioned on a target pose. The authors propose a two-stage framework, the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG²), to tackle this problem. The first stage uses a U-Net-like architecture to generate a coarse image that matches the target pose structure, guided by a novel pose mask loss to handle background changes. The second stage employs a conditional GAN to refine this coarse output, adding photorealistic details. This work builds upon established techniques like U-Nets and conditional GANs but applies them to a new, fine-grained conditional generation task, distinguishing itself from prior work on more general view synthesis.

The paper’s strengths are numerous.
**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Originality and Significance:** The problem of explicit pose transfer is both novel and highly relevant for applications in data augmentation, media production, and e-commerce. The proposed method offers a degree of control not present in previous view-synthesis literature.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The two-stage, coarse-to-fine approach is well-motivated and effectively decouples the challenges of global structure transformation and local texture synthesis. The design choices, such as using pose heatmaps for conditioning and a mask-based loss, are sound and well-justified.
3.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The claims are strongly supported by comprehensive experiments on two distinct datasets (DeepFashion and Market-1501). The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed pose embedding, the pose mask loss, and the two-stage design. The inclusion of a user study alongside quantitative metrics (SSIM, IS) provides a robust evaluation of the generated image quality.

The paper has a few minor weaknesses that could be addressed.
**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Dependence on Pose Estimation:** The framework's performance is implicitly tied to the quality of the upstream pose estimator. A brief discussion on the model's robustness to potential inaccuracies or missing keypoints in the estimated poses would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Clarity on Refinement Stage:** The description of the Stage-II generator (G2) as learning a "difference map" could be clearer. The formulation suggests G2 directly outputs a refined image rather than a residual to be added to the coarse input. Clarifying this mechanism would improve reproducibility.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel problem, a solid technical solution, and a thorough experimental validation. The strengths significantly outweigh the minor weaknesses, and the work represents a valuable contribution to the field of conditional image generation.
