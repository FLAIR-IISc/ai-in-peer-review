- Proposes PG2: a two-stage Pose Guided Person Generation Network (Stage I pose integration for coarse structure; Stage II adversarial refinement for details) with a pose mask loss and a Stage II difference-map training trick.  
- Situates work within GAN-based conditional image generation literature but omits an important related paper (Wang et al., CVPR 2017) with the same title/problem.  
- Strength: Thorough ablation studies justify design choices (pose embeddings, loss functions, two-stage vs. one-stage).  
- Strength: Pose mask loss reduces background interference; supported by qualitative (Fig.4) and quantitative (Table 1) results.  
- Strength: Evaluated on both high-resolution DeepFashion and low-resolution Market-1501, showing robustness across datasets.  
- Strength: User study (Table 2) provides additional evidence of realism.  
- Weakness: Claims that end-to-end would fail lack empirical evidence or explanation for why two-stage is necessary rather than convenient.  
- Weakness: Comparison with [36] is superficial and does not convincingly argue why pose keypoints are more expressive than a target-view word vector.  
- Weakness: No discussion or comparison of computational efficiency versus alternatives, important for practical use.  
- Weakness: Failure cases (e.g., leg artifacts, gender misclassification) are shown but not analyzed or addressed.  
- Weakness: Evaluation metrics (SSIM, IS) do not fully capture perceptual image quality and can be misleading.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient network and training details for reproducibility.  
- Originality: Pose-conditioned person generation is a meaningful application; two-stage design is common but the pose mask loss is a useful incremental contribution.  
- Significance: Practically valuable but does not convincingly advance state-of-the-art without comparison to closely related work (Wang et al. CVPR 2017).  
- Recommendation/actions for camera-ready: add comparison to Wang et al. (CVPR 2017), provide empirical justification for two-stage design, expand limitations/failure-case analysis, include computational efficiency comparisons, and clarify effects of pose-estimation errors.
