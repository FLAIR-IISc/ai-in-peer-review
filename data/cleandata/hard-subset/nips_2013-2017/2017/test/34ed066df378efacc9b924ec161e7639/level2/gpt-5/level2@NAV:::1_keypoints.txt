- Paper introduces PG2, a two-stage pose-guided person image generator conditioned on a reference image and explicit 2D keypoint heatmaps.
- Stage I is a U-Net variant integrating pose heatmaps and a pose-mask L1 loss to produce a coarse foreground-focused result.
- Stage II is a conditional GAN that predicts a residual difference map to refine details, with a pairwise discriminator D(I_A, Î_B) enforcing consistency with the conditioning image.
- Experiments on DeepFashion and Market-1501 include ablations (pose embeddings, mask loss, one- vs two-stage) and small-scale AMT user studies.
- Builds on conditional generative modeling, pose estimation, and multi-view synthesis literature while using explicit keypoints instead of coarse view labels or segments.
- Claims are supported by convincing qualitative results and thorough ablations; residual refinement and pairwise conditioning are effective.
- Quantitative metrics (SSIM/IS and masked variants) are informative but imperfect for perceptual fidelity; AMT study is small and lacks statistical analysis.
- Paper is generally well-written and organized with clear figures, though some references and minor editorial issues exist.
- Novelty lies in combining explicit keypoint conditioning with a learned difference map refinement and a simple pose-mask loss.
- Method is practically useful for content creation and data augmentation and shows state-of-the-art results relative to closest prior (VariGAN).
- Limitations include background mismatch, lack of quantitative identity preservation measures, and incomplete failure-mode analysis.
- Suggested improvements: use learned person segmentation/background inpainting, evaluate identity with re-id or face/body embeddings, add perceptual/feature-matching losses and FID/LPIPS (masked), include statistical tests for AMT, add stronger baselines (e.g., pix2pix with keypoint channels), and discuss robustness to pose estimator errors and occlusions.
- Arguments for acceptance: clear, well-motivated two-stage design, thorough ablations, practical impact, and reproducible components likely to influence follow-up work.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited quantitative perceptual/identity evaluation, background artifacts, missing strong baselines, and lack of statistical rigor in the user study.
