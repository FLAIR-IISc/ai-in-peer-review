

The paper proposes a novel two-stage framework called Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) for synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses based on a reference image and target pose. The work addresses a challenging task that requires both pose transfer and appearance preservation, which previous end-to-end approaches struggled with due to the simultaneous need to generate correct poses and detailed appearance. The authors propose a divide-and-conquer strategy: Stage-I integrates pose information with a reference image using a U-Net-like architecture with a novel pose mask loss to focus on human body rather than background, producing a coarse image capturing global structure. Stage-II refines this result using a conditional GAN that generates a difference map between the coarse result and target image, enabling faster convergence and sharper details. The method is evaluated on Market-1501 (re-identification) and DeepFashion (fashion) datasets, with comprehensive ablation studies on pose embedding methods, loss functions, and the two-stage vs. one-stage comparison.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a well-justified approach. The pose mask loss effectively addresses the background interference problem, as evidenced by both qualitative and quantitative results. The two-stage design is well-motivated, with ablation studies showing clear advantages over one-stage alternatives and alternative pose embeddings. The use of difference maps in Stage-II is a clever solution to accelerate training and improve detail generation. The evaluation metrics (SSIM, Inception Score, user studies) are appropriate and well-explained, with the authors thoughtfully acknowledging the limitations of these metrics for photo-realism assessment. The work is clearly differentiated from related approaches, particularly Zhao et al. [36], with compelling qualitative comparisons demonstrating superior results.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The method section provides precise architectural specifications, loss functions, and implementation details. The figures are well-chosen to illustrate key concepts and results, including failure cases that help contextualize the method's limitations. The discussion of background interference in Market-1501 and the resulting trade-offs in user studies adds valuable nuance to the evaluation.

The contribution has significant originality in both the problem formulation and technical approach. While conditional image generation with pose information has been explored, the explicit conditioning on pose keypoints rather than viewpoint labels or other abstractions, combined with the two-stage refinement framework, represents a meaningful advance. The pose mask loss is a simple but effective innovation that could benefit other generation tasks. The paper clearly articulates how this work differs from previous contributions, particularly in addressing the more expressive pose condition.

The significance of the work is strong for both practical applications (movie making, pose estimation data generation) and theoretical contributions to conditional image generation. The ability to generate high-quality person images with convincing details at various poses has clear utility in numerous domains. The two-stage approach could inspire similar strategies for other challenging generation tasks requiring both structural and detail fidelity.

A few areas could be strengthened. The paper could better explain why the pose mask is computed from the target pose rather than the reference image, which would clarify the rationale for its use. While the user study provides useful human evaluation, including more specific metrics about what aspects of the images users found convincing (e.g., pose correctness vs. appearance details) would strengthen this section. The paper mentions "rare but important poses" as an application, but could elaborate on how the model might handle poses outside the training distribution, which relates to the failure cases shown in Figure 6. The connection to related work on pose estimation (e.g., the pose estimator used for keypoint generation) could be more explicitly discussed in terms of how the pose estimation errors might propagate to the generation results.

The paper is technically sound, well-executed, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of conditional image generation. The two-stage approach is both novel and effective, with strong experimental validation across multiple datasets. The method advances the state of the art in pose-guided person image generation, providing a framework that is both practical and theoretically grounded. The paper is clear, well-written, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction. The limitations are acknowledged and appropriately contextualized.

The work is well-suited for NIPS, addressing a significant challenge in image generation with a technically sound and innovative solution. The results demonstrate clear advantages over previous approaches, and the method has potential for broader application in conditional image synthesis tasks. The paper meets all four evaluation criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) at a high level, with only minor areas for improvement in the discussion of limitations and related work. The contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance to NIPS.
