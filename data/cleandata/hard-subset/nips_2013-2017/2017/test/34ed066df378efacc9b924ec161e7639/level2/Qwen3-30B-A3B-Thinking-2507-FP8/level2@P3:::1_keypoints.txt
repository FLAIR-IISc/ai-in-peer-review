- Proposes PG2, a novel two-stage framework for synthesizing person images in arbitrary target poses from a reference image and target pose.
- Addresses the dual challenge of pose transfer and appearance preservation that single-stage methods struggle with.
- Stage-I: U-Net-like architecture integrates pose with reference image and uses a novel pose mask loss to focus on the human body, producing a coarse global-structure image.
- Stage-II: conditional GAN refines the coarse output by predicting a difference map, improving detail and accelerating convergence.
- Evaluated on Market-1501 and DeepFashion with comprehensive ablation studies (pose embeddings, loss functions, two-stage vs one-stage).
- Strong technical quality; pose mask loss effectively reduces background interference with quantitative and qualitative support.
- Ablations show clear advantages of the two-stage design and the chosen pose embedding over alternatives.
- Difference-map formulation is a clever, empirically effective solution to speed training and sharpen details.
- Uses appropriate evaluation metrics (SSIM, Inception Score, user studies) and acknowledges their limitations for photo-realism.
- Clearly differentiated from related work (e.g., Zhao et al.), with compelling qualitative improvements.
- Well-written, organized, and reproducible: detailed architectures, losses, implementation, illustrative figures, and failure cases.
- Original contribution: explicit conditioning on pose keypoints and a two-stage refinement pipeline; pose mask is a simple, transferable innovation.
- Significant practical and theoretical impact for applications like filmmaking and data augmentation; method likely to inspire similar strategies.
- Areas to strengthen: explain why pose mask is computed from target pose not reference, add finer-grained user-study metrics (pose vs appearance), discuss handling of rare/out-of-distribution poses, and address pose-estimation error propagation.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, novel, significant, and well-validated — recommended for acceptance (NIPS).
