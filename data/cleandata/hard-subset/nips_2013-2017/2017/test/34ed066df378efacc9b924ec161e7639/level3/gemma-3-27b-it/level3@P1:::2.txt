This paper proposes a novel Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) for synthesizing person images given a reference image and a target pose. The authors address a challenging problem by adopting a divide-and-conquer strategy with a two-stage generative model. Stage-I utilizes a U-Net to generate a coarse image conditioned on the reference image and target pose, while Stage-II refines this initial result using a conditional DCGAN to predict a residual/difference map, leading to sharper details. The inclusion of a tailored mask loss to focus on the human body region and avoid background artifacts is a notable contribution.

The work builds on the rapidly growing field of generative image modeling, referencing key papers in VAEs [14], GANs [6], and autoregressive models [30].  It specifically situates itself within the category of conditional image generation, citing work on attribute-conditioned generation [32], category-conditioned generation [18], and image-conditioned generation [34, 33, 11, 8].  The paper accurately identifies its novelty as explicitly conditioning generation on pose information, distinguishing it from approaches that rely on abstract visual cues [22, 23] or viewpoints [36].  The connection to prior work on stacked GANs like StackGAN [46] is acknowledged, positioning the current approach as a specific application and refinement of these techniques within the domain of pose-guided person image generation.

**Strengths:**

The paper is well-organized and clearly presents a reasonable approach to a difficult problem.  The two-stage generation strategy, separating global structure from detailed appearance, is a sensible design choice, drawing parallels to successful methodologies in other image generation tasks [35, 9, 3, 19].  The use of a difference map for refinement (Stage-II) is an intelligent idea, potentially easing the training process compared to directly generating a high-resolution image. The proposed pose mask loss demonstrably encourages the model to focus on transferring the appearance of the human body, effectively mitigating background changes.  The quantitative and qualitative results on both the Market-1501 and DeepFashion datasets provide evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The ablation studies exploring different pose embeddings and loss functions are a valuable addition, strengthening the analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the sound approach, the generated images exhibit noticeable artifacts and struggle to recover high-frequency details.  The example provided in the paper (ID 346) demonstrates this issue, with visible black dots and a lack of sharp textures. While the images are generally pose-consistent, the overall quality falls short of state-of-the-art image generation results. This limits the practical applicability of the method.  The authors acknowledge this limitation in their conclusion. While the task is novel, the core technical contributions (stacked generators, conditional GANs) are evolutions of existing ideas. The overall significance of this work, while demonstrable, might be considered incremental rather than groundbreaking. The user study reveals a gap between quantitative scores and human perception, suggesting the current metrics don’t fully capture the quality issues in generated images.

**Originality:**

The combination of conditioning on both appearance *and* pose for person image generation is a novel contribution. While the building blocks – U-Nets, GANs, difference maps – are not new, their application in this specific context, with the tailored mask loss, demonstrates a degree of originality.  However, the reviewers note that the general strategy of stacking generators has been explored in previous work (e.g. StackGAN), so it is not entirely novel. 

**Significance:**

The results are important in that they demonstrate a feasible approach to pose-guided person image generation. Potential applications in areas like virtual try-on, movie making, and data augmentation for pose estimation are plausible.  However, the current image quality may hinder widespread adoption.  Further improvements in image realism are needed to elevate the significance of this work and facilitate real-world applications.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a novel and interesting problem.
*   The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated.
*   The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach.
*   The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the design choices.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The generated images suffer from visible artifacts and lack high-frequency details.
*   The significance of the work could be viewed as incremental.
*   The improvement in image quality needed to be practically useful seems considerable.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and reasonably novel contribution to the field of generative image modeling. While the image quality is not yet on par with state-of-the-art methods, the approach is promising and the experimental results are encouraging. The two-stage architecture with difference map refinement is a clever design choice.  Despite the identified weaknesses, I believe the paper warrants acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the artifact issue and clearly articulating the potential for future improvements to address the limitations. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a discussion of potential strategies for improving image fidelity (e.g., higher-resolution training, more sophisticated loss functions, alternative network architectures).
