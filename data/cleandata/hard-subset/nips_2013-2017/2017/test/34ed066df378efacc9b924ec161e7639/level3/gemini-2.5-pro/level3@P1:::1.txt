This paper introduces a novel task: generating an image of a specific person in a new, target pose, conditioned on a single source image of that person. To tackle this challenging problem, the authors propose the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), a two-stage, coarse-to-fine framework. The first stage uses a U-Net-like generator (G1) to produce a coarse rendering of the person in the target pose, taking the source image and a heatmap representation of the target pose as input. This stage is trained with a proposed masked L1 loss to focus learning on the foreground person. The second stage refines the coarse output using another U-Net-like generator (G2) and an adversarial loss to add finer details and improve realism. The approach builds upon recent advances in conditional generative adversarial networks (GANs) but applies them to the new and difficult problem of explicit pose manipulation. The authors demonstrate their method on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel and well-defined problem that is of significant interest to the community. The ability to realistically re-render a person in an arbitrary pose has clear applications in data augmentation (e.g., for pose estimation or person re-identification), content creation for media and gaming, and e-commerce (e.g., virtual try-on). The proposed method of conditioning on explicit pose keypoints is more direct and flexible than prior work that uses abstract view labels. This work opens up a promising new research direction.

2.  **Clarity and Quality of the Method:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The proposed two-stage approach is logical and well-motivated; separating the complex task into generating the global structure first and then refining the details is a sensible strategy. The architectural choices, such as using pose heatmaps as input and a masked L1 loss, are well-justified.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** A major strength of this work is the extensive set of ablation studies presented in Section 4. The authors carefully evaluate different design choices, including three types of pose embeddings, the effect of their proposed pose mask loss, and the benefit of the two-stage model over a one-stage adversarial approach. This rigorous analysis provides valuable insights and strengthens the contributions of the paper.

4.  **Promising Qualitative Results:** The qualitative results, particularly on the DeepFashion dataset, are compelling. The model successfully transfers the appearance of the person (e.g., clothing color and style) from the source image to the target pose while generating a plausible image. The refinement from Stage 1 to Stage 2 clearly demonstrates the effectiveness of the two-stage approach, with the final results being significantly sharper and more detailed.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

1.  **Discernible Artifacts in Results:** While promising, the generated images are not yet photo-realistic and contain noticeable artifacts. This is especially true on the more challenging Market-1501 dataset, where backgrounds are complex and varied. Common failure modes include blurry textures, distorted faces, inconsistent backgrounds, and strange artifacts on clothing (e.g., the blue blob in Figure 4, ID 53). The failure cases in Figure 6 also highlight current limitations.

2.  **Weak Quantitative Evaluation:** The quantitative evaluation is not fully convincing. The authors rightly note that metrics like SSIM and Inception Score (IS) often fail to capture perceptual quality in generative tasks, where blurry images can score highly. While the proposed mask-SSIM and mask-IS are a reasonable attempt to focus the evaluation on the foreground, they are non-standard and do not fully resolve the fundamental issue of metrics not aligning with human judgment.

3.  **Preliminary User Study:** The user study on Amazon Mechanical Turk is too limited to draw strong conclusions. A simple real-vs-fake classification task where each image is shown for only one second is a very low bar for realism. This setup does not assess key aspects of the task, such as the accuracy of the generated pose, the preservation of the person's identity, or a direct comparison of realism against baselines or ablations. A more substantive human evaluation would be necessary to properly validate the claims of generating high-quality images.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Related Work:** The idea of a coarse-to-fine or iterative refinement architecture is common in other areas, such as human pose estimation. It would strengthen the paper to connect your two-stage approach to this broader context by citing relevant works like Newell et al. (2016) on stacked hourglass networks or Carreira et al. (2016) on iterative error feedback.

*   **Role of Residual Blocks:** In Section 3.1, you state that "using residual blocks as basic component improves the generation performance." Could you please elaborate on this? Was this improvement observed qualitatively, or was it measured with a specific metric? Providing this detail would substantiate the claim.

*   **Cause of Blurring in One-Stage Model:** The comparison shows that the one-stage adversarial model (G1+D) is blurrier than the final two-stage model. Your G1 generator contains a fully-connected layer, whereas G2 does not. It is possible that this FC layer acts as an information bottleneck that hinders the generation of high-frequency details, causing the blurriness. Have you considered an ablation where a one-stage model uses a fully convolutional architecture like G2? This might help disentangle the benefits of the two-stage training strategy from the architectural differences between G1 and G2.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and interesting problem, a well-motivated and clearly described method, and a thorough evaluation of its design choices. While the results are not yet perfect and the quantitative evaluation could be stronger, the originality of the task and the quality of the proposed approach make this a valuable contribution to the field. It lays a solid foundation for future work on this exciting problem. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
