

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a two-stage GAN-based framework (PG2) for synthesizing person images in novel poses guided by target skeleton/keypoints. The approach divides the pose transfer task into pose integration (stage-I) and image refinement (stage-II). Stage-I uses a U-Net-like network to integrate pose information (encoded as 18 heatmaps) with a reference image to generate a coarse pose-aligned image, while stage-II refines this result using a conditional DCGAN that generates a difference map between the coarse result and target image. The authors compare their approach to related work including [36] (which generates multi-view cloth images from a single view), and argue their explicit pose guidance provides more expressive conditioning than previous attribute or text-based conditioning methods.

The paper builds upon established GAN techniques (DCGAN, U-Net) and poses a novel application of these techniques to person image generation with explicit pose control. While the method shows promise for pose transfer, it does not introduce fundamentally new theoretical or algorithmic contributions to GANs themselves.

## Strengths

The paper's main strengths include:

1. **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and thorough ablation studies. The two-stage approach is logically motivated and well-justified.

2. **Interesting Application**: The task of pose-guided person image generation is well-motivated with practical applications in movie making, pose estimation data generation, and human-computer interaction.

3. **Qualitative Results**: Some generated images do show reasonable pose transfer and appearance details, particularly on the DeepFashion dataset (Figure 4), suggesting the approach has potential.

## Weaknesses

Despite these strengths, the paper has significant weaknesses that prevent it from meeting NIPS standards:

1. **Lack of Quantitative Evaluation**: The most critical weakness is the absence of rigorous quantitative evaluation. The paper reports SSIM and Inception Score (IS) metrics, but as the authors themselves note, these metrics do not correlate well with human perception of image quality (Table 1, Section 4.3). Crucially, they lack user studies or human evaluation metrics that would measure how often generated images fool humans (e.g., the percentage of generated images rated as "real" by humans). The limited user study (Section 4.4) is insufficient for a top-tier venue, as it only reports basic "real vs. generated" classification rates without addressing the quality of the generated images.

2. **Poor Visual Quality**: The generated images (Figures 4-6) show numerous artifacts, particularly on the Market-1501 dataset (low-resolution surveillance images), including incorrect body parts, color mismatches, and background artifacts. The images often look "blurred" or "unrealistic" compared to the target images. The supplementary results suffer from the same issues.

3. **Overstated Claims**: The abstract and introduction claim "high-quality person images with convincing details" and "photo-realistic" results, but the evidence provided does not support these strong claims. The figures are too small to properly assess the quality, and the generated images frequently contain noticeable errors.

4. **Inadequate Comparison**: The comparison to [36] (Figure 5) is superficial, as [36] addresses a different problem (multi-view synthesis from a single view with word vector conditioning) rather than explicit pose guidance.

5. **Lack of Analysis of Failure Modes**: The failure cases (Figure 6) are presented without sufficient analysis of why these failures occur or how they might be mitigated.

## Evaluation Against Review Criteria

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound but lacks rigorous evaluation. The absence of human evaluation metrics and the poor visual quality of results undermine the claim of "high-quality" generation. The quantitative results (SSIM, IS) are misleading as noted by the authors themselves.

**Clarity**: The paper is very well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and good ablation studies. However, the visual quality of the results is poor, making the paper less convincing.

**Originality**: The approach is application-focused rather than methodologically novel. It combines existing techniques (U-Net, DCGAN) in a new application context, but the novelty is limited to this specific use case.

**Significance**: The application is interesting, but the paper fails to demonstrate significant progress over existing methods or convincingly show the potential impact on the field. The lack of rigorous evaluation means the significance is overstated.

## Recommendation

This paper presents an interesting application of existing GAN techniques to pose-guided person image generation. While the idea is promising, the paper falls short of NIPS standards due to the lack of rigorous quantitative evaluation and the poor visual quality of the generated images. The authors' rebuttal did not satisfactorily address these concerns, as they did not provide additional human evaluation data or better visual examples.

The paper would benefit from significant additional work, including:
1. A comprehensive user study measuring how often generated images fool humans
2. Better visual examples with larger, higher-quality images
3. More thorough analysis of failure modes
4. More conservative claims about the quality of generated images

Given these shortcomings, I recommend rejection. While the application is promising, the work is premature for presentation at a top venue like NIPS. The authors should address the quantitative evaluation issues before resubmitting to a venue that would value this line of work.
