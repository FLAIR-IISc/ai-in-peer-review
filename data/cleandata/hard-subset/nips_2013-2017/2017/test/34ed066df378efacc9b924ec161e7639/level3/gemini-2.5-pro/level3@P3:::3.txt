This paper introduces a novel task: pose-guided person image generation. The goal is to synthesize a new image of a specific person in a target pose, given an input image of that person and a representation of the target pose (i.e., keypoints). The proposed method, the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), tackles this problem with a two-stage approach. Stage I uses a U-Net-like architecture to generate a coarse image that matches the target pose while retaining the general appearance from the source image. Stage II employs a conditional GAN, also with a U-Net generator, to refine the coarse output, aiming to add finer details and produce a more realistic result. The approach is an application of now-standard components in the generative modeling toolkit (U-Nets, DCGANs) to a new and challenging conditional generation problem. The authors test their method on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Interesting and Significant Problem:** The problem of synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses is compelling and has significant potential applications in areas like data augmentation for pose estimation and person re-identification, virtual clothes try-on, and special effects in media production. The paper's framing of the problem is clear and well-motivated.
2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The proposed two-stage architecture is logical, and the motivations for each stage (coarse structure followed by refinement) are explained clearly. The architecture details and training objectives are described with sufficient detail.
3.  **Promising Initial Results:** The method demonstrates a clear ability to transfer the appearance of a person from a source image to a target skeleton. The coarse results from Stage I (e.g., Figure 4, column 7) successfully capture the global structure, pose, and color palette of the target, which is a non-trivial accomplishment. This serves as a solid proof-of-concept for the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Convincing Quantitative Evaluation:** The paper's primary weakness is its evaluation. The authors rely on SSIM and Inception Score (IS), but they correctly note that these metrics often fail to correlate with human perception of image quality. Indeed, they state that the blurrier Stage I results can achieve higher SSIM scores than the refined Stage II results, which undermines the utility of these metrics for comparing the models. The user study presented in Section 4.4 is a step in the right direction, but the results are not strong enough to support the paper's claims. On the DeepFashion dataset, generated images were mistaken for real ones only 14.9% of the time, and this drops to a mere 7.8% on the more challenging Market-1501 dataset. These low "fooling rates" strongly suggest that the generated images are not yet at a level that could be considered "photo-realistic" or competitive with real images.
2.  **Poor Visual Quality and Overstated Claims:** The qualitative results do not support the paper's claims of generating "high-quality" and "photo-realistic" images. The generated images in Figure 4 and the supplement contain numerous and easily noticeable artifacts. Common failure modes include:
    *   Heavily distorted or blurred faces.
    *   Inconsistent or nonsensical textures on clothing (e.g., the stripes on the T-shirt for ID 53 are mostly lost in the one-stage model and poorly rendered in the final result).
    *   Anatomically strange limbs and body parts.
    *   Blurry boundaries between the person and the background.
    The figures in the manuscript are also quite small, which makes a detailed assessment difficult but fails to hide the most prominent flaws. The gap between the generated images and the ground truth targets remains very large. While the problem is difficult, the current results feel preliminary.
3.  **Limited Originality in Method:** While the application is novel, the technical approach is a fairly direct combination of existing, well-established techniques. The architecture is composed of a U-Net and a conditional DCGAN, which are standard choices for image-to-image translation tasks. The primary technical novelties are the "pose mask loss" and the two-stage refinement process, which are reasonable engineering choices but represent incremental contributions.

**Summary of Review and Recommendation:**

This paper tackles an interesting and important problem with a clearly explained and logical approach. The idea of a two-stage process for pose-guided person synthesis is promising. However, the work in its current form feels premature for a top-tier conference like NIPS. The main issue is the significant disconnect between the paper's claims of "high-quality" and "photo-realistic" generation and the actual results presented. The visual results contain major artifacts, and the quantitative evaluation (especially the user study) confirms that the generated images are far from fooling a human observer. The work serves as a good proof-of-concept but does not yet demonstrate a level of quality that advances the state of the art in generative imaging. Therefore, I recommend rejecting this paper. The authors should focus on significantly improving the generation quality and conducting a more robust evaluation to substantiate their claims before resubmitting to a future venue.
