This paper proposes a novel task of generating person images by conditioning on a reference image and a target pose, and introduces a two-stage network architecture to tackle this task. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through extensive experiments on two datasets, and provide a thorough evaluation of their design decisions.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its components. The authors also provide a comprehensive review of related work, although some recent papers on iterative refinement could be added to further strengthen the discussion.

The proposed two-stage network architecture is a key contribution of this paper. The first stage, which focuses on capturing the global structure of the person, uses a U-Net-like architecture with residual blocks to improve generation performance. The second stage, which refines the initial result through adversarial training, uses a variant of the Deep Convolutional GAN (DCGAN) model. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this two-stage approach through qualitative and quantitative evaluations.

One of the strengths of this paper is its thorough evaluation of design decisions. The authors provide extensive experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of different pose embeddings, losses, and network architectures. These experiments provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches and help to justify the design choices made in the paper.

However, the quantitative evaluation of the paper is somewhat weak, partly due to the lack of suitable metrics. The authors use Structural Similarity (SSIM) and the Inception Score (IS) to evaluate the quality of synthesized images, but these metrics may not be sufficient to capture the nuances of person image generation. The authors also report the results of a user study, but this study is preliminary and unconvincing, and should be replaced by a more substantive human evaluation.

In terms of novelty, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of person image generation. The proposed two-stage network architecture is a new approach to this task, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness through extensive experiments. The paper also provides a thorough evaluation of design decisions, which helps to advance the state of the art in this field.

Overall, I recommend acceptance of this paper based on its novelty, approach, and thorough evaluation of design decisions. However, I suggest that the authors revise the paper to address some of the weaknesses mentioned above, including the addition of recent papers on iterative refinement to the related work section, and the replacement of the user study with a more substantive human evaluation.

* The paper proposes a novel task and architecture for person image generation.
