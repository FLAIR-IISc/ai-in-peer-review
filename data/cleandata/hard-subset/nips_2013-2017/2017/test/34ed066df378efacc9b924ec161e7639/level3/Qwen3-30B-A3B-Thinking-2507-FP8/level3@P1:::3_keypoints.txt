- Proposes a two-stage GAN/U-Net framework for pose-guided person image synthesis (pose integration then image refinement).
- Introduces a pose mask loss to focus generation on the human body and suppress background influence.
- Builds on existing GAN/U-Net components; application to pose-guided person synthesis is the main novelty.
- Evaluated qualitatively on Market-1501 and DeepFashion with some reasonable pose transfer and appearance consistency.
- Writing and organization are clear, with well-explained methodology and mask-loss motivation.
- No rigorous quantitative evaluation of realism; reported SSIM and Inception Score do not directly measure person-image realism.
- Lacks adequate human user studies to assess whether images can fool observers; presented user study is small and weak (9.3–14.9% misclassification).
- Visual results contain notable artifacts: incorrect body parts, color mismatches, blurriness, background inconsistencies, and “blue blobs” for clothing.
- Figures are too small to assess details; supplementary material does not fix visual quality issues.
- Claims of generating “high-quality” and “photo-realistic” person images are unsupported and exaggerated.
- Authors acknowledge worse performance of two-stage model on Market-1501, undermining key claims.
- Methodological novelty is limited; pose heatmaps and two-stage design reuse existing techniques with minor loss modification.
- Pose mask loss viewed as a minor incremental change rather than a significant theoretical advance.
- Significance is limited because current results are not convincing for practical applications (fashion, film) and struggle with complex poses and gender errors.
- Implementation appears technically sound but evaluation gaps and poor visual quality weaken the contribution.
- Pro-acceptance points: interesting idea, clear writing, and reasonable two-stage strategy.
- Con-acceptance points: lack of convincing quantitative/human evaluation, poor visual quality, exaggerated claims, and insufficient comparisons to related work.
- Authors did not adequately address reviewers’ concerns in the rebuttal.
- Recommendation: reject; work is promising but premature—needs improved results, rigorous human evaluation, and stronger comparisons to meet conference standards.
