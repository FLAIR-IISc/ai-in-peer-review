## Review of “Pose Guided Person Image Generation”

This paper proposes a novel approach to person image generation, conditioning the process on both a reference image and a target pose. The core idea leverages a two-stage generative network: a coarse U-Net generates an initial image based on the input conditions, followed by a second generator which predicts a residual map to refine the output and introduce higher-frequency details using an adversarial training setup. The work is evaluated on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets. 

The paper is well-organized and clearly presents the proposed method. The motivation for a coarse-to-fine approach is sensible, given the complexity of the pose transfer and appearance consistency requirements. The use of a difference map for refinement is a clever way to focus the second generator’s learning, and the proposed pose mask loss is a reasonable attempt to mitigate background interference. This builds upon existing work in conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014; Reed et al., 2016) and stacked generative architectures (Huang et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2017), presenting a novel combination for this specific task. 

However, while the approach is technically sound and the idea is promising, the generated images suffer from noticeable artifacts and lack high-frequency details, as exemplified by the authors’ own example (ID346). This severely limits the practical applicability of the method. While the quantitative results (SSIM, IS) show improvement with the proposed techniques, these metrics don’t fully capture the perceptual deficiencies. The generated images, while consistent in pose, often appear blurry or contain unrealistic distortions.

A significant concern is the originality and broader appeal of the approach. While introducing conditioning on both appearance and pose is a contribution, it can be viewed as an incremental extension to existing conditional GAN frameworks. The novelty feels somewhat limited given the existing landscape of stacked and conditioned generators. Furthermore, the performance improvements, while statistically significant, might not be substantial enough to warrant significant attention from the broader NIPS community.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Technically novel, well-organized, and presents a reasonable solution to a challenging problem. Demonstrates understanding of related work.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Image quality is insufficient for practical applications, limited novelty beyond combining existing techniques, potential concerns about broader impact.

**Overall Assessment:** Despite the quality issues, I lean towards acceptance. The paper proposes a well-defined and technically sound approach, and the initial results are encouraging. However, the authors should address the concerns about image quality and further highlight the significance of their contribution beyond a simple combination of existing methods. Future work should focus on improving the generated image fidelity.
