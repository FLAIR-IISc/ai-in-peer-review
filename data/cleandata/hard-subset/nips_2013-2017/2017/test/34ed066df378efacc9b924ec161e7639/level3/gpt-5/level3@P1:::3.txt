This paper proposes Pose-Guided Person Generation (PG2), a two-stage conditional generative framework that synthesizes an image of a person in a novel pose given a single reference image and a target pose. The method uses an explicit pose representation (18 keypoint heatmaps) concatenated with the reference image. Stage-I is a U-Net-like encoder–decoder with skip connections trained with a masked L1 loss to integrate pose and appearance and produce a coarse result focusing on global structure. Stage-II refines details via adversarial training: a second U-Net predicts a residual/difference map conditioned on the Stage-I output and the reference, while the discriminator takes image–condition pairs to enforce consistency with the provided identity. Experiments are shown on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64), with ablations for pose embeddings, loss masking, and one-stage vs. two-stage training. The method relates to conditional GANs and image-to-image translation (e.g., pix2pix), stacked/coarse-to-fine GANs, and view/pose-conditioned generation; the main novelty lies in explicit pose conditioning via heatmaps and the residual refinement strategy.

Strengths
- Clear problem setup with explicit pose control; the task is practically relevant (editing, data augmentation for pose estimation or re-id).
- Sensible, modular architecture: coarse global structure first, then adversarial refinement; the “difference map” generator is a practical engineering choice that likely stabilizes training.
- Explicit exploration of pose encodings; the heatmap concatenation is simple and effective.
- Masked loss to de-emphasize background changes is reasonable for cross-camera settings (Market-1501).

Weaknesses
- Limited and unconvincing quantitative evaluation relative to the claims. Reported similarity metrics (SSIM/IS) are known to correlate poorly with perceptual quality in conditional settings; there is no rigorous perceptual study quantifying the fooling rate, nor task-based evaluation (e.g., whether generated images preserve identity for re-id).
- Visual quality is uneven: many outputs exhibit noticeable artifacts, color bleeding, distorted limbs/hands, and background leakage; figures are small and do not convincingly demonstrate “high-quality” results. The supplementary material reflects similar issues.
- The contributions primarily combine known components (U-Nets, conditional GANs, coarse-to-fine stacking, heatmap conditioning), with relatively incremental novelty beyond the application framing and residual refinement.
- Scope of comparisons is narrow. There is no comparison to strong image-to-image baselines tailored to this task (e.g., pix2pix with pose heatmaps/segmentation cues) or to identity-preserving methods; no evaluation of identity consistency (e.g., via a re-id network).
- Methodological gaps: no analysis of pose estimator failures, occlusion handling, or identity preservation; no diversity control or uncertainty estimates when the target pose reveals unseen regions.

Quality
- Technically sound in architecture and training; the masked L1 and pairwise discriminator are reasonable design choices.
- However, evidence is insufficient to support strong claims of “high-quality” generation. The quantitative metrics used are not compelling for this problem, and there is no task-oriented or perceptual validation that would substantiate the qualitative claims.

Clarity
- Writing is generally clear and well-organized; the two-stage pipeline is described in sufficient detail for reproduction.
- Figures are too small to assess fine details; failure cases and limitations could be discussed more prominently.

Originality
- Moderate. Explicit pose heatmaps for conditioning, U-Net backbones with skip connections, and coarse-to-fine adversarial refinement are well-known. Using a residual/difference map in Stage-II is a pragmatic, minor twist rather than a conceptual advance.

Significance
- Potentially useful for applications requiring pose control, but current empirical quality limits practical impact. Without stronger perceptual or task-based validation (e.g., identity preservation, data augmentation benefits), the contribution is primarily an engineering combination rather than a demonstrable advance.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear task definition and explicit conditioning signal.
- Conceptually simple, modular approach; some ablation analyses on poses and losses.
- Potentially useful baseline for pose-conditioned person synthesis.

Cons for acceptance
- Visual results are not yet convincing; many noticeable artifacts.
- Weak quantitative evidence; no rigorous human perceptual or task-based evaluation to back claims.
- Limited comparisons and missing evaluation of identity preservation.
- Incremental novelty over existing conditional GANs and stacked generation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a rigorous human perceptual evaluation (e.g., fooling rate with adequate sample size, controlled presentation) and report confidence intervals.
- Include task-based metrics: identity preservation via a fixed re-id model (rank-1/mAP), and possibly the utility of generated images for data augmentation.
- Report more appropriate generative metrics (e.g., FID) and semantic similarity measures focused on the person region; consider pose/structure consistency metrics (keypoint error).
- Strengthen baselines: pix2pix with pose heatmaps/segmentation, single-stage adversarial U-Net with perceptual losses; compare fairly at matched capacity.
- Improve figures (larger crops, zoomed-in regions), expand failure analysis (occlusions, background mismatch), and consider identity-preserving losses or perceptual features.

Post-rebuttal update
After reading the rebuttal, I remain unconvinced. The response did not address the core concerns about the lack of compelling quantitative and perceptual evidence, nor did it provide stronger baselines or identity preservation metrics. Other reviewers share similar reservations regarding both the qualitative fidelity and the evaluation depth.

Recommendation: Reject. The work is promising and the problem is important, but the current submission does not provide sufficiently convincing empirical evidence or novelty for a top venue.
