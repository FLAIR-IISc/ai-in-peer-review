

# Review: Pose Guided Person Image Generation

This paper introduces a two-stage generative network for synthesizing human images conditioned on appearance (reference image) and pose (target pose). The authors propose a coarse-to-fine approach where Stage I (G1) generates a coarse image using a U-Net architecture with pose information encoded as heatmaps, and Stage II (G2) refines this result by generating a residual map that corrects appearance details. The key technical contributions include a novel pose mask loss to focus on human body rather than background, and a difference-map approach that accelerates convergence by training the second generator to predict the difference between the coarse result and target image rather than generating from scratch.

The work builds upon conditional GANs and recent stacking approaches like StackGAN [35], but differs by explicitly conditioning on pose keypoints rather than textual descriptions or coarse pose representations. The paper relates well to prior work on human pose estimation [2] and image-to-image translation [32, 11], though it could better clarify how it differs from Zhao et al.'s multi-view person image synthesis [36] which it cites as the most related work.

**Quality**: The paper demonstrates strong technical execution with clear ablation studies comparing pose embedding methods, loss functions, and two-stage vs. one-stage approaches. The quantitative results (SSIM, Inception Score) and user studies provide reasonable evaluation metrics. However, the quality of generated images is limited by visible artifacts (e.g., black dots in ID346 as noted in the key points) and poor recovery of high-frequency details, which the authors acknowledge but don't adequately address. The training procedure is well-described, but the paper could better discuss failure modes and their causes.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear figures (Figures 2-5) that effectively illustrate the architecture and results. The method section provides sufficient detail for reproduction, though the supplementary materials would be needed for full implementation details. The writing is concise and accessible to the NIPS audience.

**Originality**: The core contribution is a well-motivated task (human image generation conditioned on appearance and pose) with a practical coarse-to-fine solution. The difference-map refinement strategy is novel and useful, though the stacked architecture itself draws from prior work like StackGAN [35] and Stacked GANs [9]. The pose mask loss is a valuable technical contribution that addresses a specific challenge in this domain.

**Significance**: While the task is well-motivated for applications like movie making and pose estimation, the paper's significance is somewhat limited by the current quality of results. The approach appears to be another conditional GAN variant rather than a fundamentally new direction. The authors acknowledge that image quality still needs significant improvement for practical use, which tempers the potential impact.

**Strengths**: The paper's strongest aspects are its clear problem formulation, thoughtful coarse-to-fine strategy that aligns well with the challenge of simultaneously handling pose and appearance, and the useful pose mask loss that effectively focuses the model on human body transfer rather than background. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the advantage of the two-stage approach over one-stage alternatives.

**Weaknesses**: The most significant weakness is the persistent image quality issues, particularly visible artifacts and poor high-frequency detail recovery. The paper's claim of "high-quality" results is questionable given the examples in Figure 4 and the specific artifact mentioned in the key points. The comparison to prior work on stacked generators is insufficiently detailed, and the paper doesn't adequately address why their specific implementation is better than existing approaches.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance**:
*Pro*: Well-organized, technically sound approach with clear contribution to a meaningful problem; strong experimental validation of the coarse-to-fine strategy; pose mask loss is a useful technical contribution; good comparison to relevant baselines.

*Con*: Limited practical utility due to current image quality; approach is derivative of prior stacked GAN work; failure modes (artifacts, high-frequency detail loss) aren't sufficiently addressed; significance is constrained by the limited quality improvements over existing methods.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of conditional image generation, particularly for human pose transfer. While the quality of results is not yet suitable for practical applications, the approach is well-motivated and the technical innovations (pose mask loss, difference-map refinement) are valuable. The authors acknowledge the limitations, and the work represents a reasonable step forward in this specific task. Given the careful evaluation and clear contributions, I recommend acceptance despite the quality concerns, as the paper provides a solid foundation for future work in this area. The approach could be further improved by addressing the artifact problem, which would significantly increase its practical impact.
