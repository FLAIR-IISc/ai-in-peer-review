This paper presents a novel approach for pose-guided human image generation, conditioning a generative model on both a source appearance image and a target pose representation. The core of the proposal is a two-step adversarial network: a coarse U-Net generator first synthesizes a low-fidelity image in the target pose, which is then refined by a second generator that learns to predict a residual/difference map. This coarse-to-fine strategy builds upon a well-established line of work in conditional image generation, relating to stacked architectures like StackGAN (Zhang et al., 2017) that construct high-resolution images in sequential stages. The method is validated through experiments on the DeepFashion dataset.

**Strengths:**
*   **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The proposed coarse-to-fine strategy is a technically sound and logical fit for the complexity of the dressing and pose transfer problem. The architectural choice to have the second stage predict a difference map is an elegant refinement technique that effectively focuses the model on high-frequency details.
*   **Originality:** The paper introduces a novel and interesting task: explicitly conditioning human image synthesis on both appearance and a target pose. The tailored architecture and specific loss design represent a solid technical contribution to this problem.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Image Quality and Practicality:** The main weakness is the quality of the final images. As the authors demonstrate, the generated results contain noticeable artifacts (e.g., black dots in example ID346) and often fail to recover fine-grained textures and details. This currently limits the method's readiness for practical applications, which would require higher fidelity.
*   **Significance and Novelty:** While the specific application is new, the underlying technical approach could be viewed as an incremental variation on existing conditional GAN frameworks. The concept of stacking or conditioning generators has been previously explored (e.g., StackGAN; Isola et al., 2017), which may temper the broader significance of the contribution. The work could be perceived as a strong application paper rather than a fundamental advance in generative modeling.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper tackles a novel problem with a well-motivated and technically sound architecture. The results, while imperfect, are visually consistent and successfully demonstrate the viability of the proposed two-stage approach. Despite the image quality issues and concerns about its broader significance, the novelty of the task and the cleverness of the technical design warrant acceptance. My initial recommendation is **Accept**. I suggest the authors expand the discussion on how their stacking approach specifically differs from or improves upon prior stacked GAN models.
