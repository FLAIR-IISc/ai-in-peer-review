This paper addresses the novel and challenging task of pose-guided person image generation: synthesizing an image of a specific person in a target pose, given a single source image of that person. The authors propose a two-stage, coarse-to-fine generative framework called the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2). The first stage uses a U-Net-like architecture to generate a coarse image that matches the target pose (represented as keypoint heatmaps), focusing on the overall structure and color. This stage introduces a "pose mask loss" to prioritize the generation of the person over the background. The second stage refines the coarse output using a conditional Generative Adversarial Network (GAN), which is trained to add realistic high-frequency details and correct artifacts. This work is related to general conditional image generation but distinguishes itself from prior art by using explicit pose information for more flexible and direct control over the generated person's structure, rather than relying on more abstract conditions like text or viewpoint labels.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel and valuable problem. The ability to realistically re-render a person in an arbitrary pose has significant potential applications in data augmentation for tasks like pose estimation and person re-identification, as well as in areas like virtual try-on, gaming, and film.
*   **Quality of Method:** The proposed two-stage architecture is a sound and well-motivated approach to this difficult problem. Decomposing the task into first establishing the coarse global structure (Stage 1) and then adding fine-grained details (Stage 2) is an effective strategy that appears to work well.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The method is described clearly, the figures are illustrative, and the overall structure of the manuscript is logical.
*   **Thoroughness of Evaluation:** A major strength is the extensive evaluation of the proposed design decisions. The authors provide clear qualitative and quantitative comparisons for different pose embedding strategies and the effect of their proposed pose mask loss, which strongly supports their final model design.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Quality of Results:** While the results are promising and clearly demonstrate the viability of the approach, the generated images often contain discernible artifacts, blurring, and inconsistent textures, especially on clothing and facial details. This indicates that while the method is a strong first step, it has not yet fully solved the problem of generating truly photorealistic results.
*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** The quantitative evaluation is a weak point of the paper. The authors acknowledge that metrics like SSIM are poorly correlated with human perception of quality for GAN-generated images. The provided Inception Score helps, but its utility for this specific synthesis task is not fully established. This weakness is partly a consequence of the field lacking standard metrics for this novel task.
*   **User Study:** The Amazon Mechanical Turk user study is preliminary and not entirely convincing as a measure of realism. A brief, timed exposure test is insufficient to rigorously assess quality. A more substantive human evaluation is needed to properly support the claims of photorealism.
*   **Related Work:** The related work section could be improved by including and discussing work on iterative refinement, which shares a conceptual similarity with the proposed coarse-to-fine strategy. For instance, methods like Stacked Hourglass Networks for pose estimation (Newell et al., ECCV 2016) or iterative error feedback (Carreira et al., CVPR 2016) also use a staged process to refine an initial estimate, which would be relevant context for the architectural choices made here.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarification on Residual Blocks:** The paper mentions that using residual blocks in the generator "improves the generation performance." Could the authors please clarify how this improvement was measured? Was it based on a qualitative assessment, or was there a quantitative improvement in metrics like SSIM or IS? An ablation study would be helpful here.
2.  **On Blurring in One-Stage Models:** The results for the one-stage adversarial model (G1+D) are noticeably blurrier than the two-stage model's output. I wonder if the fully connected layer in the G1 architecture is acting as an information bottleneck, preventing the propagation of high-frequency details. Have the authors considered an experiment where the Stage 2 generator (which has no fully connected layer) is trained adversarially from the start, using the same inputs as Stage 1? This could help determine if the two-stage approach is inherently better or if the bottleneck in G1 is the primary cause of blurring in the one-stage model.
3.  **Human Evaluation:** I strongly suggest replacing the current user study with a more rigorous human evaluation. A two-alternative forced-choice (2AFC) task, asking participants to choose the more realistic image between the model's output and a baseline, or between real and generated images, would provide much stronger evidence of generation quality.

**Recommendation:**

Despite the weaknesses noted, this is a strong contribution. The paper introduces a novel and important problem, proposes a well-designed and sensible solution, and supports it with extensive analysis. The strengths in novelty, clarity, and the thoroughness of the component-wise evaluation significantly outweigh the current limitations in result quality and quantitative metrics, which are understandable for an initial foray into such a challenging task. I recommend **acceptance**.
