This paper proposes a novel Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG) that allows synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses, based on an image of that person and a novel pose. The generation framework PG utilizes the pose information explicitly and consists of two key stages: pose integration and image refinement. The authors' approach is novel and well-motivated, and the paper is well-written.

The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work, including various generative models and image-to-image translation techniques. However, they could strengthen this section by including more recent works on iterative refinement, such as Carreira et al. (2016) and Newell et al. (2016).

The proposed two-stage coarse-to-fine network architecture is a key contribution of the paper. The first stage, pose integration, uses a U-Net-like network to generate an initial but coarse image of the person with the target pose. The second stage, image refinement, uses a variant of Deep Convolutional GAN (DCGAN) to further refine the initial generation result. The authors provide extensive evaluation of their design decisions, including different pose embeddings, losses, and network architectures.

The results presented in the paper are promising, but they contain discernible artifacts. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on two person datasets, Market-1501 and DeepFashion, and provide both qualitative and quantitative results. However, the quantitative evaluation is weak, partly due to the lack of suitable metrics. The authors propose a variant of Structural Similarity (SSIM) and the Inception Score (IS) to measure the quality of synthesis, but these metrics may not fully capture the nuances of the generated images.

The authors also conduct a user study to evaluate the realism of their generated images. However, the study is preliminary and unconvincing, and the reviewer recommends replacing it with a more substantive human evaluation.

One potential issue with the proposed approach is the blurring that occurs in the first stage of the network. The authors suggest that this is due to the use of L1 loss, but it is unclear whether this is the primary cause. The reviewer suggests experimenting with training Stage 2 with Stage 1 inputs adversarially to see if this reduces blurring. Additionally, the reviewer questions whether the blurring with Stage 1+GAN is caused by the intermediate fully connected layer.

Despite these limitations, the reviewer recommends acceptance of the paper based on its novelty and approach. The proposed Pose Guided Person Generation Network is a significant contribution to the field of computer vision, and the authors' evaluation provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and well-motivated approach to person image generation.
* The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work and a clear explanation of their approach.
* The proposed two-stage coarse-to-fine network architecture is a key contribution of the paper.
* The results presented in the paper are promising, despite containing discernible artifacts.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The quantitative evaluation is weak, partly due to the lack of suitable metrics.
* The user study is preliminary and unconvincing.
* The blurring that occurs in the first stage of the network is a potential issue.

Overall, the reviewer believes that the paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses and recommends acceptance. However, the authors should address the limitations mentioned above and provide a more thorough evaluation of their approach.
