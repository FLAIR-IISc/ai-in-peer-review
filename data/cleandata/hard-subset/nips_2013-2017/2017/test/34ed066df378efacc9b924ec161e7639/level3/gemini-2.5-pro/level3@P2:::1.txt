**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel task: generating an image of a specific person in an arbitrary target pose, conditioned on a single source image of that person and a representation of the target pose. To tackle this, the authors propose the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG²), a two-stage framework. The first stage uses a U-Net-like generator (G1) to create a coarse image that matches the target pose, integrating the source image appearance with target pose heatmaps. This stage introduces a "pose mask loss" to focus learning on the foreground person rather than the background. The second stage refines this coarse output using another U-Net-like generator (G2) within a conditional GAN framework, adding finer details and improving sharpness. The paper provides extensive experiments on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets, demonstrating the model's ability to generate plausible images. This work relates to the broad field of conditional image generation, but distinguishes itself from prior work on viewpoint synthesis (e.g., Zhao et al. 2016) by conditioning on a more explicit and flexible representation of body configuration (keypoint heatmaps). The two-stage, coarse-to-fine strategy is reminiscent of iterative refinement approaches seen in other domains, such as pose estimation.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)**

*   **Originality & Significance:** The paper proposes a novel and challenging problem of explicit pose-guided image generation. This is a significant step beyond conditioning on abstract labels like viewpoint, offering finer-grained control over the output. The potential applications in data augmentation, content creation, and e-commerce are substantial. The proposed two-stage architecture is a sensible and effective approach to disentangle the challenges of global structure transfer and fine-detail synthesis.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The methodology is explained clearly, and the motivations behind key design choices (e.g., two-stage design, pose mask loss, GAN refinement) are well-articulated.
*   **Thorough Experimental Analysis:** A major strength of this work is the comprehensive ablation study. The authors systematically evaluate different pose embedding strategies, the effect of their proposed pose mask loss, and the benefit of the two-stage model over a single-stage adversarial approach. This rigorous analysis provides convincing support for their final architecture.
*   **Qualitative Results:** The qualitative results, particularly on the DeepFashion dataset, are promising. The model successfully transfers appearance details from the source image onto the new pose, generating images that are often plausible and detailed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection)**

*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** The quantitative evaluation is a notable weakness. As the authors correctly point out, metrics like SSIM and Inception Score do not always correlate well with perceptual quality, and blurry images can often score deceptively high on SSIM. This makes it difficult to quantitatively assess the improvement from the refinement stage (G1+G2+D), which is qualitatively clear but not reflected in the scores.
*   **Result Quality and Artifacts:** While promising, the generated images still contain discernible artifacts, especially on the more complex Market-1501 dataset. Faces can be distorted, textures can be smeared, and complex clothing patterns are not always preserved faithfully. This indicates that while the approach is sound, there is still a considerable gap to achieving photorealism.
*   **User Study:** The user study on AMT feels preliminary. A 1-second exposure time may be too short for users to make a reliable judgment, potentially leading to noisy data. The results, such as the one-stage model being rated more "real" than the two-stage model on Market-1501, are counter-intuitive and suggest the evaluation protocol may need refinement. While including a user study is commendable, a more substantive human evaluation would be needed to make strong claims about photorealism.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Related Work:** The concept of iterative refinement is central to your two-stage approach. It would strengthen the paper to connect this to related ideas in other areas, such as the stacked hourglass networks for pose estimation (Newell et al., 2016) or iterative error feedback (Carreira et al., 2016), which also use multi-stage processes to refine an initial estimate.
2.  **Clarification on Residual Blocks:** In Section 3.1, you state that using residual blocks "improves the generation performance." Could you please elaborate on this? Was this improvement measured quantitatively (e.g., higher SSIM) or observed qualitatively (e.g., fewer artifacts, sharper results)?
3.  **On Blurring in Stage I:** The results from the one-stage model (G1+D) are significantly blurrier than the two-stage model, despite being trained adversarially. You remove the fully-connected layer from the generator in Stage II to "preserve more details". Is it possible that this fully-connected layer in G1 is the primary bottleneck causing information loss and blurring, and that its removal is a more critical factor than the two-stage process itself?
4.  **Alternative Experiment:** A valuable comparison would be to train a single-stage model that has the same architecture as G2 (i.e., no fully-connected layer) adversarially from scratch, taking the source image and pose heatmaps as input. This would help isolate whether the benefit comes purely from the two-stage refinement process or also from the improved generator architecture in the second stage.

**Overall Recommendation**

Despite the weaknesses in the quantitative metrics and the remaining artifacts in the results, I recommend acceptance. The paper introduces a novel and significant problem, proposes a well-motivated and technically sound solution, and supports its design with an excellent set of ablation studies. The clarity of the presentation and the potential impact of this work on future research in controllable image generation outweigh its current limitations. This is a solid contribution to the field.
