## Review of Pose Guided Person Image Generation

This paper introduces a novel task – generating person images in arbitrary poses given a reference image and a target pose (represented as heatmaps) – and proposes a two-stage coarse-to-fine network, Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), to tackle it. The approach leverages a U-Net-like architecture for initial pose integration and a conditional DCGAN for refinement, employing a mask loss to focus on the person’s appearance rather than the background. 

The work builds on recent advancements in image generation using GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014), and conditional image generation (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), extending them to the specific problem of pose-guided person image manipulation. It aligns with the growing interest in controllable image synthesis, aiming for more explicit control over the generation process – a theme frequently explored at NIPS. While several works explore conditioning image generation on labels, texts or images (Reed et al., 2016; Zhao et al., 2017), this paper uniquely focuses on direct pose manipulation. 

The paper’s strengths lie in its clear motivation, well-defined task, and a thoughtful exploration of design choices through ablation studies. The authors provide extensive analysis on the impact of different pose embedding methods and the proposed mask loss, demonstrating a solid understanding of their model. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the paper easy to follow.

However, the quantitative evaluation is weak. While SSIM and Inception Score are reported, they appear insufficient for assessing the quality of generated images, particularly considering the artifacts observed. The lack of more suitable metrics for pose-guided image generation is a notable limitation. Furthermore, the user study, as acknowledged in the rebuttal, is preliminary and unconvincing. A more robust human evaluation is needed. The blurring observed, particularly with the Stage1+GAN combination, warrants further investigation – specifically addressing the question of whether the intermediate fully connected layer contributes to this issue. Training Stage 2 with Stage 1 inputs adversarially, as suggested, is a promising avenue to explore. Finally, the related work section could benefit from including recent iterative refinement approaches (Carreira et al., 2016; Newell et al., 2016). 

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The novelty of the task and the proposed approach, combined with the thorough experimental analysis of design decisions, make this work a valuable contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** Weak quantitative evaluation and preliminary user study. Observable artifacts in generated images.

**Overall:** Despite the limitations, I recommend acceptance. The proposed task and architecture are novel and promising, and the paper demonstrates a strong understanding of the challenges involved in pose-guided image generation. Addressing the suggested improvements would further strengthen the work.
