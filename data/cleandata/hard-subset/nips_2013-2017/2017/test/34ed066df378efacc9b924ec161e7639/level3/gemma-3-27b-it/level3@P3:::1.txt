This paper introduces a novel task – pose-guided person image generation – and a corresponding two-stage network (PG2) to address it. The core idea is to transfer the appearance of a person from one pose to another, conditioned on a reference image and a target pose represented as heatmaps. The network architecture combines a U-Net-like structure for initial pose integration with a conditional DCGAN for refinement. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem, proposed solution, and experimental setup.

The work builds upon the recent surge in generative image modeling, particularly GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014), and extends the idea of conditional generation (Mirza & Osindero, 2014; Reed et al., 2016) by explicitly incorporating pose information. While related work acknowledges image-to-image translation (Isola et al., 2017) and view synthesis (Chen et al., 2017; Zhao et al., 2017), it differentiates itself by focusing on explicit pose control using heatmaps, offering a more expressive conditioning mechanism than previous approaches. It’s worth noting the emergence of iterative refinement techniques (Carreira et al., 2016; Newell et al., 2016) which, while not directly addressing image generation, share a similar coarse-to-fine strategy and should be included in the related work.

The strengths of the paper lie in its novelty, the clear two-stage architecture, and the extensive ablation studies that evaluate design choices like pose embedding methods and loss functions. The qualitative results are visually compelling, demonstrating the network's ability to transfer appearance while respecting the target pose. The inclusion of a pose mask loss is a sensible addition to focus the generation process on the human body. 

However, the quantitative evaluation is weak. While SSIM and Inception Score are reported, they are known to sometimes correlate poorly with perceptual quality (Johnson et al., 2016). The observed discrepancies between quantitative scores and qualitative observations reinforce this concern. Furthermore, suitable metrics for assessing the quality of pose transfer are lacking, making it difficult to objectively compare the generated images to the ground truth.  A more robust evaluation, perhaps involving perceptual studies, would significantly strengthen the paper.

Several questions and potential improvements arise. First, it would be helpful to understand *how* residual blocks improve generation performance. The paper mentions improvement, but lacks specific metrics or qualitative observations illustrating the effect. Did residual blocks lead to sharper images, reduced artifacts, or faster convergence?  Second, the blurring observed in the Stage 1 results, even with the pose mask loss, is a concern. I suggest an experiment where Stage 2 is trained with Stage 1 inputs adversarially, aiming to further reduce this blurring. This could encourage Stage 2 to learn more effective refinement strategies.  Finally, the paper mentions the fully connected layer in Stage 1 might be a source of information loss contributing to blurring. Investigating whether removing this layer, or replacing it with convolutional layers, improves performance would be insightful.

Despite these weaknesses, I recommend acceptance. The novelty of the task and the proposed approach are significant, and the paper provides a solid foundation for future research in pose-guided image generation. The extensive ablation studies demonstrate a careful consideration of design choices and a strong understanding of the underlying challenges.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel task and approach.
*   Clear and well-structured architecture.
*   Extensive experimental evaluation of design choices.
*   Qualitatively promising results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak quantitative evaluation.
*   Lack of strong baseline comparisons.
*   Need for clarification on the benefits of residual blocks.

**Rebuttal Consideration:** The rebuttal notes the user study was preliminary and unconvincing. I concur with this assessment. Replacing it with a more substantive human evaluation – asking participants to rate the realism and pose accuracy of generated images – would address a critical weakness and further strengthen the paper. However, the overall positive recommendation stands given the novelty and methodological soundness of the work.
