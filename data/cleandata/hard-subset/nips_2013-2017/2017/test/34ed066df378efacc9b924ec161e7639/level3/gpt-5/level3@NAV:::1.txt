This paper tackles a compelling and timely problem: generating an image of a specific person in a new pose by combining the appearance from a source image with an explicit target pose encoded as keypoint heatmaps. The proposed two-stage, coarse-to-fine architecture first integrates pose and appearance with a U-Net-like generator and masked L1 loss, then refines results via an adversarially trained U-Net that predicts a difference map, using a pairwise conditional discriminator. The work is related to conditional image generation (e.g., cGANs, image-to-image translation), pose-conditioned human synthesis and view synthesis, and echoes iterative/refinement paradigms common in vision (e.g., iterative error feedback, Carreira et al. 2016; stacked hourglass, Newell et al. 2016), which should be discussed explicitly.

Strengths
- Novel task formulation with explicit pose conditioning and a well-motivated, two-stage design.
- Clear architectural choices (pose heatmaps, masked L1, difference-map GAN) and thorough ablations on pose embeddings, losses, and stages.
- Well-written and easy to follow; qualitative results are often convincing and show meaningful control.

Weaknesses
- Quantitative evaluation is weak: SSIM/IS (even masked variants) are ill-suited for perceptual fidelity; metrics that correlate with human judgment or identity preservation are missing.
- Results still contain discernible artifacts; some blurring remains, especially from Stage I.
- Related work omits iterative refinement literature (Carreira et al. 2016; Newell et al. 2016).
- The user study is preliminary (brief exposure, small scale) and not conclusive.

Questions/requests for clarification
- Residual blocks: how exactly do they improve generation? Please quantify (e.g., SSIM, FID, LPIPS, re-id matching) and describe qualitative effects.
- Is the blurring observed with Stage1+GAN attributable to the intermediate fully connected layer? An ablation that removes the FC in Stage I would be informative.
- Why not backpropagate the adversarial loss from Stage II through Stage I (end-to-end fine-tuning) to reduce Stage I blur?

Suggestions
- Add Carreira et al. (IEF) and Newell et al. (stacked hourglass) to related work as conceptually aligned iterative refinement.
- Evaluate with perceptual metrics (FID, LPIPS) and identity preservation (re-id matching/verification). Replace the preliminary AMT test with a more substantive human study.
- Try adversarially fine-tuning Stage I together with Stage II, and/or feature/perceptual losses, to mitigate blurring.

Pros
- Clear novelty in task and approach; strong ablations; good clarity.

Cons
- Weak quantitative evidence; artifacts; missing discussion of closely related iterative refinement work.

Recommendation: Accept. Despite limitations in metrics and some artifacts, the paper’s novelty, clear design, and thorough analysis make it a valuable contribution. Rebuttal note: the current user study is unconvincing; a stronger human evaluation is encouraged, but my positive recommendation stands.
