This paper introduces a novel and interesting task: generating a photorealistic image of a person by combining an appearance from a source image with an explicit target pose, represented by keypoint heatmaps. This work extends prior research in conditional image generation, but is distinct from view-synthesis methods by conditioning on a precise and flexible pose representation rather than abstract viewpoints. To tackle this challenging problem, the authors propose a two-stage, coarse-to-fine network. Stage 1 uses a U-Net-like architecture to generate a coarse image that captures the global structure and pose, while Stage 2 employs a conditional GAN to refine the initial output, adding details and sharpness.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper proposes a novel and well-defined problem with significant potential applications, from data augmentation for pose estimation to creative tools. The proposed two-stage approach is sensible and well-motivated.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and includes extensive ablation studies that thoroughly evaluate key design decisions, such as the pose embedding method and the use of a masked loss function. This demonstrates a careful and rigorous research methodology.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Results:** While the qualitative results are promising, the generated images still contain discernible artifacts and blurriness, which are not fully resolved by the refinement stage.
*   **Evaluation:** The quantitative evaluation is weak, a fact the authors acknowledge is partly due to the lack of suitable metrics for generative tasks. The provided user study is preliminary and unconvincing; a more substantive human evaluation is needed to properly assess perceptual quality.
*   **Related Work:** The discussion could be strengthened by including connections to iterative refinement methods from related fields, such as human pose estimation (e.g., Carreira et al., 2016; Newell et al., 2016).

**Questions and Suggestions:**
1.  The authors state that residual blocks improve performance. Could they clarify this claim with specific metrics or qualitative effects?
2.  The blurring from Stage 1 seems to persist. As an experiment, have the authors considered training Stage 2 adversarially with the Stage 1 *inputs* (source image and pose map) to see if this reduces blurring?
3.  Is the blurring observed in the one-stage "G1+D" model potentially exacerbated by the information bottleneck of the intermediate fully-connected layer?

Despite the limitations in the final image quality, the novelty of the task, the soundness of the proposed architecture, and the thorough experimental analysis are major strengths. I recommend acceptance.
