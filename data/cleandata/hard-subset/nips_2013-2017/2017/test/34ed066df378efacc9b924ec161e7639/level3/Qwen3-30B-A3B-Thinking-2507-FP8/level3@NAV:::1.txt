

This paper proposes a novel task of pose-guided person image generation, where the goal is to synthesize person images in arbitrary poses based on a reference image and target pose (represented as heatmaps). The authors introduce a two-stage coarse-to-fine architecture: Stage I integrates pose and appearance using a U-Net-like network with a novel pose mask loss to focus on human body regions, while Stage II refines the coarse result using adversarial training on a difference map. The approach represents a meaningful extension of conditional image generation work (e.g., [36]) by explicitly conditioning on pose information rather than abstract attributes.

The paper demonstrates strong strengths: it is exceptionally well-written with clear organization, provides extensive ablation studies on pose embeddings and loss functions, and shows promising qualitative results on both DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets. The two-stage architecture effectively addresses the challenging pose transfer task by separating global structure learning from detail refinement. The novel pose mask loss is particularly well-motivated and validated through thorough experiments.

However, several weaknesses warrant attention. The quantitative evaluation is notably weak due to reliance on SSIM and Inception Score, which fail to correlate with perceptual quality as evidenced by the paper's own qualitative results. The user study is preliminary and unconvincing (as noted in the rebuttal), requiring replacement with a more substantive human evaluation. The paper also fails to cite recent iterative refinement works (Carreira et al. 2016; Newell et al. 2016), which are highly relevant to the two-stage approach. While the authors note that residual blocks improve performance, they provide no specific metrics or qualitative evidence to support this claim.

The paper's novelty and technical soundness are strong, with a clear contribution to the field of conditional image generation. The significance is evident through applications in movie making and pose estimation data synthesis. Despite the weaknesses in evaluation, the core approach is compelling and well-executed.

Arguments for acceptance: Novel task, well-motivated architecture, strong qualitative results, extensive design evaluation, and clear writing.  
Arguments against acceptance: Weak quantitative evaluation, unconvincing user study, and incomplete related work.

I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the evaluation weaknesses in the camera-ready version, particularly by implementing a more rigorous human evaluation and incorporating relevant iterative refinement literature. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of conditional image generation.
