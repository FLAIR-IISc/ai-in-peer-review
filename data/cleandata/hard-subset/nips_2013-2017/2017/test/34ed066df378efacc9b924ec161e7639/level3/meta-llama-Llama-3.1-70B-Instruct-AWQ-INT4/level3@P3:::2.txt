This paper proposes a novel task of generating person images conditioned on both a reference image and a specified pose, with the goal of manipulating a person in an image to an arbitrary pose. The authors introduce a two-stage approach, the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), to address this task. The first stage, pose integration, uses a U-Net-like network to generate a coarse image that captures the global structure of the human body in the target image. The second stage, image refinement, employs a variant of a conditional DCGAN to fill in more appearance details via adversarial training.

The authors' contribution is three-fold. Firstly, they propose a novel task of conditioning image generation on a reference image and an intended pose. Secondly, they explore several ways to integrate pose information with a person image, including a novel mask loss that encourages the model to focus on transferring the human body appearance instead of background information. Lastly, they divide the problem into two stages, with the first stage focusing on global structure and the second stage on filling in appearance details.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. The use of a two-stage architecture is a good strategy for addressing the challenging task of pose transfer. The idea of using a difference map to refine the initial result is also useful, as it speeds up the convergence of model training and preserves more details from the input.

The experimental results on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The authors provide both qualitative and quantitative results, including comparisons with alternative methods and a user study. The results show that the proposed method can generate high-quality person images with convincing details.

However, the generated images still contain visible artifacts, and the method fails to recover high-frequency details in some cases. For example, in Figure 4, the result of G1+G2+D for person ID 346 contains black dots that are not present in the target image. This suggests that the method still needs significant improvement in terms of image quality.

In terms of practicality, the proposed method may not be directly applicable to real-world applications due to the quality issues mentioned above. However, the idea of using a two-stage architecture and a difference map to refine the initial result is a useful contribution that can be built upon in future work.

The significance of the paper is that it introduces a novel task and a new approach to addressing it. However, the approach can be viewed as another variant of conditional GANs, and its broad appeal may be limited. Additionally, the idea of stacking generators has been proposed before, such as in StackGAN and Stacked GANs.

In terms of related work, the authors provide a comprehensive review of existing methods for generative image modeling, including Variational Autoencoders, Generative Adversarial Networks, and Autoregressive models. They also discuss the limitations of existing methods and how their approach addresses these limitations.

Overall, the paper is technically novel and produces visually consistent results. However, the quality issues and limited broad appeal of the approach are significant weaknesses. Despite these weaknesses, the reviewer's initial recommendation is to accept the paper.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper introduces a novel task and a new approach to addressing it.
* The two-stage architecture and the use of a difference map to refine the initial result are useful contributions.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
