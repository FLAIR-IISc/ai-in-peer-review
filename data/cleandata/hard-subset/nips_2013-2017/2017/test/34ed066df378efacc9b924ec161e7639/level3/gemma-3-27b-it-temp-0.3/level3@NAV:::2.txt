## Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

This paper introduces a novel approach to human image generation, specifically focusing on transferring a person’s appearance to a new pose given a reference image. The core idea is a two-stage adversarial generative network: a coarse U-Net generates an initial image based on the input image and target pose, followed by a second generator that predicts a residual/difference map to refine the output. This coarse-to-fine strategy is well-motivated for the complexities of pose and clothing manipulation. 

The work builds upon the recent surge in GAN-based image generation techniques (Goodfellow et al., 2014), and specifically draws parallels to stacked GAN architectures (Huang et al., 2016; StackGAN) in its multi-stage refinement process. It also acknowledges related work in conditional image generation (Mirza & Osindero, 2014; Reed et al., 2016) but distinguishes itself by explicitly conditioning on pose information, a relatively unexplored area. The use of a difference map for refinement is a clever technique, aiming to simplify the learning process for the second generator. 

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the proposed methodology. The experimental setup on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets is appropriate, and the ablation studies (different pose embeddings, loss functions, and stage configurations) provide valuable insights. The quantitative and qualitative results demonstrate the potential of the approach, although the generated images still exhibit visible artifacts, as noted in the paper itself (e.g., black dots, blurry details). 

However, the image quality remains a significant weakness. While the results are visually consistent, they lack the high-frequency details necessary for realistic applications. The practicality of the approach is therefore limited until further improvements are made. Furthermore, the significance of the contribution is somewhat debatable. While the task novelty is present, the underlying technique is largely a conditional GAN variant, and the broader applicability beyond pose transfer might be limited. The prior art section acknowledges similar stacking/conditioning approaches, but could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the differences and advantages of this specific implementation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-executed.
*   Introduces a novel task – conditioning image generation on both appearance and pose.
*   The coarse-to-fine strategy with a difference map is a useful refinement technique.
*   Clear writing and organization.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Image quality needs substantial improvement.
*   Limited significance – largely a conditional GAN variant.
*   Prior work on stacked generators exists.



**Overall Assessment:** Despite the quality issues, I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a technically novel approach to a challenging problem, and the results, while not perfect, are promising. The authors are aware of the limitations and the work provides a solid foundation for future research in this area.
