

This paper introduces Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), a two-stage framework for synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses based on a reference image and target pose. The work extends conditional image generation by explicitly incorporating pose information as a conditioning factor, addressing a challenging problem in person image manipulation. The authors propose a divide-and-conquer strategy: Stage-I integrates pose information using a U-Net-like architecture with a novel pose mask loss to generate a coarse but structurally correct image, while Stage-II refines this result by generating a difference map via adversarial training to add high-frequency details.

The paper makes several meaningful contributions. First, it introduces a novel task of conditioning image generation on both appearance and pose, which is more expressive than previous approaches that used abstract conditions like viewpoint labels. Second, the pose mask loss effectively handles background inconsistencies between reference and target images by focusing generation on the human body. Third, the two-stage architecture with difference map refinement is particularly clever, as it simplifies the refinement task and accelerates convergence compared to end-to-end approaches.

The technical execution is strong. The pose embedding using 18 heatmaps (rather than keypoint coordinates) proves effective, as shown in ablation studies where it outperforms alternative embedding methods. The pose mask loss significantly improves results by reducing background influence, particularly on the Market-1501 dataset with complex backgrounds. The two-stage approach consistently produces more photo-realistic results than one-stage alternatives, with the difference map refinement strategy being a notable technical contribution. The experiments are thorough, evaluating multiple aspects (pose embeddings, losses, architecture) on two datasets (DeepFashion and Market-1501), with quantitative metrics (SSIM, Inception Score), qualitative comparisons, and user studies.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear explanations of the method and thoughtful ablation studies. The related work section properly contextualizes the contribution, distinguishing it from prior conditional GAN approaches like StackGAN and [36]. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the current image quality not being sufficient for practical applications.

However, the contribution has some limitations. While the two-stage approach is technically sound, the core components (U-Net, GAN, difference maps) are not entirely novel—stacked generator approaches have been explored before in works like StackGAN. The significance is somewhat constrained to the specific person image generation domain, with limited broader appeal compared to more general image generation techniques. The generated images still contain visible artifacts (e.g., black dots in ID346 as noted in the key points), and the quality requires significant improvement before practical application.

The paper's quality is strong technically, with well-supported claims and careful experimental design. The clarity is excellent, with sufficient detail for reproduction. Originality is moderate—while the specific application is novel, the technical approach combines existing techniques in a clever way for this domain. Significance is moderate within the person image generation task, but more limited for the broader field.

The main strengths are the well-executed two-stage approach, the effective pose mask loss, and the difference map refinement strategy. The main weaknesses are the persistent image artifacts, limited broader significance, and somewhat incremental novelty compared to related work.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper presents a technically sound solution to a challenging problem with clear methodological contributions (pose mask loss, difference map refinement). The evaluation is thorough and convincing, demonstrating clear advantages over baselines. The contribution is significant for the specific task of pose-guided person generation.

Arguments against acceptance: The image quality still contains visible artifacts that would limit practical utility. The novelty is somewhat incremental as it combines existing techniques rather than introducing fundamentally new approaches. The broader significance is constrained to a specific application domain.

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution to the field of conditional image generation, specifically for person image manipulation. While not transformative for the broader field, it makes a well-executed and significant contribution to its specific application domain. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides a valuable solution to a challenging problem. Despite the image quality limitations, the technical contribution is sufficient for acceptance at NIPS.
