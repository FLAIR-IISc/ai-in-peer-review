- Introduces pose-guided person image generation transferring appearance between poses using pose heatmaps and a reference image.  
- Proposes Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) with a two-stage pipeline: pose integration followed by image refinement, using U-Net-like structures and adversarial training.  
- Extends conditional GANs to incorporate explicit pose control and emphasizes keypoint-based pose representations over multi-view methods.  
- Strength: clearly defines a novel, practically useful problem and a sensible two-stage decomposition inspired by prior work.  
- Strength: thoughtful architecture design with extensive ablation studies demonstrating the importance of components like the pose mask loss.  
- Strength: explores different pose embedding strategies and analyzes L1 vs. mask loss effects.  
- Experimental validation: demonstrates promising results on Market-1501 (re-ID) and DeepFashion datasets, showing method versatility.  
- Weakness: generated images still contain noticeable artifacts and visual defects.  
- Weakness: quantitative evaluation relying on SSIM and Inception Score is inadequate for perceptual quality; mask-SSIM is an imperfect fix.  
- Weakness: related work omits some recent iterative refinement advances (e.g., Carreira et al., Newell et al.).  
- Weakness: unclear explanation and quantification of how residual blocks improve generation performance.  
- Weakness: the role of the intermediate fully connected layer in Stage-I and its potential contribution to blurriness needs further investigation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel task with explicit pose control, well-motivated architecture and losses, strong ablation evidence, and clear writing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: persistent image artifacts, insufficient quantitative/perceptual evaluation, and limited related-work discussion.  
- Overall recommendation: accept, contingent on addressing evaluation and related-work issues.  
- Rebuttal note: authors ran a preliminary user study, but a more rigorous human evaluation is still needed.
