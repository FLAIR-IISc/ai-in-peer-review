This paper introduces a novel approach to pose-guided person image generation, utilizing a two-stage adversarial network (PG2). The core idea is to first generate a coarse image based on the input appearance and desired pose using a U-Net, followed by a refinement stage employing a second U-Net-like generator to predict and apply a difference map, enhancing detail via adversarial training. Experiments are conducted on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets.

The work builds upon the recent surge in generative image modeling, particularly GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014), and extends conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) by explicitly incorporating pose as a conditioning factor. While previous works have explored conditional image generation with attributes (Yan et al., 2015) or images (Chen et al., 2017; Zhao et al., 2017), this work uniquely focuses on manipulating pose, offering a more direct and flexible control mechanism. The two-stage approach, with its difference map prediction, echoes strategies seen in StackGAN (Zhang et al., 2017) and related stacked architectures, though adapted for pose-guided generation. The use of pose heatmaps as input is a reasonable encoding of pose information, avoiding the need for costly pose annotations.

**Strengths:**

* **Technically Sound:** The proposed architecture is well-defined and leverages established components like U-Nets and DCGANs in a thoughtful manner. The coarse-to-fine strategy is well-suited for the complexities of pose transfer and clothing deformation.
* **Well-Organized & Clear:** The paper is exceptionally well-structured, clearly explaining the motivation, method, experiments, and results. The diagrams are helpful, and the writing is concise and easy to follow.
* **Novelty (Task):** Introducing conditioning on both appearance *and* pose for human image generation is a genuine contribution. The potential applications in areas like virtual try-on, character animation, and data augmentation for pose estimation are compelling.
* **Useful Technique (Difference Map):** The idea of predicting a difference map for refinement is a clever way to focus the generator’s learning, leading to faster convergence and potentially better results. The pose mask loss is a sensible addition to regularize the training process.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Image Quality:** Despite the technical soundness, the generated images, particularly those from Market-1501, exhibit noticeable artifacts and lack high-frequency details. The examples provided (e.g., ID346) clearly demonstrate these shortcomings, with visible black dots and blurry regions.
* **Practicality:** The current image quality is insufficient for real-world applications. Significant improvements are needed before this method could be used for tasks like virtual try-on or realistic character animation.
* **Significance (Incremental):** While technically novel, the approach could be viewed as another incremental step within the broader field of conditional GANs. Its broad appeal beyond the specific task of pose-guided person generation is somewhat limited.
* **Prior Art (Stacked Generators):** The concept of stacking generators to refine images has been explored previously (Zhang et al., 2017; Huang et al., 2017), diminishing the perceived originality of this specific architectural choice.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a novel task with a technically sound and well-explained approach.
* The coarse-to-fine strategy and difference map prediction are valuable contributions.
* The experiments demonstrate the feasibility of pose-guided image generation, even with current limitations.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The generated image quality is significantly lacking, hindering practical applicability.
* The technical novelty, while present, is incremental and builds heavily on existing work.
* The limited scope of the task may restrict broader impact within the NIPS community.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically novel and well-executed contribution to the field of generative modeling. While the generated image quality is currently a significant limitation, the proposed approach demonstrates promising results and introduces valuable techniques like difference map prediction. The clear writing and well-organized structure make the paper easy to understand and appreciate. Despite the quality concerns, I recommend **acceptance** based on the technical merit and novelty of the task, with the strong caveat that future work must address the image quality issues to unlock the full potential of this approach. The authors should focus on exploring techniques to generate sharper details and reduce artifacts.
