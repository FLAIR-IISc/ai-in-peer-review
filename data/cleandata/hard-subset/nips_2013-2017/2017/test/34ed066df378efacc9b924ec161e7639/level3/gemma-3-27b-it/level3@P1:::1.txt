This paper introduces a novel approach to person image generation, specifically focusing on transferring a person's appearance from one pose to another. The core idea is to explicitly guide the generation process using pose information, represented as heatmaps, alongside a reference image. The proposed Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) employs a two-stage architecture – pose integration followed by image refinement – leveraging U-Net-like structures and adversarial training. This work builds upon the rapidly evolving field of image generation using GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014), particularly extending conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) to incorporate explicit pose control. Related work includes methods for attribute-conditioned image generation (Yan et al., 2015) and image generation from images (Chen et al., 2017; Zhao et al., 2017), but those methods lack the precise pose control offered by this approach.  The authors differentiate their work from existing multi-view synthesis (e.g., Zhao et al., 2017) by emphasizing the expressive power of keypoint-based pose representations.

The paper’s strengths lie in its clear articulation of a novel problem, the thoughtful design of the two-stage architecture, and the extensive ablation studies demonstrating the effectiveness of key components like the pose mask loss. Dividing the problem into pose integration and refinement stages, inspired by similar strategies in other domains (authors cite [35, 9, 3, 19]), is a sensible approach, acknowledging the difficulty of end-to-end pose transfer. The exploration of different pose embedding methods and the analysis of the impact of the L1 loss vs. the proposed mask loss are valuable contributions. The experimental results on both a re-identification dataset (Market-1501, Zheng et al., 2017) and a fashion photo dataset (DeepFashion, Liu et al., 2016) showcase the method's versatility. 

However, the paper has several weaknesses. While the visual results are promising, noticeable artifacts remain in the generated images. The quantitative evaluation, relying on SSIM (Wang et al., 2004) and Inception Score (Salimans et al., 2016), appears inadequate for fully capturing the perceptual quality of the generated images, particularly given the observed artifacts. SSIM, for example, is known to be sensitive to blurriness and may not accurately reflect the visual fidelity of the generated images. The authors acknowledge this and introduce ‘mask-SSIM’ but that is still an imperfect measure. More robust metrics, or a detailed human evaluation, would significantly strengthen the assessment. The related work section, while reasonably comprehensive, could benefit from including more recent advancements in iterative refinement approaches for pose estimation and image generation (Carreira et al., 2016; Newell et al., 2016).  Further clarification is needed regarding the mechanism by which residual blocks improve generation performance; the authors mention an improvement but don't quantify it or provide specific insights. Finally, while the ablation studies are thorough, the impact of the intermediate fully connected layer in Stage-I's U-Net warrants further investigation – could it be contributing to the blurring observed when directly training a GAN in a single stage?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The task of pose-guided person image generation with explicit pose control is novel and addresses a challenging problem with potential applications in areas like movie making and pose estimation training data generation.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed architecture and loss functions are well-motivated and the ablation studies provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the key design choices.
*   **Experimental Validation:**  The method demonstrates promising results on two different datasets.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the technical details are clearly explained.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Image Quality:** Generated images still exhibit noticeable artifacts.
*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** The reliance on SSIM and Inception Score is insufficient for a comprehensive assessment of image quality.
*   **Limited Discussion of Related Work:**  Missing citations to recent advancements in iterative refinement approaches.


**Overall Recommendation:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The novelty of the proposed task and approach, coupled with the thoughtful experimental evaluation and generally strong results, make a significant contribution to the field of image generation.  Addressing the weaknesses related to quantitative evaluation and related work would further enhance the paper's impact.
