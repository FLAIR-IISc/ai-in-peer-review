Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses pose-guided person image synthesis: given a reference image of a person and a target human pose (keypoints), synthesize the same person in the new pose. The proposed PG2 framework is two-stage. Stage I integrates the condition image and target pose using a U-Net-like encoder–decoder with residual blocks and skip connections to produce a coarse image, trained with a masked L1 loss that emphasizes foreground human pixels. Stage II refines the coarse output using an adversarially trained U-Net generator to predict a difference map between the coarse output and the target image, with a pairwise discriminator that takes the condition image and either the refined output or the ground truth. The pose is represented as 18 keypoint heatmaps; the mask is derived from connecting body parts and morphology. Experiments are on DeepFashion and Market-1501.

The problem is timely and in scope for NIPS, touching on controllable image synthesis and conditioning, with roots in conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero), image-to-image translation with adversarial losses (e.g., pix2pix), view synthesis (e.g., Reed et al.; Zhao et al.), and coarse-to-fine generation (e.g., stacked GANs). The paper mainly combines known building blocks—U-Nets, adversarial refinement, and pose heatmaps—into an application-focused pipeline for pose control.

Strengths
- Relevance and clarity: The task is important for controllable generation and potential data augmentation for vision problems. The paper is generally well written and easy to follow.
- Explicit conditioning: Using pose heatmaps is a sensible, explicit control signal compared to more abstract conditioning; the mask-based L1 is a reasonable engineering choice for foreground emphasis.
- Decomposition and difference-map idea: The two-stage coarse-to-fine design is logical, and generating a difference map in Stage II is a simple idea that could ease adversarial refinement.
- Some qualitative successes: In favorable cases, the method captures target poses and preserves coarse appearance attributes.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation: There is no rigorous quantitative evidence that the method achieves “high-quality” or near photo-realistic synthesis. Key evaluations are missing, such as:
  - Human perceptual studies measuring fooling rate.
  - Identity preservation metrics (e.g., re-ID feature similarity, identity classification accuracy).
  - Pose accuracy in outputs (e.g., PCK/PCKh against the target pose).
  - Strong baseline comparisons with standard conditional image-to-image methods using pose maps (e.g., a pix2pix-style baseline with the same inputs), or appearance-flow/warping baselines.
- Visual quality concerns: The presented images are small and often contain visible artifacts (distorted limbs, texture smearing, wrong colors), especially on challenging poses and cluttered scenes. The supplementary samples show similar issues. The claim of “high-quality” results is overstated given the noticeable errors.
- Incremental novelty: The method is primarily an application-driven combination of U-Nets, adversarial refinement, and pose heatmaps; the architectural contributions (mask loss and difference-map GAN) are modest and not deeply analyzed.
- Limited analysis: There is little ablation beyond pose embedding and mask loss; aspects such as the impact of the difference-map formulation, the pairwise discriminator versus image-only discrimination, the choice of loss weights, training stability, and generalization are not thoroughly examined.
- Figures and presentation: Figures are too small to adequately judge realism and fine details; some implementation details (e.g., robustness of the mask construction, sensitivity to pose estimation errors) would benefit from deeper discussion.

Quality
The approach is technically sound at a high level. However, the experimental validation is not sufficient to substantiate the paper’s strongest claims. Without rigorous perceptual evaluation and identity/pose metrics, it is difficult to assess whether the method truly advances the state of the art versus straightforward baselines. Many qualitative examples exhibit artifacts that undermine the realism.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and organized. The task, architecture, and training objectives are explained well. The main clarity issues are the small figure sizes (hindering assessment) and limited detail on robustness to pose detector errors and the mask pipeline.

Originality
The proposed components are largely known: U-Net-based conditional generators with skip connections, adversarial refinement in a second stage, keypoint heatmaps as spatial conditioning, and foreground-emphasized reconstruction losses. The difference-map formulation is a nice practical tweak but not conceptually substantial. Overall originality is incremental.

Significance
The problem is important, and a strong solution could have impact on controllable generation and data augmentation. As presented, the combination is promising but falls short of demonstrating a clear advance over simpler baselines or prior conditional GAN setups. The lack of convincing quantitative evidence limits the paper’s significance.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Important, in-scope problem with a clear and practical formulation.
- Explicit pose conditioning and two-stage design are reasonable and easy to reproduce.
- Some compelling qualitative results on easier cases; ablations on pose embedding and mask are informative.

Cons:
- Inadequate quantitative evaluation; no convincing human study or identity/pose metrics; weak comparison to strong baselines.
- Visual quality remains far from photo-realistic on many examples; figures too small.
- Incremental novelty with limited analysis of what drives improvements.
- Claims of high-quality results are premature.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add rigorous quantitative evaluation: human fooling rates, identity preservation via re-ID networks, pose accuracy metrics, and distributional metrics (e.g., Inception Score/FID) focused on the person foreground.
- Include strong baselines: a pix2pix-style U-Net with the same inputs; warping/appearance-flow baselines; simple one-stage GAN with pose maps.
- Report cross-dataset generalization and sensitivity to pose estimation errors; show larger, high-resolution figures.
- Deeper ablations: effect of difference-map vs direct prediction; pairwise vs non-pairwise discriminator; mask design; λ sensitivity; training stability.
- Provide code/models for reproducibility.

After rebuttal
I remain unconvinced. The response did not supply the missing rigorous evaluations nor address the core qualitative shortcomings; other reviewers similarly noted the limited quantitative evidence and artifacts in results.

Overall assessment
This is a promising direction with a clear formulation and some engineering insights. However, the current evidence does not support the strong quality claims, and the novelty is incremental. I recommend rejection at NIPS; with stronger quantitative evaluation, clearer baselines, and improved visual quality, this work could be competitive in the future.
