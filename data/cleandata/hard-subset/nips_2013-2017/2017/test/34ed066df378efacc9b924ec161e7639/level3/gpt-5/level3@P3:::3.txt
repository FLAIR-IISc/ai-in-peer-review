Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a pose-guided person image generation system (PG2) that, given a source image and a target pose (keypoints/heatmaps), synthesizes the person in the new pose. The approach is two-stage: (i) a U-Net-like network integrates the target pose with the source image to produce a coarse, pose-aligned rendering trained with a masked L1 loss to de-emphasize background; (ii) a refinement stage uses a conditional adversarial network to predict a residual (difference) map that sharpens details. The discriminator is conditioned on the pair (condition image, generated image). Experiments are shown on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64).

Methodologically, the paper largely assembles known components: pose as keypoint heatmaps, U-Nets with skip connections, residual blocks, a conditional DCGAN-style discriminator, and a two-stage/coarse-to-fine design reminiscent of stacked/“refine” generators in prior work (e.g., pix2pix, stacked GANs, staged refinement for image-to-image translation). Closely related are works on conditional image synthesis from text/attributes, multi-view or pose-conditioned generation, and people-in-clothing synthesis (e.g., Reed et al., Isola et al., Lassner et al., Zhao et al.). The main novelty is the application focus (pose-guided person synthesis), the particular mask-weighted reconstruction loss, and casting stage-II as residual refinement.

Strengths
- Interesting and timely problem with clear potential applications (data augmentation for pose estimation/re-ID, content creation).
- Clear exposition and sensible architecture choices; the system design is easy to follow and likely reproducible.
- Using pose heatmaps and a masked reconstruction loss are practical choices for conditioning and for mitigating background mismatch.
- Some generated images look reasonably consistent in pose and color; the two-stage strategy is intuitive and aligns with common practice.

Weaknesses
Quality and evaluation
- Lack of rigorous quantitative evaluation of perceptual realism and faithfulness. There is no convincing user study or statistically grounded human evaluation to measure fooling rates or user preference on a reasonably powered sample; nor are there robust perceptual metrics beyond basic image similarity or inception-like scores. For this problem, metrics such as human A/B tests, time-unlimited Turing tests, or pairwise preference studies are crucial.
- No assessment of identity preservation or pose accuracy. For person synthesis, it is important to evaluate whether the identity and clothing details are preserved (e.g., via a pretrained re-ID model similarity) and whether the generated pose matches the target (e.g., PCK from an off-the-shelf pose estimator).
- Baselines are weak. Comparisons to strong, widely used conditional image-to-image methods (e.g., pix2pix/cGAN variants) or concurrent pose-conditioned works are absent or insufficient.
- Claims of “high-quality” and “photo-realistic” are not supported: many results display artifacts, wrong limb geometry, texture smearing, and background bleeding; even the curated figures show irregularities.

Qualitative/visual concerns
- Figures are too small to judge detail; supplementary material exhibits similar issues. Even at small sizes, visible errors are frequent, particularly on hands/arms, legs, faces, and garment textures.

Originality
- The contribution is largely an application-driven combination of known building blocks (U-Nets, DCGAN, two-stage refinement, residual prediction). The masked L1 and pairwise conditioning of the discriminator are reasonable but incremental.

Clarity
- Writing is clear and the method description is organized. However, figure sizing/layout hinders assessment, and some key implementation details and ablations are either brief or missing (e.g., systematic analysis of the residual prediction vs direct prediction; stability/variance across runs).

Significance
- The problem is important, but the current level of image quality and evaluation makes it premature to claim progress toward real-image-level synthesis. Without stronger qualitative and quantitative evidence, it is hard to conclude that this advances the state of the art in a way the community can confidently build on.

Detailed suggestions for improvement
- Provide a rigorous human evaluation: sufficiently powered user studies with time-unlimited A/B tests, fooling rates with CIs, and preference judgments against strong baselines.
- Measure identity preservation (feature distance or rank-1 retrieval using a re-ID network) and pose accuracy (PCK/OKS from a pose estimator on generated vs. target images).
- Add stronger baselines: pix2pix-style models conditioned on pose heatmaps, and concurrent pose-guided generation works; ensure fair training and hyperparameter selection.
- Report additional perceptual metrics (e.g., FID when applicable for the camera-ready timeline) and per-body-part analyses to localize errors.
- Improve figure quality: larger images, zoomed-in crops on faces, hands, and garment textures; thorough failure case discussion.
- Evaluate generalization to diverse backgrounds and occlusions; analyze the impact of the masked loss vs. explicit background modeling/segmentation.

Pros
- Interesting application and clear problem statement.
- Straightforward, well-explained architecture with practical design choices.
- Some outputs are plausible; two-stage refinement is intuitive.

Cons
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation of realism, identity preservation, and pose accuracy; no convincing user study.
- Visual quality is not competitive; figures are too small and artifacts are common.
- Limited novelty; primarily a combination of known components.
- Overstated claims relative to evidence; weak baselines.

After rebuttal
I remain unconvinced. The authors did not provide new, convincing quantitative evidence (e.g., robust user studies, identity/pose metrics, or stronger baselines) to address the main concerns. Other reviewers also noted both quantitative and qualitative shortcomings. The work is promising, but the current evidence does not support presentation at a top-tier venue.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is promising and clearly written, but the paper lacks rigorous evaluation and the visual quality is not yet at a level to justify acceptance at NIPS. With stronger baselines, more convincing metrics and user studies, and higher-quality results, this could become a solid contribution.
