## Review of Pose Guided Person Image Generation

This paper introduces a novel task – generating images of a person in a new pose given a reference image and a target pose (represented as heatmaps). The authors propose a two-stage coarse-to-fine network, PG2, leveraging U-Net-like architectures and GANs to achieve this. The work builds upon the recent surge in generative image modeling with GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014), extending it to a pose-conditioned setting. While related works exist on conditional image generation (e.g., Reed et al., 2016) and multi-view synthesis (Zhao et al., 2017), this paper uniquely focuses on explicit pose control and a two-stage refinement process. 

The strengths of this paper lie in its novelty and the well-defined approach to a challenging problem. The two-stage architecture is a sensible strategy, breaking down the complex task into manageable components. The extensive ablation studies, evaluating different pose embeddings and loss functions, demonstrate a thorough understanding of the design choices and their impact. The paper is also well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the methodology and results.

However, the quantitative evaluation is weak. While SSIM and Inception Score are reported, they appear insufficient to fully capture the quality of the generated images, particularly given the observed artifacts. The authors acknowledge this limitation. Furthermore, the user study is preliminary and unconvincing, as noted in the rebuttal. A more robust human evaluation is needed to validate the perceptual quality of the generated images. The paper would benefit from a discussion of iterative refinement techniques (Carreira et al., 2016; Newell et al., 2016) which are relevant to the refinement stage. 

I have a few specific questions. First, how do the residual blocks in Stage 1 improve generation performance? Are there specific metrics or qualitative effects that demonstrate their benefit? Second, an experiment to train Stage 2 with Stage 1 inputs adversarially could potentially reduce the observed blurring. Finally, is the blurring observed with Stage 1+GAN potentially caused by the intermediate fully connected layer? Removing it, as done in Stage 2, might be a worthwhile investigation.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel task and approach, well-defined methodology, thorough ablation studies, clear writing.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Weak quantitative evaluation, preliminary user study, potential for improvement with adversarial training and architectural exploration.

Despite the limitations, I recommend acceptance based on the novelty of the task and the promising approach. Addressing the quantitative evaluation and conducting a more substantial human evaluation would significantly strengthen the paper. The rebuttal should replace the preliminary user study with a more substantive evaluation.
