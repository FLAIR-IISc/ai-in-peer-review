This paper proposes a novel Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG) that allows synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses, based on an image of that person and a novel pose. The generation framework PG utilizes the pose information explicitly and consists of two key stages: pose integration and image refinement. The authors employ a U-Net-like network to integrate the target pose with the person image, and a variant of Deep Convolutional GAN (DCGAN) model to further refine the initial generation result. The paper is well-written, and the idea of using pose information to guide the generation process is interesting.

The authors provide a thorough review of related work in generative image modeling with deep learning techniques, including Variational Autoencoders (VAE), Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), and Autoregressive models. They also discuss the challenges of transferring a person from one pose to another and propose a divide-and-conquer strategy to address this task.

The proposed method is evaluated on two person datasets, Market-1501 and DeepFashion, which contain person images with diverse poses. The authors present qualitative and quantitative results for three main aspects of PG: different pose embeddings, pose mask loss vs. standard L1 loss, and two-stage model vs. one-stage model. They also compare their method with the most related work [36].

However, there are some concerns regarding the visual quality of the generated images. While the authors claim that their method generates high-quality person images with convincing details, the results shown in the paper are not visually appealing, and the figures are too small to appreciate the details. Additionally, the generated images show many noticeable errors versus the target images.

Moreover, the authors do not provide a quantitative evaluation of their method, such as user studies or metrics to measure how often generated images fool humans. This makes it difficult to assess the quality of the generated images and the effectiveness of the proposed method.

In the rebuttal, the authors address some of the concerns raised by the reviewers, but they do not satisfactorily address the quantitative and qualitative shortcomings of their method. Therefore, I remain unconvinced that the paper is ready for presentation at a top venue like NIPS.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The idea of using pose information to guide the generation process is interesting and novel.
* The authors provide a thorough review of related work in generative image modeling with deep learning techniques.
* The proposed method is evaluated on two person datasets, which contain person images with diverse poses.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The visual quality of the generated images is not convincing, and the figures are too small to appreciate the details.
* The authors do not provide a quantitative evaluation of their method, such as user studies or metrics to measure how often generated images fool humans.
* The generated images show many noticeable errors versus the target images.
* The authors' rebuttal does not satisfactorily address the quantitative and qualitative shortcomings of their method.

Recommendation:
I recommend rejecting the paper due to its premature claim of high-quality or real-image-level results and the lack of quantitative evaluation. However, I encourage the authors to continue working on their method and to provide a more thorough evaluation of its effectiveness.
