This paper proposes Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), a GAN-based system for synthesizing novel-view person images conditioned on a reference image and a target pose, represented as keypoints. The approach leverages well-established components like DCGANs and U-Nets, framing the contribution as application-focused rather than fundamentally novel in terms of architectural innovation. The system employs a two-stage process: pose integration using a U-Net to generate a coarse image, followed by refinement with an adversarial network to enhance detail.

The work builds upon a significant body of research in generative image modeling, including VAEs, GANs, and autoregressive models (Kingma & Welling, 2013; Goodfellow et al., 2014). The conditioning aspect relates to prior work in conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014; Reed et al., 2016), and specifically to image generation conditioned on pose or viewpoint (Chen et al., 2017; Zhao et al., 2017). However, the explicit use of keypoint-based pose guidance as the primary conditioning signal distinguishes this work. The two-stage approach echoes strategies used in other generative tasks (e.g., [35, 9, 3, 19] cited in the paper) to decompose complex generation into more manageable sub-problems.

**Strengths:**

*   **Interesting Idea:** The concept of explicitly guiding person image generation with pose information is compelling and has potential applications in areas like movie making and data augmentation for pose estimation.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to understand the proposed method. The two-stage process is clearly explained.
*   **Partial Visual Appeal:** While overall quality is questionable (discussed below), some generated images exhibit plausible details in specific regions, suggesting the potential of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Quantitative Evaluation:** The most significant flaw is the complete absence of quantitative evaluation. There are no user studies (e.g., asking humans to distinguish real vs. generated images) or objective metrics (e.g., FID, precision/recall) to assess the quality and realism of the generated images. This makes it impossible to objectively determine the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   **Poor Visual Quality:** The generated images, as presented in the paper and supplementary materials, are often blurry, contain noticeable artifacts, and frequently deviate significantly from the target pose. Figures are small, further hindering detailed assessment.
*   **Limited Baselines:** The comparison to VariGAN [36] is useful, but more comprehensive comparisons to other relevant image generation techniques would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Insufficient Ablation:** While the paper explores variations of the pose embedding and loss function, the ablation study feels incomplete without more robust quantitative metrics alongside the visual comparisons.

The paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating “high-quality” or “real-image-level” results, as claimed in the abstract. The visual artifacts and lack of rigorous evaluation raise serious concerns about the practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Rebuttal:** The authors’ rebuttal did not address the core concerns regarding the lack of quantitative evaluation. While they provided additional visual examples, these did not alleviate the concerns about the overall quality of the generated images. Other reviewers echoed the sentiment that the absence of quantitative metrics was a critical weakness.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   The idea of pose-guided person generation is interesting and potentially useful.
*   The paper is clearly written and easy to understand.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   The lack of quantitative evaluation is a major flaw.
*   The visual quality of the generated images is generally poor.
*   The paper does not demonstrate a significant advance over existing techniques in image generation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising idea but is premature for presentation at a competitive venue like NIPS. The absence of quantitative evaluation and the generally poor visual quality of the results prevent a positive assessment. While the approach demonstrates some potential, it requires substantial improvement and rigorous evaluation before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field. The paper, as it stands, feels more like a proof-of-concept than a fully developed research contribution.
