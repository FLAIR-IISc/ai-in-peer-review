This paper proposes a novel Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG) to synthesize person images in arbitrary poses, based on an image of that person and a novel pose. The generation framework PG utilizes the pose information explicitly and consists of two key stages: pose integration and image refinement. The authors' contribution is three-fold: (i) proposing a novel task of conditioning image generation on a reference image and an intended pose, (ii) exploring several ways to integrate pose information with a person image, and (iii) dividing the problem into two stages to address the challenging task of pose transfer.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, and the related work section is comprehensive. The proposed method is interesting, and the idea of using a two-stage approach to address the task of pose transfer is reasonable. The experimental results on both the Market-1501 and DeepFashion datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

However, there are several concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, the paper lacks quantitative evaluation. While the authors provide some quantitative results, such as SSIM and Inception Score, these metrics are not sufficient to evaluate the quality of the generated images. A more comprehensive evaluation, such as user studies or metrics that measure how often generated images fool humans, is necessary to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

Secondly, the visual quality of the generated images is not impressive. The figures are too small, and the generated images show many noticeable errors versus the targets. The supplementary results suffer from the same issues. While the authors claim that their method generates high-quality person images with convincing details, the results do not support this claim.

Thirdly, the paper is premature to claim high-quality or real-image-level results. The generated images are not photo-realistic, and the method has several limitations. For example, the method is not able to imagine what the new background looks like, and the generated images often have artifacts.

In the rebuttal, the authors address some of the concerns raised by the reviewers. However, the rebuttal does not satisfactorily address the quantitative and qualitative shortcomings of the paper. The authors should provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their method and address the limitations of their approach.

In conclusion, while the paper proposes an interesting approach to pose-guided person image generation, it has several limitations that need to be addressed. The lack of quantitative evaluation, the poor visual quality of the generated images, and the premature claims of high-quality or real-image-level results are major concerns. Therefore, I do not recommend accepting this paper for presentation at NIPS.
