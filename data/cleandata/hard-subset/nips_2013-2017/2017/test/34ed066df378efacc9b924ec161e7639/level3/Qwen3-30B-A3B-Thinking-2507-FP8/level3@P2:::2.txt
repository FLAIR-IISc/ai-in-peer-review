

This paper introduces Pose Guided Person Image Generation (PG2), a two-stage generative framework for synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses conditioned on a reference image and target pose. The authors address the challenging pose transfer task by decomposing it into pose integration (Stage-I) and image refinement (Stage-II). Stage-I employs a U-Net with pose heatmaps to generate coarse structural outputs, using a novel pose mask loss to suppress background interference. Stage-II refines results via a conditional GAN that predicts appearance difference maps, avoiding the need to generate images from scratch. The method is evaluated on DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets, with qualitative and quantitative comparisons against variants and related work (e.g., Zhao et al. [36]).

The paper makes several clear contributions: (1) formalizing pose-conditioned person generation as a novel task, (2) proposing pose heatmaps and mask loss for effective pose integration, and (3) introducing a coarse-to-fine strategy with difference-map refinement. The work builds on prior conditional GANs (e.g., StackGAN [Zhang et al., 2017]) and pose transfer methods (e.g., Zhao et al. [36]), but distinguishes itself through explicit pose guidance via keypoints and the two-stage refinement.

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The technical approach is sound, with thorough ablation studies (pose embeddings, mask loss, two-stage vs. one-stage) and rigorous evaluation using SSIM, Inception Score, and user studies. The pose mask loss effectively addresses background interference, and the difference-map strategy improves training stability. Results on both datasets demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines, with user studies confirming perceptual quality gains (e.g., 14.9% "real" ratings for refined results vs. 9.3% for one-stage).  
*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear problem formulation, methodological details, and visual examples (Fig. 4). The two-stage decomposition is logically motivated, and the network architecture (Fig. 2) is described precisely. Supplementary materials further clarify implementation.  
*Originality:* While conditional GANs and pose transfer are established, the specific combination of pose heatmaps, mask loss, and difference-map refinement for *person-specific* pose transfer is novel. The task definition (conditioning on *both* appearance and pose) differs from prior work like [36], which uses textual pose descriptors.  
*Significance:* The approach enables practical applications (e.g., movie character animation, pose data augmentation), and the focus on identity preservation addresses a gap in existing methods. The framework’s modularity (e.g., interchangeable pose estimators) enhances applicability.

**Weaknesses:**  
*Quality:* Generated images exhibit visible artifacts (e.g., "black dots" in ID346, inconsistent textures in Fig. 6), particularly in high-frequency details like fabric patterns. The refinement stage struggles with complex poses (e.g., legs in top failure case), and Market-1501 results degrade due to background clutter. The user study reveals limitations (e.g., 14.9% "real" rating for refined results, vs. 9.3% for one-stage), indicating room for improvement.  
*Originality:* The two-stage generator stacking (Stage-I + Stage-II) is conceptually similar to StackGAN [Zhang et al., 2017], though adapted for pose transfer. The difference-map refinement is clever but not entirely novel (see [17] for similar approaches).  
*Significance:* While technically sound, the method’s impact may be limited to niche applications (e.g., fashion/pose synthesis) rather than broad AI advancement. The quality gap with human-like images (Fig. 5) suggests it is not yet ready for real-world deployment, reducing practical significance.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Novel task definition, strong empirical validation, clear methodological innovations (mask loss, difference maps), and well-executed experiments.  
*Con:* Artifact-prone generation, incremental contribution over StackGAN, and limited significance beyond pose-transfer niche.  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to pose-conditioned generation with a well-motivated, technically sound approach. While the image quality requires further refinement, the method’s novelty, thorough evaluation, and clear decomposition of the pose-transfer challenge align with NIPS’ focus on high-impact technical advances. The weaknesses are common in early-stage generative work and can be addressed in future iterations. The authors successfully demonstrate a new direction for person image synthesis, distinct from prior conditional GANs, and the work is sufficiently rigorous and novel for NIPS.
