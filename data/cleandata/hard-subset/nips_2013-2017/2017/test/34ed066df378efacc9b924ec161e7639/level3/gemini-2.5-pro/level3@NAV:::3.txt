This paper proposes a two-stage GAN-based framework, the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG²), to synthesize novel-view images of a person conditioned on a source image and a target pose represented by skeletal keypoints. The method first uses a U-Net architecture to generate a coarse image matching the target pose and then employs a second conditional GAN to refine this output, adding detail and texture. This work fits into the broader research area of conditional image synthesis, building upon established architectures like U-Nets and DCGANs, and applies them to the specific and interesting problem of human pose transfer, similar in spirit to prior work on view synthesis for faces or objects.

The strengths of the paper lie in the novelty of its application and its clarity. The problem of generating person images in arbitrary poses is compelling and has significant potential for applications in data augmentation, e-commerce, and creative tools. The paper is well-written, and the proposed two-stage approach is explained clearly and is methodologically straightforward.

However, the work suffers from significant weaknesses in its evaluation and the quality of its results. The primary concern is the lack of convincing quantitative evaluation to support its claims of "high-quality" and "photo-realistic" synthesis. While a user study is included, the reported realism scores are not strong enough to demonstrate that the generated images are consistently mistaken for real ones. The reliance on SSIM is also a limitation, as this metric often fails to capture perceptual quality.

Qualitatively, the visual results are not persuasive. The figures in the paper are small, but even so, they reveal noticeable artifacts, blurriness, and a failure to preserve identity-specific details and clothing textures. The generated outputs often appear distorted and do not reach the level of realism suggested by the authors. The technical contribution is also limited, as the framework is primarily a novel combination of well-established components, which places a greater onus on the experimental results to demonstrate a clear state-of-the-art advance.

In its current state, this work is a promising proof of concept but feels premature for a top-tier venue like NIPS. The authors' rebuttal did not sufficiently address these fundamental concerns regarding qualitative and quantitative shortcomings, a view echoed by other reviewers. Therefore, I cannot recommend acceptance.
