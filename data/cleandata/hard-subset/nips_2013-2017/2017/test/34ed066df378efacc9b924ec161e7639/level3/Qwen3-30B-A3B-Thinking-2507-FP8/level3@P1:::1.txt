

This paper proposes a novel task of pose-guided person image generation, where the goal is to synthesize person images in arbitrary poses based on a reference image of that person and a target pose (represented as heatmaps). The authors introduce a two-stage architecture called Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) that first integrates pose information with the reference image to generate a coarse pose-aligned image (Stage I), then refines this result to add high-frequency details (Stage II). The approach builds on U-Net and GAN frameworks but makes significant contributions by explicitly incorporating pose information and dividing the challenging pose transfer task into two manageable stages.

The paper demonstrates strong novelty in its problem formulation and architecture. While prior work has explored conditional image generation (e.g., [32, 18, 24, 36]), none have explicitly used pose heatmaps as a conditioning signal for person image generation with the goal of transferring both pose and appearance. The two-stage approach is particularly well-motivated: Stage I focuses on global structure (using a novel pose mask loss to suppress background interference), while Stage II uses adversarial refinement to add details. This divide-and-conquer strategy effectively addresses the challenge of simultaneously generating correct pose and high-quality appearance.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with thorough ablation studies that evaluate key design decisions: pose embedding methods (heatmaps vs. coordinates), loss functions (standard L1 vs. pose mask loss), and the two-stage vs. one-stage architecture. The qualitative results on both DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) datasets demonstrate convincing person image synthesis with reasonable details, particularly for the two-stage model. The user study, while preliminary, shows that the refined results (G1+G2+D) are more likely to be mistaken for real images than the one-stage model (G1+D).

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. The quantitative evaluation is notably weak, relying primarily on SSIM and Inception Score, which are known to poorly correlate with human perception of image quality (as the authors themselves note). The mask-SSIM and mask-IS metrics are a good attempt to address background interference, but more appropriate metrics for image generation (e.g., FID, user studies with proper statistical significance) would strengthen the evaluation. The user study is particularly underwhelming, with only 30 participants and no statistical analysis, and the authors admit it's preliminary.

The paper would benefit from incorporating recent work on iterative refinement in pose estimation (Carreira et al. 2016; Newell et al. 2016), which shares conceptual similarities with the two-stage approach. Additionally, the claim that residual blocks improve performance is not adequately supported - the authors should provide specific metrics or qualitative examples showing how residual blocks contribute to the results.

I have two specific technical concerns: First, the paper questions whether the blurring observed in the Stage 1 + GAN (G1+D) model is caused by the intermediate fully connected layer in Stage I. A controlled experiment removing this layer would clarify this issue. Second, the authors suggest training Stage II with Stage I inputs adversarially to see if it reduces blurring - this would be a valuable experiment to include.

The significance of this work is high, as pose-guided person generation has clear applications in fashion, movie making, and pose estimation data augmentation. The paper advances the state-of-the-art in conditional image generation by providing a novel framework for pose transfer that outperforms the most relevant prior work [36], as demonstrated in Figure 5.

In summary, this is a well-executed paper that introduces a novel and valuable problem with a thoughtful two-stage solution. The quality is good, with careful experimental design and clear presentation. The main weaknesses are the limited quantitative evaluation and preliminary user study, but these do not undermine the core contributions. The paper demonstrates significant technical merit and potential impact, and the proposed approach is clearly differentiated from prior work.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (with a clear architecture and ablation studies), well-written, highly original in its problem formulation and approach, and addresses a meaningful problem with clear applications. The minor weaknesses in evaluation are outweighed by the strong novelty and technical contributions. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the evaluation concerns in the camera-ready version.
