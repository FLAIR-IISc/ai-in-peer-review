

This paper proposes a two-stage GAN-based approach for synthesizing person images in novel poses, guided by target skeleton/keypoints. The authors divide the task into pose integration (Stage-I) and image refinement (Stage-II), using U-Net architectures and a pose mask loss to focus on human body transfer rather than background. While the core components (DCGANs, U-Nets) are well-established, the application to pose-guided person generation is novel.

Strengths include a clear writing style, well-structured methodology, and reasonable visual results in some cases. The pose mask loss effectively addresses background interference, and the two-stage framework provides a logical decomposition of the complex pose transfer task. The comparison with related work on multi-view person synthesis (Zhao et al., 2017) demonstrates clear advantages.

However, the paper suffers from significant shortcomings. Most critically, it lacks any quantitative evaluation of image quality beyond SSIM and Inception Score, which fail to capture perceptual realism. The user study (Section 4.4) is insufficiently detailed and lacks statistical significance testing - with only 30 users rating 55 images each, the results (14.9% "real" ratings for refined images) are not robust. The visual quality is inconsistent, with many noticeable artifacts in generated images (e.g., mismatched clothing textures, incorrect body parts), and figures are too small to assess properly.

The paper falls short on all four evaluation criteria: Quality (no robust metrics or ablation studies), Clarity (insufficient visual evidence), Originality (primarily application of known techniques), and Significance (lacks convincing evidence of real-world utility). The authors' rebuttal failed to address the quantitative evaluation gap or provide stronger visual evidence.

While the application is promising, the current work lacks the scientific rigor expected at NIPS. The absence of metrics that measure human perception of realism (e.g., standardized user studies with statistical validation) makes it impossible to claim "high-quality" results as stated in the abstract. The paper would benefit from substantial additional evaluation and refinement before submission to a top venue. I recommend rejection.
