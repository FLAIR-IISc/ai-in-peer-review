

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

This paper proposes a two-stage generative framework (PG2) for synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses given a reference image and target pose. The approach uses a U-Net-like network at stage-I to integrate pose information (represented as 18 keypoint heatmaps) with a reference image to generate a coarse pose-aligned image, followed by a refinement stage-II that uses adversarial training to add appearance details. The authors evaluate their method on Market-1501 (low-resolution re-identification) and DeepFashion (high-resolution fashion) datasets.

The work relates to recent advances in conditional image generation, particularly building on GAN-based approaches for pose transfer such as those by Zhao et al. (2017) and Lassner et al. (2017). While the authors acknowledge these works, their contribution differs in explicitly using pose keypoints as conditioning information rather than viewpoint labels, and introducing a two-stage framework to separate pose structure generation from appearance refinement.

**Quality**: The technical approach is well-structured and uses standard deep learning components (U-Net, DCGAN) with reasonable modifications. However, the paper lacks sufficient quantitative evaluation to support its claims of high-quality generation. The authors report SSIM and Inception Score metrics but fail to provide user studies or other human evaluation metrics to assess whether the generated images are convincing to human observers. The qualitative results (Figure 4) show significant artifacts, with many generated images containing noticeable errors (e.g., incorrect body parts, poor texture synthesis) that undermine the paper's claim of "photo-realistic" results. The supplementary material does not address these visual quality concerns.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to experiments. The technical details of the two-stage approach and pose mask loss are well-explained, and the network architecture is clearly described. However, the presentation of results could be improved - the figures are too small to properly assess the generated images, and the paper does not provide sufficient visual comparison of the most compelling examples.

**Originality**: The application of pose-guided person image generation is a novel problem direction within the broader context of image synthesis. The two-stage approach to separate pose structure from appearance details is a reasonable design choice, though the technical components used (U-Net, DCGAN) are not themselves novel. The pose mask loss is a minor but useful modification that helps focus generation on the human body rather than background. However, the paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from related work such as Zhao et al.'s multi-view synthesis approach, which is only briefly compared.

**Significance**: The problem of pose-guided person image generation has potential applications in fashion, movie making, and human pose estimation data augmentation. However, the significance of the contribution is limited by the lack of convincing evidence that the results are of high quality or useful for practitioners. The generated images often contain significant artifacts (e.g., Figure 4 shows blurry clothing, incorrect body parts, and background inconsistencies), which makes it questionable whether the method would be useful in real-world applications.

**Arguments for acceptance**: 
- The problem is well-motivated with clear applications.
- The two-stage framework is a reasonable approach to a challenging problem.
- The writing is clear and well-organized.
- The pose mask loss is a useful technical contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance**:
- The paper fails to provide adequate quantitative evaluation of image quality (no user studies, limited metrics).
- The visual results contain significant artifacts that undermine claims of photo-realistic generation.
- The figures are too small to properly assess the quality of generated images.
- The supplementary material does not address the visual quality concerns.
- The paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from related work in terms of both methodology and results.

The authors' rebuttal did not satisfactorily address these concerns, particularly regarding the lack of convincing quantitative evaluation and the visible artifacts in the generated images. While the problem is interesting and the approach is reasonable, the evidence provided does not support the claims of high-quality image synthesis. The paper presents a promising direction but is premature for a top-tier venue like NIPS, as it does not demonstrate results that would be useful to the broader research community.

Overall, I recommend rejection. The work is interesting but the current evidence does not demonstrate significant progress over existing methods, and the visual quality concerns are too substantial to overlook. The authors should address the quantitative evaluation gaps and improve the visual quality of their results before considering submission to a top venue.
