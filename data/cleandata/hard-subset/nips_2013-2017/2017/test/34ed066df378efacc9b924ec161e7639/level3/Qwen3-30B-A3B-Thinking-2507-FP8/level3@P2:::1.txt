

# Review for "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

This paper proposes a novel task of person image generation conditioned on a reference image and a target pose, addressing the challenge of transferring a person's appearance to a new pose while preserving identity. The authors introduce a two-stage architecture (Pose Integration followed by Image Refinement) that explicitly uses pose heatmaps as conditioning information. This work builds upon GANs and U-Net architectures but makes significant contributions through its novel two-stage approach and pose mask loss, which focuses generation on the human body rather than background. Related work is generally well-cited, though it could better incorporate recent iterative refinement techniques (Carreira et al. 2016; Newell et al. 2016).

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a well-motivated two-stage approach. The pose mask loss effectively addresses the challenge of background differences between condition and target images, as shown in the ablation studies. The authors provide extensive experimental validation of design choices, including different pose embeddings and loss functions. However, the quantitative evaluation is weak, relying on SSIM and Inception Score which do not correlate well with visual quality (as noted in their own analysis). The user study is preliminary and lacks statistical significance, failing to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method. The paper would benefit from additional quantitative metrics that better align with visual quality, such as FID or human evaluation with proper statistical analysis.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The architecture is clearly explained with helpful figures, and the ablation studies provide concrete evidence for design choices. The explanation of the pose mask loss and the two-stage approach is particularly clear. The only minor weakness is the insufficient explanation of how residual blocks improve generation performance—specifically, which metrics or qualitative effects are improved by this design choice.

**Originality:** The core contribution is novel: a two-stage approach for pose-guided person image generation that explicitly conditions on pose heatmaps. This differs significantly from previous work that used coarse pose annotations or text descriptions. While the overall approach builds on GANs and U-Net, the specific application to pose-guided person generation with the two-stage architecture and pose mask loss represents a meaningful advancement. The related work section should be expanded to include recent iterative refinement techniques (Carreira et al. 2016; Newell et al. 2016) to better position the work.

**Significance:** The proposed task has clear applications in movie making, human pose estimation, and image retrieval. The results demonstrate the ability to generate realistic person images with convincing details, outperforming the most related work [36] in qualitative comparisons. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the weak quantitative evaluation and the lack of compelling evidence for the method's superiority over alternatives. The paper would be strengthened by a more rigorous comparison with recent methods and a clearer demonstration of how this work advances the state of the art.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel two-stage architecture specifically designed for pose-guided person generation
- Strong qualitative results with convincing details in generated images
- Comprehensive ablation studies that validate key design choices
- Well-written and well-organized paper with clear explanations
- Clear application potential in multiple domains

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Weak quantitative evaluation that doesn't convincingly demonstrate superiority
- Preliminary user study lacks statistical significance and methodological rigor
- Insufficient explanation of residual block benefits
- Related work section could better incorporate recent iterative refinement techniques

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of conditional image generation with its novel pose-guided person image generation framework. While the quantitative evaluation could be strengthened, the technical quality of the work, clear presentation, and significant novelty justify acceptance. The authors should address the weak quantitative evaluation and user study limitations in the camera-ready version. The paper represents a solid contribution to NIPS, advancing the state of the art in conditional image generation with a clear, well-executed approach.

I recommend acceptance.
