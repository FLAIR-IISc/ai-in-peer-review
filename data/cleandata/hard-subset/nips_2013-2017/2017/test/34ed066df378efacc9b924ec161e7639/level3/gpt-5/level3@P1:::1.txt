This paper introduces Pose Guided Person Generation (PG2), which synthesizes an image of a person in a new pose given a reference image and a target pose. The method encodes pose as 18 keypoint heatmaps and adopts a two-stage, coarse-to-fine pipeline: Stage-I integrates the target pose and the reference image with a U-Net-like encoder-decoder (with skip connections and residual blocks) trained with a masked L1 loss to emphasize the person over the background, producing a coarse result that matches global structure. Stage-II refines this result using a conditional DCGAN trained to predict a difference (residual) map with an adversarial loss plus the same masked L1. Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) provide ablations on pose embedding, loss design (masked L1 vs L1), and single- vs two-stage architectures, along with qualitative comparisons to VariGAN [36]. The authors also report SSIM/masked-SSIM and Inception/masked-IS, and a small AMT user study.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself among conditional generative models (CVAE, conditional GANs), view/pose-conditioned image generation, and human synthesis. It goes beyond prior single-view/multi-view generation by conditioning on explicit pose heatmaps, which affords finer control than viewpoint labels [36]. The staged refinement resonates with iterative refinement ideas in vision and pose estimation (e.g., Carreira et al., iterative error feedback; Newell et al., stacked hourglass), and with stacked GANs and image-to-image translation. The related-work discussion could explicitly connect to multi-stage refinement in pose estimation and synthesis to better position the two-stage design.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-motivated task and architecture. Conditioning on explicit keypoint heatmaps provides controllable generation.
- Two-stage design is sensible and empirically effective: Stage-I captures global structure; Stage-II sharpens details via adversarial residual learning.
- Solid ablations: comparison of pose embeddings (coordinates vs heatmaps vs direct concatenation), masked L1 vs standard L1, and one-stage vs two-stage.
- Qualitative results are compelling on DeepFashion; improvements over VariGAN are visible.
- Masked losses and metrics thoughtfully address background mismatch.

Weaknesses:
- Quantitative evaluation is limited: SSIM/IS (and masked variants) do not necessarily correlate with perceptual quality; the user study is too small and uses brief exposure, limiting conclusions.
- Some generated images exhibit artifacts (background bleeding, texture distortions, gender/limb errors), particularly on Market-1501.
- The role of residual blocks and the fully connected bottleneck is asserted but not rigorously quantified.
- One-stage adversarial baseline (G1+D) remains blurry; the attribution of blur is unclear (loss vs architecture).

Quality:
- Methodologically sound and complete. Losses and conditioning are appropriate, and the residual-map refinement is well-justified.
- Training details and architectures are described; ablations support design choices. However, more rigorous metrics and analysis (e.g., FID; identity preservation; pose accuracy) would strengthen claims.
- Claims are mostly calibrated; limitations are acknowledged.

Clarity:
- The paper is well written and organized. Figures communicate the pipeline and qualitative effects. Minor suggestions: explicitly define the pose-mask construction; aggregate ablation results into a concise table; and clarify the role and impact of residual blocks and the FC layer.

Originality:
- Novel problem formulation (explicit pose-guided, identity-preserving person generation) and a practical two-stage residual refinement approach. While staged refinement and conditional GANs are known, the combination with pose heatmaps, masked losses, and residual adversarial refinement is a meaningful contribution.

Significance:
- Promising for controllable human synthesis, data augmentation for pose estimation/re-ID, and content creation. The approach is likely to be used or extended by others; demonstrating downstream benefits (e.g., improved re-ID or pose estimation using generated data) would further increase impact.

Questions for the authors:
- Residual blocks: What measurable improvements do they bring (SSIM/mask-SSIM/IS, convergence speed, stability)? Please include an ablation of with/without residual blocks.
- Fully connected layer in Stage-I: Does removing the FC layer reduce blur or artifacts? Given Stage-II removes FC to preserve detail, a clear ablation on Stage-I would clarify its effect.
- One-stage adversarial training: Your G1+D remains blurry. Is this due to the FC bottleneck, the masked L1 dominating, or discriminator conditioning? Could a fully convolutional G1+D reduce blur?
- How sensitive are results to λ in Stage-II (beyond the supplement)? Please show qualitative sweeps to illustrate the artifact/blurriness trade-off.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Related work: Add explicit discussion of iterative refinement/stacked architectures (Carreira et al. 2016; Newell et al. 2016) and how your two-stage design is similar/different.
- Evaluation: Complement SSIM/IS with FID (preferably masked-FID); report identity preservation (e.g., re-ID feature cosine similarity or retrieval performance) and pose accuracy (PCK between generated and target keypoints).
- User study: Replace the brief-exposure AMT with a more substantive 2AFC or Turing-style test with adequate viewing time, more participants, randomized pairs, inter-rater agreement, and statistical significance.
- Baselines: Include a strong image-to-image baseline (e.g., pix2pix conditioned on keypoint heatmaps) and a variant where Stage-II is trained end-to-end with Stage-I (joint fine-tuning).
- Failure analysis: Provide quantitative breakdowns by pose rarity, occlusion, and background complexity.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and practical problem setting with clear motivation.
- Simple, effective two-stage architecture with well-argued design choices.
- Strong qualitative results and informative ablations.
- Likely to stimulate follow-up work in controllable human synthesis.

Cons against acceptance:
- Quantitative evidence and human evaluation are relatively weak.
- Remaining artifacts and failure cases, especially on challenging backgrounds.
- Some architectural claims (residual blocks/FC impact) need more rigorous support.

Overall recommendation: Accept. The paper presents a timely, well-executed approach to pose-guided person generation with clear novelty and promising results. Strengthening the evaluation (metrics and user study) and clarifying specific architectural impacts would further improve the work but do not outweigh the contribution.
