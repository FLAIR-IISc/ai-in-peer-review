This paper proposes a novel Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG) to synthesize person images in arbitrary poses, based on an image of that person and a novel pose. The idea is interesting, and the writing is clear. The authors provide a thorough overview of the related work and clearly explain their approach, which is a two-stage process that first captures the global structure of a person and then fills in more appearance details via adversarial training.

However, the paper has several major weaknesses. Firstly, there is no quantitative evaluation of the generated images, such as user studies or metrics to measure how often generated images fool humans. This makes it difficult to assess the effectiveness of the proposed method. The visual quality of the generated images is also a concern, as the results are not visually appealing, and the figures are too small to evaluate the quality of the images. Furthermore, the generated images show many noticeable errors compared to the target images.

The authors' rebuttal did not satisfactorily address these concerns, and other reviewers echoed the quantitative and qualitative shortcomings. Therefore, I do not believe that this paper is ready for presentation at a top venue like NIPS.

Here is a list of arguments pro and con acceptance:

Pro:

* The idea of pose-guided person image generation is interesting and has potential applications.
* The writing is clear, and the authors provide a thorough overview of the related work.
* The proposed two-stage approach is a novel contribution to the field.

Con:

* Lack of quantitative evaluation of the generated images.
* Poor visual quality of the generated images.
* Noticeable errors in the generated images compared to the target images.
* Insufficient rebuttal to address the concerns raised by reviewers.

In terms of the NIPS criteria, I would rate the paper as follows:

* Quality: 5/10 (the paper is technically sound, but the claims are not well-supported by experimental results)
* Clarity: 8/10 (the writing is clear, but the figures are too small to evaluate the quality of the images)
* Originality: 6/10 (the idea is interesting, but the approach is not significantly different from previous contributions)
* Significance: 5/10 (the results are not impressive, and the paper does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way)

Overall, I would not recommend accepting this paper for presentation at NIPS.
