

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

This paper introduces a novel task of generating person images in arbitrary poses by combining appearance from an input image with a target pose (represented as heatmaps). The authors propose a two-stage network architecture: Stage-I integrates pose information with the input image to generate a coarse pose-aligned image, and Stage-II refines this result using adversarial training to produce sharper, more detailed images. This work builds upon recent advances in conditional image generation (e.g., [10, 17, 32]) but makes a significant contribution by explicitly conditioning on pose heatmaps rather than abstract attributes or labels.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology, experiments, and conclusions. The authors effectively relate their approach to prior work in pose estimation [2, 15, 36] and conditional image generation [17, 32, 36], though they could strengthen the related work section by including recent iterative refinement methods (e.g., Carreira et al. 2016; Newell et al. 2016) which share conceptual similarities with their coarse-to-fine approach.

**Originality**: The paper's core contribution is the novel task of pose-guided person image generation, which is well-motivated by applications in movie making and pose estimation data augmentation. The two-stage architecture is a clever solution to the challenging problem of simultaneously transferring appearance and pose, and it effectively divides the problem into manageable subtasks (global structure vs. detail refinement). The pose mask loss is also a novel technical contribution that addresses the challenge of background interference. The work is sufficiently distinct from previous NIPS papers, particularly from the related work on pose estimation and conditional generation.

**Quality**: The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with extensive ablation studies on pose embeddings, loss functions, and architecture choices. The results on both DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach. However, the quantitative evaluation is weak due to the use of metrics that don't correlate well with perceptual quality (SSIM, Inception Score). As noted in the paper, blurry images often achieve high SSIM scores but are less convincing than more detailed (but slightly lower SSIM) results. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide more perceptually meaningful metrics.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear explanations of the methodology, particularly the two-stage architecture and the pose mask loss. The figures effectively illustrate the key concepts, though some technical details (like the exact implementation of residual blocks) could be clarified. The authors should address the question of whether residual blocks improve performance in terms of specific metrics or qualitative effects (e.g., sharper edges, better pose consistency).

**Significance**: The proposed task has clear practical applications in entertainment, fashion, and computer vision. The approach advances the state of the art in pose transfer, as demonstrated by comparisons with Zhao et al. (2017) and qualitative improvements over single-stage models. However, the user study is preliminary and unconvincing (as noted in the rebuttal), and the paper would benefit from a more substantive human evaluation with larger sample sizes and better experimental design.

**Strengths**:
- Novel and well-motivated task of pose-guided person image generation
- Effective two-stage coarse-to-fine architecture that addresses a challenging problem
- Comprehensive ablation studies on design choices
- Clear writing and well-organized presentation
- Promising qualitative results with convincing details

**Weaknesses**:
- Quantitative evaluation relies on metrics that don't correlate with perceptual quality
- User study is preliminary and should be replaced with a more robust human evaluation
- Lack of clarity on how residual blocks specifically improve performance
- Blurring artifacts in Stage-I+GAN results remain unexplained (could be due to the intermediate fully connected layer)

**Recommendation**: I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field with a novel task and a clever architectural solution. The results are promising, and the paper is well-written. While there are weaknesses in the quantitative evaluation and user study, the core contribution is strong and the paper would be valuable to the NIPS community.

**Suggestions for Improvement**:
1. Replace the preliminary user study with a more substantial human evaluation
2. Clarify the specific benefits of residual blocks (provide quantitative metrics or qualitative examples)
3. Investigate the potential cause of blurring in Stage-I+GAN (particularly the role of the fully connected layer)
4. Consider adding the suggested iterative refinement works to the related work section
5. Include a suggested experiment: train Stage 2 with Stage 1 inputs adversarially to see if this reduces blurring

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of conditional image generation, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS 2017 proceedings. The authors have addressed the main challenges of pose transfer through a well-structured two-stage approach that produces compelling results, despite some remaining artifacts. The novelty of the task combined with the effective solution makes this a strong candidate for acceptance.
