This paper proposes the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), a novel approach to synthesizing person images given a reference image and a desired pose. The core idea is to leverage a two-stage architecture – pose integration followed by image refinement – to address the challenging task of pose transfer while maintaining identity. The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the problem, proposed solution, and experimental setup. The extensive ablation studies demonstrating the impact of different design choices are a significant strength.

The work builds upon the recent surge in generative image modeling, particularly GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014), and conditional image generation (Mirza & Osindero, 2014; Reed et al., 2016). While existing methods condition generation on attributes, text, or images, this paper distinguishes itself by explicitly incorporating pose information via keypoint heatmaps, providing a more direct and flexible control mechanism. The two-stage approach, inspired by prior work (e.g., Chen et al., 2017; Zhao et al., 2017) decomposing complex tasks, is a sensible strategy for tackling the complexities of pose-guided image generation.  The use of a masked L1 loss to focus on the human body during training is a clever addition.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The task of generating person images conditioned on both a reference image *and* a specified pose is a novel contribution. The explicit representation of pose via heatmaps and the two-stage generation framework are also novel.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed architecture appears technically sound, drawing upon established techniques like U-Nets (Ronneberger et al., 2015) and DCGANs (Radford et al., 2015) in a thoughtful manner. The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the individual components.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The diagrams and explanations are clear and concise.
* **Extensive Evaluation:** The authors perform comprehensive ablation studies investigating the impact of pose embedding methods, the mask loss, and the two-stage approach.  They evaluate their method on two datasets of differing resolutions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Image Artifacts:** While the generated images are generally realistic, noticeable artifacts are present in some results, particularly in the Market-1501 dataset. This suggests room for improvement in the refinement stage.
* **Quantitative Evaluation:** The quantitative evaluation, relying on SSIM (Wang et al., 2004) and Inception Score (Salimans et al., 2016), feels somewhat weak. SSIM can be misleading when assessing the perceptual quality of generated images, and the Inception Score doesn’t necessarily correlate with visual fidelity.  The authors acknowledge this limitation.
* **Related Work Gaps:**  The related work section could benefit from including more recent methods involving iterative refinement for pose estimation or image generation (e.g., Carreira et al., 2016; Newell et al., 2016).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a novel and challenging problem with a well-designed and technically sound approach.
* The extensive ablation studies demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method and provide valuable insights into the importance of different design choices.
* The clear writing and organization make the paper easy to understand and appreciate.
* The results are promising and demonstrate the potential of pose-guided person image generation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The presence of noticeable artifacts in the generated images.
* The limitations of the quantitative evaluation metrics.
* The minor gap in the related work section.



**Specific Questions/Suggestions:**

* The paper mentions that residual blocks improve generation performance. Could the authors provide more details on *how* this improvement manifests itself, either through quantitative metrics or qualitative observations?
*  It would be interesting to see an experiment where the Stage 2 generator is trained adversarially *with* the Stage 1 outputs as conditioning inputs, rather than using the intermediate output as a direct input. This could potentially address the blurring issue observed in the one-stage model.
*  The authors speculate that the fully connected layer in Stage 1 might contribute to blurring.  Investigating an architecture without this layer could be insightful.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential. The novelty of the task, the well-designed approach, and the extensive evaluation make it a valuable contribution to the field.  I recommend **acceptance** based on the strength of the technical approach and the potential impact of the work. The user study reported in the rebuttal does not change this recommendation, even if it is preliminary; the core technical contribution is compelling regardless.
