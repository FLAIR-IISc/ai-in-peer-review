This paper presents a method for synthesizing an image of a person in a novel pose, conditioned on a single source image of that person and a target pose represented by keypoints. The proposed framework, called the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), operates in two stages. The first stage uses a U-Net-like architecture to generate a coarse initial image that matches the target pose, integrating the source image and pose heatmaps. The second stage refines this coarse output using a conditional generative adversarial network (GAN) to add finer details and improve realism. The approach is related to the broader field of conditional image generation and image-to-image translation, particularly works like pix2pix, but focuses specifically on the challenging task of human pose manipulation.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper is the problem it addresses. Pose-guided person generation is an interesting and practically significant task with potential applications in areas like data augmentation for pose estimation, virtual try-on systems, and content creation for film and gaming. The authors' framing of the problem is clear, and the proposed two-stage, coarse-to-fine strategy is a logical and intuitive way to decompose this complex synthesis task. The paper is also well-written and easy to follow, clearly explaining the architecture and training procedure of the proposed model.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the interesting problem formulation, the work suffers from several major weaknesses, primarily concerning the quality of the results and the evaluation.

1.  **Visual Quality of Generated Images:** The paper claims to generate "high-quality" and "photo-realistic" results, but the visual evidence presented does not sufficiently support these claims. The generated images, while capturing the rough pose and color layout, exhibit numerous and noticeable artifacts. For example, in Figure 4, faces are often blurry and lack identity, clothing textures are smeared (e.g., the stripes on the t-shirt for ID 53 on Market-1501 become a blue blob), and fine details are consistently lost. The results are not visually appealing and appear far from real images. Furthermore, the figures in the paper are presented at a very small scale, which makes a thorough qualitative assessment difficult but also seems to hide some of the imperfections. The supplementary material suffers from the same issues. The work feels premature in its claims of achieving high-quality synthesis.

2.  **Quantitative Evaluation:** The quantitative evaluation is not convincing. The authors report Structural Similarity (SSIM) and Inception Score (IS). However, as the authors themselves note (and as is widely known in the generative modeling community), these metrics are poor proxies for perceptual quality. SSIM often favors blurry results over sharper but slightly imperfect ones, and IS can be manipulated and does not always correlate with human judgment of single-sample quality. The provided user study is a welcome addition, but the results are not strong enough to be conclusive. A fooling rate of 14.9% on the easier DeepFashion dataset suggests that the vast majority of generated images are still easily identified as fake by human observers. A more rigorous perceptual study would be needed to make strong claims about realism.

3.  **Originality:** The technical contribution of the paper is limited. The proposed method is largely a combination of well-established components: a U-Net architecture for the initial generation and a conditional DCGAN for refinement. This overall structure is reminiscent of other coarse-to-fine generative models and image-to-image translation frameworks. While the application to pose-guided person synthesis is novel, the underlying techniques do not represent a significant departure from prior work. The contribution is thus more application-focused than a fundamental advance in generative modeling.

**Summary of Arguments**

**Pro-Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an interesting and relevant problem in conditional image generation.
*   The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and the proposed two-stage method is easy to understand.
*   The approach shows some promise in transferring person appearance to a new pose.

**Con-Acceptance:**
*   The visual quality of the generated results is low, with many artifacts, and does not support the claims of being "high-quality" or "photo-realistic."
*   The quantitative evaluation is weak. The chosen metrics (SSIM, IS) are not well-suited for measuring perceptual quality, and the user study results indicate that the generated images are not very realistic.
*   The technical originality is limited, as the work primarily combines existing components (U-Net, DCGAN) for a new application.

**Conclusion**

In its current state, this paper feels like a promising but preliminary piece of work. The idea is compelling, but the execution and evaluation are not yet at the level required for a top-tier venue like NIPS. The gap between the claimed quality of the results and the visual evidence presented is too large. Before this work is ready for publication, the authors need to substantially improve the visual quality of the generated images and provide a more rigorous and convincing evaluation that truly demonstrates that the synthesized images are realistic and preserve identity. Therefore, I cannot recommend acceptance at this time.
