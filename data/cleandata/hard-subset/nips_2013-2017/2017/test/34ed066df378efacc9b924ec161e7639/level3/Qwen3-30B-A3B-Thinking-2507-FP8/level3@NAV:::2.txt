

This paper proposes a two-stage generative approach for pose-guided person image generation, where a coarse U-Net first integrates appearance and pose information to produce a low-resolution image, followed by a refinement stage that predicts a residual map to generate high-resolution outputs. The authors introduce a pose mask loss to focus on human body regions and use a difference map approach in the refinement stage to speed up training and improve detail recovery.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate experimental validation on DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets. The coarse-to-fine strategy is logically sound for pose transfer, and the difference map approach offers a useful refinement mechanism that appears to converge faster than standard GAN training. The authors properly address the challenges of background interference and pose transfer through their pose mask loss and two-stage architecture.

However, the image quality still contains visible artifacts (e.g., black dots in ID346) and fails to recover high-frequency details, significantly limiting practical applicability. The authors acknowledge these limitations, but the paper doesn't sufficiently address why this specific approach is superior to more sophisticated single-stage alternatives. While the task of conditioning on both appearance and pose is novel, the stacked architecture with difference map is conceptually similar to StackGAN [29] and other stacked GAN approaches, and the paper doesn't clearly differentiate from these prior works.

The technical contribution represents a useful incremental improvement rather than a fundamental advance. The significance is limited by the quality issues and the fact that the problem could be reasonably addressed with more sophisticated single-stage models. The experiments demonstrate improved results over a one-stage baseline, but the quality still lags behind state-of-the-art image generation methods.

In summary, the paper has good clarity and a reasonable technical approach, but its novelty is constrained by prior work, and its significance is limited by the remaining quality issues. The contribution is acceptable as a solid but incremental advance to the field. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the quality limitations in future work.
