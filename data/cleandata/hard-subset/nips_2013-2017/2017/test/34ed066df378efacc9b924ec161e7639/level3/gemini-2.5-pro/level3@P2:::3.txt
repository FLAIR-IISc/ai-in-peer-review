**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG²), a two-stage generative model for synthesizing an image of a person in a novel target pose, conditioned on a single source image of that person. The method leverages a two-stage pipeline to tackle this challenging task. The first stage employs a U-Net-like architecture to generate a coarse image that matches the target pose, integrating the source image and a heatmap representation of the target pose. This stage is trained with a novel "pose mask L1 loss" designed to focus learning on the person's body rather than the background. The second stage refines the coarse output from the first stage using a conditional GAN, where a second U-Net-like generator learns to add finer details and textures, and a discriminator is trained to distinguish between real image pairs and generated pairs. The authors demonstrate their approach on the DeepFashion and Market-1501 datasets, showing its ability to transfer a person's appearance to a new pose. This work builds upon the extensive literature on conditional generative models, particularly GANs like DCGAN and image-to-image translation frameworks, but applies them to the novel task of explicit, keypoint-guided full-body pose manipulation.

**Strengths**

1.  **Interesting and Significant Problem:** The task of manipulating a person's pose in an image is a challenging problem with significant potential applications in areas like data augmentation for pose estimation or person re-identification, virtual clothes try-on, and content creation for media. The proposed framework directly addresses this interesting problem.
2.  **Sound Methodological Approach:** The two-stage "coarse-to-fine" strategy is a logical and well-motivated design choice. Decomposing the problem into first establishing the global structure (pose) and then refining local appearance details is a sensible way to manage the complexity of the task. The architecture, combining U-Nets and a conditional GAN, is appropriate for this type of image synthesis problem.
3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The method is explained in sufficient detail, and the motivations behind the design choices (e.g., the pose mask loss, the two-stage pipeline) are well-articulated.
4.  **Ablation Studies:** The paper includes useful ablation experiments that validate specific design choices, such as the effectiveness of the proposed pose heatmap embedding over alternative coordinate-based methods, and the benefit of the pose mask loss over a standard L1 loss.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Discrepancy Between Claims and Visual Results:** The primary weakness of this paper is the significant gap between the claims of generating "high-quality," "photo-realistic" images with "convincing details" and the actual visual results presented. The images in Figure 4 and the supplementary material exhibit numerous and noticeable artifacts, including blurry textures, distorted faces, loss of clothing patterns (e.g., the stripes on the T-shirt for ID 53), and strange anatomical structures. While the model successfully captures the rough pose and color layout, the results are far from photo-realistic and would not be mistaken for real images. The figures are also quite small, which makes a detailed inspection difficult.
2.  **Unconvincing Quantitative Evaluation:** The quantitative results do not adequately support the paper's main claims.
    *   The authors rightly point out that SSIM can be misleading, often favoring blurry images, which undermines its value as a primary metric for realism.
    *   The results from the user study are particularly weak. On the DeepFashion dataset, generated images were mistaken for real ones only 14.9% of the time, and this number drops to a mere 7.4% for the more challenging Market-1501 dataset. This strongly suggests that the generated images are easily identifiable as fake by human observers, directly contradicting the "photo-realistic" claims.
3.  **Limited Originality:** The technical contributions of the paper are incremental. The method is primarily a novel application of existing and well-established components: U-Nets with skip connections and conditional GANs trained with a combination of L1 and adversarial loss are standard in the field of image-to-image translation. While the composition of these elements is tailored to the problem, the work does not introduce fundamentally new techniques in generative modeling.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a novel and challenging problem in generative modeling with clear practical relevance.
*   The proposed two-stage framework is intuitive and the paper is well-written and easy to understand.
*   The ablation studies provide useful insights into what works for this specific task.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The visual quality of the generated images is low and does not support the paper's claims of producing "high-quality" or "photo-realistic" results.
*   The quantitative evaluation, especially the user study, confirms that the generated images are not realistic.
*   The technical novelty is limited, as the approach mainly combines existing components. The work feels promising but premature for a top-tier venue like NIPS.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this paper presents a promising direction for person image generation. However, the work feels premature in its current state. The claims of generating high-quality, photo-realistic images are strongly overstated and not supported by the visual or quantitative evidence provided. While the idea is interesting, the execution and results need significant improvement before this work is ready for publication at a premier conference like NIPS.
