This paper proposes a novel task of Pose Guided Person Image Generation, which aims to synthesize person images in arbitrary poses based on an input image and a target pose. The authors introduce a two-stage coarse-to-fine network architecture to address this task. The paper is well-written, and the authors provide an extensive evaluation of their design decisions.

The proposed Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) consists of two stages: pose integration and image refinement. The first stage uses a U-Net-like network to integrate the target pose with the person image, generating an initial coarse result that captures the global structure of the human body. The second stage refines the initial result using a variant of Deep Convolutional GAN (DCGAN) model, which learns to fill in more appearance details via adversarial training.

The authors evaluate their method on two person datasets: Market-1501 and DeepFashion. The results show that the proposed method can generate high-quality person images with convincing details. The authors also compare their method with the most related work, VariGAN, and demonstrate the advantage of their approach.

The strengths of this paper include:

* The authors propose a novel task of Pose Guided Person Image Generation, which has many potential applications.
* The two-stage coarse-to-fine network architecture is well-designed and effective in addressing the task.
* The authors provide an extensive evaluation of their design decisions, including different pose embeddings, losses, and network architectures.
* The results are promising, and the authors demonstrate the advantage of their approach over the most related work.

However, there are also some weaknesses:

* The quantitative evaluation is weak, partly due to the lack of suitable metrics. The authors use Structural Similarity (SSIM) and the Inception Score (IS) to measure the quality of synthesis, but these metrics may not fully capture the quality of the generated images.
* The user study is preliminary and unconvincing. The authors show that their generated images can confuse users, but the study is not well-designed, and the results are not conclusive.
* The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how residual blocks improve the generation performance. They mention that using residual blocks as basic components improves the generation performance, but they do not provide any metrics or qualitative effects to support this claim.
* The authors do not discuss the potential failure modes of their approach. For example, what if the input image is of poor quality or the target pose is not well-defined?

In terms of related work, the authors mention several papers that are relevant to their approach, including VariGAN, which is the most related work. However, they do not discuss other relevant papers, such as those on iterative refinement, which could be useful in improving their approach.

In conclusion, this paper proposes a novel task of Pose Guided Person Image Generation and introduces a two-stage coarse-to-fine network architecture to address this task. The results are promising, but the quantitative evaluation is weak, and the user study is preliminary and unconvincing. The authors should provide more evidence to support their claims and discuss the potential failure modes of their approach.

Recommendations:

* The authors should provide more evidence to support their claims, including metrics and qualitative effects.
* The authors should discuss the potential failure modes of their approach and provide examples of failure cases.
