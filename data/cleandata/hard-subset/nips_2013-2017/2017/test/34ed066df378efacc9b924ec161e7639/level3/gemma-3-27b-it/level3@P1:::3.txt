## Review of Pose Guided Person Image Generation

This paper introduces a Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) for synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses given a reference image and a target pose. The approach utilizes a two-stage GAN-based framework – a U-Net for initial pose integration and a DCGAN for refinement – to address the challenges of simultaneously generating correct pose and detailed appearance. The authors highlight the novelty of conditioning image generation on both reference image *and* pose, drawing parallels to existing work in image generation (VAE, GANs, autoregressive models) and conditional image generation (attribute/text/image conditioning). 

The paper’s strengths lie in its clear articulation of the problem and the overall framework. The decomposition into two stages—global structure followed by detail refinement—is a sensible approach. The use of pose heatmaps for encoding pose information and the introduction of a pose mask loss to focus on body appearance are both reasonable design choices. The writing is clear and well-organized, enabling a good understanding of the proposed method.  The connection to existing methods like [35, 9, 3, 19] for divide-and-conquer strategies is appropriately cited.

However, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses, primarily in terms of evaluation and visual quality. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of L1 loss and utilize an adversarial loss for refinement, the resulting images, even in the qualitative comparisons, often exhibit noticeable artifacts and blurriness. The figures provided are small and make detailed assessment difficult. More importantly, the paper lacks robust quantitative evaluation. Despite acknowledging the importance of demonstrating the quality of the generated images, the authors rely solely on SSIM and Inception Score, both of which are known to have limitations in accurately reflecting perceptual quality, especially in generative modeling. Crucially, there is *no* user study performed to assess whether the generated images are convincingly realistic, a vital component for a work focused on visual fidelity. 

The comparison to related work [36] is helpful, but the presented example highlights the limitations of their system in generating high-quality, detailed images. The authors’ acknowledgement of failure cases in the conclusions (e.g., incomplete legs, inaccurate gender) further underscores the current limitations of the approach. While the two datasets used – Market-1501 and DeepFashion – provide some diversity in image resolution and complexity, the lack of a more comprehensive benchmark, or comparison to state-of-the-art image generation models, weakens the claim of significant advancement.  

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The idea of pose-guided person image generation is potentially useful and has applications in areas like movie making and data augmentation for pose estimation.
*   The two-stage framework with pose integration and refinement is a logical and well-motivated approach.
*   The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized, making it easy to understand the proposed method.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Lack of compelling quantitative evaluation beyond standard metrics with known limitations. Absence of a user study.
*   Visual quality of generated images is often subpar, with noticeable artifacts and blurriness.  Figures are too small for detailed analysis.
*   The improvement over existing methods is not convincingly demonstrated, particularly given the lack of strong quantitative results.
*   The claim of “high-quality person images with convincing details” is not adequately supported by the presented evidence.



**Overall Assessment:**

I find the paper to be a promising initial exploration of pose-guided person image generation, but it falls short of the standards expected for acceptance at NIPS. The lack of rigorous evaluation, combined with concerns about visual quality, makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of this work. While the idea is interesting and the writing is clear, the paper requires substantial improvement in both experimental evaluation and image generation quality before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field. 

**Recommendation:** Reject.
