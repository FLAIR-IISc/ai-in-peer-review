This paper tackles pose-guided person image synthesis: given a source image and a target skeleton/keypoint configuration, the system generates a novel-view image of the same person. The architecture is a largely standard two-stage pipeline: a U-Net-like network integrates the target pose to produce a coarse image, followed by an adversarial U-Net-style refinement stage. Masked L1 losses and a difference-map formulation are employed to encourage body-focused updates and sharper details. The problem is timely and in scope for NeurIPS, relating to conditional GANs and image-to-image translation (e.g., Goodfellow et al.; Isola et al.), multi-view/pose-conditioned generation (e.g., Zhao et al.; Lassner et al.), and coarse-to-fine GANs.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and presentation; the paper is easy to follow.
- The application—controllable human pose manipulation from a single image—is interesting and potentially useful (e.g., data augmentation, content creation).
- Some generated results look plausible, indicating the approach can align appearance with target skeletons.

Weaknesses
- Quality: Claims of “high-quality” results are not convincingly supported. There is no rigorous quantitative evaluation: no human fooling rate/user study, and no modern perceptual or distributional metrics (e.g., FID, LPIPS). There is also no assessment of pose accuracy (e.g., PCK on generated images), identity preservation (e.g., similarity in a re-ID embedding), or downstream utility (e.g., retrieval gains from augmentation).
- Visual quality: Many artifacts and mismatches to target keypoints remain; textures often smear, hands/faces deform, and backgrounds bleed. Figures are small, making it hard to assess fidelity; supplementary suffers the same issue.
- Originality: The components (U-Nets, DCGAN, masked L1, coarse-to-fine) are well-known; the contribution is mainly an application-driven combination rather than a conceptual advance over prior conditional generation frameworks.
- Significance: Without strong quantitative evidence or state-of-the-art comparisons (e.g., to pix2pix/appearance-flow/pose-transfer baselines), it is premature to claim near–real-image quality or clear SOTA.

Suggestions
- Provide robust evaluation: FID/LPIPS; user studies with fooling rates and A/B tests; pose consistency (PCK), identity similarity (re-ID embedding distance), and failure mode analysis by pose difficulty/occlusion.
- Include stronger baselines and ablations (single-stage vs. two-stage, with/without difference map).
- Improve figure size/legibility; report runtime and training stability.

After rebuttal: The authors did not adequately address the lack of quantitative and qualitative rigor; other reviewers shared these concerns. While promising, the work is not ready for a top venue.

Recommendation: Reject. Prospective future acceptance would require solid quantitative evaluation and clearer advances beyond standard components.
