

This paper proposes a two-stage GAN-based framework for synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses given a reference image and target pose. The authors divide the challenging pose transfer task into pose integration (stage-I) and image refinement (stage-II), using U-Net architectures for both stages with a novel pose mask loss to focus generation on the human body rather than background. The approach builds on existing GAN and U-Net components but applies them to a novel application domain. The work relates to previous NIPS contributions in conditional image generation (e.g., [22, 23, 24, 32]) and pose estimation (e.g., [2, 35]), but the specific application of pose-guided person image synthesis represents a novel direction.

The paper demonstrates the method on two datasets (Market-1501 and DeepFashion) with qualitative results showing some reasonable pose transfer and basic appearance consistency. The writing is clear, with well-organized sections explaining the two-stage approach, pose embedding, and mask loss. The pose mask loss is a thoughtful contribution that addresses a specific challenge in pose transfer (background interference) and shows some improvement in qualitative results.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses that prevent it from meeting NIPS standards. Most critically, the authors provide no quantitative evaluation of the quality of the generated images. While they report SSIM and Inception Score metrics, these are standard metrics for general image quality that do not directly measure how realistic or convincing the generated person images are. The paper lacks user studies to assess whether generated images can fool human observers, which is a standard evaluation for image synthesis work (e.g., [10, 15, 26]). The user study presented in Table 2 is insufficient, as it reports only a small percentage of generated images being misclassified as real (9.3-14.9%), which is significantly lower than state-of-the-art image synthesis methods. This suggests the images are not convincing to human observers.

The visual quality of the results is also problematic. Figure 4 shows generated images with numerous artifacts: incorrect body parts, color mismatches, blurry details, and background inconsistencies. For example, in the Market-1501 samples, the two-stage model (G1+G2+D) sometimes generates "blue blobs" as clothing instead of detailed textures. The qualitative results are not visually appealing, and the figures are too small to assess details properly. The supplementary material does not address these visual quality issues.

The paper's claims about generating "high-quality person images with convincing details" are not supported by the evidence. The authors acknowledge in the supplementary material that their method performs worse than the one-stage model on Market-1501, and the results show many noticeable errors compared to targets. The claim that their method "generates photo-realistic results" is exaggerated given the visual evidence.

In terms of originality, the paper's main contribution is application-focused rather than methodological. The use of pose heatmaps and two-stage architecture builds on existing components (U-Net, GANs) without introducing novel techniques. The pose mask loss is a minor modification to existing loss functions and does not represent a significant theoretical advance. The work would be more impactful if it introduced new modeling approaches rather than applying existing techniques to a new application.

The significance of the work is limited by the lack of convincing results. While pose-guided person image generation has potential applications in fashion and movie making, the current results do not demonstrate sufficient quality to be useful in these domains. The method's limitations (e.g., struggling with complex poses, gender misidentification) suggest it's not ready for practical use.

Quality: The paper is technically sound in its implementation but lacks sufficient evaluation to support its claims. The absence of human evaluation metrics and the poor visual quality of results undermine the quality assessment.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to understand the approach.

Originality: The application is novel, but the technical approach is largely based on existing methods without substantial innovation.

Significance: The significance is limited by the poor quality of results, which do not demonstrate the promised "high-quality" or "convincing" images.

Pro-acceptance arguments: The idea is interesting, the writing is clear, and the two-stage approach is a reasonable strategy for pose transfer.

Con-acceptance arguments: Lack of convincing quantitative evaluation, poor visual quality of results with numerous artifacts, exaggerated claims about image quality, and insufficient comparison to relevant work (e.g., [36] is only compared visually without metrics).

The paper's claims are not supported by the evidence provided. The results do not demonstrate high-quality, photo-realistic person images as claimed. The authors acknowledge the limitations in their results but do not sufficiently address them or provide evidence that their method produces results that would be useful in practice. After the rebuttal, the authors did not adequately address the concerns about the quality of results or the lack of quantitative evaluation.

Given the conference's standards for quality, the paper should demonstrate convincing results that meet the bar for NIPS. The current results, as presented, do not meet this standard. The work is promising but premature for presentation at NIPS. The authors would need to substantially improve the quality of results, provide rigorous quantitative evaluation (especially human evaluation), and better support their claims about image quality before this work would be suitable for NIPS. I recommend rejection.
