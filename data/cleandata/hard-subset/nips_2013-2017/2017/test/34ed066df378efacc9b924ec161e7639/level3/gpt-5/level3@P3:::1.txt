Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles a novel conditional generation task: synthesize an image of a specific person under a target pose given a single reference image. The method, PG2, explicitly encodes pose via keypoint heatmaps and uses a two-stage, coarse-to-fine architecture. Stage I (G1) integrates the reference image and target pose with a U-Net-like generator (with residual blocks and a bottleneck fully connected layer) trained with a masked L1 loss to emphasize the human foreground. Stage II (G2) refines the coarse output adversarially by predicting a difference map conditioned on the coarse output and the reference image; a pairwise conditional discriminator judges (generated, reference) versus (real, reference). Experiments on Market-1501 (128×64) and DeepFashion (256×256) show visually convincing pose transfer with remaining artifacts. The paper positions itself among conditional generation (CVAE/GANs, pix2pix) and pose-aware generation, and compares to the closest multi-view synthesis baseline.

The two-stage design resonates with iterative refinement ideas well known in pose estimation (Carreira et al. 2016; Newell et al. 2016). The explicit pose conditioning via heatmaps is in line with image-to-image translation and structured conditioning trends, while the difference-map refinement and masked losses are pragmatic innovations.

Strengths
- Novel and useful task definition (pose-guided identity-preserving person generation) with clear applications.
- Well-motivated, clean two-stage coarse-to-fine architecture; insightful design choices (pose heatmaps, masked L1, difference-map refinement, pairwise discriminator).
- Thorough ablations on pose embedding strategies, loss variants, and one-stage vs two-stage training.
- Generally clear, well-written manuscript; figures effectively convey qualitative gains.
- Promising qualitative results across both low- and higher-resolution datasets.

Weaknesses
- Quantitative evaluation is limited: SSIM/IS (even masked) are poorly aligned with perceptual quality and identity/pose fidelity; user study is preliminary and not convincing.
- Some generated images contain noticeable artifacts (ghost limbs, texture smearing, background leakage), especially on cluttered Market-1501 scenes.
- Certain claims (e.g., benefit of residual blocks; role of FC in G1) are not quantified.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to pose-estimation noise and of identity preservation.

Quality
Technically sound and carefully engineered. The coarse-to-fine decomposition, masked loss, and pairwise conditioning are appropriate and well justified. However, stronger, task-aligned quantitative measures and more diagnostic ablations would better support claims about specific components and failure modes.

Clarity
Very clear and well organized. The architecture and losses are described in enough detail to reproduce. Please add more explicit comparisons to iterative refinement literature and clarify ablation metrics (see questions).

Originality
The task and the explicit combination of identity image with target pose heatmaps are novel. The two-stage approach and difference-map refinement are reasonable extensions of existing GAN practices but combined here in a new way for person pose transfer.

Significance
Likely to influence subsequent work on controllable human image synthesis, data augmentation for pose and re-ID, and film/media applications. Despite imperfect metrics, the qualitative impact is evident.

Questions for the authors
1) Residual blocks: You state they improve generation performance. By how much and under which metrics? Please report quantitative (e.g., SSIM/mask-SSIM/FID) and qualitative gains, and show visuals isolating the effect.
2) Fully connected layer in G1: Stage II removes the FC layer to preserve details. Could the blurring observed for G1+GAN be due to this FC bottleneck? Please provide an ablation of G1 with and without the FC layer, both with L1 and with adversarial training.
3) Difference map vs direct prediction: How much does predicting a difference map matter? Please compare G2 trained to output full images vs differences, under equal training budgets.
4) Sensitivity to pose estimation: How robust is PG2 to noisy or missing keypoints? Provide a study with corrupted/ablated keypoints and, if possible, an upper bound using ground-truth annotations.
5) Identity preservation: Do you have any quantitative proxy (e.g., re-ID matching score when using generated images as queries/galley, face recognition similarity on visible faces)?

Suggested experiments/analyses
- Strengthen metrics: In addition to SSIM/IS, report FID (preferably on person crops or with pose masks) and a re-ID based identity consistency metric (rank-1/mAP when mixing generated and real images). Also consider keypoint PCK on generated images to assess pose fidelity.
- Human evaluation: Replace the brief 1-second AMT test with a more substantive study: pairwise A/B with unlimited viewing, Likert ratings for pose faithfulness, identity preservation, realism, and background plausibility; report inter-rater agreement.
- Joint/iterative training: Explore end-to-end fine-tuning of G1+G2+D and iterative refinement (multiple G2 steps), akin to Carreira et al. (IEF) and Newell et al. (stacked hourglass), with intermediate supervision on coarse outputs.
- Adversarial training to reduce blurring: Train Stage II more aggressively against Stage I inputs (e.g., lower λ, multi-scale discriminators, or feature matching/perceptual losses) and test whether this reduces residual blur; alternatively, adversarially train a FC-free variant of G1 to assess the FC’s role in blur.
- Background handling: Evaluate a background inpainting or segmentation-guided variant to reduce background artifacts and leakage from the condition image.

Related work
Please expand the related work to explicitly discuss iterative refinement frameworks: Carreira et al., “Human Pose Estimation with Iterative Error Feedback” (CVPR’16) and Newell et al., “Stacked Hourglass Networks” (ECCV’16), and relate your two-stage refinement to these paradigms. Also discuss pix2pix-style conditioning in the context of pose heatmaps.

Pros
- Novel task with clear utility.
- Effective, simple, and extensible architecture; solid ablations.
- Strong qualitative results, especially on DeepFashion.
- Clear writing and reproducible methodology.

Cons
- Weak quantitative evaluation and preliminary user study.
- Visible artifacts in challenging cases.
- Limited component-wise quantification (residual blocks, FC bottleneck, difference-map choice).

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a timely and influential problem formulation and a practical, well-argued approach. Despite limitations in metrics and some artifacts, the contribution is novel and likely to spur follow-up work. Please address the questions, strengthen quantitative and human evaluations, and expand the related work discussion as noted.

Rebuttal note
The attempted user study is too limited to be persuasive. I encourage replacing it with a substantive human evaluation (pairwise preferences and attribute-specific ratings), and adding task-relevant metrics (FID on person crops, re-ID identity preservation). My overall positive recommendation stands given the novelty and approach.
