The paper "Pose Guided Person Image Generation" proposes a novel task of generating person images conditioned on a reference image and a target pose. The authors present a two-stage approach to address this task, using a variant of U-Net-like architecture for pose integration and a variant of conditional DCGAN for image refinement. The method is evaluated on two person datasets, Market-1501 and DeepFashion, and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

The paper builds upon existing work on generative image modeling with deep learning techniques, specifically using GANs and U-Nets. The authors' contribution lies in applying these techniques to a specific task of pose-guided person image generation, which is an interesting and practical application. The writing is clear, and the generated images look somewhat realistic in places.

However, there are several major concerns with the paper. Firstly, the lack of quantitative evaluation is a significant limitation. The authors rely solely on qualitative results, which are not sufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach. A user study or metrics to measure how often generated images fool humans would be essential to validate the results.

Furthermore, the visual quality of the generated images is not visually appealing, with many noticeable errors versus the target images. The figures are also too small, making it difficult to assess the quality of the generated images. The supplementary results suffer from the same issues.

Given these concerns, it is premature to claim high-quality or real-image-level results. The work is promising, but it requires further development and evaluation to demonstrate its effectiveness.

After the rebuttal, the reviewer remains unconvinced, and other reviewers echo the quantitative and qualitative shortcomings. The authors' rebuttal did not satisfactorily address these concerns.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting application of GANs and U-Nets, it falls short in terms of quantitative evaluation and visual quality. Therefore, it is not ready for presentation at a top venue like NIPS.

Strengths:

* Interesting application of GANs and U-Nets to pose-guided person image generation
* Clear writing and somewhat realistic generated images in places

Weaknesses:

* Lack of quantitative evaluation
* Poor visual quality of generated images
* Figures are too small
* Supplementary results suffer from the same issues

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel task and application of GANs and U-Nets
* The approach is well-motivated and has potential for practical applications

Arguments against acceptance:

* The lack of quantitative evaluation is a significant limitation
* The visual quality of the generated images is not sufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach
* The paper requires further development and evaluation to demonstrate its effectiveness

Recommendation:

* The paper is not ready for presentation at a top venue like NIPS
* The authors should address the quantitative and qualitative shortcomings and resubmit the paper to a more suitable venue.
