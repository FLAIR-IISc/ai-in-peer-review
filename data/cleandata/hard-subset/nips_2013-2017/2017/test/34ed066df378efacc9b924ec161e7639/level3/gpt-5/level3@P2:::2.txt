Summary
The paper proposes PG2, a pose-guided person image generation framework that synthesizes an image of a person in a target pose given a reference image of the same person. The method is two-stage: (i) a U-Net-like generator G1 integrates appearance and pose to produce a coarse, pose-correct image using a masked L1 loss that downweights background; (ii) a second U-Net-like generator G2 refines the result adversarially by predicting a difference (residual) map between the coarse output and the target, with a pairwise conditional discriminator D that takes (generated image, condition image) versus (real target, condition image). Poses are represented as 18 keypoint heatmaps. The authors evaluate on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64), conduct ablations on pose embedding, the mask loss, and one-stage vs. two-stage training, and report SSIM/IS and masked variants, plus an AMT user study. Qualitatively, the two-stage approach improves sharpness and preserves more details than a one-stage adversarial baseline.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on conditional generative models (VAEs, autoregressive models, GANs) and on conditional image generation (e.g., Mirza & Osindero; pix2pix-like architectures), stacked/Coarse-to-fine GANs (StackGAN, Stacked GANs), and keypoint/structure-conditioned generation (Reed et al.: “Learning what and where to draw,” “Generating interpretable images with controllable structure”). Compared to multi-view generation for clothing (e.g., VariGAN), this paper explicitly conditions on 2D pose heatmaps and a reference image, providing finer control over articulated structure. Novel aspects include the use of a foreground-weighted mask in the L1 term, a difference-map generator at stage II, and a pairwise discriminator that conditions on the reference image to discourage trivial copying.

Quality
- Soundness: The two-stage design is sensible and well motivated: G1 solves global structure and color; G2 focuses on high-frequency details via adversarial refinement. The masked L1 addresses background mismatch. The pairwise discriminator design is appropriate for conditioning on IA. The difference-map formulation is a reasonable way to stabilize/refocus training.
- Empirical support: The paper provides ablations on pose embedding (coordinates vs. heatmap encoders vs. direct heatmap concatenation), mask loss versus plain L1, and one-stage vs. two-stage training. Results on both datasets show consistent improvements, and the user study supports perceptual gains.
- Limitations: Despite improvements, generated images still exhibit visible artifacts and fail to recover fine details reliably (e.g., speckle/black dots and texture loss in challenging poses such as ID 346). Background handling remains brittle, especially on Market-1501. The paper does not quantitatively assess identity preservation (which is central to the application), e.g., via re-ID retrieval consistency or a face/body identity classifier. Claims about faster convergence of the difference-map approach are not substantiated with training curves or timing. Comparisons to strong image-to-image baselines (e.g., a pix2pix-style single-stage conditioned on IA and pose heatmaps) are not fully explored beyond the G1+D variant.
- Reproducibility: Architecture and training details are described reasonably well; some choices (e.g., no batch norm/dropout) are stated but not fully justified. The mask construction is outlined but would benefit from exact morphological parameters. Sensitivity to the λ trade-off is deferred to the supplement.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The task setup, architecture diagrams, and ablations are easy to follow. Figures effectively illustrate qualitative differences across variants. Minor suggestions: explicitly define the mask generation pipeline and λ sensitivity in the main text; clarify why removing fully connected layers in G2 is beneficial beyond intuition; discuss failure cases more systematically; and improve figure readability for low-resolution examples.

Originality
Moderate. Coarse-to-fine stacking and conditional adversarial training are known. Keypoint heatmaps as pose input are established in prior work. The main originality lies in combining (i) explicit pose-heatmap conditioning with (ii) a person-specific appearance reference, (iii) a foreground-weighted L1 mask tailored to pose transfer, and (iv) an adversarial refinement that predicts a difference map with a pairwise discriminator. While each piece has antecedents, the integration for pose-guided person generation is novel and practically useful.

Significance
The problem is relevant to controllable generation, data augmentation, and graphics applications. The method advances the visual plausibility of pose-conditioned synthesis compared to prior multi-view and structure-conditioned baselines, especially on DeepFashion. However, the broader impact is tempered by remaining artifacts, sensitivity to background, and the absence of identity preservation metrics. As presented, this is a solid step with clear empirical gains but may be viewed as an incremental variant of conditional stacked GANs by some readers.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear problem formulation and a coherent, well-motivated two-stage design.
- Effective pose conditioning via heatmaps; foreground mask loss reduces background leakage.
- Difference-map refinement with pairwise discriminator is a practical and useful idea.
- Thorough ablations and user study; results on two datasets with diverse conditions.
- Paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Visible artifacts and loss of high-frequency details remain; some examples contain speckle/black dots and texture collapse.
- Limited quantitative evaluation of identity preservation, which is central to the task’s utility.
- Incremental with respect to stacked/conditional GAN literature; prior coarse-to-fine and keypoint-conditioned approaches exist.
- Background mismatch remains a notable failure mode, especially on Market-1501.
- Missing comparisons to strong generic image-to-image baselines beyond the one-stage variant, and lacking convergence/runtime evidence for the difference-map claim.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please evaluate identity preservation quantitatively (e.g., re-ID retrieval consistency or a classifier-based identity metric), and report a retrieval-based metric on Market-1501.
- Include a baseline using a pix2pix-style model conditioned on IA and pose heatmaps (one-stage adversarial + L1) with tuned hyperparameters.
- Provide training curves or epoch counts to substantiate convergence speed claims for the difference-map approach, and report runtime/parameters.
- Elaborate the foreground mask construction and its sensitivity; report results versus λ in the main paper.
- Analyze robustness to pose estimator errors and to occlusions; could temporal smoothing or uncertainty in keypoints help?
- Consider perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) and FID for a more faithful alignment with human judgments.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a well-executed, practically useful approach to pose-guided person generation, with solid ablations and clear improvements, despite remaining artifacts and some incremental aspects.
