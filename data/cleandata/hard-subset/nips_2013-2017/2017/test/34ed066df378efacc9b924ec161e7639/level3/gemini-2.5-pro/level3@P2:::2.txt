This paper introduces a novel task: pose-guided person image generation. The goal is to synthesize a new image of a person from a source image, but in a new, specified target pose. The authors propose the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG²), a two-stage generative model to tackle this problem. The first stage consists of a U-Net-like generator (G1) that takes the source image and a heatmap representation of the target pose as input to produce a coarse image that captures the overall structure and color. This stage is trained with a novel "pose mask L1 loss" to focus learning on the person's body rather than the background. The second stage refines this coarse output using another U-Net-like generator (G2), which learns to predict a residual (difference map) between the coarse output and the ground truth. This refinement stage is trained adversarially with a discriminator that distinguishes between real and generated image pairs. This coarse-to-fine strategy is related to prior work in stacked generative models like StackGAN and other image-to-image translation methods, but the application to explicit pose conditioning for person generation, along with specific design choices like the difference map refinement, is new.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Task:** The paper formalizes and provides a strong baseline for the challenging and valuable problem of pose-guided person synthesis. This task offers more explicit control than previous view-synthesis works and has clear potential applications in e-commerce (virtual try-on), entertainment (character animation), and as a form of data augmentation for tasks like pose estimation and person re-identification.

2.  **Sound and Well-Motivated Method:** The proposed two-stage, coarse-to-fine approach is a logical and effective way to decompose this complex problem. Stage 1 focuses on the difficult global rearrangement of pixels according to the new pose, while Stage 2 focuses on adding local, high-frequency details. The idea of the second generator predicting a difference map is a clever refinement technique that helps stabilize training by focusing on correcting errors from the first stage.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very clearly written and well-structured. The method is explained in sufficient detail, and Figure 2 provides an excellent architectural overview. The motivation behind each design choice, such as the pose heatmap embedding and the masked loss, is well-justified.

4.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments on two distinct datasets (DeepFashion and Market-1501). The ablation studies systematically validate the key components of the model, demonstrating the superiority of the proposed pose embedding, the benefit of the pose mask loss, and the advantage of the two-stage model over a single-stage one. The inclusion of quantitative metrics, qualitative results, and a user study provides a well-rounded evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Quality of Generated Images:** The primary weakness of the work is the visual quality of the final generated images. While the model successfully transfers the pose and general appearance, the outputs suffer from noticeable artifacts and a lack of high-frequency detail. For instance, in Figure 4, the textures on clothing are often blurred (ID 53), and strange artifacts appear (e.g., black dots on the shirt of ID 346). This suggests that the current model struggles to preserve or synthesize fine details, which may limit its immediate practical applicability for the use cases mentioned in the introduction.

2.  **Limited Originality of the Core Framework:** While the task is novel, the architectural framework of using a stacked, coarse-to-fine GAN is an established concept (e.g., StackGAN, Stacked GANs by Huang et al., which are cited). The contribution is more in the successful adaptation and combination of these ideas for a new problem, rather than a fundamentally new generative modeling technique. The significance could be viewed as somewhat incremental from a purely methodological perspective on GANs.

3.  **Failure Cases:** The paper shows failure cases in Figure 6, attributing them to rare poses and data imbalance. This is an honest assessment, but it highlights significant limitations. The model seems to struggle with complex clothing (e.g., jackets over shirts) and highly non-standard poses, which are common in real-world scenarios.

**Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   To improve the sharpness and detail of the generated images, the authors could consider incorporating a perceptual loss (e.g., VGG loss) in addition to the L1 and adversarial losses. Perceptual losses have been shown to be effective at preserving high-frequency details in image-to-image translation tasks.
*   The discriminator architecture is fairly standard. Exploring a multi-scale discriminator (like in pix2pixHD) might help the generator produce finer details at different resolutions.
*   It would be interesting to see an analysis of how errors in the initial pose estimation (from the off-the-shelf pose estimator) propagate through the generation pipeline and affect the final output quality.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper tackles a novel and important problem with a well-designed and thoroughly evaluated method. The two-stage architecture is sensible, and the results, while not perfectly photo-realistic, are visually consistent and represent a strong first step on this task. The paper is well-written, and the experiments are comprehensive. Despite the limitations in output quality and the fact that the underlying framework builds on existing ideas, the novelty of the problem and the solid baseline presented make this a valuable contribution to the field of generative models and computer vision. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
