## Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

This paper presents a GAN-based system (PG2) for generating novel-view person images conditioned on a reference image and a target pose, represented as keypoints. The approach employs a two-stage architecture: an initial U-Net-like network for pose integration followed by a DCGAN-based refinement stage. While the idea of explicitly guiding image generation with pose information is interesting and relevant to areas like virtual try-on and animation, the work largely relies on established components (U-Nets, DCGANs) and positions itself as application-focused. This is reminiscent of work in conditional GANs (e.g., [6, 18, 21]) and recent advancements in view synthesis [4, 36], demonstrating an awareness of the existing literature.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method. The organization is logical, and the motivation for the two-stage approach is justified. The inclusion of ablation studies on pose embedding methods and the pose mask loss is a positive aspect, as it demonstrates a careful analysis of design choices.  Qualitatively, some generated images show plausible results, particularly with the DeepFashion dataset, indicating the potential of the approach. 

However, the most significant weakness is the complete lack of quantitative evaluation. The authors rely solely on visual inspection, making it difficult to assess the true quality and effectiveness of the system. A user study comparing generated images to real images (e.g., a Turing test) or metrics like Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) would be crucial to substantiate the claims of “high-quality” images. The visual examples provided are also somewhat limited in scope and resolution, and the figures are quite small, hindering detailed assessment. Furthermore, the results often exhibit noticeable artifacts and inconsistencies when compared to the target poses, particularly on the more challenging Market-1501 dataset.

The rebuttal failed to address these fundamental concerns, offering only superficial justifications for the lack of quantitative metrics. Other reviewers echoed these concerns regarding the qualitative shortcomings and the absence of rigorous evaluation. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Clear writing and logical organization.
* Interesting application of existing GAN architectures.
* Initial exploration of pose-guided image generation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* No quantitative evaluation.
* Visual quality concerns - artifacts, inconsistencies.
* Limited scope and resolution of presented results.
* Rebuttal did not adequately address concerns.


In conclusion, while the idea presented has merit, the current work is premature for a top-tier venue like NIPS. The absence of quantitative evaluation and the visual shortcomings prevent a strong endorsement. Further development and rigorous evaluation are needed before this work can be considered a significant contribution to the field.
