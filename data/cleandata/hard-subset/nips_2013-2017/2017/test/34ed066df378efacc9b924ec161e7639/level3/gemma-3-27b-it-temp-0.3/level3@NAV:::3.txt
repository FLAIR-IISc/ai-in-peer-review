## Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

This paper presents a GAN-based system, Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), for synthesizing novel-view person images conditioned on a reference image and a target pose. The approach utilizes a two-stage process: pose integration via a U-Net, followed by image refinement using a conditional DCGAN. While the idea of explicitly guiding image generation with pose information is interesting and aligns with the broader trend of controllable image synthesis explored in prior NIPS work (e.g., Reed et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2017), the core components – U-Nets and DCGANs – are well-established and the contribution appears application-focused rather than fundamentally novel in terms of architectural innovation.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it easy to follow the proposed methodology. The motivation for the two-stage approach is also well-explained, drawing parallels to existing divide-and-conquer strategies in related fields. However, a significant weakness is the complete lack of quantitative evaluation. There are no user studies or metrics (e.g., Inception Score, FID) to objectively assess the quality of the generated images or to demonstrate whether they are perceptually realistic or successfully transfer the desired pose. The qualitative results, while showing some promise, are hampered by small figure sizes and noticeable artifacts in the generated images, particularly when compared to the target poses. The supplementary material suffers from the same visual quality issues. 

The authors explore variations in pose embedding and loss functions, but the improvements demonstrated are marginal and not convincingly supported without quantitative analysis. The comparison to Zhao et al. (2017) is helpful, but a more comprehensive comparison to recent state-of-the-art image generation techniques would strengthen the paper. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** The idea is interesting and the writing is clear. The two-stage approach is a reasonable strategy for tackling the problem.
**Arguments against acceptance:** No quantitative evaluation, poor visual quality of generated images, reliance on established components without significant innovation, and lack of a thorough comparison to related work.

**Overall:** While the work demonstrates a potentially promising direction, it is premature to claim high-quality or real-image-level results without rigorous quantitative evaluation. As it stands, the paper is not ready for presentation at a top venue like NIPS. The lack of quantitative validation severely limits the impact and reproducibility of the research.
