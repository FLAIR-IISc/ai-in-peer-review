This paper proposes a novel two-stage approach, Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), for generating person images in arbitrary poses given a reference image and a target pose. The first stage uses a U-Net to generate a coarse image with the target pose, and the second stage refines this image using an adversarial network (DCGAN) to produce sharper, more detailed results. The key contributions are the task formulation, the exploration of pose integration methods, and the two-stage generation strategy.

This work builds upon the recent surge in image generation methods utilizing GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014), VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2013), and autoregressive models. It specifically addresses the problem of controllable image generation, which has seen advancements through conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014; Reed et al., 2016) and methods leveraging image-to-image translation (Isola et al., 2017). The authors differentiate their work by explicitly incorporating pose information for person image manipulation, a concept related to, but more specific than, multi-view synthesis (Zhao et al., 2017) or view-dependent image generation (Chen et al., 2017). The two-stage approach echoes strategies employed in previous work focusing on separating global structure from detailed appearance (e.g., Wang et al., 2017).

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Task Formulation:** The task of pose-guided person image generation is well-defined and addresses a practical problem with potential applications in areas like movie making and data augmentation for pose estimation.
*   **Effective Two-Stage Approach:** Dividing the problem into pose integration and image refinement is a sensible strategy given the complexity of simultaneously generating pose and detailed appearance. The authors convincingly argue for its effectiveness.
*   **Pose Mask Loss:** The proposed pose mask loss is a clever way to focus the generation process on the person's body, mitigating the influence of background changes, which is a key concern in this task.
*   **Quantitative & Qualitative Results:** The experiments on both the re-identification and fashion datasets demonstrate the method's capability to generate plausible and detailed images. The qualitative results showcase convincing pose transfers.
*   **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The paper investigates different pose embedding methods, loss functions, and model configurations, providing valuable insights into the contributions of each component.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the method and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparison to Related Work:** While related work is discussed, the comparison to Zhao et al. (2017) is limited to a single example. A more thorough comparison to existing multi-view synthesis or image manipulation techniques would strengthen the paper.
*   **Failure Cases & Limitations:** While acknowledged, the discussion of failure cases (e.g., incomplete body parts, inaccurate gender) could be more detailed. It would be helpful to analyze *why* these failures occur and discuss potential solutions. The user study results also highlight inconsistencies, and an explanation for these would be beneficial.
*   **Dependency on Pose Estimator:** The method relies on the accuracy of an external pose estimator. Errors in pose estimation could directly impact the quality of the generated images. This dependency isn't extensively discussed.
*   **SSIM vs. Perceptual Quality:** As noted by the authors, SSIM may not accurately reflect perceptual quality. While reported, relying heavily on SSIM as a primary evaluation metric is questionable. Consideration of other metrics like FID or user studies providing a more holistic assessment would be valuable.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The discussion on the importance of the lambda parameter in Stage II is brief. More analysis of its sensitivity and guidance on selecting appropriate values would improve reproducibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated task with a practical application.
*   The proposed method demonstrates strong performance on two different datasets.
*   The two-stage approach and pose mask loss are effective contributions.
*   The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the method’s design choices.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The comparison to related work could be more comprehensive.
*   The discussion of failure cases and limitations is somewhat limited.
*   The reliance on an external pose estimator introduces a potential source of error.
*   The heavy reliance on SSIM as an evaluation metric is concerning.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that presents a novel and effective approach to pose-guided person image generation. While there are some minor weaknesses related to the comparison to related work, analysis of failure cases, and evaluation metrics, the strengths of the work outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
