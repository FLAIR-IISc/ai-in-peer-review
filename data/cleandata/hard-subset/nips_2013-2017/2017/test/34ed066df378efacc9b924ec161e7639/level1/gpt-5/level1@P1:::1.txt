Summary
This paper addresses conditional person image synthesis: given a reference person image and a target pose (in keypoint form), synthesize the same person in the new pose. The proposed Pose-Guided Person Generation Network (PG2) uses a two-stage, coarse-to-fine pipeline. Stage-I integrates the conditioning image and pose by concatenating pose heatmaps with the image and passing them through a U-Net-like generator with skip connections and a bottleneck fully connected layer. Training uses a masked L1 loss that emphasizes foreground pixels derived from pose masks, producing a coarse but pose-correct result. Stage-II refines details via a conditional GAN: the generator predicts a residual “difference map” conditioned on the coarse output and the conditioning image; a pairwise discriminator takes (generated-or-real, conditioning image) to encourage realism while staying consistent with the condition. Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) show qualitative improvements and ablations on pose embeddings, loss, and one- vs two-stage training. Quantitative evaluation uses SSIM, Inception Score (and masked variants), plus an AMT user study. Comparison to VariGAN [36] qualitatively favors PG2.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on conditional generation methods (cVAE, cGANs [Mirza & Osindero], pix2pix [Isola et al.], StackGAN), and coarse-to-fine GANs (e.g., LAPGAN). Unlike prior multi-view methods conditioned on view labels or text [36, 24], PG2 conditions explicitly on 2D pose keypoints, offering finer control. It does not employ explicit warping/flow (e.g., appearance flow for view synthesis, Zhou et al. ECCV’16) or spatial transformers; instead, it relies on learned mappings via U-Nets.

Quality
- Methodology: Sound and well-motivated. The decomposition into global structure (Stage-I) and appearance refinement (Stage-II) is reasonable, and the residual/difference-map formulation for Stage-II is a practical stabilization trick. The pairwise discriminator is a good design choice for conditional fidelity.
- Ablations: Clear comparisons across pose embeddings (coordinate vs separate-encoder heatmaps vs concatenation), loss (L1 vs masked L1), and one- vs two-stage adversarial setup. The masked variants of SSIM/IS are thoughtful given background mismatch.
- Evaluation: Qualitative results are strong, especially on DeepFashion. However, quantitative measures are limited: SSIM/IS (and masked variants) are not fully aligned with human perception; user study improvements are modest and time-limited (1s). Critically, identity preservation (central to “person” generation) is not evaluated with a re-ID model or identity classifier, which would strengthen claims.
- Details: Architecture/training hyperparameters are mostly given; λ’s effect is deferred to supplement. Some choices (no batch norm, FC bottleneck in Stage-I) would benefit from justification/parameter counts.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. Figures effectively illustrate the pipeline and ablation results. Minor issues: some typos and incomplete references; the rationale for “1+MB” vs a tunable foreground weight in the masked L1 could be clarified; reporting of final λ and stability would help reproducibility.

Originality
- Task: Pose-guided person generation conditioned on keypoints and a reference image is a timely, relatively novel formulation in 2017 compared to view- or attribute-conditioned generation.
- Approach: Coarse-to-fine with adversarial residual refinement and pairwise discriminator is not entirely new conceptually, but its instantiation for pose transfer with masked losses and keypoint heatmaps is a meaningful contribution. The observation that directly learning residuals improves convergence is pragmatic and useful.

Significance
The work has clear practical impact for content creation (editing, movie production), data augmentation for re-ID/pose estimation, and controllable generation. It advances the state of the art qualitatively on two benchmarks and provides useful design insights (pose heatmaps as inputs, masked loss, residual refinement). Demonstrating downstream benefits (e.g., augmenting re-ID training) would further increase significance.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated two-stage design; effective use of pose heatmaps and masked loss.
- Pairwise discriminator and residual refinement improve detail and stability.
- Thorough ablations isolating key design choices.
- Strong qualitative results and a fair attempt at quantitative proxies (including masked variants and a user study).
- Comparison to the closest prior [36] shows visible gains.

Weaknesses
- No explicit identity preservation metric; missing re-ID–based evaluation to verify that identity is retained.
- Quantitative metrics (SSIM/IS) are known to misalign with realism; user study protocol is limited.
- Discriminator is not conditioned on the target pose; enforcing pose adherence explicitly in D might reduce pose drift.
- Baseline fairness: the alternative HME/CE models may be underpowered relative to the main architecture; stronger single-stage baselines (e.g., pix2pix-style U-Net with perceptual loss) are not reported.
- No exploration of explicit geometric alignment (warping/appearance flow) which could help under large pose changes.
- Background handling: masked loss partially ignores backgrounds, but artifacts remain on Market-1501; no mechanism to hallucinate unseen backgrounds.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add identity preservation evaluation (e.g., cosine similarity in a pretrained re-ID embedding; retrieval consistency).
- Condition the discriminator on the target pose PB (or both PB and IA) to better enforce pose correctness.
- Explore perceptual feature losses (e.g., VGG) and/or feature matching losses to reduce blur without over-relying on IS/SSIM.
- Provide a stronger single-stage baseline (pix2pix with keypoints) and a Stage-II ablation generating full images rather than residuals.
- Consider explicit geometric modules (e.g., learned warping/flow) to better handle large pose changes.
- Report λ values used, parameter counts, and training stability; release code for reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and timely problem with clear practical utility.
- Solid technical design with simple, effective components and insightful ablations.
- Strong qualitative improvements on two datasets; convincing comparisons to closest prior.
- Clear writing and transparent methodology.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient quantitative evidence on identity preservation and downstream utility.
- Limited and sometimes misaligned metrics; modest user study gains.
- Missing stronger baselines and explicit pose-conditioning in the discriminator.
- Background/artifact issues on in-the-wild surveillance images remain.

Overall assessment
A well-executed and relevant contribution that advances pose-conditioned image generation with a pragmatic two-stage design. Despite weaknesses in quantitative evaluation and baselines, the paper is technically sound, clear, and likely to influence follow-up work. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening evaluation of identity preservation and adding a stronger single-stage baseline.
