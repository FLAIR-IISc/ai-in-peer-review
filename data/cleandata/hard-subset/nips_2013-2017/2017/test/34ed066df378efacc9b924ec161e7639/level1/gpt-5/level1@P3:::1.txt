Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses pose-guided person image synthesis: given a reference image IA of a person and a target pose PB, generate an image IB̂ depicting the same person in PB. The proposed Pose-Guided Person Generation (PG2) network is a two-stage pipeline: Stage-I integrates IA with a pose representation to produce a coarse result using a U-Net-like encoder-decoder (with residual blocks and a bottleneck fully connected layer) trained with an L1 loss reweighted by a pose mask to downweight background. Stage-II refines the result via a conditional adversarial setup: a second U-Net takes the Stage-I output and IA, and predicts a difference map, trained with a pairwise conditional discriminator D that distinguishes (IA, IB) from (IA, G2(IA, ÎB1)), plus a masked L1 term. Pose is encoded as 18 heatmaps from a state-of-the-art pose estimator (Cao et al.). Experiments on DeepFashion (256×256) and Market-1501 (128×64) include ablations on pose encoding, loss, and one- vs two-stage training, with qualitative examples, SSIM/IS (and masked variants), and a small AMT study. They compare to VariGAN (Zhao et al.) as the most related baseline.

The work is situated at the intersection of conditional generation and structure-guided synthesis. It relates to conditional GANs (Isola et al., Mirza & Osindero), pose/structure conditioning (Reed et al.), and human synthesis from segmentation (Lassner et al.). The specific focus on explicit 2D keypoint guidance for person pose transfer and the two-stage “coarse-to-fine with difference map” strategy appear novel within the cited body of work.

Quality
- Methodology: The decomposition into global structure (Stage-I) and detail (Stage-II) is well motivated and empirically validated. Encoding pose as spatial heatmaps concatenated with the image is a sound choice and outperforms coordinate-based embeddings in ablations. The masked L1 is an appropriate heuristic to mitigate unknown background; the pairwise discriminator conditioned on IA is a sensible design that prevents trivial copying of IA. Predicting a residual/difference map is a practical way to stabilize refinement.
- Experimental support: Qualitative results convincingly show improved pose correctness and detail compared to single-stage training and to VariGAN. The ablations on pose encoding and losses are informative. However, quantitative evaluation relies on SSIM/IS (and masked variants), which are known to be imperfect proxies; the paper acknowledges SSIM’s bias for blur. The AMT study (short presentation, 30 users) shows modest fooling rates and is limited in scale.
- Missing evaluations: The central promise is preserving identity while changing pose. There is no quantitative identity preservation measure (e.g., using a re-identification model to score whether the generated image matches the identity of IA). Robustness to pose estimation errors and to extreme pose changes is only anecdotally discussed. There is no ablation isolating the contribution of predicting a difference map versus directly predicting the refined image within the same architecture and loss.
- Implementation details are mostly clear; the choice of a fully connected bottleneck in Stage-I is unusual for image-to-image models and may harm spatial fidelity/scalability; an ablation removing it (as done in Stage-II) would strengthen claims that it is beneficial.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The problem setup, architecture, and losses are clearly described, with figures that aid understanding. The notion of the pose mask and how it is derived is explained. Some quantitative results (e.g., AMT exact numbers per setting, λ sensitivity) are deferred to the supplement; brief summaries in the main text would help. The references contain several arXiv entries with incomplete bibliographic details; please standardize.

Originality
- The task formulation (explicit keypoint-guided person image generation) and two-stage pipeline are a meaningful contribution relative to contemporaneous works focused on text-conditioning, category labels, or coarse view control.
- The masked L1 and pairwise conditional discriminator are incremental but well adapted to this setting. Predicting a difference map in Stage-II is a pragmatic twist not commonly used in conditional GANs at the time.
- While U-Net-based conditioning with pose heatmaps is a natural extension of image-to-image translation, the thorough ablation and the staged adversarial refinement add novelty.

Significance
This problem is relevant to controllable synthesis, data augmentation for pose/re-id, and content creation. The results improve over a strong related baseline and demonstrate synthesis at 256×256 with convincing details. That said, the lack of quantitative identity preservation and limited comparisons reduce the immediate impact. Subsequent work is likely to build on the idea of explicit pose conditioning and coarse-to-fine refinement.

Strengths
- Clear problem definition and practical, modular architecture.
- Effective use of pose heatmaps; well-executed ablations supporting design choices.
- Coarse-to-fine training with residual refinement stabilizes adversarial learning and improves detail.
- Masked loss neatly addresses background mismatch; pairwise conditioning in D prevents trivial solutions.
- Demonstrated on two datasets with realistic challenges; qualitative gains over related work.

Weaknesses
- No quantitative evaluation of identity preservation; AMT study is small and not diagnostic.
- Metrics (SSIM/IS) are limited; no FID or task-driven metrics (re-id accuracy with generated images).
- Limited comparisons: pix2pix-like baselines conditioned on pose heatmaps could be included; ablation isolating the “difference map” contribution is missing.
- The Stage-I fully connected layer’s benefit is asserted but not validated; may hinder scalability and spatial fidelity.
- Background handling remains problematic (transfer of background and artifacts in Stage-II); methods leveraging segmentation or attention could help.
- Sensitivity to pose estimator errors and missing/incorrect keypoints is not analyzed.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Report identity preservation using a pre-trained re-id model: cosine similarity in embedding space, or re-id retrieval accuracy when substituting generated images.
- Include a pix2pix baseline conditioned on pose heatmaps, trained end-to-end adversarially, to contextualize gains.
- Provide an ablation where Stage-II directly predicts the full refined image (same architecture/loss) to isolate the value of difference-map prediction.
- Analyze robustness to pose estimation noise (e.g., jitter/occluded keypoints) and to larger pose changes; quantify failure rates.
- Justify the Stage-I fully connected layer with an ablation; consider a fully-convolutional design for scale/generalization.
- Consider perceptual losses (feature matching) to further improve fidelity without over-reliance on adversarial loss, and/or instance normalization for stability.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a principled, effective architecture.
- Strong qualitative results and insightful ablations.
- Clear, reproducible methodology with practical tricks (masking, pairwise D, residual refinement) likely to be adopted.

Cons for acceptance
- Incomplete quantitative evaluation of the core objective (identity preservation).
- Limited baselines and comparisons; reliance on imperfect metrics.
- Some architectural choices not fully justified; background artifacts persist.

Overall, this is a solid, well-motivated contribution with clear empirical benefits. Strengthening the quantitative evaluation of identity and adding a couple of targeted ablations/comparisons would substantially improve the paper.
