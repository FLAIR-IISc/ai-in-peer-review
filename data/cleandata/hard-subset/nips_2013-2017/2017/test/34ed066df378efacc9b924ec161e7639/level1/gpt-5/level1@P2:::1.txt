Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses conditional person image synthesis: given an input image of a person and a target pose (as 2D keypoints), generate a new image of the same person in the target pose. The proposed PG2 framework uses a two-stage, coarse-to-fine pipeline. Stage-I integrates the condition image and pose using a U-Net-like encoder-decoder with skip connections and a fully connected bottleneck, trained with a pose-weighted L1 loss (“pose mask”) to emphasize the person over background. Stage-II refines details via an adversarially trained U-Net that predicts a residual (difference map) to add to the Stage-I output; a conditional discriminator receives pairs (condition image, generated/real image), and a masked L1 term regularizes the generator.

The work builds on GANs, cGANs, and coarse-to-fine generation (e.g., DCGAN, pix2pix, StackGAN), as well as pose-guided conditioning used in prior multi-view or attribute/text-conditioned synthesis. Compared to view-conditioned synthesis (e.g., Zhao et al., 2017), PG2 uses explicit pose keypoint heatmaps to enable finer pose control. Relative to pix2pix-like baselines, the two-stage design and difference-map refinement are the main architectural choices. The paper situates itself among related person generation works (e.g., Lassner et al.) and shows comparisons to the most related multi-view baseline [36].

Quality (technical soundness, empirical support)
- Method: The architectural choices are reasonable and well-motivated: pose encoded as heatmaps concatenated with the image is a solid design; masked L1 focuses learning on the subject; pairwise conditional discriminator avoids trivial copying; predicting residuals is a plausible way to ease adversarial training.
- Ablations: The paper evaluates pose embedding variants (coordinates vs heatmaps vs concatenation), loss variants (L1 vs masked L1), and one-stage vs two-stage adversarial training. These are helpful and support the design choices.
- Metrics: SSIM and IS (mask variants) are reported, though the authors acknowledge their limits; a user study complements them. However, IS is not ideal for conditional evaluation and mask-IS is ad hoc. No FID is reported, and there is no identity preservation metric (e.g., re-ID similarity), which is critical for this task.
- Baselines: The one-stage adversarial baseline is included, and a comparison to [36] is shown. A stronger baseline would be a pix2pix-style cGAN that takes (condition image + pose heatmaps) with a PatchGAN discriminator and perceptual loss, which is very relevant and would calibrate gains due to the two-stage design. Also missing are comparisons to perceptual/feature losses (VGG) or appearance flow/deformation baselines.
- Robustness/limitations: The approach depends on pose estimator quality; robustness to pose noise and handling of occlusions/self-occlusions are not systematically analyzed. Failure cases are briefly discussed. Background leakage/artifacts remain an issue, especially on Market-1501.
- Reproducibility: High-level architecture, losses, datasets, and training schedules are described; λ ablation is deferred to the supplement. Some implementation details (e.g., exact mask construction, λ values used in main experiments) could be clearer.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-structured. The motivation for a two-stage pipeline, pose encoding, and difference-map refinement is explained. Figures convey the pipeline and qualitative effects. Writing is mostly clear with minor grammatical issues. A few details would benefit from clarification: exact pose mask generation procedure, training hyperparameters (especially λ), and a more explicit statement of how the difference map is combined (additive vs other) and normalized.

Originality
The task formulation (explicit pose-guided person image generation) is timely and useful. Using pose heatmaps is known, and the two-stage coarse-to-fine strategy has precedents (e.g., StackGAN). The novelty lies in combining (i) pose-masked reconstruction loss, (ii) two-stage U-Nets specialized for structure vs details, (iii) conditional pairwise discriminator, and (iv) residual (difference map) refinement for pose-driven person synthesis. Overall, the contribution is incremental but nontrivial: a cohesive system with targeted design choices for this application.

Significance
The problem is significant for controllable image synthesis, virtual try-on, data augmentation for pose/re-id tasks, and content creation. Qualitative results on DeepFashion and Market-1501 are compelling. That said, the lack of identity preservation evaluation and stronger baselines makes it hard to quantify impact versus simpler cGAN+perceptual setups. Demonstrating benefit to downstream tasks (e.g., improving re-id via augmentation) would markedly increase significance.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and practical importance.
- Effective use of explicit pose conditioning via heatmaps.
- Well-motivated two-stage design with residual refinement and pairwise conditional discriminator.
- Useful ablations (pose embedding, loss, one- vs two-stage) and solid qualitative results at 128×64 and 256×256.
- Acknowledges metric limitations and includes a user study.

Cons
- Missing strong baselines (e.g., pix2pix with pose maps and perceptual loss) and FID.
- No quantitative measure of identity preservation; no demonstration of utility for re-id augmentation.
- Limited analysis of robustness to pose estimation noise/occlusion and background variation; artifacts remain, especially on Market-1501.
- Evaluation metrics (mask-IS/SSIM) are limited; user study numbers are modest and mixed across datasets.
- Some implementation details deferred or underspecified (mask construction, λ, exact residual application).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a pix2pix-style baseline with PatchGAN and VGG perceptual loss using (condition image + pose heatmaps).
- Report FID (if feasible) and, crucially, identity preservation metrics (face ID or re-id embedding similarity). Optionally evaluate re-id performance with/without synthetic augmentation.
- Analyze sensitivity to pose estimator errors and occlusions; include experiments with noisy/missing keypoints and corresponding robustness strategies (e.g., confidence-weighted heatmaps).
- Improve background handling (e.g., explicit foreground segmentation/attention, background inpainting) to reduce artifacts.
- Provide more details on mask generation and ablate λ in the main paper; clarify inference speed and compute.

Overall assessment
A solid, well-executed contribution to pose-guided person image generation with a clear, effective two-stage design and thoughtful loss/conditioning choices. The paper meets the quality and clarity criteria reasonably well, offers moderate originality, and addresses a significant problem. Strengthening baselines and adding identity-preservation and robustness evaluations would substantially solidify the case. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on improved experimental comparisons and metrics.
