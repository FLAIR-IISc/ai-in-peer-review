**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel task: synthesizing an image of a specific person in an arbitrary target pose, conditioned on a single source image of that person. To tackle this challenging problem, the authors propose a two-stage framework called the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG²). The first stage uses a U-Net-like architecture (G1) to generate a coarse image. It takes the source person image and a heatmap representation of the target pose as input, aiming to correctly map the person's appearance onto the new skeletal structure. A key contribution here is a proposed "pose mask loss," a weighted L1 loss that encourages the network to focus on the foreground person rather than trying to hallucinate a new background. The second stage refines the coarse output from the first. It employs another U-Net-like generator (G2) within a conditional GAN framework to add finer details and increase photorealism. The discriminator is conditioned on the source image to enforce identity preservation. The authors demonstrate their method's effectiveness on two datasets, the high-resolution DeepFashion dataset and the more challenging low-resolution Market-1501 re-identification dataset, showing significant improvements over related view-synthesis methods.

This work fits well within the context of conditional generative models, which were a major focus of research at NIPS and other top conferences around 2017. It builds upon foundational architectures like U-Nets (Ronneberger et al., 2015) and conditional GANs, particularly the image-to-image translation framework of Isola et al. (2017). The two-stage, coarse-to-fine refinement strategy is also conceptually similar to other contemporary works like StackGAN (Zhang et al., 2017). However, the paper's primary contribution is the novel problem formulation and the explicit use of pose keypoints for fine-grained spatial control over the generation process, which represents a significant step beyond conditioning on simpler signals like class labels or coarse viewpoint categories (e.g., Zhao et al., 2017).

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and well-executed piece of research.
*   **Strengths:** The two-stage decomposition of the problem into global structure generation (Stage I) and detail refinement (Stage II) is a logical and effective strategy for this complex task. The claims are well-supported by an extensive set of experiments. The authors perform thorough ablation studies that justify their key design choices: the heatmap-based pose embedding, the pose mask loss, and the two-stage architecture. The qualitative results, particularly on the DeepFashion dataset, are impressive and clearly demonstrate the model's ability to preserve appearance while accurately transferring pose. The inclusion of a user study provides a valuable perceptual evaluation that complements the automated metrics. The authors are also honest about the limitations and failure cases (e.g., complex poses, background artifacts), which strengthens the paper's scientific integrity.
*   **Weaknesses:** The proposed quantitative metrics, masked-SSIM and masked-IS, while a reasonable attempt to mitigate the background issue, are ad-hoc and not standard. The authors rightly note that these metrics (and SSIM/IS in general) do not always align with perceptual quality, as blurry images can score highly. The system's performance is also inherently dependent on the accuracy of the upstream pose estimator, and errors from that module will inevitably propagate, a dependency that could be discussed further.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Strengths:** The motivation, methodology, and experimental setup are presented in a clear and logical progression. Figure 2 provides an excellent overview of the entire framework. The breakdown of the experiments in Section 4.2 directly corresponds to the key architectural and loss function choices, making the results easy to interpret.
*   **Weaknesses:** There is a minor point of confusion in the description of the Stage II generator (G2) in Section 3.2. The text states that G2 "aims to generate an appearance difference map," which might imply a residual learning setup (i.e., final_output = coarse_input + G2_output). However, the loss function in Eq. 4 suggests that G2 directly outputs the final refined image. Clarifying that "generating a difference map" is a conceptual description of the network's *task* (learning to correct the coarse input) rather than its literal output would improve precision.

**Originality:** The paper makes several original contributions.
*   **Strengths:** The primary novelty is the problem formulation itself: pose-guided person generation from a single image. This is a significant and more challenging variant of general image-to-image translation. Using an explicit skeletal pose as a conditioning variable provides a level of direct, non-rigid spatial control that was largely absent in prior work. While the architectural components (U-Net, cGAN) are familiar, their combination and adaptation for this task, including the pose heatmap concatenation and the masked loss, are novel. The work clearly distinguishes itself from related efforts in view synthesis, which typically rely on coarser conditioning signals.

**Significance:** The results are important and the ideas presented are likely to be influential.
*   **Strengths:** This work addresses a problem with clear and compelling applications, including virtual try-on for e-commerce, data augmentation for tasks like person re-identification and pose estimation, and creative tools for film and gaming. It represents a tangible step forward in controllable generative modeling, demonstrating how to disentangle a person's appearance from their pose. This is a foundational capability that has inspired a great deal of subsequent research in human image synthesis. The method demonstrably advances the state of the art, as shown by the comparison with Zhao et al. [36].

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Important Problem:** The paper introduces a new, challenging, and practically relevant task in conditional image generation.
2.  **Effective and Well-Designed Method:** The proposed two-stage framework is an elegant and effective solution that successfully decomposes the problem.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The claims are backed by strong qualitative results, comprehensive ablation studies, quantitative metrics, and a user study across two distinct datasets.
4.  **High Clarity and Impact:** The paper is very well-written, and its ideas have high potential for impact on both the research community and practical applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Incremental Algorithmic Novelty:** The core building blocks of the network (U-Net, cGAN) are adaptations of existing methods rather than fundamentally new inventions. The novelty is primarily in the problem formulation and system integration.
2.  **Limited Background Handling:** The model's inability to generate novel, coherent backgrounds is a clear limitation, which is sidestepped by the masked loss rather than solved.

**Conclusion:**
This is an excellent paper. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It introduces a novel and significant problem, proposes a well-motivated and effective solution, and supports its claims with a thorough and convincing set of experiments. It is a clear contribution to the field of generative models and is highly suitable for publication at NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
