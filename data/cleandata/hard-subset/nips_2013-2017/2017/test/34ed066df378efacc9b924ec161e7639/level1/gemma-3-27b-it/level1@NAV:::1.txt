## Review of “Pose Guided Person Image Generation”

This paper introduces a novel task: generating person images in arbitrary poses given a reference image and a target pose. The core idea is to decompose this challenging problem into two stages: pose integration via a U-Net-like network to create a coarse image, followed by refinement using a conditional DCGAN to enhance detail and realism. The model leverages keypoint heatmaps to represent pose information and proposes a masked L1 loss to focus generation on the person’s body, mitigating background influence. 

The work builds upon the recent surge in GAN-based image generation (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and extends them to the specific context of pose-guided person image manipulation. It's related to image-to-image translation (Isola et al., 2017) and multi-view synthesis (Chen et al., 2017; Zhao et al., 2017), but differentiates itself by explicitly leveraging pose information through keypoints, providing more direct control.  The two-stage approach is reminiscent of multi-stage refinement strategies seen in other generation tasks (e.g., Huang et al., 2017).

**Strengths:** The paper addresses an interesting and potentially useful problem with a clear motivation. The two-stage approach seems reasonable, breaking down the complexity of simultaneous pose and appearance generation. The use of pose heatmaps is a clever way to integrate pose information into the network. The inclusion of a masked L1 loss is also a good design choice to focus learning.  The quantitative and qualitative results on both datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, showing improved image quality compared to baselines and a related work (VariGAN).

**Weaknesses:** The paper lacks a thorough comparison to more recent and advanced image generation techniques beyond VariGAN (e.g., StyleGAN, BigGAN). While the ablation studies (different pose embeddings, loss functions, and stage configurations) are helpful, they could be more comprehensive. The user study, while present, relies on AMT and has limitations in controlling for potential biases.  The discussion of failure cases is limited. The implementation details could also be more complete.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel task and a promising approach to pose-guided person image generation.
*   Clear and well-motivated methodology with a reasonable two-stage architecture.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness through quantitative and qualitative results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited comparison to state-of-the-art generative models.
*   User study has limitations.
*   The discussion of failure cases could be more in-depth.




**Overall:** I believe this paper presents a solid contribution to the field. While there are areas for improvement, the novelty of the task, the technical soundness of the approach, and the experimental validation make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions to address the points above, specifically expanding the comparison with more recent GAN architectures and a more detailed analysis of failure cases.
