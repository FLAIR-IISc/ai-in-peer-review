This paper tackles the problem of pose-guided person image generation, aiming to synthesize images of a person in a new pose given a reference image and a target pose. The approach utilizes a two-stage framework: a U-Net-like network for initial pose integration, followed by a DCGAN-based refinement stage. The core novelty lies in explicitly incorporating pose information and dividing the problem into global structure and appearance detail generation.

This work builds upon the rapidly evolving field of image generation, particularly GAN-based methods [6, 21, 5, 1]. It differs from existing conditional generation approaches [32, 18, 15, 24, 22, 23] by focusing on explicit pose guidance rather than relying on abstract labels, text, or other visual cues. While [34, 33, 11, 8] address conditional face generation with viewpoint control, they lack the flexibility of arbitrary pose control presented here. Furthermore, [36], a related work on multi-view cloth synthesis, considers viewpoint, which the authors argue is less expressive than their keypoint-based pose representation. The two-stage approach, dividing the problem into structure and detail, is inspired by existing works [35, 9, 3, 19], but adapted to this specific task.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The task of pose-guided person image generation with explicit pose control is a valuable contribution. The keypoint-based pose representation offers greater flexibility than viewpoint-based approaches.
* **Technical Soundness:** The two-stage framework, utilizing U-Nets and GANs, is a reasonable and well-motivated approach. The introduction of the pose mask loss is a clever way to focus generation on the person's appearance.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on both low-resolution (Market-1501) and high-resolution (DeepFashion) datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The ablation studies (different pose embeddings, loss functions, and the two-stage model) provide valuable insights.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The diagrams (Figure 2, 3) are helpful in understanding the architecture and process.
* **User Study:** Including a user study adds further confidence to the quality of the generated images.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison:** While the authors compare against [36], a more thorough comparison with recent image manipulation techniques (e.g., those using image warping or deformation fields) would be beneficial. These methods might offer alternative approaches to pose transfer.
* **Failure Cases:** The discussion of failure cases is limited. More detailed analysis of these failures would provide valuable insights for future work.  The issues with complex poses and gender imbalance should be explored further.
* **Quantitative Metrics:** The quantitative metrics (SSIM, IS) have limitations, as acknowledged by the authors.  More relevant metrics, such as perceptual distance metrics or human evaluation of pose accuracy, could be considered. The observation that SSIM doesn’t always align with perceptual quality should be highlighted more strongly.
* **Pose Estimation Dependency:** The reliance on a separate pose estimator introduces a potential source of error. The performance of the generation network is therefore limited by the accuracy of the pose estimation. This dependency should be discussed further.
* **Lack of Detail on Implementation:** Certain implementation details are lacking (e.g. specific network hyperparameters) making exact reproduction difficult.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a novel and challenging problem with a well-motivated approach.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The ablation studies provide valuable insights.
* The user study adds further confidence in the quality of the generated images.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The comparison with related work is limited.
* The discussion of failure cases could be more thorough.
* The reliance on an external pose estimator is a potential limitation.
* Quantitative evaluation could be improved with more appropriate metrics.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of image generation. The novelty of the task, the technical soundness of the approach, and the thorough experimental validation are all commendable. While there are some weaknesses, they are not critical, and the potential impact of this work is significant.  I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS 2017, with minor revisions. I suggest the authors to: 1) Expand the related work section to include more relevant methods, particularly those involving image manipulation and deformation. 2) Provide a more detailed analysis of failure cases and discuss potential solutions. 3) Investigate alternative quantitative metrics. 4) Provide more implementation details to facilitate reproducibility.
