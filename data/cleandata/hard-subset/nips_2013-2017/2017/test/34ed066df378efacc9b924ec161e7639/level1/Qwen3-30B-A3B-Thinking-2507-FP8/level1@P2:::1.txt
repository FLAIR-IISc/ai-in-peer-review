

# Review of "Pose Guided Person Image Generation"

This paper introduces the Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), a two-stage framework for synthesizing person images in arbitrary poses given a reference image and target pose. The authors propose a novel task of conditioning image generation on both a reference image and a specific pose, which differs from prior work that conditioned on labels, text, or limited pose information. The framework first integrates pose information (represented as heatmaps of keypoints) with the reference image using a U-Net-like network to generate a coarse pose-aligned image. The second stage then refines this coarse result using adversarial training to add appearance details. The authors introduce a pose mask loss to focus generation on the human body rather than background, and use a difference map approach for refinement to speed up training.

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work on conditional image generation, particularly building on [17, 21, 22, 23, 24, 32, 36] while making significant distinctions. Unlike [36] which uses word vectors for pose condition, this work uses explicit pose keypoints. The two-stage approach differs from end-to-end methods [10, 17] and addresses the challenge of simultaneously generating correct pose and detailed appearance.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper is technically sound with thorough experiments across two datasets (DeepFashion and Market-1501). The ablation studies are well-designed, comparing different pose embeddings (coordinate vs. heatmap), losses (standard L1 vs. pose mask), and two-stage vs. one-stage approaches. The authors properly acknowledge limitations, such as failure cases for rare poses and gender imbalance. The quantitative results (SSIM, Inception Score) and user studies provide strong empirical support for the approach.

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The methodology is clearly explained with detailed network architectures, loss functions, and implementation details. The figures (particularly Figure 4) effectively illustrate qualitative results and ablation studies. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results.

*Originality:* The paper makes three significant contributions: (1) Proposing the novel task of pose-guided person image generation, (2) Introducing a pose mask loss to focus on human body rather than background, and (3) Developing a two-stage framework that separates pose structure generation from detail refinement. This work is distinct from prior NIPS papers on conditional image generation [17, 21, 22, 23, 24, 32, 36] by using explicit pose information rather than labels or text.

*Significance:* The application of pose-guided person generation has clear value for movie making, human pose estimation, and person re-identification. The results demonstrate significant improvements over the most relevant prior work [36], particularly in generating photo-realistic details. The two-stage approach could inspire similar strategies for other complex image generation tasks. The work advances the state of the art in conditional image generation for person images by addressing a challenging but practical problem.

**Weaknesses:**

*Quality:* While the paper addresses background interference with the pose mask loss, the approach still struggles with complex backgrounds (as seen in Market-1501 results). The user study shows lower performance on Market-1501 due to background complexity, suggesting the approach has limitations in highly cluttered scenes. The paper could better address how to handle background generation when it's necessary for the application.

*Clarity:* The paper doesn't fully explain how the pose estimator (used to generate the keypoint heatmaps) affects the overall pipeline. While they mention using a state-of-the-art pose estimator, they don't discuss how errors in pose estimation might propagate through their framework.

*Originality:* While the pose mask loss is novel, the two-stage approach is similar to prior work on image-to-image translation [11, 35]. The paper could better differentiate its specific contributions from these similar approaches.

*Significance:* The paper doesn't sufficiently address how their approach scales to generating images with multiple people in complex scenes, which would be a more challenging application. The limitations in handling rare poses (as shown in Figure 6) also suggest the method has constraints that need to be acknowledged.

**Overall Recommendation:**

This paper is a strong contribution to the field of conditional image generation. It addresses a novel and practically important problem with a technically sound, well-executed approach. The quality of the experiments, clarity of presentation, and significance of the contributions make it a compelling NIPS submission.

Pro-acceptance arguments: Novel task formulation, effective two-stage approach, innovative pose mask loss, thorough experimental validation across multiple datasets, clear presentation, significant improvements over prior work.

Con-acceptance arguments: Limited handling of complex backgrounds, no discussion of pose estimation error propagation, some similarity to prior image-to-image translation approaches, and limitations in handling rare poses.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS 2017 as it makes a clear, significant contribution to the field of generative modeling with strong empirical validation. While there are minor limitations, they don't undermine the overall contribution. The authors have provided a complete piece of work with careful consideration of strengths and weaknesses, meeting all NIPS quality standards. I recommend acceptance.
