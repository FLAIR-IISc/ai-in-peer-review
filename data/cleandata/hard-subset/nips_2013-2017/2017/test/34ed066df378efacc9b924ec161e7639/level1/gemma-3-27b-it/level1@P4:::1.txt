This paper introduces a novel two-stage approach, Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), for generating person images in arbitrary poses given a reference image and a target pose. The first stage uses a U-Net to generate a coarse image, and the second stage employs a DCGAN to refine the image and add details. The paper explores different pose embedding methods and proposes a pose mask loss to focus on transferring the human body appearance.

The paper builds upon recent advances in image generation using GANs [6, 21, 5] and conditional image generation [32, 18, 22, 23]. It addresses a specific instance of conditional generation – pose transfer – which is less explored compared to attribute or viewpoint conditioning. The two-stage approach draws parallels to work in image editing [35, 9, 3, 19], breaking down the problem into global structure and appearance details.  The related work section provides a reasonable overview of relevant literature, although some connections could be more explicitly drawn (e.g., the similarities and differences with [36] beyond a brief mention in the experiments).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The task of pose-guided person image generation is a worthwhile contribution, offering potential applications in areas like movie making and pose estimation data augmentation. The explicit use of pose information as a conditioning factor is a clear strength.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed two-stage framework with U-Net and DCGAN components is a reasonable approach. The use of the pose mask loss is a clever idea to focus generation on the human body. The inclusion of both quantitative and qualitative experiments strengthens the evaluation.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The figures are helpful in illustrating the network architecture and results. The motivation for the two-stage approach is clearly explained.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on both low-resolution (Market-1501) and high-resolution (DeepFashion) datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.  The ablation studies (different pose embeddings, loss functions, and the one vs. two-stage comparison) provide valuable insights.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison:** While the paper acknowledges [36] as related work, the comparison is brief and only shown qualitatively. A more thorough quantitative comparison with this work, and ideally others addressing similar tasks, would strengthen the contribution.
* **Failure Cases:** The discussion of failure cases (last paragraph) is brief. A more detailed analysis of the limitations of the method, along with potential solutions, would be beneficial. For instance, it is unclear why the method sometimes struggles with generating legs or accurately reflecting gender.
* **Quantitative Metrics:** The reliance on metrics like SSIM and IS, which are known to have limitations in evaluating generative models [10, 17], is a potential drawback. Although the user study partially addresses this, it would be helpful to consider other metrics more directly relevant to perceptual quality.
* **Implementation Details:** Some implementation details could be more specific. For example, the size of the latent space used in the GAN is not mentioned.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a novel and interesting problem with a technically sound and well-motivated approach.
* The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method on two different datasets.
* The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the contribution of different components of the framework.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The comparison with related work is limited.
* The discussion of failure cases is brief and lacks in-depth analysis.
* The reliance on potentially misleading quantitative metrics.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution that addresses a novel problem in image generation. The proposed two-stage framework and the pose mask loss are valuable ideas. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding the comparison with related work and the discussion of failure cases, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns.

**Recommendation:**

Accept. However, the authors should be encouraged to address the weaknesses mentioned above in the final version of the paper. Specifically, expanding the comparison with related work and providing a more thorough analysis of failure cases would significantly strengthen the contribution.
