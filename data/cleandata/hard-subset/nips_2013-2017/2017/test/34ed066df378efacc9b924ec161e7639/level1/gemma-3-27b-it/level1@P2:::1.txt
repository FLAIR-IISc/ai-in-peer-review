This paper proposes a novel two-stage framework, Pose Guided Person Generation Network (PG2), for generating person images in arbitrary poses given a reference image and a target pose. The first stage generates a coarse image integrating the reference image and target pose, while the second stage refines it using an adversarial network to generate sharper details. The key contributions lie in the task formulation, the proposed pose integration techniques (specifically using pose heatmaps), and the two-stage architecture with a masked L1 loss.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a challenging problem with practical applications in areas like movie making and data augmentation for pose estimation. It builds upon existing work in image generation (GANs, VAEs, autoregressive models) and conditional image generation. The authors correctly identify relevant literature, including recent NIPS papers (e.g., [6, 14, 21, 30]) and closely related work on multi-view synthesis [36]. The two-stage approach is reminiscent of strategies used in other areas [35, 9, 3, 19] as the authors point out. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The task of explicitly conditioning image generation on both a reference image *and* a pose is a reasonable extension of existing conditional generation techniques. The use of pose heatmaps to encode pose information is a sensible and potentially effective approach.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed framework appears technically sound, leveraging well-established techniques like U-Nets and GANs. The two-stage approach seems justified by the complexity of the problem and the difficulty of generating both accurate pose and detailed appearance simultaneously. The masked L1 loss addresses a potential issue with background changes.
* **Experimental Validation:** The authors provide extensive experiments on two datasets (DeepFashion and Market-1501), demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method through both quantitative (SSIM, IS) and qualitative results. The ablation studies (different pose embeddings, loss functions, and the two-stage vs. one-stage comparison) are valuable for understanding the contribution of each component.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The architecture diagram (Figure 2) is helpful, and the explanation of each stage is clear.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to related work is somewhat limited. While [36] is mentioned, a more thorough comparison to existing methods for pose transfer or image manipulation would strengthen the paper. The authors acknowledge this in the limitations, but a more detailed discussion of why existing methods are unsuitable would be beneficial.
* **Quantitative Metrics:** The reliance on SSIM and IS as the primary quantitative metrics is questionable, as the authors themselves note. These metrics can be misleading, particularly when evaluating image realism. A user study, while included, seems somewhat limited in scope and the results are a bit contradictory (higher scores on one dataset but lower on another for the full model).
* **Failure Cases:** The discussion of failure cases is brief. Providing more detailed analysis of *why* the model fails in certain situations (e.g., complex poses, specific clothing types) would be valuable. The examples in Figure 6 are helpful, but a more in-depth discussion is needed.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention the influence of the lambda parameter, a more comprehensive analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would be beneficial. How robust is the method to different parameter settings?



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a novel and challenging problem with potential applications.
* The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated.
* The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach.
* The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the contribution of each component.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The comparison to related work is limited.
* The quantitative metrics may not fully capture the quality of the generated images.
* The discussion of failure cases is brief.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**. While there are some minor weaknesses, the novelty, technical soundness, and experimental validation are strong enough to warrant acceptance to NIPS. The two-stage approach with pose heatmaps is a clever and effective way to address the challenging problem of pose-guided person image generation. Addressing the comparison to existing work more thoroughly would further strengthen the paper, but it is already a valuable contribution to the field.
