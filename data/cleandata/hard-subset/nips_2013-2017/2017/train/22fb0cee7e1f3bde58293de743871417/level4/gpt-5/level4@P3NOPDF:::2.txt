Summary
The paper studies how to design optimal patient-specific screening policies when disease progression can be modeled as a finite-state stochastic process. The objective is to minimize a combination of screening costs and diagnostic delays. The authors propose a computationally efficient approximate solution method and evaluate it on simulated breast cancer data, showing reduced delays at comparable costs relative to an annual-screening baseline.

Strengths
- Personalized screening is an important and timely application area with clear potential impact.
- The formulation is sensible and the proposed algorithm appears technically sound and well developed.
- The empirical results suggest meaningful improvements over a simple baseline, indicating the approach could reduce delays without increasing costs.
- The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

Weaknesses
- The central assumption—that the disease model for a given patient (transition dynamics, observation model, etc.) is known—is unrealistic in practical settings. In most applications, these models must be inferred from limited, noisy, and often biased data. The main challenge in personalized screening is not solving a known model, but handling model uncertainty and learning patient-specific parameters.
- Methodological novelty appears incremental. The contribution seems to be an application of known ideas (finite-state stochastic control/MDP/POMDP-style optimization with an approximate solution) to the screening domain, rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic technique.
- The experimental evaluation is limited. Comparisons are only against a trivial annual-screening baseline. There is no analysis of robustness to model misspecification, which is crucial given the strong “known model” assumption. Without stress-testing against inaccuracies in the disease model, it is difficult to assess the practical reliability of the proposed policy.
- It is unclear how the proposed method would perform relative to risk-stratified heuristics commonly used in practice (e.g., stratification by age, family history, genetic markers, and prior imaging results with adaptive intervals), or relative to stronger algorithmic baselines (e.g., exact dynamic programming for small models, myopic policies, or rollout/imitation of clairvoyant policies).

Comments and Suggestions
- Address model uncertainty: The paper would be significantly strengthened by incorporating and evaluating robustness to misspecification. Options include:
  - Sensitivity analysis: Vary transition and observation parameters over plausible ranges and quantify degradation in performance.
  - Distributionally robust optimization: Optimize a policy against a set (ambiguity set) of models consistent with data or confidence intervals.
  - Bayesian model averaging: Maintain posterior distributions over patient-specific parameters and optimize policies under this uncertainty.
  - Off-policy evaluation under model error: Use importance sampling or doubly robust estimators on simulated cohorts to estimate real-world performance when the deployed model differs from the training model.
- Learning from data: Provide a pathway (even if preliminary) for estimating patient-level model parameters from historical screening/diagnostic data, including:
  - A two-stage approach (fit the model, then optimize the policy) and/or a joint approach (e.g., EM-style or reinforcement learning with latent states).
  - An assessment of sample complexity and the impact of limited data on policy quality.
  - Calibration checks and uncertainty quantification for the learned models.
- Baselines and ablations:
  - Compare against practical risk-stratified policies (e.g., biennial/annual schedules conditioned on risk tiers; adaptive intervals based on last test results).
  - Include stronger algorithmic baselines (e.g., optimal policies in small instances, greedy/myopic policies, rollout with short horizons).
  - Provide ablations isolating the contributions of different components of the approximation (e.g., value function approximation, heuristics).
- External validity:
  - Move beyond purely synthetic data, if possible. Semi-synthetic experiments seeded with real-world summary statistics or anonymized registry distributions would improve credibility.
  - Report clinically relevant metrics such as stage at detection, false-positive/recall rates, and cumulative radiation exposure, in addition to cost and delay.
- Practical considerations:
  - Discuss how the policy would integrate with clinical workflows, including constraints on maximum screening frequency, patient adherence, and guideline compliance.
  - Consider fairness and subgroup performance (e.g., by age, risk level), since miscalibration can differentially harm subpopulations.
- Clarity and reproducibility:
  - Include pseudocode, complexity analysis, and convergence/approximation guarantees or empirical evidence of stability.
  - Provide code and parameter settings for reproducibility.

Overall Assessment
The paper tackles an important problem and presents a reasonable, computationally efficient approach with promising initial results. However, the practical relevance is limited by the assumption of a known patient-level disease model and by the absence of robustness analyses and stronger baselines. Methodological novelty appears modest.

I encourage the authors to (1) explicitly address model uncertainty and learning from data, (2) expand the empirical evaluation to include realistic baselines and robustness to misspecification, and (3) clarify the scope and limitations of the approximation. With these improvements, the work would make a stronger case for its applicability to real-world personalized screening. In its current form, I am not convinced the contribution meets the bar for novelty and practical impact.
