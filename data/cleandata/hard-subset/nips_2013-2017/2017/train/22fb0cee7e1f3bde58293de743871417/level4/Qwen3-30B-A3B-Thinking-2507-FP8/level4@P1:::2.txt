

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing dynamic, personalized screening policies that balance screening frequency against disease detection delays. The authors model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating external information (e.g., patient self-exams) that may trigger early clinic visits. They address the computational intractability of exact optimal policies by proposing a tractable approximation method based on point-based value iteration, providing theoretical bounds on approximation error. Using a breast cancer dataset, they demonstrate that their policy reduces screening frequency by 28–68% compared to standard annual screening while maintaining equivalent detection delays—personalizing schedules based on patient risk features and dynamic test histories. The work extends prior POMDP-based screening models (e.g., [7, 9]) by relaxing restrictive Markov assumptions for disease progression and test outcomes, and accommodates non-stationary external information processes.

**Strengths**  
*Quality & Technical Soundness*: The paper rigorously formalizes the screening problem beyond prior work, explicitly handling trajectory-dependent delay costs (unlike standard POMDPs) and external information. The approximation algorithm (DPSCREEN) is theoretically grounded, with a provable error bound (Proposition 1) and computational complexity analysis. The breast cancer experiments are well-designed: they use realistic data (45,000 patients), clearly define risk groups (5% threshold), and compare against clinically relevant baselines (annual/biennial screening). The robustness analysis (Section 3.3) shows that estimation errors in model parameters have minimal impact on policy actions—a critical practical consideration often overlooked.  
*Originality*: The framework’s key novelty lies in its generality: it applies to arbitrary finite-state disease processes (e.g., dynamic survival models [11–13], first-hitting-time models [7, 14–17]), unlike prior work restricted to Markov disease processes [7, 9, 10]. The inclusion of external information as a dynamic trigger (e.g., breast lumps [25, 26]) also distinguishes it from existing models.  
*Significance*: The 28–68% reduction in screenings represents a substantial practical gain for healthcare systems, directly addressing a major cost driver in screening programs. The policy’s adaptability to disease-specific delay costs (e.g., linear for breast cancer vs. quadratic for pancreatic cancer in Fig. 2) demonstrates its potential for broad clinical impact.  
*Clarity*: The exposition is exceptionally clear, with well-structured sections, precise mathematical definitions, and a helpful graphical illustration of personalization (Fig. 1). The supplementary materials (Appendices A–F) provide complete algorithmic details and proofs, enabling reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**  
*Practical Implementation Gap*: While the paper addresses model estimation (Section 3.3), the experimental validation relies on *perfectly estimated* disease models derived from the same dataset used for policy construction. In real-world settings, model estimation from observational data is noisy and prone to errors (e.g., due to missing comorbidity data). The authors should explicitly test policy robustness to *estimation errors*—e.g., by corrupting model parameters in the breast cancer dataset and measuring performance degradation. This is critical, as the primary barrier to personalized screening is model estimation, not policy optimization (as noted in the draft review).  
*Limited Baseline Comparison*: The breast cancer results (Table 1) only contrast DPSCREEN with annual screening. Comparisons to *other personalized policies* (e.g., [10]’s Bayesian design approach for cardiac disease) would strengthen significance. While the paper correctly notes that prior methods [7, 9, 10] cannot handle their model assumptions, a brief discussion of how DPSCREEN’s results would differ from these approaches would clarify its unique value.  
*Computational Scalability*: The complexity analysis (O(TB²T²K|Y||Z|)) assumes bounded action spaces (e.g., screening intervals ≤ W). For large T or high-dimensional features (e.g., genomic data), the algorithm may become impractical. The authors should discuss strategies to mitigate this (e.g., action-space pruning or parallelization details).

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro*:  
- Groundbreaking framework for personalized screening under non-Markov disease dynamics.  
- Strong theoretical guarantees (error bounds, robustness to estimation).  
- Clinically significant results (28–68% fewer screenings with same delay).  
- Well-executed experiments with realistic data and clear presentation.  

*Con*:  
- Limited empirical validation of robustness to *real-world estimation errors*.  
- Baseline comparisons could better contextualize novelty against existing personalized methods.  

**Conclusion**  
This is a high-quality, technically rigorous paper that advances personalized screening beyond the limitations of prior POMDP-based approaches. Its contributions to methodology (general disease modeling, external information handling) and practical impact (substantial screening reduction) are compelling. The primary gap—lack of robustness testing under model estimation errors—can be addressed with minor experimental additions. Given NIPS’ emphasis on *significant* methodological advances with clear empirical validation, this work merits acceptance. The authors should revise the experiments to include robustness analysis (e.g., perturbing model parameters) to fully close the practical relevance gap.
