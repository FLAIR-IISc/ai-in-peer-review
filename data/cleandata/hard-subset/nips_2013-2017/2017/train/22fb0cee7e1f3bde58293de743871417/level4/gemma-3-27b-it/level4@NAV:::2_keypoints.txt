- Addresses optimal patient screening as decision-making under a finite-state stochastic disease model to minimize screening costs and delays.  
- Proposes a computationally efficient approximate policy and demonstrates algorithmic implementation.  
- Simulation on breast cancer data shows reduced delays while keeping costs comparable to annual screening.  
- Strength: tackles a relevant and impactful problem of personalized screening.  
- Strength: algorithm is well-articulated, sound, and computationally efficient.  
- Strength: experimental results are encouraging and the paper is clearly written.  
- Major limitation: assumes availability of a known, patient-specific disease model, limiting practical relevance.  
- Avoids the central challenge of inferring disease models from data, making the contribution largely theoretical.  
- Methodological novelty is incremental rather than substantial.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited (only compares to annual screening) and omits sensitivity to model inaccuracies.  
- Recommendation: assess robustness to model uncertainty and incorporate model inference to strengthen real-world impact.
