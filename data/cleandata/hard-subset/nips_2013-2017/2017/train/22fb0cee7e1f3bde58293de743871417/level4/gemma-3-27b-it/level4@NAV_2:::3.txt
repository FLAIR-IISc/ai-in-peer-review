This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the trade-off between screening frequency and the delay in disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and address key challenges in personalized screening, including dynamic patient features, external information impacting screening decisions, and computational intractability. This builds upon prior work in screening, such as the POMDP-based approaches of Ayer et al. [7] and Maillart et al. [8], which typically rely on Markovian disease processes and stationary test outcome models – assumptions relaxed here. The method is evaluated using a large breast cancer dataset, demonstrating substantial reductions in the number of screenings (28-68%) while maintaining comparable detection delays compared to existing clinical policies.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Generality:** The framework is highly general, accommodating a wide class of disease models (e.g., dynamic survival models [11, 12, 13, 36, 37]) and incorporating external information. This contrasts with prior work [7, 9, 10] which often impose stronger modeling assumptions.
*   **Computational Tractability:** The authors address the computational intractability of optimal policy construction by providing a computationally tractable approximation with provable error bounds (Proposition 1).
*   **Personalization:** The proposed policy demonstrably personalizes screening schedules based on both patient features and screening history, as illustrated in Figure 1 and the results.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The application to a large breast cancer dataset provides strong empirical evidence of the method’s effectiveness, showcasing significant reductions in screening frequency while preserving detection delays.
*   **Robustness Analysis:** The consideration of estimation error and its impact on policy performance (Theorem 1) adds a layer of practical relevance.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly presents the model, proposed approach, and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Model Complexity:** While the generality of the model is a strength, the complexity could limit its adoption in practical settings. The number of parameters required to define the disease and testing processes might be substantial for some applications.
*   **Assumptions:** The method relies on several assumptions, such as independence of test results given the state trajectory and patient features. The sensitivity of the results to violations of these assumptions is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:**  The evaluation is primarily focused on breast cancer. While the authors demonstrate the impact of different cost functions, expanding the experimental evaluation to other disease scenarios would strengthen the paper.
*   **Implementation Details:** More details on the implementation of the MCMC estimation for model parameters would be beneficial for reproducibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in personalized medicine – optimizing screening policies – with a novel and technically sound approach.
*   The method’s generality and computational tractability make it a valuable contribution to the field.
*   The empirical results on a large breast cancer dataset demonstrate substantial improvements over existing clinical practices.
*   The theoretical analysis (approximation error bounds, robustness to estimation error) provides a strong foundation for the proposed method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The model complexity might hinder practical adoption.
*   The assumptions made by the model could limit its applicability in some scenarios.
*   The evaluation is primarily focused on a single disease (breast cancer).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of personalized screening. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and empirically validated. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend accepting this paper to the conference.
