- Proposes DPSCREEN to compute optimal screening policies balancing screening costs against delay costs using clinical history and patient features.  
- Models disease as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and allows external arrival events (e.g., self-exams).  
- Demonstrates method on breast cancer data, achieving similar expected delay while greatly reducing the number of screens.  
- Reports expected screen reductions of ~57–68% with self-examinations and ~28–45% without, varying by risk group.  
- Analyzes both linear and quadratic delay-cost regimes and shows policy operation points change significantly with the cost function.  
- Produces personalized policies that allocate far fewer tests to low-risk patients compared to high-risk patients.  
- Provides a concise, thorough mathematical exposition and claims computational tractability; paper is well-written and technically sound.  
- Includes a robustness analysis addressing the effect of estimation error on policy performance.  
- Weakness: assumes model parameters are known, which may not hold in practice.  
- Weakness: does not provide a detailed analysis of computational complexity, raising practicality concerns.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically sound approach with real-data validation and clear potential for practical impact.  
- Arguments for rejection: reliance on known parameters and insufficient complexity analysis could limit real-world applicability.  
- Overall recommendation: accept.
