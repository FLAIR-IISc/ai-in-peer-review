- Introduces DPSCREEN: a framework for personalized screening that balances screening frequency against detection delay using a finite-state stochastic disease model with an absorbing state and external triggers (e.g., self-exams).  
- Core technical contribution: a tractable approximation of trajectory-dependent, non-Markovian optimal policies using point-based value iteration with provable error bounds.  
- Theoretical guarantees: proof of value-function convexity (Lemma 1), tight approximation error bound (Proposition 1), and robustness to model estimation errors (Theorem 1).  
- Empirical gains: evaluated on breast cancer data (45k patients), reducing screening frequency by 28–68% versus standard annual/biennial policies while maintaining equivalent expected detection delays.  
- High-risk subgroup result: 57–68% fewer screenings for high-risk patients who perform self-examinations.  
- Methodology: risk scoring with the Gail model, transition-parameter estimation via MCMC, plus ablation (with/without self-exams) and risk-group comparisons.  
- Originality/significance: relaxes Markov and stationarity assumptions from prior work, enabling application to general disease models (e.g., survival, first-hitting time) and dynamic external information.  
- Quality: rigorous theory and validation on a large real-world dataset, with convincing empirical and robustness analyses.  
- Clarity strengths: well-structured formalization and clear algorithm description; effective personalization illustration (Figure 1).  
- Clarity issues: belief-space reduction sampling strategy insufficiently motivated for non-POMDP readers; main-text pseudocode would improve reproducibility.  
- Scope/generalizability weakness: experimental validation limited to breast cancer and lacks discussion of barriers/data requirements for other diseases.  
- Medical-literature comparison weakness: misses explicit comparison to USPSTF guidelines and specific simulation-based models (e.g., MISCAN), leaving clinical-context comparison incomplete.  
- Algorithmic-transparency weakness: reports asymptotic complexity but omits typical parameter values (e.g., B) and practical runtime/parallelization details to substantiate tractability.  
- Recommendation: Accept — significant, technically sound contribution with practical impact despite limited scope and minor clarity/implementation details.  
- Pro-accept points: first framework for general disease models with external info; tight approximation bounds and robustness; large, clinically meaningful screening reductions.  
- Con-accept points: validation mainly on breast cancer and insufficient practical contextualization of algorithmic complexity.
