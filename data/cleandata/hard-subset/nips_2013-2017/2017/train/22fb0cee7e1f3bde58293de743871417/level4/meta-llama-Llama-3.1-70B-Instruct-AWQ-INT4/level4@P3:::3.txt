The paper "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening" presents a novel approach to constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The authors develop a framework that models the disease as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating the possibility of external information that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment.

The main idea of the paper is to use a dynamic programming approach to solve for an approximately optimal policy that minimizes a weighted sum of the screening cost and the delay cost. The authors show that the optimal policy can be computed using a point-based value iteration algorithm, which is a tractable method for solving partially observable Markov decision processes (POMDPs). The algorithm is tested on a large breast cancer data set, and the results show that the constructed policy leads to significant reductions in the number of screens performed while achieving the same expected delays in disease detection.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method and its advantages over existing approaches. The use of a dynamic programming approach allows for a more flexible and personalized screening policy, which can take into account the individual patient's medical history and external information. The results of the experiment on the breast cancer data set are promising and demonstrate the potential of the proposed method.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to address the problem of personalized screening in a general setting, without relying on specific models for disease progression, test outcomes, and external information. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method, making it accessible to readers from different backgrounds.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The authors assume that the model parameters are known, which may not be the case in practice. The estimation error and its impact on the policy are not fully explored in the paper. Additionally, the computational complexity of the algorithm may be a concern for large data sets or complex disease models.

In terms of related work, the authors provide a comprehensive review of existing approaches to personalized screening, including POMDP-based methods and Bayesian design-based methods. However, the comparison with other works could be more detailed, and the authors could provide more insights into how their approach differs from existing methods.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and promising approach to personalized screening, and the results of the experiment on the breast cancer data set are encouraging. However, further work is needed to address the limitations of the paper, including the estimation error and computational complexity.
