Summary
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for computing dynamic, personalized screening policies that balance screening frequency and delay in disease detection. Diseases are modeled as finite-state stochastic processes with an absorbing disease state; test outcomes and an external information process (e.g., self-exams, comorbidities) influence patient arrival and scheduling. The objective minimizes a weighted sum of discounted screening costs and delay costs that depend on the full disease trajectory, not just current state. The authors derive Bellman equations over beliefs defined on state trajectories and diagnosis status, prove the value function is convex and piecewise linear, and show that computing the exact alpha-vector solution is intractable. They introduce a tractable PBVI-style approximation that samples likely state trajectories to construct a lower-dimensional belief set and performs value backups on reachable beliefs. They provide a bound on approximation error and a robustness result indicating local constancy of optimal actions under small model perturbations.

Empirically, they estimate a two-state breast cancer model (Healthy/Disease) using a large de-identified dataset, incorporate self-exam signals via a tumor growth model, and tune the policy weight to match the expected detection delay of current clinical practice. Compared to annual screening (and, in the supplement, biennial screening), DPSCREEN reduces the expected number of screens by 28–68% while maintaining comparable expected delays. The method also adapts screening intervals to initial risk and to prior test outcomes, and it illustrates how different delay cost functions (linear vs quadratic) lead to markedly different testing regimes. The approach relates to prior POMDP-based screening for cancer [e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart; Erenay et al.] and to information-gain/bayesian design approaches [Rizopoulos; Alaa et al.], while aiming to handle more general disease processes, trajectory-dependent costs, and external information-driven arrivals.

Strengths
- Important problem: dynamic, risk- and history-personalized screening is clinically and societally relevant.
- Method integrates external information, nonstandard costs depending on trajectories, and endogenous arrival times in a unified decision-theoretic framework.
- Theoretical properties: piecewise linearity, an approximation-error bound, and a robustness result for optimal actions under small model perturbations.
- Practical promise: substantial reductions in screening burden while preserving expected detection delay; personalization by baseline risk and test history.
- Generally clear exposition with a principled optimization setup.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Reliance on an accurately specified and estimated disease/test model. Although the paper discusses estimation via MCMC and provides asymptotic/qualitative robustness, the empirical evaluation does not thoroughly test policy sensitivity to model misspecification (e.g., transition hazards, test characteristics, or the external information process).
- Baselines are limited. Comparisons are primarily to fixed annual (and in the supplement, biennial) screening; there is no comparison to other adaptive policies such as prior POMDP heuristics (myopic, QMDP), risk/density-tailored schedules from the clinical literature, or decision-support systems like ConfidentCare.
- Novelty is incremental methodologically: the approximation relies on tailored PBVI with trajectory-focused belief sampling; the conceptual advances (trajectory-dependent costs, exogenous info-driven arrivals) are valuable but build on known POMDP machinery.
- Some assumptions are strong or inconsistently treated: the theory assumes no false positives/negatives in parts, yet experiments use realistic sensitivities/specificities; independence assumptions and a single absorbing disease state may be restrictive for some diseases or multi-stage conditions.
- Evaluation limitations: outcomes are modeled rather than observed counterfactuals; there is limited reporting on uncertainty (e.g., confidence intervals for delays/screens), adherence effects, false-positive/biopsy rates, or overdiagnosis proxies. Scalability to richer state spaces and competing risks is discussed but not empirically demonstrated.

Quality
- The optimization formulation and proofs (piecewise linearity, error bound) appear sound, though core proofs are in the supplement. The construction of the belief set and the practical choices for sampling reachable beliefs could use more detail in the main text for reproducibility. Clarify the handling of test errors across theory and experiments.

Clarity
- The paper is well written overall. Suggestions: precisely state where assumptions (e.g., no false positives) hold and where they are relaxed; summarize the belief-sampling algorithm, complexity, and parameter estimation steps in the main text; detail how w is tuned and how the constraint is enforced.

Originality
- Moderate. The problem framing extends prior screening POMDPs by allowing trajectory-dependent costs and external information-driven arrivals; the algorithm tailors PBVI to a high-dimensional belief over trajectories. However, the computational approach leverages established POMDP techniques.

Significance
- Potentially high if robust in practice: substantial reductions in screening load at fixed expected delay would be impactful. Demonstrating robustness to misspecification and stronger baselines would strengthen the case for adoption.

Suggestions for improvement
- Perform systematic robustness studies: perturb transition hazards, initial risk calibration, test sensitivities/specificities, and the external information process; quantify policy performance degradation and action stability.
- Compare against stronger baselines: risk/density-tailored intervals from clinical guidelines, prior POMDP-based policies (e.g., myopic, QMDP), and recent decision-support systems (e.g., ConfidentCare).
- Report additional outcomes: false-positive/biopsy rates, downstream procedures, and variability (CIs) via bootstrap or posterior draws; analyze adherence and early-arrival behavior.
- Provide ablations on external information and belief-sampling strategies; report runtime and scalability on larger state spaces or multi-state disease models/competing risks.
- Calibrate and validate the risk model (e.g., Gail-based features) with calibration plots and subgroup analyses; discuss fairness and subgroup performance.
- Temper “first framework” claims and more explicitly position contributions relative to prior POMDP screening work.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important clinical decision problem with a unified, general framework.
- Solid theoretical underpinnings and a tractable algorithm.
- Promising empirical reductions in screening burden with maintained detection delay.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical robustness and comparative evaluation; practical relevance hinges on model accuracy.
- Methodological novelty is modest relative to existing POMDP approximations.
- Some assumptions and evaluation choices may limit external validity.

Overall assessment
An interesting and well-presented approach with encouraging results, but the paper would be significantly strengthened by more comprehensive robustness analyses and comparisons to stronger baselines to support practical relevance.
