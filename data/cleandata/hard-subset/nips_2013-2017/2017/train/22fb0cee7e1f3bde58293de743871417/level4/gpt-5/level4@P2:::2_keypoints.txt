- Proposes DPSCREEN, a POMDP-style framework for dynamic, personalized screening that balances screening cost and delay using trajectory-dependent costs and external information-driven arrivals.
- Derives Bellman equations over beliefs on full state trajectories and diagnosis status, proving the value function is convex and piecewise linear.
- Shows exact alpha-vector solution is intractable and introduces a PBVI-style approximation sampling likely state trajectories to form a reduced belief set and perform reachable-belief backups.
- Provides an approximation-error bound and a robustness result showing local constancy of optimal actions under small model perturbations.
- Empirically fits a two-state breast cancer model using large de-identified data, incorporates self-exam signals, and tunes policy weight to match current practice detection delay.
- Reports DPSCREEN reduces expected number of screens by 28–68% versus annual screening while maintaining comparable expected delays, and adapts intervals by baseline risk and test history.
- Strength: Addresses an important, clinically relevant problem of personalized, history-aware screening.
- Strength: Integrates external information, trajectory-dependent costs, and endogenous arrivals in a unified decision-theoretic model.
- Strength: Provides theoretical guarantees (piecewise linearity, error bound, robustness) and clear exposition.
- Strength: Demonstrates practical promise for reducing screening burden with personalization.
- Weakness: Heavy reliance on accurately specified and estimated disease/test models without thorough empirical sensitivity analyses to misspecification.
- Weakness: Limited baselines — lacks comparisons to adaptive heuristics (myopic, QMDP), clinical risk-tailored schedules, and decision-support systems.
- Weakness: Methodological novelty is incremental, mainly adapting known PBVI techniques to trajectory-focused beliefs.
- Weakness: Some strong or inconsistent assumptions (parts assume no test errors while experiments use realistic sensitivities/specificities; single absorbing state may be restrictive).
- Weakness: Evaluation models outcomes rather than observing counterfactuals; limited reporting of uncertainty, false positives/biopsy rates, overdiagnosis proxies, adherence effects, and scalability.
- Quality: Optimization and proofs appear sound but core proofs and belief-sampling implementation details are mostly in the supplement; clarify treatment of test errors.
- Clarity: Well written overall; recommend explicitly stating where assumptions hold, summarizing belief-sampling algorithm/complexity, and detailing w tuning and constraint enforcement.
- Originality: Moderate — extends prior POMDP screening work to trajectory-dependent costs and external arrivals but leverages established POMDP methods.
- Significance: Potentially high if robust; could meaningfully reduce screening burden, but needs stronger robustness and baseline comparisons.
- Suggestions: Perform systematic robustness studies to model perturbations, compare to stronger adaptive baselines, and report additional outcomes (CIs, false-positive/biopsy rates, adherence).
- Suggestions: Provide ablations on external information and belief-sampling, report runtime/scalability, and validate/calibrate risk models with subgroup analyses and fairness discussions.
- Arguments for acceptance: Important problem, solid theory, tractable algorithm, and promising empirical reductions in screening burden.
- Arguments against acceptance: Limited robustness and comparative evaluation, modest methodological novelty, and assumptions that may limit external validity.
- Overall: Interesting, well-presented approach with encouraging results but needs more comprehensive robustness analyses and stronger baseline comparisons to support practical relevance.
