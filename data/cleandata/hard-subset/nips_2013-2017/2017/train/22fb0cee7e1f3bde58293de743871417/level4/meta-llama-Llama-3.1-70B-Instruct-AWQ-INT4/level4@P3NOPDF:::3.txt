The paper proposes a method for predicting the optimal screening time for breast cancer patients, aiming to minimize both screening costs and delay costs. This personalized approach takes into account the individual's medical history, as well as external information. The paper is generally well-written, and the proposed method demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods.

The authors address a crucial problem in breast cancer screening, where striking a balance between early detection and unnecessary screenings is essential. By incorporating personal medical history and external information, the method provides a tailored approach to screening, which could lead to more effective and efficient screening protocols.

The paper is well-structured, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their methodology. The use of relevant literature and background information effectively sets the stage for the proposed approach. The experimental results are also clearly presented, allowing for easy comparison with existing methods.

The strengths of the paper include its innovative approach to personalized breast cancer screening and the significant improvements demonstrated over existing methods. The authors' use of a comprehensive dataset and rigorous evaluation methodology adds credibility to the results.

However, there are a few areas that could be improved upon. Firstly, the authors could provide more insight into the features used in the model and their relevance to breast cancer screening. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion on the potential limitations and challenges of implementing this method in real-world clinical settings.

Furthermore, while the paper mentions the use of external information, it would be helpful to provide more details on the types of external data used and how they are integrated into the model. This would provide a clearer understanding of the method's capabilities and limitations.

In terms of the broader impact, the proposed method has the potential to significantly improve breast cancer screening outcomes by reducing unnecessary screenings and detecting cancer at an earlier stage. The personalized approach could also lead to more targeted and effective treatment plans.

In conclusion, the paper presents a promising method for personalized breast cancer screening, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods. With some minor revisions to address the mentioned areas, the paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance of this paper, pending minor revisions.
