

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing dynamic personalized screening policies that balance screening frequency against disease detection delay. The authors model disease progression as a general finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating external information (e.g., patient self-exams) and dynamic history—addressing critical limitations in prior work. Existing approaches like Ayer et al. (2014) restrict to Markov disease models and stationary test outcomes, while DPSCREEN generalizes to non-Markov processes (e.g., dynamic survival models) and handles time-varying external information. The method provides a computationally tractable approximation of the optimal policy with rigorous error bounds (Proposition 1), validated on a large breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients). Crucially, it achieves 28–68% fewer screenings than clinical standards (annual/biennial screening) while maintaining identical expected detection delays, demonstrating significant practical impact.

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The theoretical foundation is robust. The authors rigorously characterize the optimal policy via Bellman equations, prove the value function’s convexity (Lemma 1), and derive an approximation error bound (Proposition 1). The empirical validation uses a realistic breast cancer dataset with clinically relevant risk stratification (5% prior risk threshold), and the policy’s performance (Table 1) is quantitatively compelling—reducing screenings by 28–68% without compromising detection delay. The analysis of cost-function sensitivity (Fig. 2) further strengthens the argument for disease-specific policy design.  
*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-structured, with clear notation and logical progression from problem formulation to solution. Key concepts (e.g., belief states, external information process) are precisely defined. The supplementary materials (Appendices A–F) provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including algorithm pseudocode and theoretical proofs. The graphical representation of personalization (Fig. 1) effectively illustrates how the policy adapts to patient risk and dynamic history.  
*Originality:* This is the first framework to handle *general* disease models (beyond Markov/Semi-Markov processes) with external information and dynamic history in screening policies. Prior works (e.g., Ayer et al., 2014; Rizopoulos et al., 2010) assume restrictive disease/test models, while medical literature relies on simulation-based policy comparisons (e.g., MISCAN-COLON). DPSCREEN’s generality and approximation guarantees fill a critical gap.  
*Significance:* The results are highly impactful for healthcare practice. Reducing screenings by 28–68% directly lowers costs for patients and systems while maintaining clinical efficacy. The method’s flexibility for different diseases (e.g., quadratic vs. linear delay costs for pancreatic vs. breast cancer) enables tailored implementation. This advances the state of the art beyond the "one-size-fits-all" screening approaches prevalent in clinical guidelines (e.g., USPSTF).

**Weaknesses:**  
While the framework is general, the disease model is limited to *finite-state* processes (e.g., two-state breast cancer model). For complex diseases with continuous state spaces (e.g., tumor growth dynamics), the approximation quality would require additional validation. The synthetic disease comparison (Fig. 2) is insightful but lacks concrete examples of how cost functions would be selected for specific diseases beyond the breast cancer case. The implementation details (e.g., computational complexity trade-offs in Algorithm 1) could be expanded to address scalability for larger populations.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:* Groundbreaking generalization of screening policies; rigorous error bounds; strong empirical results (28–68% screening reduction); clear clinical relevance; well-structured for reproducibility.  
*Con:* Finite-state disease model constraint; limited discussion of cost-function selection for new diseases; computational details could be more explicit.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This work makes a substantial, well-executed contribution to the field. It addresses a critical gap in personalized screening with a novel, theoretically sound framework validated on real-world data. The results are not only statistically significant but also clinically meaningful, offering a clear path toward optimizing healthcare resource allocation. The paper’s clarity and thoroughness align perfectly with NIPS’ standards for high-impact, reproducible research.
