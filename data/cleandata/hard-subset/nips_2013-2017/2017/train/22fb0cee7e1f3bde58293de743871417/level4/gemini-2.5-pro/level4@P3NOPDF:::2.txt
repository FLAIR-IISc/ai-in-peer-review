### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of optimizing patient screening schedules by modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process. The goal is to derive a policy that minimizes a combination of screening costs and delays in diagnosis. Recognizing the computational complexity of finding an exact optimal policy, the authors propose a computationally efficient approximate solution. The method is evaluated on simulated data for breast cancer, where it is shown to reduce diagnostic delays compared to a standard annual screening baseline while maintaining the same cost.

### Strengths

1.  **Problem Importance:** The paper tackles a problem of significant societal importance. Developing personalized and adaptive screening policies has the potential to improve patient outcomes and optimize the allocation of healthcare resources. The application of machine learning and optimization to this domain is both timely and valuable.

2.  **Sound Formulation:** The proposed algorithmic approach is sound and well-motivated. The formulation of the problem within a stochastic process framework is clear, and the approximation strategy developed to overcome the computational challenges is sensible.

3.  **Promising Results:** The experimental results, though conducted on simulated data, are promising. They effectively illustrate the potential benefits of a personalized policy over a fixed, one-size-fits-all screening schedule like annual check-ups.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The problem, proposed method, and results are presented clearly.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Assumption of a Known Disease Model:** A significant limitation of the current work is its core assumption that the patient-specific disease model (i.e., the parameters of the stochastic process) is known *a priori*. In any practical clinical setting, this model would be unknown and would need to be inferred from population-level data, patient-specific covariates, and historical observations. The paper sidesteps what is arguably the most challenging aspect of personalized screening: learning or inferring these models under uncertainty. By assuming the model is given, the work feels more like an academic exercise in planning, addressing only one part of a much larger end-to-end problem. This assumption significantly limits the claimed practical relevance of the work.

2.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The methodological novelty of the proposed approximation algorithm appears to be modest. While its application to this specific problem is new, the underlying techniques could be more clearly situated within the existing literature on approximate dynamic programming or Partially Observable Markov Decision Processes (POMDPs) to better highlight the specific contributions. As it stands, the contribution seems to be more in the application of existing methods rather than the development of a new one.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation could be substantially improved in two key ways:
    *   **Baselines:** The proposed method is only compared against a single, simple baseline (annual screening). To provide a stronger case for the method's effectiveness, it should be compared against other, more sophisticated baselines from the medical or operations research literature.
    *   **Robustness:** More critically, the experiments do not assess the robustness of the proposed policy to inaccuracies in the disease model. Given the difficulty of obtaining a perfect model in practice (as mentioned in point 1), a crucial question is: how much does the policy's performance degrade if the true disease dynamics differ from the model used for optimization? A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of model misspecification is essential for convincing readers of the method's practical utility.

### Conclusion

Overall, this paper addresses an important and timely problem with a reasonable and well-explained approach. However, its potential impact is currently limited by the strong assumption of a known disease model, which distances the work from practical application. The modest methodological novelty and the lack of a robustness analysis in the experiments are also significant concerns.

To be a strong candidate for publication at this venue, the paper would need to substantially strengthen its contribution. This could be achieved by either addressing the model uncertainty problem more directly (e.g., by integrating model learning with planning) or, at a minimum, by providing a thorough experimental analysis of the policy's robustness to model misspecification. Furthermore, a more comprehensive comparison against stronger baselines is necessary to convincingly demonstrate the method's advantages.
