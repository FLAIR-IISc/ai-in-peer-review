- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized screening that trades off screening frequency against disease detection delay.  
- Applicable to arbitrary finite-state disease processes (not limited to Markov models).  
- Incorporates dynamic patient history and external information (e.g., self-examinations) triggering visits.  
- Provides a computationally tractable approximation to the optimal policy with provable error bounds.  
- Validated on a large real-world breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients) showing 28–68% fewer screenings for equivalent expected detection delay.  
- Extends prior POMDP-based screening work by removing stationarity/Markov assumptions and adding external-info handling.  
- Rigorous mathematical foundation: value function shown convex and piecewise-linear, enabling the approximation method.  
- Thorough computational complexity analysis and a tight approximation error bound (Proposition 1).  
- Empirical evaluation is strong, statistically significant, and includes robustness to model estimation errors (Theorem 1).  
- Clear writing, consistent notation, well-organized exposition, and reproducible supplementary materials.  
- Original contribution: first to handle arbitrary finite-state disease models with external information processes.  
- Shows importance of cost modeling by comparing linear vs. quadratic delay costs and their policy effects.  
- High practical significance: potential to reduce healthcare costs and patient burden while maintaining outcomes.  
- Strengths summarized: novel framework, solid theory, large-scale empirical validation, clear practical impact, comprehensive comparisons, effective personalization illustrations.  
- Weaknesses noted: approximation method limitations could be discussed more explicitly; single-disease (breast cancer) empirical focus; some supplementary technical details may need clearer exposition for non-experts.  
- Arguments for acceptance: technical novelty, sound theory with bounds, strong empirical impact, clear presentation, and real-world relevance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: empirical scope limited to one disease and some technical depth may require specialist expertise.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept — technically sound, well-presented, and practically significant contribution.
