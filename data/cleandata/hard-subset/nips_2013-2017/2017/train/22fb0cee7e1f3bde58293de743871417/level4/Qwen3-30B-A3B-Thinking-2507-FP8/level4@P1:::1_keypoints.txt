- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized screening that trades off screening frequency vs. detection delay.  
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating patient-specific features and dynamic external triggers.  
- Key innovation is a computationally tractable approximation to the optimal policy with rigorous approximation-error bounds.  
- Provides solid theoretical development: Bellman equations, proof that the value function is convex and piecewise-linear, enabling point-based value iteration.  
- Proposition 1 gives a concrete bound on performance loss relative to the optimal policy.  
- Validated on a 45,000-patient breast cancer dataset, showing 28–68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining equivalent expected detection delays.  
- Empirical analyses show clear personalization across risk groups and sensitivity to different delay cost functions (linear vs. quadratic).  
- Strong comparison to prior work and discussion of robustness to model estimation errors (Theorem 1), increasing real-world applicability.  
- Minor weaknesses: computational complexity scaling is not demonstrated with concrete dataset-size examples, paper focuses only on breast cancer, and empirical validation of the approximation-error bound is limited.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, original, practically significant contribution recommended for acceptance.
