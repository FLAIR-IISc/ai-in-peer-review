This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for computing dynamic, personalized screening policies that balance test frequency against detection delay. The disease is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; observations include both clinical test results and an external information process (e.g., self-examination, comorbidities) that can trigger unscheduled visits. The objective minimizes a weighted sum of discounted screening costs and trajectory-dependent delay costs. Exact optimization via belief-state dynamic programming is intractable; the authors prove convexity/piecewise linearity of the value function, then develop a point-based approximation (PBVI-style) that samples likely state trajectories and reachable beliefs to construct an approximately optimal policy with a bound on approximation error. They also show a form of robustness: near-identical recommended actions under small model-estimation errors. Applied to a large breast cancer dataset (45k patients), the method yields substantial reductions in screening frequency (28–68%) while maintaining the same expected detection delay as standard annual/biennial schedules. The paper further illustrates how delay-cost shape (linear vs quadratic) changes the recommended regime.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to POMDP-based screening (e.g., personalized mammography and colorectal screening), Bayesian-design approaches for biomarker scheduling, medical simulation frameworks (e.g., MISCAN-COLON), and controlled sensing. It claims broader generality by accommodating external information, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and decision epochs that depend on actions and exogenous arrivals, beyond standard Markov assumptions and stationary observation models used in many prior screening models.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation with explicit modeling of external information and trajectory-dependent delay costs.
- Solid theoretical development: Bellman equation, piecewise-linear convex value function, alpha-vector perspective, complexity discussion.
- Practical approximate algorithm tailored to high-dimensional beliefs via sampled trajectories and reachable beliefs, with an explicit approximation-error bound.
- Robustness argument showing local constancy of the optimal policy under small model perturbations.
- Extensive application on a large real-world dataset with clinically meaningful gains (fewer screens at matched expected delay), and demonstration of personalization by risk and history.
- Insightful sensitivity to cost shape (linear vs quadratic), emphasizing disease-specific policy tuning.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Evaluation is model-based: policies are assessed using a learned disease model rather than off-policy causal evaluation. Consider reporting additional robustness/sensitivity analyses to model misspecification (transition hazards, test accuracy, external information dynamics, discount factor).
- Some theoretical results assume perfect tests (no false positives/negatives) and a single absorbing disease state; practical deployments face test errors and multi-stage disease. Discuss how violations affect performance and how to incorporate multiple tests and imperfect accuracy into the guarantees.
- External information is instantiated via a specific tumor growth/self-exam model; this may be simplistic and condition-specific. Provide ablations across alternative external-information models and thresholds; report how early-arrival behavior affects outcomes.
- Scalability: the stated complexity can still be high; include runtime, hardware, and scaling curves (K, |B̄|, T, |Y|, |Z|), and compare to strong baselines (e.g., risk-tiered intervals, ConfidentCare, Bayesian-design/GLMM approaches, recent POMDP approximations).
- Generalizability: empirical study is limited to women aged 60–65 and a two-state disease model. Broader age ranges, multi-state progression, and comorbidities would strengthen claims.
- Policy selection: w is tuned to match the delay of existing clinical policies. Clarify the mapping between w and clinically interpretable constraints; report the Pareto frontier and uncertainty intervals for E[N], E[Δ|R], and E[Δ|R,D].
- Clarity: the belief construction and reachable-belief sampling could use more intuition and diagrams in the main text; move key pseudo-code from the supplement; streamline notation where possible.

Quality
- Technically sound overall, with clear derivations and principled approximations. Some assumptions (perfect tests in bounds) are strong; empirical robustness mitigates but does not eliminate concerns.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The main algorithm and belief-construction steps would benefit from more concrete examples and runtime details in the main paper.

Originality
- The combination of external information, trajectory-based delay costs, and action/endogenous-timing-dependent decision epochs in a unified, approximately solved framework is novel relative to prior screening work.

Significance
- Promising practical impact: large reductions in screening burden without sacrificing detection timeliness, and a general methodology applicable to multiple diseases given appropriate models.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a principled, general framework.
- Theoretical contributions (structure, approximation bound, robustness).
- Strong empirical results on a large dataset; clear personalization benefits.
- Connection to and extension of existing POMDP literature.

Cons against acceptance
- Model-based evaluation without causal OPE; limited sensitivity analyses.
- Some assumptions limit realism in theory; unclear scalability details.
- Empirical scope restricted to a narrow cohort and two-state disease model.
- Comparisons to alternative personalized screening systems could be expanded.

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed paper with both methodological and applied contributions. With added analyses on robustness, scalability, and broader baselines, it would be even stronger.
