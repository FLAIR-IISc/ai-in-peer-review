- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies for general finite-state disease models with endogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent costs.
- Objective balances screening frequency/cost against detection delay; exact optimal policy is intractable, leading to Bellman equations and piecewise-linear value functions.
- Proposes a tractable point-based value iteration (PBVI)-style approximation using sampled trajectory-based belief sets with a bound tied to belief sampling density.
- Provides a robustness result: optimal recommended actions are locally insensitive to small model-estimation errors.
- Empirical results on a 45k-patient breast cancer dataset show 28–68% fewer screenings at matched expected detection delay vs. annual (and biennial) policies.
- Synthetic experiments illustrate how linear vs. quadratic delay costs change screening behavior.
- Positions DPSCREEN relative to POMDP and Bayesian design work, highlighting relaxations of Markov/test-independence assumptions and inclusion of endogenous arrivals.
- Claims novelty in integrating external endogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent delay costs into a partially observed control framework.
- Strength: clear, clinically relevant formulation that personalizes by baseline features and evolving history/external signals.
- Strength: solid theoretical framing (Bellman, alpha-vectors, piecewise-linear value functions) and an explicit (though high-level) approximation bound.
- Strength: empirical demonstration on large real-world data with interpretable gains and exploration of disease-specific delay-cost effects.
- Weakness: several theoretical results assume perfect test accuracy or no false positives—needs clarification where this holds and extension to realistic error rates.
- Weakness: evaluation is model-based; calls for sensitivity analyses to transition hazards, test sensitivity/specificity, external signal parameters, and belief-sampling density, plus confidence intervals.
- Weakness: empirical instantiation uses a simple two-state Markov model; should include non-Markov/semi-Markov simulations to demonstrate generality and scalability.
- Weakness: computational reporting is incomplete—need wall-clock time, parallel resources, typical K and belief-set sizes, effect of max follow-up W, and an ablation on belief-set construction.
- Weakness: tuning weight w to match benchmarks raises sensitivity concerns; recommend Pareto frontiers (delay vs. tests) with confidence bands.
- Weakness: additional baselines (POMDP policies, density/risk-based intervals, ConfidentCare) should be included to contextualize gains.
- Quality: technical development is sound but approximation bound depends on an unspecified constant ζ; suggest instantiating ζ or empirical proxies for approximation error.
- Clarity: well written but heavy notation—suggest a schematic timeline and a worked example of belief updates and a consolidated assumptions subsection.
- Significance: high potential impact for personalized screening across diseases and tests; framework is extensible.
- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, solid theory, scalable approximation, strong empirical evidence on large dataset with meaningful reductions.
- Arguments for rejection: strong theoretical assumptions (test accuracy), model-based evaluation with limited uncertainty quantification, and simple empirical disease model undermining generality.
- Questions for authors: quantify runtime/scalability and sensitivity to K and |B̄[t]|; performance with false negatives; selection/sensitivity of ỹ and σ; provide Pareto curves with CIs and additional baselines.
