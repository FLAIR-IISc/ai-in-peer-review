

The paper "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening" proposes a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the frequency of medical screenings against the delay in disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporate external information processes (e.g., patient self-examinations), and develop a computationally tractable method to construct approximately optimal screening policies. The approach extends beyond previous work in personalized screening by accommodating more general disease models, including dynamic survival processes and non-stationary test outcome models, which were restricted in prior POMDP-based approaches [7, 9, 10].

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a unified framework for personalized screening that accounts for both patient features (e.g., age, family history) and dynamic history (previous test results and external information). Second, it establishes theoretical bounds on the approximation error when using their computationally efficient policy instead of the exact optimal solution. Third, it demonstrates substantial reductions (28-68%) in screening frequency compared to standard clinical policies while maintaining equivalent expected delays in disease detection, using a large breast cancer dataset.

The paper excels in clarity and organization. The problem formulation is precise, the mathematical development is well-structured, and the experimental design is thoughtful. The authors effectively illustrate how their approach personalizes screening based on both patient risk profiles and dynamic health history through Figure 1. The supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including algorithmic pseudocode and implementation specifics.

The quality of the work is strong, with sound theoretical foundations and comprehensive experimental validation. The authors properly address the limitations of their approach by acknowledging the computational intractability of exact solutions and providing meaningful approximation error bounds. The experimental section carefully evaluates the policy across different risk groups and disease models, demonstrating how cost function choices affect screening frequency.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on personalized screening. While prior work has used POMDPs for specific disease models [7, 9], this paper generalizes to a much broader class of disease models, including dynamic survival processes and non-stationary test outcome processes. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from previous works in Section 5, particularly in handling external information processes and non-Markovian disease progression.

The significance of the work is substantial. Personalized screening has the potential to reduce healthcare costs and patient burden while maintaining or improving health outcomes. The paper demonstrates that their method can achieve significant reductions in screening frequency without compromising detection timeliness, which is particularly valuable given the high costs and potential harms of frequent screenings. The approach could be adapted to various diseases beyond breast cancer, as the authors demonstrate through their synthetic experiments with different cost structures.

However, the paper has notable limitations that warrant consideration. The most significant concern is the assumption that the disease model is known with certainty, which is rarely the case in clinical practice. The authors acknowledge the need for model estimation from observational data but do not adequately address how model uncertainty affects the screening policy. This is a critical gap, as the primary challenge in implementing personalized screening is not deriving the optimal policy given a model, but accurately estimating the disease model from real-world data. The experiments only compare against annual screening policies without examining how their method performs when the disease model is misspecified or estimated from limited data.

The methodological novelty, while present, is not particularly groundbreaking. The approach builds on standard POMDP techniques with some adaptations for the specific screening context. The computational efficiency claims are reasonable, but the paper could better articulate how their approach compares to existing scalable POMDP solution methods.

For acceptance consideration, I note the following arguments:

PROS:
- Addresses an important clinical problem with significant potential impact
- Provides a theoretically grounded solution with approximation error bounds
- Demonstrates substantial improvements over standard clinical practices
- Well-written with clear exposition and comprehensive experimental validation
- Extends the scope of personalized screening to more general disease models

CONS:
- Fails to adequately address the critical issue of model uncertainty in clinical practice
- The assumption of known disease models is unrealistic for practical implementation
- Limited comparison to alternative personalized screening methods beyond annual screening
- Methodological novelty is incremental rather than transformative

The paper presents a promising approach to personalized screening that advances the state of the art in modeling and policy construction. However, the lack of analysis regarding model uncertainty significantly limits its practical relevance. For this work to have broader impact, the authors would need to demonstrate how their approach performs when the underlying disease model is estimated from data, including the sensitivity of the screening policy to model estimation errors.

Given the strong theoretical foundation and promising empirical results, I recommend the paper for acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the model uncertainty issue in future work. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on personalized healthcare, and the methodological approach could be adapted to other sequential decision problems in healthcare. The authors should consider expanding their discussion of model estimation and robustness in the revised version.
