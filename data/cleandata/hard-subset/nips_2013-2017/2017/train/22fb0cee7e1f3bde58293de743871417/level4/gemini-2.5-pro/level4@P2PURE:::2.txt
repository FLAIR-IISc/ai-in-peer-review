This paper addresses the problem of determining an optimal patient screening policy by modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process. The objective of the policy is to minimize a combination of screening costs and diagnostic delays. To this end, the authors propose a computationally efficient algorithm to find an approximate solution. Experiments on simulated breast cancer data demonstrate that the proposed algorithm can reduce diagnostic delays at a comparable cost to a simple annual screening baseline.

Positives:
+ The problem of personalized screening is timely and important.
+ The proposed algorithmic approach is sound and well-developed.
+ The empirical results presented are promising.
+ The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

Negatives:
- The primary weakness is the strong assumption that the patient-specific disease model is known a priori. This assumption is unlikely to hold in any practical setting, which frames the work as more of a theoretical exercise. The central challenge in personalized screening lies in inferring these patient-specific models from data, a problem this paper does not address.
- The methodological novelty appears to be limited.
- The experimental evaluation is insufficient. The proposed method is only compared against a simple baseline of annual screening. Crucially, given the reliance on a known model, the experiments do not investigate the robustness of the resulting policy to inaccuracies in this model. An analysis of the policy's sensitivity to model uncertainty is essential for assessing its potential practical value.

Overall, the paper tackles an interesting problem with a sound algorithm. However, the limited methodological novelty and, more significantly, the strong assumption of a known disease model weaken the practical relevance of the work. The paper would need to better justify its contributions, perhaps by addressing the critical issue of model uncertainty, to convince the reader of its practical impact.
