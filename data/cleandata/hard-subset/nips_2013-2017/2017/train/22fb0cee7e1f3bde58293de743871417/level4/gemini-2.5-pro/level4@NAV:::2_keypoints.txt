- Tackles personalized screening, an important and timely problem.
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process to derive cost-delay–minimizing screening policies.
- Proposes a computationally tractable approximate solution that is logically sound and well developed.
- Initial simulated breast cancer results are encouraging, showing reduced diagnostic delays without increased costs versus baseline.
- Manuscript is clearly written and easy to follow.
- Core assumption that each patient’s disease model is known a priori is unrealistic and undermines practical applicability.
- The work sidesteps the central challenge of learning patient-specific models from limited data.
- Methodological novelty is incremental.
- Experimental evaluation is limited—only compared to a simple annual screening baseline.
- No robustness or sensitivity analysis to model misspecification; fails to test performance under parameter uncertainty.
- Overall: important problem and sound approach, but limited impact unless the gap to real-world applicability and model inference is addressed.
