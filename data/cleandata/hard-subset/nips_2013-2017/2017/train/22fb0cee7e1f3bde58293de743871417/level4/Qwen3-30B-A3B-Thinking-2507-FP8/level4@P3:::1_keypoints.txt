- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized screening that adapts to patient features and clinical history.  
- Novelty: general approach applicable to a wide range of disease models (including non‑Markovian), not tied to restrictive progression assumptions.  
- Formulates personalized screening as a constrained optimization balancing screening costs against detection delay.  
- Proposes a computationally tractable approximation algorithm (DPSCREEN) with provable bounds on approximation error.  
- Empirical validation on a large breast cancer dataset (45k patients, 84k mammograms) showing 28–68% reduction in screening frequency while maintaining timeliness.  
- Provides rigorous mathematical foundations, proving properties of the value function (Lemma 1) and bounding approximation error (Proposition 1).  
- Thorough experimental methodology with careful model estimation, appropriate metrics, and robustness checks to model estimation errors.  
- Clear, well-structured exposition with helpful figures (notably Figure 1) and precise writing targeted to decision-theory/ML readers.  
- Original conceptual advance: handles time-dependent decision epochs and trajectory-dependent delay costs where standard POMDPs fail.  
- Demonstrates practical significance: potential to reduce healthcare costs without compromising outcomes, and insights on cost-structure effects (linear vs quadratic).  
- Strength: general framework that can accommodate external information (e.g., self-exams) triggering visits.  
- Weakness: computational complexity analysis could use more concrete scaling examples for large problems.  
- Weakness: paper could better clarify limitations when the disease model is highly non‑stationary.  
- Weakness: some technical details (belief space construction, Algorithm 1) need clearer explanation for readers unfamiliar with POMDPs.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theory, substantial empirical impact, well-written, and advances state of the art in personalized screening.  
- Arguments against acceptance: focus is empirically narrow on breast cancer and potential scalability challenges for very large implementations.  
- Overall recommendation: technically sound, practically significant, and strongly recommended for acceptance.
