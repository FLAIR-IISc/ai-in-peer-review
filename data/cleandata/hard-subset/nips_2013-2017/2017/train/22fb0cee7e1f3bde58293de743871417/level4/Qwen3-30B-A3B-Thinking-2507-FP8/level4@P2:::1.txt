

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the frequency of medical screenings against the delay in disease detection. The authors address a critical gap in existing literature by developing a method applicable to a broad class of disease models (not restricted to Markov processes), incorporating dynamic patient history and external information (such as patient self-examinations), and providing a computationally tractable approximation to the optimal policy. The work is validated on a large breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients) from the Athena Health Network, demonstrating significant efficiency gains—reducing screening frequency by 28-68% while maintaining equivalent expected detection delays compared to current clinical practices.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of personalized healthcare decision-making. It relates directly to previous NIPS work on POMDPs for screening (e.g., Ayer et al. 2013, Alaa et al. 2016), but extends these approaches by addressing key limitations: 1) existing methods assume stationary Markov disease processes and test outcome models, while DPSCREEN accommodates arbitrary finite state stochastic processes; 2) previous work does not incorporate external information that triggers early patient visits; 3) the authors provide computationally tractable solutions with approximation error bounds, whereas prior methods are intractable for realistic problem sizes.

The paper excels in Quality: The mathematical foundation is rigorous, with careful definitions of the disease model, external information processes, and screening policies. The authors prove that the value function is convex and piecewise linear, enabling their approximate solution method. The computational complexity analysis is thorough, and they provide a tight bound on the approximation error (Proposition 1). The experimental validation is strong, using a large real-world dataset with appropriate metrics (expected number of screenings, expected delay conditional on risk group), and the results are statistically significant. The paper also demonstrates robustness to model estimation errors (Theorem 1), enhancing confidence in practical implementation.

Clarity is excellent: The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions and logical flow from problem formulation to solution to validation. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The graphical representation of personalized screening (Figure 1) effectively illustrates how the policy adapts to patient features and history. The discussion of the breast cancer application is particularly accessible to non-experts.

Originality is strong: While some prior work (e.g., Ayer et al. 2013, Rizopoulos et al. 2012) has addressed personalized screening, DPSCREEN is the first to handle arbitrary finite state disease models with external information processes. The authors demonstrate this through a comprehensive comparison with existing approaches in Section 5, showing how their framework overcomes specific limitations of prior methods (e.g., inability to handle non-Markov disease processes or external information). The paper also explores how different delay cost functions (linear vs. quadratic) yield substantially different screening policies, highlighting the importance of appropriate cost modeling for specific diseases.

Significance is high: The potential practical impact is substantial, as personalized screening could significantly reduce healthcare costs and patient burden while maintaining clinical outcomes. The demonstrated 28-68% reduction in screenings represents a meaningful improvement over current clinical practices (annual or biennial screening). The framework is broadly applicable across diseases, as evidenced by their synthetic experiments across different cost structures. The paper also provides a valuable methodological contribution that could influence future work in personalized healthcare decision-making.

Strengths:
- Novel framework that extends beyond previous POMDP-based screening approaches
- Rigorous theoretical foundation with approximation error bounds
- Strong empirical validation on large real-world dataset
- Clear demonstration of practical impact (significant screening reduction)
- Comprehensive comparison with related work that highlights key advances
- Effective illustration of personalization through Figure 1

Weaknesses:
- The paper could more explicitly discuss limitations of their approximation method (though Proposition 1 provides error bounds)
- The breast cancer application, while well-executed, could be complemented with additional disease examples to demonstrate broader applicability
- Some technical details in the supplementary materials might benefit from additional clarification for non-specialist readers

Arguments for acceptance:
1. The paper makes a significant technical contribution by extending personalized screening to a much broader class of disease models
2. The theoretical foundation is sound with appropriate error bounds
3. The empirical results demonstrate substantial practical impact (28-68% reduction in screenings)
4. The paper is well-written and clearly organized
5. The work addresses an important problem with clear relevance to healthcare applications

Arguments against acceptance:
1. The scope is limited to a single disease application (breast cancer), though the framework is generalizable
2. Some technical details may require expertise to fully appreciate (though this is reasonable for NIPS)

Overall, DPSCREEN represents a high-quality contribution that advances both the technical understanding of personalized screening policies and their practical implementation. The paper meets all four NIPS criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) at a high level. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, significantly extends previous approaches, and has clear potential for real-world impact in healthcare. I strongly recommend acceptance.
