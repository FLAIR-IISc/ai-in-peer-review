## Review of NIPS 2017 Paper: [Paper Title - Please Insert]

This paper addresses the important problem of optimal patient screening policies, aiming to minimize both screening costs and delays in disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and propose an approximate, computationally efficient algorithm to determine the best screening policy given a patient’s disease model. Experiments on simulated breast cancer data demonstrate promising results, suggesting the algorithm can reduce delays while maintaining comparable costs to a fixed annual screening baseline.

**Strengths:**

The topic of personalized screening is highly relevant and warrants significant research attention. The proposed algorithm is well-developed and presented in a clear, logical manner. The results obtained on simulated data are encouraging, indicating the potential of this approach. Furthermore, the paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary limitation of this work lies in its strong reliance on the assumption of a known disease model for each patient. While mathematically convenient, this assumption severely restricts the practical applicability of the proposed algorithm. The core challenge in personalized screening is not *solving* for an optimal policy *given* a known model, but rather *inferring* the underlying disease model from limited and noisy patient data. This paper does not address this crucial inference problem. 

The methodological novelty presented is also relatively modest. While the algorithm provides a reasonable approach to policy optimization, it builds upon established dynamic programming techniques and does not introduce a fundamentally new algorithmic contribution. 

Finally, the experimental evaluation lacks breadth. Comparing the proposed algorithm solely to a fixed annual screening baseline is insufficient, particularly in light of the aforementioned assumption about known disease models. A more compelling evaluation would investigate the robustness of the policy to inaccuracies and uncertainties within the disease model. Specifically, the paper should explore how sensitive the optimal policy is to errors in model parameters or even the selection of an incorrect model structure. Understanding this sensitivity is critical for assessing the practical viability of the approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile investigation into optimal screening policies under idealized conditions. The proposed algorithm is sound and offers a potentially beneficial approach within the confines of the assumed disease model. However, the lack of consideration for the crucial model inference problem and the limited experimental evaluation significantly detract from the paper’s practical relevance. 

To strengthen this work, the authors should consider the following:

* **Address Model Inference:** Acknowledge the limitations of the known-model assumption and discuss potential strategies for incorporating model inference into the framework. This could include outlining future research directions or briefly exploring the impact of model misspecification.
* **Robustness Analysis:** Conduct experiments to assess the sensitivity of the policy to inaccuracies in the disease model. This is arguably the most important factor in determining the algorithm's real-world applicability. Consider introducing noise into model parameters or evaluating performance across a range of plausible models.
* **Comparison to More Sophisticated Baselines:** Compare the proposed algorithm to more adaptive baselines that attempt to learn or update the disease model over time.

Currently, the paper feels more like a theoretical exploration than a contribution with immediate practical impact. While the topic is interesting and the algorithm is reasonable, substantial revisions are needed to convincingly demonstrate the real-world relevance of this work. I recommend a borderline accept/reject decision, contingent upon significant revisions addressing the concerns outlined above.
