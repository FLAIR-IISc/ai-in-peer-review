This paper addresses the important problem of personalized screening policies, aiming to balance the costs of testing against the risks of delayed disease detection. The authors propose a framework modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and develop a computationally tractable algorithm for constructing approximately optimal screening policies. Their approach accounts for both patient-specific features and the history of screening results. Experiments using a breast cancer dataset demonstrate promising results, showing potential reductions in screening frequency while maintaining comparable detection delays.

The paper is well-written and clearly presents a reasonable and well-developed algorithm. The topic of personalized screening is undoubtedly important, and the authors have made a valuable contribution by proposing a solution within a general stochastic framework. The reported results are encouraging and suggest the potential for improved screening strategies.

However, a significant limitation is the strong assumption of known disease models. While the algorithm effectively optimizes policy *given* a model, the far more challenging practical problem lies in accurately inferring these models from patient data. The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t sufficiently address how their framework could integrate with model estimation techniques, diminishing its immediate practical relevance. The methodological novelty is also incremental; while the combination of elements is well-executed, it doesn’t represent a radical departure from existing decision-making approaches. 

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited. Comparing against a fixed annual screening baseline is a good starting point, but a more compelling analysis would investigate the algorithm’s robustness to inaccuracies in the underlying disease model. Specifically, evaluating the sensitivity of the constructed policy to model uncertainty – i.e., how much does performance degrade with imperfect model estimation? – would significantly strengthen the paper’s conclusions.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and relevant problem in healthcare.
*   Presents a well-defined framework and a computationally feasible algorithm.
*   Demonstrates promising results in a realistic setting.
*   The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Relies on a strong and potentially unrealistic assumption of known disease models.
*   Methodological novelty is relatively incremental.
*   Experimental evaluation lacks a robustness analysis regarding model uncertainty.
*   Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid technical contribution to the area of personalized screening. However, the assumption of known disease models and the limited robustness analysis raise concerns about its practical impact. While the algorithm is sound, the path to real-world deployment requires addressing the critical challenge of model inference and demonstrating resilience to model inaccuracies. I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon the authors addressing the limitations outlined above—specifically, a discussion of how model estimation can be incorporated and a preliminary analysis of policy sensitivity to model uncertainty.
