- Proposes DPSCREEN: a framework for dynamic, personalized screening that trades off test frequency against detection delay using a finite-state disease model with an external information process.  
- Objective minimizes a weighted sum of discounted screening costs and trajectory-dependent delay costs.  
- Exact belief-state dynamic programming is intractable; authors prove value-function convexity and piecewise linearity (alpha-vector view).  
- Develop a PBVI-style point-based approximation that samples likely trajectories and reachable beliefs, with an explicit approximation-error bound.  
- Show local robustness: recommended actions are near-identical under small model-estimation errors.  
- Empirical application on a 45k-patient breast cancer dataset yields 28–68% fewer screens while matching expected detection delay.  
- Demonstrate that delay-cost shape (linear vs quadratic) substantially alters recommended screening regimes.  
- Situates work relative to POMDP-based screening, Bayesian-design, simulation frameworks, and controlled sensing, claiming broader generality.  
- Strength: clear problem formulation including external information and trajectory-dependent delay costs.  
- Strength: solid theoretical development (Bellman equation, piecewise-linear convexity, complexity discussion).  
- Strength: practical approximation algorithm tailored to high-dimensional beliefs with an error bound.  
- Strength: robustness argument and extensive real-world results showing personalization benefits.  
- Weakness: evaluation is model-based; lacks off-policy causal evaluation and comprehensive robustness/sensitivity analyses to model misspecification.  
- Weakness: some theoretical results assume perfect tests and a single absorbing state; need discussion on imperfect tests and multi-stage disease.  
- Weakness: external-information instantiation (tumor growth/self-exam) may be simplistic; request ablations across alternative models and thresholds.  
- Weakness: scalability concerns remain; request runtime, hardware, scaling curves (K, |B̄|, T, |Y|, |Z|) and comparisons to strong baselines.  
- Weakness: empirical scope limited to women aged 60–65 and a two-state disease model, limiting generalizability.  
- Weakness: policy selection via weight w needs clearer clinical mapping; ask for Pareto frontier and uncertainty intervals for E[N], E[Δ|R], E[Δ|R,D].  
- Clarity: paper is well-written but belief construction, reachable-belief sampling, and key pseudo-code should be clarified and moved into the main text.  
- Originality: novel unified treatment of external information, trajectory-based delay costs, and action/endogenous-timing-dependent decision epochs.  
- Significance: promising practical impact—substantial screening reductions without sacrificing detection timeliness.  
- Overall: technically sound and well-executed with meaningful empirical gains; would be stronger with added robustness, scalability, and broader-baseline analyses.
