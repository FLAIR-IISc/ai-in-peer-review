- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized dynamic screening that balances screening costs against delayed disease detection and generalizes POMDPs.
- Model supports non-Markovian disease progression, an external information process (e.g., self-exams), and trajectory-dependent cost based on onset and detection times.
- Exact optimization is intractable; proposes a tractable approximation using Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) on sampled belief states.
- Provides theoretical results: a bound on approximation error (Proposition 1) and robustness to small model estimation errors (Theorem 1).
- Validated on a large real-world breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients), showing 28–68% fewer screenings with no increase in expected detection delay.
- Personalization leverages both static risk factors and dynamic test-history to adapt screening schedules.
- High technical quality: well-justified formulation, clear algorithmic description, and solid theoretical backing.
- Experiments are a major strength: large-scale, practical validation and analysis under different delay cost functions.
- Exceptionally clear writing and effective use of figures to build intuition and explain the model.
- Highly original: generalizes PBVI to dynamic screening, relaxes Markov assumptions, and models external information-triggered visits.
- High potential impact across multiple diseases with implications for reducing costs and patient burden.
- Strengths summarized: novel general framework, strong empirical results, technical soundness, high impact, and excellent clarity.
- Weaknesses/questions: sensitivity to substantial model misspecification needs more discussion and evaluation.
- Weaknesses/questions: scalability concerns remain regarding state space, horizon length, and size of sampled belief set.
- Overall conclusion: outstanding paper recommended for acceptance.
