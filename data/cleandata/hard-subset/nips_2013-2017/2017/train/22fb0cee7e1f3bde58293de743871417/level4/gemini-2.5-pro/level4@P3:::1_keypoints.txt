- Summary: DPSCREEN — a framework for dynamic, personalized screening using a general finite-state stochastic disease model with an external information process; optimizes screening vs delay, approximates solution via PBVI with trajectory-based belief point selection, provides theoretical error/robustness bounds, and shows large screening reductions on a 45k-patient breast cancer dataset.

- Strength 1 (Significance & Originality): Tackles an important real-world problem with a novel, more flexible non‑Markovian model and patient-initiated visit modeling, generalizing prior work.

- Strength 2 (Technical Quality): Sound formulation, identifies intractability, adapts PBVI cleverly via trajectory sampling, and provides theoretical approximation and robustness results.

- Strength 3 (Empirical Validation): Strong evaluation on a large real-world dataset showing 28–68% fewer screenings at equal detection delay and personalization by static and dynamic features.

- Strength 4 (Clarity & Organization): Well-written, clear model description, good distinction from standard POMDPs, and intuitive visualizations of personalization.

- Weakness 1 (Computational Scalability): High computational complexity (exponential in horizon T, quadratic in sampled beliefs B); limited discussion on scaling to lifelong/chronic monitoring and how B must grow with T.

- Weakness 2 (Model Assumptions): Uses a single absorbing disease state for simplicity; extension to competing risks is claimed but not demonstrated or analyzed for computational/theoretical impact.

- Weakness 3 (Model Estimation Details): Key estimation details (e.g., MCMC for transition probabilities) are deferred to supplements, reducing self-containedness and reproducibility.

- Argument for Acceptance: Novel, general, and practically significant framework with solid theory and impressive real-world empirical gains.

- Argument for Acceptance: Technically well-motivated approximation with provable error/robustness properties.

- Argument for Acceptance: Empirical results demonstrate clear clinical and practical benefits over standard guidelines.

- Argument for Acceptance: Advances state of the art in personalized medical decision-making and will influence researchers and practitioners.

- Argument against Acceptance: Computational complexity may limit applicability for long horizons despite being a common challenge.

- Argument against Acceptance: Paper could better address implications of simplifying assumptions and scalability limitations.

- Overall conclusion: Strengths substantially outweigh minor weaknesses; the paper is a significant, well-supported contribution and suitable for acceptance.
