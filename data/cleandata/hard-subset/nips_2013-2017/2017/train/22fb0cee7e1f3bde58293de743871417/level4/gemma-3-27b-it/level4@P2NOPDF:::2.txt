This paper addresses the important problem of optimizing patient screening policies based on individual patient information. The authors model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and aim to develop a computationally efficient policy that minimizes both screening costs and delays. Their proposed approximate solution demonstrates promising results on simulated breast cancer data, achieving reduced delays at comparable costs to a standard annual screening baseline.

The strengths of this work are considerable. Personalized screening is a highly relevant and impactful research area. The proposed algorithm is logically sound, well-developed, and clearly presented within the paper. The experimental results are encouraging and support the efficacy of the approach. Furthermore, the paper is exceptionally well-written, making it easy to understand the methodology and findings.

However, several concerns limit the practical impact of this work. The most significant weakness is the strong assumption of known disease models for each patient. While this allows for the derivation of an optimal policy, it represents a substantial disconnect from real-world clinical scenarios. The primary challenge in personalized screening isn’t *solving* the policy given a known model, but rather *inferring* the individual disease model from limited and noisy patient data. The paper does not address this crucial aspect of the problem.

Additionally, the methodological novelty presented is incremental rather than groundbreaking. The core contribution lies in the application of established techniques to a well-defined problem, rather than the development of fundamentally new algorithms or theoretical insights. 

The experimental evaluation also falls short of fully demonstrating the robustness of the proposed method. Comparing performance solely to a fixed annual screening baseline is insufficient. Given the aforementioned reliance on accurate disease models, a critical experiment would assess the sensitivity of the policy to inaccuracies and uncertainties within those models. Specifically, how does the performance degrade as the estimated disease parameters deviate from the true (simulated) values? Understanding this sensitivity is vital for evaluating the real-world applicability of the algorithm.

In summary, while the topic is interesting and the proposed algorithm is a reasonable solution under idealized conditions, the strong assumptions regarding disease model knowledge and the limited experimental validation raise concerns about its practical relevance. To strengthen this work and increase its impact, the authors should address the challenge of model inference and conduct a more thorough robustness analysis investigating the policy's sensitivity to model uncertainty. The paper, as it stands, requires further development to convincingly demonstrate its value to the broader research community and clinical practice.
