

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel method for optimizing disease screening policies by integrating both a patient's clinical history and disease-specific features. Unlike prior approaches that typically consider only static patient attributes or generic screening protocols, DPSCREEN's incorporation of longitudinal clinical data enables personalized screening schedules. This personalization achieves significant efficiency gains—reducing unnecessary screenings while maintaining detection timeliness—without compromising early disease identification. The core technical contribution lies in formulating the screening problem as a structured dynamic programming model that efficiently handles high-dimensional state spaces.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and technically rigorous. The motivation is compelling, clearly articulating the limitations of existing methods in handling patient-specific historical data. The related work is thoroughly contextualized, and the mathematical foundation is presented concisely yet comprehensively, including a clear analysis of computational complexity (O(n log n) per policy iteration, where n is the number of clinical features). The validation is robust and appropriately scaled: experiments on a 45,000-patient dataset from the Athena Health Network demonstrate consistent performance improvements across multiple disease models (e.g., diabetes, hypertension). Results show a 15–22% reduction in unnecessary screenings versus baseline policies, with no degradation in detection rates for early-stage disease. The cross-disease validation strengthens the generality of the approach, suggesting applicability beyond the specific models tested.

The practical relevance is evident. By optimizing screening frequency based on individual risk trajectories, DPSCREEN directly addresses resource constraints in healthcare systems while improving patient outcomes. The authors provide a clear discussion of implementation considerations, including how their framework could integrate with existing electronic health record systems. The clarity of the presentation—particularly the algorithmic description and ablation studies—ensures the method is accessible to both ML and healthcare audiences.

Minor suggestions for clarity: The paper could briefly clarify the specific clinical features used in the Athena dataset (e.g., lab values, prior diagnoses) to better contextualize the feature space. Additionally, a short discussion on how the method handles missing historical data would further bolster its real-world applicability.

Overall, DPSCREEN represents a significant advancement in personalized screening policy design. The technical contribution is sound, the validation is thorough and appropriately scaled, and the potential for real-world impact is strong. This work effectively bridges algorithmic innovation with clinical utility, making it a compelling candidate for acceptance at NIPS. The paper’s clarity, depth, and practical relevance meet the highest standards of the conference.
