The paper aims to determine the optimal policy for patient screening, given relevant information, by modeling a disease as a finite state stochastic process. The proposed policy seeks to minimize screening costs and delays. The authors present an approximate solution that is computationally efficient. Experiments using simulated breast cancer data suggest that the proposed algorithm can reduce delays while maintaining similar costs compared to a trivial baseline.

Positives:
+ Personalized screening is a significant and timely research topic.
+ The proposed algorithm is well-developed and reasonable.
+ The results are promising.
+ The paper is well-written.

Negatives:
- The proposed algorithm is primarily an academic exercise, as it relies on the assumption that the disease model for a given patient is known, which is rarely the case in practice. The primary challenge in personalized screening lies in inferring the model from data, rather than developing a policy based on a known model.
- The methodological novelty of the paper is limited.
- The experiments only compare the proposed algorithm to a baseline that performs annual screenings. Given the assumption of a known disease model, a crucial experimental question is the robustness of the proposed method to inaccuracies in the disease model. Specifically, it is essential to investigate the policy's sensitivity to uncertainties in the disease model.

Overall, while the topic is interesting and the proposed algorithm is reasonable, the paper's methodological novelty is limited. To demonstrate practical relevance, the authors need to provide more convincing evidence, particularly in addressing the critical issue of disease model uncertainty.
