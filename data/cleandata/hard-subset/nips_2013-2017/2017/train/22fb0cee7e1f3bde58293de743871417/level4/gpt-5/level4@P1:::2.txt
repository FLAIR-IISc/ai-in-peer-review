Summary
This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing dynamic, personalized screening policies for diseases modeled as finite-state stochastic processes with an absorbing disease state. The key features are: (i) an external information process (e.g., self-exams, comorbidities) that can trigger earlier-than-scheduled visits; (ii) endogenous screening intervals that depend on patient features, past test outcomes, and external information; and (iii) an objective that trades off screening costs against delay costs, where delay costs depend on the time between disease onset and detection. Exact optimization is shown to be computationally intractable. The authors formulate a belief-state dynamic program, establish piecewise-linear convexity of the value function, and propose a tractable point-based approximation using sampled state trajectories and reachable beliefs. They provide an approximation-error bound and a robustness result showing local constancy of optimal actions under small model perturbations. An illustrative application to breast cancer uses a large observational dataset: model parameters are estimated (via Gail-based risk features and MCMC), and DPSCREEN is shown to reduce the number of screenings substantially while maintaining a similar expected delay compared with annual screening. The authors also examine the effect of different delay-cost shapes (linear vs. quadratic).

Relation to prior work
The approach builds on POMDP formulations for screening (e.g., Ayer et al. for mammography; Maillart et al. for breast cancer; Erenay et al. for colonoscopy) and on point-based value iteration methods. Compared to this literature, the paper emphasizes (i) a general finite-state disease model (not restricted to stationary Markov or semi-Markov), (ii) an explicit external information process, and (iii) delay costs depending on the disease trajectory. It contrasts with Bayesian design approaches for information gain and with medical simulation studies (e.g., MISCAN-COLON) that compare fixed policies.

Strengths
- Important and timely problem: personalized screening that adapts to individual risk and dynamic history.
- Clear formulation that unifies endogenous arrivals, external information, and trajectory-dependent delay costs.
- Approximation method is reasonable and well-developed, leveraging sampled trajectories and reachable beliefs.
- Provides an approximation-error bound and a (local) robustness result to model-estimation error.
- Empirical results are promising: large reductions in screening volume at comparable detection delay relative to current clinical schedules.

Weaknesses
- Assumption of a known, correctly specified disease and observation model is strong. While the paper outlines a parameter-estimation procedure and provides some robustness discussion, the main practical challenge is learning and validating the model from observational data. The experiments do not systematically assess sensitivity to model misspecification.
- Methodological novelty is moderate. Piecewise-linear convexity and PBVI are known; the contribution lies in applying them to this richer screening setup rather than in fundamentally new algorithmic ideas.
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scope. Comparisons focus on annual screening (and mention biennial in the supplement). There is no head-to-head evaluation against prior POMDP-based screening policies or strong heuristics. The impact of external information and test accuracy is not stress-tested via sensitivity analyses.
- Some theoretical assumptions (e.g., parts of the error bound relying on perfect tests, bounded inter-screen intervals) are idealized relative to clinical reality.

Quality
- Technical formulation is sound, and the approximation strategy is coherent. The derivation of piecewise-linear value functions and the construction of reachable beliefs are reasonable.
- However, the empirical validation relies on a model learned from observational data without counterfactuals and lacks rigorous robustness studies. The absence of comparisons to established POMDP screening methods makes it hard to attribute gains to the proposed innovations rather than to modeling choices.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The high-level intuition, role of beliefs, and algorithmic steps are understandable, though the notation is dense.
- Suggestion: move more proof sketches and computational details from the supplement into the main text to clarify the key assumptions driving the approximation and robustness guarantees. Summarize the estimation pipeline and its diagnostics more prominently.

Originality
- Moderate. The setting combines known ideas (belief-state DP, PBVI) with a broader disease model and external information. This is a meaningful extension but not a major methodological advance.

Significance
- Potentially high, given the importance of personalized screening. That said, practical impact depends critically on the robustness of policies to model misspecification and on validation against strong baselines and real-world constraints. The current experiments are encouraging but do not yet establish practical readiness.

Suggestions for improvement
- Robustness to model error: conduct systematic sensitivity analyses to misspecification in hazards/transition probabilities, initial risk calibration, test sensitivity/specificity, external information noise/thresholds, and tumor growth parameters. Evaluate the stability of recommended intervals and outcomes under these perturbations.
- Baselines: compare against prior POMDP screening policies (e.g., Ayer, Maillart, Erenay) and modern clinical decision-support approaches (e.g., ConfidentCare), with and without external information.
- Evaluation design: report out-of-sample performance using cross-validation of the disease model; assess policy fairness across subgroups (e.g., age, density, family history). Consider direct constrained optimization (fixed delay tolerance) rather than tuning w post hoc.
- Practical assumptions: relax perfect-test assumptions in the theory where possible, and discuss how to integrate confirmatory tests and referral pathways. Clarify computational budgets and parallelization in realistic clinical deployments.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a coherent, general framework.
- Provides a practical approximation procedure with some theoretical backing.
- Promising empirical results indicating substantial screening reductions at similar delay.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited methodological novelty relative to existing POMDP work.
- Insufficient experimental validation against strong baselines and under model misspecification.
- Practical relevance remains uncertain without robust sensitivity analyses and broader comparisons.

Overall
The topic is interesting and the proposed algorithm is reasonable, but the methodological advance is modest, and the current evaluation does not convincingly demonstrate practical relevance. A stronger empirical study emphasizing robustness and comparisons to established methods would significantly improve the paper.
