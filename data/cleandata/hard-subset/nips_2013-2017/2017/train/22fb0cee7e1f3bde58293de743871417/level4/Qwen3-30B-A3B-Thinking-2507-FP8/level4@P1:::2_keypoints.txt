- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies balancing screening frequency and disease detection delays.  
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and incorporates external triggers (e.g., self-exams).  
- Proposes a tractable approximation based on point-based value iteration with provable error bounds and computational complexity analysis.  
- Demonstrates on a 45,000-patient breast cancer dataset that DPSCREEN reduces screening frequency by 28–68% while maintaining equivalent detection delays.  
- Personalization is driven by patient risk features and dynamic test histories.  
- Extends prior POMDP-based screening work by relaxing Markov assumptions and handling non-stationary external information.  
- Strength: rigorous formalization that handles trajectory-dependent delay costs and external information beyond standard POMDPs.  
- Strength: theoretical grounding with provable approximation error bound (Proposition 1) and algorithmic complexity characterization.  
- Strength: realistic, well-designed experiments with clear risk group definitions and clinically relevant baselines (annual/biennial).  
- Strength: robustness analysis (Section 3.3) indicates minimal policy sensitivity to parameter estimation errors in experiments.  
- Originality: applies to arbitrary finite-state disease processes and explicitly models external triggers, broadening applicability.  
- Significance: substantial practical gains (fewer screenings) and flexibility to disease-specific delay cost functions.  
- Clarity: clear exposition, precise math, helpful figures, and comprehensive supplementary materials enabling reproducibility.  
- Weakness: experimental validation uses perfectly estimated models from the same dataset, lacking tests of robustness to realistic estimation noise.  
- Weakness: limited baseline comparisons—should compare against other personalized methods to better contextualize gains.  
- Weakness: potential scalability issues for large time horizons or high-dimensional features; mitigation strategies (pruning, parallelization) are not fully discussed.  
- Recommendation: overall high-quality, significant contribution meriting acceptance, with suggested minor experiments adding robustness tests (e.g., perturbing model parameters).
