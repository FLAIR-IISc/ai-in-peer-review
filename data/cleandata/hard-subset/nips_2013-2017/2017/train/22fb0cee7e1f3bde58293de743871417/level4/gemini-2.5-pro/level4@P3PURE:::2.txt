This paper addresses the problem of determining an optimal patient screening policy given patient-specific information. The authors model the progression of a disease as a finite-state stochastic process and formulate the problem as finding a policy that minimizes a combination of screening costs and delays in treatment. To solve this, they propose a computationally efficient approximate algorithm. Experiments on a simulated breast cancer model indicate that the proposed algorithm can reduce treatment delays compared to an annual screening baseline, while maintaining a similar cost.

**Strengths:**
+ The paper tackles the important and clinically relevant problem of personalized medical screening.
+ The proposed algorithmic approach is technically sound and well-motivated.
+ The experimental results, though preliminary, are promising and suggest the potential benefits of the proposed method.
+ The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
- A major limitation of the current work is its assumption that the patient-specific disease model is known a priori. This is a strong assumption that is unlikely to hold in practice. The central challenge in personalized screening is often not the policy optimization itself, but rather the accurate inference of these underlying disease models from patient data. The paper sidesteps this critical and difficult aspect of the problem, which makes the contribution feel more like a theoretical exercise.
- The methodological novelty of the proposed algorithm appears to be limited.
- The empirical evaluation is limited. The comparison is made only against a simple annual screening baseline. Crucially, given the strong assumption of a known disease model, the experiments do not investigate the robustness of the proposed policy to inaccuracies in this model. An analysis of the policy's sensitivity to model uncertainty would be essential for assessing its potential for real-world application.

**Overall:**
In summary, this paper addresses an interesting problem with a reasonable technical approach. However, its impact is diminished by its limited methodological novelty and, more significantly, by the strong assumption that the patient's disease model is perfectly known. To be more convincing, the paper would need to better address the practical relevance of its contribution, for instance by including a robustness analysis in the experimental evaluation to show how the policy performs under model uncertainty.
