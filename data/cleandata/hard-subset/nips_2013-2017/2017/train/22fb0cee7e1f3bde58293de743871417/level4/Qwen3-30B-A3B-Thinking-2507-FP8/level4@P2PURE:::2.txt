

The paper aims to determine the optimal policy for patient screening given relevant information, modeling disease progression as a finite state stochastic process to minimize screening costs and delays. The authors propose a computationally efficient approximate solution, validated through experiments on simulated breast cancer data. Results indicate the proposed algorithm reduces screening delays while maintaining equivalent costs compared to a baseline that performs annual screening.  

Positives:  
+ Personalized screening represents a significant research area.  
+ The proposed algorithm is well-motivated and thoroughly developed.  
+ Results demonstrate clear promise.  
+ The paper is clearly and professionally written.  

Negatives:  
- The approach constitutes an academic exercise, as it assumes the patient-specific disease model is known—a condition that cannot be realistically assumed in practice. The core challenge in personalized screening lies not in deriving a policy given a known model, but in inferring the model from observational data.  
- Methodological novelty is limited.  
- Experiments only compare against annual screening, neglecting a critical practical concern: the robustness of the proposed method to inaccuracies in the disease model. The paper should experimentally assess how sensitive the policy is to uncertainties in model parameters.  

Overall, while the topic is compelling and the algorithm is reasonable, the methodological contribution lacks novelty. The paper must strengthen its practical relevance by addressing model uncertainty and demonstrating broader applicability beyond the idealized setting.
