Review- The paper aims to determine the optimal patient screening policy given available information. To derive such a policy, the disease is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process. The goal is to minimize screening costs and delays. The authors present an approximate solution that is computationally efficient. Experiments on simulated breast cancer data indicate that the proposed algorithm can reduce delays while maintaining the same cost relative to a simple baseline.
 Positives:
+ personalized screening is an important research area
+ the proposed algorithm is sound and well-developed
+ the results are encouraging
+ the paper is clearly written
 Negatives:
- the proposed algorithm is largely an academic exercise. It assumes the patient’s disease model is known, which is unrealistic in practice. The primary challenge in personalized screening is not designing a policy given the model, but inferring the model from data.
- the methodological novelty is limited
- the experiments only compare the method to an annual screening baseline. Given the earlier point about the disease model, a key question that should be tested experimentally is the method’s robustness to model inaccuracies. In other words, how sensitive is the policy to uncertainty in the disease model?
 Overall, the topic is interesting and the proposed algorithm is reasonable. However, the methodological novelty is modest, and the paper needs stronger evidence to persuade readers of its practical relevance.
