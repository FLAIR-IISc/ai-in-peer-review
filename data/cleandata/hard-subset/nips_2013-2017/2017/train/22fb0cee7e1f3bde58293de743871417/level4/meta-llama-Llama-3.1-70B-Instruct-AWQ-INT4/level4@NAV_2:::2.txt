The paper "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening" proposes a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The authors model the disease as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and incorporate the possibility of external information, such as self-examination or discovery of co-morbidities, that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment.

The authors' approach is to formulate the problem as a partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) and use point-based value iteration (PBVI) to compute an approximately optimal policy. The proposed algorithm, DPSCREEN, is computationally efficient and can handle complex disease models. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach using a large breast cancer dataset and show that their policy can reduce the number of screenings performed while maintaining the same delay in detection of disease.

The paper is well-written, and the results are promising. However, there are some limitations to the approach. The authors assume that the disease model of a given patient is known, which may not be the case in practice. The main obstacle in personalized screening is not coming up with a policy when the model is known, but inferring the model from data. The authors do not provide any experimental results on the robustness of their method to inaccuracies in the disease model, which is an important question that should be studied.

In terms of methodological novelty, the paper does not present a significant breakthrough. The use of PBVI for solving POMDPs is a well-established approach, and the authors do not introduce any new techniques or algorithms. However, the application of this approach to personalized screening is novel and interesting.

Overall, the paper is a good contribution to the field of personalized screening, but it would benefit from more experimental results on the robustness of the method to inaccuracies in the disease model. The authors should also provide more details on how their approach can be used in practice, where the disease model is not known a priori.

Strengths:

* Personalized screening is an important topic of research.
* The proposed algorithm is reasonable and well-developed.
* The results are promising.
* The paper is well-written.

Weaknesses:

* The proposed algorithm assumes that the disease model of a given patient is known, which may not be the case in practice.
* The methodological novelty is not large.
* The experiments only compare the proposed algorithm to a baseline doing annual screening.
* The paper does not provide any experimental results on the robustness of the method to inaccuracies in the disease model.

Suggestions for improvement:

* Provide more experimental results on the robustness of the method to inaccuracies in the disease model.
* Discuss how the approach can be used in practice, where the disease model is not known a priori.
* Consider comparing the proposed algorithm to other approaches, such as stochastic simulation-based methods.
* Provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm and how it can be implemented in practice.
