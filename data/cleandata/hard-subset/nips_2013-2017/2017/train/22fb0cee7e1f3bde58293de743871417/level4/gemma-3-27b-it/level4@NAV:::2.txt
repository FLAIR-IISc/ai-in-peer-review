The paper addresses the important problem of optimal patient screening policies, framing it as a decision-making process under a finite-state stochastic disease model with the goal of minimizing both screening costs and patient delays. The authors present a computationally efficient approximate solution and demonstrate, through simulations based on breast cancer data, that their algorithm can reduce delays while maintaining comparable costs to a standard annual screening approach.

Strengths of the work include its focus on a relevant and impactful area – personalized screening – and the sound, well-articulated nature of the proposed algorithm. The experimental results are encouraging, and the paper is clearly and effectively written. 

However, the work’s practical relevance is limited by a key assumption: the availability of a known, patient-specific disease model. While the proposed policy optimization is a valuable theoretical contribution, it sidesteps the central challenge of personalized screening – namely, inferring the underlying disease model from patient data. Consequently, the work feels largely academic in its current form. Furthermore, the methodological novelty presented is incremental. The experimental evaluation, limited to a comparison against annual screening, does not address a critical concern given the reliance on known models: the algorithm's sensitivity to model inaccuracies. Assessing the robustness of the proposed policy under uncertainty in disease model parameters would significantly strengthen the findings.

In summary, this is an interesting paper with a reasonable algorithmic approach. However, the limited novelty and disconnect from practical challenges necessitate further work to demonstrate the potential for real-world impact.
