

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening frequency against detection delay in disease management. The authors model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating external information (e.g., patient self-examinations) that may trigger earlier clinical visits. The core contribution is a computationally tractable method to approximate the optimal policy—previously intractable due to trajectory-dependent costs and non-Markovian disease dynamics—using point-based value iteration with provable error bounds. Evaluated on breast cancer data, the approach reduces screening frequency by 28–68% compared to standard annual/biennial clinical policies while maintaining equivalent expected detection delays. This work extends beyond prior NIPS contributions (e.g., Ayer et al., NIPS 2014; Maillart et al., NIPS 2015), which assumed Markov disease processes and stationary test outcomes, by accommodating general disease models (e.g., survival processes, first-hitting time models) and dynamic external information.

**Strengths**  
*Originality and Significance (4/4):* The framework addresses a critical gap in personalized screening literature by relaxing restrictive assumptions (e.g., Markov disease dynamics) prevalent in prior work [7,9,10]. This enables application to diverse diseases (e.g., breast, pancreatic cancer) with non-stationary progression. The significance is demonstrated through clinically meaningful results: 57–68% fewer screenings for high-risk patients with self-examinations, directly translating to reduced patient burden and healthcare costs. The analysis of cost-function sensitivity (linear vs. quadratic delay costs) further underscores the framework’s adaptability to disease-specific priorities, advancing the state of the art beyond simulation-based approaches [44–48] used in medical guidelines.  

*Quality (4/4):* The theoretical foundation is rigorous, with a clear proof of the value function’s convexity (Lemma 1) and a tight bound on approximation error (Proposition 1). The methodology is validated on a large, real-world dataset (45k patients) with thorough model estimation (Gail model for risk scoring, MCMC for transition parameters). The ablation study (with/without self-examinations) and risk-group comparisons (Table 1) provide convincing empirical support. The robustness analysis (Theorem 1) also mitigates concerns about model estimation errors, a common weakness in similar works [10].  

*Clarity (3.5/4):* The paper is well-structured, with clear formalization of the problem (Section 2) and a concise explanation of the DPSCREEN algorithm (Section 3.2). The graphical illustration of personalization (Figure 1) effectively conveys how policies adapt to patient history. Minor clarity issues arise in the belief-space reduction (Section 3.2), where the sampling strategy could be better motivated for readers unfamiliar with POMDPs. A brief pseudocode in the main text (vs. Appendix A) would further enhance reproducibility.  

**Weaknesses**  
*Scope and Generalizability (2.5/4):* While the framework is theoretically broad, the experimental validation is limited to breast cancer. The paper acknowledges this but does not discuss barriers to applying DPSCREEN to other diseases (e.g., cardiac or pancreatic cancer) beyond the synthetic quadratic-cost example (Figure 2). A brief discussion of data requirements (e.g., need for longitudinal external information) would strengthen the practical relevance.  

*Comparison with Medical Literature (3/4):* The related work (Section 5) effectively contrasts with operations research frameworks [7,9,10] but overlooks key medical literature. For instance, the US Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF) guidelines [27,28] for breast cancer screening rely on simulation-based models (MISCAN-COLON [46]), yet the paper does not explicitly compare DPSCREEN’s policy performance against *these specific guidelines* beyond stating "current clinical policies." A sentence clarifying how DPSCREEN’s 28–68% reduction compares to USPSTF’s recommended thresholds would bolster significance.  

*Algorithmic Transparency (3/4):* The computational complexity (O(T B² T² K|Y||Z|)) is stated but not contextualized. For a real-world implementation, it would be valuable to note: (1) typical values of B (e.g., B=500) in the breast cancer setting, and (2) how parallelization reduces runtime (e.g., "24-hour runtime on 100-core cluster"). Without this, the tractability claim remains somewhat abstract.  

**Recommendation**  
**Accept**. This paper makes a significant, technically sound contribution to personalized healthcare decision-making. The framework’s generality, provable guarantees, and substantial empirical gains (28–68% screening reduction) address a clear gap in the literature. While the scope is limited to breast cancer and minor clarity improvements are needed, the work’s theoretical rigor and real-world impact align strongly with NIPS’ focus on high-impact machine learning.  

*Pro-accept arguments:*  
- First framework for general disease models with external information.  
- Tight approximation error bounds (Proposition 1) and robustness to estimation errors (Theorem 1).  
- Clinically actionable results (57–68% fewer screenings for high-risk patients).  

*Con-accept arguments:*  
- Validation restricted to breast cancer (though synthetic extension to pancreatic cancer is provided).  
- Algorithmic complexity could be better contextualized for practitioners.  

This work advances the field beyond NIPS 2014–2017 contributions by enabling dynamic, personalized screening for heterogeneous patient populations—a critical step toward adaptive healthcare systems.
