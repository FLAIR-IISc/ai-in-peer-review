### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel method for personalizing breast cancer screening schedules. The core contribution is a predictive model that recommends an optimal time for a patient's next screening by balancing two competing factors: the cost associated with the screening itself and the cost incurred by a potential delay in diagnosis. To achieve this personalization, the model leverages a patient's individual medical history alongside other relevant external data. The authors frame this as an optimization problem and demonstrate through empirical evaluation that their approach offers a significant improvement in cost-effectiveness compared to current standard-of-care guidelines and other baseline methods.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance and Novelty:** The problem addressed is of high clinical and societal importance. Moving beyond one-size-fits-all screening protocols towards personalized, risk-stratified strategies is a critical direction for modern healthcare. The paper’s formulation of this problem as a trade-off between screening and delay costs is clear and compelling.

2.  **Personalized Approach:** The main strength of the proposed method is its ability to tailor recommendations to individual patients. By incorporating specific features from a patient's history, the model can move beyond the population-level guidelines that may over-screen low-risk individuals and under-screen those at high risk.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides a convincing empirical evaluation of the proposed model. The method is shown to substantially outperform existing approaches, including static clinical guidelines. The experiments appear to be well-designed, and the results clearly support the authors' claims of improved efficiency and cost savings.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, explaining their methodology, and presenting their results in an accessible manner.

### **Weaknesses and Questions for the Author**

While the work is promising, there are several areas where the paper could be strengthened with additional detail and discussion.

1.  **Model and Feature Specification:** The paper is somewhat vague regarding the specific data used. The term "external information" is mentioned but not fully defined. The authors should provide a more detailed description of the complete feature set.
    *   What specific variables from a patient's medical history were most predictive?
    *   What constitutes "external information"? Does this include demographic data, genetic markers, or population-level statistics? How is this information integrated into the model?
    *   A more thorough feature analysis or ablation study would help clarify the key drivers of the model's predictions.

2.  **Cost Function Formulation:** The performance of the model is fundamentally tied to the definition of the "screening cost" and "delay cost."
    *   How were these costs quantified? Were they purely financial, or did they incorporate other factors like quality-adjusted life years (QALYs), patient anxiety, or risks from radiation exposure?
    *   The relative weighting of these two costs is a critical hyperparameter. A sensitivity analysis showing how the recommended screening intervals vary with different cost assumptions would significantly bolster the paper's claims of robustness.

3.  **Interpretability and Clinical Utility:** For any machine learning model to be adopted in a clinical setting, interpretability is paramount.
    *   Can the model provide an explanation for its recommendation? For example, can it highlight the specific risk factors that led it to suggest a shorter or longer screening interval for a particular patient?
    *   A discussion on the model's interpretability and how clinicians might interact with and trust its outputs would be a valuable addition. Without it, the model risks being a "black box," which could be a major barrier to real-world adoption.

4.  **Generalizability and Dataset Bias:** The experiments appear to be conducted on a specific patient cohort.
    *   Could the authors provide more details on the demographics of the dataset used?
    *   It is crucial to discuss how well the model might generalize to different populations (e.g., other ethnicities, geographic locations, or healthcare systems).
    *   Was there any consideration of potential biases in the training data, and what steps were taken to mitigate them? An unfair model could inadvertently worsen health disparities.

### **Minor Comments**

*   Please define all acronyms upon their first use.
*   The captions for some figures could be made more descriptive to allow them to be better understood independently of the main text.
*   A brief discussion on the computational complexity of the method would be helpful.

### **Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the important field of personalized medicine. The proposed method is elegant, and the results are impressive. My primary concerns relate to the lack of detail in certain areas, particularly regarding the feature set, the cost function definition, and the model's interpretability. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and improve its potential for real-world impact. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors' ability to clarify these issues in the final version.
