The purpose of this research paper is to identify the best policy for patient screening, considering relevant information. To provide a policy, a disease should be modeled as a finite-state stochastic process. The policy aims to reduce screening costs and delays. The authors have proposed an approximate solution that is computationally efficient. Experiments conducted on simulated data related to breast cancer indicate that the proposed algorithm can reduce delays while maintaining a similar cost compared to a modest baseline.

**Positive Aspects:**

*   Personalized screening is an important topic of research.
*   The proposed algorithm is reasonable and well-developed.
*   The results are promising.
*   The research paper is well-written.

**Negative Aspects:**

*   The proposed algorithm is purely an academic exercise. It assumes that a disease model of a given patient is known, which can never be assumed in practice. The main obstacle to personalized screening is not coming up with a policy when the model is known, but rather estimating the model from the data.
*   Methodological novelty is not significant.
*   The experiments only compare the proposed algorithm to a baseline of annual screening. Given the previous point about the disease model, an important question that should be studied experimentally is the robustness of the proposed method for inaccuracies in the disease model. In other words, how sensitive the policy is to uncertainties in the disease model.

Overall, the topic is interesting and the proposed algorithm is reasonable. However, methodological novelty is not significant, and the research paper would need to do a better job to convince readers that this work is practically relevant.
