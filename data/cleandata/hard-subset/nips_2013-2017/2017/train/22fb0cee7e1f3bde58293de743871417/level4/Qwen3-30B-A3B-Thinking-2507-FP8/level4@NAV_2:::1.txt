

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that account for both patient-specific features and dynamic screening history, addressing a significant gap in existing literature. The authors model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating external information (e.g., patient self-examinations) that may trigger early clinic visits. Unlike prior work such as Ayer et al. (NIPS 2014) and Maillart et al. (Operations Research 2015), which restricted disease models to stationary Markov processes, DPSCREEN generalizes to non-stationary processes and provides computationally tractable approximate optimality guarantees via a point-based value iteration approach. The method is validated on a large breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients from Athena Health Network), demonstrating 28-68% reductions in screening frequency versus clinical standards while maintaining equivalent disease detection timeliness.  

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound, with rigorous derivation of the value function's convexity and approximation error bounds (Proposition 1). The methodology correctly handles the non-Markovian delay cost and external information processes that invalidate standard POMDP approaches. The breast cancer validation includes thorough model estimation (using Gail risk scores) and comprehensive comparisons against annual/biennial screening policies. The robustness analysis (Theorem 1) appropriately addresses model estimation errors.  

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem formulation to algorithmic design. The graphical representation of personalization (Figure 1) effectively illustrates how policies adapt to patient risk and dynamic history. Minor improvements could be made to the explanation of belief space dimensionality reduction in Section 3.2.  

**Originality**: This work makes a significant contribution by being the first to propose a unified framework for personalized screening across diverse disease models (including dynamic survival and first-hitting-time processes). It clearly differentiates itself from prior NIPS-relevant work (e.g., Ayer et al. 2014, Balancing Suspense and Surprise, NIPS 2016) by relaxing the stationary Markov assumptions for both disease progression and test outcomes.  

**Significance**: The results demonstrate substantial practical impact—reducing screenings by up to 68% without compromising detection timeliness. The framework's flexibility to incorporate disease-specific delay costs (e.g., linear for breast cancer, quadratic for pancreatic cancer) highlights its broad applicability across medical domains, potentially guiding future clinical guidelines.  

**Strengths**:  
- First framework addressing general disease models with external information.  
- Strong theoretical guarantees (approximation error bounds) and practical validation.  
- Clear demonstration of personalization via risk-group analysis (Table 2).  
- Comprehensive comparison with clinical policies using real-world data.  

**Weaknesses**:  
- Limited discussion of scalability for very high-dimensional state spaces beyond the breast cancer case.  
- The connection to recent NIPS work on disease trajectory mapping (Schulam et al., NIPS 2016) could be more explicit.  

**Recommendation**: Accept. The paper makes a clear technical contribution that advances the state-of-the-art in personalized screening, with strong theoretical foundations and compelling empirical results. The significance for medical practice is well-supported by the validation on a large-scale dataset.
