

This paper proposes a framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening frequency against disease detection delay, modeling disease progression as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state. The authors extend previous personalized screening frameworks [7,9,10] by incorporating external information processes (e.g., patient self-examinations) and generalizing beyond Markovian disease models. They develop an approximately optimal policy using point-based value iteration, providing theoretical error bounds (Proposition 1), and evaluate the approach on a large breast cancer dataset, demonstrating 28-68% reductions in screening frequency compared to standard annual screening while maintaining equivalent detection delays.

Strengths:
+ The problem of personalized screening is highly significant for healthcare, with clear potential for reducing costs and improving patient outcomes.
+ The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical foundations, including a proof of the approximation error bound.
+ The experimental evaluation on a substantial breast cancer dataset is well-executed, with appropriate metrics (expected number of screenings, expected delay).
+ The framework's ability to incorporate external information (e.g., patient self-examinations) and adapt to different delay cost functions (Figure 2) is particularly valuable.
+ The paper is clearly written with well-structured sections and effective graphical illustrations (e.g., Figure 1).

Weaknesses:
- The paper makes a strong assumption that the disease model is known precisely, which is unrealistic in clinical practice. The main challenge in personalized screening is not policy optimization but model estimation from observational data, which is not adequately addressed.
- Methodological novelty is limited; the approach primarily applies existing point-based value iteration techniques to a new domain rather than introducing fundamental methodological advances.
- The experimental evaluation only compares against standard annual screening, without addressing the critical question of robustness to model estimation errors. The paper fails to analyze how policy performance degrades when the disease model is imperfectly estimated from data.
- The discussion of the relationship between delay cost functions and screening policy (Figure 2) is insightful but not sufficiently integrated into the main experimental results to demonstrate practical implications.

The topic is important and the proposed framework is technically sound, but the paper would benefit from addressing the practical challenges of disease model estimation and demonstrating robustness to model inaccuracies. Without these elements, the practical relevance of the approach remains questionable despite promising theoretical results.
