- Proposes DPSCREEN, a novel method for personalizing screening policies using patient clinical history and features.  
- Demonstrates personalization reduces screenings while maintaining timely disease detection.  
- Paper is well-written, technically rigorous, and positions contribution within screening and personalized medicine literature.  
- Provides a concise mathematical formulation and discusses computational complexity.  
- Uses point-based value iteration to make the dynamic decision problem tractable.  
- Empirical validation on a large real-world dataset (45,000 patients, Athena Health) shows clear improvements over current clinical policies.  
- Analysis explores different disease characteristics and cost functions, supporting generalizability.  
- Related work summary is strong but should better acknowledge dynamic treatment regimes (e.g., Murphy).  
- Novelty and potential healthcare impact are significant.  
- Technical soundness and realistic complexity discussion are strengths.  
- Clarity and organization make the core ideas accessible.  
- Weakness: simplifying model assumptions (e.g., two-state Markov, perfect tests) need deeper sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: treatment of estimation error is abstract; practical effects of model mis-specification require more concrete discussion.  
- Weakness: modeling of external information (e.g., self-exam) assumes conditional independence that may be unrealistic.  
- Weakness: practical computational scalability for very large state spaces or long horizons is not fully explored.  
- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, novel and sound method, strong empirical validation, clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: generalizability concerns from model assumptions, limited treatment of estimation error and external info, scalability questions.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions; add sensitivity analyses and expand discussion of estimation error and scalability.
