This paper aims to identify an optimal patient-screening policy given available information by modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process. The objective is to minimize screening costs and delays. The authors propose a computationally efficient approximate solution and evaluate it on simulated breast cancer data, showing reduced delays at comparable cost relative to a trivial baseline.

Positives:
- Personalized screening is an important and timely research direction.
- The proposed algorithm is reasonable and technically well developed.
- The empirical results are promising, indicating reduced delays without increased cost.
- The paper is generally well written.

Negatives:
- The approach is largely an academic exercise: it assumes the patient’s disease model is known, which is unrealistic in practice. The main challenge in personalized screening is not deriving a policy given a known model, but inferring the disease model from data.
- The methodological novelty is limited.
- The experimental evaluation is narrow, comparing only to annual screening. Given the reliance on a known disease model, a key missing component is an assessment of robustness to model misspecification. How sensitive is the learned policy to uncertainties or inaccuracies in the disease model?

Overall assessment:
The topic is important and the proposed algorithm is reasonable. However, the methodological novelty is not substantial, and the paper does not adequately demonstrate practical relevance. In particular, the assumption of a known disease model and the absence of robustness analyses limit the impact and applicability of the results.
