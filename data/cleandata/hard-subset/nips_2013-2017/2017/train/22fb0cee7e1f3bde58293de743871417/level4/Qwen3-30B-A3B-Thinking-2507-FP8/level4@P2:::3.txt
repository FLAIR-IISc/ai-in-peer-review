

This paper proposes a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the delay in disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporate external information (such as patient self-examinations) that can trigger earlier screenings, and provide a computationally tractable approximation to the optimal screening policy. The method is evaluated on a large breast cancer dataset, demonstrating 28-68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining the same expected delay in disease detection compared to current clinical practices.

The paper makes several important contributions that advance the state of the art in personalized screening. First, it generalizes beyond previous work that typically assumes stationary Markov disease processes and stationary test outcome models. The authors explicitly address the limitations of prior frameworks (such as those in [7], [9], [10]) that cannot handle non-stationary disease progression, external information processes, or delay costs that depend on the entire disease trajectory rather than just the current state. Second, the authors provide theoretical guarantees for their approximation method, including bounds on the approximation error (Proposition 1), which is crucial for practical implementation. Third, they demonstrate how the framework can be applied to real-world healthcare data, showing clear personalization to both patient features (risk factors) and dynamic history (previous test results).

The paper is technically sound (Quality) and well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the proofs of key properties (e.g., Lemma 1 showing the value function is convex and piecewise linear) are rigorous. The experimental validation on breast cancer data is thorough, with careful attention to model estimation, policy construction, and performance metrics. The results are presented clearly with appropriate comparisons to clinical standards, and the paper thoughtfully addresses the impact of different cost functions on screening policies (Figure 2).

The writing is clear and well-organized (Clarity), with logical flow from problem formulation to solution to evaluation. The supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including the pseudo-code for the DPSCREEN algorithm and detailed descriptions of the dataset and model estimation. The graphical representation of the personalization mechanism (Figure 1) effectively illustrates how the policy adapts to both patient features and dynamic history.

The work is highly original (Originality) in its general framework that accommodates a wide range of disease models, external information processes, and cost structures. While there are related works in POMDPs for screening [7], [9] and in medical literature [44]-[48], none provide the same level of generality or theoretical guarantees for the approximation error. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from prior work, particularly in handling non-Markovian disease processes and external information.

The significance (Significance) of this work is substantial. Personalized screening could dramatically reduce healthcare costs while maintaining or improving patient outcomes, as demonstrated by the 28-68% reduction in screenings. The framework could be applied to various diseases beyond breast cancer, as the authors note in Section 4. The paper's focus on a realistic healthcare setting with observational data and practical implementation considerations makes it particularly relevant for real-world impact.

Minor weaknesses include the limited discussion of how the framework would adapt to diseases with more complex progression models (beyond the two-state Markov model used for breast cancer), and the assumption of no false positives in test outcomes, which might not reflect real-world screening scenarios. While the authors acknowledge the importance of choosing appropriate delay costs based on disease characteristics (Figure 2), a more detailed discussion of how to determine these costs for different diseases would strengthen the paper.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of personalized healthcare decision making. The theoretical framework is novel, the empirical validation is convincing, and the potential impact on healthcare resource allocation is significant. The authors have addressed the limitations of prior work while providing a method that is both theoretically sound and practically applicable.

Strengths: Novel general framework for personalized screening that extends beyond previous work; rigorous theoretical guarantees with approximation error bounds; strong empirical results showing significant improvements over clinical standards; clear writing and organization.

Weaknesses: Limited discussion of adaptation to diseases beyond breast cancer with more complex progression models; assumption of no false positives may limit real-world applicability.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper addresses an important problem with a technically sound, novel approach that has clear potential for real-world impact. The theoretical contributions are significant, the empirical results are convincing, and the writing is clear and well-organized. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution to the field.
