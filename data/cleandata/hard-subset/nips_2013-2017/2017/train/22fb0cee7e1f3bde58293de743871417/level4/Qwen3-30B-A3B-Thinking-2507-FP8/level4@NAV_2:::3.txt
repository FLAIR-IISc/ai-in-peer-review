

The paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that dynamically balance screening frequency against disease detection delay, addressing a critical gap in prior NIPS work on clinical decision-making. While recent NIPS contributions like Ayer et al. (2016) and Rizopoulos et al. (2015) developed personalized screening models, they relied on restrictive Markov disease processes and stationary test outcome assumptions—limitations DPSCREEN overcomes by modeling disease as a general finite-state stochastic process with absorbing states, incorporating non-stationary external information (e.g., self-examinations) and dynamic history. The framework provides a computationally tractable approximation algorithm with provable error bounds, validated on a large breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients), achieving 28–68% reductions in screenings versus clinical benchmarks while maintaining equivalent detection delays.  

**Strengths:**  
- **Technical rigor:** The model generalizes beyond prior Markov/Semi-Markov disease assumptions (e.g., handles dynamic survival models, non-stationary test outcomes), and the DPSCREEN algorithm with approximation error bounds (Proposition 1) addresses computational intractability.  
- **Significance:** The 28–68% screening reduction (Table 1) demonstrates tangible clinical impact, particularly for high-risk patients (personalization via initial risk and dynamic test results). The synthetic disease cost analysis (Fig. 2) further underscores the importance of tailoring delay cost functions to disease-specific dynamics.  
- **Clarity:** The problem formulation (Section 2) and algorithmic description (Section 3) are logically structured, with clear graphical illustrations of personalization (Fig. 1).  

**Weaknesses:**  
- **Scope limitation:** The single-absorbing-state disease model (footnote 1) restricts applicability to diseases with competing risks (e.g., multiple cancer types), though the authors acknowledge this in footnote 3.  
- **Validation depth:** The breast cancer case study relies on retrospective observational data; prospective validation against real-world clinical protocols (e.g., USPSTF guidelines) would strengthen claims of practical utility.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:*  
- First framework for personalized screening under dynamic history and external information, with rigorous approximation guarantees.  
- Demonstrable 28–68% screening reduction over current clinical policies (annual/biennial), validated on large-scale real-world data.  
- Addresses key limitations of prior NIPS screening work (Ayer, Rizopoulos) via general disease modeling.  

*Con:*  
- Limited to single-absorbing-state disease models (though acknowledged as a constraint).  
- Synthetic disease cost analysis (Fig. 2) lacks empirical validation for non-breast cancers.  

The paper makes a strong contribution to NIPS by advancing personalized decision-making under uncertainty with clear theoretical and empirical grounding, warranting acceptance.
