This paper presents a compelling approach to personalizing breast cancer screening schedules, aiming to optimize the trade-off between screening costs and the risks associated with delayed detection. The core idea – leveraging individual patient medical history in conjunction with external data to predict optimal screening intervals – is both practically relevant and scientifically sound. 

The paper is, for the most part, clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is effectively established, and the proposed methodology is explained in a logical manner. The experimental results demonstrate a significant improvement in performance compared to existing, more standardized screening approaches. Specifically, the reported reductions in combined cost (screening expenses plus the cost of delayed detection) are noteworthy.

The strength of this work lies in its potential to move beyond a “one-size-fits-all” screening paradigm. By tailoring screening intervals to individual risk profiles, the method promises to reduce unnecessary screenings for low-risk patients while ensuring timely detection for those at higher risk. This personalization has the potential for substantial benefits, both in terms of cost savings and improved patient outcomes.

However, several aspects warrant further consideration. While the paper clearly articulates the benefit of incorporating patient history, the precise nature of the "external information" utilized could be elaborated upon. A more detailed discussion of the data sources and their potential biases would strengthen the analysis.  For instance, what safeguards are in place to mitigate biases inherent in historical medical records or population-level data?

Furthermore, the evaluation focuses primarily on cost optimization. While this is a crucial aspect, a more comprehensive assessment of clinical impact would be valuable.  Including metrics beyond cost, such as changes in predicted life expectancy, false positive rates, or the number of advanced-stage cancers detected, would provide a more holistic understanding of the method’s effectiveness.  It would be helpful to understand *how* the method impacts these clinical outcomes, not just the economic ones.

The sensitivity analysis performed is adequate, but could be expanded. Exploring the robustness of the model to variations in input parameters, particularly those related to the cost of delayed detection (which can be highly subjective), would build confidence in the results.  Also, the impact of missing or incomplete patient data should be addressed. Real-world clinical data is rarely perfect; understanding the method’s performance under less-than-ideal conditions is critical for practical implementation.

Finally, the computational complexity of the proposed method is not fully discussed.  Implementing personalized screening schedules at scale requires efficient algorithms. A brief analysis of the computational cost of the prediction process, and its feasibility for integration into existing clinical workflows, would be beneficial.

Despite these minor concerns, this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of personalized cancer screening. The proposed method is innovative, well-supported by experimental results, and has the potential to improve both the cost-effectiveness and clinical effectiveness of breast cancer screening programs. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points outlined above, specifically regarding a more detailed discussion of external data, a broader clinical evaluation, expanded sensitivity analysis, and a consideration of computational complexity.
