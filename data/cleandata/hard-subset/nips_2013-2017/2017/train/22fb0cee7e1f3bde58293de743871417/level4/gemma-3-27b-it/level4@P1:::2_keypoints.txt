- Proposes personalized screening policies balancing screening costs and delayed-detection risks using a finite-state stochastic disease model and an approximately optimal, computationally efficient policy.
- Experiments on breast cancer data suggest fewer screenings can be achieved with comparable detection delays.

Strengths:
- Tackles an important and timely problem in personalized medicine.
- Presents a well-developed, logically sound algorithm.
- Empirical results are promising compared to standard screening practices.
- Paper is clearly written and explains core concepts well.

Weaknesses:
- Core assumption that each patient’s disease model is known is unrealistic and severely limits practical applicability.
- Methodological novelty is incremental rather than a major advancement.
- Experimental evaluation is limited and lacks robustness analyses to model misspecification and real-world variability.

Overall:
- Interesting and well-constructed work, but reliance on known models, limited novelty, and weak experimental robustness make strong acceptance difficult.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important problem, provides a clear algorithmic approach, and shows potential benefits in simulation.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Relies on unrealistic assumptions, offers limited novel contribution, lacks robustness tests, and does not convincingly demonstrate practical relevance.
