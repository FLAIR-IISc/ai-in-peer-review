- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework that personalizes screening by combining prior screening results with patient features to reduce screening frequency while maintaining detection timeliness.  
- Technically sound, well-written, and clearly motivated with appropriate literature context.  
- Concise, thorough mathematical formulation including computational complexity discussion.  
- Validated on a large real-world dataset (45,000 patients), providing strong empirical support.  
- Demonstrated adaptability across disease models via synthetic experiments and a breast cancer example, supporting generalizability.  
- Novel contribution: personalized policies that handle general stochastic disease models.  
- Potential high impact: fewer screenings without compromising timeliness could benefit patients and healthcare systems.  
- Weakness: relies on a finite-state stochastic process assumption for disease progression; impact of this simplification is not fully explored.  
- Weakness: experiments focus mainly on breast cancer; broader disease validation is needed to strengthen generality claims.  
- Weakness: computational complexity may impede practical adoption; further optimization work is desirable.  
- Weakness: use of a linear delay cost function is arbitrary; sensitivity analysis over different cost shapes is lacking.  
- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, novel and rigorous approach, strong empirical improvements, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments for rejection: simplifying model assumptions, computational complexity concerns, and limited disease-specific validation.  
- Overall recommendation: accept—strengths substantially outweigh weaknesses, with suggestions to address assumptions, cost functions, and broader validation in future work.
