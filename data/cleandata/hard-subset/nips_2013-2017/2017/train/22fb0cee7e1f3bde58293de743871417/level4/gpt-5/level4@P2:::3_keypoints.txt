- Tackles dynamic, personalized screening schedule design balancing screening frequency and disease-detection delay using a finite-state stochastic disease model with an absorbing state.  
- Incorporates external information (self-exams, comorbidities) that can trigger unscheduled visits and endogenous inter-decision times.  
- Formulates policy optimization in belief space with Bellman equations where costs depend on full-state trajectories, not just current state.  
- Develops DPSCREEN: a scalable point-based value iteration that samples likely state trajectories to build a reduced belief set for backups.  
- Proves the value function is piecewise-linear and convex and provides an approximation error bound tied to belief-space sampling density.  
- Gives a robustness result showing local policy stability under small model-estimation errors.  
- Empirical evaluation on a large breast-cancer dataset (Athena) shows 28–68% reduction in expected screens while matching expected detection delay of annual/biennial guidelines.  
- Demonstrates personalization by patient features (Gail risk) and dynamic history, and sensitivity of policies to delay-cost choice (linear vs quadratic).  
- Extends prior POMDP-based screening work by allowing general finite-state dynamics, endogenous arrivals, and trajectory-dependent costs.  
- Contrasts with Bayesian and simulation-based guideline approaches by optimizing policies over belief states rather than comparing fixed schedules.  
- Well-motivated, practically relevant problem capturing personalization and dynamic external signals.  
- Clear formalization of a complex decision process with endogenous timing and trajectory-dependent delay costs.  
- Presents a scalable, parallelizable approximate solution tailored to high-dimensional belief spaces, with complexity discussion.  
- Strong theoretical contributions: piecewise-linear convexity, sampling-based approximation guarantee, and robustness theorem.  
- Empirical results are compelling on a real dataset with sensible risk-stratified personalization.  
- Theoretical assumptions (perfect tests, bounded interval) differ from empirical settings with imperfect tests; needs clarification or discussion of extensions.  
- External-information model is stylized (monthly self-exams, thresholded proxy); sensitivity analyses to parameters, adherence, and alternative arrival processes are needed.  
- Evaluation is model-based without counterfactual validation; recommend off-policy evaluation, calibration checks, and uncertainty quantification (bootstrap CIs, posterior predictive checks).  
- Baseline comparisons are limited to annual/biennial schedules; include stronger personalized heuristics and systems for better benchmarking.  
- Reporting focuses on expected delay and may hide tail risks; include distributional metrics (percentiles, worst-case proxies).  
- Generalizability concerns: cohort restricted to ages 60–65; discuss broader age ranges, comorbidities, and behavioral adherence effects.  
- Clarity/reproducibility issues: define Ω(B̄) and ζ concretely, unify false-positive assumptions, explain tuning of w, action-space limits (W), and provide implementation/code.  
- Quality: technically sound but some theoretical assumptions are stronger than experimental setup; empirical study careful but model-based.  
- Clarity: generally well written but needs tighter links between theoretical assumptions and experiments and clearer metric/parameter definitions.  
- Originality: novel combination of general dynamics, trajectory costs, endogenous arrivals, and PBVI with guarantees relative to prior screening POMDPs.  
- Significance: high potential to reduce unnecessary screens while preserving timeliness; adaptable to other diseases and cost structures.  
- Pros for acceptance: important clinical problem, solid algorithmic and theoretical contributions, strong empirical gains, and cross-community relevance.  
- Cons for acceptance: entirely model-based policy evaluation, theoretical-practice mismatches, simplified external-information/adherence modeling, and limited baselines.  
- Overall: strong, timely contribution that would benefit from more rigorous validation, stronger baselines, and clearer assumption alignment — reviewer leans positive.
