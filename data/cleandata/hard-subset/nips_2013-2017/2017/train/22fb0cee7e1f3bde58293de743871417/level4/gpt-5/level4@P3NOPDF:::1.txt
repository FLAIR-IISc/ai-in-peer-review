Summary
The paper introduces DPSCREEN, a method for optimizing screening policies across broad classes of diseases by leveraging both a patient’s clinical screening history and disease-specific patient features. By explicitly modeling history and heterogeneity, DPSCREEN personalizes the screening schedule, improving efficiency without sacrificing the timeliness of detection. The method is rigorously motivated, mathematically grounded, and evaluated on a large dataset, demonstrating clear benefits over existing, less personalized approaches.

Strengths
- Clear contribution: Personalization of screening policies using both patient history and disease-relevant features is a meaningful advance over one-size-fits-all or static approaches.
- Technical soundness: The paper provides a concise, well-structured mathematical exposition, including complexity analysis, which makes the approach credible and reproducible in principle.
- Thoughtful positioning: The related work section is appropriate and situates DPSCREEN relative to prior screening and policy-optimization methods.
- Empirical validation: Evaluation on a sizeable (≈45k patients) Athena Health dataset supports the claim that personalization yields measurable gains. The fact that the method is tested across multiple disease model types further reinforces generality and robustness.
- Practical relevance: The approach addresses a real and important problem with potential for near-term clinical impact if integrated properly into workflow.

Questions and Suggestions for Improvement
- Assumptions and robustness: The approach appears to rely on a specified disease progression model and utility/cost parameters. Please discuss how sensitive the learned policies are to misspecification of these assumptions. A systematic sensitivity analysis would strengthen the empirical case.
- Evaluation metrics: Beyond “timeliness of detection,” clarify the operational metrics used (e.g., false positives, number of screenings per patient, average time to detection, cost per detected case). Confidence intervals and statistical tests would help establish the significance of reported improvements.
- Baselines and ablations: Include comparisons against strong personalized baselines (e.g., risk-thresholding with individualized screening intervals, learned hazard models with fixed policies) and relevant reinforcement-learning/MDP heuristics. An ablation that isolates the contribution of screening history versus static features would be informative.
- Generalization and external validity: While the Athena dataset is large, external validation on a different health system or population would bolster claims of generalizability. At minimum, stratified results across demographics and comorbidity profiles would help assess stability.
- Data realism: Clarify how the method handles common EHR issues such as missingness, irregular screening intervals, censoring, and delayed or noisy labels. If imputations or specific modeling choices are used, describe their impact.
- Computational considerations: The paper includes complexity analysis; please also report wall-clock performance and memory usage for training and inference, and discuss feasibility for real-time or near-real-time clinical deployment.
- Policy constraints and safety: In practice, screening schedules are often bounded by clinical guidelines (e.g., maximum intervals, age restrictions). Describe how such constraints are incorporated. Likewise, discuss any guardrails to prevent overly sparse screening for high-risk patients.
- Fairness and equity: Screening performance can vary across subgroups. Report subgroup analyses to ensure personalization does not inadvertently widen disparities.
- Interpretability and workflow: How are recommended policies presented to clinicians? Are decisions explainable (e.g., which aspects of history/features drive earlier or later screening)? Consider usability aspects and potential integration with clinical decision support systems.
- Reproducibility: If possible, provide code or pseudo-code sufficient for implementation, along with details on hyperparameters and model selection.

Minor Comments
- Ensure consistent naming (e.g., “DPSCREEN” vs “DPScreen”) throughout the paper.
- A brief schematic or pseudo-code for the algorithm would aid readers who are less familiar with the underlying optimization framework.
- Clarify notation early (state, action, reward/cost), especially if multiple disease models share a common formalism.

Overall Assessment
This is a well-written, technically solid paper that addresses an important problem with a principled and practically motivated solution. The empirical results on a large real-world dataset, combined with a clear algorithmic contribution, make a compelling case for publication. Addressing the points above—especially sensitivity to modeling assumptions, stronger baselines, and subgroup/robustness analyses—would further strengthen the work and its potential for real-world adoption. I recommend acceptance.
