- Proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing dynamic, personalized screening policies balancing screening cost and delay to disease detection.
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic (non-Markovian) process and incorporates an external information process that can trigger unscheduled visits.
- Uses a tractable PBVI-based approximation on sampled belief states to handle computational intractability of the exact solution.
- Generalizes prior POMDP-based screening work by accommodating non-Markovian disease dynamics and trajectory-dependent costs.
- Demonstrates on a large breast cancer dataset that the policy reduces screening frequency by 28–68% for similar expected detection delay versus annual screening.

- Strength: Addresses an important, clinically relevant problem of personalized, history-dependent screening policies.
- Strength: Rigorous decision-theoretic formulation with technically sound PBVI adaptation and theoretical approximation bounds.
- Strength: Compelling empirical results on real-world data showing substantial screening reductions without increasing detection delay.
- Strength: Paper is clearly written and well-organized, explaining model and methods effectively.

- Weakness: Relies on the strong assumption that an accurate disease progression model can be learned; practical utility hinges on model fidelity.
- Weakness: Methodological novelty is limited—mainly an application/adaptation of existing PBVI rather than a fundamentally new algorithm.
- Weakness: Lacks empirical robustness analysis to model misspecification despite a theoretical robustness result; sensitivity experiments are missing.

- Overall: Solid, well-executed study with promising results but would be strengthened by deeper engagement with model uncertainty and empirical robustness evaluation.
