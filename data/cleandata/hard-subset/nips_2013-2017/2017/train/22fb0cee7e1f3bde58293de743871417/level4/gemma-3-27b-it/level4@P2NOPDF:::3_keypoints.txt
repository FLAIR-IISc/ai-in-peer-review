- Presents a compelling approach to personalize breast cancer screening schedules by optimizing the trade-off between screening costs and delayed-detection risk using patient history and external data.  
- Methodology is clearly written, well-organized, and logically motivated.  
- Experimental results show significant improvement over standardized screening approaches, notably reductions in combined cost.  
- Main strength: moves beyond one-size-fits-all screening by tailoring intervals to individual risk, potentially reducing unnecessary screens and improving timely detection.  
- Needs clearer description of the "external information" sources and discussion of their potential biases and safeguards.  
- Evaluation is too cost-centric; should include clinical metrics (e.g., predicted life expectancy, false positive rates, advanced-stage cancers detected) to assess clinical impact.  
- Sensitivity analysis should be expanded to test robustness to parameter variations (especially cost of delayed detection) and to handle missing/incomplete patient data.  
- Computational complexity and scalability are not sufficiently addressed; include analysis of runtime/cost and feasibility for clinical integration.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address external data details, broader clinical evaluation, expanded sensitivity analyses, and computational considerations.
