This paper addresses the important problem of optimizing patient screening policies based on available information, framing it as a finite-state stochastic process to minimize screening costs and delays. The authors present a computationally efficient, approximate solution and demonstrate promising results on simulated breast cancer data, showing potential for reduced delays at comparable costs to a standard annual screening approach.

Strengths of the work include its focus on a significant area – personalized screening – and the sound, well-articulated development of the proposed algorithm. The experimental results are encouraging, and the paper is clearly written.

However, the work’s practical relevance is questionable. A key limitation is the assumption of known patient-specific disease models, a scenario unlikely to hold in real-world applications. The core challenge in personalized screening lies in *inferring* these models from data, rather than optimizing a policy *given* a known model. Furthermore, the methodological novelty presented is incremental. The experimental evaluation, currently limited to comparison with a fixed annual screening baseline, would be significantly strengthened by investigating the algorithm’s robustness to inaccuracies in the assumed disease model. Specifically, assessing the sensitivity of the resulting policy to uncertainties in model parameters is crucial. 

In summary, while the topic is compelling and the proposed algorithm is well-reasoned, the limited novelty and concerns regarding practical applicability require further attention to convincingly demonstrate the work’s impact.
