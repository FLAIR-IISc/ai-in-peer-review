

This paper addresses the problem of optimizing patient screening policies through modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process, with the goal of minimizing both screening costs and delays. The authors propose a computationally efficient approximate solution and evaluate it using simulated breast cancer data, reporting reduced delays without increased costs compared to an annual screening baseline.  

**Strengths:**  
- The topic of personalized screening is highly relevant to healthcare optimization.  
- The proposed algorithm is logically developed and well-motivated.  
- Results demonstrate clear improvements over the baseline in simulated settings.  
- The paper is clearly written and technically sound.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Lack of practical relevance due to unrealistic assumptions:** The core limitation is the assumption that a patient’s disease model is known a priori—a condition rarely achievable in real-world practice. The primary challenge in personalized screening lies not in policy derivation *given* a model, but in reliably inferring the model from observational data. Without addressing this fundamental hurdle, the proposed method remains an academic exercise rather than a practical tool.  
2. **Limited methodological novelty:** The approach builds on established stochastic control frameworks with minimal innovation. The incremental contribution to the field is not sufficiently articulated or justified.  
3. **Inadequate experimental validation:** The evaluation solely compares against annual screening, omitting critical comparisons to more sophisticated baselines (e.g., adaptive policies). Crucially, the paper fails to assess robustness to model inaccuracies—a key concern when disease dynamics are estimated from data. The authors should explicitly test how policy performance degrades under varying degrees of model uncertainty (e.g., via sensitivity analysis or synthetic data with perturbed parameters).  

**Overall Assessment:**  
While the topic is compelling and the algorithm is reasonable, the paper’s practical impact is undermined by its narrow focus on a scenario (known disease models) that does not reflect real-world constraints. The methodological novelty is modest, and the experimental design lacks depth in addressing the central challenge of model uncertainty. To strengthen the contribution, the authors must:  
- Demonstrate how the disease model can be inferred from data (e.g., via learning from historical patient records),  
- Validate the method against realistic baselines that account for model learning,  
- Provide empirical evidence of robustness to model inaccuracies.  

Without these elements, the work falls short of establishing a meaningful advance for personalized screening. The results are promising *in isolation* but fail to convince readers that the proposed framework solves a clinically actionable problem. The paper would benefit significantly from reframing its evaluation around the *actual* data-driven challenge of model inference rather than assuming perfect model knowledge.
