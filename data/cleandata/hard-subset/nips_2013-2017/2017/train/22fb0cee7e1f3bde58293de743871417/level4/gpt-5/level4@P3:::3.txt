Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that balance screening costs with delay costs in disease detection. The disease process is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, without restricting to Markov or semi-Markov dynamics. The key novelty is allowing screening times to be endogenously determined by both clinician recommendations and exogenous “external information” (e.g., self-exams, comorbidities) that can trigger early arrivals. The decision objective combines discounted screening cost and delay cost, where delay depends on the trajectory (incidence and detection times). The authors show the value function is convex, piecewise linear in belief, and they derive an algorithmic approach based on point-based value iteration (PBVI) over a sampled, reduced belief space constructed from likely state trajectories and reachable beliefs. They provide a bound relating performance loss to the density of sampled beliefs and argue robustness to model estimation error via a local constancy result for the optimal policy.

Empirically, they estimate a two-state (H, D) disease model for breast cancer from a large real-world dataset (Athena, ~45k patients, ~84k mammograms), use Gail scores for risk stratification, and model external information via a tumor growth process that triggers early visits when a lump exceeds a threshold. Policies are tuned to minimize expected screenings subject to matching the expected delay of standard clinical schedules (annual or biennial). DPSCREEN reduces screenings by 28–68% while maintaining comparable expected delays, and the policy adapts to both baseline risk and evolving test/external information history.

Relative to prior work, DPSCREEN generalizes POMDP-based screening for specific diseases with Markov assumptions and fixed decision epochs [7, 9], Bayesian design approaches focused on information gain rather than delay costs [10], and simulation-based guideline generation (e.g., MISCAN-COLON [46]). It also connects to controlled sensing [21, 34, 38], but relaxes stationarity/Markov structure and incorporates endogenous arrival.

Strengths
- Problem importance and scope: Personalized screening with dynamic histories and exogenous arrivals is clinically relevant across diseases.
- Modeling generality: Allows arbitrary finite-state disease trajectories, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and external information processes correlated with disease.
- Decision-theoretic formulation: Belief-state dynamic program with a non-standard cost structure; proof that the value function remains convex and piecewise linear.
- Algorithmic contribution: A practical PBVI-style approximation tailored to a very high-dimensional belief space via trajectory sampling and reachable-belief construction; complexity and parallelization discussion.
- Theoretical support: An approximation bound tied to belief sampling density and a robustness result indicating local policy stability to model estimation error.
- Empirical illustration: Real-world dataset, personalization by risk group and history, and sizable reductions in screening burden at comparable detection delays. Analyses with and without self-exams and exploration of disease-dependent delay-cost curvature.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Evaluation relies on a learned, simplified disease model (two-state Markov) rather than the general non-Markov setting emphasized. It would strengthen the case to show at least one example beyond two-state Markov.
- The choice of tuning weight w by matching expected delay to clinical benchmarks is reasonable, but the fairness of comparisons depends on the delay metric. Unconditional delay E[Δ|R] is dominated by non-cases; conditional E[Δ|R, D] helps but the paper does not discuss other harms (e.g., false positives, biopsies, anxiety) beyond counting all tests equally. Incorporating distinct costs (e.g., mammogram vs biopsy, false-positive follow-ups) would improve realism.
- Assumptions and consistency: Early sections state “no false positives” when z ∈ Z+ triggers diagnosis; later the empirical model includes mammogram-positive/biopsy-negative outcomes (z = 2). Clarify that definitive diagnosis corresponds to biopsy-positive and reconcile assumptions across sections and the bound in Proposition 1 (which also assumes no false negatives).
- Scalability and sample efficiency: The reduced belief construction depends on sampling K trajectories and reachable beliefs under random and policy-guided exploration. Practical guidance on K, |B̄|, runtime, and sensitivity of performance to sampling density is limited; reporting wall-clock times and ablations would help.
- External information model: The lump-threshold arrival process can materially drive the gains; sensitivity analyses to ι, σ, and ỹ are mentioned but the extent of robustness is unclear. More systematic sensitivity/ablation would increase confidence.
- Theoretical results: The approximation bound is high-level (via Ω(B̄)) and the local constancy theorem is standard argmax stability; both are helpful but do not yield actionable sample-complexity guidance.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a careful formulation and sensible approximation strategy. Some modeling assumptions and evaluation choices could be better justified, and more detail on computational resources and sensitivity would strengthen the empirical claims.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, though dense. A few issues to address:
- Fix typos and formatting (e.g., “st Conference …”).
- Clarify sign conventions in the Bellman equation and the cost function C̃ (ensure consistent “cost minimization” presentation).
- Reconcile assumptions about false positives/negatives across sections.
- Define the action space more explicitly (bounds on inter-screening intervals, discrete vs continuous time-to-next-screen).

Originality
Good. The combination of endogenous arrivals, trajectory-based delay costs, and a practical PBVI variant over sampled belief subsets appears novel in screening contexts and extends beyond standard POMDP treatments.

Significance
Promising. If robust, the approach could materially reduce screening burden while preserving timely detection, and the framework is adaptable across diseases and cost structures. The real-world dataset and personalization are compelling.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide runtime, memory, K and |B̄| statistics, and parallelization details for the breast cancer experiments.
- Add sensitivity analyses for external-information parameters and mis-specification of test accuracy; consider heterogenous test costs and explicit false-positive/biopsy harms.
- Demonstrate at least one non-Markov disease model or multi-state progression in simulation to support generality claims.
- Clarify how ỹ is set (patient- vs population-level) and whether adherence/behavioral variability is modeled.
- Expand discussion on selecting w (e.g., via constraints or multi-objective trade-off curves) and on clinical interpretability of policies.

Pros
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a general framework.
- Sound decision-theoretic formulation and practical approximate solution.
- Strong empirical reductions in screening with maintained delays.
- Personalization to both baseline risk and dynamic history.
- Theoretically grounded (value function structure, approximation/robustness).

Cons
- Empirical validation limited to a simplified two-state Markov model.
- Limited accounting for varied test harms and false positives.
- Sparse computational and sensitivity reporting.
- Some assumption inconsistencies and notation issues.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper makes a meaningful methodological and applied contribution to dynamic personalized screening. With clarifications and additional sensitivity/complexity reporting, it would be a strong addition to the program.
