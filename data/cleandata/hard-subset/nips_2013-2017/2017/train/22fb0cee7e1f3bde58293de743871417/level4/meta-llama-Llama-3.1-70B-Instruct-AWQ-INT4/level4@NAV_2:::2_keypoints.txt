- Paper proposes DPSCREEN, a method for constructing personalized screening policies balancing screening cost and delay cost in disease detection.  
- Models disease as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and allows external info (self-exam, comorbidities) to trigger visits.  
- Formulates the problem as a POMDP and uses point-based value iteration (PBVI) to compute approximately optimal policies.  
- DPSCREEN is claimed to be computationally efficient and able to handle complex disease models.  
- Empirical evaluation on a large breast cancer dataset shows reduced number of screenings while maintaining comparable detection delay.  
- Paper is well-written and presents promising results.  
- Major limitation: assumes each patient’s disease model is known a priori, which is unrealistic in practice.  
- No experiments assessing robustness to inaccuracies or misspecification of the disease model.  
- Methodological novelty is limited since PBVI for POMDPs is established; novelty lies mainly in the application to personalized screening.  
- Experimental comparison is limited, only benchmarked against annual screening baseline.  
- Lacks practical guidance on using the approach when the disease model must be inferred from data.  
- Suggestions: evaluate robustness to model error, discuss practical deployment without known models, compare to other approaches (e.g., stochastic simulation-based methods), and detail computational complexity and implementation considerations.
