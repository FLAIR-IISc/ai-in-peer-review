The paper aims to derive an optimal screening policy for patients given available information, modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process. The policy seeks to minimize both screening costs and diagnostic delays. The authors propose a computationally efficient approximate solution. Experiments on simulated breast cancer data suggest that the algorithm can reduce delays while maintaining the same cost compared to a trivial annual-screening baseline.

Positives:
+ Personalized screening is an important research area with clear potential impact.
+ The proposed algorithm is reasonable, clearly described, and technically well developed.
+ The empirical results are promising.
+ The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

Negatives:
- The approach is largely an academic exercise. It assumes the disease model for a given patient is known, which is not realistic in practice. The central challenge in personalized screening is not devising a policy when the model is known, but inferring or learning the model from data—often limited, noisy, and heterogeneous. Without addressing model estimation or uncertainty, the practical utility of the method is limited.
- The methodological novelty appears modest.
- The experimental evaluation is narrow, comparing only to a baseline of annual screening. Given the reliance on a known disease model, a crucial question—best addressed empirically—is the robustness of the policy to model misspecification. In particular, how sensitive is the screening policy to inaccuracies or uncertainties in the disease model? An analysis of robustness to parameter errors, structural misspecification, or patient heterogeneity would significantly strengthen the claims.

Overall, the topic is important and the proposed algorithm is reasonable. However, the methodological novelty is not substantial, and the paper needs to do more to convince readers of its practical relevance—especially by addressing how the disease model would be obtained in practice and by evaluating the robustness of the policy to model uncertainty.
