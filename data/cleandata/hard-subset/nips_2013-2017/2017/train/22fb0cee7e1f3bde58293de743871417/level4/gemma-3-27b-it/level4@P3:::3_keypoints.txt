- Introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for personalized screening that balances screening costs and detection delays using a finite-state stochastic disease model.
- Incorporates patient features and dynamically updated external information (e.g., self-exams) and test results, moving beyond common Markovian and stationary test-outcome assumptions.
- Provides a computationally tractable approximation to the optimal screening policy and bounds on approximation error.
- Demonstrates empirical effectiveness on a large breast cancer dataset, reporting 28–68% reductions in screening frequency with comparable detection delays.
- Positions the work relative to operations research and medical-simulation approaches and builds on POMDPs and dynamic survival models.
- Includes a robustness analysis assessing the impact of model estimation error.
- Strength: addresses an important, high-impact problem in personalized screening with a novel, expressive framework.
- Strength: method appears theoretically sound with clear model formulation and approximation-error bounds.
- Strength: strong empirical validation on real-world data supporting practical benefits.
- Strength: paper is well-written and aided by a graphical model for clarity.
- Weakness: computational complexity (point-based value iteration complexity) remains a concern for large state spaces and long horizons.
- Weakness: simplifying test-outcome model (assuming no false positives) limits realism.
- Weakness: reliance on a two-state Markov disease model may not capture all disease progression nuances.
- Weakness: assumption that external information is independent of past test results conditional on state/features may be too restrictive.
- Weakness: use of a linear delay cost is arbitrary; broader sensitivity analysis of cost functions is needed.
- Weakness: limited benchmarking against other personalized or rule-based screening strategies.
- For acceptance: addresses a relevant challenge, offers a significant technical advance, and shows promising empirical and robustness results.
- Against acceptance: applicability may be limited by computational cost and simplifying modeling assumptions.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to detail scalability/optimizations, perform sensitivity analysis on delay costs, expand comparisons to alternative screening approaches, and discuss correlations between external information and past test results and relaxing the no–false-positive assumption.
