

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that dynamically balance the trade-off between screening frequency and disease detection delay. The authors address a critical gap in the literature by developing a general model where disease progression is represented as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, while incorporating both patient-specific features (e.g., age, family history) and dynamic external information (e.g., self-examinations, comorbidities) that can trigger earlier screenings. The key innovation is a computationally tractable approximation to the optimal policy, which the authors rigorously bound in terms of approximation error. Their approach is validated on a large breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients) from Athena Health Network, demonstrating 28-68% reductions in screening frequency compared to standard clinical policies while maintaining equivalent expected detection delays.

The paper excels in several dimensions. First, it provides a compelling theoretical foundation, clearly articulating the limitations of prior approaches (e.g., [7], [9], [10]) that restrict disease models to Markov processes and stationary test outcomes, while DPSCREEN accommodates more complex disease dynamics. The mathematical development is thorough, with well-motivated Bellman equations and a convincing proof that the value function is convex and piecewise linear—enabling their point-based value iteration approach. The approximation error analysis (Proposition 1) is particularly strong, providing a concrete bound on performance loss relative to the optimal policy, which is rare in applied decision theory papers.

The empirical evaluation is robust and well-executed. The authors carefully model breast cancer progression using a two-state Markov framework, estimate parameters from real data, and validate their policy against standard clinical practices (annual/biennial screening). The results are striking: personalized screening reduces unnecessary tests by up to 68% without compromising detection timeliness, with clear evidence of personalization across risk groups (Table 1). The additional analysis showing how different delay cost functions (linear vs. quadratic) yield dramatically different screening regimes (Figure 2) further underscores the method's flexibility and practical relevance.

The paper also demonstrates strong awareness of related work. In Section 5, the authors effectively contrast their approach with both operations research frameworks (e.g., [7], [9]) and medical literature (e.g., MISCAN-COLON [46]), highlighting how DPSCREEN's minimal assumptions about disease progression and external information processes make it broadly applicable. The discussion of robustness to model estimation errors (Theorem 1) is particularly valuable for real-world deployment.

While the paper is technically sound and well-motivated, a few minor areas could be strengthened. The discussion of computational complexity (O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)) would benefit from concrete examples of how this scales with realistic dataset sizes. Additionally, the paper focuses exclusively on breast cancer; while the methodology is general, a brief discussion of how the framework might adapt to diseases with different progression patterns (e.g., pancreatic cancer with rapid decline in survival) would further emphasize its broad applicability. The validation of the approximation error bound (Ω(B̄)) in practice—beyond the theoretical Proposition 1—would also add empirical credibility.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances both the theoretical and practical understanding of personalized screening. The paper clearly meets NIPS criteria: it's technically sound with rigorous analysis and validation; well-written with clear organization; original in its general framework and approximation approach; and significant in its potential to reduce healthcare costs without compromising patient outcomes. The results are not merely incremental—they demonstrate substantial practical gains (28-68% fewer screenings) that could directly inform clinical guidelines. The authors' careful treatment of limitations (e.g., the absorbing state restriction in Section 2) and their thoughtful comparison to prior work further enhance the paper's credibility.

I recommend acceptance as a strong contribution to the field. The work is well-positioned to influence both academic research in sequential decision making and practical applications in healthcare systems.
