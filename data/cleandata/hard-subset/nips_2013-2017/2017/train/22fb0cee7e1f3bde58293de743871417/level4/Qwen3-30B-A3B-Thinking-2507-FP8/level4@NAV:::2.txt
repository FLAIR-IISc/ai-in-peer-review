

The paper aims to determine optimal patient screening policies using a finite state stochastic disease model, with the objective of minimizing screening costs and delays. The authors introduce a computationally efficient approximate solution and evaluate it on simulated breast cancer data, demonstrating that their algorithm reduces delays while maintaining equivalent costs relative to a baseline annual screening approach.  

Positives:  
+ Personalized screening represents a significant and timely research direction.  
+ The proposed algorithm is logically sound and well-structured.  
+ Results show clear promise for practical application.  
+ The paper is clearly and professionally written.  

Negatives:  
- The solution constitutes a purely academic exercise, as it assumes the disease model for a specific patient is known—a condition rarely feasible in real-world settings. The core challenge in personalized screening lies not in policy derivation given a known model, but in model inference from clinical data.  
- The methodological novelty is limited.  
- Experiments only benchmark against annual screening, neglecting a critical practical concern: the proposed method's robustness to inaccuracies in the disease model. Specifically, the sensitivity of the policy to model uncertainties must be empirically assessed.  

Overall, while the topic is compelling and the algorithm is reasonable, the work lacks substantial methodological novelty and insufficiently addresses practical relevance. The paper requires stronger evidence to justify its real-world applicability.
