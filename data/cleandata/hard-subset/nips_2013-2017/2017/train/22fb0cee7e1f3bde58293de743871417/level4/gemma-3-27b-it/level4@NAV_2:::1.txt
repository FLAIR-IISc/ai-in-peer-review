This paper presents DPSCREEN, a novel method for constructing optimal screening policies applicable to a broad range of diseases. A key contribution lies in integrating both patient-specific clinical history (screening results) and relevant disease-related features into the policy construction process, enabling personalized screening strategies. This personalization demonstrably improves efficiency—reducing the frequency of screenings—without compromising the timeliness of disease detection.

Overall, I found this paper technically sound and well-written. The approach is well-motivated, appropriately contextualized within related work (e.g., [7], [9], [10]), and provides a concise yet thorough presentation of the underlying mathematical framework, including a discussion of computational complexity. The validation on a large dataset (45,000 patients) from the Athena Health Network provides strong evidence of the benefits of personalization, and the exploration of different disease models further strengthens the findings. The work represents a clear technical contribution with potential for practical impact.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The framework addresses a significant gap in personalized screening policies, moving beyond population-based approaches.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The methodology is mathematically well-defined and grounded in established decision-making theory.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The use of a real-world dataset (Athena Health Network) and demonstration of improved efficiency make the work highly relevant.
*   **Generalizability:** The method’s applicability to diverse disease models (illustrated with breast cancer and consideration of different cost functions) is a significant strength.
*   **Thoroughness:** The paper addresses challenges such as estimation error and provides bounds on approximation error (Proposition 1).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Model Assumptions:** The reliance on a two-state Markov model for disease progression, while simplifying the analysis, may not fully capture the complexity of all diseases.
*   **Computational Cost:** While the paper acknowledges computational complexity, scaling to extremely large datasets or more complex disease models may remain a challenge.
*   **External Information:** The modeling of external information (e.g., self-examination) relies on several assumptions and parameter estimates which may be sensitive to the specific population studied.
*   **Limited Comparison:** While comparisons to current clinical policies are provided, comparing against other personalized screening approaches (if available) would further strengthen the analysis.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles an important problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The experimental results demonstrate substantial improvements in screening efficiency.
*   The work has the potential to significantly impact clinical practice.
*   The authors acknowledge limitations and potential avenues for future work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The model assumptions may limit the generalizability of the findings.
*   The computational complexity could hinder practical application in certain scenarios.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend accepting this paper. The strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and the contribution is substantial. The authors have presented a well-researched and thoughtfully written paper that advances the state of the art in personalized screening. Addressing the weaknesses mentioned above in future work would further solidify the impact of this research.
