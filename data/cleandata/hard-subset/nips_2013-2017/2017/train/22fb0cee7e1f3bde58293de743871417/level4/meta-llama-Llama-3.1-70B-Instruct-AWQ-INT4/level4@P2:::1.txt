This paper presents DPSCREEN, a method for determining optimal screening policies for large classes of diseases. Compared to other work in the area, DPSCREEN takes into account both the patient's clinical (screening) history, in addition to patient features pertinent to the specific disease model. As a result, DPSCREEN is able to achieve personalization in the screening process, which yields efficiency gains over existing screening policies without negatively impacting the timeliness of disease detection.

The authors provide a thorough discussion of the mathematical basis of the algorithm, including computational complexity, and demonstrate its effectiveness on a large dataset of breast cancer patients. The paper also considers various disease models and shows that the proposed approach can be applied to different types of diseases.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to balance technical soundness with practical relevance. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the algorithm and its underlying assumptions, making it accessible to a wide range of readers. At the same time, the paper demonstrates the potential practical impact of the proposed approach, which could lead to more efficient and effective screening policies.

The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with a clear structure that guides the reader through the problem formulation, algorithm development, and experimental evaluation. The authors also provide a thorough review of related work, highlighting the contributions of their approach and its differences from existing methods.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors provide a detailed discussion of the algorithm's computational complexity and its scalability to large datasets.

The originality of the paper lies in its ability to develop a personalized screening policy that takes into account both patient features and clinical history. This approach is novel and has the potential to lead to more efficient and effective screening policies.
