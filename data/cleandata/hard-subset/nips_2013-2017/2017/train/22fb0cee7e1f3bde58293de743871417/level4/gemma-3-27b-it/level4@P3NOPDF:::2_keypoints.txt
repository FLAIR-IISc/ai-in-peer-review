- Addresses important problem of personalized patient screening to minimize screening costs and detection delays.  
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and proposes an approximate, computationally efficient policy algorithm.  
- Algorithm is well-developed, clearly presented, and experimentally promising on simulated breast cancer data.  
- Paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.  
- Major limitation: assumes a known disease model per patient, which severely limits practical applicability.  
- Does not address the crucial problem of inferring disease models from limited/noisy patient data.  
- Methodological novelty is modest, building on existing dynamic programming techniques rather than introducing new algorithms.  
- Experimental evaluation is narrow: only compares to a fixed annual screening baseline.  
- Lacks robustness analysis to model misspecification or parameter uncertainty, which is critical for real-world use.  
- Recommended improvements: acknowledge model-inference limitations, test sensitivity to model errors, and compare to adaptive baselines that learn/update models.  
- Overall assessment: interesting theoretical exploration with sound algorithmic work but limited practical impact; borderline accept/reject pending major revisions.
