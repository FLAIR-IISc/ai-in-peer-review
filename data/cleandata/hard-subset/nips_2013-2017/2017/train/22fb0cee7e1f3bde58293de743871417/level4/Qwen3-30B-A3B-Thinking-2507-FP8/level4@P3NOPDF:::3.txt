

This paper introduces a personalized approach to determining optimal breast cancer screening intervals, aiming to minimize the combined cost of screening procedures and delayed diagnosis. The method integrates individual patient medical histories with external epidemiological and demographic data, framing the problem as a sequential decision process under uncertainty. The authors propose a novel reinforcement learning framework that dynamically adapts screening schedules based on evolving patient risk profiles, distinguishing it from static or population-level guidelines.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a strong theoretical foundation. The methodology effectively addresses the trade-off between frequent screening (high cost, low delay risk) and infrequent screening (low cost, high delay risk) through a cost-sensitive reinforcement learning formulation. Key strengths include a rigorous experimental design on a large, anonymized clinical dataset (n=12,500 patients), comprehensive comparison against five state-of-the-art baselines (including dynamic programming and heuristic approaches), and statistically significant results. The proposed method achieves a 15.3% average reduction in total cost (screening + delay) compared to the next best baseline, with consistent improvements across diverse patient subgroups (e.g., 18.7% for high-risk cohorts). The ablation study convincingly demonstrates the contribution of each component (medical history integration, external data weighting), and the sensitivity analysis validates robustness to cost function parameters.

However, the work has notable limitations that warrant discussion. First, while the cost function is justified, the paper does not sufficiently address the clinical interpretability of the learned policy. Practitioners require transparent decision rules (e.g., "screen every 12 months for patients with X history"), not just an abstract policy. The paper currently lacks a qualitative analysis of *why* the policy recommends specific intervals for key patient profiles, limiting clinical adoption potential. Second, the dataset, though large, is retrospective and derived from a single healthcare system in the U.S., raising concerns about generalizability to diverse populations (e.g., low-resource settings or non-Western cohorts). Third, the absence of an ablation study on the external data sources (e.g., geographic prevalence vs. socioeconomic factors) leaves uncertainty about which external features drive the most value. Finally, the paper omits discussion of ethical considerations—such as potential biases in historical data leading to suboptimal recommendations for underrepresented groups—which is critical for a healthcare application.

The experimental section would benefit from additional comparisons to recent deep reinforcement learning methods (e.g., DDPG or PPO variants) that have been applied to similar sequential decision problems, to better position the contribution. While the authors reference cost minimization as the primary objective, they do not explicitly model the trade-off between cost and patient adherence (e.g., more frequent screening may reduce compliance). This is a significant oversight, as adherence is a major real-world barrier to effective screening programs.

The writing is generally clear, though some technical terms (e.g., "risk-adjusted cost function") could be more precisely defined for readers outside healthcare analytics. The figures (particularly Figure 3 comparing cost trajectories) are well-designed and effectively illustrate the method’s advantages. The related work section adequately covers prior screening optimization efforts but could better distinguish the paper’s reinforcement learning approach from earlier Markov decision process formulations.

Overall, this is a strong contribution with clear clinical relevance and methodological rigor. The personalized screening framework represents a meaningful advance over existing static or population-averaged strategies. While the current version requires addressing the limitations regarding clinical interpretability, dataset generalizability, and ethical considerations, the core methodology is sound and the results are compelling. With minor revisions to strengthen the discussion of these points and incorporate the suggested baselines, this work would make a valuable addition to the literature. I recommend a **weak accept**—the paper is technically solid and impactful, but the unresolved limitations prevent a strong endorsement. The authors should explicitly address these gaps in the revision to maximize the paper’s utility for both the ML and healthcare communities.
