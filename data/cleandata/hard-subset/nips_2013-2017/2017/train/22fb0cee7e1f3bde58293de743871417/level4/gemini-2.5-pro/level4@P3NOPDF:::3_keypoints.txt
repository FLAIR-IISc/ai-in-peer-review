- Paper proposes a personalized breast cancer screening scheduler that balances screening cost vs. delay cost using patient history and external data in an optimization framework.  
- Method reportedly outperforms standard-of-care guidelines and baselines in cost-effectiveness in empirical evaluations.  

Strengths:
- Addresses a clinically important and novel problem: moving from one-size-fits-all to risk-stratified screening.  
- Personalized recommendations leverage individual patient history to reduce over- and under-screening.  
- Strong empirical results with well-designed experiments showing significant efficiency gains.  
- Clear, well-written presentation and motivation.

Weaknesses / Questions:
- Model and feature specification are vague; "external information" and the full feature set need detailed definition and ablation/feature importance analysis.  
- Cost function formulation is unclear: how screening and delay costs were quantified (financial, QALYs, anxiety, radiation risk) and how weighting was chosen needs explanation and sensitivity analysis.  
- Interpretability concerns: model should provide explanations (e.g., highlight risk factors) and discussion of clinician interaction/trust to avoid a black-box barrier to adoption.  
- Generalizability and dataset bias: need dataset demographic details, analysis of performance across populations, and discussion of bias mitigation to avoid worsening disparities.

Minor comments:
- Define all acronyms at first use.  
- Make figure captions more descriptive.  
- Include a brief discussion of computational complexity.

Conclusion:
- Overall strong and valuable contribution; recommend acceptance pending clarifications on feature set, cost definitions, and interpretability for real-world impact.
