Summary
This paper proposes a personalized method to recommend screening intervals for breast cancer patients by optimizing a trade-off between screening costs and the costs of delayed detection. The approach incorporates each patient’s medical history together with external information to tailor recommendations. The paper is clearly written, and the empirical results suggest substantial improvements over existing strategies.

Strengths
- Important problem: Determining when to screen is clinically and economically consequential. A personalized, cost-sensitive approach is timely and relevant.
- Conceptual soundness: Explicitly modeling the trade-off between screening and delay costs aligns with real-world decision-making and health economics.
- Personalization: Leveraging individual medical histories and external covariates is likely to outperform fixed-interval guidelines or one-size-fits-all risk thresholds.
- Empirical gains: The reported performance improvements over baselines are encouraging and suggest practical utility.
- Clarity: The exposition is generally clear, with a well-motivated problem statement and intuitive rationale for the method.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Model and objective details: The paper would benefit from a precise specification of the model class, training objective, and optimization procedure. It is unclear how time-to-event outcomes are modeled, how censoring is handled, and whether time-varying covariates are supported.
- Cost modeling: How are screening and delay costs defined, estimated, and validated? Are they monetary, utility-based, or proxy metrics (e.g., recall rates, stage shift)? Are costs patient-specific (e.g., comorbidities, access) or fixed? A sensitivity analysis over cost parameters is essential.
- External information: Please define “external information” concretely (e.g., population incidence, imaging facility quality, socioeconomic context) and provide an ablation to quantify its contribution.
- Evaluation design: Are results based on retrospective/offline evaluation, counterfactual estimation, or prospective simulation? How is lead-time bias addressed? What metrics are reported beyond average cost (e.g., time to detection, stage at detection, false positive/negative rates, net benefit, decision-curve analysis)?
- Baselines: Comparison should include standard and strong time-to-event and dynamic prediction methods (e.g., Cox PH with time-dependent covariates, AFT models, survival forests, neural survival models). Also include simple risk-stratified scheduling heuristics as competitive baselines.
- Robustness and generalization: It is unclear how the method performs under distribution shift (e.g., new sites, changing screening technology), with missing or sporadically measured covariates, and across demographic subgroups. External validation on an independent cohort would strengthen claims.
- Interpretability: For clinical adoption, explanations of recommended intervals (key features, directionality, SHAP-like analyses) are desirable. Transparency about uncertainty (e.g., predictive intervals on recommended times) would help guide conservative vs. aggressive scheduling.
- Practical constraints: Real-world deployment involves constraints such as minimum/maximum intervals, recall policies, and patient adherence. How are such constraints encoded in the optimization? Does the method allow clinician overrides or shared decision-making with patients?
- Statistical rigor: Please report confidence intervals, statistical tests for differences to baselines, and calibration assessments (e.g., risk calibration, calibration-in-the-large).
- Ethical and fairness considerations: Analyze subgroup performance (age, race/ethnicity, insurance status) to ensure recommendations do not exacerbate disparities. Discuss governance and potential unintended consequences (e.g., under-screening in resource-limited populations).

Questions for the authors
1. How exactly are delay costs operationalized (e.g., missed early detection, progression risk)? How were parameter values chosen and validated with clinical input?
2. How do you handle right-censoring, competing risks, and incomplete follow-up?
3. Are covariates updated longitudinally, and how do you prevent label leakage when constructing features near event times?
4. What safeguards prevent overly long recommended intervals in high-risk individuals?
5. Can the method provide uncertainty-aware recommendations or adjustable risk tolerance levels?
6. What datasets were used, and what is the split strategy (site-level splits, temporal splits)? Is there an external test set?
7. Will code and model checkpoints be released to ensure reproducibility?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a detailed problem formulation: state the decision space (intervals), objective function, constraints, and learning algorithm; provide pseudocode if space allows.
- Include sensitivity analyses over cost parameters and threshold choices; visualize the cost–delay Pareto frontier.
- Provide ablations: without external information; with limited history length; with different feature subsets.
- Report comprehensive metrics, including calibration, net benefit, and subgroup analyses.
- Compare to strong survival/dynamic risk models and to simple risk-threshold scheduling rules.
- Demonstrate robustness to missing data and distribution shift; include an external validation cohort if possible.
- Add interpretability analyses and a clinician-oriented case study illustrating how recommendations would differ from current guidelines.
- Discuss deployment considerations (integration into screening workflows, adherence, clinician oversight) and any regulatory/ethical review.

Clarity and presentation
The manuscript is mostly clear. A schematic figure illustrating inputs, model, decision rule, and outcomes would help. Please move key implementation details (feature handling, censoring, training regimen, hyperparameters) from the supplement into the main text or summarize them prominently.

Reproducibility
The paper should specify datasets, inclusion/exclusion criteria, preprocessing, missing-data strategies, and hyperparameter search. Public code and a minimal reproducible example would be valuable.

Overall assessment
This is a relevant and promising contribution: framing personalized screening interval selection as a cost-sensitive decision problem and demonstrating improvements over existing methods. With additional methodological detail, stronger baselines, sensitivity/robustness analyses, and clarity around costs and evaluation, the paper would be significantly strengthened. I lean toward acceptance contingent on addressing the above points.

Reviewer confidence: Moderate.
