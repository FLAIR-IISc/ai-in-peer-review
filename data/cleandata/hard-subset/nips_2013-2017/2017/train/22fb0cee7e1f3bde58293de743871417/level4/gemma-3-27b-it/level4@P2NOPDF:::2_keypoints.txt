- Tackles an important problem: optimizing personalized patient screening policies to minimize screening costs and delays.  
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and proposes a computationally efficient approximate policy.  
- Empirical results on simulated breast cancer data show reduced delays at comparable costs to annual screening.  
- Strength: personalized screening is highly relevant and impactful.  
- Strength: algorithm is logically sound, well-developed, and clearly presented.  
- Strength: experiments are encouraging and the paper is exceptionally well-written.  
- Major weakness: assumes known disease models for each patient, which is unrealistic in clinical settings.  
- Key missing element: does not address inferring individual disease models from limited, noisy patient data.  
- Novelty is incremental—application of established techniques rather than fundamentally new algorithms or theory.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited by comparing only to a fixed annual screening baseline.  
- Critical missing experiment: sensitivity/robustness analysis to model parameter inaccuracies and uncertainty.  
- Recommendation: address model inference and conduct robustness analyses to improve practical relevance and impact.
