

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel method for optimizing disease screening policies by integrating patients' clinical screening histories with disease-specific features. Unlike prior approaches that typically focus on static patient attributes or generic screening rules, DPSCREEN enables personalized screening strategies that adapt to individual patient trajectories. This personalization yields significant efficiency improvements—reducing unnecessary screenings while maintaining timely disease detection—without compromising clinical timeliness. The technical foundation is rigorously developed, with clear mathematical formulations and thorough analysis of computational complexity. The authors effectively contextualize their work within relevant literature, addressing key limitations of existing methods.  

Validation is conducted on a substantial 45,000-patient dataset from the Athena Health Network, demonstrating robust performance gains across diverse disease scenarios. The model’s generalizability is further confirmed through cross-disease validation, showing consistent improvements when applied to multiple disease types. The paper is well-structured, with a concise yet comprehensive presentation of methodology, experiments, and results. The empirical evaluation convincingly supports the claim that incorporating longitudinal clinical history and disease-specific features enhances screening efficiency.  

The contribution is both technically sound and practically compelling. By bridging personalized medicine with scalable optimization, DPSCREEN offers a pathway to more efficient healthcare resource allocation without sacrificing early detection. The work addresses a critical gap in the literature, and its validation on real-world data strengthens its relevance for clinical adoption. While the paper does not explore implementation challenges in dynamic healthcare settings, the core methodology is sufficiently detailed to enable replication and extension.  

Overall, this is a strong submission that advances the state of the art in adaptive screening. The clarity of exposition, thorough validation, and clear potential for real-world impact make it a valuable addition to the literature. I recommend acceptance.
