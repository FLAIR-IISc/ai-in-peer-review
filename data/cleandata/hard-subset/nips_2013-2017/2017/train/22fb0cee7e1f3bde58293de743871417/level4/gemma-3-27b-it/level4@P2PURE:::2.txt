The paper addresses the problem of optimal patient screening policies given available information, framing disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process. The goal is to minimize both screening costs and delays, and the authors propose a computationally efficient approximate solution. Experiments using simulated breast cancer data suggest the algorithm can reduce delays while maintaining comparable costs to a simple annual screening baseline.

**Positives:**

+ Personalized screening is a valuable and important research direction.
+ The proposed algorithm is well-reasoned and developed.
+ The experimental results are promising.
+ The paper is clearly written.

**Negatives:**

- The algorithm appears largely theoretical, relying on the assumption of known, patient-specific disease models – an unrealistic premise in practical applications. The core challenge in personalized screening lies not in policy derivation *given* a known model, but in model inference *from* data.
- The methodological novelty is limited.
- The experimental evaluation is constrained to comparison with a fixed annual screening baseline. Considering the assumption of known disease models, a crucial investigation would be the robustness of the proposed method to inaccuracies within those models. Specifically, how does uncertainty in the disease model affect the policy’s performance? 

**Overall:**

The topic is interesting and the proposed algorithm is sound. However, the methodological contribution is incremental, and the paper requires further work to demonstrate its practical relevance and address the critical issue of model uncertainty.
