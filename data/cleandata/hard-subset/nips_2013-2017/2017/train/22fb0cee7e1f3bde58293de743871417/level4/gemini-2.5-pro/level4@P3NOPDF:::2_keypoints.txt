- Summary: Proposes an efficient approximate method to optimize patient screening schedules using a finite-state stochastic disease model, showing reduced diagnostic delay vs. annual screening on simulated breast cancer data.

- Strength: Tackles an important societal problem of personalized adaptive screening with potential clinical and resource benefits.

- Strength: Provides a sound, well-motivated stochastic-process formulation and a sensible approximation to address computational complexity.

- Strength: Presents promising simulated results demonstrating improved delay-cost tradeoffs over a fixed annual schedule.

- Strength: Paper is generally clear and well-written.

- Weakness: Assumes patient-specific disease model parameters are known a priori, which is unrealistic and limits practical relevance.

- Weakness: Avoids the core challenge of learning/inferencing disease models from data, making the work more a planning exercise than an end-to-end solution.

- Weakness: Methodological novelty appears modest and should be better situated within approximate dynamic programming/POMDP literature.

- Weakness: Experimental evaluation is limited—only compared to a single simple baseline (annual screening).

- Weakness: Lacks robustness/sensitivity analysis to model misspecification; unclear how performance degrades under incorrect disease dynamics.

- Recommendation: To be competitive, the paper should address model uncertainty (e.g., integrate model learning with planning) or provide thorough robustness experiments.

- Recommendation: Include stronger and more diverse baselines from medical/operations research to better demonstrate advantages.
