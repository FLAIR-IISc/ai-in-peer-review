- Proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized screening that balances screening frequency against detection delay using a finite-state stochastic disease model with absorbing disease state.  
- Incorporates external information processes (e.g., self-exams) and dynamic patient history into policy construction.  
- Develops a computationally tractable method to construct approximately optimal screening policies with theoretical approximation error bounds.  
- Extends prior POMDP-based screening work by handling more general disease models, including dynamic survival processes and non-stationary test outcomes.  
- Provides a unified formulation that accounts for both static patient features (age, family history) and dynamic test/history information.  
- Demonstrates substantial reductions (28–68%) in screening frequency versus standard clinical policies while maintaining equivalent expected detection delays on a large breast cancer dataset.  
- Experiments are well-designed, evaluate multiple risk groups and disease models, and show how cost choices affect screening frequency.  
- Paper is clear, well-organized, and includes sufficient supplementary material (pseudocode, implementation details) for reproducibility.  
- Theoretical foundations are sound and the empirical validation is comprehensive.  
- Originality is meaningful but incremental, building on standard POMDP techniques with context-specific adaptations.  
- Significance is high due to potential reductions in healthcare costs and patient burden and applicability beyond breast cancer.  
- Major limitation: assumes the disease model is known with certainty, which is unrealistic in practice.  
- Fails to analyze sensitivity or performance when the disease model is estimated or misspecified from limited observational data.  
- Limited comparison to alternative personalized screening methods beyond annual screening; methodological novelty and scalability claims could be better contextualized.  
- Recommendation: accept with the expectation that authors address model uncertainty, robustness to estimation error, and broader method comparisons in revisions.
