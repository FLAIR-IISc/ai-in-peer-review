- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic personalized screening balancing screening frequency and detection delay.  
- Models disease progression as a general finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and incorporates time-varying external information.  
- Generalizes prior work by handling non-Markov processes and dynamic history, unlike restrictive Markov/stationary test assumptions.  
- Provides a computationally tractable approximation to the optimal policy with rigorous error bounds (Proposition 1).  
- Theoretical foundations are strong: Bellman characterization, convexity of value function (Lemma 1), and proven approximation error.  
- Empirically validated on a large breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients) with clinically relevant risk stratification.  
- Achieves 28–68% fewer screenings than annual/biennial standards while maintaining identical expected detection delays.  
- Sensitivity analysis of cost functions demonstrates the need for disease-specific policy design (Fig. 2).  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-structured, with precise definitions and comprehensive supplementary materials for reproducibility.  
- Original contribution: first framework handling general disease models with external information and dynamic history in screening policies.  
- Significant practical impact by reducing screening burden and costs without compromising clinical efficacy.  
- Limitation: restricted to finite-state disease models, limiting immediate applicability to continuous-state disease dynamics.  
- Lack of concrete guidance on selecting cost functions for diseases beyond the presented breast cancer example.  
- Implementation details and computational complexity trade-offs (scalability) could be expanded.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept — substantial, well-executed contribution with strong theory, empirical validation, and clinical relevance.
