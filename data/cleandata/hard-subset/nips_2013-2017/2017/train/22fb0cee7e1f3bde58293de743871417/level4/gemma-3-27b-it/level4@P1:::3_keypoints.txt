- Problem: Develop personalized screening policies balancing early detection benefits with screening costs/risks.  
- Proposal: Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process to construct personalized screening schedules.  
- Personalization: Incorporates patient-specific features and dynamic history (external info and prior tests) for scheduling decisions.  
- Generality: Handles non-Markovian disease processes and relaxes common Markovian/population-level assumptions.  
- Technical contribution: Provides a mathematically rigorous formulation, a tractable approximation algorithm, and approximation error bounds.  
- Empirical results: On a large breast cancer dataset, shows 28–68% reduction in screening frequency with comparable detection delays.  
- Practical relevance: Demonstrates real-world applicability and potential to reduce patient burden.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-written, organized, and clearly presents model, methods, and experiments.  
- Robustness: Includes analysis of estimation error and its impact on policy performance.  
- Assumptions: Relies on assumptions like conditional independence of test results that may affect sensitivity of results.  
- Computational complexity: Approximation is tractable but worst-case complexity remains substantial, raising scalability concerns.  
- Generalizability: Evaluation limited mostly to breast cancer; broader disease/data distributions need further testing.  
- Cost modeling: Uses a linear delay cost which may be simplistic; implications of alternative cost functions are underexplored.  
- Recommendation: Strong, novel, and technically sound work with acknowledged limitations; reviewer recommends acceptance.
