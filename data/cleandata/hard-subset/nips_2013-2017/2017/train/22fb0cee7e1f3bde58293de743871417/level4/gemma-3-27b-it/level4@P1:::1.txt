This paper presents DPSCREEN, a method for determining optimal screening policies for a large class of diseases. In contrast to existing approaches, DPSCREEN incorporates both a patient’s clinical history (screening results) and individual features relevant to the disease model. This personalization leads to efficiency gains over current screening policies without compromising the timeliness of disease detection.

Overall, I found this paper to be technically sound and clearly written. The approach is well-motivated, acknowledges relevant related work, and provides a concise yet thorough discussion of the mathematical foundations of the algorithm, including its computational complexity. DPSCREEN is validated using a large dataset (45,000 patients) from the Athena Health Network, demonstrating the benefits of personalization. Furthermore, the model’s performance is evaluated across different disease model types, highlighting its generalizability. The result is a paper that makes a significant technical contribution with potential for practical impact.

The paper effectively frames the problem of personalized screening, highlighting the limitations of existing methods that often rely on Markovian assumptions or stationary processes, which may not hold true for many diseases. The authors convincingly argue for a more flexible framework that can accommodate dynamic disease progression and external information.

The strength of this work lies in its generality. By modeling the disease as a finite state stochastic process, the framework can be applied to a wide variety of diseases. The inclusion of external information, such as patient self-examinations, further enhances the realism and potential effectiveness of the proposed policies. The theoretical analysis, including the approximation error bounds, adds to the rigor of the work.

However, a few minor points could be improved. While the authors acknowledge the challenges of estimating model parameters from observational data, the specific details of the estimation procedure could be elaborated further. Providing more details regarding the sensitivity of the results to the accuracy of these estimated parameters would be beneficial. Additionally, the assumption of no false positives in the test outcomes might be overly restrictive and worth discussing in the context of real-world applications.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed methodology is mathematically well-founded and supported by theoretical analysis.
*   **Novelty:** The framework offers a novel approach to personalized screening that addresses limitations of existing methods.
*   **Practical Impact:** The potential for reducing the number of screenings while maintaining disease detection rates is significant.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The results on the breast cancer dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   **Generality:** The framework is applicable to a wide range of diseases.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   None major. The paper is strong and makes a valuable contribution.
*   Further details on the parameter estimation procedure would be beneficial.
*   Discussion of the impact of potential false positives in the test outcomes could strengthen the work.
