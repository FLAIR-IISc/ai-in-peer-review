The objective of this paper is to determine the optimal policy for patient screening given relevant information. To achieve this, the authors model a disease as a finite state stochastic process, with the goal of minimizing screening costs and delays. They propose an approximate solution that is computationally efficient. Experiments conducted on simulated breast cancer data indicate that the proposed algorithm can reduce delays while maintaining the same cost compared to a trivial baseline.

Positives:
+ Personalized screening is an important research topic.
+ The proposed algorithm is reasonable and well-developed.
+ The results are promising.
+ The paper is well-written.

Negatives:
- The proposed algorithm appears to be a purely academic exercise, as it assumes that the disease model for a given patient is known. In practice, this is often not the case, and the primary challenge in personalized screening lies in inferring the model from data rather than developing a policy based on a known model.
- The methodological novelty of this work is limited.
- The experiments only compare the proposed algorithm to a baseline that performs annual screening. Given the assumption of a known disease model, it is crucial to investigate the robustness of the proposed method to inaccuracies in the disease model experimentally. Specifically, it would be beneficial to examine the sensitivity of the policy to uncertainties in the disease model.

Overall, while the topic is interesting and the proposed algorithm is reasonable, the methodological novelty is not substantial, and the paper would need to provide more convincing evidence of its practical relevance.
