- Introduces a personalized reinforcement-learning approach to optimize breast cancer screening intervals minimizing screening + delayed-diagnosis costs.  
- Integrates individual medical histories with external epidemiological and demographic data in a sequential decision framework under uncertainty.  
- Method dynamically adapts screening schedules based on evolving patient risk, contrasting with static or population-level guidelines.  
- Well-structured, clearly written paper with a strong theoretical foundation.  
- Cost-sensitive RL formulation effectively balances frequent screening (high cost, low delay) vs infrequent screening (low cost, high delay).  
- Rigorous experiments on a large anonymized dataset (n=12,500) with comprehensive comparisons to five state-of-the-art baselines, including dynamic programming and heuristics.  
- Achieves a 15.3% average reduction in total cost vs the next best baseline, with consistent subgroup gains (e.g., 18.7% for high-risk cohorts).  
- Ablation study shows each component (medical history integration, external data weighting) contributes meaningfully; sensitivity analysis shows robustness to cost parameters.  
- Lacks clinical interpretability and qualitative explanations of why the policy recommends specific intervals, limiting practitioner adoption.  
- Dataset is retrospective and from a single U.S. healthcare system, raising concerns about generalizability to other populations and low-resource settings.  
- No ablation isolating the impact of different external data sources (geographic vs socioeconomic), leaving feature importance unclear.  
- Omits discussion of ethical issues and potential biases in historical data affecting underrepresented groups.  
- Would benefit from comparisons to recent deep RL methods (e.g., DDPG, PPO) to better situate the contribution.  
- Does not model patient adherence or the trade-off between cost and compliance, a key real-world constraint.  
- Some technical terms need clearer definitions for non-specialist readers; figures (e.g., Figure 3) are effective.  
- Related work coverage is adequate but should better differentiate this RL approach from prior MDP-based formulations.  
- Overall assessment: strong, clinically relevant, and methodologically rigorous work with a recommendation of weak accept pending revisions to address interpretability, generalizability, ethical concerns, and additional baselines.
