- Introduces DPSCREEN, a method optimizing disease screening by combining patient clinical history with disease-specific features.  
- Novelty: uses longitudinal data to create personalized screening schedules vs. prior static or generic approaches.  
- Personalization yields efficiency gains by reducing unnecessary screenings while preserving timely detection.  
- Empirical results: 15–22% reduction in unnecessary screenings with no loss in early-stage detection.  
- Technical core: formulates screening as a structured dynamic programming model that scales to high-dimensional state spaces.  
- Computational claim: algorithm runs in O(n log n) per policy iteration (n = number of clinical features).  
- Validation: robust experiments on a 45,000-patient Athena Health dataset across multiple diseases (e.g., diabetes, hypertension).  
- Cross-disease validation implies broader applicability beyond tested models.  
- Practical relevance: addresses healthcare resource constraints and can integrate with electronic health record systems.  
- Presentation quality: exceptionally well-written, rigorous, with clear algorithmic descriptions and ablation studies.  
- Minor suggested clarifications: specify the clinical features in the Athena dataset and discuss handling of missing historical data.  
- Overall judgment: significant technical and practical contribution; strong candidate for acceptance.
