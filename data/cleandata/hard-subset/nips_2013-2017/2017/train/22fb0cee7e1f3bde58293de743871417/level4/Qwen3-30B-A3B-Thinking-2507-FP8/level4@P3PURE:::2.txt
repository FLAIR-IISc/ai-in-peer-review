

The paper addresses the problem of determining an optimal patient screening policy given relevant health information, modeling the disease as a finite-state stochastic process to minimize both screening costs and delays. The authors propose a computationally efficient approximate solution, validated through experiments on simulated breast cancer data. These experiments demonstrate that the proposed algorithm reduces screening delays while maintaining equivalent costs compared to a naive annual screening baseline.

Positives:
+ The topic of personalized screening represents an important and timely research direction.
+ The proposed algorithm is reasonable, well-developed, and rigorously implemented.
+ Results are promising and support the core claims.
+ The paper is clearly and effectively written.

Negatives:
- The proposed approach constitutes a largely academic exercise. It assumes the disease model for a given patient is known, which cannot be realistically assumed in practice. The primary challenge in personalized screening lies not in deriving a policy given a known model, but in inferring the disease model from observational data.
- Methodological novelty is limited.
- The experimental evaluation only compares against a baseline of annual screening. Given the critical assumption about the known disease model, a crucial experimental question remains unaddressed: the robustness of the proposed method to inaccuracies in the disease model. Specifically, how sensitive is the derived policy to uncertainties in the model parameters?

Overall, while the topic is compelling and the algorithm is reasonable, the limited methodological novelty and the lack of experimental validation regarding model uncertainty significantly undermine the paper's practical relevance. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the practical justification to convince the community of the work's broader impact.
