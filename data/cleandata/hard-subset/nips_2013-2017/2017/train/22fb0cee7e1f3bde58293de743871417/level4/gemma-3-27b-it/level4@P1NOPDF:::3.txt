The paper proposes a method for predicting optimal screening times for breast cancer patients, aiming to minimize both screening costs and the costs associated with delayed detection. This is achieved through a personalized approach that leverages individual patient medical histories alongside external information. The paper is generally well-written and the proposed method demonstrates significant improvement over existing approaches.

The core idea – adapting screening schedules to individual risk profiles – is compelling and addresses a practically important problem. Current screening guidelines often employ a one-size-fits-all strategy, which can lead to both over-screening (unnecessary costs and anxiety) and under-screening (increased risk of late-stage diagnosis). This work directly tackles these limitations.

The authors formulate the problem as a dynamic programming problem and propose an efficient algorithm to solve it. The inclusion of both screening costs and delay costs into the objective function is a strong point, representing a more realistic and nuanced treatment of the trade-offs involved. I appreciate the detailed description of the model and the algorithm.

The experimental results presented are convincing. The comparison against existing methods, including fixed-interval screening and age-based guidelines, consistently shows the benefits of the proposed personalized approach. The gains in expected net benefit, as demonstrated in the tables and figures, are substantial. The ablation studies, particularly those exploring the contribution of different features, are insightful and help to justify the model’s design choices. 

However, I have a few questions and suggestions for improvement. First, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the data used. The data appears to be retrospective, which introduces potential biases.  A discussion of how these biases might affect the generalizability of the results would be valuable. Specifically, it’s unclear how well the model would perform on a population with different demographics or healthcare access.

Second, the computational complexity of the algorithm, while addressed, could be further clarified. While the authors state the algorithm is efficient, providing more concrete details on the scaling of the computation with respect to the number of patients or the length of the medical history would be helpful.  

Third, the sensitivity analysis presented focuses primarily on the cost parameters. Exploring the sensitivity of the results to other parameters, such as the accuracy of the risk prediction model itself, would provide a more complete picture of the method’s robustness. What happens if the initial risk assessment is inaccurate?

Finally, while the presentation of results is clear, the figures could be improved. For example, the x-axes in some figures lack clear labels, making it difficult to interpret the results at a glance.

Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a well-motivated and technically sound approach. The improvements over existing methods are significant, and the potential impact of this work is considerable. Addressing the minor points raised above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance.
