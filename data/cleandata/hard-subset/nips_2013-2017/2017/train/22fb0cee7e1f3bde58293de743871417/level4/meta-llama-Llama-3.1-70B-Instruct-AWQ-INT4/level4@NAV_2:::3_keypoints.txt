- Proposes DPSCREEN, a method for personalized screening that balances screening cost vs delay cost using a finite-state stochastic disease model with external-information triggers.  
- Relates work to POMDP-based screening, Bayesian information-gain designs, and simulation-based medical methods, highlighting their limitations.  
- Uses dynamic programming to construct an approximately optimal policy, with belief states, Bellman equations, and alpha-vector construction.  
- Provides bounds on approximation error and analyzes computational complexity.  
- Demonstrates on a large breast cancer dataset that DPSCREEN reduces the number of screenings while maintaining equivalent detection delay.  
- Discusses how disease type and choice of cost function materially affect the resulting policy.  
- Strength: novel personalized screening framework that can incorporate external information.  
- Strength: clear, detailed model and problem formulation that aids reproducibility and understanding.  
- Strength: empirically shows significant improvement over existing methods on a large dataset.  
- Weakness: high computational complexity; algorithm not scalable to large datasets as acknowledged by authors.  
- Weakness: assumes known model parameters; practical settings may have substantial estimation error.  
- Weakness: treatment of estimation error is limited and needs further work.  
- Originality: combines external-information incorporation with dynamic programming in a way not commonly seen.  
- Significance: promising potential to improve breast cancer screening but requires solutions for scalability and parameter uncertainty before wide adoption.  
- Recommendation: accept with revisions—address computational scalability, estimation error, and discuss broader healthcare applications.
