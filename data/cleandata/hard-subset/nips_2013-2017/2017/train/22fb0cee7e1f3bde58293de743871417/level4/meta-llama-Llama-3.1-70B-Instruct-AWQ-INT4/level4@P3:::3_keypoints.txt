- Proposes DPSCREEN, a method to balance screening cost against delay cost in personalized screening policies.  
- Models disease as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and allows external information triggering patient arrival.  
- Uses dynamic programming to compute an approximately optimal policy minimizing a weighted sum of screening and delay costs.  
- Implements point-based value iteration to compute the policy, treating the problem as a tractable POMDP.  
- Empirical test on a large breast cancer dataset shows fewer screens for the same expected detection delays.  
- Paper is well-written and explanations are clear and accessible to readers from different backgrounds.  
- Framework is general and does not rely on specific disease progression, test outcome, or external information models.  
- Method is flexible and can personalize screening using individual history and external signals.  
- Assumes model parameters are known, which may be unrealistic in practice.  
- Does not explore estimation error or robustness of the policy to parameter misspecification.  
- Computational complexity could be a concern for large datasets or more complex disease models.  
- Related work review is comprehensive, but direct comparisons and distinctions from existing methods could be more detailed.  
- Quality: technically sound with a solid dynamic-programming formulation.  
- Originality: presents a novel application of dynamic programming/POMDP methods to personalized screening.  
- Significance: promising potential impact in personalized medicine, evidenced by encouraging empirical results.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, clear exposition, and strong empirical gains.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on known parameters, unaddressed estimation error, and potential computational scalability issues.
