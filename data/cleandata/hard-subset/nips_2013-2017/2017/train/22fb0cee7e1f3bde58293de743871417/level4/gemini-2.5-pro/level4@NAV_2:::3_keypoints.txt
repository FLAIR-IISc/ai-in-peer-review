- Proposes DPSCREEN, a general framework modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and formulating screening as a sequential decision problem minimizing screening and delay costs.  
- Introduces an "external information process" to model unscheduled patient-triggered visits, increasing clinical realism.  
- Addresses computational intractability by proposing a PBVI-based approximation with a novel belief-space sampling that focuses on likely state trajectories and reachable beliefs.  
- Provides theoretical rigor: a bound on approximation error (Proposition 1) and robustness analysis to model estimation errors (Theorem 1).  
- Empirically validated on a large real-world breast cancer dataset, showing 28–68% reduction in screening frequency while preserving expected detection delay.  
- Extends prior POMDP and simulation-based screening work by supporting non-Markovian trajectory-dependent models and discovering near-optimal policies over a larger policy space.  
- Well-written and clearly structured presentation of problem, assumptions, and algorithm.  
- Weakness: experiments use a two-state Markov model despite claiming support for general finite-state (non-Markovian) processes; needs discussion of learning complex models from sparse clinical data.  
- Weakness: lacking reproducibility details for the approximation (e.g., values for K and |B̄[t]| and specifics of the random exploration strategy).  
- Weakness: robustness result applies to the exact optimal policy; требуется analysis of sensitivity of the approximately optimal policy to estimation errors.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion on choosing the weight w that trades off screening vs. delay costs in practical settings (elicitation or cost-effectiveness framing).
