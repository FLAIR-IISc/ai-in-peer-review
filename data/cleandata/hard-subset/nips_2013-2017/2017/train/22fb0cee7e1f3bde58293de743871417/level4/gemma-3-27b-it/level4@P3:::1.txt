This paper presents DPSCREEN, a novel method for constructing personalized screening policies across a large class of diseases. Unlike existing approaches, DPSCREEN explicitly incorporates both a patient’s clinical history (previous screening results) and relevant features, enabling personalized screening schedules. The authors demonstrate that this personalization leads to significant efficiency gains – reducing the number of screenings required – without compromising disease detection timeliness.

The paper is well-written and technically sound. It provides strong motivation for the problem, carefully positions the work within the broader literature on screening and personalized medicine, and offers a concise yet thorough mathematical treatment of the proposed algorithm, including a discussion of its computational complexity. The validation using a large (45,000 patient) dataset from the Athena Health Network provides compelling evidence of DPSCREEN’s improvements. The analysis also extends to consideration of varying disease characteristics and cost functions, strengthening the generalizability of the findings. 

**Summary of Related Work:** The introduction effectively summarizes prior work in screening, highlighting limitations in existing approaches. The authors correctly identify that previous methods often rely on restrictive assumptions (e.g., Markovian disease processes, stationary test outcomes) that limit their applicability. The connections drawn to related areas like controlled sensing and Bayesian design are also appropriate and help contextualize the contribution.  However, the paper could benefit from a slightly more detailed discussion of relevant work on dynamic treatment regimes, which shares similarities with the personalized screening problem (e.g., references to Murphy's work on optimal treatment rules).

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The approach of formulating personalized screening as a dynamic decision problem and providing a computationally tractable solution is a significant contribution. The potential impact on healthcare efficiency and patient outcomes is considerable.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation appears rigorous, and the discussion of computational complexity is realistic. The use of point-based value iteration to address the computational challenges is a well-established technique.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The use of a large, real-world dataset (Athena Health Network) lends strong support to the effectiveness of the proposed method. The comparison with current clinical policies demonstrates tangible benefits.
*   **Generalizability:** The method's applicability to a wide range of disease models and the exploration of different cost functions (linear vs. quadratic) enhance its practical relevance.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Model Assumptions:** While the paper acknowledges the simplifying assumptions made in the breast cancer model (e.g., two-state Markov process, accurate test outcomes), a more thorough discussion of the potential impact of these assumptions on the results would be valuable. Sensitivity analysis exploring the robustness of the findings to deviations from these assumptions would be helpful.
*   **Estimation Error:**  The treatment of estimation error (Section 3.3) is somewhat abstract. While the theorem regarding the robustness of the policy is promising, a more concrete discussion of how model mis-specification might affect the performance of DPSCREEN in practice would be beneficial.
*   **External Information:** The modeling of external information (e.g., self-examination) appears simplified. The assumption of independence between external information and the underlying disease state, given the patient’s features, may not always hold in reality. Investigating more complex dependencies could be a direction for future work.
*   **Computational Scalability:** Although the authors address the computational complexity, the practical limitations for very large state spaces or long time horizons are not fully explored. Further optimization techniques or approximation methods might be necessary for widespread adoption.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important and challenging problem with significant practical implications.
*   The proposed method is technically sound and offers a novel approach to personalized screening.
*   The empirical validation on a large dataset demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the core ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The simplifying assumptions made in the disease model could limit the generalizability of the results.
*   The discussion of estimation error and external information could be more thorough.
*   The computational scalability of the method for extremely large-scale problems remains a concern.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field. The strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses.  The approach is novel, technically sound, and well-validated. While there are areas for improvement, these do not detract from the overall quality and potential impact of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above. Specifically, I would suggest adding a sensitivity analysis to assess the impact of model assumptions and expanding the discussion of estimation error and computational scalability.
