This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies aimed at balancing the trade-off between screening frequency and delay in disease detection. The authors address a critical need in healthcare: moving beyond one-size-fits-all screening protocols to strategies tailored to individual patient characteristics and dynamic medical histories. They model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process, incorporating external information (e.g., patient self-exams) and developing a computationally tractable approach to approximate optimal screening policies. This work builds upon existing research in screening (e.g., [7, 9]) by extending beyond Markovian disease processes and stationary test outcome models, and addresses limitations of previous approaches that struggle with personalized policies and computational complexity. 

The core idea—dynamic, personalized screening—is significant and well-motivated. The authors clearly articulate the challenges of developing such policies, highlighting the need to account for disease variability, patient-specific risk, external information, and computational intractability. They present a comprehensive model and a practical algorithmic approach, Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI), adapted to this specific problem. The illustrative experiments using a large breast cancer dataset demonstrate substantial improvements over current clinical practices, achieving reductions of 28-68% in screenings while maintaining equivalent delays in detection. The analysis of the impact of different delay cost functions (linear vs. quadratic) further strengthens the paper, illustrating the importance of cost function selection for different diseases.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The framework directly addresses a crucial open problem in personalized medicine – optimizing screening schedules based on individual patient profiles and disease dynamics.
* **Comprehensive Modeling:** The model effectively integrates patient features, disease progression, external information, and test results within a unified framework.
* **Computational Tractability:** The development of a computationally tractable algorithm (PBVI) is a significant contribution, making the proposed approach applicable to real-world datasets.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The breast cancer data analysis provides compelling evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed policy, demonstrating substantial reductions in screening frequency without compromising detection delays.
* **Robustness Analysis:** The discussion of estimation error and its impact on policy performance is valuable and highlights the practical considerations for implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Model Assumptions:** The assumption of a finite-state stochastic process for disease progression, while simplifying the problem, may not fully capture the complexity of many diseases.  The independence assumptions regarding test results and external information could also be limiting in certain scenarios.
* **Parameter Estimation:** While the paper details parameter estimation using a breast cancer dataset, the sensitivity of the policy to the accuracy of these estimates is not thoroughly explored. A more detailed analysis of estimation error propagation would be beneficial.
* **Generalizability:** While the application to breast cancer is well-demonstrated, the extent to which the framework and algorithms can be readily generalized to other diseases with different characteristics is not fully addressed.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison is primarily with annual/biennial screening. Comparing with other personalized strategies (even simpler ones) could provide a stronger baseline for evaluation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of personalized screening.
* The proposed framework is well-motivated, technically sound, and computationally tractable.
* The empirical results demonstrate substantial improvements over existing clinical practices.
* The authors address important practical considerations such as estimation error and robustness.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The model relies on simplifying assumptions that may not hold for all diseases.
* A more in-depth analysis of parameter estimation sensitivity and generalizability would be valuable.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of personalized medicine. The proposed framework for dynamic personalized screening is innovative, well-supported by theoretical analysis and empirical results, and addresses a critical need in healthcare. While some simplifying assumptions are made, the authors acknowledge these limitations and provide a thorough discussion of the practical considerations. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly a more detailed discussion of the impact of parameter estimation error and the generalizability of the framework to other disease contexts.
