- Proposes a framework for personalized screening that balances screening frequency against detection delay, modeling disease progression as a finite-state process with an absorbing disease state.
- Extends prior personalized screening work by incorporating external information processes (e.g., self-exams) and generalizing beyond Markovian disease models.
- Develops an approximately optimal policy via point-based value iteration and provides a theoretical error bound (Proposition 1).
- Empirical evaluation on a large breast cancer dataset shows 28–68% reductions in screening frequency versus annual screening while maintaining equivalent detection delays.

Strengths:
- Tackles a highly significant healthcare problem with clear potential to reduce costs and improve outcomes.
- Technically rigorous with solid theoretical foundations and a proved approximation error bound.
- Well-executed experiments on a substantial dataset using appropriate metrics (expected screenings, expected delay).
- Framework’s ability to incorporate external information and adapt to different delay cost functions is valuable.
- Paper is clearly written with effective figures and structure.

Weaknesses:
- Assumes the disease model is known precisely, which is unrealistic; paper does not address model estimation from observational data.
- Methodological novelty is limited—mainly an application of existing point-based value iteration techniques.
- Experimental comparisons are limited (only annual screening) and do not assess robustness to model estimation errors.
- Discussion of delay cost functions’ relationship to policy is not sufficiently integrated into main experiments to demonstrate practical implications.

Overall:
- Promising and technically sound, but practical relevance is uncertain without addressing disease model estimation and robustness to model inaccuracies.
