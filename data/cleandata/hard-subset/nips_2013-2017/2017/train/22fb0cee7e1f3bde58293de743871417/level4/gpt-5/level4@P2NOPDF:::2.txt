The paper seeks the optimal patient-specific screening policy given relevant information. Disease progression is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process, and the objective is to minimize screening costs and diagnostic delays. The authors propose a computationally efficient approximate solution. Experiments on simulated breast cancer data suggest the method can reduce delays while maintaining comparable costs relative to a simple annual-screening baseline.

Positives:
- Personalized screening is an important and timely research direction.
- The proposed algorithm is reasonable and well developed.
- The empirical results are promising.
- The paper is clearly written.

Negatives:
- The approach feels primarily academic because it assumes the patient’s disease model is known, which is unrealistic in practice. The key challenge in personalized screening is not optimizing a policy given the true model, but inferring that model from data.
- The methodological novelty appears limited.
- The experiments only compare against annual screening. Given the reliance on a specified disease model, a critical missing component is an evaluation of robustness to model misspecification. In particular, the paper should study how sensitive the policy’s performance is to uncertainty or inaccuracies in the disease model.

Overall, the topic is important and the proposed algorithm is sensible. However, the methodological contribution is modest, and the paper would need stronger evidence of practical relevance—especially robustness to model uncertainty and comparisons beyond a trivial baseline—to be fully convincing.
