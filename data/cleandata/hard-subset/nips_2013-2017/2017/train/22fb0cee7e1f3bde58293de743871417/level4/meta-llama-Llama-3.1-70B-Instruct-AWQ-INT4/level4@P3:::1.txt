This paper presents DPSCREEN, a method for determining optimal screening policies for large classes of diseases. The authors address the challenging problem of balancing the cost of screening against the cost of delay in disease detection, taking into account both the patient's clinical (screening) history and patient features pertinent to the specific disease model.

The paper is well-written and technically sound. The approach is well-motivated, considering pertinent related work in decision theory, controlled sensing, and medical literature on screening. The authors provide a concise but thorough discussion of the mathematical basis of the algorithm, including computational complexity.

The authors model the disease as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating the possibility of external information, such as self-examination or discovery of co-morbidities, that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment. They use breast cancer data to develop the disease model and demonstrate the effectiveness of their policy in a real setting.

The results show that the proposed policy achieves approximately the same expected delay as the benchmark policy while doing many fewer tests (in expectation). The expected reduction in the number of screens is 57-68% (depending on risk group) with self-examinations, and 28-45% percent (depending on risk group) without self-examinations.

The authors also discuss the impact of the type of disease on the policy, considering both linear and quadratic delay costs. They show that the regime of operation of the policy (the points achieved by the policy in the 2-D plane E[N |R,Cost] vs E[∆|R,Cost]) can vary significantly depending on the choice of cost function, even though the same weights are used.

The paper makes a clear technical contribution that also has the potential for practical impact. The proposed policy is personalized, recommending significantly fewer tests to low-risk patients in contrast to high-risk patients. The authors also provide a robustness analysis, discussing the effect of estimation error on the policy.

Strengths:

* The paper presents a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in disease detection.
* The approach takes into account both the patient's clinical (screening) history and patient features pertinent to the specific disease model.
* The paper provides a thorough discussion of the mathematical basis of the algorithm, including computational complexity.
* The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed policy in a real setting, showing significant reductions in the number of screens while maintaining the same expected delay in disease detection.

Weaknesses:

* The paper assumes that the model parameters are known, which may not be the case in practice.
* The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm, which may be a limitation in practice.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to determining optimal screening policies for large classes of diseases.
* The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed policy in a real setting, showing significant reductions in the number of screens while maintaining the same expected delay in disease detection.
* The paper has the potential for practical impact, as the proposed policy is personalized and can be used to improve the efficiency of screening processes.

Arguments for rejection:

* The paper assumes that the model parameters are known, which may not be the case in practice.
* The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm, which may be a limitation in practice.

Overall, I recommend accepting the paper, as it presents a novel and technically sound approach to determining optimal screening policies for large classes of diseases, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed policy in a real setting.
