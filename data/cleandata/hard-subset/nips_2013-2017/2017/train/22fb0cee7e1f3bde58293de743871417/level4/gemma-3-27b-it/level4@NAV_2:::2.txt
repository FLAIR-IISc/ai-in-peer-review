This paper addresses the important problem of personalized screening policies, aiming to balance the trade-off between screening costs and delays in disease detection. The core idea is to model disease progression as a finite state stochastic process and develop a computationally tractable approach to approximate the optimal screening policy. The proposed method is well-motivated and the breast cancer application demonstrates promising results, suggesting potential reductions in screening frequency while maintaining comparable detection delays.

The paper demonstrates a strong understanding of the existing literature in screening policies [7, 9, 10], decision theory [18-21], and related fields. The proposed framework extends previous work by accommodating general disease models, external information, and the computational intractability inherent in optimizing screening policies. The authors appropriately reference relevant work on dynamic survival models [11-17, 36, 37] and highlight how their approach overcomes limitations of existing methods.

The strengths of this work include a clear and well-organized presentation, a rigorous mathematical formulation, and a practical demonstration using a real-world dataset. The development of a computationally efficient algorithm (DPSCREEN) is a significant contribution, particularly given the inherent complexity of the problem. The inclusion of robustness analysis considering estimation error is also commendable. The personalization aspects, illustrated in Figure 1, effectively demonstrate the adaptability of the proposed policy to individual patient characteristics and histories. 

However, the paper has some weaknesses. A key limitation is the assumption of known disease models. While acknowledged by the authors, this substantially restricts the immediate practical applicability of the proposed method. The core challenge in personalized screening often lies in accurately inferring these models from observational data, rather than optimizing policies *given* a known model. The methodological novelty, while present, is incremental. The experiments primarily compare the proposed approach to a fixed annual screening baseline. A more compelling evaluation would assess the policy’s sensitivity to inaccuracies in the estimated disease model – i.e., how robust is the optimal policy to model misspecification? This would directly address a major practical concern.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in healthcare.
*   Presents a theoretically sound and well-developed algorithm.
*   Demonstrates promising results on a real-world dataset.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Addresses model estimation error and includes robustness considerations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Relies on the unrealistic assumption of known disease models.
*   Limited experimental evaluation; robustness to model inaccuracies needs further investigation.
*   Incremental methodological novelty.
*   Practical relevance could be strengthened by addressing the model inference challenge.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of personalized screening. While the assumption of known disease models limits its immediate practical impact, the proposed algorithm and theoretical framework are solid and offer a promising direction for future research. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more thorough investigation of the policy's robustness to errors in disease model estimation.
