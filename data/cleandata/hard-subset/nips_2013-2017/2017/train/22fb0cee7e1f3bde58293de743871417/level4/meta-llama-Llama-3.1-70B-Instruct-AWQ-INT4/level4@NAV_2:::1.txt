This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The disease is modeled as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, allowing for a general setting that encompasses many disease models. The authors incorporate the possibility of external information, such as self-examination or discovery of comorbidities, that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment.

The paper is well-motivated and considers pertinent related work in the area. The authors provide a clear and concise discussion of the mathematical basis of the algorithm, including computational complexity. The proposed approach is validated on a large breast cancer dataset, demonstrating the improvements provided by personalization. The model is also validated across disease model types, highlighting the importance of choice of costs.

The authors address the problem of constructing personalized screening policies that optimally balance the frequency of testing against the delay in the detection of the disease. They develop a computationally effective procedure that solves for an approximately optimal policy and provide bounds for the approximation error. The proposed approach, DPSCREEN, is shown to outperform existing clinical policies, achieving large reductions in the number of screens performed while maintaining the same expected delays in disease detection.

The paper is technically sound, and the approach is well-motivated. The authors provide a thorough discussion of the mathematical basis of the algorithm, including computational complexity. The proposed approach is validated on a large dataset, demonstrating the improvements provided by personalization.

The paper makes a clear technical contribution that also has the potential for practical impact. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in a real setting, screening for breast cancer, and show that it can lead to significant reductions in the number of screenings performed while maintaining the same delay in detection of disease.

Strengths of the paper include:

* The authors provide a clear and concise discussion of the mathematical basis of the algorithm, including computational complexity.
* The proposed approach is validated on a large dataset, demonstrating the improvements provided by personalization.
* The model is also validated across disease model types, highlighting the importance of choice of costs.

Weaknesses of the paper include:

* The paper assumes that the model parameters are known, which may not always be the case in practice.
* The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the robustness of the proposed approach to estimation errors.

Overall, I found this paper to be well-written and technically sound. The approach is well-motivated, considers pertinent related work, and provides a concise but thorough discussion of the mathematical basis of the algorithm, including computational complexity. The proposed approach is validated on a large dataset, demonstrating the improvements provided by personalization. The model is also validated across disease model types, highlighting the importance of choice of costs.
