- Paper goal: identify optimal patient screening policies by modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process to minimize screening costs and delays, and proposing a computationally efficient approximate solution.

- Experimental claim: on simulated breast cancer data the method reduces delays while maintaining the same cost compared to an annual-screening baseline.

- Positive: personalized screening is an important and relevant research area.

- Positive: the proposed algorithm is reasonable and well developed.

- Positive: the results are promising.

- Positive: the paper is clearly written.

- Negative: the method unrealistically assumes the patient’s disease model is known, which limits practical applicability.

- Negative: the primary practical challenge—inferring the disease model from data—is not addressed; deriving a policy from a known model is less impactful.

- Negative: methodological novelty is limited.

- Negative: experiments only compare to annual screening and do not test robustness to model misspecification or uncertainty in the disease model.

- Overall: important topic and reasonable algorithm, but modest novelty and insufficient evidence of real-world practical relevance.
