- Proposes DPSCREEN: dynamic, personalized screening balancing screening costs and delay costs using finite-state stochastic disease models without requiring Markov dynamics.  
- Novelty: screening times endogenously set by clinician recommendations and exogenous “external information” (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger early arrivals.  
- Decision objective combines discounted screening cost and trajectory-dependent delay cost; value function shown convex and piecewise-linear in belief.  
- Algorithm: PBVI-style approximation over a sampled reduced belief space built from likely trajectories and reachable beliefs.  
- Theoretical: approximation bound linked to belief sampling density and a local constancy robustness result for policy stability under model error.  
- Empirical: two-state (H,D) breast cancer model estimated from Athena (~45k patients, ~84k mammograms) using Gail scores and a tumor-growth-driven external arrival model.  
- Results: DPSCREEN reduces screenings by 28–68% while maintaining comparable expected detection delays versus annual/biennial schedules, and adapts to risk and history.  
- Relation to prior work: generalizes POMDP-based screening, contrasts with Bayesian information-gain designs, and connects to controlled sensing while relaxing Markov/stationarity assumptions.  
- Strength: clinically relevant problem scope allowing dynamic histories and exogenous arrivals across diseases.  
- Strength: modeling generality supports arbitrary finite-state trajectories and trajectory-dependent delay costs.  
- Strength: sound decision-theoretic formulation with proof of value-function structure.  
- Strength: practical PBVI variant tailored for high-dimensional belief spaces with complexity and parallelization discussion.  
- Strength: theoretical support via sampling-based approximation bound and robustness result.  
- Strength: real-world dataset demonstration with personalization and substantial screening reductions.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation relies on a simplified two-state Markov model, undermining claims of non-Markov generality.  
- Weakness: tuning weight w by matching expected delay may yield unfair comparisons; lacks accounting for false positives, biopsies, and anxiety-related harms.  
- Weakness: inconsistent assumptions about false positives/negatives across sections; needs reconciliation and clearer links to Proposition 1.  
- Weakness: limited practical guidance on sampling parameters K, |B̄|, runtime, and sensitivity; missing wall-clock times and ablations.  
- Weakness: external-information model (lump-threshold) may drive gains; robustness to parameters ι, σ, ỹ needs clearer, systematic sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: theoretical results are high-level and do not provide actionable sample-complexity guarantees.  
- Quality: technically sound formulation and approximation, but some modeling/evaluation choices require better justification.  
- Clarity: generally well written but dense; fix typos, clarify Bellman sign conventions, reconcile false-positive/negative assumptions, and define action space bounds.  
- Originality: novel combination of endogenous arrivals, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and PBVI over sampled belief subsets in screening contexts.  
- Significance: promising potential to reduce screening burden while preserving timely detection; adaptable across diseases and costs.  
- Suggestions: report runtime/memory/K/|B̄| and parallelization details; add sensitivity analyses for external info and test accuracy; include heterogeneous test costs and at least one non-Markov/multi-state simulation.  
- Pros: important problem, solid decision-theoretic and practical solution, strong empirical reductions, personalization, and theoretical grounding.  
- Cons: limited empirical validation beyond two-state Markov, sparse computational/sensitivity reporting, and some assumption/notation inconsistencies.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifications and additional sensitivity/complexity reporting.
