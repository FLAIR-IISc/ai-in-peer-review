This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel method for optimizing screening policies across a broad range of diseases. A key strength of DPSCREEN lies in its ability to integrate both a patient’s clinical screening history *and* individual features relevant to the specific disease being modeled. This contrasts with existing approaches and enables personalized screening recommendations, leading to demonstrable efficiency gains without compromising timely disease detection.

Overall, I found this to be a well-written and technically strong contribution. The motivation for this work is clear and compelling, and the authors appropriately situate it within the related literature. The paper provides a concise yet thorough description of the underlying mathematical framework, including a useful discussion of computational complexity. Importantly, the efficacy of DPSCREEN is convincingly demonstrated through validation on a substantial dataset (45,000 patients) drawn from the Athena Health Network. This large-scale evaluation robustly illustrates the benefits of personalization. Furthermore, the authors bolster their findings by validating the model’s performance across diverse disease models, indicating generalizability. 

The incorporation of patient history alongside disease-specific risk factors is a particularly valuable aspect of this work. Many current screening policies rely on population-level statistics or static risk assessments, potentially leading to over-screening of low-risk individuals and under-screening of those at higher risk. DPSCREEN’s dynamic approach addresses this limitation. The clarity with which the algorithm is presented facilitates understanding and potential adoption. The discussion of computational complexity is also appreciated, as this is a crucial practical consideration for deploying such a system in a real-world healthcare setting. 

While the results are compelling, a few minor points could be clarified. Specifically, a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the Athena Health Network dataset would be helpful. What biases might be present in the data, and how might these biases affect the generalizability of the findings? Further, while the paper demonstrates improvements in efficiency, quantifying the *cost* savings associated with these improvements (e.g., reduced healthcare expenditure, decreased patient burden) could further strengthen the practical impact claim. Finally, exploring the sensitivity of DPSCREEN to the accuracy of the underlying disease models would be a worthwhile avenue for future work – how does model error propagate through the screening policy?

Despite these minor suggestions, this paper represents a significant technical contribution with considerable potential for practical impact. The proposed method offers a promising pathway towards more efficient and personalized disease screening, and the clear presentation and thorough validation make it a strong candidate for acceptance. I recommend acceptance.
