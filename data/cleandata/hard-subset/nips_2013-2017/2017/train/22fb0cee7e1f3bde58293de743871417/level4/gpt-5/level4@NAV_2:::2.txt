The paper aims to determine an optimal screening policy given the available patient information. It models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and seeks to minimize a combination of screening costs and detection delays. The authors introduce a computationally tractable approximate solution. Experiments on breast cancer–related simulated data suggest the method can reduce delays while maintaining the same cost when compared to a naive baseline.

Positives:
- Personalized screening is an important and impactful research area.
- The proposed algorithm is sensible and carefully developed.
- The results are encouraging.
- The paper is clearly written.

Negatives:
- The approach feels primarily academic: it assumes the patient-specific disease model is known, which is rarely realistic. In practice, the key challenge is estimating the model from data rather than optimizing a policy under a known model.
- The methodological novelty is modest.
- The experiments compare only to an annual-screening baseline. Crucially, the work should empirically evaluate robustness to model misspecification—i.e., how sensitive the policy is to uncertainty and errors in the disease model.

Overall, the problem is interesting and the proposed method is reasonable, but the incremental novelty is limited, and the paper needs stronger evidence to establish practical relevance.
