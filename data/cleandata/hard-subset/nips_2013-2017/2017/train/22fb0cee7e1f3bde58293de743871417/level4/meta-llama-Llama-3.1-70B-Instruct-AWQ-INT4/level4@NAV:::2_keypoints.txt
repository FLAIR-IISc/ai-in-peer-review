- Paper aims to find an optimal patient screening policy using a finite-state stochastic disease progression model to minimize screening costs and delays.
- Proposes a computationally efficient approximate policy and tests it on simulated breast cancer data.
- Experiments suggest the algorithm can reduce delays without increasing costs and outperforms an annual-screening baseline.
- Positive: Addresses an important problem—personalized screening—and contributes to the area.
- Positive: The algorithm is well-conceived and effectively developed.
- Positive: Empirical results are promising and justify further study.
- Positive: Paper is clearly and effectively written.
- Negative: Method assumes the patient’s disease model is known, which is unrealistic in practice.
- Negative: The key challenge of inferring disease models from data is not addressed.
- Negative: Methodological innovation is limited.
- Negative: Experimental comparison against only an annual baseline is insufficient.
- Negative: Lacks analysis of robustness/sensitivity to inaccuracies in the assumed disease model.
- Overall: Interesting and sound work but limited novelty and insufficient evidence of practical relevance; needs stronger empirical validation and robustness checks.
