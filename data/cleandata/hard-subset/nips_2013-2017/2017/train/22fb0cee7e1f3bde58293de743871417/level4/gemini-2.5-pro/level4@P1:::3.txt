### Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

**Summary**

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel and general framework for constructing personalized and dynamic screening policies for diseases. The core objective is to optimize the trade-off between the cost of frequent screenings and the cost of delayed disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a general finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, a formulation that is more flexible than the strictly Markovian models used in much of the prior work. A key contribution is the explicit modeling of an "external information" process (e.g., patient self-examinations or the onset of comorbidities) that can trigger unscheduled clinical visits, reflecting a crucial real-world aspect of patient care.

The problem is framed as a dynamic program over a belief space, where beliefs are distributions over entire disease trajectories rather than just the current state. Recognizing that an exact solution is computationally intractable due to the high dimensionality of this belief space, the authors propose an approximately optimal policy computed via a Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) algorithm. They develop a specific belief-point sampling strategy to make this approach feasible. The method is supported by a theoretical analysis that provides an approximation error bound and a robustness guarantee against errors in the underlying model estimation.

The framework's effectiveness is demonstrated using a large, real-world dataset for breast cancer screening. The results show that the DPSCREEN policy can achieve the same expected delay in detection as current clinical guidelines (e.g., annual screening) while significantly reducing the number of required screenings by 28-68%.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Generality:** The framework's ability to handle general, non-Markovian disease models and non-stationary test outcomes is a significant advance over previous POMDP-based screening literature (e.g., Ayer et al., 2015; Erenay et al., 2016), which typically relies on Markovian assumptions. The inclusion of an external information process that allows for patient-initiated visits is a novel and practically relevant feature that makes the model more realistic and robust.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the screening problem as a dynamic program over trajectory beliefs is rigorous and well-posed. The choice of a PBVI-based approximation is well-motivated, and the authors provide important theoretical support for their method, including an approximation error bound (Proposition 1) and a robustness analysis regarding model misspecification (Theorem 1). This theoretical grounding adds significant credibility to the proposed approach.

3.  **Significance and Impact:** The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate a high potential for real-world impact. A 28-68% reduction in screenings for the same level of diagnostic delay could lead to substantial reductions in healthcare costs, resource utilization, and the patient burden associated with over-screening. The personalization of the policy to both static patient features and dynamic medical history is clearly demonstrated and is a crucial aspect of modern, patient-centric medicine.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. The authors do an excellent job of explaining a complex model and solution methodology. The illustrative example in Figure 1 is particularly effective at communicating the intuition behind how the policy achieves dynamic personalization.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification**

1.  **Mismatch between Framework Generality and Experimental Validation:** The proposed framework is powerful in its generality, allowing for arbitrary finite-state stochastic processes. However, the experimental validation uses a standard two-state (Healthy/Disease) discrete-time Markov model. While this is a pragmatic choice for the application, it does not fully showcase the unique advantages of the proposed general framework over existing, simpler POMDP models. Demonstrating the method on a synthetic problem with a more complex, non-Markovian disease trajectory could have made the contribution even more convincing.

2.  **Scalability and Computational Cost:** The computational complexity of the algorithm appears to be a major practical concern. The authors provide a complexity formula but do not report key parameters or computation times for their experiments (e.g., the size of the horizon `T`, number of sampled trajectories `K`, number of belief points `B`, and total wall-clock time). This information is critical for assessing the feasibility of applying DPSCREEN in practice, especially if one were to use a more complex disease model with more states or a longer time horizon.

3.  **Sensitivity of Belief Sampling:** The performance of any PBVI algorithm is highly dependent on the quality of the sampled belief points. The paper's sampling strategy seems plausible, but its effectiveness is not thoroughly evaluated. A sensitivity analysis showing how the policy's performance changes with the number of sampled belief points (`B`) and trajectories (`K`) would strengthen the empirical results and provide guidance for future users of this method.

4.  **Experimental Assumptions:** The impressive reduction in screenings relies on several assumptions and parameter estimates drawn from prior literature (e.g., the tumor growth model, the sensitivity of self-exams). While this is a standard practice, it would be valuable to include a discussion on the sensitivity of the results to these assumptions. For instance, how much does the performance degrade if the self-examination model is less accurate or if the estimated disease transition probabilities are noisy? This would provide a better understanding of the policy's robustness in real-world scenarios where model parameters might not be perfectly known.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of personalized medical decision-making. It proposes a novel, general, and technically sound framework for optimizing screening schedules, supported by both theoretical analysis and compelling empirical results on a large-scale, real-world problem. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to the scope of the experimental validation and the need for more detail on computational feasibility, which could be addressed in a future version of the work. The paper is original, of high quality, and likely to be of great interest to researchers in machine learning for healthcare, operations research, and medical informatics. I recommend acceptance.
