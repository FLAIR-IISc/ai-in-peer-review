- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies balancing screening cost vs. delayed detection.
- Models disease progression as a general finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, more flexible than prior Markov assumptions.
- Incorporates an external information process (e.g., self-exams, comorbidities) that can trigger unscheduled clinician visits.
- Formulates the problem as an optimization over policies mapping patient history and features to the next screening time.
- Exact solution is intractable due to high-dimensional belief space; authors propose an approximate solution.
- Adapts point-based value iteration (PBVI) by sampling representative disease trajectories to reduce belief-space dimensionality.
- Samples reachable belief points within the reduced space to compute an approximately optimal policy.
- Demonstrated on a large real-world breast cancer dataset; policy personalizes to static risk factors and dynamic test results.
- Empirical results: similar expected detection delay to clinical guidelines but reduces screenings by 28–68%.
- High technical quality with well-formulated problem and thoughtful PBVI adaptation to trajectory-based beliefs.
- Theoretical support includes convexity of the value function (Lemma 1), an approximation error bound (Proposition 1), and robustness analysis to model errors (Theorem 1).
- Experimental support is convincing, with appropriate benchmarks and analyses showing personalization across risk groups and test outcomes.
- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, and includes helpful illustrations and clear experimental presentation.
- Original contributions: general finite-state disease model, explicit external information process, and non-trivial adaptation of PBVI to trajectory belief spaces.
- Significant potential real-world impact: reduces costs and harms of over-screening while maintaining outcomes; broadly applicable beyond breast cancer.
- Arguments for acceptance: high-impact problem, novel and general framework, strong empirical improvements, and solid theoretical foundations.
- Main weaknesses: experimental disease model is simple (two-state Markov), limiting demonstration of non-Markovian power.
- Evaluation sensitivity: reliance on simulations from a learned model; needs sensitivity analysis to parameter estimation errors despite Theorem 1.
- Practical scalability: complexity is analyzed but actual runtimes for experiments are not reported, making real-world scalability unclear.
- Overall recommendation: strengths far outweigh weaknesses; strong recommendation for acceptance.
