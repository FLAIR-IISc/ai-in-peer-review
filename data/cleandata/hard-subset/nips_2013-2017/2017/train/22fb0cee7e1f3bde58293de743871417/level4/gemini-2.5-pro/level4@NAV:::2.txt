This paper addresses the problem of determining an optimal patient screening policy based on relevant patient information. The approach relies on modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process, with the goal of deriving a policy that minimizes a combination of screening costs and diagnostic delays. To this end, the authors put forward a computationally tractable approximate solution. Experimental results, based on simulated breast cancer data, suggest that the proposed method can decrease diagnostic delays without increasing costs relative to a standard baseline.

Positives:
+ The focus on personalized screening addresses a significant and timely research area.
+ The proposed algorithm is logically sound and thoroughly developed.
+ The initial results presented are encouraging.
+ The manuscript is clearly written and easy to follow.

Negatives:
- A major limitation is the practical applicability of the proposed method. The core assumption is that a patient's specific disease model is known a priori, which is an unrealistic premise in a clinical setting. Consequently, the work sidesteps the primary challenge in personalized screening, which is not policy optimization given a perfect model, but rather the inference of such a model from limited data.
- The contribution in terms of methodological novelty is incremental.
- The experimental evaluation is limited. The comparison is made only against a simple annual screening baseline. Crucially, given the strong assumption about a known disease model, the experiments fail to investigate the algorithm's robustness to model misspecification. A sensitivity analysis examining the policy's performance under uncertainty or inaccuracies in the model parameters is a critical missing piece.

In summary, the paper tackles an important problem with a sound algorithmic approach. However, the work is constrained by its limited methodological novelty and, more significantly, by a central assumption that challenges its practical relevance. To be more impactful, the paper needs to more convincingly address the gap between its theoretical framework and real-world application.
