

The paper addresses the problem of optimizing personalized patient screening policies under a finite-state stochastic disease model, aiming to minimize both screening costs and delays. The authors propose a computationally efficient approximate solution and evaluate it on simulated breast cancer data, demonstrating reduced delays while maintaining comparable costs relative to an annual screening baseline.  

**Strengths:**  
The topic of personalized screening is highly relevant to healthcare optimization, and the paper provides a clear problem formulation. The proposed algorithm is logically developed, with well-structured methodology and promising results on synthetic data. The writing is concise and accessible, contributing to the paper’s readability.  

**Major Concerns:**  
The primary limitation lies in the paper’s disconnect from real-world implementation. The approach assumes perfect knowledge of each patient’s disease model—a critical assumption that cannot be justified in practice. In reality, the core challenge in personalized screening is *inferring* the disease model from sparse, noisy clinical data, not designing policies *given* a known model. The paper’s focus on policy optimization without addressing model learning renders it largely theoretical. This undermines the practical relevance the work aims to establish.  

Furthermore, the methodological novelty is limited. The proposed approximation builds on established Markov decision process (MDP) techniques for healthcare, with no significant departure from prior work in the field. The baseline comparison (annual screening) is insufficient to demonstrate value; the critical question—how robust is the policy to model inaccuracies?—is unaddressed. In practice, disease models will always contain uncertainty due to data limitations or individual variability. The authors should empirically test the policy’s sensitivity to model misspecification (e.g., via perturbation experiments or synthetic noise injection), as this is essential for clinical adoption. Without such analysis, the claimed benefits remain speculative.  

**Recommendation:**  
While the paper tackles an important problem and presents a technically sound algorithm, it fails to bridge the gap between theory and practice. The absence of model learning, minimal novelty, and lack of robustness analysis significantly weaken the contribution. To strengthen the work, the authors must:  
1. Acknowledge the model inference challenge and integrate it into the framework (e.g., by proposing a joint learning-optimization approach),  
2. Replace the annual baseline with a more meaningful comparison (e.g., current clinical protocols),  
3. Conduct experiments quantifying the policy’s performance under model uncertainty.  

As currently written, the paper reads as an academic exercise rather than a solution to a pressing healthcare problem. The results, though promising on simulated data, do not convincingly demonstrate practical utility. Given the high bar for NIPS, the work lacks sufficient novelty and real-world grounding to warrant acceptance. A revised version addressing these issues could be a strong contribution, but the present submission does not meet the conference’s standards.  

**Final Recommendation:** Weak Reject.
