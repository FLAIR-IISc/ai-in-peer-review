

The paper proposes a personalized framework for determining optimal breast cancer screening intervals, aiming to minimize the combined costs of screening procedures and delays in detection. This is achieved by integrating individual patient medical histories with complementary external data sources, such as socioeconomic or environmental factors. The method demonstrates a clear advancement over existing approaches, with empirical results showing consistent reductions in total costs across multiple benchmark datasets. The manuscript is well-structured and technically sound, with a thorough presentation of the methodology, experimental setup, and results. The authors effectively justify their modeling choices, particularly the use of a reinforcement learning approach to balance immediate screening expenses against the risk of delayed diagnosis.  

While the core contribution is compelling, the evaluation could be strengthened by providing more granular performance metrics. Specifically, the paper states "significant improvement" over baselines but does not quantify the magnitude of cost reduction (e.g., "X% lower total cost" or "Y% fewer missed detections") or detail how the method handles edge cases (e.g., patients with rare medical histories). Including ablation studies to isolate the impact of external data versus medical history alone would further clarify the model’s novelty. Additionally, the discussion of clinical interpretability—critical for real-world adoption—remains superficial. A brief analysis of how the model’s decision logic aligns with clinical guidelines would enhance the paper’s practical relevance.  

The writing is generally clear and precise, though a few sections (e.g., Section 3.2) contain overly dense technical descriptions that could benefit from simplification. The figures effectively visualize key results, particularly the cost trade-off curves, but the caption for Figure 3 should explicitly define the baseline methods compared. Minor grammatical issues exist (e.g., inconsistent tense in the methodology section), but these do not impede comprehension.  

Overall, this work addresses a meaningful gap in personalized healthcare optimization with a technically robust solution. The proposed method’s practical applicability is promising, and the results provide a strong foundation for future clinical integration. The paper would be significantly improved by adding quantitative performance details and deeper clinical context, but as presented, it makes a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions to address the points above.
