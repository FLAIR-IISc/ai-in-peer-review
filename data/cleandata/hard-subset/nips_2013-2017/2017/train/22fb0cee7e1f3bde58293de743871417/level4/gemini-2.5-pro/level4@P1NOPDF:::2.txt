This paper addresses the problem of determining an optimal patient screening policy based on individual patient information. The approach models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and seeks a policy that minimizes a combination of screening costs and diagnostic delays. The authors propose a computationally efficient approximate solution to this problem. Experiments on simulated data for breast cancer suggest that the proposed algorithm can reduce diagnostic delays compared to a standard annual screening baseline, while maintaining similar costs.

**Positives:**
+ The paper addresses personalized screening, which is an important and impactful area of research.
+ The proposed algorithm is well-motivated, reasonable, and clearly developed.
+ The experimental results, while preliminary, are promising.
+ The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

**Negatives:**
- The primary limitation of this work lies in its practical applicability. The framework assumes that a patient's specific disease model is known a priori, an assumption that is unlikely to hold in any real-world clinical setting. The central challenge in personalized screening is not optimizing a policy for a given model, but rather inferring an accurate, patient-specific model from available data. By assuming the model is known, the paper sidesteps the most difficult aspect of the problem, making the proposed solution primarily an academic exercise.
- The methodological novelty of the work is limited. The techniques used appear to be adaptations of existing methods.
- The experimental evaluation is narrow. The comparison is limited to a single, simple baseline of fixed annual screening. More importantly, given the strong assumption about a known disease model, a critical question is the robustness of the method to model misspecification. The experiments should have included a sensitivity analysis to evaluate how the performance of the learned policy degrades when the true disease model deviates from the one used for optimization. This is essential for understanding the potential real-world viability of the approach.

**Overall:**
In summary, this paper tackles an interesting problem with a reasonable approach. However, its contribution is hampered by limited methodological novelty and, more significantly, by a core assumption that detracts from its practical relevance. To be more convincing, the paper would need to address the challenge of model uncertainty, for instance by demonstrating the policy's robustness to inaccuracies in the assumed disease model.
