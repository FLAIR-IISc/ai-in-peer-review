- DPSCREEN: a general decision-theoretic framework balancing screening costs and delayed detection, extending beyond standard POMDPs.  
- Key contributions: arbitrary finite-state disease models, an external information process for unscheduled visits, and trajectory-dependent delay costs.  
- Exact optimal policy is intractable; authors propose a PBVI-based approximation with a novel two-stage belief-point sampling.  
- The paper provides theoretical support: an approximation error bound (Proposition 1) and robustness to model estimation errors (Theorem 1).  
- Empirical validation on a large de-identified breast cancer dataset (45k patients) shows 28–68% fewer screenings for equal expected detection delay.  
- Strength: high societal and clinical impact by enabling personalized, cost-effective screening policies.  
- Strength: originality and generality beyond prior work, handling non-Markovian/process and unscheduled arrivals.  
- Strength: strong technical formulation using Bellman equations and well-motivated PBVI approximation.  
- Strength: rigorous theoretical analysis supporting the method’s correctness and stability.  
- Strength: compelling empirical results and clear demonstration of personalization across static and dynamic patient features.  
- Weakness: experiments use a two-state Markov model, which does not fully showcase claimed generality to non-Markovian processes.  
- Weakness: lacks discussion or experiments on practical application to semi-Markov or history-dependent models and trajectory sampling challenges.  
- Weakness: computational complexity and scalability need clearer reporting (experiment parameters, runtimes) and discussion of practical limits.  
- Weakness: belief sampling method needs clearer distinction from standard PBVI techniques and justification of its necessity.  
- Argument for acceptance: novel, principled, broadly applicable approach with solid theory and strong real-world empirical results.  
- Argument against acceptance: experimental scope doesn’t fully demonstrate generality and leaves scalability questions for longer horizons or complex models.  
- Conclusion: high-quality, well-written paper with strengths outweighing minor weaknesses; recommendation to accept.
