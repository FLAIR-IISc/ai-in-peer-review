This paper presents DPSCREEN, a method for determining optimal screening policies for large classes of diseases. Compared to other work in the area, DPSCREEN takes into account both the patient's clinical (screening) history, in addition to patient features pertinent to the specific disease model. As a result, DPSCREEN is able to achieve personalization in the screening process, which yields efficiency gains over existing screening policies without negatively impacting the timeliness of disease detection.

The authors provide a clear and concise discussion of the mathematical basis of the algorithm, including computational complexity. The approach is well-motivated, considering pertinent related work and addressing the limitations of existing methods. The use of a large breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients) from the Athena Health Network to validate the model is a significant strength of the paper. The results demonstrate the improvements provided by personalization, showing a reduction in the number of screenings performed while maintaining the same delay in disease detection.

The paper also explores the impact of different disease models on the screening policy, highlighting the importance of choosing the correct cost function for the specific disease being screened for. This is a valuable contribution, as it emphasizes the need for personalized screening policies that take into account the unique characteristics of each disease.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to balance technical detail with practical relevance. The authors provide a thorough discussion of the mathematical basis of the algorithm, but also demonstrate its potential for practical impact through the use of real-world data.
