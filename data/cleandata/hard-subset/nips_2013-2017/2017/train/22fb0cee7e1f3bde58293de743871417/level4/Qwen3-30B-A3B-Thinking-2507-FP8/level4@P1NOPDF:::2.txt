

The paper addresses the problem of optimizing patient screening policies using a finite-state stochastic disease model, aiming to minimize both screening costs and delays. The authors propose a computationally efficient approximate solution and demonstrate, through simulated breast cancer data, that their algorithm reduces screening delays without increasing costs compared to a baseline performing annual screening.

**Strengths:**  
The topic of personalized screening is highly relevant and impactful. The proposed algorithm is well-constructed, methodologically sound, and yields promising results. The paper itself is clearly written and well-structured.

**Major Concerns:**  
The primary limitation lies in the unrealistic assumption that the disease model for any individual patient is known a priori—a condition rarely met in clinical practice. The core challenge in personalized screening is not deriving optimal policies *given* a known model, but accurately inferring the disease model from real-world patient data. The paper treats the model as fixed and known, thereby sidestepping the central practical hurdle. Consequently, the proposed solution remains an academic exercise rather than a clinically actionable framework.

Furthermore, the methodological novelty is modest. The experimental evaluation compounds this issue by comparing only against a simplistic annual screening baseline. Crucially, the authors omit a critical analysis of the algorithm's robustness to model inaccuracies—specifically, how sensitive the derived screening policy is to errors in the disease model parameters. Without evidence that the policy remains effective under realistic model uncertainties, the practical viability of the approach remains unconvincing. For instance, how would the policy perform if the underlying disease progression rates were misestimated by 10% or 20%? Such robustness testing is essential to establish clinical relevance.

**Overall Assessment:**  
While the topic is compelling and the algorithm itself is reasonable, the paper fails to adequately bridge the gap between theoretical formulation and practical implementation. The lack of methodological novelty, combined with the absence of robustness analysis under model uncertainty, significantly weakens the contribution. To strengthen the work, the authors should explicitly address model inference from data and demonstrate the policy's performance under plausible inaccuracies in the disease model. Without these elements, the paper does not sufficiently justify its relevance to real-world screening challenges.
