Screening is critical for the diagnosis and treatment of a wide range of diseases, and this paper addresses the important problem of developing personalized screening policies that balance the benefits of early detection with the costs and risks associated with screening procedures. The authors propose a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for constructing these policies for a broad class of disease models, specifically modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process. This framework incorporates both patient-specific features and dynamic patient history (including external information and prior screening results) to determine optimal screening schedules.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing approaches that often rely on Markovian assumptions or population-level policies. It builds upon prior work in screening [7, 9, 10] and decision theory [18-21] by relaxing these constraints and addressing the computational challenges inherent in personalized screening. The approach is innovative in its ability to handle non-Markovian disease processes, external information, and the computational intractability of finding exactly optimal policies. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method using a large breast cancer dataset, showing significant reductions in screening frequency (28-68%) while maintaining comparable delays in disease detection compared to current clinical practices.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed framework extends existing screening methodologies by incorporating a more general disease model and personalized information, offering a more flexible and potentially more effective approach.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The authors provide a mathematically rigorous formulation of the problem and a computationally tractable approximation algorithm. The inclusion of approximation error bounds further strengthens the technical validity of the work.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The application to breast cancer screening, using a real-world dataset, demonstrates the practical potential of the proposed method. The results highlighting the reduction in screening frequency are particularly compelling.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear presentation of the model, methodology, and experimental results.
*   **Robustness Analysis:** The discussion of estimation error and its impact on the policy is insightful and adds to the credibility of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The model relies on several assumptions, such as the independence of test results conditional on the state trajectory and patient features. While acknowledged by the authors, the sensitivity of the results to these assumptions could be further investigated.
*   **Computational Complexity:** Although the authors provide a computationally tractable approximation, the worst-case complexity remains substantial. Further optimization or scalability analysis may be necessary for broader applicability.
*   **Generalizability:** While demonstrated on breast cancer data, the performance of DPSCREEN across different diseases and data distributions warrants further investigation. The authors briefly touch on this with the synthetic exercise, but more extensive evaluation would be beneficial.
*   **Cost Function:** The linear delay cost assumption is somewhat simplistic. While the authors acknowledge the importance of choosing the cost function based on the disease, a more detailed discussion of the implications of different cost functions would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important and challenging problem with a novel and technically sound approach.
*   The results demonstrate significant potential for improving screening efficiency and reducing patient burden.
*   The work is well-written, clearly presented, and provides valuable insights into the design of personalized screening policies.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity may limit the applicability of the method to large-scale datasets or complex disease models.
*   The model relies on several assumptions that could affect the accuracy and generalizability of the results.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of personalized screening. The proposed framework is innovative, technically sound, and demonstrates promising results in a real-world application. While some limitations exist, they are acknowledged by the authors and do not detract significantly from the overall value of the work. I recommend acceptance.
