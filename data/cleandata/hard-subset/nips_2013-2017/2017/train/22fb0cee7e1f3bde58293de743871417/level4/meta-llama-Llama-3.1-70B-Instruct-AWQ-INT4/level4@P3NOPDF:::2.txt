This paper proposes a method for determining the optimal policy for patient screening, given relevant information about the disease. The approach models the disease as a finite state stochastic process and aims to minimize screening costs and delays. The authors present an approximate solution that is computationally efficient. Experiments using simulated data related to breast cancer indicate that the proposed algorithm can reduce delays while maintaining similar costs compared to a trivial baseline.

The paper has several strengths. Firstly, personalized screening is an important research topic, and the authors' focus on this area is commendable. Secondly, the proposed algorithm is well-developed and reasonable, demonstrating a clear understanding of the problem. The results of the experiments are also promising, suggesting the potential effectiveness of the approach. Furthermore, the paper is well-written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and understand their methodology.

However, there are several limitations to the paper. A significant concern is that the proposed algorithm is largely an academic exercise, as it assumes that the disease model for a given patient is known. In practice, this is rarely the case, and the main challenge in personalized screening is often inferring the model from data. This limitation raises questions about the practical relevance of the approach. Additionally, the methodological novelty of the paper is not substantial, and the authors could have built more significantly on existing work in the field.

The experimental evaluation of the paper is also limited. The comparison to a baseline of annual screening is not particularly informative, and more comprehensive experiments are needed to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm. Specifically, it would be essential to investigate the robustness of the method to inaccuracies in the disease model. This could involve analyzing how sensitive the policy is to uncertainties in the disease model, which would provide a more complete understanding of the approach's limitations and potential applications.

In conclusion, while the topic of personalized screening is interesting, and the proposed algorithm is reasonable, the paper falls short in demonstrating significant methodological novelty and practical relevance. To strengthen the paper, the authors should address the limitations mentioned above, particularly the assumption of a known disease model, and provide more comprehensive experimental evaluations to convince readers of the approach's potential impact.
