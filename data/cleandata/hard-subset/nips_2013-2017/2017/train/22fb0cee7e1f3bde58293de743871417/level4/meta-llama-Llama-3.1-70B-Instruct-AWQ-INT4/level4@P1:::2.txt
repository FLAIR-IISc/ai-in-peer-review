The paper "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening" proposes a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The disease is modeled as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state. The authors incorporate the possibility of external information, such as self-examination or discovery of co-morbidities, that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment.

The paper is well-written, and the proposed algorithm is reasonable and well-developed. The topic of personalized screening is important, and the results of the experiments on simulated data related to breast cancer are promising, indicating that the proposed algorithm could reduce delays while keeping the same cost when compared to a trivial baseline.

However, there are several limitations to the paper. Firstly, the proposed algorithm is purely an academic exercise, as it assumes that the disease model of a given patient is known, which is rarely the case in practice. The main obstacle in personalized screening is not coming up with a policy when the model is known, but rather inferring the model from data. This limitation is not addressed in the paper.

Secondly, the methodological novelty of the paper is not large. While the authors propose a new algorithm for constructing personalized screening policies, the underlying ideas are not significantly different from existing work in the field.

Thirdly, the experiments only compare the proposed algorithm to a baseline doing annual screening. Given the previous point about the disease model, an important question that should be studied experimentally is the robustness of the proposed method to inaccuracies in the disease model. In other words, how sensitive is the policy to uncertainties in the disease model? This question is not addressed in the paper.

Overall, while the topic is interesting and the proposed algorithm is reasonable, the paper would need to do a better job to convince readers that this work is practically relevant. The authors should address the limitations mentioned above and provide more experimental results to demonstrate the robustness of their method.

Positives:

* Personalized screening is an important topic of research
* The proposed algorithm is reasonable and well-developed
* The results are promising
* The paper is well-written

Negatives:

* The proposed algorithm is purely an academic exercise, as it assumes that the disease model of a given patient is known
* The methodological novelty is not large
* The experiments only compare the proposed algorithm to a baseline doing annual screening, and do not address the robustness of the method to inaccuracies in the disease model.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies
* The results of the experiments on simulated data related to breast cancer are promising
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The proposed algorithm is not practically relevant, as it assumes that the disease model of a given patient is known
* The methodological novelty is not large
* The experiments do not address the robustness of the method to inaccuracies in the disease model.
