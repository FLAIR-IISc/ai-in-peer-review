- Proposes a novel framework for personalized screening that balances screening cost against delay in disease detection using a finite-state stochastic model with an absorbing disease state.  
- Incorporates external information (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger earlier screenings and models delay costs depending on the entire disease trajectory.  
- Provides a computationally tractable approximation to the optimal screening policy with theoretical guarantees and approximation error bounds (Proposition 1).  
- Shows key value-function properties (convexity and piecewise linearity, Lemma 1) and provides rigorous proofs.  
- Evaluates method on a large breast cancer dataset, achieving 28–68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining the same expected detection delay.  
- Demonstrates personalization to patient features (risk factors) and dynamic history (previous test results) in policy construction.  
- Generalizes beyond prior work by handling non-stationary disease progression, external information processes, and trajectory-dependent delay costs.  
- Empirical validation is thorough with careful model estimation, policy construction, and appropriate comparisons to clinical standards.  
- Writing is clear and well-organized, with useful supplementary materials (pseudo-code, dataset/model details) that aid reproducibility.  
- Graphical illustrations (e.g., Figure 1) effectively communicate how policies adapt to features and history.  
- Highly original in scope and generality compared to POMDP-based and medical literature approaches.  
- Significant potential real-world impact by reducing healthcare costs and applicable to diseases beyond breast cancer.  
- Minor weaknesses: limited discussion of adapting to more complex progression models and assumption of no false positives in test outcomes.  
- Recommendation: Accept — technically sound, novel, empirically convincing, and practically relevant.
