This paper aims to determine the optimal patient screening policy based on relevant information, leveraging a finite state stochastic process to model disease progression. The proposed policy seeks to minimize screening costs and delays, offering a computationally efficient approximate solution. Experimental results on simulated breast cancer data suggest the algorithm can reduce delays without incurring additional costs, outperforming a baseline approach.

 Positives:
+ Personalized screening is a significant research area, and this paper contributes to its development.
+ The proposed algorithm is well-conceived and effectively developed.
+ The results show promise and warrant further exploration.
+ The paper is clearly and effectively written.

 Negatives:
- The proposed algorithm is largely theoretical, as it relies on the assumption that the disease model for a given patient is known, which is rarely the case in practice. A more significant challenge in personalized screening lies in inferring the disease model from available data rather than developing a policy based on a known model.
- The methodological innovation presented is limited.
- The experimental comparison to a baseline annual screening approach is insufficient. A critical aspect to investigate is the robustness of the proposed method to inaccuracies in the disease model, specifically its sensitivity to uncertainties in the disease model.

 Overall, while the topic is engaging, and the proposed algorithm is sound, the methodological novelty is not substantial. To demonstrate practical relevance, the paper would need to provide more convincing evidence.
