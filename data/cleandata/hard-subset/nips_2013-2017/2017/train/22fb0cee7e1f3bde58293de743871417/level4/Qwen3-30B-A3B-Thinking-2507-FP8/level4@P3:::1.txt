

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that dynamically adapt to both patient-specific features and the patient's clinical history. The authors address a significant gap in the literature by developing a general approach applicable to a wide range of disease models, rather than being restricted to specific assumptions about disease progression (e.g., Markovian processes). This represents an important advancement over existing works that typically assume stationary models for disease progression and test outcomes [7,9,10].

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it formulates the personalized screening problem as a constrained optimization task balancing screening costs against delay in disease detection. Second, it develops a computationally tractable approximation algorithm (DPSCREEN) that provides provable performance bounds on the approximation error. Third, it demonstrates significant practical gains through extensive validation on a large breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients with 84,000 mammograms) from the Athena Health Network, showing 28-68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining equivalent detection timeliness compared to current clinical practices.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound and well-supported. The authors provide a rigorous mathematical formulation of the problem, including careful modeling of the disease process, external information, and screening policies. They prove important properties of the value function (Lemma 1) and establish a bound on the approximation error (Proposition 1). The experimental validation is thorough, with careful attention to model estimation from real-world data and appropriate performance metrics. The paper also thoughtfully addresses robustness to model estimation errors, showing that the proposed policy remains effective even when the underlying disease model is imperfectly estimated.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The problem formulation is well-structured, with clear definitions of key concepts (disease model, external information, screening policies). The technical content is presented logically, building from the problem statement through the mathematical formulation to the algorithmic implementation and experimental validation. The authors effectively use figures (particularly Figure 1) to illustrate the personalization mechanisms of their approach. The writing is precise and accessible to readers familiar with decision theory and machine learning.

The originality of the work is strong. While previous works have developed personalized screening policies for specific diseases (e.g., breast cancer [7,8]), they typically make restrictive assumptions about the disease model and test outcomes. DPSCREEN's key innovation is its generality—it accommodates a wide range of disease models, including non-Markovian processes, and incorporates external information (like self-examinations) that can trigger early visits. This represents a significant conceptual advance beyond the existing literature, as the authors correctly note that standard POMDP methods cannot be directly applied to this setting due to the time-dependent nature of decision epochs and the trajectory-dependent delay costs.

The significance of this work is substantial. Personalized screening has the potential to reduce healthcare costs while maintaining or improving patient outcomes, particularly for diseases where early detection is critical. The paper demonstrates clear practical impact through its empirical results, showing that DPSCREEN can substantially reduce screening frequency (28-68%) without compromising detection timeliness. The authors also provide important insights into how different cost structures (linear vs. quadratic delay costs) lead to different screening regimes, highlighting the importance of tailoring policies to specific diseases.

Strengths:
- General framework applicable to diverse disease models
- Rigorous mathematical foundation with provable approximation bounds
- Extensive validation on a large real-world dataset
- Clear demonstration of practical impact (28-68% reduction in screenings)
- Thoughtful analysis of cost structures and disease-specific policy differences
- Strong attention to robustness against model estimation errors

Weaknesses:
- The computational complexity analysis, while thorough, could benefit from more concrete examples of how the algorithm scales with problem size
- The paper could better clarify the limitations of the framework (e.g., when the disease model is highly non-stationary)
- Some technical details about the belief space construction (Algorithm 1) might benefit from additional explanation for readers unfamiliar with POMDPs

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear technical contribution with strong theoretical foundations
- Demonstrated practical significance through comprehensive empirical validation
- Addresses a meaningful problem with substantial real-world impact
- Significantly advances the state of the art by providing a general framework where previous work was limited to specific disease models
- Well-written with clear exposition of both theoretical and practical aspects

Arguments against acceptance:
- The paper is somewhat narrowly focused on breast cancer, though the framework is general
- The computational complexity, while theoretically bounded, might be challenging for very large-scale implementations

Overall, this paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of personalized healthcare decision-making. It successfully bridges theoretical decision theory with practical clinical application, demonstrating how a general framework can yield substantial efficiency gains in screening practices. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and has clear potential for real-world impact. I strongly recommend acceptance.
