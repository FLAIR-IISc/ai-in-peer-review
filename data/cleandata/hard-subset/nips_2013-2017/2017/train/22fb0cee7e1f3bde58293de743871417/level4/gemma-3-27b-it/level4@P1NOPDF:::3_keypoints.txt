- Proposes a personalized method to predict optimal breast-cancer screening times minimizing screening and delayed-detection costs using patient histories and external data, showing significant improvement over existing approaches.  
- Core idea of adapting screening schedules to individual risk profiles is compelling and addresses over- and under-screening from one-size-fits-all guidelines.  
- Problem is formulated as a dynamic programming model with an efficient algorithm; objective includes both screening and delay costs.  
- Model and algorithm are described in detail.  
- Experimental results convincingly outperform fixed-interval and age-based guidelines with substantial gains in expected net benefit.  
- Ablation studies illuminate the contribution of different features and support design choices.  
- Data limitation: retrospective dataset may introduce biases and raises questions about generalizability to other demographics or healthcare access.  
- Computational complexity needs clearer, concrete quantification of scaling with patient count and medical-history length.  
- Sensitivity analysis is focused on cost parameters; should also assess robustness to inaccuracies in the risk-prediction model.  
- Presentation issue: some figures lack clear x-axis labels, reducing interpretability.  
- Overall assessment: strong, well-motivated, and technically sound work with significant impact; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
