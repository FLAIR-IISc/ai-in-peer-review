This paper proposes a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The disease is modeled as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, allowing for the incorporation of external information that may trigger patient arrival at the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment. The authors apply their method to breast cancer data and demonstrate that their personalized screening policies can reduce the number of screenings performed while maintaining the same delay in detection of disease compared to current clinical policies.

The paper is well-written, and the topic of personalized screening is important and relevant. The proposed algorithm is reasonable and well-developed, and the results are promising. However, there are several limitations that need to be addressed.

Firstly, the methodological novelty of the paper is not large. The authors build upon existing work in decision theory and POMDPs, and the key contribution of the paper is the application of these methods to the problem of personalized screening. While the authors do provide some new insights and results, the overall approach is not significantly different from existing work in the field.

Secondly, the experiments presented in the paper are limited. The authors only compare their proposed algorithm to a baseline of annual screening, and do not consider other possible screening policies or the robustness of their method to inaccuracies in the disease model. Given that the disease model is a critical component of the proposed algorithm, it is surprising that the authors do not investigate the sensitivity of their policy to uncertainties in the disease model.

Finally, the authors assume that the disease model is known, which is not a realistic assumption in practice. The main obstacle in personalized screening is not coming up with a policy when the model is known, but rather inferring the model from data. The authors do provide some discussion of model estimation in the supplementary materials, but this is not a major focus of the paper.

Overall, while the topic of personalized screening is interesting and the proposed algorithm is reasonable, the paper would need to do a better job of convincing readers that this work is practically relevant. The authors should consider addressing the limitations mentioned above in future work.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel framework for personalized screening that is well-developed and reasonable.
* The results are promising, and the authors demonstrate that their method can reduce the number of screenings performed while maintaining the same delay in detection of disease.
* The paper is well-written, and the topic of personalized screening is important and relevant.

* The methodological novelty of the paper is not large, and the authors build upon existing work in decision theory and POMDPs.
* The experiments presented in the paper are limited, and the authors do not consider other possible screening policies or the robustness of their method to inaccuracies in the disease model.
* The authors assume that the disease model is known, which is not a realistic assumption in practice.
