- Proposes a personalized framework to determine optimal breast cancer screening intervals that minimizes combined screening and delay costs.  
- Integrates individual medical histories with complementary external data (socioeconomic, environmental).  
- Empirical results show consistent reductions in total costs across multiple benchmark datasets, outperforming existing approaches.  
- Manuscript is well-structured and technically sound, with clear presentation of methodology, experiments, and results.  
- Authors justify using a reinforcement learning approach to balance immediate screening costs against delayed diagnosis risk.  
- Evaluation lacks granular quantitative metrics (e.g., percent cost reduction, fewer missed detections) to contextualize “significant improvement.”  
- Paper does not detail handling of edge cases (e.g., patients with rare medical histories).  
- Needs ablation studies to isolate the contribution of external data versus medical history alone.  
- Clinical interpretability is superficial; should analyze alignment of model decisions with clinical guidelines for real-world adoption.  
- Writing is generally clear but Section 3.2 is overly dense, Figure 3 caption should define baselines, and there are minor grammatical/tense inconsistencies.  
- Overall promising and practically applicable; recommended for acceptance pending minor revisions addressing the above points.
