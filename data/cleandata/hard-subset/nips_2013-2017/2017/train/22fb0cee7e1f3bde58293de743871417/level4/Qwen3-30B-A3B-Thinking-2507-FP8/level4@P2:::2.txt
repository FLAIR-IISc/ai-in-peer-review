

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against detection delays. The authors model disease progression as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating external information (e.g., patient self-examinations) that may trigger earlier visits to clinicians. The key contribution is a computationally tractable approximation to the otherwise intractable optimal screening policy, which is evaluated on breast cancer data showing 28-68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining equivalent expected detection delays compared to current clinical practices.

The paper relates well to prior work in decision theory and medical screening. It builds on POMDP-based approaches like Ayer et al. (2015) for mammography screening but generalizes beyond their Markov disease process and stationary test outcome assumptions. Unlike previous works that focus on specific disease models (e.g., Ayer et al. for breast cancer, Erenay et al. for colon cancer), this framework accommodates a broader class of disease models, including dynamic survival processes and first-hitting time models. The paper also distinguishes itself from medical literature that relies on simulation-based comparisons of fixed policy sets (e.g., MISCAN-COLON for colon cancer) by providing a principled method to derive personalized policies.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The theoretical foundation is solid, with clear derivation of the Bellman equations and rigorous approximation error bounds (Proposition 1). The authors provide a well-structured proof of the convexity and piecewise linearity of the value function (Lemma 1), extending results from POMDP literature to their more general setting. The experimental methodology is sound, using observational data to estimate disease progression parameters and evaluating the policy on the learned model.

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear organization and effective graphical representations. The exposition of the model and problem formulation is particularly strong, making the technical content accessible. The supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including the complete algorithmic procedure and implementation details.

*Originality:* The framework generalizes previous approaches by accommodating non-Markovian disease processes and external information, which is significant given that many diseases (e.g., HIV, colon cancer) do not follow simple Markov assumptions. The incorporation of external information processes (like patient self-examinations) represents a meaningful extension beyond standard POMDP formulations.

*Significance:* Personalized screening addresses an important clinical problem with substantial potential impact on healthcare resource allocation. The demonstrated 28-68% reduction in screenings while maintaining detection delays suggests meaningful practical benefits, particularly for high-risk populations.

**Weaknesses:**

*Quality:* The assumption that the disease model parameters are known represents a significant limitation for practical implementation. While the paper acknowledges model estimation challenges (Section 3.3), it fails to provide experimental evidence of how robust the policy is to estimation errors. The claim that "the impact of estimation error on the exactly optimal policy is minimal" (Theorem 1) is not validated through experiments, which is a critical omission given that model estimation from observational data is the primary practical hurdle.

*Originality:* While the framework is more general than prior works, the core algorithm (Point-Based Value Iteration) is not novel. The main contribution lies in the application to screening rather than methodological innovation. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from existing POMDP solution methods in the literature.

*Significance:* The practical relevance of the proposed method is undermined by the unrealistic assumption of known disease models. Without evidence that the policy remains effective when model parameters are estimated from data (as is the case in real-world implementation), the significance claim is difficult to support. The experiments only compare to annual screening policies, not to other personalized approaches or to policies that account for model uncertainty.

*Clarity:* While the paper is generally well-written, the discussion of the belief space construction (Section 3.2) could be clearer. The authors describe sampling trajectories from the disease model but do not specify how this sampling procedure relates to the actual data available in practice.

**Recommendation:**

The paper addresses an important problem with a theoretically sound approach that extends previous work. However, the practical relevance is significantly compromised by the unrealistic assumption that disease models are known, and the lack of empirical validation of robustness to model misspecification. While the results are promising, the paper would benefit from additional experiments demonstrating how the policy performs when disease parameters are estimated from data, and how it compares to alternative approaches that handle model uncertainty.

The topic is highly relevant to NIPS, but the contribution does not sufficiently advance the state of the art in methodology. The paper would be stronger with a more thorough exploration of the practical implementation challenges and a clearer demonstration of how this framework offers advantages over existing approaches in real-world settings. Given the current version, I recommend rejection, but with encouragement to address these limitations in future work.
