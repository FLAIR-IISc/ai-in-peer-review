- Introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for personalized screening that dynamically trades off screening frequency against detection delay.  
- Models disease as a general finite-state stochastic process with absorbing states, overcoming restrictive Markov/semi-Markov assumptions in prior work.  
- Incorporates non-stationary external information (e.g., self-exams) and dynamic patient history into screening policy decisions.  
- Provides a computationally tractable approximation algorithm with provable error bounds (Proposition 1).  
- Validated on a large breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients), showing 28–68% reductions in screenings versus clinical benchmarks while maintaining equivalent detection delays.  
- Strength: Technical rigor—generalizes disease models and offers formal approximation guarantees.  
- Strength: Significant clinical impact—substantial screening reductions, especially for high-risk patients; highlights importance of disease-specific delay costs.  
- Strength: Clear problem formulation and algorithmic presentation with helpful graphical illustrations.  
- Weakness: Limited to single-absorbing-state disease models, restricting applicability to competing-risk settings.  
- Weakness: Validation is retrospective observational; lacks prospective clinical validation against real-world protocols (e.g., USPSTF).  
- Weakness: Synthetic disease cost analysis not empirically validated for non-breast cancers.  
- Pro: First framework handling dynamic history and external information with rigorous guarantees.  
- Pro: Demonstrable large-scale empirical gains and addresses limitations of prior NIPS screening work.  
- Con: Modeling scope and validation limitations temper generalizability despite strong contributions.
