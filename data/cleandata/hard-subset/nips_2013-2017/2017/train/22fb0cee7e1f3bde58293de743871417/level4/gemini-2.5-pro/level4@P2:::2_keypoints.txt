- Proposes DPSCREEN: a framework for dynamic, personalized screening by modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with external triggers for unscheduled visits.  
- Objective: find policies that trade off screening frequency costs against costs of delayed detection.  
- Formulates problem as a dynamic program solved via Bellman equations and approximates it using point-based value iteration (PBVI).  
- Empirical validation: learned a disease model from a large breast cancer dataset and showed simulated policies reduce screenings by 28–68% versus annual screening while keeping expected detection delay constant.  

Strengths:
- Addresses a clinically significant and impactful problem of personalized medical screening with clear motivation.  
- Technically sound formulation that generalizes standard POMDPs (action-dependent transition times, trajectory-dependent costs).  
- Strong empirical results on large-scale data demonstrating substantial screening reductions without increased delay.  
- Paper is generally well-written and clearly structured.  

Weaknesses:
- Assumes an accurate personalized disease model is known a priori; dependency on model quality is not sufficiently explored.  
- Methodological novelty is limited since the core solver is an application of existing PBVI techniques.  
- Lacks empirical robustness analysis to model misspecification; experiments only compare to fixed-interval baselines.  

Overall assessment:
- Solid and promising planning contribution with practical relevance limited by reliance on a known model and insufficient sensitivity analysis; would be stronger with experiments showing robustness to model inaccuracies.
