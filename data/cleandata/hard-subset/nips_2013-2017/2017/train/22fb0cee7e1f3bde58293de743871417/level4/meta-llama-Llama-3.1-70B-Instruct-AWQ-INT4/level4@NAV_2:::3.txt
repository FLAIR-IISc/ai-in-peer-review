The paper "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening" proposes a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The disease is modeled as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating the possibility of external information, such as self-examination or discovery of co-morbidities, that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment.

The authors relate their work to previous studies on screening policies, including POMDP-based screening models and Bayesian design for maximizing information gain. They also compare their approach to stochastic simulation-based methods used in medical literature, highlighting the limitations of existing methods in terms of model restrictions and the inability to incorporate external information.

The proposed method, DPSCREEN, uses a dynamic programming approach to construct an approximately optimal policy. The authors provide a detailed description of the model and problem formulation, including the definition of beliefs, Bellman equations, and the construction of alpha vectors. They also discuss the computational complexity of the algorithm and provide bounds for the approximation error.
