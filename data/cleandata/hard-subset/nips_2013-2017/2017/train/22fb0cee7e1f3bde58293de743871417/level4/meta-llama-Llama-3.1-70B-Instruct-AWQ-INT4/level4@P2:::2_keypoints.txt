- Proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized screening that trades off screening cost against detection delay.
- Models disease as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and allows external arrival triggers.
- Demonstrates on breast cancer data that personalized policies can reduce screenings while preserving detection delay versus current clinical policy.
- Paper is well-written and addresses an important, relevant problem.
- Proposed algorithm is reasonable, well-developed, and shows promising results.
- Methodological novelty is limited—approach largely builds on existing decision theory and POMDP work.
- Experiments are limited: only compared to annual screening and do not evaluate alternative policies.
- Lacks robustness/sensitivity analysis to inaccuracies in the disease model, despite model being central to the method.
- Assumes the disease model is known, which is unrealistic; model estimation is only briefly discussed in supplement.
- Needs stronger evidence of practical relevance and real-world applicability to convince readers.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, well-developed framework; promising empirical reductions in screenings; clear writing and important topic.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited novelty, insufficient experimental breadth, and unrealistic modeling assumptions.
