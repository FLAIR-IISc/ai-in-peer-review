This paper addresses the important problem of personalized screening policies, aiming to balance the trade-off between screening costs and delays in disease detection. The authors propose a framework modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and develop a computationally tractable approach to approximate the optimal screening policy. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on a breast cancer dataset, showing reductions in screening frequency while maintaining comparable detection delays compared to standard clinical practices.

The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the challenges in personalized screening. The proposed approach, building on POMDPs and incorporating external information, is reasonable and appears well-developed, demonstrating a solid understanding of the relevant literature in decision theory and medical screening. The results presented are promising and suggest potential benefits in reducing unnecessary screenings. The inclusion of a discussion on robustness to model estimation error is a strength, acknowledging a key practical challenge.

However, a significant weakness lies in the strong assumptions required for practical applicability. The method relies on a known disease model for each patient, which is unrealistic in most clinical scenarios. The core challenge in personalized screening isn't *solving* for an optimal policy *given* a known model, but rather *inferring* an accurate model from limited and noisy patient data. While the authors acknowledge this in the discussion, the paper doesn't address how to bridge this gap, limiting its immediate practical impact.

The methodological novelty is incremental. The paper extends existing POMDP-based frameworks but doesn't introduce fundamentally new algorithmic techniques. While the extension to handle external information and dynamic risk assessment is valuable, it builds upon established principles. 

The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating improvement over a fixed annual screening baseline, feels incomplete. Given the sensitivity of the policy to the underlying disease model, a crucial experiment would investigate the robustness of the proposed method to inaccuracies and uncertainties in model parameters. A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of model misspecification on policy performance is lacking. Furthermore, comparing against more sophisticated existing clinical policies (e.g., risk-adjusted screening guidelines) would provide a stronger benchmark. It would also be useful to examine the impact of varying the weighting factor 'w' in the objective function and explore its clinical interpretation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and clinically relevant problem: personalized screening.
*   Presents a well-defined and mathematically sound framework.
*   Demonstrates potential benefits in reducing screening frequency without compromising detection delays.
*   Acknowledges and begins to address the issue of model estimation error.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Relies on the unrealistic assumption of known patient-specific disease models.
*   Methodological novelty is incremental.
*   Experimental evaluation is limited and lacks a robustness analysis to model uncertainty.
*   Does not offer a solution to the crucial problem of model inference.



**Overall:** This paper presents a sound technical contribution to the field of personalized screening. However, its practical relevance is limited by the strong modeling assumptions and the lack of a discussion on model inference. While the results are promising, further work is needed to address the challenges of applying this method in real-world clinical settings. I recommend a **weak reject**. The paper could be significantly strengthened by either addressing the model inference problem or by focusing on scenarios where a reasonable disease model *is* available, such as for patients with well-characterized genetic predispositions.
