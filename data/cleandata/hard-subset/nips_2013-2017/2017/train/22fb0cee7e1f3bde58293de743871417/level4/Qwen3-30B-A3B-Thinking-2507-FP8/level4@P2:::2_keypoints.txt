- Introduces DPSCREEN: a framework for personalized screening that trades off screening costs and detection delays using a finite-state stochastic disease model with external information triggers.  
- Main contribution is a computationally tractable approximation to the intractable optimal screening policy, evaluated on breast cancer data showing 28–68% fewer screenings for equivalent detection delays.  
- Extends prior POMDP-based screening work by accommodating non-Markovian disease processes, dynamic survival/first-hitting time models, and external information processes.  
- Distinguishes itself from medical simulation-based comparisons by deriving personalized policies from a principled decision-theoretic framework.  
- Theoretical strength: solid derivation of Bellman equations and rigorous approximation error bounds (Proposition 1).  
- Value function analysis: proof of convexity and piecewise-linearity (Lemma 1), extending POMDP results to the generalized model.  
- Experimental methodology: sound—uses observational data to estimate progression parameters and evaluates policies on the learned model.  
- Clarity: paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, with clear exposition, effective figures, and reproducible supplementary materials.  
- Originality: meaningful extension by handling non-Markovian disease dynamics and incorporating external patient-generated information.  
- Significance: potential high practical impact by substantially reducing screening frequency while maintaining detection performance.  
- Major weakness: assumes disease model parameters are known, which is unrealistic for practical deployment.  
- Lacks empirical validation of robustness to parameter estimation errors despite acknowledging estimation challenges; Theorem 1’s claim is not experimentally tested.  
- Methodological novelty is limited: uses existing Point-Based Value Iteration techniques, so primary novelty is application rather than algorithmic innovation.  
- Experimental comparisons are limited—only compared to annual screening and not to other personalized or uncertainty-aware policies.  
- Clarity issue: belief space construction and how sampled trajectories relate to real-world available data is not clearly specified.  
- Recommendation: promising and relevant work but practical relevance is weakened by unrealistic assumptions and insufficient empirical robustness checks; reviewer recommends rejection with encouragement to address these limitations and add experiments on estimated models and comparisons to alternative approaches.
