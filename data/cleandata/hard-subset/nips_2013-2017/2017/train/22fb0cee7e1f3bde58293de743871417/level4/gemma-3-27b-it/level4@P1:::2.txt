Screening is crucial for the diagnosis and treatment of a wide range of diseases. This paper addresses the important problem of developing personalized screening policies that balance the costs of screening with the risks associated with delayed detection. The authors model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and propose a computationally efficient, approximately optimal policy for determining screening schedules. Their experiments, using breast cancer data, demonstrate the potential for reducing the number of screenings while maintaining comparable detection delays.

**Strengths:**

*   Personalized screening is a significant and increasingly relevant research area.
*   The proposed algorithm is well-developed and logically sound.
*   The results presented are promising and suggest potential improvements over standard screening practices.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The proposed algorithm, while theoretically sound, relies on the assumption of a known disease model for each patient. This is a substantial limitation, as inferring accurate disease models from patient data remains a primary challenge in personalized screening. The practical relevance of an algorithm predicated on known models is questionable.
*   The methodological novelty is incremental. While the framework extends existing approaches, it doesn’t represent a paradigm shift in the field.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited. The comparisons are primarily made against a baseline of annual screening. A critical experiment, given the model dependence, would assess the robustness of the proposed method to inaccuracies in the estimated disease model. Specifically, how sensitive is the resulting policy to uncertainties in model parameters?

**Overall:**

This paper tackles an important problem with a reasonable algorithmic approach. However, the assumption of known disease models significantly limits its practical impact. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the robustness of the proposed policy to model uncertainty and demonstrating its performance in scenarios more closely resembling real-world clinical settings. While the topic is interesting and the algorithm is well-constructed, the limited novelty and questionable practical relevance currently make a strong recommendation for acceptance difficult.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in personalized medicine.
*   Presents a well-defined and logically sound algorithmic approach.
*   Demonstrates potential benefits in simulated experiments.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Relies on an unrealistic assumption of known disease models.
*   Methodological novelty is limited.
*   Experimental evaluation lacks robustness analysis.
*   Practical relevance is not convincingly demonstrated.
