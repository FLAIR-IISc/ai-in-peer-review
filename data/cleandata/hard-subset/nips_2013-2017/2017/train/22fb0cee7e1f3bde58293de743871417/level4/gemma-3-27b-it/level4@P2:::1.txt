This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies across a wide range of diseases. The key innovation lies in its ability to integrate both a patient’s clinical history – specifically, the results of previous screenings – with individual patient features relevant to the disease model. This personalization leads to more efficient screening schedules, achieving comparable disease detection timeliness to existing policies while reducing the frequency of screenings.

The paper is technically sound and well-written. The motivation for the work is clear, and the authors appropriately situate their approach within the context of existing literature in screening, decision theory, and related fields. The mathematical formulation is concise yet thorough, including a discussion of computational complexity. The validation using a large dataset (45,000 patients) from the Athena Health Network provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed personalization strategy. Furthermore, the demonstration of the framework's adaptability across different disease models enhances its generalizability. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of constructing personalized screening policies considering both patient history and features is a significant contribution, particularly the ability to handle general stochastic disease models.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation and analysis appear rigorous. The inclusion of computational complexity analysis is valuable.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The use of a large, real-world dataset strengthens the claims regarding the practical benefits of the proposed method.
*   **Generalizability:** The framework's applicability to different disease models, as demonstrated by the synthetic exercise and breast cancer example, is a significant strength.
*   **Potential Impact:** The potential for reduced screening frequency without compromising detection timeliness could have substantial benefits for both patients and healthcare systems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Model Assumptions:** The reliance on a finite-state stochastic process for disease progression is a simplification. While acknowledged, the impact of this assumption on the accuracy of the results is not fully explored.
*   **Data Specificity:** The experiments are primarily focused on breast cancer. While the framework is presented as general, further validation on data from other diseases would strengthen the claims.
*   **Complexity of Implementation:** The computational complexity, while discussed, could be a barrier to practical adoption. Further work on optimization or simplification of the algorithm might be needed.
*   **Cost Function:** The choice of a linear delay cost function is somewhat arbitrary. A more thorough sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of different cost function shapes would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem with a novel and technically sound approach.
*   The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements over existing screening policies.
*   The framework’s generalizability and potential for real-world impact are promising.
*   The authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The simplifying assumptions made in the model might limit the applicability of the framework.
*   The computational complexity could hinder practical implementation.
*   The validation is primarily focused on a single disease.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance. The strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The proposed methodology represents a valuable contribution to the field of personalized medicine and has the potential to improve healthcare outcomes. While the limitations are acknowledged, they do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work. I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses in future work, particularly by exploring the impact of different model assumptions and cost functions, and by validating the framework on a wider range of diseases.
