The purpose of this paper is to identify the best policy for patient screening based on relevant data. To provide this policy, the disease should be modeled as a stochastic process with a finite state. This policy attempts to reduce the cost and delay of screening. The authors propose an approximate solution that is computationally efficient. Experiments conducted on simulated data related to breast cancer indicate that the proposed algorithm can reduce delays while maintaining the same cost as a baseline.

**Pros:**
+ Personalized screening is an important topic in research.
+ The proposed algorithm is reasonable and well-developed.
+ The results are promising.
+ The paper is well-written.

**Cons:**
- The proposed algorithm is entirely an academic exercise. It assumes that a patient's disease model is known, which can never be assumed in reality. The main obstacle to personalized screening is the inability to create a policy based on knowing the model, but rather to estimate the model from the data.
- The methodological novelty is not very high.
- The experiments only compare the proposed algorithm with a baseline of annual screening. In terms of prior knowledge of the disease model, an important question that should be experimentally studied is the robustness of the proposed method in the event of errors in the disease model. In other words, how sensitive the policy is to the uncertainty of the disease model.

Overall, the topic is interesting and the proposed algorithm is reasonable. However, the methodological novelty is not very high, and the paper needs to do a better job to convince readers that this work is practically relevant.
