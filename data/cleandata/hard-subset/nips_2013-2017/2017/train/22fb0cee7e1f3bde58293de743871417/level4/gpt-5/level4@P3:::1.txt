Summary
The paper introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing dynamic, personalized screening policies across a broad class of disease models. Diseases are modeled as finite-state stochastic processes with an absorbing disease state, and screening decisions depend on patient features and dynamic history, including test outcomes and an external information process (e.g., self-exams, comorbidities) that can trigger earlier-than-scheduled visits. The objective trades off the frequency (and cost) of screening against delay in detection. The authors show that computing the exact optimal policy is intractable, derive Bellman equations with piecewise-linear value functions, and propose a tractable, point-based approximation using sampled trajectory-based belief sets. They provide a bound on approximation error tied to belief sampling density and a robustness result showing that optimal recommended actions are locally insensitive to small model-estimation errors. Using a 45,000-patient breast cancer dataset, DPSCREEN reduces the number of screenings by 28–68% while maintaining the same expected detection delay as annual (and in the appendix, biennial) clinical policies. A synthetic experiment further demonstrates how the screening strategy shifts under linear versus quadratic delay costs.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates DPSCREEN relative to POMDP-based screening methods for breast and colon cancer (e.g., Ayer et al.; Maillart et al.) and Bayesian design approaches (e.g., Rizopoulos et al.; Alaa et al.). Unlike prior works that assume Markovian disease processes and stationary/independent test outcomes, DPSCREEN allows a general finite-state disease process and incorporates endogenous arrivals via external signals. It also handles trajectory-dependent delay costs, which are not directly accommodated by standard POMDP/POSMDP formulations. Compared to medical simulation studies (e.g., MISCAN-COLON, USPSTF modeling), which compare a finite set of policies, DPSCREEN optimizes over policies within a general model class.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of a difficult, clinically relevant problem that unifies personalization by baseline features and by evolving test history/external information.
- Solid theoretical framing: Bellman equations, piecewise-linear value function, alpha-vector characterization, and a PBVI-style approximation with an explicit (albeit high-level) bound.
- Robustness analysis showing local constancy of optimal actions under small parameter estimation errors.
- Empirical demonstration on a large real-world dataset with clear, interpretable gains in reduced screening burden at matched expected delay.
- Thoughtful exploration of how disease-specific delay costs (linear vs. quadratic) induce different operating regimes, underscoring the importance of problem-specific objectives.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Assumptions about test accuracy. Several results (e.g., Proposition 1) assume no false positives/negatives; the main model also assumes no false positives at diagnosis. While confirmatory testing can mitigate false positives, real-world screening has both types of errors. Please clarify precisely where perfect accuracy is assumed (theoretical bounds vs. policy) and discuss how the approximation and robustness results extend under realistic error rates.
- Evaluation relies on model-based counterfactuals. This is standard, but the paper could strengthen credibility with sensitivity analyses: performance as a function of plausible variation in transition hazards, test sensitivity/specificity, external signal parameters (σ, ỹ), and the sampling density B̄. Reporting confidence intervals for expected delay and test counts would help.
- Generality vs. instantiation. The empirical study instantiates a two-state Markov process; although the framework is more general, it would be valuable to include simulations with non-Markov or semi-Markov disease trajectories to illustrate applicability and runtime/scalability in those cases.
- Action space and computational details. The complexity bound is informative but conservative. Please report wall-clock time, number of parallel workers, typical K and |B̄[t]|, and the effect of limiting the maximum follow-up interval W on performance and runtime. An ablation on the construction of the belief set (random exploration vs. seeding with clinical policies) would also be useful.
- Choice of weight and constraints. The paper tunes w to match the benchmark’s expected delay. How sensitive are results to this tuning? Providing a Pareto frontier (expected delay vs. expected tests) with confidence bands would make trade-offs transparent and policy-relevant.
- Baselines. Beyond annual/biennial screening, consider including additional baselines: POMDP-based policies from prior OR work under their assumptions, risk/density-based interval guidelines, and the ConfidentCare approach, to contextualize gains from external information and trajectory-dependent costs.

Quality
The technical development is sound, and proofs are deferred appropriately. The approximation bound is high level (Ω depends on an unspecified constant ζ). It would help to instantiate ζ or provide empirical proxies for approximation error. The empirical pipeline is reasonable, with clear feature modeling (Gail risk), external signal modeling from literature, and MCMC estimation.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Given the heavy notation, a schematic timeline of events (external signal, arrival, test, recommendation) and a small worked example of belief updates would improve readability. Consolidating assumptions (about test errors and independence) in a single subsection would reduce ambiguity.

Originality
The integration of external, endogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent delay costs into a partially observed control framework, with a tailored PBVI belief-construction procedure, is novel relative to existing screening and controlled sensing literature.

Significance
The potential impact is high: a principled, data-driven approach to personalizing screening that can reduce cost and burden while maintaining detection timeliness. The framework can plausibly extend to multiple diseases and richer test sets.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important healthcare decision problem with a principled, general framework.
- Solid theory plus a practical, scalable approximation.
- Strong empirical evidence on a large dataset; meaningful reductions in screening burden.
- Clear advancement over existing screening models in both scope and personalization.

Arguments for rejection
- Some strong assumptions (test accuracy) are embedded in theory; robustness to realistic errors is not empirically demonstrated.
- Evaluation is model-based; limited sensitivity analysis and uncertainty quantification.
- The empirical instantiation uses a relatively simple disease model, undercutting the claimed generality.

Questions for the authors
- Can you quantify runtime, scalability, and sensitivity to K and |B̄[t]|? How do you choose these in practice?
- How does DPSCREEN perform when false negatives are present, especially for policies that delay testing?
- How is ỹ selected, and how sensitive are results to this threshold and to σ?
- Could you provide Pareto curves with confidence intervals and include additional baselines (e.g., density/risk-based intervals, ConfidentCare, prior POMDP policies)?
