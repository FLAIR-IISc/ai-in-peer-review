The paper aims to identify the optimal patient screening policy given relevant information. It models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and seeks to minimize both screening costs and delays. The authors propose a computationally efficient approximate solution. Experiments on simulated breast cancer data indicate that the method can reduce delays while maintaining the same cost compared to a trivial baseline.

Positives:
+ Personalized screening is an important research area.
+ The proposed algorithm is reasonable and well developed.
+ The results are promising.
+ The paper is clearly written.

Negatives:
- The algorithm is largely an academic exercise. It assumes that a patient’s disease model is known, which is not realistic in practice. The primary challenge in personalized screening is not deriving a policy once the model is known, but inferring the model from data.
- The methodological novelty is limited.
- The experiments only compare the proposed algorithm to annual screening. Given the reliance on the disease model, an essential empirical question is the robustness of the method to model misspecification. In other words, how sensitive is the policy to uncertainty in the disease model?

Overall, the topic is important and the proposed algorithm is reasonable. However, the methodological novelty is modest, and the paper would need to provide stronger evidence to convince readers of its practical relevance.
