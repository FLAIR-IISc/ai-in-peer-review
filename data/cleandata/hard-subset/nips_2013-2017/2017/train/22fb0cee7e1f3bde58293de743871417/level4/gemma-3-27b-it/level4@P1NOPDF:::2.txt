The paper addresses the problem of determining an optimal patient screening policy given available information. The authors model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and aim to minimize both screening costs and delays. They propose a computationally efficient approximate solution to this problem, and demonstrate through simulations using breast cancer data that their algorithm can reduce delays while maintaining comparable costs to a simple annual screening baseline.

**Positives:**

+ Personalized screening is an important and relevant research area.
+ The proposed algorithm is reasonable, well-developed, and clearly presented.
+ The results obtained are promising.
+ The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Negatives:**

- The proposed algorithm currently appears to be primarily a theoretical exercise. It relies on the strong assumption that the disease model for a given patient is known *a priori*, which is unlikely to hold in a real-world clinical setting. The core challenge in personalized screening is not designing a policy *given* a known model, but rather inferring the model from patient data.
- The methodological novelty of the work is relatively limited.
- The experimental evaluation only compares the proposed algorithm against a baseline of annual screening. Given the significant assumption regarding known disease models, a crucial experiment would assess the robustness of the proposed method to inaccuracies in those models. Specifically, how sensitive is the resulting policy to uncertainties and errors in the disease model itself?

**Overall:**

The topic is interesting and the proposed algorithm is a reasonable approach. However, the methodological novelty is not substantial, and the paper needs to more convincingly demonstrate its practical relevance. Addressing the limitations regarding model inference and robustness to model uncertainty would significantly strengthen the contribution.
