This paper presents a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The disease is modeled as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, and the method incorporates the possibility of external information, such as self-examination or discovery of comorbidities, that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment.

The authors provide a clear and concise summary of the main ideas in the paper, including the disease model, the screening policy, and the optimization problem. They also provide a thorough discussion of the related work in the field, including decision theory, operations research, and medical literature.

The proposed approach is based on a dynamic programming formulation, which is a reasonable and well-developed method for solving the optimization problem. The authors also provide a detailed description of the algorithm, including the Bellman equations, the value function, and the recommendation plan.

The experiments on simulated data related to breast cancer indicate that the proposed algorithm could reduce delays while keeping the same cost when compared to a trivial baseline. However, the methodological novelty of the paper is not large, and the experiments only compare the proposed algorithm to a baseline doing annual screening.

One of the main limitations of the paper is that it assumes that the disease model of a given patient is known, which could be never assumed in practice. The main obstacle in personalized screening is not coming up with a policy when the model is known, but inferring the model from data. Therefore, an important question that should be studied experimentally is the robustness of the proposed method to inaccuracies in the disease model.

In terms of the quality of the paper, the writing is clear and concise, and the paper is well-organized. However, the paper would need to do a better job to convince readers that this work is practically relevant. The authors should provide more discussion on how the proposed method can be used in practice, and how it can be integrated with existing healthcare systems.

Overall, the topic of personalized screening is interesting, and the proposed algorithm is reasonable. However, the paper needs to address the limitations mentioned above to increase its practical relevance and methodological novelty.
