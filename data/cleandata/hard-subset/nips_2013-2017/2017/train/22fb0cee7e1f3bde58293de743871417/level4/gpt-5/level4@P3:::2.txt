Summary
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for designing dynamic, personalized screening policies for disease detection. The disease is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state. A key feature is an external information process (e.g., self-exams, comorbidities) that may trigger early, unscheduled visits. Screening policies map patient features and full histories of observations and recommendations to the next screening time or to diagnosis. The objective balances discounted screening costs and delay costs, where delay depends on the time between disease incidence and detection and may be disease-specific (e.g., linear or convex penalties).

The authors formulate a belief-state dynamic program and derive Bellman equations over beliefs on state trajectories and diagnosis status. They show the value function is convex and piecewise linear and characterize exact optimal policies via alpha-vectors, but exact computation is intractable. They therefore introduce a PBVI-style approximation tailored to their high-dimensional belief space: sample likely disease trajectories, construct reachable beliefs under a mixture of random and clinically inspired policies, and perform point-based backups. They provide an approximation error bound that scales with a sampling-density measure and a robustness result showing local constancy of the optimal policy under small model perturbations.

Empirically, they estimate a two-state (H/D) disease model for breast cancer using Gail risk as features, literature-based sensitivity/specificity for mammography/biopsy, and a self-exam signal based on tumor growth. Policies are tuned to match the expected delay of current practice (annual screening), and DPSCREEN reduces expected numbers of screens by 28–68% (depending on risk group and whether self-exam information is used) while maintaining similar expected detection delays. They also demonstrate how the choice of delay cost (linear vs quadratic) shifts the screening–delay trade-off.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on POMDP/POSMDP methods (e.g., Smallwood & Sondik; PBVI) but departs from standard assumptions: the decision intervals are endogenous; costs depend on full trajectories; and disease/test processes may be non-Markov and non-stationary. It compares to operations research and medical simulation literatures (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Erenay et al., MISCAN-COLON), which either assume more restrictive disease/test models or evaluate fixed policy sets via simulation. Compared to ConfidentCare and risk-based interval recommendations, DPSCREEN aims to compute policies directly from a flexible generative model with formal approximation guarantees.

Strengths
- Important problem with high practical impact; explicit personalization by patient features and history.
- Clear formulation with a belief-based DP and piecewise-linear value characterization.
- PBVI adaptation to trajectory-based beliefs is sensible; error and robustness results provide some theoretical assurances.
- Empirical evaluation on a large breast cancer cohort with realistic components (risk stratification, test properties, external information).
- Demonstrates substantial reductions in screening burden at similar expected delays; explores disease-dependent delay costs.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Practical realism of modeling assumptions: The theoretical analysis at times assumes perfect tests (no false positives/negatives), while the empirical model uses noisy tests. Clarify the gap, and, if possible, extend bounds/robustness to imperfect tests.
- Model misspecification: Although Theorem 1 argues for local stability of optimal actions, empirical sensitivity analyses are missing. Please quantify robustness to errors in pin(x), ptrans(x), self-exam noise σ, tumor growth ι, and arrival thresholds. Show how policy performance and decisions change under plausible perturbations.
- Comparison baselines: The main text compares to annual screening; biennial appears only in the appendix. Include in the main results and add comparisons to risk-based interval policies (e.g., density- and risk-tailored intervals, ConfidentCare; Ayer/Maillart POMDP policies if computable under their assumptions).
- Outcome measures: Expected time-to-detection is informative but does not directly capture clinical utility (e.g., stage at detection, overdiagnosis, unnecessary biopsy rates). Consider reporting surrogate clinical outcomes and harms (false positives, biopsies, cumulative radiation) to strengthen medical relevance.
- Scalability and generality: Complexity grows with B, T, K, |Y|, |Z|. Provide empirical runtime/memory scaling, and discuss extension to richer state spaces (e.g., preclinical “risky” states, competing risks) and multiple tests with different costs.
- Clarity: Some notation is inconsistent (ptr vs ptran); a few typos remain; the role of w versus constraint-based formulations could be streamlined (e.g., describe the Lagrangian relationship and how w is tuned).

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The methodology is carefully developed; the DP and PBVI adaptation are technically sound. Approximation and robustness statements are useful, though some assumptions (perfect tests) limit direct applicability. Empirical setup uses plausible components and a large dataset; stronger sensitivity and baseline comparisons would improve rigor.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized; technical flow is clear. Minor edits to notation and a concise figure/table of assumptions would help.
- Originality: The problem formulation (trajectory-dependent costs, endogenous arrivals, external information) and the adaptation of PBVI in this setting constitute a meaningful extension of existing POMDP methods, though the computational approach is conceptually incremental.
- Significance: Potentially high. If robustness and clinical outcomes are substantiated, the approach could influence personalized screening strategies and decision support tools.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are policies and performance to misestimation of pin/ptrans, σ, and ι? Please include a thorough sensitivity analysis.
2. Can you quantify harms (false positives, biopsies) and resource utilization under DPSCREEN vs baselines?
3. How does DPSCREEN perform relative to existing risk-based interval guidelines and ConfidentCare on the same data?
4. What is the typical runtime and memory footprint for your largest experiments, and how does it scale with T and K?
5. Can your theory be extended to imperfect tests, and would the error bounds meaningfully change?

Reasons to accept
- Addresses an important, underexplored dimension of personalized screening with a principled framework.
- Solid technical formulation and adaptation of PBVI with theoretical guarantees.
- Promising empirical reductions in screening burden without increasing expected delay.

Reasons to reject
- Limited empirical robustness to model misspecification; theoretical robustness relies on strong assumptions.
- Baseline comparisons are narrow; lack of clinically oriented outcome metrics.
- Methodological novelty is moderate, largely repurposing known POMDP machinery.

Overall assessment
A thoughtful and well-executed paper on a timely problem. I lean toward acceptance if the camera-ready addresses robustness to model error, expands baselines (including risk-based/dynamic policies), and reports clinically meaningful outcomes and harms. Otherwise, the contribution risks being viewed as primarily methodological with uncertain practical impact.
