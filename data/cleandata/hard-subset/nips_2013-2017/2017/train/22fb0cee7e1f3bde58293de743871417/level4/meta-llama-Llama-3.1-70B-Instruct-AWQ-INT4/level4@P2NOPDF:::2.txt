The paper aims to determine the optimal policy for patient screening by modeling a disease as a finite state stochastic process. The proposed policy seeks to minimize screening costs and delays. The authors present an approximate solution that is computationally efficient. Experiments on simulated breast cancer data demonstrate that the proposed algorithm can reduce delays while maintaining similar costs compared to a baseline approach.

Positives:
+ Personalized screening is a crucial research topic, and this paper contributes to its development.
+ The proposed algorithm is well-reasoned and effectively developed.
+ The results are promising and indicate potential for practical application.
+ The paper is well-written and clearly communicates its ideas.

Negatives:
- The proposed algorithm relies on the assumption that the disease model for a given patient is known, which is not a realistic assumption in practice. The primary challenge in personalized screening lies in inferring the disease model from data, rather than developing a policy based on a known model.
- The methodological novelty of the paper is limited.
- The experimental comparison to a baseline annual screening approach is insufficient. A critical aspect to investigate is the robustness of the proposed method to inaccuracies in the disease model. Specifically, it is essential to examine the sensitivity of the policy to uncertainties in the disease model.

Overall, while the topic is interesting and the proposed algorithm is reasonable, the methodological novelty is limited, and the paper falls short in demonstrating its practical relevance. To strengthen the paper, the authors should address the issue of disease model uncertainty and provide more comprehensive experimental evaluations.
