- Proposes DPSCREEN, a method integrating patient-specific screening history and disease features to build personalized screening policies.  
- Personalization reduces screening frequency while maintaining timely disease detection.  
- Method is well-motivated, clearly written, and situated within related work.  
- Mathematical framework is concise, includes computational complexity discussion, and is technically sound.  
- Validated on a large real-world dataset (45,000 patients from Athena Health Network).  
- Demonstrates improved efficiency and explores multiple disease models, supporting generalizability.  
- Addresses estimation error and provides approximation error bounds (Proposition 1).  
- Novel contribution by moving beyond population-based screening to personalized policies.  
- Practical relevance shown through real-world data and efficiency gains.  
- Reliance on a two-state Markov disease model may oversimplify some diseases.  
- Computational cost and scalability to larger datasets or complex models could be problematic.  
- Modeling of external information (e.g., self-exams) depends on assumptions and population-specific parameters.  
- Limited comparisons to other personalized screening approaches weakens empirical evaluation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, novel/theoretically sound approach, strong experimental results, and practical impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: generalizability concerns from model assumptions and potential computational barriers.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — strengths outweigh weaknesses, with suggested future work to address limitations.
