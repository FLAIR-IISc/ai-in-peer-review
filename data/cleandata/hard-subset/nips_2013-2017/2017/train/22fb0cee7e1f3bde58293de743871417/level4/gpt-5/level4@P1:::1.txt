This paper presents DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing screening policies across a broad family of disease models. In contrast to prior work, DPSCREEN explicitly accounts for both patient features and the evolving clinical history (including past screening outcomes), enabling true personalization of screening schedules. The method aims to balance the frequency of testing against delays in disease detection. Empirically, it yields substantial efficiency gains over existing clinical policies without compromising timeliness.

Overall, the paper is well-written and technically sound. The problem is clearly motivated, and the authors situate their work appropriately within the literature on screening, decision theory, and POMDP-based approaches. The mathematical formulation is clearly presented, including the modeling assumptions, the Bellman-equation-based solution strategy, and the use of point-based value iteration to obtain a tractable, approximately optimal policy. The discussion of computational complexity is concise and informative.

DPSCREEN is validated on a large real-world dataset (45,000 patients) from the Athena Health Network, which is a meaningful scale for this application. The empirical evaluation convincingly demonstrates that personalization reduces the number of screens while maintaining comparable expected detection delays relative to standard clinical policies. The approach is also examined across different disease model types, underscoring its generality.

In summary, the paper makes a clear technical contribution and shows strong potential for practical impact.
