- Proposes DPSCREEN: a framework for dynamic, personalized screening with external information, endogenous intervals, and trajectory-dependent delay costs.
- Formulates a belief-state dynamic program, proves piecewise-linear convexity of the value function, and uses point-based approximation (PBVI) with sampled trajectories.
- Provides an approximation-error bound and a local robustness result to small model perturbations.
- Empirical case study on breast cancer using observational data shows large screening reductions with similar expected detection delay to annual screening.
- Extends prior POMDP screening work by allowing a general finite-state disease model, explicit external information, and trajectory-dependent delay costs.
- Builds on known PBVI techniques; methodological novelty is moderate as core algorithmic ideas are established.
- Strength: tackles an important, timely problem and unifies endogenous arrivals, external information, and delay-cost modeling.
- Strength: approximation approach is well-developed and theoretically supported.
- Strength: promising empirical reductions in screening volume at comparable delay.
- Weakness: strong assumption of a known, correctly specified disease and observation model; limited sensitivity to misspecification in experiments.
- Weakness: experimental evaluation is narrow—mainly annual screening comparison, lacking head-to-head POMDP or heuristic baselines and stress tests on external info/test accuracy.
- Weakness: some theoretical assumptions (e.g., perfect tests, bounded inter-screen intervals) are idealized for clinical settings.
- Quality: technical formulation and derivations are sound, but empirical validation relies on observational models without counterfactuals and lacks rigorous robustness checks.
- Clarity: paper is generally well written though notation is dense; suggest moving key proof sketches and estimation diagnostics into main text.
- Originality: moderate—meaningful extension of known ideas to a richer setting but not a major methodological advance.
- Significance: potentially high if policies are robust and validated, but current results do not yet establish practical readiness.
- Suggestion: perform systematic sensitivity analyses to model misspecification, test accuracy, and external-information noise.
- Suggestion: compare against established POMDP screening methods and strong clinical decision-support baselines.
- Suggestion: report out-of-sample performance, fairness across subgroups, and consider constrained optimization formulations.
- Suggestion: relax idealized theoretical assumptions (e.g., imperfect tests) and clarify computational deployment details.
- Pro for acceptance: addresses an important problem with a coherent, theoretically-backed approximation and promising empirical gains.
- Con for acceptance: limited methodological novelty, insufficient experimental validation, and unclear practical relevance without robustness and broader comparisons.
- Overall: interesting and reasonable approach with modest methodological advance; stronger empirical robustness and comparisons needed for practical impact.
