This paper presents DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing dynamic and personalized screening policies. The authors model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process, where the objective is to find a policy that optimally balances the cost of screening against the delay in detecting an absorbing disease state. A key feature of the model is its incorporation of an external information process (e.g., patient self-examination), which can trigger unscheduled clinical visits. Recognizing that solving for the exactly optimal policy is computationally intractable, the paper proposes a tractable approximation based on point-based value iteration (PBVI) on a sampled set of belief states. This work generalizes prior POMDP-based approaches to screening (e.g., Ayer et al., 2017; Erenay et al., 2012) by accommodating a broader class of non-Markovian disease models and trajectory-dependent costs. The framework's effectiveness is demonstrated using a disease model learned from a large-scale breast cancer dataset, showing significant reductions in screening frequency compared to standard clinical guidelines for a similar level of detection delay.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Relevance:** The paper addresses the important and clinically relevant problem of personalized screening. Developing policies that can adapt to individual patient features and history has the potential to improve patient outcomes and optimize the use of healthcare resources.
*   **Technical Quality:** The problem is rigorously formulated within a general decision-theoretic framework. The proposed solution, which adapts PBVI to handle challenges like trajectory-dependent costs and unscheduled arrivals, is technically sound and well-motivated. The paper also provides theoretical bounds on the approximation error.
*   **Promising Experimental Results:** The empirical evaluation, based on a model derived from a large, real-world breast cancer dataset, is compelling. The demonstration that the proposed policy can reduce the number of screenings by 28-68% while maintaining the same expected detection delay as annual screening is a significant result.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. It effectively explains the complex model, the limitations of existing methods, and the details of the proposed solution.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Practicality and Model Dependence:** The primary weakness is the strong assumption that an accurate disease progression model is known or can be reliably learned from data. While the authors follow a standard approach of learning a model and then optimizing a policy for it, the practical utility of the entire framework is highly sensitive to the fidelity of this model. The main hurdle in personalized medicine is often the robust inference of such models from complex, noisy, and potentially biased observational data, a challenge that is largely abstracted away in this work.
*   **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core solution method is an application and adaptation of the well-established PBVI algorithm. While the adaptation to this specific problem with its unique cost structure and state transitions is non-trivial, the work does not introduce a fundamentally new algorithmic technique. The main contribution lies in the formulation and application rather than in novel methodology.
*   **Lack of Experimental Robustness Analysis:** The paper provides a theoretical result concerning robustness to model estimation errors (Theorem 1), but this is not validated empirically. A crucial question is how sensitive the resulting policy is to inaccuracies in the learned model parameters. The experimental evaluation would be substantially more convincing if it included a sensitivity analysis, for instance, by optimizing the policy on a model with perturbed parameters and evaluating its performance on the original model. This would provide a more practical assessment of the method's robustness.

In summary, this paper presents a solid and well-executed study on an important problem. The framework is general, and the results are promising. However, the work would be significantly strengthened by a more direct engagement with the challenge of model uncertainty, both in the discussion and through a more comprehensive experimental evaluation that assesses the policy's robustness to model misspecification.
