- Proposal: DPSCREEN — a framework to construct personalized screening policies trading off screening frequency vs detection delay using a finite-state stochastic disease model with absorbing disease state and external info.
- Method: Uses point-based value iteration with theoretical approximation error bounds and robustness guarantees.
- Empirical result: Validated on a large breast cancer dataset showing 28–68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining equivalent expected detection delays.
- Literature positioning: Extends prior POMDP-based work by supporting non-Markov disease processes and non-stationary test outcomes; contrasts with simulation-only medical studies.
- Technical soundness: Proves value function is convex and piecewise linear, provides approximation/error bounds, and analyzes robustness to estimation error.
- Computational tractability: Provides a theoretically grounded approximation approach and acknowledges exact solution intractability.
- Practical significance: Demonstrates meaningful potential to reduce healthcare burden and shows how different cost functions (linear vs quadratic) affect policies.
- Originality: Generalizes to a wider class of disease models and incorporates external information (e.g., self-exams) in decision-making.
- Experimental limitation: Lacks direct comparisons to other state-of-the-art personalized screening methods and no head-to-head baseline on the same dataset (e.g., Ayer et al.).
- Scalability/implementation gap: Claims parallelizability and reports abstract complexity, but provides little practical speedup data or concrete scaling guidance for high-dimensional problems.
- Generalizability concern: Demonstrated only on breast cancer; no empirical results on other disease types (e.g., pancreatic cancer) to show broader applicability.
- Visualization and clarity: Effectively illustrates personalization (Figure 1) and carefully treats estimation error (Section 3.3).
- Modeling advance: Handles non-stationary test outcomes and external information processes beyond prior approaches.
- Ethical and dataset transparency gaps: Does not discuss ethical implications of personalized screening or dataset representativeness and potential biases.
- Statistical reporting gap: Improvements shown vs clinical policies lack statistical significance testing.
- Recommendation: Strong accept with minor revisions to include broader method comparisons and more implementation/scalability details.
- Acceptance arguments: Novel, theoretically grounded, strong empirical gains, demonstrates personalization and robustness.
- Rejection concerns: Limited comparative baselines, incomplete implementation details, and limited demonstration beyond one disease.
