- Paper proposes DPSCREEN: dynamic, personalized screening policies balancing screening frequency vs detection delay using a finite-state stochastic disease model with external signals triggering visits.
- Objective formulated as a weighted sum of discounted screening cost and trajectory-dependent delay cost rather than state-only cost.
- Belief-state dynamic program yields a piecewise-linear, convex value function; exact alpha-vector recursion derived but intractable.
- Develop a tractable point-based approximation that samples typical state trajectories and reachable beliefs (PBVI-style).
- Provide complexity bound, an approximation-error bound in terms of belief-space coverage, and a robustness result that optimal actions are locally constant almost everywhere.
- Empirical illustration on Athena breast cancer data shows 28–68% reductions in expected screening counts versus annual/biennial policies at similar expected detection delays.
- Relates to prior POMDP-based screening, Bayesian design, and controlled sensing work, claiming broader scope than prior OR screening models.
- Technical soundness: belief-state reduction and piecewise-linear convexity are natural; exact construction standard and approximation reasonable given high-dimensional beliefs.
- Error bound is qualitative and depends on an unspecified constant ζ and strong assumptions (no false positives/negatives, bounded inter-screening, convex delay cost, large T).
- Robustness theorem is plausible but nonconstructive; key regularity conditions and proof details need clarification.
- Modeling: allows general disease processes, but empirical work uses a two-state Markov model, narrowing demonstrated generality.
- Contradictory assumptions: core model assumes no false positives (biopsy-confirmed Z+), while approximation error assumes both no false positives and no false negatives—unrealistic for mammography/biopsy.
- External-information arrivals modeled via fixed threshold ỹ; optimizing this threshold within the policy is not considered but could improve performance.
- Evaluation is simulation-based using learned disease model, but lacks treatment of estimation uncertainty, censoring/verification bias, missingness, and cohort shift.
- No confidence intervals or sensitivity analyses reported; tuning of weight w to match benchmarks may mask trade-offs.
- Recommend presenting full Pareto frontiers (screens vs delay) and sensitivity analyses to model misspecification and external-information parameters.
- Clarity is generally good, but Z+ should be precisely defined and assumptions reconciled across sections.
- More guidance needed on constructing belief-support sets (trajectory sampling K, policy exploration, pruning/dominance heuristics).
- Detail runtime and scalability: report wall-clock times, cores used, and sensitivity to T, |Y|, |Z|, K, and belief set size.
- Make approximation bound more interpretable by bounding ζ and reporting achieved belief-coverage Ω(B̄) in experiments.
- Originality: combining trajectory-aware screening, external-information-driven arrivals, and PBVI-style approximations is novel in screening literature.
- Significance: potentially impactful reductions in tests for similar delays, but clinical significance limited by strong assumptions and restricted evaluation.
- Strengths: clear motivation; flexible modeling; appropriate use of belief-state DP and PBVI; incorporation of external signals; empirical demonstration on sizable dataset.
- Weaknesses: qualitative error bounds with unrealistic assumptions, limited computational detail, lack of uncertainty quantification, overclaimed generality, and exogenous threshold/weight settings.
- Suggested improvements: provide Pareto curves and CIs via posterior sampling, relax perfect-test assumptions in theory, report implementation and runtime details, explore optimizing external-information thresholds, and validate on additional cohorts.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, well-motivated formulation; sound methodological adaptation of PBVI; promising empirical gains; relevance to decision-making under uncertainty and healthcare.
- Arguments against acceptance: theoretical guarantees rely on unrealistic assumptions and lack quantitative constants; insufficient robustness/uncertainty quantification; unclear scalability and deployment details; empirical claims of generality not fully supported.
