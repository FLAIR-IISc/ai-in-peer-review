This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies for diseases modeled as finite-state stochastic processes. The core idea is to balance the delay in disease detection against the frequency of screenings, accounting for patient features, screening history, and external information. The authors address the computational intractability of finding the optimal policy by proposing an approximately optimal construction, validated on a large breast cancer dataset. They demonstrate significant reductions in screening frequency (28-68%) while maintaining comparable detection delays compared to existing clinical policies.

This work builds upon a considerable body of research in screening optimization (e.g., [7, 9, 10]) and decision theory [18-21]. However, it distinguishes itself by tackling several limitations of prior approaches. Previous work often restricts itself to Markovian disease processes and stationary test outcome models, which don't hold for many real-world scenarios. DPSCREEN relaxes these assumptions, allowing for more general disease models and incorporating external information, such as patient self-examinations.  The incorporation of a dynamic, patient-specific risk assessment throughout the screening process is also a significant contribution. Related areas include controlled sensing [34, 38] which shares the idea of optimizing information acquisition, but typically focuses on different problem structures.  The authors appropriately position their work within the broader context of personalized medicine [31].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The framework provides a general approach to personalized screening policies applicable to a wide range of diseases and model complexities. The ability to handle non-Markovian processes and external information is a key strength.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a computationally tractable approximation with a bounded error (Proposition 1), giving confidence in the quality of the proposed policy.
* **Empirical Validation:** The application to a large breast cancer dataset demonstrates significant improvements over existing clinical policies, providing strong evidence of the practical value of the approach. The personalization aspects, as illustrated in Figure 1, are clearly demonstrated.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The model and problem formulation are well-defined, and the key concepts are explained with sufficient detail. The mathematical notation is largely consistent and understandable.
* **Robustness Analysis:** The discussion of estimation error and its impact on policy performance (Theorem 1) is valuable and realistic.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a strong awareness of the relevant literature, placing their work in context and highlighting its contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors acknowledge the computational cost, the O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|) complexity remains a concern for very large-scale problems or more complex models.  More detailed discussion on practical optimizations and scalability would be beneficial.
* **Model Assumptions:** The finite-state assumption, while simplifying the problem, may not be appropriate for all diseases.  The impact of violating this assumption deserves further consideration. The independence assumptions regarding test results and external information, while standard, might be too strong in some contexts.
* **Parameter Estimation:** The method relies on accurate estimation of model parameters. While the authors describe their approach for breast cancer, it is unclear how easily this approach could be applied to other diseases with less readily available data.  A more detailed discussion of parameter sensitivity and potential biases would be useful.
* **Cost Function:** The choice of the delay cost function (linear vs. quadratic) can significantly impact the policy. While explored in Figure 2, a more systematic analysis of the sensitivity of the policy to different cost functions would be helpful.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison is primarily focused on annual vs. biennial screening. Comparing with other, more sophisticated personalized policies (if they exist) would strengthen the evaluation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and challenging problem in healthcare – personalized screening.
* The proposed framework is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated.
* The results demonstrate a clear potential for improving healthcare outcomes and reducing costs.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity could limit the applicability of the method to large-scale problems.
* The model assumptions may not hold for all diseases.
* Some aspects of the parameter estimation and cost function sensitivity require further investigation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of personalized screening. The framework is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance to NIPS 2017, particularly given its potential impact on healthcare. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the concerns raised above, specifically regarding computational complexity and model assumptions.
