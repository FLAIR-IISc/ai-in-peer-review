Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper formulates personalized, history-dependent screening as a sequential decision problem with endogenous arrival times and trajectory-dependent delay costs. Disease evolution is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing “disease” state; tests with discrete outcomes are made only at clinician visits; and an exogenous “external information” process (e.g., self-exams, comorbidities) can trigger early, unscheduled visits. The objective balances discounted screening costs and delay-to-detection costs. The authors show the value function is convex and piecewise-linear in beliefs (as in POMDPs) despite nonstandard features (endogenous decision epochs and trajectory-dependent costs), derive an intractable exact dynamic program via alpha-vectors, and propose DPSCREEN, a point-based approximation that samples typical physiological trajectories and reachable beliefs. They provide a sampling-density-based bound on approximation error under strong assumptions and a robustness result showing local constancy of optimal actions under small model perturbations. Experiments on a breast cancer cohort (45k patients) use a simple two-state disease model with a self-exam signal to illustrate that DPSCREEN reduces expected screenings by 28–68% while holding expected delay comparable to annual/biennial policies.

This work is connected to OR-based screening POMDPs (Ayer et al. 2012; Maillart et al. 2008; Erenay et al. 2011) and Bayesian/joint survival modeling for screening (Rizopoulos 2014). It relies on point-based POMDP approximations (Pineau et al. 2003) and relates to POSMDPs (Yu 2007) and controlled sensing (Krishnamurthy). It differs by allowing external information to drive endogenous arrivals and by explicitly treating delay costs that depend on incidence and detection times rather than only on current state. It would benefit from discussion of modern point-based solvers such as Perseus (Spaan & Vlassis, JAIR 2005), HSVI (Smith & Simmons, UAI 2004/JAIR 2012), and SARSOP (Kurniawati et al., IJRR 2008), and Monte Carlo POMDPs (POMCP; Silver & Veness, NIPS 2010).

Quality (soundness and evaluation):
Strengths
- A clear and general formulation that unifies personalized risk, dynamic history, and exogenous early-arrival signals—elements often omitted in previous screening models.
- Proof that the value function remains piecewise-linear and convex in beliefs is nontrivial given endogenous decision epochs and trajectory-dependent costs.
- A practical point-based approximation tailored to the very high-dimensional belief space (over state trajectories), with constructive heuristics for sampling likely trajectories and reachable beliefs.
- Simple error bound linking performance loss to belief coverage (though under strong assumptions) and a robustness argument showing local constancy of optimal actions with respect to model estimation error.

Weaknesses
- The approximation error bound (Proposition 1) relies on restrictive assumptions (no false positives/negatives; convex delay cost; bounded inter-screening interval; large horizon) and does not provide computable constants or data-driven stopping criteria. In the empirical section, tests are noisy with known sensitivity/specificity, contradicting the no-FP/FN assumptions used in the theory; the implications for guarantees are not discussed.
- The empirical evaluation learns a very simple two-state Markov disease model and adopts a stylized self-exam signal; key parameters (e.g., lump threshold) are sourced from literature rather than jointly estimated/validated on the same population. There is no external validation, goodness-of-fit assessment, or sensitivity analysis to model misspecification beyond the qualitative robustness theorem.
- Baselines are limited to annual/biennial screening. Comparisons to optimized screening frameworks (e.g., Ayer 2012; Maillart 2008), Bayesian-design approaches (Rizopoulos 2014), or modern POMDP solvers applied to the same model (e.g., SARSOP/HSVI) are missing, making it hard to attribute gains to the proposed belief-sampling strategy rather than to matching the constraint via weight tuning.
- The objective ignores harms from false positives/biopsies and overdiagnosis; screening cost is unitary regardless of downstream procedures. Because policy aggressiveness is tuned by w to match delay, a more comprehensive cost model would strengthen conclusions.
- Computational aspects are only partially addressed. Complexity is high (quadratic in B and exponential in T in worst case), and practical runtimes, scalability with |Z|, |Y|, and K, and parallelization details are not reported.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The modeling assumptions and DP are carefully laid out, and the personalization examples are helpful. However:
- There is an important mismatch between theoretical assumptions (no FP/FN) and the experimental setting (noisy tests); this should be reconciled, with guarantees or at least ablations under realistic noise.
- Core algorithmic details (belief construction, backups) are deferred to the appendix; a concise pseudo-code and a small schematic of the graphical model in the main text would help reproducibility.
- Notation for beliefs over trajectory space ST × {0,1} is heavy; discussing memory/representation (e.g., factorization or sufficient statistics) would improve clarity.

Originality:
Formulating screening with endogenous arrival due to external information and trajectory-dependent delay costs within a POMDP-like framework, and showing piecewise-linear value functions in this setting, is novel and interesting. The approximation is a pragmatic variant of PBVI; the belief-space construction via trajectory sampling is reasonable but conceptually close to existing point-based methods. Overall, originality is moderate to good on the problem formulation and theoretical framing, modest on the approximation machinery.

Significance:
If validated, the approach could influence personalized screening policy design across diseases, a problem of societal importance. The experimental gains are promising but rely on a simple learned model and limited baselines. Strengthening empirical validation and comparisons could elevate impact. The theoretical results help justify approximate dynamic programming in this nonstandard setting, which is valuable beyond the specific application.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, underexplored setting (endogenous visits + trajectory-dependent costs) with a principled DP framework.
- Provides theory (PWL value function, robustness; albeit with caveats) and a practical algorithm.
- Meaningful personalization behaviors demonstrated; promising reductions in screening load at fixed expected delay.

Cons for acceptance:
- Theoretical guarantees hinge on unrealistic assumptions relative to experiments; mismatch is not analyzed.
- Limited empirical baselines and sensitivity analyses; model calibration/validation is weak.
- Approximation method is close to known point-based POMDP techniques; missing comparisons to state-of-the-art solvers.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Reconcile theoretical assumptions with practice: extend guarantees to noisy tests or report ablations quantifying performance under realistic FP/FN rates.
- Include comparisons to SARSOP/HSVI/Perseus and to prior optimized screening policies on the same model.
- Provide calibration/validation of the disease and self-exam models (goodness-of-fit, posterior predictive checks) and sensitivity analyses (e.g., to delay cost shape, noise levels, weight w).
- Report computational performance and scalability; release code if possible.
- Consider richer cost models incorporating downstream harms and overdiagnosis.

Overall evaluation:
A solid and timely contribution with a compelling formulation and promising early results, but with gaps in empirical validation and alignment of theory and practice. I lean toward weak accept if the authors can address baseline comparisons and clarify the impact of noise and modeling assumptions; otherwise borderline.
