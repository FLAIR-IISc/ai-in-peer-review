- Novel, ambitious framework (DPSCREEN) for personalized dynamic screening policies addressing limits of static schedules and simplified disease models.
- Core idea: model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and optimize screening using patient features, history, and external data.
- Significant methodological contribution: extends POMDP-like frameworks to include dynamic disease models, external info, and computationally efficient approximations.
- Provides approximation construction with bounds and robustness analysis showing convergence under reasonable assumptions.
- Practical relevance strengthened by application to breast cancer using a large dataset.
- Experiments show personalization based on risk and test results and substantial screening reductions versus clinical policies while maintaining delay.
- Framework adaptable to different delay cost functions (e.g., linear vs. quadratic).
- Related work section is thorough and situates the paper well within screening, personalized medicine, and controlled sensing literature.
- Experimental validation is limited; broader evaluation across diseases/datasets is needed to support generalizability.
- Parameter estimation details are insufficient; sensitivity of policies to estimation errors is not analyzed.
- The assumption of no false positives in tests is optimistic and should be relaxed or justified.
- Computational complexity discussion lacks concrete runtime and scalability figures (states, population size), hindering practicality assessment.
- Claim of personalization would benefit from qualitative examples illustrating policy adaptations for specific patient scenarios.
- Choice of belief space construction (Algorithm 1) needs more intuition and clarification of how chosen trajectories affect approximation error.
- Connection to modern reinforcement learning is underexplored; casting as a partially observable RL problem could enable alternative algorithms.
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, technical soundness, significance for healthcare, and overall clarity.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited experimental validation, unclear computational practicality, and insufficient parameter sensitivity analysis.
- Overall recommendation: accept pending revisions addressing experimental breadth, runtime/scalability evidence, and parameter sensitivity.
