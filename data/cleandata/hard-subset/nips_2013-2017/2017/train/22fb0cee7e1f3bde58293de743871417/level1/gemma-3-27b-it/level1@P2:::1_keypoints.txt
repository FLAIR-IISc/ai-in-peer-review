- Presents DPSCREEN, a novel framework modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process for personalized screening.
- Relaxes strong Markovian assumptions and incorporates external information, broadening applicability beyond existing approaches.
- Connects to decision theory, POMDPs, controlled sensing, and survival analysis, situating the work within related literature.
- Provides an approximately optimal policy with a bounded approximation error (Proposition 1), a notable theoretical contribution.
- Applies the framework to a large real-world breast cancer dataset, demonstrating feasibility.
- Shows personalization based on patient features and screening history, reducing number of screenings without increasing detection delay.
- Discusses estimation error and its impact on policy performance, offering robustness analysis.
- Overall presentation and motivation are clear and logically structured, though dense in places.
- Computational complexity remains high (O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)), potentially limiting scalability to complex models or larger datasets.
- Lacks detailed runtime/performance reporting for the breast cancer experiments.
- Relies on assumptions (e.g., independence of test results given state trajectory, accurate tests) whose impact on robustness is not fully investigated.
- Mathematical formulation is dense and some notations (~s, B̄) and Bellman equation explanations are unclear or hard to follow.
- Limited empirical comparison to other state-of-the-art personalized screening methods; comparisons to clinical policies are useful but not comprehensive.
- Insufficient discussion on generalizability to other diseases and how framework modifications might be required.
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important problem with a novel, theoretically sound approach and strong empirical proof-of-concept.
- Arguments against acceptance: high computational cost, reliance on simplifying assumptions, and clarity/presentation issues.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to improve mathematical clarity, provide computational/runtime details, expand generalizability discussion, and include sensitivity analyses.
