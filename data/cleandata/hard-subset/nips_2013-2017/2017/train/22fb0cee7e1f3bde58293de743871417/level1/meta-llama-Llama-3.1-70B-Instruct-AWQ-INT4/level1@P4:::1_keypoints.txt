- Proposes DPSCREEN: a method for personalized screening that trades off screening cost against delay in disease detection.  
- Models disease as a general finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and allows external information (self-exam, comorbidities) to trigger visits.  
- Main technical contribution: a computationally tractable Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) approximation to construct approximately optimal screening policies.  
- PBVI approach selects finite belief vectors and builds alpha vectors to handle high-dimensional belief spaces.  
- Claims computational effectiveness in producing near-optimal policies when exact optimization is intractable.  
- Analyzes robustness to parameter estimation error and reports minimal impact on the exactly optimal policy.  
- Empirical validation on a large breast cancer dataset: achieves similar expected detection delay as benchmark while substantially reducing expected number of tests.  
- Strength: novel, general methodology applicable to multiple diseases and a comprehensive screening/disease progression framework.  
- Strength: incorporation of external information and practical evaluation on real data.  
- Weakness: assumes model parameters are known; real-world parameter uncertainty may be larger than addressed.  
- Weakness: PBVI approximation may not perform well in all settings; more algorithmic evaluation and alternatives needed.  
- Weakness: empirical focus on breast cancer limits demonstrated generality; additional disease studies required.  
- Pro-accept: novel, comprehensive framework with promising empirical reduction in tests for comparable delays.  
- Con-accept: reliance on known parameters, limited evaluation of PBVI’s broader effectiveness, and narrow disease evaluation scope.
