

# Peer Review: DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening frequency against delay in disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating external information (e.g., patient self-examinations) that can trigger earlier visits to clinicians. The problem is formulated as a POMDP with a non-standard cost structure that depends on the entire disease trajectory rather than just the current state. The authors provide a computationally tractable approximation algorithm using point-based value iteration tailored to their high-dimensional belief space, with theoretical guarantees on approximation error. Demonstrated on a breast cancer dataset, their policy achieves 28-68% reduction in screenings while maintaining equivalent expected detection delays compared to current clinical practices.

## Quality

The paper makes a technically sound contribution with a well-motivated mathematical formulation. The authors correctly extend standard POMDP theory to their setting (Lemma 1), and Proposition 1 provides a meaningful bound on approximation error. The experimental design is appropriate, using a real-world breast cancer dataset with a clear comparison to current clinical policies. However, the assumption of no false positives in tests (a strong limitation in real-world mammography) weakens the practical applicability. The model also simplifies disease progression to a two-state process (Healthy/Disease), which may not adequately capture intermediate risk states that exist in many diseases (e.g., benign breast lumps that may become malignant). The computational complexity analysis appears optimistic without empirical evidence of scalability for larger datasets.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem formulation is precise and logically developed, with clear definitions of all mathematical components. The supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The graphical representation of personalization (Figure 1) effectively illustrates the key contribution. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from previous work in the related work section, making it easy to understand the novelty. The writing is accessible to both machine learning and healthcare researchers.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant contribution by generalizing previous screening frameworks that restricted to Markov disease processes and stationary test outcome models (e.g., Ayer et al. 2014, Maillart et al. 2013). The incorporation of external information as a trigger for earlier visits (e.g., breast lumps detected through self-examination) is novel in this context. While the use of POMDPs for screening is not entirely new, DPSCREEN's generalization to non-Markovian disease processes and its tailored computational approach represent meaningful advances. The framework could be applied to multiple diseases beyond breast cancer, as the authors demonstrate with a synthetic exercise comparing linear vs. quadratic delay costs.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. The demonstrated 28-68% reduction in screenings while maintaining equivalent detection delays represents a meaningful practical improvement over current clinical practices. The framework addresses an important gap in personalized healthcare, where screening policies should account for individual risk factors and dynamic changes in patient condition. The authors provide a compelling case for why personalized screening is necessary, citing the varying benefits of early detection across different diseases. The work could lead to more efficient healthcare resource allocation and reduced patient burden, with potential applications across multiple disease domains.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Novel general framework for personalized screening that accommodates complex disease models
- Strong theoretical guarantees on approximation error
- Compelling empirical results on a real-world dataset
- Clear exposition and well-organized presentation
- Practical demonstration of how policy adapts to patient risk and history

**Weaknesses:**
- Strong assumption of no false positives in test results
- Overly simplified two-state disease model (ignores intermediate risk states)
- Limited discussion of how the framework handles multiple concurrent diseases/comorbidities
- Computational complexity analysis lacks empirical validation
- Comparison with clinical policies limited to annual vs. biennial screening

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a general framework for personalized screening that addresses key limitations of previous work. The combination of theoretical rigor and practical demonstration on a real healthcare dataset is particularly compelling. While the assumptions about test accuracy and disease progression are somewhat limiting, the core framework is robust and could be extended to more complex scenarios. The work has clear potential for impact in healthcare applications, making it highly relevant to NIPS.

The paper meets all four criteria for acceptance: it is technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), presents a meaningful generalization of previous work (Originality), and addresses a practically important problem with significant potential impact (Significance). The minor weaknesses noted do not detract from the overall contribution.

**Recommendation: Accept**
