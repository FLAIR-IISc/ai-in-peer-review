This paper presents a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The disease is modeled as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state. The authors incorporate the possibility of external information, such as self-examination or discovery of co-morbidities, that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment.

**Summary of Main Ideas**

The paper proposes a framework for constructing personalized screening policies using dynamic programming. The disease model is a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state. The authors use a point-based value iteration algorithm to compute an approximately optimal policy. The policy is personalized to the features of the patient and to the dynamic history of the patient. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their policy using a large breast cancer data set.

**Strengths**

1. **Personalization**: The proposed policy is personalized to the features of the patient and to the dynamic history of the patient, which is a significant improvement over existing clinical policies.
2. **Flexibility**: The framework can be applied to many disease models, including dynamic survival models, first hitting time models, and Semi-Markov processes.
3. **Robustness**: The authors provide bounds for the approximation error and show that the policy is robust to estimation errors.
4. **Effectiveness**: The proposed policy reduces the number of screenings performed while maintaining the same delay in detection of disease, as demonstrated using a large breast cancer data set.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Computational Complexity**: The worst-case computation of the policy requires O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|) steps, which can be computationally expensive for large problem instances.
2. **Assumptions**: The authors make several assumptions, such as the accuracy of the test outcome and the bounded maximum screening interval, which may not hold in practice.
3. **Limited Comparison**: The authors only compare their policy with existing clinical policies and do not provide a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods.
