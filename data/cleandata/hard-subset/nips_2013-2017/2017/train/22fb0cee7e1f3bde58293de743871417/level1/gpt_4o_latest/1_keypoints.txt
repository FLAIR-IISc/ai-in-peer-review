- Introduces a novel, general framework for personalized screening policies using finite-state stochastic disease models and external information.  
- Models disease progression with an absorbing state and incorporates arrival-influencing external factors (e.g., self-exams, comorbidities).  
- Uses point-based value iteration (PBVI) to compute approximately optimal policies, addressing computational intractability.  
- Provides theoretical rigor: formal formulation, Bellman equations, bounded approximation error, and robustness discussion.  
- Validated on a large breast cancer dataset, showing 28–68% reduction in screenings while keeping detection delays comparable to clinical policies.  
- Personalizes screening schedules based on patient features and dynamic history, filling a gap in current practice.  
- Compares favorably to existing clinical policies and adapts to different diseases and cost structures.  
- Paper is dense and may be hard to follow for readers unfamiliar with POMDPs or stochastic-process terminology.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited primarily to breast cancer, leaving generalizability to other diseases untested.  
- Assumes perfect test accuracy (no false positives/negatives), which may not hold in real-world settings and could affect results.  
- PBVI reduces computation but scalability concerns remain for high-dimensional state spaces or long horizons; further optimization discussion needed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, offers rigorous methodology with guarantees, and demonstrates clear practical benefits.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited clarity/accessibility, narrow evaluation scope, and potentially unrealistic assumptions about test accuracy.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to improve clarity and expand discussion/experiments addressing limitations.
