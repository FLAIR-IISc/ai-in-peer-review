**Summary**

The paper "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening" proposes a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The disease is modeled as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, and the framework incorporates the possibility of external information, such as self-examination or discovery of co-morbidities, that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their policy using a large breast cancer dataset and show that it reduces the number of screenings performed while maintaining the same delay in detection of disease.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach**: The paper proposes a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balances the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease.
2. **Flexibility**: The framework can be applied to various diseases and can incorporate different types of external information.
3. **Personalization**: The policy is personalized to the features of the patient and to the history of the patient, including the history of screening.
4. **Effectiveness**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their policy using a large breast cancer dataset.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Computational complexity**: The algorithm to compute the sets of alpha vectors is computationally intractable, and the authors propose an approximate algorithm to compute an approximately optimal policy.
2. **Assumptions**: The authors make several assumptions, such as the cost of delay being a convex function of the delay time, and the test outcome being accurate.
3. **Limited comparison**: The authors only compare their policy with current clinical policies and do not compare it with other existing frameworks for personalized screening.
