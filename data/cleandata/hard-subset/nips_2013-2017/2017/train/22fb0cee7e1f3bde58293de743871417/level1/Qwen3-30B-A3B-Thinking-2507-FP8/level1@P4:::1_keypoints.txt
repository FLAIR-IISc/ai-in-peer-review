- Proposes a novel personalized screening framework balancing screening cost vs. detection delay using a finite-state stochastic disease model with external information triggers.  
- Extends POMDP theory to handle non-stationary disease progression, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and external information processes.  
- Provides a computationally tractable method to construct approximately optimal screening policies with a theoretical approximation bound (Proposition 1).  
- Framework is generalizable to various disease models (dynamic survival, first-hitting-time), overcoming limitations of prior Markov/stationary models.  
- Empirical results show 28–68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining the same expected detection delay; breast cancer case study demonstrates practical utility.  
- Technical soundness: clear mathematical model, consistent notation, and theoretical justification for the approximation.  
- Novelty: uniquely combines personalization, dynamic external information, and general disease modeling.  
- Significance: compelling empirical gains suggest meaningful clinical impact.  
- Clarity and reproducibility: well-organized writing, consistent notation, and adequate supplementary materials; effective illustrative example (Figure 1).  
- Scalability concerns: limited discussion on scaling to very large datasets or real-time clinical implementation.  
- Restrictive model assumptions: assumes no false positives and specific progression models; limited analysis of robustness to assumption violations.  
- Limited comparative evaluation: comparison only to annual vs. biennial screening; lacks comparisons to other risk-stratified clinical protocols.  
- Implementation/ethical issues: does not address practical or ethical barriers to clinical adoption.  
- Arguments for acceptance: fills a significant gap with a novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically effective framework that is broadly applicable.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unresolved scalability, potentially limiting model assumptions, and insufficiently comprehensive clinical comparisons.  
- Overall assessment: significant, well-executed contribution with strong theoretical and empirical support; recommended for acceptance despite noted limitations.
