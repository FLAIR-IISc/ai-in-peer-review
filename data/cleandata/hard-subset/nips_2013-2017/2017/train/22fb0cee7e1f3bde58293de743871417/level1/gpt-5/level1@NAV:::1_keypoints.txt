- Introduces DPSCREEN: a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that tailor screening frequency to patient features and evolving histories and allow unscheduled arrivals from external signals.  
- Models disease as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and delay costs that depend on the full disease trajectory.  
- Formulates a belief-state dynamic program, proves the value function is convex and piecewise-linear, and argues exact alpha-vector computation is intractable.  
- Proposes a PBVI-style approximation that samples likely disease trajectories and reachable beliefs to approximate the value function.  
- Derives an approximation-error bound tied to belief-sampling density and proves a robustness result showing local constancy of optimal actions under small model-estimation error.  
- Breast cancer case study (age 60–65) uses Gail risk features, a two-state Markov disease model, and a simple lump-growth external signal.  
- Learned policy reduces expected screens by 28–68% at similar expected detection delays compared to annual/biennial guidelines in simulation.  
- Situates work relative to prior POMDP-based screening, Bayesian joint models, PBVI and controlled sensing literature, noting complementary NeurIPS work.  
- Quality: modeling and DP formulation are sound and PBVI adaptation is reasonable.  
- Concern: theoretical performance bound relies on strong assumptions (e.g., no false positives/negatives, bounded inter-screen intervals) that are relaxed in experiments; implication of this mismatch needs clarification.  
- Evaluation is model-based using parameters learned from observational data; stronger sensitivity analyses, calibration, and held-out predictive checks are needed.  
- Clarity issues: key algorithmic details in supplement; need definition and practical magnitude of ζ, runtimes, values of K and |B̄|, how w is tuned, and whether ỹ is learned.  
- Originality: high—novel combination of external arrivals and trajectory-based costs in a general POMDP with tractable approximation.  
- Significance: potentially impactful for clinical decision support, but would be strengthened by prospective or off-policy evaluation.  
- Pros: general principled formulation; PBVI adaptation with approximation and robustness results; compelling simulated gains and clear personalization.  
- Cons: mismatch between theoretical assumptions and experimental setting; limited validation beyond model-based simulation; “first” claim should be tempered; scalability, code, and data availability not discussed.
