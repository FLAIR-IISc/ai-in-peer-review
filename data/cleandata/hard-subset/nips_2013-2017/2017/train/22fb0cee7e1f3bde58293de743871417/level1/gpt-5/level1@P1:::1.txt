Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off test frequency against detection delay across a broad class of disease models. The disease process is modeled as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; screening decisions depend on a belief over state trajectories and a diagnosis label. A key modeling element is an external information process (e.g., self-exam or comorbidity) that can trigger early arrival, so inter-decision times are endogenous. The objective is a weighted sum of discounted screening cost and delay cost, where delay depends on incidence and detection times.

Technically, the authors derive Bellman equations on the belief over entire trajectories and show the value function is piecewise-linear and convex in the belief, extending classical POMDP results (Smallwood & Sondik; Pineau et al.) to a setting where actions affect timing and costs depend on trajectories. Exact dynamic programming is intractable; they propose a point-based approximation that (i) samples likely state trajectories to form a reduced belief space and (ii) performs value backups on reachable beliefs gathered via random exploration and clinical policies. They provide an approximation-error bound that scales with the density of belief sampling under assumptions (convex delay cost, perfect test accuracy, bounded screening intervals). They also show a robustness property: the exact optimal policy is locally constant in model-belief space almost everywhere, suggesting stability to estimation error.

Empirically, they fit a two-state breast cancer model to a large EHR dataset (45k patients, 84k mammograms), incorporate a self-exam external process based on tumor growth literature, and construct policies for low/high Gail risk groups. Compared to annual or biennial schedules, DPSCREEN achieves similar expected detection delay with 28–68% fewer screenings; they illustrate how history and risk change inter-visit times, and show that more convex delay costs lead to more aggressive screening. Related work in operations research (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Erenay et al.) and medical simulation (e.g., MISCAN-COLON) is discussed; controlled sensing and joint-modeling literatures are referenced, as are NIPS works on disease trajectories and information acquisition.

Quality
Strengths:
- Sound formulation that captures features not handled by standard POMDP/POSMDP screening models: action-dependent inter-arrival times via external information, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and non-Markov disease processes.
- Clear extension of piecewise-linear convexity of the value function to this setting; the PBVI-style approximation is reasonable given state-space explosion.
- Approximation error bound (though high-level) and a robustness result that policies are locally constant almost everywhere enhance credibility.
- Empirical study on a sizable real dataset, with realistic modeling of self-exams and mammogram/biopsy; strong reductions in testing rate at matched expected delay.

Weaknesses:
- Theoretical guarantees rely on restrictive assumptions (no false positives/negatives, convex delay cost, bounded inter-screening interval), yet experiments use imperfect tests (mammogram+/biopsy–), and the mapping between Z+ and diagnosis is not fully clarified. The mismatch between theory and practice should be addressed or bounds extended.
- Evaluation baselines are limited to fixed-interval clinical policies. A comparison to prior personalized POMDP screening works (Ayer et al., Maillart et al.) and to information-gain policies (e.g., ConfidentCare) would more convincingly demonstrate improvements.
- The external information process (monthly self-exam with parameters derived from literature) is simulated rather than observed; sensitivity analyses and ablations (with/without self-exam, varying threshold ỹ and noise) are only partially reported.
- Personalization in experiments is largely through Gail risk binning (low/high). It would be useful to show individualized schedules as a function of continuous risk/features, and outcomes stratified beyond two bins.
- Computational aspects: complexity and practical runtimes are reported at a high level; scalability with K (trajectory samples), |Z|, |Y|, and T is not fully demonstrated, nor is guidance on choosing K/B for a desired performance gap. The term Ω(B̄) is not actionable for practitioners.
- Some modeling/notation clarity issues: the discount factor δ used in experiments is not specified; the time horizon T and mapping to ages are unclear; the policy does not optimize the arrival threshold ỹ, which could be an important decision variable.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, with a careful formulation and intuitive narrative. However, several important algorithmic and experimental details are deferred to the supplement (Algorithms 1–3, graphical model, estimation specifics). Clarifying the Z+ set in the main text, the role of δ, the horizon T, and providing a concise pseudo-code summary in the main paper would improve readability.

Originality
The combination of (i) action-dependent timing via external information, (ii) trajectory-dependent costs, and (iii) a PBVI approach over sampled trajectory beliefs is novel within screening. While PBVI and piecewise-linear results are established in POMDPs, their adaptation here is non-trivial. Claims of being “the first” general personalized screening framework should be nuanced given prior POMDP-based screening for specific diseases, but the generality and modeling breadth here are clear advances.

Significance
Personalized, dynamic screening is an important application with real societal impact. The framework is broadly applicable beyond breast cancer and could influence practice if validated prospectively. The empirical gains are compelling, though stronger baselines and robustness analyses would increase confidence. The theoretical insights are likely to be of interest to NIPS audiences working on decision-making under uncertainty and healthcare.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a high-impact problem with a general and flexible framework.
- Solid formulation extending POMDP theory; principled approximation and robustness arguments.
- Demonstrates substantial potential cost savings at matched delay on real data.
- Clear connections to and extension of prior OR and POMDP literatures; relevant to NIPS topics.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited baselines; lack of head-to-head comparisons to prior personalized screening methods.
- Theory-experiment mismatch on test accuracy assumptions; limited practical guidance on approximation error control.
- Several key experimental details and ablations missing; personalization demonstrated in coarse risk bins.
- Computational scalability not empirically characterized.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to Ayer et al. (OR) and Maillart et al., and to recent machine learning approaches (e.g., ConfidentCare), using the same disease model to isolate policy optimization gains.
- Clarify Z+, diagnosis mapping, and reconcile “no false positives” assumptions with the mammogram/biopsy model; extend bounds to imperfect tests if possible.
- Report δ, T, runtimes, K and B, and sensitivity of performance to these; provide guidance on setting K/B for a target error.
- Evaluate robustness to model misspecification (bootstrap parameters; perturb growth/arrival thresholds; cross-validate).
- Consider optimizing the self-exam threshold ỹ as part of the policy or justify fixing it.
- Show personalization beyond binary risk groups (continuous risk quantiles; feature-dependent schedules).

Overall
A strong and timely contribution with solid modeling and promising empirical results. With strengthened baselines, clarified assumptions, and additional robustness/scalability evidence, this work would be a very good fit for NIPS. I lean toward acceptance after revision.
