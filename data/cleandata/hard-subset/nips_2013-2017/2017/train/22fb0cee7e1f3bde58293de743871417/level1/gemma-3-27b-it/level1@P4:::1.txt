This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for constructing personalized screening policies balancing detection delay and screening frequency. It models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process, incorporating external information and patient-specific features. The core contribution lies in providing a computationally tractable approximation to the optimal policy, addressing a significant challenge in personalized medicine. The work is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing approaches which often rely on Markovian assumptions and stationary test outcome models.

The paper builds upon established decision-making frameworks (POMDPs) but extends them to handle non-Markovian disease processes, external information, and computationally intractable optimization problems. The authors clearly position their work within the broader context of screening policies in operations research and medical literature, highlighting the advantages of their approach over existing simulation-based methods or those limited by strong modeling assumptions. The inclusion of a robustness analysis considering model estimation error is a strong point, demonstrating a practical awareness of real-world implementation challenges.

The strengths of the paper are substantial. The mathematical formulation is rigorous, and the proposed algorithm (DPSCREEN) appears well-designed, incorporating point-based value iteration with careful consideration of computational complexity. The breast cancer application is relevant and well-executed, demonstrating significant reductions in screening frequency (28-68%) while maintaining comparable detection delays. The illustrative exercise across different cost functions effectively showcases the importance of cost parameter selection. The detailed discussion of personalization, illustrated with specific patient examples, adds clarity to the practical implications of the approach. The inclusion of related work, referencing key papers in POMDPs, screening, and related fields, is comprehensive.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The complexity analysis, while presented, remains abstract and might benefit from concrete examples illustrating the impact of key parameters (K, T, B) on runtime. The assumption of a finite state space for the disease process, while simplifying the problem, may limit applicability to certain diseases with continuous state representations. The description of the model estimation process (using the Gail model and MCMC) is relatively brief; more details on parameter tuning and validation would strengthen the empirical results.  The discussion regarding the choice of cost function C (linear vs quadratic) is insightful but could be expanded to explore other cost function forms and their impact. While the robustness analysis addresses model estimation error, it doesn’t explicitly consider potential errors in the external information process or test outcomes. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the chosen breast cancer dataset (e.g., representativeness, potential biases). The connection between the presented algorithm and existing PBVI algorithms could be made more explicit, discussing specific adaptations made to address the unique challenges of this problem.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Addresses a crucial gap in personalized screening by providing a computationally tractable framework for non-Markovian disease processes with external information.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation and algorithm are rigorous and well-motivated.
*   **Significance:** Demonstrates substantial improvements in screening efficiency in a real-world application (breast cancer).
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Complexity analysis could be more concrete.**
*   **Model estimation details are brief.**
*   **Limited discussion of limitations regarding dataset and model assumptions.**
*   **While thorough, the related work section could benefit from more explicit connections to prior PBVI research.**



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of personalized screening. The proposed framework is novel, technically sound, and demonstrates promising results in a real-world application. While some areas could benefit from further clarification and elaboration, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
