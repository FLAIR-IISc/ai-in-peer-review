- Proposes DPSCREEN: a dynamic, personalized screening framework trading screening burden vs. detection delay using a finite-state disease model with endogenous arrivals.
- Formulates a belief-state dynamic program, proves convex piecewise-linear value function, and derives an intractable exact alpha-vector recursion.
- Introduces a tractable PBVI approximation using trajectory-sampled, lower-dimensional belief subsets and derives a sampling-density-based approximation bound.
- Argues robustness to model estimation error via a local-constancy property of piecewise-linear value functions.
- Empirically fits a simple two-state (Healthy/Diseased) breast cancer model with logistic Gail-risk and parametric self-exam signals on a 45k-patient cohort.
- Reports large reductions (28–68%) in expected screens at comparable expected delay versus fixed annual/biennial schedules under the learned model.
- Builds on POMDP/PBVI literature and extends prior screening/OR/Bayesian design work by allowing trajectory-dependent delay costs and exogenous early arrivals.
- Belief-state formulation and piecewise-linearity extension are plausible but require rigorous supplement proofs for full validation.
- Exact solution rightly identified as intractable; the PBVI adaptation via trajectory sampling is a reasonable and practical compromise.
- Approximation bound (Proposition 1) is qualitative, relies on strong assumptions (convex delay, perfect tests, bounded intervals, large T) and uses an unspecified constant ζ limiting interpretability.
- Robustness claim is standard for piecewise-linear functions and does not provide quantitative sensitivity or regret bounds under model misspecification.
- Empirical instantiation is much simpler than the general framework and does not stress-test non-Markovian, multi-state, or competing-risk settings.
- Experiments lack calibration/validation diagnostics, uncertainty quantification, and sensitivity analyses, making empirical savings vulnerable to model misspecification.
- Assumptions about diagnostics are inconsistent across sections (theory assumes perfect/no false positives, experiments include test sensitivity/specificity) and need reconciliation.
- Presentation suffers from heavy and sometimes inconsistent notation, unclear Bellman sign convention, and informal specification of central distributions and belief definitions.
- Complexity statement and definitions (e.g., T(B), Ω(B̄)) are unclear and mix notation for sets and points.
- Reproducibility would benefit from more details on parameter estimation, goodness-of-fit, and tuning of cost weights to benchmarks.
- Novelty lies in combining trajectory-dependent delay, external information-driven arrivals, and PBVI over trajectory-sampled beliefs; structural extension is nontrivial.
- Potentially significant for personalized screening policy design, but current empirical support is limited to a narrow setting without robust validation.
- Pros: clear practical problem framing, theoretical extension of value-function structure, practical PBVI algorithm, and promising initial personalized gains.
- Cons: approximation analyses rest on strong/partly unrealistic assumptions and noncomputable constants, simplified empirical model, clarity/inconsistency issues, and limited scalability/runtime evidence.
- Suggested improvements: reconcile diagnostic modeling across theory/experiments; add calibration plots, held-out performance, and sensitivity analyses; quantify uncertainty (bootstrap/posterior predictive); clarify Bellman formulation and Pr(·) definitions.
- Further suggestions: tighten or label the approximation bound as qualitative and include empirical ablations on belief sampling density and runtimes; discuss adherence, missed follow-ups, and overdiagnosis implications.
