- Paper formulates personalized, history-dependent screening as a sequential decision problem with endogenous arrival times and trajectory-dependent delay costs.  
- Disease evolution modeled as finite-state stochastic process with tests only at clinician visits and external signals triggering unscheduled visits.  
- Objective balances discounted screening costs and delay-to-detection costs; value function shown convex and piecewise-linear in beliefs despite nonstandard features.  
- Derives exact DP via alpha-vectors (intractable) and proposes DPSCREEN, a point-based approximation sampling likely trajectories and reachable beliefs.  
- Provides a sampling-density-based approximation error bound under strong assumptions and a robustness result on local constancy of optimal actions.  
- Experiments on a 45k-patient breast cancer cohort using a simple two-state model show DPSCREEN reduces expected screenings by 28–68% at comparable expected delay.  
- Connects to OR-based screening POMDPs, Bayesian/joint survival modeling, POSMDPs, controlled sensing, and point-based POMDP methods.  
- Would benefit from discussion and comparison to modern point-based solvers (Perseus, HSVI, SARSOP) and Monte Carlo POMDPs (POMCP).  
- Strength: clear, general formulation unifying personalized risk, dynamic history, and exogenous early-arrival signals.  
- Strength: nontrivial proof that value function remains piecewise-linear and convex with endogenous epochs and trajectory-dependent costs.  
- Strength: practical point-based approximation tailored to very high-dimensional belief space with constructive sampling heuristics.  
- Strength: simple error bound linking performance loss to belief coverage and robustness argument for model estimation error.  
- Weakness: approximation error bound relies on restrictive assumptions (no FP/FN, convex delay cost, bounded interval, large horizon) and lacks computable constants or stopping criteria.  
- Weakness: experiments contradict theoretical assumptions by using noisy tests; implications for guarantees are not discussed.  
- Weakness: empirical model is simplistic (two-state disease, stylized self-exam), parameters not jointly estimated or externally validated; limited sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: baselines limited to annual/biennial screening; missing comparisons to optimized screening frameworks and modern POMDP solvers.  
- Weakness: objective ignores harms from false positives, biopsies, and overdiagnosis; screening cost is overly simplistic.  
- Weakness: computational complexity and scalability not fully addressed; practical runtimes, dependence on |Z|, |Y|, K, and parallelization details missing.  
- Clarity: generally well-written, but theoretical/experimental mismatch on noise needs reconciliation and ablations under realistic FP/FN.  
- Clarity: core algorithmic details deferred to appendix; concise pseudo-code and schematic in main text would aid reproducibility.  
- Clarity: heavy notation for trajectory-beliefs; discussion of memory/representation or sufficient statistics would improve clarity.  
- Originality: novel in modeling endogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent delay costs within a POMDP-like framework and proving PWL value functions; approximation approach is pragmatic but close to existing PBVI methods.  
- Significance: approach could influence personalized screening if validated, but current empirical gains depend on a simple model and limited baselines.  
- Pros for acceptance: tackles important setting, provides theory and a practical algorithm, and demonstrates meaningful personalization and screening reductions.  
- Cons for acceptance: theoretical guarantees mismatch experiments, limited empirical baselines and validation, and approximation similar to known point-based techniques.  
- Suggestions: extend guarantees to noisy tests or provide ablations; compare to SARSOP/HSVI/Perseus and prior screening policies; validate and calibrate disease/self-exam models and run sensitivity analyses.  
- Suggestions: report computational performance and scalability, release code, and consider richer cost models including downstream harms and overdiagnosis.  
- Overall: solid, timely contribution with promising results but gaps in empirical validation and theory-practice alignment; reviewer leans weak accept if baseline/noise issues are addressed, otherwise borderline.
