- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies minimizing screening costs plus detection delay costs.
- Frames disease progression as a general finite-state stochastic process (not restricted to Markovian models).
- Incorporates external information (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger unscheduled visits.
- Allows flexible, trajectory-dependent delay cost functions.
- Casts the problem as a belief-space dynamic program over distributions on entire trajectories, which is high-dimensional.
- Proposes a PBVI-based approximation with a two-stage sampling: sample typical trajectories to reduce dimensionality, then generate reachable belief points via simulated policies.
- Provides theoretical support: Proposition 1 gives an error bound depending on belief point density; Theorem 1 shows robustness to small model estimation errors.
- Empirically validates on a large breast cancer dataset, reporting 28–68% fewer screenings for the same expected detection delay.
- Policy personalization demonstrated with both static risk features and dynamic test histories.
- Strength: significant originality and generality beyond prior POMDP screening models.
- Strength: technically sound DP formulation and a practical PBVI adaptation to make the problem tractable.
- Strength: solid theoretical justification for the approximation and robustness to estimation error.
- Strength: high potential real-world impact through reduced screenings and maintained detection performance.
- Weakness: scalability—worst-case complexity is exponential in horizon T and practical limits on T are underdiscussed.
- Weakness: key algorithmic details for belief-point generation (Algorithm 1) are high-level and may hinder reproducibility.
- Weakness: theoretical results assume “accurate tests,” which contrasts with experimental use of imperfect sensitivity/specificity.
- Weakness: model estimation (MCMC) details, validation, and handling of data sparsity are insufficiently described.
- For acceptance: novel, significant, technically deep work with strong empirical evidence.
- Against acceptance: reproducibility concerns, scalability limitations, and possible mismatch between theoretical assumptions and experimental setup.
