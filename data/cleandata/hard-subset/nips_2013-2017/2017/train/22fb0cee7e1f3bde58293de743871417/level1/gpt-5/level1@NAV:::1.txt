This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that jointly tailor screening frequency to patient features and evolving histories, while allowing unscheduled arrivals triggered by external information (e.g., self-exams). The disease is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; delay costs depend on the full disease trajectory, not just the current state. The authors formulate a belief-state dynamic program, show the value function is convex and piecewise-linear, and argue exact alpha-vector computation is intractable. They propose a PBVI-style approximation that samples likely disease trajectories and reachable beliefs, derive an approximation-error bound tied to belief-sampling density, and prove a robustness result showing local constancy of optimal actions under small model-estimation error. A breast cancer case study (age 60–65 cohort) uses Gail risk as features, a two-state Markov disease model, and a simple lump-growth external signal; the learned policy reduces expected screens by 28–68% at similar expected detection delays versus annual/biennial guidelines.

Relation to prior work: Compared to POMDP-based screening in OR (e.g., mammography and colonoscopy: Ayer et al., 2012; Maillart et al., 2008; Erenay et al., 2014) and Bayesian design/joint models (Verdinelli et al., 1995; Rizopoulos, 2012), DPSCREEN generalizes by accommodating trajectory-dependent delay costs and endogenous arrivals. Methodologically, it builds on PBVI (Pineau et al., 2003), constrained POMDPs (Kim et al., 2011), and controlled sensing (Krishnamurthy, 2017). It also complements NeurIPS work on disease trajectories and adaptive information acquisition (Schulam & Saria, 2015; Alaa et al., 2016).

Quality: The modeling and DP formulation are sound, and the PBVI adaptation is reasonable. However, the performance bound hinges on assumptions (e.g., no false positives/negatives, bounded inter-screen intervals) that are relaxed in experiments; the implications of this mismatch should be clarified. Evaluation is model-based using parameters learned from observational data; stronger sensitivity analyses and calibration/validation (e.g., held-out predictive checks) would help.

Clarity: Generally clear, but key algorithmic details are relegated to the supplement. Define ζ and provide practical magnitudes; report runtimes and K, |B̄|; explain how w is tuned; and clarify whether ỹ is learned.

Originality: High in combining external arrivals and trajectory-based costs within a general POMDP framework with tractable approximation.

Significance: Potentially impactful for clinical decision support, though prospective or off-policy evaluation beyond model-based simulation would strengthen claims.

Pros for acceptance:
- General, principled formulation; novel integration of external information and trajectory-dependent costs.
- PBVI adaptation with approximation and robustness results.
- Compelling simulated gains on a real dataset; clear personalization.

Cons/concerns:
- Theoretical assumptions vs. experimental setting mismatch.
- Limited validation beyond model-based evaluation; need sensitivity analyses.
- “First” claim should be tempered given OR and NeurIPS precedents.
- Scalability details and code/data availability not discussed.
