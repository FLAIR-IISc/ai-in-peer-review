**Summary of Main Ideas**

The paper proposes a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The framework models the disease as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and incorporates external information, such as self-examination or discovery of co-morbidities, that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment. The authors use breast cancer data to develop the disease model and demonstrate the effectiveness of their policy in reducing the number of screenings performed while maintaining the same delay in detection of disease.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds upon previous work in decision theory, particularly in the area of Partially Observable Markov Decision Processes (POMDPs) [18]-[21]. However, the authors note that standard POMDP methods are not applicable to their problem due to the complexity of the disease model and the need to incorporate external information. The paper also relates to previous work in screening frameworks for different diseases in operations research [7][9][10] and medical literature [44]-[48]. However, the authors argue that their method imposes fewer restrictions on the disease model, test outcomes, and external information, making it more general and applicable to a wider range of diseases.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel framework**: The paper proposes a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balances the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease.
2. **General disease model**: The framework models the disease as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, making it applicable to a wide range of diseases.
3. **Incorporation of external information**: The framework incorporates external information, such as self-examination or discovery of co-morbidities, that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment.
4. **Effective algorithm**: The authors propose an effective algorithm, DPSCREEN, for constructing approximately optimal policies.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Computational complexity**: The algorithm has a high computational complexity, which may limit its applicability to large-scale problems.
2. **Assumptions**: The paper makes several assumptions, such as the accuracy of the test outcomes and the bounded maximum screening interval, which may not hold in practice.
3. **Limited evaluation**: The paper only evaluates the framework using breast cancer data and may not be generalizable to other diseases.
