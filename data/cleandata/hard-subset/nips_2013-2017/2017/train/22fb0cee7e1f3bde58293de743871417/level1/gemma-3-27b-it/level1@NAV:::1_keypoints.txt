- Tackles personalized screening by modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process to create adaptive screening policies.  
- Core method (DPSCREEN) builds a computationally tractable framework using patient features and screening history.  
- Extends prior work (POMDPs, survival analysis) by handling a broader class of disease models and incorporating external information like self-exams.  
- Theoretical contribution includes tractability analysis and approximation error bounds.  
- Empirical application to breast cancer (Gail model + observational data) shows promising results: 28–68% reduction in screens with comparable delay.  
- Robustness analysis addresses model estimation error.  
- Presentation and logical flow of the paper are clear.  
- Limitation: reliance on Markovian disease progression may not capture complex disease dynamics.  
- Limitation: assumes perfect test accuracy (no false positives/negatives), which may hurt real-world applicability.  
- Computational complexity is discussed but needs more detailed analysis and empirical scaling results.  
- Cost function discussion (Figure 2) feels somewhat disconnected from the breast cancer application.  
- Related-work coverage is comprehensive but needs more direct comparison to recent reinforcement-learning approaches in healthcare.  
- Recommendation: strong paper with significant potential; accept with minor revisions requesting deeper complexity discussion and comparison to RL methods.
