Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a general framework to design dynamic, personalized screening policies that balance test frequency against delay in disease detection. The disease process is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; external information (e.g., self-exam findings) can trigger earlier-than-scheduled visits; and test outcomes are observed at visits. The authors cast the problem as optimizing a weighted sum of discounted screening cost and delay cost, where the delay cost depends on the trajectory (incidence time and detection lag), not just the current state. They formulate a belief-state dynamic program with a piecewise-linear, convex value function, derive an exact alpha-vector recursion (intractable), and develop a tractable point-based approximation that samples “typical” state trajectories and reachable beliefs. They provide a complexity bound, an approximation-error bound in terms of belief-space coverage, and a robustness result stating that optimal actions are locally constant in the joint model–belief space outside a measure-zero set. An empirical illustration on breast cancer data (Athena network) shows large reductions (28–68%) in expected screening counts versus annual/biennial policies while holding expected detection delays roughly constant.

This work relates to POMDP-based screening (Ayer et al., Maillart et al.), Bayesian design approaches (Rizopoulos; Alaa & van der Schaar), and controlled sensing. Relative to prior OR screening models that assume Markov disease dynamics and i.i.d. observation processes, DPSCREEN claims broader modeling scope (arbitrary finite-state stochastic disease trajectories, trajectory-dependent costs, and endogenous arrival driven by external signals), and brings PBVI-style approximations to this domain.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The belief-state reduction and piecewise-linear convexity are natural extensions of classic POMDP theory. The exact alpha-vector construction is standard, and the intractability is expected. The approximation uses point-based backups over a sampled set of trajectories and reachable beliefs; this is reasonable given the very high-dimensional belief over state trajectories. The error bound, however, is qualitative: the bound is proportional to a sampling-density term Ω(B̄) with an unspecified constant ζ and requires strong assumptions (no false positives and no false negatives, bounded inter-screening interval, convex delay cost, large T). The robustness theorem about local constancy of optimal actions is plausible but nonconstructive; key regularity conditions and proof sketches should be clarified.
- Modeling choices: The paper allows very general disease processes, but the empirical section uses a two-state Markov model estimated from EHR features (Gail risk) and literature sensitivities/specificities. This is practical but narrows the demonstrated generality. The “no false positives” assumption in the core model (z ∈ Z+ implies certain disease) is clinically reasonable if Z+ denotes biopsy-confirmed disease, yet the approximation error result later assumes both no false positives and no false negatives—unrealistic for mammography and biopsy. The external information–driven arrival is modeled via a fixed threshold ỹ; optimizing this threshold as part of the policy might further improve performance but is not considered.
- Evaluation: The empirical evaluation is simulation-based using a learned disease model, which is typical in this literature. However, there is limited treatment of estimation uncertainty, censoring/verification bias, missingness, or cohort shift. There are no confidence intervals on the reported gains; the tuning of the weight w to match the benchmark delay could mask trade-offs. It would be helpful to present full Pareto frontiers (expected screens vs expected delay) and sensitivity analyses to test accuracy, external-information parameters, and adherence.

Clarity
The paper presents a complex setup clearly overall, with consistent notation and a well-motivated distinction from standard POMDP/POSMDP settings (endogenous decision epochs, trajectory-dependent costs). Some points that could be clarified:
- Define Z+ precisely (e.g., biopsy-positive only) and reconcile assumptions on false positives/negatives across sections.
- Provide more guidance on constructing the belief-support set: trajectory sampling K, policy exploration strategies, and practical heuristics for pruning/dominance.
- Detail runtime and scalability: wall-clock times, cores used, and sensitivity to T, |Y|, |Z|, K, and B̄.
- Make the approximation bound more interpretable by bounding ζ and reporting Ω(B̄) achieved in experiments.

Originality
The combination of (i) a general trajectory-aware screening formulation with (ii) external-information-driven arrival and (iii) point-based approximate dynamic programming over sampled trajectory-beliefs appears novel in the screening literature. While PBVI and piecewise-linear analysis are not new, their adaptation to this nonstandard decision-epoch and cost structure is original. The robustness result is interesting but would benefit from tighter connection to existing sensitivity/perturbation analyses in POMDPs.

Significance
Personalized, dynamic screening is important, and the reported reductions in tests for similar delays are potentially impactful. As a methodological contribution, DPSCREEN could influence how simulation-based medical guideline development considers optimization beyond fixed policy sets. The immediate clinical significance is tempered by strong assumptions (e.g., perfect tests in theory section), limited handling of data issues, and evaluation on a single age band and risk stratification.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation; general and flexible modeling framework.
- Solid use of belief-state DP with piecewise-linear structure; appropriate recourse to PBVI.
- Incorporation of external signals triggering unscheduled visits.
- Empirical illustration with sizable dataset and clinically relevant benchmarks.
- Preliminary robustness and approximation analyses.

Weaknesses
- Error bounds are qualitative with unrealistic assumptions; constants and achieved coverage not quantified.
- Limited details on computational performance and scalability.
- Evaluation lacks uncertainty quantification and sensitivity to model misspecification/adherence.
- Generality claims exceed what is demonstrated empirically; estimation of highly general disease processes from EHRs is not addressed.
- The arrival threshold and cost weights are exogenously set/tuned; no principled calibration to utilities or patient preferences.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide Pareto curves and confidence intervals via posterior sampling (e.g., MCMC draws of model parameters) and report variability of outcomes.
- Relax the perfect-test assumptions in theory; at minimum, extend the approximation bound to bounded misclassification rates.
- Report implementation details: K, |B̄[t]|, pruning strategies, runtime/cores; include ablation on trajectory sampling policies.
- Explore optimizing the external-information threshold and adherence models.
- Validate on additional cohorts (ages, densities) and compare to risk/density-based guidelines (e.g., Trentham-Dietz, Vilaprinyo).

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated formulation of personalized screening with external information and trajectory-dependent costs.
- Methodologically sound adaptation of PBVI to a challenging, nonstandard setting.
- Promising empirical results suggesting substantial resource savings without increased delay.
- Relevant to NIPS audience interested in decision-making under uncertainty, healthcare, and POMDPs.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical guarantees rely on strong, clinically unrealistic assumptions and lack quantitative constants.
- Evaluation does not quantify uncertainty or robustness sufficiently; generalization is unclear.
- Computational scalability and practical deployment details are underdeveloped.
- Some claims of generality are not borne out by experiments.

Overall, this is a thoughtful and potentially impactful contribution. With stronger empirical robustness analyses and clearer theoretical/implementation details, it would be a strong fit for NIPS.
