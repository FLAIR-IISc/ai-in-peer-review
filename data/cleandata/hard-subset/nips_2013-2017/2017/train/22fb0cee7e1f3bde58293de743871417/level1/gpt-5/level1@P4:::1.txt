Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework to design dynamic, personalized screening schedules that trade off screening burden against delay in disease detection. The disease process is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; patients may also arrive early to clinic based on an external information process (e.g., self-examination). Because costs depend on detection delay (a trajectory-dependent quantity) and arrival times are endogenous (via scheduled follow-ups and early arrivals), standard POMDP/POSMDP methods do not directly apply. The authors formulate a belief-state dynamic program, show the value function is convex and piecewise linear in beliefs, outline an exact alpha-vector recursion that is intractable, and introduce a tractable approximation based on point-based value iteration (PBVI) over a sampled, lower-dimensional belief subset constructed from likely state trajectories. They derive a bound on approximation error as a function of the sampling density and argue robustness to model estimation error via a generic local-constancy result. Empirically, they estimate a two-state (H/D) model for breast cancer using a 45k-patient dataset (ages 60–65) and compare to annual/biennial schedules. The policy yields large reductions (28–68%) in expected number of screens at comparable expected delay.

The work builds on POMDP methods (Smallwood & Sondik; Pineau et al.) and connects to OR screening models (Ayer et al. for mammography; Maillart et al.; Erenay et al.), Bayesian design approaches for screening (Rizopoulos; Alaa et al.), and controlled sensing. Relative to prior screening work, it allows general finite-state disease processes, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and exogenous early arrivals through an external signal, and it proposes a PBVI tailored to an enormous trajectory-based belief space.

Quality (technical soundness):
- The belief-state formulation and extension of piecewise-linearity to this setting are plausible and, if rigorously established in the supplement, justify an alpha-vector representation despite trajectory-dependent costs and endogenous inter-visit times.
- The exact solution is correctly identified as intractable; the proposed PBVI uses trajectory sampling to reduce dimensionality and reachable-belief sampling under random and heuristic policies. This is a reasonable adaptation given the high intrinsic complexity.
- The approximation bound (Proposition 1) qualitatively connects performance loss to belief sampling density, but it relies on strong assumptions (convex delay, perfect tests, bounded screening intervals, large T) and introduces an unspecified constant ζ (maximum one-step loss), which limits practical interpretability and verifiability. Notation around Ω(B̄) is imprecise (mixing sets and points in maxima/minima).
- The robustness result (local constancy of optimal actions off a measure-zero set) is a standard genericity/property of piecewise-linear value functions. It supports stability to small model estimation error but does not yield quantitative sensitivity or end-to-end regret bounds under model misspecification.
- The empirical evaluation estimates a simple two-state Markov model (H→D) with logistic risk in the Gail score and models self-exam signals by a parametric growth function. This instantiation is much simpler than the general framework; while acceptable for illustration, it does not stress-test the claimed generality (e.g., non-Markov/semi-Markov, multiple preclinical states, competing risks). The evaluation compares policies via simulation under the learned model; this is standard, but the paper lacks calibration/validation diagnostics and uncertainty quantification, making claims of 28–68% savings without delay compromise sensitive to model misspecification.
- Some assumptions are inconsistent across sections: the main model text assumes no false positives when diagnosing upon Z∈Z+; the bound assumes perfect tests; the experiments incorporate mammogram sensitivity/specificity and biopsy confirmation. The paper should reconcile the diagnostic model (what constitutes Z+, and where false positives/negatives may occur) with the theory’s assumptions.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-organized, but notation is heavy and sometimes inconsistent. For example, the Bellman equation (3) maximizes the negative expected cost; a minimization formulation would be clearer. The distribution Pr(z, y, τ̃|b, τ, x) is central but only informally specified. The definition of beliefs over full state trajectories and label l is unusual and merits more intuition in the main text, as does the projection to a sampled subspace.
- The complexity statement O(T(B)2T2K|Y||Z|) needs clearer definitions of T(B) and B to avoid confusion. The definition of Ω(B̄) mixes notation; define the sup over the full belief simplex at each t explicitly.
- Reproducibility would benefit from more details on parameter estimation, goodness-of-fit, and how weights w are tuned to match benchmark delays.

Originality:
- The combination of (i) trajectory-dependent delay costs, (ii) external information leading to endogenous arrivals, and (iii) a PBVI approach over trajectory-sampled beliefs appears novel relative to prior personalized screening models that assume Markov disease/test processes and fixed decision epochs. The structural extension of piecewise-linearity to this setting is a nontrivial theoretical contribution, though it follows the POMDP tradition.

Significance:
- If validated broadly, the framework could impact personalized screening policy design across diseases and modalities, potentially reducing screening burden without increasing detection delay. However, the current empirical support is limited to a simplified two-state breast cancer model on a narrow age band, with limited uncertainty analysis. The theoretical bound is qualitative rather than actionable.

Pros:
- Clear problem formulation capturing practical aspects (external arrivals, delay costs).
- Theoretical extension of value function structure to a nonstandard setting.
- Practical PBVI-based algorithm with reachable-belief construction.
- Promising empirical gains relative to common clinical schedules; personalization to risk and history is demonstrated.

Cons:
- Approximation/error analyses rely on strong and partially unrealistic assumptions (perfect tests) and provide loose, non-computable constants.
- Empirical instantiation uses a much simpler model than the general theory; limited validation and no uncertainty quantification.
- Some clarity/notation issues; minor inconsistencies in assumptions about test accuracy and cost modeling.
- Computational complexity and scalability claims would benefit from empirical runtime and convergence evidence; no code or ablation studies reported.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Reconcile diagnostic modeling across theory and experiments; specify where false positives/negatives occur and adjust assumptions or statements in the theory accordingly.
- Provide calibration plots, held-out predictive performance, and sensitivity analyses (e.g., to tumor growth parameters, self-exam compliance, sensitivity/specificity) to support empirical claims.
- Quantify uncertainty in performance metrics (e.g., via bootstrap over patients or posterior predictive simulation).
- Clarify the Bellman equation sign convention; fully specify Pr(z, y, τ̃|b, τ, x) and belief updates in the main text.
- Tighten the approximation bound with explicit constants where possible, or clearly label it as qualitative; consider empirical ablations showing how performance changes with belief sampling density K and B̄ size.
- Discuss adherence/missed follow-ups and overdiagnosis, which are relevant in clinical screening.

Overall, the paper presents a compelling and general framework with a reasonable approximation strategy and promising initial results, but it would benefit from clearer theoretical assumptions, stronger empirical validation, and improved exposition.
