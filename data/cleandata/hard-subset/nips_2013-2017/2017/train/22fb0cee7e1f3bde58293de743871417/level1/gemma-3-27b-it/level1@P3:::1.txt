This paper presents a novel and ambitious framework, DPSCREEN, for personalized dynamic screening policies, addressing a critical gap in existing approaches that often rely on simplified disease models and static screening schedules. The core idea – modeling disease progression as a finite state stochastic process and optimizing screening policies based on patient features, screening history, and external information – is well-motivated and potentially impactful. The authors tackle significant challenges related to computational intractability by proposing an approximately optimal policy construction and providing bounds on the approximation error. The application to breast cancer, utilizing a large dataset, strengthens the practical relevance of the work.

The paper's strengths are considerable. The methodological contribution is substantial; extending POMDP-like frameworks to handle dynamic disease models, external information, and computationally efficient approximation is a significant achievement. The robustness analysis, considering estimation error and demonstrating convergence to the optimal policy under reasonable assumptions, adds confidence in the proposed approach. The clear explanation of the framework, combined with the illustrative experiments demonstrating personalization based on risk and test results, effectively conveys the benefits of DPSCREEN. The comparisons against existing clinical policies, showing substantial reductions in screenings while maintaining delay, are compelling.  The inclusion of considerations for different delay cost functions (linear vs. quadratic) highlights the adaptability of the framework. The related work section is thorough, accurately situating the paper within the broader literature on screening, personalized medicine, and controlled sensing.

However, several weaknesses require attention. While the paper demonstrates the feasibility of DPSCREEN, the experimental validation, though promising, feels somewhat limited. The breast cancer dataset is utilized effectively, but a more comprehensive evaluation across different disease models or datasets would strengthen the generalizability claim. The parameter estimation process for the breast cancer model, while described, lacks sufficient detail. Specifically, how sensitive are the resulting policies to errors in the estimated parameters? A sensitivity analysis would be valuable. The assumption of no false positives in the tests feels overly optimistic and could be relaxed in future work.

The computational complexity discussion, while acknowledging the challenges, doesn't provide concrete runtime figures for realistic problem instances.  For instance, how does the computation time scale with the number of states in the disease model or the size of the patient population?  This information is crucial for assessing the practical applicability of the algorithm. The claim of personalization, while demonstrated quantitatively, could benefit from more qualitative examples showcasing how the policy adapts to specific patient scenarios. The explanation regarding the choice of the belief space construction (Algorithm 1) could be more intuitive. Why are the chosen trajectories 'typical'? How does the choice affect the approximation error?

Furthermore, the paper’s connection to recent advances in reinforcement learning (RL) is underexplored. While the authors mention POMDPs, the framework could potentially be cast as a partially observable RL problem, allowing for the application of modern RL algorithms, perhaps with modifications to address the computational challenges. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** DPSCREEN presents a genuinely novel approach to personalized screening, addressing limitations of existing methodologies.
* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation is strong, with well-defined models and approximation bounds.
* **Significance:** The potential impact on healthcare, through optimized screening policies and reduced costs, is significant.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Limited Experimental Validation:** The evaluation could be more comprehensive and robust.
* **Computational Complexity:**  Lack of concrete runtime figures limits assessment of practicality.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** Insufficient analysis of the impact of parameter estimation errors.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The proposed framework is innovative, technically sound, and has the potential to significantly improve healthcare decision-making. I recommend **acceptance** with the caveat that the authors address the reviewers’ concerns regarding experimental validation, computational complexity, and parameter sensitivity in a revised version. Addressing these points will strengthen the paper’s impact and solidify its contribution to the NIPS community.
