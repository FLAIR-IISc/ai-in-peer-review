- Proposes DPSCREEN, a novel framework for personalized screening that balances detection delay and screening frequency using a finite-state stochastic disease model.  
- Incorporates external information and patient-specific features to personalize screening policies.  
- Provides a computationally tractable approximation to the optimal policy, addressing intractability in personalized screening.  
- Extends POMDP-based decision frameworks to handle non-Markovian disease processes and nonstationary test outcomes.  
- Positions the work clearly within screening, operations research, and medical literature, highlighting advantages over simulation-based and strongly-assumed models.  
- Includes a robustness analysis for model estimation error, demonstrating practical awareness of implementation challenges.  
- Mathematical formulation is rigorous and DPSCREEN algorithm uses point-based value iteration with attention to computational complexity.  
- Empirical breast cancer application shows substantial reductions in screening frequency (28–68%) while maintaining comparable detection delays.  
- Illustrative experiments across cost functions clarify the importance of cost parameter selection.  
- Detailed personalization examples clarify practical implications for individual patients.  
- Related work coverage is comprehensive, citing key POMDP and screening literature.  
- Complexity analysis is abstract and would benefit from concrete runtime examples illustrating effects of parameters (K, T, B).  
- Finite-state disease assumption may limit applicability to diseases with continuous state dynamics.  
- Model estimation description (Gail model and MCMC) is brief; more detail on tuning and validation is needed.  
- Discussion of cost function choices (beyond linear vs quadratic) is limited and could be expanded.  
- Robustness analysis omits explicit consideration of errors in external information processes or test outcomes.  
- Paper lacks a thorough discussion of limitations of the breast cancer dataset (representativeness and potential biases).  
- Connection and specific adaptations to existing PBVI algorithms could be made more explicit.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach to non-Markovian personalized screening, rigorous and well-motivated methods, significant real-world improvements, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: need for more concrete complexity analysis, fuller model estimation details, deeper limitations discussion, and clearer ties to prior PBVI work.  
- Overall assessment: strong, significant contribution recommending acceptance with minor revisions to address the noted clarifications.
