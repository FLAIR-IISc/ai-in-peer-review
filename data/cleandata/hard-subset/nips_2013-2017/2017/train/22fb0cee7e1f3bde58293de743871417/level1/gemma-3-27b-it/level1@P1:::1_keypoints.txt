- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized screening that balances detection delay and screening frequency using patient features, history, and external information.  
- Handles non-Markovian disease processes and incorporates external signals, relaxing common restrictive assumptions in prior work.  
- Proposes a computationally tractable approximate policy with a bounded error guarantee (Proposition 1).  
- Empirically validated on a large breast cancer dataset, showing 28–68% reductions in screening frequency with comparable detection delays.  
- Demonstrates dynamic, patient-specific risk assessment and clear personalization examples (e.g., Figure 1).  
- Presents a coherent model and problem formulation with largely consistent and understandable notation.  
- Analyzes robustness to estimation error and provides theoretical bounds on its impact (Theorem 1).  
- Situates the work well within related literature on screening optimization, decision theory, and controlled sensing.  
- Computational complexity (O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)) may limit scalability to very large or more complex models; practical optimizations are under-discussed.  
- Finite-state model assumption and independence assumptions for tests/external info may not hold for all diseases and need further examination.  
- Relies on accurate parameter estimation; applicability to diseases with limited data and sensitivity/bias issues are not fully addressed.  
- Policy performance may be sensitive to the chosen delay cost function (linear vs. quadratic); more systematic sensitivity analysis is needed.  
- Comparisons are mainly against annual and biennial screening; lacks benchmarking against other sophisticated personalized policies.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important healthcare problem with a novel, theoretically sound, and empirically effective approach.  
- Arguments against acceptance: concerns about computational scalability, model assumptions, and incomplete exploration of parameter/cost sensitivities.  
- Overall assessment: strong, well-motivated, and validated contribution with limitations that warrant minor revisions focused on complexity and modeling assumptions; recommend acceptance.
