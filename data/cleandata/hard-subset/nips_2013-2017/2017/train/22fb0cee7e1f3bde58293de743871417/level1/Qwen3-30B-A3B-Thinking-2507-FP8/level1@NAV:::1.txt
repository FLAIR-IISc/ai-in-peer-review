

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing dynamic personalized screening policies that adapt to both patient-specific features and evolving health history. The authors model disease progression as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating external information (e.g., self-examinations) that can trigger earlier clinician visits. They address the computational intractability of finding exact optimal policies by developing a point-based value iteration approach with approximation error bounds. The method is validated on breast cancer data, demonstrating 28-68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining equivalent disease detection delays compared to current clinical practices.

The paper makes significant contributions to the literature. Previous works on personalized screening (e.g., Ayer et al., 2014; Maillart et al., 2015) typically assume Markov disease processes and stationary test outcome models, which limit their applicability. DPSCREEN generalizes beyond these restrictions while maintaining theoretical rigor. The authors properly contextualize their work against related POMDP approaches (e.g., Pineau et al., 2006) and controlled sensing frameworks, clearly articulating their novel contributions.

Strengths include strong theoretical foundations with approximation error bounds, well-designed experimental validation showing substantial practical impact, and clear articulation of the framework's flexibility across different disease models. The method's ability to personalize to both initial patient features and dynamic history (as demonstrated in Figure 1) represents a meaningful advancement. The paper is clearly written with appropriate technical depth.

Weaknesses include the reliance on several simplifying assumptions (no false positives, specific disease modeling choices) that may limit direct applicability to all clinical scenarios. The breast cancer case study, while well-executed, could be strengthened by demonstrating the approach on at least one additional disease type to better establish generalizability. The computational complexity analysis, while thorough, could more explicitly address scalability to very large patient populations.

Pro: Novel framework addressing important gaps in personalized screening; strong theoretical foundation with error bounds; compelling empirical results showing significant practical impact (28-68% screening reduction); well-positioned against prior work.

Con: Limited disease generalization beyond breast cancer; assumptions (no false positives) may restrict real-world applicability; computational complexity could be a barrier for large-scale implementation.

Overall, this is a high-quality, significant contribution that advances the state of the art in personalized healthcare decision-making. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses a meaningful problem with tangible impact. It should be accepted at NIPS.
