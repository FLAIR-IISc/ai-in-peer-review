- Proposes DPSCREEN: a framework that balances screening cost against delayed-detection cost for personalized, dynamic screening policies.
- Models disease progression as a general finite-state (non-Markovian) stochastic process, extending prior work.
- Introduces an explicit external information process to model unscheduled clinician visits triggered by patient events.
- Casts the problem as a POMDP-like optimization with endogenous decision timing and trajectory-dependent delay costs.
- Derives Bellman equations over a belief space (distributions over disease trajectories) but shows exact solution is intractable.
- Proposes a tractable approximate policy using a tailored Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) algorithm.
- Key algorithmic innovation: two-step belief sampling—sample typical trajectories, then sample reachable beliefs within that subspace.
- Provides theoretical support: an approximation error bound (Proposition 1) and a robustness theorem for model estimation errors (Theorem 1).
- Empirical evaluation on a large breast cancer dataset shows 28–68% fewer screenings while maintaining expected detection delay.
- Demonstrates personalization: policies adapt to static risk features and dynamic history (test results, external events).
- Situates work relative to prior POMDP-based screening models, showing substantial generalization over Markov/stationary assumptions.
- Contrasts with medical simulation studies by constructing near-optimal policies from a large policy space rather than only evaluating fixed policies.
- Relates to controlled sensing literature but relaxes strong structural/stationarity assumptions common there.
- Adapts and extends PBVI methodology to handle a much larger, more complex state/belief space.
- Strength: high practical significance and potential real-world impact on screening efficiency and outcomes.
- Strength: high originality—trajectory-dependent costs and external information process are major conceptual advances.
- Strength: strong technical quality with clear formulation, clever approximation, and supporting theory.
- Strength: paper is clear, well-organized, and includes intuitive illustrations (e.g., Figure 1).
- Weakness: application uses a two-state Markov model, not fully showcasing the framework’s non-Markovian/general power.
- Weakness: scalability concerns—quadratic complexity in sampled beliefs and horizon; practical runtimes and larger-scale behavior need clarification.
- Weakness: practical utility of the approximation bound is unclear due to dependence on constants (ζ) and sampling density (Ω(B̄)).
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, high-impact problem, general and realistic modeling, strong empirical gains, solid theory, and clear presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: empirical demonstration doesn’t fully showcase framework generality and scalability limitations are underexplored.
- Conclusion: reviewer strongly recommends acceptance, calling the paper an excellent, influential contribution with minor weaknesses.
