- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic personalized screening policies that adapt to patient-specific features and evolving health history.  
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and incorporates external triggers (e.g., self-exams) for earlier visits.  
-Addresses intractability with a point-based value iteration approximation and provides approximation error bounds.  
- Validated on breast cancer data, achieving 28–68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining equivalent disease detection delays.  
- Generalizes beyond prior personalized screening work by relaxing Markov and stationary test outcome assumptions while retaining theoretical rigor.  
- Properly situates the approach relative to POMDP and controlled sensing literature and clearly states novel contributions.  
- Strengths: strong theoretical foundations, error bounds, compelling empirical impact, flexible framework, clear writing, and personalization to initial features and dynamic history.  
- Weaknesses: relies on simplifying assumptions (e.g., no false positives) that may limit clinical applicability.  
- Weaknesses: evaluation limited to breast cancer; needs demonstration on additional diseases for broader generalizability.  
- Weaknesses: computational complexity and scalability to very large patient populations could be a barrier.  
- Overall recommendation: technically sound, significant contribution; should be accepted.
