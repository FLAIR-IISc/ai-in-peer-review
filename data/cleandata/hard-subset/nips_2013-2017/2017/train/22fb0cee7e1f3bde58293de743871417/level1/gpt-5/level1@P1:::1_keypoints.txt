- Proposes DPSCREEN: a framework for dynamic, personalized screening balancing test frequency and detection delay using finite-state disease trajectories and an external information process that makes inter-decision times endogenous.  
- Derives Bellman equations on beliefs over full trajectories and proves the value function is piecewise-linear and convex, extending POMDP results to action-dependent timing and trajectory-dependent costs.  
- Introduces a PBVI-style approximation: sample likely trajectories to reduce belief space and perform value backups on reachable beliefs gathered via exploration and clinical policies.  
- Provides an approximation-error bound (under assumptions) and a robustness result showing the optimal policy is locally constant almost everywhere.  
- Empirical study fits a two-state breast cancer model to a large EHR (45k patients, 84k mammograms), models self-exam arrivals, and constructs policies for low/high Gail risk groups.  
- DPSCREEN achieves similar expected detection delay to annual/biennial screening with 28–68% fewer screenings in experiments.  
- Strength: models features standard POMDP/POSMDP screening misses (action-dependent arrivals, trajectory-dependent delay, non-Markov disease processes).  
- Strength: clear theoretical extension of piecewise-linear convexity and a sensible PBVI approximation for large state spaces.  
- Strength: approximation bound and robustness claim add credibility to the approach.  
- Strength: sizable real-data empirical evaluation with realistic modeling yields substantial testing reductions.  
- Weakness: theoretical guarantees rely on restrictive assumptions (perfect tests, convex delay cost, bounded intervals) that conflict with experiments using imperfect mammogram/biopsy tests.  
- Weakness: limited baselines—only fixed-interval policies—no head-to-head with prior personalized POMDP or information-gain methods (Ayer et al., Maillart et al., ConfidentCare).  
- Weakness: external information (self-exam) is simulated from literature rather than observed; sensitivity analyses and ablations are incomplete.  
- Weakness: personalization is coarse (Gail low/high bins); lacks demonstration of individualized schedules based on continuous features.  
- Weakness: computational scalability and runtime details are high-level; no concrete guidance on choosing trajectory sample size K or belief budget B.  
- Weakness: several modeling/notation details are unclear or missing in the main text (Z+ set, discount δ, horizon T, optimization of arrival threshold ỹ).  
- Clarity: paper is generally well written but important algorithmic/experimental details are relegated to the supplement and should be brought into the main text.  
- Originality: novel combination of action-dependent timing, trajectory-dependent costs, and PBVI over trajectory-sampled beliefs within screening, though PBVI/POMDP components are established.  
- Significance: framework is broadly applicable and impactful for personalized screening; empirical gains are promising but need stronger validation.  
- Pros for acceptance: high-impact problem, principled extension of POMDP theory, credible approximation/robustness arguments, and strong real-data savings.  
- Cons for acceptance: weak baselines, theory-experiment mismatch on test accuracy, limited experimental detail and ablations, and unclear computational scalability.  
- Suggested improvement: add comparisons to prior OR and ML screening methods using the same disease model to isolate policy gains.  
- Suggested improvement: reconcile/extend theory to handle imperfect tests and clarify the Z+/diagnosis mapping.  
- Suggested improvement: report δ, T, K, B, runtimes, and sensitivity of performance to these hyperparameters; give guidance for choosing K/B.  
- Suggested improvement: evaluate robustness to model misspecification (bootstrap/perturb parameters) and include more ablations (with/without self-exam, threshold/noise variations).  
- Suggested improvement: consider optimizing the self-exam threshold ỹ or justify fixing it, and demonstrate personalization beyond binary risk bins (continuous features/quantiles).  
- Overall: strong, timely contribution with promising empirical results; recommend acceptance after revision to strengthen baselines, clarify assumptions, and add robustness/scalability evidence.
