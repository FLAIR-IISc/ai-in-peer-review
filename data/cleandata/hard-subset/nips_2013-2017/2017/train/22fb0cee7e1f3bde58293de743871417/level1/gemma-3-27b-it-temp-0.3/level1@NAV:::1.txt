## Review of DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening costs and the delay in disease detection. The core idea is to model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and develop a computationally tractable approach to approximate the optimal screening policy, considering both patient features and screening history, as well as potential external information triggering earlier visits. This work builds upon existing research in personalized screening (e.g., [7, 9, 10]) by relaxing the assumptions of Markovian disease processes and stationary test outcomes, allowing for a more general and realistic modeling approach. It also addresses the computational intractability of finding optimal policies through a point-based value iteration method.

The strengths of this paper lie in its generality and practical relevance. The framework is applicable to a wide range of diseases, as demonstrated through a breast cancer case study using a large dataset. The results show significant reductions in the number of screenings (28-68%) while maintaining comparable detection delays compared to current clinical policies. The inclusion of external information, like self-exams, further enhances the personalization aspect. The theoretical analysis, including the approximation error bound (Proposition 1) and robustness to model estimation errors (Theorem 1), adds to the rigor of the work. The detailed discussion of computational complexity and potential parallelization is also valuable.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is present, the connection between the approximation error bound and practical performance is not fully explored. The choice of specific parameters (e.g., ι = 0.9 for tumor growth) feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks detailed justification. The experiments, while demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach, are limited to breast cancer. Expanding the empirical evaluation to other diseases would strengthen the claims of generality. The description of the dataset and model estimation process, while present in the appendix, could be more integrated into the main body for clarity. Additionally, the discussion of related work could be more comprehensive, particularly regarding recent advances in reinforcement learning for healthcare.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and general framework for personalized screening.
*   Significant improvements over existing clinical policies in the breast cancer case study.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with approximation error bounds and robustness analysis.
*   Addresses a practically important problem with potential for real-world impact.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical evaluation beyond breast cancer.
*   Some parameter choices lack detailed justification.
*   Connection between theoretical bounds and practical performance could be strengthened.



**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of personalized screening. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the work is of high quality and relevance to the NIPS audience. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
