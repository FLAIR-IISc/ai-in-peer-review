

# Peer Review: DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

This paper proposes a novel framework called DPSCREEN for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening frequency against delay in disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating external information (e.g., self-examinations, comorbidities) that can trigger earlier patient visits. The key contribution is a computationally tractable method to construct approximately optimal screening policies by adapting point-based value iteration (PBVI) to this specific problem, with theoretical bounds on approximation error. The approach is validated on a large breast cancer dataset, demonstrating 28-68% reduction in screenings compared to current clinical policies while maintaining the same expected detection delay.

The paper builds on previous work in personalized screening [7,9,10] but addresses critical limitations. Prior approaches [7,9] restrict to Markov disease processes and stationary test outcome models, while [10] uses Bayesian design principles without incorporating external information. The authors correctly identify that these frameworks cannot handle the general disease models and dynamic patient behavior observed in real-world scenarios. The paper also relates to controlled sensing literature [21,34,38], but the authors properly distinguish their approach by addressing the more complex, non-stationary disease progression models common in medical applications.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides strong theoretical foundations with Lemma 1 (convexity of value function) and Proposition 1 (approximation error bound), which are crucial for justifying the approximate solution. The computational complexity analysis is thorough, and the proposed PBVI adaptation for high-dimensional belief spaces is well-motivated.

2. **Empirical Impact:** The breast cancer case study demonstrates significant practical improvements (28-68% reduction in screenings) with clear clinical relevance. The comparison to current clinical policies (annual vs. biennial screening) provides meaningful benchmarks.

3. **Novel Integration of External Information:** The framework's ability to incorporate external information processes (like self-examinations) that trigger early visits represents a substantial advance over existing methods that treat patient arrivals as exogenous.

4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper carefully examines the impact of different delay cost functions (linear vs. quadratic) on screening policies, highlighting how policy design must be disease-specific.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Simplifying Assumptions:** The paper assumes no false positives (Section 2) and uses a two-state disease model (healthy/disease), which may not capture the complexity of many diseases (e.g., breast cancer with benign/malignant stages). While these are reasonable simplifications for the case study, the paper could better address how the framework would extend to more complex disease models.

2. **Limited Scope of Disease Models:** While the authors claim generality, their empirical validation is limited to breast cancer with a two-state Markov model. The synthetic exercise in Section 4.2 is helpful but doesn't demonstrate the framework's applicability to other disease models (e.g., survival models with competing risks as mentioned in Section 2).

3. **Implementation Details:** The paper could provide more concrete guidance on practical implementation challenges, such as how to handle the high dimensionality of belief spaces in real-world applications with large patient populations.

4. **Comparison to Alternative Methods:** While the paper contrasts with prior screening frameworks, it could better address how the proposed method compares to alternative machine learning approaches (e.g., reinforcement learning with neural networks) that have been applied to similar problems.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis and well-executed empirical validation. The approximation error bound (Proposition 1) is particularly strong. The authors are careful to address both strengths (personalization to patient features/history) and limitations (simplifying assumptions, computational complexity).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical formulation is precise, though the PBVI adaptation might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with POMDPs. The empirical section clearly explains the dataset, model estimation, and evaluation metrics. The graphical representation of personalization (Figure 1) is particularly effective.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant contribution by providing the first general framework for personalized screening that accommodates non-Markov disease processes and external information. It goes beyond prior works [7,9,10] by removing restrictive assumptions about disease progression and test outcomes. The integration of external information processes is particularly novel and addresses an important gap in the literature.

**Significance:** The problem of personalized screening has substantial real-world impact, with potential to improve healthcare outcomes while reducing costs. The demonstrated 28-68% reduction in screenings represents a meaningful improvement over current clinical practices. The framework's flexibility to accommodate different delay cost structures (linear vs. quadratic) highlights its adaptability across disease contexts, making it broadly applicable.

**Recommendation:** I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper addresses an important problem with a technically sound and well-motivated solution that advances the state of the art in personalized healthcare. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate clear practical significance. While there are some limitations (mainly the simplifying disease model assumptions), these are acknowledged by the authors and do not undermine the core contribution. The paper would benefit from slightly more discussion of how the framework could be extended to more complex disease models, but this is a minor point given the significant contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel framework for personalized screening that handles general disease models and external information; strong theoretical foundation with error bounds; impressive empirical results showing significant reduction in screenings; clear clinical relevance.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Limited to two-state disease model in empirical validation; some simplifying assumptions (no false positives) that may limit applicability to all disease contexts.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of healthcare optimization with clear potential for real-world impact. The work is rigorous, well-executed, and addresses an important gap in personalized screening methodology.
