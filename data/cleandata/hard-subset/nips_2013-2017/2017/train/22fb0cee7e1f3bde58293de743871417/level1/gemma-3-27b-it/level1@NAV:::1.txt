## Review of DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

This paper tackles the important problem of personalized screening policies, moving beyond the limitations of traditional, static approaches. The core idea is to model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and develop a computationally tractable framework for constructing screening policies that adapt to patient features and screening history. This work builds on previous research in personalized medicine and decision-making under uncertainty, particularly relating to POMDPs and survival analysis (e.g., [7, 9, 10]). However, it distinguishes itself by addressing a broader class of disease models and incorporating external information sources (like self-exams) not typically considered. 

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The proposed approach, DPSCREEN, is a significant step towards practical personalized screening. The theoretical framework is well-developed, including a computational tractability analysis and approximation error bounds. The application to breast cancer data, leveraging the Gail model and observational data, provides a concrete example and demonstrates promising results – a 28-68% reduction in screens with comparable delay. The inclusion of robustness analysis addressing model estimation error is a valuable addition. The clear presentation of the method and the logical flow of the paper are also strengths.

However, the paper isn't without weaknesses. The reliance on a Markovian structure for the disease progression, while acknowledged as a simplification, could limit its applicability to diseases with more complex dynamics. The assumption of accurate test outcomes (no false positives/negatives) is also a strong one, potentially impacting real-world performance. While the computational complexity is discussed, a more detailed analysis, perhaps including empirical results with varying dataset sizes, would be helpful. The discussion of the cost function and its influence (Figure 2) feels somewhat disconnected from the main application to breast cancer. Finally, while the related works section is comprehensive, it could benefit from more direct comparison with recent advancements in reinforcement learning for healthcare. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a clinically relevant and important problem.
*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound framework.
*   Demonstrates promising empirical results with real data.
*   Includes robustness analysis and error bounds.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Relies on simplifying assumptions (Markovian disease progression, accurate tests).
*   Computational complexity could be a limitation for large-scale applications.
*   The connection between cost function analysis and the main example could be strengthened.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The technical contributions are substantial, and the empirical results are encouraging. While some limitations exist, the benefits of the proposed framework outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more detailed discussion of computational complexity and a more thorough comparison to recent RL-based approaches.
