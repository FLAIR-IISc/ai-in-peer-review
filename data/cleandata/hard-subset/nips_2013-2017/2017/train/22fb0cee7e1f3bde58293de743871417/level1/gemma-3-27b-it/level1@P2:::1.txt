This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for constructing personalized screening policies, addressing a crucial problem in healthcare. The core idea of modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process, incorporating external information, and handling computational intractability through approximation is sound and represents a significant step forward compared to existing approaches. The application to breast cancer screening with a real-world dataset strengthens the paper.

**Summary of the Paper & Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper tackles personalized screening, a field where existing work (e.g., [7, 9, 10]) often relies on strong Markovian assumptions or simplified disease models.  DPSCREEN relaxes these assumptions, offering a more general framework applicable to a wider range of diseases.  The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of decision theory, POMDPs, and controlled sensing ([18-21, 34, 38]), acknowledging the limitations of standard methods for this specific problem. They distinguish themselves by handling non-Markovian disease processes, incorporating external information, and addressing computational complexity. The work builds upon established concepts in survival analysis ([11-13, 36, 37]) by integrating them into a dynamic screening policy framework.  Connections to related work on disease modeling (e.g., MISCAN-COLON [46]) and clinical guidelines are appropriately made.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Generality:** The framework is more general than existing approaches in its ability to handle non-Markovian disease progression and external information.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The development of an approximately optimal policy with bounded approximation error (Proposition 1) is a significant theoretical contribution.
*   **Practical Application:** Applying the framework to a large breast cancer dataset demonstrates its feasibility and potential impact.
*   **Personalization:** The results clearly show personalization based on patient features *and* screening history, leading to a reduction in the number of screenings without compromising detection delay.
*   **Robustness Analysis:** The discussion of estimation error and its impact on policy performance is valuable.
*   **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The motivation for the work is well established. The discussion of the framework, though dense, is logically structured.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** While the authors address computational intractability, the stated complexity (O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)) remains high and could limit scalability to very complex disease models or larger datasets. More detail on actual runtime performance with the breast cancer dataset would be helpful.
*   **Model Assumptions:** Several assumptions are made (e.g., independence of test results given state trajectory, accurate test outcomes). While standard, their impact on the robustness of the policy isn't thoroughly investigated. A sensitivity analysis to these assumptions would strengthen the paper.
*   **Clarity in Certain Sections:** The mathematical formulation is dense and could be made more accessible. For instance, the meaning of several notations (e.g., ~s, B̄) is not immediately apparent and requires careful tracing back through the text.  More intuitive explanations of the Bellman equations would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to existing clinical policies is valuable, but the scope is somewhat limited. Comparing against other state-of-the-art personalized screening approaches (even if they have stronger assumptions) would provide a more comprehensive benchmark.
*   **Lack of Discussion on Generalizability:**  The breast cancer example is strong, but the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the applicability of the framework to *other* diseases, and what modifications might be necessary. While they briefly mention quadratic delay costs, a more in-depth exploration of different disease characteristics would be useful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a significant problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The results demonstrate the potential for substantial improvements in screening efficiency and personalization.
*   The work is well-motivated, clearly positioned within the existing literature, and rigorously analyzed.
*   The breast cancer application provides a compelling proof-of-concept.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The high computational complexity may limit the practical applicability of the method.
*   The reliance on several simplifying assumptions requires further investigation of their impact on robustness.
*   Areas of the mathematical presentation lack clarity and accessibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field. While the computational complexity and certain clarity issues are concerns, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and potential impact of the work outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should address the clarity issues in the mathematical formulation, provide more detail on computational performance, and consider expanding the discussion on generalizability to other diseases. A sensitivity analysis of the key assumptions would also be beneficial.
