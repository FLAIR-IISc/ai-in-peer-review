- Proposal: DPSCREEN framework for personalized screening that balances screening frequency against delay in disease detection using a finite-state stochastic disease model with an absorbing disease state and external triggers.

- Main contribution: Adaptation of point-based value iteration (PBVI) to construct computationally tractable approximately optimal screening policies with theoretical approximation error bounds.

- Empirical validation: Large breast cancer dataset shows 28–68% reduction in screenings versus current clinical policies while maintaining the same expected detection delay.

- Relation to prior work: Extends personalized screening literature by handling non-Markov disease processes and external information that prior methods [7,9,10] could not.

- Distinction from controlled sensing: Addresses more complex, non-stationary disease progression models common in medical applications, differentiating it from standard controlled sensing approaches.

- Strength — Technical soundness: Provides rigorous theory (Lemma 1 convexity, Proposition 1 error bound), detailed computational complexity analysis, and a well-motivated PBVI adaptation.

- Strength — Empirical impact: Demonstrates clinically meaningful reductions in screenings with clear benchmarks against annual and biennial policies.

- Strength — External information integration: Novelly incorporates external information processes (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger earlier visits, improving realism.

- Strength — Comprehensive evaluation: Thoroughly studies the effect of different delay cost functions (linear vs. quadratic) on policy design.

- Weakness — Simplifying assumptions: Assumes no false positives and uses a two-state healthy/disease model, which may not capture complex disease progression.

- Weakness — Limited empirical scope: Validation is restricted to a two-state breast cancer model; synthetic experiments don't fully show applicability to other disease models like competing risks or survival models.

- Weakness — Implementation guidance: Lacks concrete practical guidance for handling high-dimensional belief spaces in large real-world patient populations.

- Weakness — Comparison to alternatives: Could better compare to other ML approaches (e.g., reinforcement learning with neural networks) applied to similar problems.

- Quality: Technically rigorous and well-executed with a strong approximation error bound and careful acknowledgment of limitations.

- Clarity: Well-organized and clearly written with precise mathematical formulation, though PBVI adaptation may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with POMDPs.

- Originality: Significant contribution as the first general framework to accommodate non-Markov disease processes and integrate external information in personalized screening.

- Significance: High real-world impact potential by reducing screenings and adapting to different delay cost structures, making it broadly applicable across diseases.

- Recommendation: Strong accept — important problem, technically sound solution, and compelling empirical results.

- Arguments for acceptance: Novel framework handling general disease models and external info, strong theoretical foundations, impressive empirical reductions, and clear clinical relevance.

- Arguments against acceptance: Empirical validation limited to a two-state model and simplifying assumptions (e.g., no false positives) that may limit general applicability.
