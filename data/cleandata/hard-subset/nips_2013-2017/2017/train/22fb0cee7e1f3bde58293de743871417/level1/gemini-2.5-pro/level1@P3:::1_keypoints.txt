- Proposes DPSCREEN: a generalized POMDP framework for personalized, dynamic disease screening minimizing screening and delayed-detection costs.  
- Key capabilities: handles arbitrary finite-state (non‑Markovian) disease models, non‑stationary arrivals, and trajectory-dependent costs.  
- Computational approach: approximate solution via Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) with a two-stage belief sampling strategy (sample representative trajectories, then explore reachable beliefs).  
- Theoretical contributions: extension of Bellman equations, proof that value function is convex piecewise-linear, approximation error bounds and robustness to model estimation error.  
- Empirical validation: applied to a 45,000-patient breast cancer dataset showing 28–68% fewer screenings for comparable detection delay.  
- Positions itself as a substantial generalization of prior POMDP screening work (e.g., Ayer et al. 2015, Erenay et al. 2016).  
- Strength — significance/novelty: addresses an important real-world problem with broader modeling assumptions than prior work.  
- Strength — technical quality: rigorous formulation, well-motivated PBVI adaptation, and useful theoretical guarantees.  
- Strength — clarity: well-written, well-organized, with effective visualizations demonstrating personalization.  
- Strength — experiments: large-scale, convincing empirical improvements and analyses across risk groups and cost functions.  
- Weakness — generality vs. experiments: experiments use a simple two-state Markov model, not fully exercising claimed non‑Markovian generality.  
- Weakness — learning complex models: lacks discussion on practical challenges of estimating trajectory distributions Pr(~s|x) for non‑Markovian disease models.  
- Weakness — scalability/practicality: missing specifics on time horizon T, number of trajectories K, belief points B, and overall runtimes.  
- Weakness — belief sampling details: Algorithm 1 needs more detail on action exploration, sampling strategy, and how clinical policies guide sampling.  
- Weakness — theoretical assumptions: important guarantees rely on strong assumptions (e.g., perfect test accuracy, bounded intervals) that should be acknowledged and discussed.  
- Acceptance reasons: novel, significant, technically sound, and backed by strong empirical results on real data.  
- Rejection reasons: experiments don’t fully validate generality and practical implementation/scalability details are sparse.  
- Overall conclusion: strong paper with minor addressable issues; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
