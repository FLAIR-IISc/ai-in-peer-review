- Paper introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized dynamic screening that balances screening frequency/costs against delayed-detection costs via sequential decision-making under uncertainty.
- Contribution: a general finite-state disease progression model that includes an external information process triggering unscheduled visits and trajectory-dependent cost functions.
- Contribution: a computationally tractable PBVI-based approximation with a two-step belief sampling strategy (sample likely trajectories, explore reachable beliefs).
- Contribution: theoretical results giving an upper bound on approximation error and robustness to small model-estimation errors.
- Contribution: empirical validation on a large breast-cancer dataset showing 28–68% fewer screenings for the same expected detection delay, with personalization by static risk and dynamic history.
- Quality assessment: technically sound formulation and sensible PBVI approximation; clever belief-sampling adaptation for high-dimensional trajectory beliefs.
- Quality assessment: theoretical guarantees add rigor, though assumptions (e.g., perfect tests, convex costs) are strong.
- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized, with clear model presentation, logical method exposition, useful figures, and reproducibility details in appendices.
- Minor clarity suggestion: report concrete computational-complexity details and runtimes (e.g., values for T, K, B) to clarify scalability.
- Originality: high — combines non-Markovian dynamics, unscheduled arrivals, and trajectory-dependent costs in a unified framework, extending prior POMDP work.
- Significance: high — potential large societal and clinical impact if empirical gains translate to practice; broadly applicable to other diseases.
- Pros: addresses a high-impact problem, offers a more general and realistic model, presents a sound algorithmic approach, and shows strong empirical results.
- Cons: experiments use a simpler two-state Markov disease model that undercuts practical generality claims; theoretical assumptions may not match experimental settings.
- Cons: some model-estimation details (e.g., lump growth parameters) are taken from literature or under-detailed, leaving sensitivity unclear.
- Conclusion: strengths substantially outweigh weaknesses; paper is an excellent, impactful contribution and is strongly recommended for acceptance.
