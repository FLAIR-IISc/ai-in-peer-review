This paper presents DPSCREEN, a novel and general framework for constructing personalized, dynamic screening policies for diseases. The authors model disease progression as a finite state stochastic process and uniquely incorporate an "external information process," such as patient self-examinations, which can trigger unscheduled clinical visits. The core problem is to balance the frequency of screening (cost) against delays in detection (harm). Recognizing that an exact solution to this complex, non-standard POMDP formulation is intractable, the authors propose a computationally feasible approximation based on Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) over a carefully sampled belief space. This work extends prior research in medical decision-making (e.g., Ayer et al., 2012), which was often restricted to simpler Markovian disease models, by providing a more flexible framework applicable to a broader class of disease dynamics and observation models.

The paper has several significant strengths.
**Quality:** The work is technically sound, providing a rigorous problem formulation and a well-justified approximation algorithm. The inclusion of a theoretical approximation error bound (Proposition 1) and a robustness analysis concerning model estimation errors (Theorem 1) significantly strengthens the paper's contributions.
**Significance:** The potential impact is very high. The application to a large breast cancer dataset demonstrates impressive results, suggesting a 28-68% reduction in screenings compared to clinical standards without increasing detection delay. This is a substantial potential improvement in public health efficiency and patient experience. The framework's generality suggests it could be adapted to many other chronic diseases.
**Originality:** While leveraging the known PBVI algorithm, its application to this novel formulation—particularly the integration of an external information process that affects the timing between decision epochs—is a novel and important contribution. The ability to handle non-Markovian disease models is a key differentiator from previous work.

The paper also has minor weaknesses.
**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but it is dense. Some key details of the belief sampling and value iteration algorithms are in the appendix, making full comprehension from the main text challenging.
**Quality:** The computational complexity is high, which may be a practical barrier to deployment, though the authors rightly note the potential for parallelization.

Arguments for acceptance:
[+] Addresses a highly significant, real-world problem in personalized medicine.
[+] Proposes a novel, general framework that advances the state of the art beyond more restrictive models.
[+] Provides strong empirical validation on a large dataset, showing substantial practical benefits.
[+] Theoretical analysis of approximation error and robustness adds rigor.

Arguments against acceptance:
[-] The core approximation method is an adaptation of an existing algorithm, albeit in a novel context.
[-] High computational cost could limit real-world applicability without significant engineering.

Overall, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. This is a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the field of machine learning for healthcare.
