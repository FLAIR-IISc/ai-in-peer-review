Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces DPSCREEN, a dynamic, personalized screening framework that selects follow-up times to balance screening burden against delay in disease detection. Diseases are modeled as finite-state stochastic processes with an absorbing disease state, and the framework explicitly incorporates (i) patient-specific risk features and (ii) a dynamic history that includes prior test outcomes and external information (e.g., self-exams). Exact optimization is intractable; the authors prove piecewise-linear convexity of the value function in beliefs and construct an approximately optimal policy via a point-based value iteration scheme over a sampled, lower-dimensional belief space. They provide complexity analysis and an approximation-error bound that shrinks with the density of belief-space coverage. A robustness result shows policy recommendations are locally invariant to small model-estimation errors.

Relative to prior OR and medical screening literature, the paper generalizes beyond Markov/stationary assumptions on disease and test processes common in POMDP-based screening (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Erenay et al.), and beyond simulation-only comparisons of fixed policy sets (USPSTF/CISNET-style). It subsumes survival and first-hitting-time models, incorporates external information that can trigger early arrivals, and handles trajectory-dependent delay costs. Empirically, DPSCREEN is instantiated for breast cancer using a large Athena Health dataset (45k patients), showing substantial reductions (28–68%) in screenings at comparable expected detection delay, with and without self-exams, and across different delay-cost shapes.

Strengths
- Clear technical contribution: a general, tractable approach to dynamic personalized screening that fuses patient features and full screening histories, with proofs of value-function structure, an explicit PBVI-based algorithm, complexity analysis, and a bound on approximation error.
- Personalization is substantive: policies adapt both to baseline risk (via features/Gail score) and to evolving evidence (test outcomes, self-exams), and the paper isolates the impact by stratifying low vs high-risk groups and with/without external information.
- Strong empirical validation: large real-world dataset (45k patients; ~84k mammograms), realistic modeling choices (mammogram sensitivity/specificity, lump growth model), and substantial efficiency gains are demonstrated without increasing expected detection delay.
- Robustness insight: local constancy of optimal actions under small model perturbations supports the practicality of learning a model then optimizing against it.
- Broad applicability: the modeling and algorithmic setup applies to dynamic survival, hitting-time, and other finite-state trajectories; experiments also illustrate sensitivity to disease-specific delay costs (linear vs quadratic), reinforcing that disease type matters.
- Well written and well situated: the exposition is concise yet thorough, with appropriate citations across OR, controlled sensing, and clinical guidelines; the graph-model perspective and supplementary algorithms aid reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions for improvement
- Evaluation relies on a learned disease model: while standard in this literature, there are no off-policy evaluation or sensitivity analyses to model misspecification beyond the theoretical robustness statement. Suggest adding empirical sensitivity to key parameters (e.g., transition hazards, test characteristics) and/or partial-identification bounds.
- Generalizability: experiments focus on women aged 60–65 and one disease. A second empirical case (e.g., colorectal or cardiac) or at least a semi-synthetic study under a non-Markov/semi-Markov disease trajectory would better showcase the promised breadth.
- Computational aspects: complexity is analyzed, but empirical runtime, scaling with K (trajectory samples) and |B̄| (reachable beliefs), and the impact of the action-space restriction (max follow-up interval) are not reported. Include timing and memory profiles and guidance on choosing K/|B̄|.
- Ablations to isolate personalization effects: beyond risk-stratified results, it would help to compare (a) feature-only personalization, (b) history-only personalization, and (c) both (full DPSCREEN) to quantify the contribution of each component.
- Assumptions on diagnosis and tests: parts of the theory assume no false positives/negatives to simplify analysis, whereas the empirical model uses realistic sensitivity/specificity at the mammogram stage and confirmatory biopsy. Clarify which assumptions apply where, and discuss incorporating diagnostic errors into the theoretical guarantees.
- Policy-selection procedure: tuning the weight w to match a target delay is practical, but a constrained optimization view or presentation of the Pareto frontier (tests vs delay) would make the trade-off clearer for clinicians/policymakers.
- Fairness/ethics: personalization can reduce screenings for low-risk groups. Consider reporting subgroup analyses (e.g., by race, density) to ensure equity and avoid disparate impact.

Quality
Technically sound. Claims are supported by rigorous derivations (value-function structure, PBVI adaptation, approximation and robustness results) and careful experiments with realistic parameters. Some empirical sensitivity and computational performance reporting would strengthen the evaluation.

Clarity
Well organized and clearly written. The mathematical exposition is concise yet complete, with a clean formulation of beliefs over trajectories and diagnosis status. Minor suggestions: collect key assumptions in a single subsection; add a brief intuition box for Theorem 1; include an illustrative toy example of belief updates.

Originality
High. The formulation with exogenous external information triggering arrivals, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and general disease/test processes goes beyond prior POMDP screening work and typical simulation comparisons. The PBVI tailoring to a trajectory-belief space is novel in this context.

Significance
High. Personalized, dynamic screening is clinically relevant and resource-critical. Demonstrated 28–68% reductions in expected screens at equal delay suggest real-world impact. The framework is general enough to influence future research and practice across multiple diseases.

Pro acceptance
- General, principled framework with theoretical guarantees.
- Strong real-world validation showing substantial efficiency gains without worse detection delay.
- Clear, well-motivated, and positioned relative to relevant literature.
- Practical algorithm with complexity analysis and robustness insights.
- Demonstrates benefits of personalization and of modeling external information.

Con acceptance
- Single-disease empirical case and limited age range.
- Lack of empirical sensitivity analyses and computational scaling results.
- Some simplifying assumptions not fully reconciled with clinical realities.
- Limited ablations to disentangle personalization components.

Overall assessment
A well-executed and impactful paper that advances the state of the art in personalized screening. With minor additions on sensitivity, scalability, and broader validation, it would be even stronger. Recommendation: accept.
