- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized breast cancer screening that balances screening costs against detection delay costs.  
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and incorporates external information (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger earlier visits.  
- Extends beyond prior work by handling more general (non-Markov/stationary) disease and test-outcome dynamics.  
- Provides a computationally tractable approximation with theoretical error bounds.  
- Demonstrates large empirical gains: 28–68% reduction in screening frequency while keeping expected detection delays equivalent.  
- Technically sound with clear theoretical foundations (e.g., Lemma 1 showing convexity of the value function).  
- Validated on a large real-world breast cancer dataset (Athena Health Network).  
- Personalization mechanism effectively adapts screening schedules to initial risk and dynamic test results.  
- Strengths: broadly applicable framework, novel incorporation of external information processes, and strong practical results.  
- Addresses estimation error and model robustness, increasing clinical relevance.  
- Minor weakness: computational complexity analysis could be better contextualized for real-world implementation.  
- Suggests discussing potential implementation barriers in resource-limited healthcare settings.  
- Overall assessment: high quality, clear, original, and impactful contribution likely to influence personalized screening protocols.
