This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a general framework for constructing dynamic, personalized screening policies that minimize a weighted sum of screening costs and detection delays. The disease process is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; tests are performed at clinician visits and an external information process (e.g., self-examinations, comorbidities) can trigger earlier-than-scheduled arrivals. The decision problem departs from standard POMDP/POSMDP settings because (i) the time between decisions is endogenous (depends on recommended follow-up dates, the external information process, and the disease trajectory) and (ii) the delay cost depends on the full trajectory rather than the current state. The authors formulate Bellman equations over beliefs defined on trajectory-label pairs and show that the value function is convex and piecewise-linear. Exact alpha-vector computation is intractable; they propose a point-based value iteration (PBVI) approximation that constructs a lower-dimensional, reachable belief set via sampling likely disease trajectories and action exploration (including clinical policies). They provide a bound on the approximation error in terms of the density of belief sampling and establish a robustness result: the optimal action mapping is locally constant in model-belief space outside a lower-dimensional set, implying stability to small estimation errors. 

Empirically, DPSCREEN is instantiated for breast cancer using a de-identified dataset of ~45k women (60–65y, ~84k mammograms). The disease is modeled as two-state (H/D) with feature-dependent initial/transition probabilities learned from data (features via Gail model); the external information is a self-exam lump-size process driven by a universal tumor growth model plus noise. Using sensitivity/specificity from the literature and monthly self-exams, the authors tune the Lagrangian weight to match the expected delay of current guidelines and report large reductions in expected number of screenings: 57–68% with self-exams and 28–45% without, for low- and high-risk strata, while keeping expected delays comparable. They also show how policies differ under linear vs quadratic delay costs, highlighting disease-specific cost design. The framework personalizes both to baseline risk and to dynamic histories (e.g., adverse recent tests, external info shocks).

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to POMDP-based screening for mammography and colorectal cancer (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Erenay et al.), Bayesian design approaches (Rizopoulos; Verdinelli), and simulation-based guideline development (e.g., USPSTF, MISCAN-COLON). It also relates to controlled sensing under partial observability. Unlike prior work, DPSCREEN explicitly handles trajectory-dependent delay costs, endogenous arrivals via external information, and does not restrict the disease process to Markov/semi-Markov in the theory (although the breast cancer case is Markov). Compared to simulation-based comparisons of a fixed policy set, DPSCREEN optimizes over policies and provides (admittedly coarse) approximation guarantees.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The modeling and Bellman formulation are careful, and the piecewise-linear value function result is consistent with POMDP theory. The PBVI-style approximation is appropriate given intractability. The error bound, while useful, depends on an unspecified constant and sampling density; the robustness theorem establishes generic stability but without rates. Some assumptions (no false positives/negatives in parts of the theory; bounded maximum screening interval; large T) are strong and may limit realism of the guarantees.
- Empirical support: The breast cancer study is substantial and aligns with clinical intuition: higher-risk and worse-history patients screen more often; external information improves efficiency. However, evaluation is via a learned simulator (standard in this area) and relies on weight tuning to match baseline delays; uncertainty (confidence intervals), sensitivity to misspecification, and runtime/scale details are limited.

Clarity:
- The manuscript is generally well written and organized. The modeling choices (state/observation/external info, arrivals) and deviations from standard POMDPs are clearly articulated. Pseudocode and supplementary details are referenced. Some aspects would benefit from clarification: consistency of “no false positives” with the use of literature sensitivity/specificity; how w is tuned for the constraint; exact settings of K, B, W, computational budget, and wall-clock runtimes.

Originality:
- The problem formulation with endogenous arrival times plus trajectory-dependent costs, incorporation of external information, and a tailored PBVI with reachable-belief construction is novel in the screening context. Establishing piecewise-linear convexity and alpha-vector recursion in this nonstandard setting is a nontrivial extension. The theoretical robustness/approximation components are incremental but valuable.

Significance:
- Personalized, dynamic screening is clinically important; the framework is broadly applicable across diseases/models. The reported reductions in screening burden at matched delay are meaningful and could impact policy design. The algorithmic ideas (belief sampling over trajectories, handling exogenous information-triggered visits) should be of interest to the NeurIPS community working on sequential decision-making under uncertainty.

Strengths:
- General, flexible formulation capturing key clinical realities (external info, trajectory costs).
- Sound dynamic programming treatment and clear connection to alpha-vector methods.
- Tractable approximation with an interpretable error bound and robustness insight.
- Large, realistic empirical study with substantial improvements over guidelines.
- Persuasive personalization to both baseline risk and history.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Assumptions used in theory (no false positives/negatives; bounded intervals) should be relaxed or their empirical impact quantified; provide sensitivity analysis to test accuracy and to growth/noise parameters.
- Provide uncertainty estimates (CIs) for E[N], E[Δ], and ablations (with/without external info are reported; add without history personalization).
- Expand baselines: risk-/density-based intervals, ConfidentCare, and alternative RL/POMDP approximations.
- Describe computational footprint (K, B, W, |Z|, |Y|, runtimes, parallelization gains) and practical deployment guidance (e.g., limiting action space).
- Clarify the mapping from constrained problem to weight w and discuss potential duality gaps; consider reporting Pareto frontiers.
- Discuss generalization beyond two-state disease models and to multiple tests with different costs.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a principled, general framework.
- Provides both theoretical structure and practical algorithms with strong empirical gains.
- Clear writing and good positioning within prior literature.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Some theoretical assumptions are unrealistic for clinical screening; guarantees are qualitative.
- Evaluation is simulation-based on a learned model with limited uncertainty quantification and baseline breadth.
- Computational and implementation details are insufficiently documented for full reproducibility.

Recommendation: Weak accept. Confidence: High.
