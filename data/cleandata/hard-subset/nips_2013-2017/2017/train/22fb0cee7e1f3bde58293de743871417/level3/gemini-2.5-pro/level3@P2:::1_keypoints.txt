- Introduces DPSCREEN, a computationally tractable framework for dynamic, personalized screening that balances screening costs against delayed detection costs.
- Models disease as a finite-state stochastic process with clinician-scheduled screenings based on features, past tests, and external triggers.
- Generalizes beyond prior POMDP approaches to handle non‑Markovian dynamics, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and action-dependent arrival processes.
- Uses Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) approximation by sampling representative belief points to construct an approximately optimal value function.
- Provides theoretical guarantees including an approximation error bound (Proposition 1) and robustness to model estimation errors (Theorem 1).
- Demonstrates strong empirical gains on a real-world breast cancer dataset: 28–68% fewer screenings without increasing expected detection delay.
- Uses a large dataset (45,000 patients) and appropriate clinical baselines, showing personalized policies differ by risk level and cost structure.
- Clear, well-organized exposition with intuitive illustrations and precise mathematical presentation.
- Technical contribution is novel and significant for ML in healthcare and sequential decision-making.
- Weakness: model estimation from observational EHR data is challenging and sensitivity to estimation errors needs more empirical analysis despite theoretical results.
- Weakness: computational complexity is high; practical scalability limits (max T or K feasible) should be discussed.
- Weakness: choice of weight parameter w is critical; guidance (e.g., Pareto trade-off curves) for real clinical selection would improve applicability.
- Overall assessment: strong, novel, technically rigorous, well-validated work with minor clarifications needed; strong recommendation for acceptance.
