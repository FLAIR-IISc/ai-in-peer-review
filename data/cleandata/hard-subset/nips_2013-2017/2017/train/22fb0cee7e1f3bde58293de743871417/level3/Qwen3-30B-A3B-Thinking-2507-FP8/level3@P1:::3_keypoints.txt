- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized screening that balances screening costs against delay costs in disease detection.  
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and incorporates external information processes (e.g., self-exams).  
- Key innovation: a computationally tractable approximate policy construction using point-based value iteration on a reduced belief space.  
- Generalizes prior POMDP screening methods to handle dynamic survival and first-hitting-time disease models.  
- Differs from medical literature by optimizing policies instead of comparing fixed policy sets via simulation.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical guarantees: an approximation error bound (Proposition 1) and robustness to model estimation errors (Theorem 1).  
- Assumptions are well-justified and supplementary materials supply necessary technical detail.  
- Empirical results on a 45,000-patient breast cancer dataset show 28–68% fewer screenings while maintaining the same expected detection delay.  
- Presentation is clear and precise, with effective illustrations of how screening adapts to patient features and history (Figure 1).  
- Originality: accommodates arbitrary disease models, external information, and disease-specific cost functions.  
- Significance: potential for substantial cost savings and reduced patient burden; broadly applicable beyond breast cancer.  
- Strengths: novel framework, strong theoretical foundation, significant empirical improvements, clear exposition, and practical implementation considerations.  
- Minor weaknesses: evaluation limited to breast cancer, assumes no false positives, and computational complexity may challenge very large real-time systems.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, original, and significant — strong case for acceptance despite minor issues.
