This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The approach incorporates both patient screening history and disease-relevant patient features for personalization, and is applicable to a wide range of disease models.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with a clear and concise mathematical exposition. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of their approach and demonstrate its effectiveness on a large real-world dataset of breast cancer patients. The results show that DPSCREEN can reduce the number of screenings performed while maintaining the same delay in detection of disease, with significant efficiency gains over existing screening policies.

The paper makes a clear technical contribution with potential practical impact, and the authors have done a good job of situating their work within the relevant literature. The use of breast cancer data to illustrate the approach is particularly effective in demonstrating the potential benefits of personalized screening policies.

Strengths:

* The paper introduces a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balances the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease.
* The approach incorporates both patient screening history and disease-relevant patient features for personalization.
* The paper provides a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of the approach.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of DPSCREEN on a large real-world dataset of breast cancer patients.
* The results show significant efficiency gains over existing screening policies.

Weaknesses:

* The paper assumes a specific disease model (a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state), which may not be applicable to all diseases.
* The approach relies on accurate estimation of model parameters, which can be challenging in practice.
* The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other personalized screening frameworks.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper makes a clear technical contribution with potential practical impact.
* The approach is well-motivated and situated within the relevant literature.
* The use of breast cancer data to illustrate the approach is effective in demonstrating the potential benefits of personalized screening policies.
* The results show significant efficiency gains over existing screening policies.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper assumes a specific disease model that may not be applicable to all diseases.
* The approach relies on accurate estimation of model parameters, which can be challenging in practice.
* The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other personalized screening frameworks.

Overall, I believe that the paper makes a significant contribution to the field and should be accepted for publication. However, I would like to see the authors address the weaknesses mentioned above in future work.
