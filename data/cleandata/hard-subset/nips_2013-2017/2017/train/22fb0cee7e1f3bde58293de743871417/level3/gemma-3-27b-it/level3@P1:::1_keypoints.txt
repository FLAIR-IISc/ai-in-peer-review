- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized screening policies that balances early detection benefits with screening costs/risks.  
- Proposes a computationally tractable method to optimize individualized screening schedules using patient features and screening history.  
- Overcomes limitations of prior work by handling non-Markovian disease progression and adaptive policies.  
- Motivates practicality via limitations of current clinical guidelines and increased EHR availability.  
- Situates work within decision theory, personalized medicine, and disease modeling literature and differentiates from prior stationary or disease-specific models.  
- Incorporates external information (e.g., self-exams) and addresses the computational intractability of optimal policies.  
- Presents a clear model formulation using belief states and a point-based value iteration (PBVI) approximation.  
- Provides analysis of computational complexity and a proposition bounding approximation error.  
- Acknowledges and discusses the impact of parameter estimation error for real-world application.  
- Paper is well-organized with accessible notation and effective visualizations (e.g., personalization figure).  
- Empirical results on a large breast cancer dataset show reduced screening frequency with comparable detection delays.  
- Synthetic experiments demonstrate the importance of tailoring policies to different cost functions.  
- Originality lies in integrating stochastic modeling, Bayesian updating, and PBVI for dynamic personalized screening.  
- Significant potential impact: improved healthcare outcomes, resource allocation, and reduced patient burden across diseases.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong technical contribution, practical relevance, generality, clarity, and tackling key challenges.  
- Arguments against acceptance: implementation may require expertise and data; relies on assumptions (accurate tests, cost functions) needing further robustness analysis.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept—minor concerns outweighed by contributions and real-world potential.
