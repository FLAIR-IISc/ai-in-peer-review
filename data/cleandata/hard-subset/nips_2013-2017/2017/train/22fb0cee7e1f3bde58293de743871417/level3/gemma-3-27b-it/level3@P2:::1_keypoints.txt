- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process to optimize personalized screening schedules.  
- Addresses intractability of exact optimal policies by developing a computationally tractable approximation.  
- Demonstrates significant improvements over existing clinical policies on a large breast cancer dataset.  
- Well-motivated discussion of limitations of current screening approaches and need for personalization.  
- Thorough literature positioning and extensive referencing, citing relevant POMDP and joint modeling work.  
- Clear, rigorous model formulation and derivation of the approximation based on point-based value iteration.  
- Includes computational complexity analysis and robustness discussion (Theorem 1) supporting practical applicability.  
- Lemma 1 shows piecewise linear convexity of the value function, strengthening the approximation approach.  
- Strong experimental validation using 45,000 patients from Athena Health Network lends credibility.  
- Results show 28–68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining equivalent detection delays.  
- Analysis of different cost functions (linear vs. quadratic) and personalization effects (risk-based and result-based adjustments).  
- Presentation of results (Table 1 and Table 2) effectively communicates DPSCREEN benefits.  
- Novelty: provides a generalized personalized screening approach that surpasses prior method limitations.  
- Technical soundness: rigorous mathematical and algorithmic contributions with theoretical support.  
- Practical relevance: real-world dataset and demonstrated clinical improvements indicate high impact potential.  
- Clarity and completeness: well-written, organized, with complexity, robustness, and literature coverage.  
- Weakness: specific implementation assumptions (e.g., two-state Markov model) may limit generalizability.  
- Weakness: scalability and computational cost for larger datasets or more complex models remain uncertain.  
- Weakness: modeling of external information (e.g., self-exams) is somewhat simplified and could be refined.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant advance, substantial technical contributions, clear clinical impact, and strong presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: model assumptions and computational costs could limit generalizability and adoption.  
- Overall assessment: high-quality, novel, technically sound paper with promising real-world results; strong recommendation for acceptance.
