- Introduces DPSCREEN, a dynamic personalized screening framework that balances screening costs against delayed disease-detection costs.  
- Models disease as a finite-state stochastic process and uniquely incorporates external information (e.g., patient self-examinations) that can trigger unscheduled visits.  
- Formulates an optimization problem that is computationally intractable in general and develops a tractable approximation algorithm based on Point-Based Value Iteration.  
- Generalizes prior personalized screening work by moving beyond restrictive Markovian assumptions and adding patient-initiated appointment mechanisms.  
- High significance: addresses an important healthcare problem with potential to reduce invasive/costly screenings without worsening outcomes.  
- Strong technical quality: rigorous formulation, appropriate approximation algorithm, and theoretical performance/robustness analysis.  
- Compelling empirical results on a large real-world breast cancer dataset showing 28–68% fewer screenings for the same expected detection delay.  
- Originality: novel general framework and explicit inclusion of an external information process increases realism over prior work.  
- Clarity: well-written and clearly organized presentation of a complex model and solution.  
- Main limitation: practical application depends on accurate estimation of underlying disease-progression models from observational (noisy/messy) data.  
- Additional concern: need for more discussion on practical scalability to diseases with more states or longer time horizons.  
- Overall verdict: strengths far outweigh weaknesses; strong recommendation for acceptance.
