- Presents DPSCREEN, a general framework to construct dynamic, personalized screening policies using a finite-state disease progression model with an absorbing disease state.
- Formulates screening as minimizing a weighted sum of screening costs and delayed-detection costs, accommodating non-Markovian models, trajectory-dependent costs, and external triggers.
- Exact solution is intractable; authors propose an approximate solver using Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) on sampled trajectories and reachable belief points.
- Demonstrates the framework on a breast cancer task derived from real-world data, reporting 28–68% fewer screenings for equal expected detection delay versus annual/biennial policies.
- Paper is well-written and clearly organized, with helpful figures illustrating personalization based on prior risk and history.
- Technical adaptation of PBVI is solid; includes a belief-point sampling strategy and theoretical results (approximation error bound and robustness analysis).
- Main practical weakness: assumes a reliable patient-specific disease model is known—paper treats learned models as ground truth and does not address model-estimation uncertainty sufficiently.
- Limited methodological novelty: frames the problem as a POMDP and applies established PBVI techniques, so algorithmic contributions are incremental.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: comparisons only to fixed-interval baselines rather than other adaptive/data-driven methods.
- Experimental robustness is lacking: no simulation-based sensitivity analysis showing performance under model misspecification despite only local theoretical stability results.
- Overall: interesting, well-executed work with promising results but constrained by strong modeling assumptions, limited novelty, and insufficient robustness validation.
