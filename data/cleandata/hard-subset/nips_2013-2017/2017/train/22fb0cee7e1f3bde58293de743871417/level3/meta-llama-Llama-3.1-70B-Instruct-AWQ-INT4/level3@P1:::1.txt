**Summary of Main Ideas**

The paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The method incorporates both patient screening history and disease-relevant patient features for personalization. The disease is modeled as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, allowing for the possibility of external information that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach using a large real-world dataset of 45,000 patients undergoing breast cancer screening.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper situates itself within the context of previous work on screening policies, including POMDP-based approaches and Bayesian design methods. However, the authors highlight the limitations of these existing methods, such as their reliance on specific models for disease progression and test outcomes, and their inability to incorporate external information. In contrast, DPSCREEN is designed to be more flexible and adaptable to different disease models and settings.

**Strengths**

1. **Technical soundness**: The paper provides a thorough and concise mathematical exposition of the DPSCREEN methodology, including a computational complexity analysis.
2. **Personalization**: The approach incorporates both patient screening history and disease-relevant patient features, allowing for personalized screening policies that yield efficiency gains over existing policies.
3. **Validation**: The authors validate their approach using a large real-world dataset, demonstrating improvements attributable to personalization.
4. **Technical contribution**: The paper makes a clear technical contribution with potential practical impact, as it provides a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Computational complexity**: While the authors provide a computational complexity analysis, the worst-case computation time of the algorithm may be high, particularly for large datasets.
2. **Model assumptions**: The paper assumes that the disease model is known, which may not always be the case in practice. The authors acknowledge this limitation and discuss the potential impact of estimation error on the exactly optimal policy.
3. **Limited generalizability**: While the authors validate their approach using a large dataset, it is unclear how well the methodology will generalize to other disease models and settings.

**Originality**

The paper presents a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that incorporates both patient screening history and disease-relevant patient features. The approach is well-motivated and situates itself within the context of previous work on screening policies.

**Significance**

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of screening policies, as it provides a flexible and adaptable methodology for constructing personalized screening policies. The approach has the potential to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of screening policies, leading to better health outcomes and reduced healthcare costs.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated methodology for constructing personalized screening policies.
