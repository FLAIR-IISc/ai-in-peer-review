

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized breast cancer screening policies that balances screening costs against delay costs in disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating external information (e.g., self-examinations) that may trigger earlier patient visits. Unlike prior work that restricted to Markov disease processes [7,9] or stationary test outcome models, DPSCREEN handles more general disease dynamics while providing a computationally tractable approximation with theoretical error bounds.

The paper demonstrates significant empirical improvements over clinical standards: reducing screening frequency by 28-68% while maintaining equivalent expected detection delays. The approach is well-motivated and technically sound, with clear theoretical foundations (Lemma 1 establishing convexity of the value function) and rigorous validation on a large breast cancer dataset from Athena Health Network. The personalization mechanism (Figure 1) effectively shows how screening schedules adapt to both initial risk profiles and dynamic test results, addressing a key gap in existing screening frameworks [10,7].

Strengths include the general framework applicable to multiple diseases, the incorporation of external information processes (a novelty over prior POMDP-based approaches), and strong empirical results with practical significance. The paper also carefully addresses estimation errors and model robustness, which enhances its clinical relevance.

A minor weakness is that the computational complexity analysis (O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)) could be more thoroughly contextualized against real-world implementation constraints. While the paper shows 28-68% reductions in screenings, it would be valuable to briefly discuss potential implementation barriers in resource-limited healthcare settings.

The work makes a strong contribution to the field: it's technically rigorous, addresses an important healthcare problem with meaningful practical impact, and advances beyond previous screening frameworks by accommodating more realistic disease dynamics and external information. The results demonstrate significant improvements over current clinical practices, with clear potential for influencing breast cancer screening protocols. The paper meets all four criteria: high quality (sound theory and experiments), excellent clarity (well-organized with helpful illustrations), strong originality (first framework for this general disease model), and significant impact (substantial cost reduction with maintained efficacy). This is a valuable contribution to the literature on personalized healthcare decision-making.
