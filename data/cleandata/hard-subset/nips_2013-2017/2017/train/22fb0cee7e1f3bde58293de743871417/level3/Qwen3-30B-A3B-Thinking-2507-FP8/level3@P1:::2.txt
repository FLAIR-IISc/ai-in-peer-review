

This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against delays in disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating external information (e.g., patient self-examinations) that may trigger early visits to clinicians. The problem of finding the exact optimal screening policy is shown to be computationally intractable, so the authors propose a computationally tractable approximate solution based on point-based value iteration for POMDPs.

The paper makes several contributions. First, it extends standard POMDP formulations to handle delay costs that depend on state trajectories rather than just current states, and incorporates external information processes that trigger early visits. Second, it provides theoretical guarantees on the approximation error of their proposed solution. Third, they demonstrate their approach on breast cancer screening using a large dataset from Athena Health Network, showing that their personalized policy reduces the number of screenings by 28-68% compared to annual screening while maintaining similar expected delays in detection.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The theoretical formulation is sound, with careful attention to modeling details and assumptions. The experimental results are presented clearly, with appropriate comparisons to clinical practice. The authors address the key challenges of personalized screening: disease progression modeling, external information incorporation, and the computational intractability of optimal policies.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with a strong theoretical foundation. The experimental validation on a real-world dataset provides convincing evidence of the approach's potential benefits. The paper appropriately acknowledges limitations, including the need for accurate disease model estimation.

Regarding originality, while the core approach builds on existing POMDP techniques, the extension to handle disease progression with absorbing states and external information processes represents a meaningful contribution to the problem of personalized screening. However, the novelty is somewhat limited as the methodological approach (point-based value iteration) is well-established in the POMDP literature. The authors could have better contextualized their work against recent advances in POMDPs and related decision-theoretic approaches.

The significance of this work is substantial, as personalized screening has the potential to reduce unnecessary medical procedures while maintaining effective disease detection. The demonstrated reductions in screening frequency (28-68%) compared to standard annual screening could have meaningful implications for healthcare costs and patient burden. The authors also highlight how the optimal screening policy varies depending on the choice of delay cost function, emphasizing the importance of disease-specific modeling.

However, the paper has several important limitations that affect its practical relevance. The most significant is the unrealistic assumption that the disease model parameters are known, whereas in practice, these would need to be estimated from observational data. The authors acknowledge this in Section 3.3 but do not adequately address how estimation errors affect the policy's performance. The experimental evaluation only compares against a simple annual screening baseline without assessing robustness to model misspecification or evaluating against more sophisticated clinical guidelines.

The paper also lacks thorough comparison with other personalized screening approaches. While the authors briefly mention related work in Section 5, they don't provide a detailed comparison with other personalized screening frameworks beyond stating their method is more general. A more comprehensive evaluation against other state-of-the-art approaches would strengthen the significance claim.

The paper demonstrates a promising application of decision-theoretic methods to healthcare, but the practical impact is limited by the lack of robustness analysis to model uncertainty. The authors acknowledge this gap but don't provide a convincing argument for why their approach would work well in practice despite model misspecification.

Pro arguments for acceptance: Well-structured theoretical framework, clear experimental validation on a meaningful healthcare problem, potential for real-world impact in reducing unnecessary screenings, strong mathematical foundation.

Con arguments against acceptance: Limited methodological novelty, unrealistic assumption about known disease models, insufficient evaluation of robustness to model uncertainty, limited comparison with other personalized screening approaches.

Overall, this is an interesting and reasonably well-executed paper that addresses an important healthcare problem. While the methodological contribution is modest, the practical implications are significant. The work would be stronger with more thorough evaluation of robustness to model misspecification and comparison with other personalized screening approaches. The paper demonstrates a promising application of decision-theoretic methods to healthcare, but the lack of practical validation prevents it from being fully convincing as a contribution to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the evaluation of robustness and contextualize the novelty more clearly.
