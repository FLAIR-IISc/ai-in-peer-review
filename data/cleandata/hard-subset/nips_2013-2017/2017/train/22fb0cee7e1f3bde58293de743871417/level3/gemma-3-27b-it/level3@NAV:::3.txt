## Review of DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for constructing personalized breast cancer screening policies that dynamically adapt to individual patient features, screening history, and external information. The core idea—modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and optimizing screening schedules to balance cost and delay—is a valuable contribution. The work builds upon existing decision-theoretic approaches to screening (e.g., [7, 9]) but significantly expands their applicability by relaxing assumptions of Markovian disease progression and stationary test outcomes, addressing limitations highlighted in the introduction. 

The strength of this paper lies in its ability to formulate a complex problem—personalized screening—into a tractable optimization framework. The proposed Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) approximation, combined with a clever sampling strategy for belief spaces, allows for computation even with high-dimensional state spaces.  Empirical results using a large breast cancer dataset are compelling, demonstrating substantial reductions in screening frequency (28-68%) while maintaining comparable delays to current clinical policies. The personalization aspect, illustrated in Figure 1, effectively showcases how the policy adapts based on both initial risk and subsequent test results. The authors also thoughtfully discuss robustness to model estimation error and the impact of different cost functions. 

However, the paper could benefit from further clarity in certain areas. While the mathematical formulation is sound, the connection between the abstract model and the specific implementation details for breast cancer (e.g., the choice of parameters for the lump growth model) could be more clearly articulated.  The sensitivity of the results to the various modeling assumptions (e.g., the accuracy of the Gail model) deserves more attention. Furthermore, while the comparisons to current clinical practices are valuable, a more in-depth comparison with other personalized screening strategies, even if less theoretically grounded, would strengthen the work. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** Addresses a crucial problem (personalized screening) with a novel, generalizable framework.
* **Technical Soundness:**  Provides a mathematically rigorous solution with a well-defined approximation scheme.
* **Empirical Validation:** Demonstrates significant improvements over existing clinical practices on a real-world dataset.
* **Significance:** Offers a promising path towards more efficient and patient-centric screening strategies.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Complexity:** The mathematical formulation might be challenging for readers outside of the core ML/OR community.
* **Assumptions:** The reliance on specific modeling choices (e.g., the tumor growth law) warrants further scrutiny.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with significant potential impact.  The strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon minor revisions to enhance clarity and discussion of modeling assumptions.
