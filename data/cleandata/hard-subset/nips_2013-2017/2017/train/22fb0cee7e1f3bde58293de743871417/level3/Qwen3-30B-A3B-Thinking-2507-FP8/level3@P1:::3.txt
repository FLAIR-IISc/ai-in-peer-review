

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against delay costs in disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating external information processes (e.g., patient self-examinations) that can trigger earlier clinic visits. The key innovation is a computationally tractable method for constructing approximately optimal screening policies, addressing the intractability of exact solutions through point-based value iteration on a reduced belief space.

The paper makes significant contributions over prior work. Unlike previous POMDP-based screening approaches [7,9] that assume stationary Markov disease processes and test outcomes, DPSCREEN accommodates more general disease models including dynamic survival models [11-13,36-37] and first hitting time models [7,9,14-17]. The framework also differs from medical literature approaches [44-48] that rely on simulation-based comparisons of fixed policy sets rather than optimizing the policy itself. The authors clearly articulate these distinctions in their related work section.

The technical contributions are strong. The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees including a bound on approximation error (Proposition 1) and demonstrates robustness to model estimation errors (Theorem 1). The authors carefully justify their assumptions about the model structure and test outcomes, and the supplementary materials provide necessary technical depth. The empirical evaluation using a large breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients) demonstrates significant improvements: 28-68% reduction in screening frequency compared to current clinical policies (annual/biennial screening) while maintaining the same expected delay in disease detection.

The paper excels in clarity and presentation. The model formulation is precise and well-motivated, with careful definitions of all components. The authors effectively illustrate their personalization mechanism through Figure 1, showing how screening intervals adapt to both patient features (e.g., risk group) and dynamic history (e.g., test results). The writing is exceptionally clear, making complex theoretical concepts accessible to a broad audience.

The originality is substantial. The framework generalizes beyond previous approaches by accommodating arbitrary disease models and external information processes. The paper properly contextualizes its contribution against prior work, noting that existing methods [7,9,10] make restrictive assumptions about disease progression and test outcomes that are not satisfied by many real-world diseases. The authors also highlight the importance of disease-specific cost functions (e.g., linear for breast cancer vs. quadratic for pancreatic cancer), a critical insight for practitioners.

The significance is compelling. The demonstrated reductions in screening frequency would lead to substantial cost savings and reduced patient burden in healthcare systems. The framework's flexibility to accommodate different disease models and cost structures makes it broadly applicable beyond breast cancer. The paper also provides valuable practical guidance for choosing appropriate cost functions based on disease characteristics.

Strengths include the novel framework, strong theoretical foundation with error bounds, significant empirical results, clear exposition, and careful consideration of practical implementation challenges. The paper addresses an important healthcare problem with meaningful scientific contributions.

Minor weaknesses include the empirical evaluation being limited to breast cancer (though the framework's generality is well-justified), and the assumption of no false positives in test results (which could be relaxed in future work). The computational complexity, while reduced, might be challenging for real-time implementation in very large healthcare systems, though the authors note it can be parallelized.

The paper strongly addresses NIPS's criteria: it's technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written, original in its approach to personalized screening, and significant for both the machine learning community and healthcare applications. The authors have carefully considered the limitations and provided appropriate justifications for their assumptions.

The main arguments for acceptance are the paper's novel framework that generalizes beyond previous screening approaches, strong theoretical guarantees, significant empirical improvements over clinical practices, and clear presentation. The minor weaknesses do not substantially undermine the paper's contributions.

This is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to both the machine learning and healthcare domains. The framework has clear potential for real-world impact, and the methodological advances will likely inspire further work in personalized decision-making under uncertainty. The paper aligns well with NIPS's focus on principled approaches to complex decision problems.
