This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing personalized screening policies that dynamically adjust to patient features and screening history. The authors model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, aiming to balance the trade-off between screening frequency and detection delay. Recognizing the computational intractability of finding an exactly optimal policy, they propose a computationally tractable approximation algorithm. The method is illustrated using a breast cancer dataset, demonstrating reductions in screening frequency (28-68%) while maintaining comparable detection delays to existing clinical policies.

This work builds upon a substantial body of research in screening and personalized medicine. Prior work in operations research has often focused on population-based screening schedules, lacking individualization. Recent POMDP-based approaches (Ayer et al., 2017; Maillart et al., 2017) and Bayesian design methods (Rizopoulos et al., 2017) have explored personalized screening, but these often rely on restrictive assumptions regarding disease models and test outcome processes (e.g., Markovian disease progression, stationary test results). DPSCREEN distinguishes itself by offering a more general framework applicable to a wider range of disease models and incorporating external information (e.g., patient self-exams). The authors appropriately acknowledge related work in controlled sensing (Alaa et al., 2017; Krishnamurthy, 2017) and decision theory (Smallwood & Sondik, 1973).

The strengths of this paper lie in its clear presentation, well-defined problem formulation, and the development of a computationally feasible approach to a challenging problem. The model is sufficiently general to encompass a variety of disease scenarios, and the demonstration on a breast cancer dataset provides a concrete illustration of the method's potential. The inclusion of a robustness analysis, albeit limited, is commendable. The authors carefully discuss the limitations of observational data and address model estimation challenges, adding to the paper's credibility. The mathematical formulation is rigorous and the proposed algorithm, while complex, is described in sufficient detail.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The primary limitation is a lack of significant methodological novelty. While the combination of elements – the general stochastic process model, external information integration, and approximate optimization – is reasonable, none of these components represent a breakthrough. The algorithm, while efficient, builds upon existing techniques like point-based value iteration and doesn't introduce fundamentally new algorithmic contributions. A more substantial comparison with existing personalized screening approaches, beyond the trivial annual screening baseline, is needed. 

Furthermore, the reliance on known disease models is a significant practical drawback. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't adequately address how the framework would perform with imperfectly estimated models. The robustness analysis presented is insufficient to convince readers of the method's real-world applicability. Sensitivity analyses to variations in model parameters (e.g., transition probabilities, hazard rates) are crucial to demonstrate the policy's resilience to model misspecification. The experiments lack this sensitivity analysis. The performance improvement over the annual screening baseline, while positive, might not be particularly compelling given the added complexity of the proposed method. A comparison to more sophisticated baseline policies, or a demonstration of superior performance in scenarios with more complex disease dynamics, would strengthen the findings.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in personalized medicine.
*   Presents a well-defined and mathematically sound framework.
*   Demonstrates a potential reduction in screening frequency while maintaining detection delays.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited methodological novelty.
*   Reliance on unrealistic assumption of known disease models.
*   Insufficient robustness analysis and sensitivity to model misspecification.
*   Limited comparison to more sophisticated baseline policies.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically sound and well-written paper that addresses a relevant problem. However, the lack of significant novelty and the limited practical validation raise concerns. While the approach shows promise, the current form is unlikely to substantially advance the state of the art. I recommend **rejecting** the paper in its current form, but encouraging the authors to address the identified weaknesses – particularly focusing on robustness analysis, comparison to more sophisticated baselines, and exploration of model estimation – before resubmission. It has the potential to be a valuable contribution with further development.
