Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the design of dynamic, personalized screening policies that balance screening burden against delay in disease detection. It models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, augments it with an external information process (e.g., self-exams, comorbidities) that can trigger early clinical arrival, and defines the objective as a weighted sum of expected discounted screening costs and delay costs. The setting departs from standard POMDP/POSMDP formulations because (i) decision epochs are endogenous (depend on recommendations, external information, and latent trajectory), and (ii) costs depend on state trajectories (delay) rather than only the current state.

The paper formulates a belief-state dynamic program, shows the value function is convex and piecewise linear in beliefs, and characterizes the optimal policy via alpha-vectors, but notes exact computation is intractable. It proposes DPSCREEN, a point-based value iteration approach tailored to high-dimensional belief spaces by sampling likely latent trajectories and reachable beliefs under a mix of random and clinically motivated policies. The authors provide a bound on performance loss in terms of belief sampling density and argue local policy stability under small model estimation error. Empirically, they estimate a disease model from a large breast cancer dataset (Athena Health), incorporate a stylized model of self-examination/lump growth, and report that DPSCREEN reduces the expected number of screens by 28–68% relative to annual or biennial screening while matching expected detection delays, with stronger reductions when self-exams are included. They also illustrate how different delay-cost shapes (linear vs quadratic) alter the aggressiveness of screening.

This builds on prior work on POMDP-based screening (Ayer et al., Maillart et al.), dynamic survival/joint models for personalized scheduling (Rizopoulos et al.), and simulation-based guideline development (e.g., MISCAN-COLON). The algorithmic core leverages known POMDP structure (piecewise linearity; PBVI), applying it to a more general screening model with external information and trajectory-dependent costs.

Evaluation along review criteria

Quality
- The modeling framework is carefully laid out, and the belief-DP is well motivated. The convex/piecewise-linear property and alpha-vector formulation follow standard POMDP arguments; the extension to endogenous arrivals is plausible and useful.
- The approximation algorithm is reasonable: sampling likely trajectories and reachable beliefs is a standard but practical PBVI strategy. The complexity and parallelization discussion are helpful.
- The theoretical approximation bound depends on a problem-dependent constant and sampling density; while informative, it is loose and offers limited actionable guidance on how much sampling is enough. The local stability result for model estimation error is reassuring but does not quantify error propagation in realistic misspecification.
- Empirical evaluation estimates a breast cancer model and assesses policies on the learned model. This is standard in the screening literature, but it raises concerns about optimism under misspecification. No empirical sensitivity analyses are provided.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. The problem formulation, belief updates, and role of external information are well explained. Some technical details, including algorithms, are deferred to the supplement; this is acceptable, though a brief in-paper schematic of the DP recursion and action space would help.
- Minor suggestions: clarify assumptions about false positives/negatives (diagnosis assumed error-free when z ∈ Z+), and explicitly distinguish “screening test” vs “diagnostic confirmation.” Define the range and interpretation of w and how constraints on delay are enforced via tuning w.

Originality
- The application of PBVI with tailored belief sampling to this screening setting, including explicit modeling of external information and trajectory-dependent delay costs, is a meaningful extension. However, the core algorithmic components (alpha-vectors, PBVI, belief sampling) are known, and related POMDP-based screening has appeared in OR/biostatistics venues. Novelty is thus moderate.

Significance
- Personalized screening is important. The empirical results suggest substantial reductions in screening frequency at fixed expected delay, which could have real impact.
- However, significance is limited by evaluation choices: comparison only to annual/biennial screening and lack of robustness analysis to model misspecification, adherence, or more realistic diagnostic error models. Without stronger baselines and sensitivity studies, practical readiness is uncertain.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem with a general formulation that accommodates external patient-driven arrivals and trajectory-dependent costs.
- Provides a tractable approximation with some theoretical backing.
- Clear exposition; results illustrate personalization both by features and by history.
- Uses a sizable real dataset for model estimation and demonstrates large reductions in expected screening counts.

Cons
- Methodological novelty is incremental relative to established POMDP/PBVI techniques.
- Assumes the disease and observation models are known/estimated and then evaluates on the same model; no empirical robustness to misspecification or model uncertainty.
- Comparisons are limited to simple annual/biennial baselines rather than prior adaptive policies (e.g., Ayer/Maillart; joint modeling approaches; ConfidentCare).
- Some modeling assumptions are optimistic (error-free final diagnosis, stylized self-exam process, no nonadherence), and their impact is not assessed.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are the policy and its performance to errors in pin(x), ptr(x), the lump growth parameters, and the self-exam sensitivity/specificity? Can you provide stress tests (e.g., ±20–50% perturbations, alternative hazard models)?
- How does DPSCREEN compare against existing adaptive POMDP screening policies (Ayer, Maillart) or joint-model-based scheduling (Rizopoulos) when instantiated on your dataset/model?
- Can your framework accommodate false positives/negatives in diagnosis (z ∈ Z+), and what is the impact on results?
- How does nonadherence (missed or delayed screenings) and imperfect early-arrival behavior affect policy performance?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add robustness analyses to model misspecification and observation noise; report detection-delay and screening counts under multiple plausible models.
- Compare against stronger baselines, including published POMDP/joint-model policies and risk/density-based interval recommendations.
- Provide ablations (with/without external information; different belief sampling strategies) and timing/scaling results.
- Clarify the mapping from the weighted objective to a constrained delay target, and report performance across a range of w.
- Consider releasing code and a synthetic benchmark to facilitate replication.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper tackles an important problem with a sensible and well-presented approach and promising preliminary results. However, the methodological novelty is limited, and the empirical validation lacks robust baselines and sensitivity analyses needed to establish practical relevance and generality. Strengthening the experimental section along the suggested lines would substantially improve the case for acceptance. Confidence: medium.
