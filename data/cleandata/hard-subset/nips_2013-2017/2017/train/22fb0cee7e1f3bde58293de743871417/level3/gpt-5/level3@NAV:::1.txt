This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a general framework for dynamic, personalized screening that optimizes the trade-off between screening frequency and detection delay across a broad class of disease models. The authors formulate screening as a belief-state control problem with endogenous arrivals driven by both clinician-initiated tests and patient-initiated visits via external information (e.g., self-exams). Unlike prior POMDP-based screening work (e.g., Ayer et al.; Maillart et al.), DPSCREEN handles non-Markov disease trajectories, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and exogenous information processes, thereby moving beyond common stationarity and Markov assumptions. The paper establishes piecewise-linear convexity of the value function and proposes a tractable PBVI-style approximation on a sampled, reachable belief set, with computational complexity characterization and an approximation bound. Robustness to model estimation error is argued via local constancy of optimal actions. Empirically, the method is validated on a large real-world dataset (45k Athena Health patients), showing 28–68% fewer screens at comparable expected detection delay, with clear gains attributable to personalization based on both features and screening history. The framework is also explored under distinct delay-cost models, underscoring disease-specific policy regimes.

Quality: The work is technically careful and largely sound. The mathematical exposition is concise yet thorough, with clear Bellman formulations, complexity analysis, and an approximation error bound. One caveat is that some theoretical guarantees assume perfect tests (no FP/FN), while the empirical section uses realistic sensitivities/specificities; clarifying how guarantees degrade under noisy tests would strengthen the story. The model-based evaluation approach is standard in this literature but could benefit from sensitivity analyses and calibration checks.

Clarity: The paper is well written and well organized. Suggestions: clarify scaling of the belief space with trajectory sampling K and reachable set size B, and report ablations on K/B/W versus accuracy/runtime; provide more details on tuning w for constrained objectives.

Originality: High. The endogenous-arrival, trajectory-cost setting and belief construction over sampled trajectories offer a novel, generalizable alternative to disease-specific simulators and standard POMDP formulations.

Significance: Strong practical relevance and potential impact on screening policy design; results are convincing and likely to spur follow-on work.

Pros for acceptance:
- General, principled framework with clear technical contribution.
- Solid theory (structure, complexity, approximation bound, robustness).
- Large-scale real-world validation; substantial efficiency gains without delaying detection.
- Personalization demonstrably matters.

Cons for acceptance:
- Theoretical guarantees rely on stronger test assumptions than the empirical setting.
- Model-based evaluation risks residual misspecification; limited sensitivity analyses.
- Code/implementation details and ablations could be expanded for reproducibility.
