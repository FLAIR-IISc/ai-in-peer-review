

**Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"**

This paper proposes a framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against delay in disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating external information (e.g., self-examinations) that can trigger earlier patient visits. They develop a computationally tractable approximate solution to the otherwise intractable optimal screening policy problem, validated on a breast cancer dataset showing 28-68% reductions in screenings while maintaining the same expected delay in disease detection compared to standard annual screening policies.

The work extends previous POMDP-based screening approaches (Ayer et al. 2014, Maillart et al. 2015) by not restricting to Markov disease processes or stationary test outcome models. While prior works assume specific disease models (Markov processes for breast/colon cancer), the authors' framework accommodates more general disease dynamics. The paper also relates to controlled sensing literature (Krishnamurthy 2017), though it generalizes beyond structural assumptions like stationarity.

**Strengths:**
1. Well-written, clearly organized, and technically sound with precise mathematical formulation.
2. The theoretical contribution is solid, with a proof that the value function is convex and piecewise linear, and a bound on the approximation error.
3. The experimental results are promising, demonstrating significant reductions in screenings (28-68%) compared to standard annual screening.
4. The personalization approach—adapting screening frequency based on both patient features (risk factors) and dynamic history (previous test results)—is clinically intuitive and well-explained.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The key limitation is the unrealistic assumption that the disease model is known. In practice, the primary challenge would be estimating the disease model from observational data, which the paper addresses superficially in Section 3.3 without adequate depth for establishing practical relevance.
2. The experiments only compare to a trivial annual screening baseline, omitting more sophisticated clinical policies (e.g., biennial screening for low-risk patients) that would provide meaningful context for the contribution.
3. The paper lacks sensitivity analysis to evaluate the policy's robustness to model misspecification, which is critical for real-world deployment.
4. Methodological novelty is limited; the core approach (POMDP with point-based value iteration) is standard in the literature, with the main contribution being extension to a more general setting rather than fundamentally new techniques.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The problem of personalized screening is important and relevant to healthcare applications.
- The paper provides a clear generalization of existing POMDP frameworks for screening.
- The results demonstrate potential for reducing unnecessary screenings, with promising numerical gains.
- The paper is well-written and technically rigorous.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Practical relevance is undermined by the unrealistic assumption of known disease models.
- The experimental comparison is weak, lacking meaningful baselines beyond annual screening.
- The paper does not adequately address the critical challenge of model estimation from observational data.
- Methodological novelty is incremental rather than transformative.

**Overall assessment:**
This is a well-executed paper that addresses an important problem with a technically sound approach. The results are promising, but the paper's contribution is limited by its focus on the optimization problem without properly addressing the data challenges that would make the approach practical. The lack of meaningful baseline comparisons and insufficient analysis of model estimation challenges significantly reduce the paper's impact.

**Recommendation:** Weak reject (3)

**Additional comments:**
The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Providing a detailed discussion of how the disease model would be estimated from observational data in practice
2. Comparing against more sophisticated screening baselines (e.g., risk-based screening policies)
3. Conducting sensitivity analysis to evaluate robustness to model misspecification
4. More clearly articulating the methodological novelty beyond extending existing POMDP frameworks

While the problem is important and the technical execution is good, the paper fails to establish sufficient practical relevance for NIPS. The authors should address the model estimation challenge more thoroughly to demonstrate how their framework would work in real-world clinical settings. Without this, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative.
