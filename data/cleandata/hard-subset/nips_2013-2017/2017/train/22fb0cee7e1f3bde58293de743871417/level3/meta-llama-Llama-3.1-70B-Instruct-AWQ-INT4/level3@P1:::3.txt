This paper proposes a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The authors develop a framework to construct screening policies for a large class of disease models, which is applicable to many diseases, including cancer, cardiovascular disease, and HIV. The framework takes into account the patient's features, the dynamic history of the patient, and external information that may trigger the patient to arrive at the clinician earlier than scheduled screenings.

The authors model the disease as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and use a partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) to solve for the optimal screening policy. However, they note that the standard decision theory methods used in screening cannot be applied to their problem due to the complexity of the disease model and the need to consider external information. Instead, they propose a computationally effective procedure that solves for an approximately optimal policy and provide bounds for the approximation error.

The authors illustrate their approach using a large breast cancer data set and show that their constructed policy leads to significant reductions in the number of screens performed while achieving the same expected delays in disease detection. They also demonstrate the personalization of their policy to individual patients' features and dynamic history.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The development of a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that can be applied to a wide range of diseases.
* The use of a POMDP to solve for the optimal screening policy, which takes into account the complexity of the disease model and external information.
* The provision of bounds for the approximation error, which provides a guarantee on the performance of the approximately optimal policy.
* The empirical evaluation of the approach using a large breast cancer data set, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the method in reducing the number of screens performed while maintaining the same expected delays in disease detection.

The weaknesses of the paper include:

* The computational complexity of the algorithm, which may limit its applicability to large-scale problems.
* The need for further evaluation of the approach using different disease models and data sets to demonstrate its generalizability.
* The assumption of a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, which may not be applicable to all diseases.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of personalized medicine and screening policies. The proposed framework and algorithm have the potential to improve the effectiveness and efficiency of screening policies for a wide range of diseases.
