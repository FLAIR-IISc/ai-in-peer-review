This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel and general framework for constructing dynamic and personalized screening policies for a wide class of diseases. The proposed method formulates the problem as an optimization that balances screening frequency against delays in disease detection. Crucially, it personalizes recommendations based on both static patient features and dynamic screening history. This work builds upon prior research in personalized screening, such as POMDP-based approaches (Ayer et al., 2012), but significantly generalizes them by accommodating arbitrary finite-state stochastic disease models, rather than being restricted to Markovian processes. It also formally incorporates external information processes (e.g., self-examinations) that can trigger unscheduled clinical visits, a feature often overlooked in previous models.

The paper’s strengths are numerous and substantial:

*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound, presenting a complete and rigorous approach. The mathematical exposition is clear and thorough, including a Bellman formulation for the problem, a tractable point-based value iteration (PBVI) approximation algorithm, and formal analysis of computational complexity and approximation error. The authors are careful to address the practical issue of robustness to model estimation errors.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem, situates the contribution within the relevant literature, and walks the reader through the model and proposed solution effectively.

*   **Originality & Significance:** The primary contribution is a unified, computationally tractable framework for a problem of immense practical importance. The ability to handle a broad class of disease models and external information sources is a significant advance. The significance is powerfully demonstrated through validation on a large real-world dataset of 45,000 patients. The results, showing a 28-68% reduction in screenings without increasing detection delay compared to standard clinical policies, are highly compelling and suggest a strong potential for real-world impact in improving healthcare efficiency and patient outcomes.

The paper has very few weaknesses. One minor point for consideration is that while robustness to model estimation error is proven theoretically, the experimental validation could be strengthened by including a sensitivity analysis on the learned disease model parameters. However, this does not detract from the core contribution.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a clear technical contribution to an important problem. It is well-executed, thoroughly validated, and has high potential significance. I strongly recommend acceptance.
