- Presents a framework for personalized screening policies balancing screening costs and delayed detection for diseases modeled as finite-state stochastic processes.  
- Proposes a computationally tractable, approximately optimal policy construction with demonstrated effectiveness on a breast cancer dataset.  
- Shows significant reductions in screening frequency while maintaining comparable detection delays versus annual screening.  
- Properly situates the work within literature on disease progression (Markov models, survival analysis) and controlled sensing.  
- Model is general, accommodates diverse disease dynamics, and incorporates patient-specific risk factors and screening history.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees on approximation error (Proposition 1).  
- Clear, well-organized presentation with helpful illustrations and detailed model/algorithm descriptions.  
- Experiments demonstrate personalization by risk/history and concrete screening reductions on real data.  
- Major limitation: strong assumption of known disease models, which limits practical applicability.  
- Insufficient sensitivity analysis and robustness checks for model misspecification.  
- Comparison baseline (annual screening) is too simplistic; stronger clinical or simulation comparisons needed.  
- Limited exploration of alternative cost functions to show broader applicability.  
- Methodological novelty is incremental—components largely build on existing techniques though integration is well-executed.  
- Overall assessment: technically solid but practically limited; rated as weak accept with recommendation for more validation on model uncertainty and broader evaluations.
