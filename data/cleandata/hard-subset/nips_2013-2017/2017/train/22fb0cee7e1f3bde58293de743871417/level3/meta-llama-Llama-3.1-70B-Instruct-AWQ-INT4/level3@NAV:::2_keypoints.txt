- Proposes a method (DPSCREEN) for personalized screening that trades off screening cost against delay in disease detection.  
- Models disease as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and allows external information to trigger unscheduled clinician visits.  
- Demonstrates on breast cancer data that personalized policies can substantially reduce screenings while maintaining the same detection delay.  
- Addresses a significant practical problem: personalization of screening based on patient characteristics and disease progression.  
- Methodology is clearly explained and well-developed, making the approach accessible to experts.  
- Empirical use of real breast cancer data strengthens the practical relevance of the work.  
- Results are promising, showing large screening reductions without increasing detection delay.  
- Core assumption that each patient’s disease model is known is unrealistic and undermines practical applicability.  
- Methodological novelty is limited because the approach builds on existing POMDP literature.  
- Experiments are weakly comparative, only benchmarked against a trivial annual baseline.  
- Paper lacks robustness analysis: no assessment of sensitivity to model misspecification or uncertainty.  
- Paper is technically sound, claims are supported by theory and experiments, and it is well-organized and reproducible for experts.  
- Overall recommendation: accept conditional on addressing limitations—justify/relax the known-model assumption and provide robustness/sensitivity evaluations.
