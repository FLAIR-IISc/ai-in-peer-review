- Proposes a novel methodology for personalized screening policies balancing screening cost and delay cost in disease detection.  
- Models disease as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state.  
- Incorporates external information (self-exam, comorbidities) that can trigger unscheduled clinician visits.  
- Uses breast cancer data to illustrate and validate the proposed approach.  
- Develops a computationally tractable construction of an approximately optimal policy.  
- Employs a point-based value iteration (PBVI) algorithm to compute the approximate policy.  
- Provides bounds on the approximation error to quantify performance guarantees.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly explained, and well-motivated.  
- Gives a detailed model description and problem formulation aiding understanding of complexity.  
- Reviews related work comprehensively, covering POMDP and Bayesian design frameworks and their limitations.  
- Empirical results show fewer screenings while maintaining equal detection delay compared to existing clinical policies.  
- Assumes model parameters are known, which may not hold in practice.  
- Evaluation is limited to breast cancer data and lacks tests on other cancer types.  
- Does not sufficiently discuss methodological limitations (e.g., finite-state assumption).  
- Overall recommendation: paper is a significant, tractable contribution and is recommended for acceptance despite noted limitations.
