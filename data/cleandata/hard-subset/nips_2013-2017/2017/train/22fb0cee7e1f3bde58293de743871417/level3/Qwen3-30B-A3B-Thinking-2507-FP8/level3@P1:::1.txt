

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the frequency of medical screenings against the delay in disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating both patient-specific features (age, family history, etc.) and dynamic patient history (including test results and external information such as self-examinations). The key technical contribution is a computationally tractable method for approximating the optimal screening policy, which addresses the intractability of exact solutions through a point-based value iteration approach with provable approximation error bounds.

The paper situates itself well within the literature, clearly distinguishing its approach from previous works that either assume stationary Markov disease processes (e.g., [7, 9]), ignore external information sources (e.g., [10]), or rely on simulation-based comparisons of fixed policy sets (e.g., [44-48]). The authors effectively demonstrate that their framework accommodates a broader class of disease models, including dynamic survival models and first hitting time models, while incorporating the critical element of patient-triggered early visits based on external information.

The technical contribution is strong and well-executed. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of the problem, including the Bellman equations for the optimal policy, and establish a key theoretical result (Lemma 1) showing the value function is convex and piecewise linear. They then develop a practical approximation algorithm (DPSCREEN) that samples relevant belief states to construct an approximately optimal policy with bounded error (Proposition 1). The computational complexity analysis is thorough, and the authors provide concrete implementation details in the supplementary materials.

The empirical evaluation on a large breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients from Athena Health) is compelling. The results demonstrate significant efficiency gains: DPSCREEN reduces the number of screenings by 28-68% compared to current clinical policies (annual or biennial screening) while maintaining the same expected delay in disease detection. The personalization aspect is particularly well-demonstrated through Figure 1, which shows how the policy adapts to both patient risk profiles (ex ante survival curves) and dynamic history (test results and external information shocks). The authors also conduct a thoughtful analysis of how different cost structures (linear vs. quadratic delay costs) lead to different screening regimes, highlighting the importance of appropriately modeling disease-specific costs.

The paper excels in clarity and completeness. The writing is precise and well-organized, with a thorough mathematical exposition that would allow an expert to reproduce the results. The related work section is comprehensive and properly contextualizes the contribution. The authors also thoughtfully address the robustness of their approach to model estimation errors (Theorem 1) and validate their method across multiple disease model types.

Strengths:
- Clear problem statement with strong motivation from real-world clinical practice
- Novel technical contribution addressing a fundamental limitation of previous approaches
- Thorough theoretical analysis with provable approximation guarantees
- Rigorous empirical validation on large real-world dataset
- Strong demonstration of personalization effects and efficiency gains
- Comprehensive related work comparison showing clear differentiation from prior work
- Well-organized and clearly written with sufficient detail for reproducibility

Weaknesses:
- The paper could benefit from more explicit discussion of potential clinical implementation challenges (e.g., how to integrate with existing EHR systems)
- While the approximation error bound is theoretically sound, the empirical validation of this bound across different sampling densities could be strengthened
- The paper focuses primarily on breast cancer; while they discuss the generalizability to other diseases, more concrete examples of application to different disease models would strengthen the argument

The significance of this work is substantial. Personalized screening has the potential to significantly reduce healthcare costs while maintaining or improving patient outcomes, and this paper provides a theoretically sound and practically implementable framework for achieving this. The demonstrated 28-68% reduction in screenings represents a major efficiency gain over current clinical practices, which is particularly important given the rising costs of healthcare and the increasing prevalence of chronic diseases.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written with excellent organization, makes a clear original contribution that builds on but extends beyond previous work, and addresses a significant problem with potential for real-world impact. The empirical results are convincing, and the theoretical foundation is solid.

In summary, DPSCREEN makes a strong contribution to the field of personalized healthcare decision making. The framework is general enough to apply to a wide range of diseases, the technical approach is novel and well-executed, and the empirical results demonstrate meaningful practical benefits. The paper is well-written, thoroughly validated, and addresses an important problem with clear potential for impact in clinical practice. I recommend acceptance.
