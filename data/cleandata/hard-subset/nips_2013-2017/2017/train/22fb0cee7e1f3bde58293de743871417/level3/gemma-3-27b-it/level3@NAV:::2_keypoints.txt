- Proposes DPSCREEN: a framework for personalized screening using a finite-state stochastic disease progression model.  
- Tailors screening frequency to individual risk and screening history; addresses a clinically relevant need.  
- Positions work within decision-theoretic screening literature, highlighting dynamics and external information limitations in prior models.  
- Uses an approximate dynamic programming approach appropriate for the problem’s computational complexity.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly presents the model, algorithm, and results.  
- Simulated breast cancer experiments show 28–68% reduction in screening frequency while maintaining comparable detection delays versus an annual baseline.  
- Methodological novelty is incremental; extending POMDPs here is well-executed but not a paradigm shift.  
- Key unrealistic assumption: each patient’s disease model is known, limiting real-world applicability.  
- Evaluation is weak: only compared to a simplistic annual screening baseline, not to risk-stratified or adaptive policies.  
- Lacks robustness/sensitivity analysis to model misspecification and discussion of parameter estimation limitations.  
- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, technically sound algorithm, clear presentation, promising simulated results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited novelty, unrealistic known-model assumption, insufficient baselines, and missing robustness analysis.  
- Overall recommendation: interesting but insufficient novelty and practical validation; recommend rejection pending work on model inference, robustness, and stronger evaluations.
