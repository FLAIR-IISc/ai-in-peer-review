This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a general framework for constructing dynamic, personalized screening policies that minimize a weighted combination of screening burden and delay-to-detection. The disease evolves as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, while an external information process (e.g., self-exams, comorbidities) can trigger unscheduled visits. The clinician observes test outcomes when the patient arrives and sets the next follow-up date. The key modeling novelty is that both the cost and the decision epochs depend on full state trajectories and endogenous arrivals, moving beyond standard POMDP/POSMDP formulations used in prior screening work.

Technically, the authors derive Bellman equations over beliefs defined on state trajectories and diagnosis status, proving the value function is convex and piecewise linear (as in POMDPs) but in a much higher-dimensional belief space. Exact dynamic programming via alpha-vectors is intractable, so DPSCREEN uses a point-based approach tailored to this setting: sample likely physiological trajectories and then sample reachable beliefs under policies (including clinical policies), performing value backups only on this reduced belief set. They provide a computational complexity characterization, an approximation-error bound tied to the sampling density, and a robustness result showing the optimal policy map is locally constant under small model perturbations. On a large breast cancer dataset (45k women; 84k mammograms), DPSCREEN achieves 28–68% reductions in expected screenings while matching expected delays of current annual/biennial practices; it also adapts to patient risk and history. A synthetic study shows how different delay-cost functions (linear vs quadratic) materially change recommended screening intensity, underscoring disease-specific cost modeling.

Relation to prior work: Compared to OR-based POMDP screening for breast/colon cancer [Ayer; Maillart] and Bayesian design approaches [Rizopoulos; Verdinelli], this framework admits non-Markov disease models, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and exogenous information-driven arrivals. It also goes beyond simulation-only guideline modeling (e.g., MISCAN-COLON) by optimizing over a large policy space rather than comparing a fixed menu. The controlled sensing literature offers structural results under stationarity/Markov assumptions that are not met here; DPSCREEN fills that gap.

Strengths
- General and principled framework that unifies multiple disease models, external information, and history-dependent personalization.
- Solid technical development: Bellman formulation, piecewise linearity, tailored point-based value iteration, complexity analysis, approximation error bound, and robustness to model estimation.
- Strong empirical illustration on a large real-world dataset with meaningful reductions in screening volume at maintained detection delays; clear evidence of personalization to both baseline risk and evolving history.
- Careful positioning within OR/medical literature; good discussion of why standard POMDP/POSMDP tools are insufficient.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Evaluation is model-based: policies are assessed on outcomes simulated from an estimated model rather than from counterfactuals in observational data. While standard in this domain, it limits causal validity. Suggest: off-policy evaluation where feasible, or sensitivity analyses to plausible model misspecification.
- Error bound relies on strong assumptions (e.g., perfect tests for the bound, bounded inter-screen interval, large horizon) and introduces an abstract constant ζ; no empirical calibration of the bound is provided. Suggest quantifying how performance varies with belief-sampling density (K, |B̄|) and reporting run-time/memory vs accuracy trade-offs.
- Computational scalability: worst-case complexity grows with T, |Y|, |Z|, belief points, and sampled trajectories. Provide wall-clock timings, parallelization gains, and scaling plots to reassure feasibility in larger state/test spaces or longer horizons.
- Baselines: comparisons are limited to annual/biennial schedules. Add personalized baselines (e.g., density-and-risk–based intervals, ConfidentCare, information-gain designs) for a fairer assessment of personalization benefits.
- External information modeling (self-exams) plays a key role; more sensitivity to ỹ, σ, and examination frequency would clarify robustness. An ablation without external information is reported; include subgroup analyses to avoid over-attributing gains to self-exams.
- Practical deployment: guidance on choosing w or constraints, handling multiple tests with varying costs, and interpretability of recommended intervals would aid clinical translation.

Assessment by review criteria
- Quality: The methodology is technically sound and carefully argued. Theoretical results are appropriate for the approximations used. Main caveat is the reliance on an estimated disease model for evaluation; additional robustness and baseline comparisons would strengthen the empirical case.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well organized, with concise math. The notation-heavy sections could benefit from moving key pseudo-code and the graphical model from the supplement into the main text, and adding an illustrative small example of belief updates and action selection.
- Originality: High. Endogenous decision epochs, trajectory-dependent costs, and the belief-space construction tailored to high-dimensional trajectory beliefs distinguish this work from prior screening and controlled-sensing approaches.
- Significance: High. The framework is broadly applicable across diseases and shows substantial potential to reduce burden without sacrificing timeliness. If validated prospectively, it could influence screening guidelines and personalized scheduling systems.

Arguments for acceptance
- General, novel formulation addressing important limitations of prior screening models.
- Strong theoretical underpinnings with tractable approximate solution.
- Compelling empirical evidence on a large dataset and clear personalization effects.
- Potentially high practical impact.

Arguments against acceptance
- Model-based evaluation without causal validation; limited personalized baselines.
- Scalability and bound tightness not empirically characterized.
- Sensitivity to external information assumptions and cost specification needs further exploration.

Questions for the authors
- Can you report run-time, memory, and scaling with K and |B̄|, and provide sensitivity of performance to these sampling parameters?
- How sensitive are results to self-exam parameters (ỹ, σ, frequency) and to alternative test accuracy assumptions?
- Can you compare against risk- and density-based interval policies and ConfidentCare?
- How would you set w in practice, or enforce hard delay constraints directly?

Recommendation: Accept. This is a well-motivated, technically solid, and broadly applicable contribution with promising empirical results and clear potential impact. Confidence: high.
