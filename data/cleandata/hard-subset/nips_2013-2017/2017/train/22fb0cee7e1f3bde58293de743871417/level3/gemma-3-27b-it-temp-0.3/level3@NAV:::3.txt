## Review of DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for constructing personalized breast cancer screening policies that dynamically adjust to individual patient characteristics and screening history. The core idea – modeling disease progression as a stochastic process and optimizing screening schedules to balance cost and delay – is a valuable contribution. The work builds upon existing decision-theoretic approaches to screening (e.g., [7, 9]) but significantly expands their applicability by relaxing assumptions of Markovian disease processes and stationary test outcomes, addressing limitations highlighted in the introduction. The authors convincingly argue that personalized screening, accounting for external information like self-exams, is crucial for optimizing resource allocation and patient outcomes.

The strength of this paper lies in its technical soundness and empirical validation. The proposed approach tackles a computationally intractable problem with a tractable approximation, and the authors provide a bound on the approximation error. The application to a large breast cancer dataset demonstrates significant improvements over existing clinical policies, achieving substantial reductions in the number of screenings (28-68%) while maintaining comparable delays in detection. The personalization aspect is clearly illustrated, with higher-risk patients screened more frequently and schedules adapting to test results. The discussion of robustness to model estimation error and the exploration of different cost functions (linear vs. quadratic) further strengthen the work. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the complex methodology accessible.

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the model. While the authors acknowledge estimation error, a more thorough sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of different parameter settings and data quality issues would be valuable. The assumption of a two-state disease model (Healthy/Disease) might be a simplification for breast cancer, and exploring more complex state representations could be considered. Additionally, while the comparison to current clinical policies is strong, a comparison to other personalized screening strategies (even if less theoretically grounded) would provide further context. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and practically relevant problem.
*   Presents a technically sound and novel approach with a theoretical guarantee.
*   Empirically demonstrates substantial improvements over existing methods.
*   Well-written and clearly explained.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Model simplification (two-state disease) may limit applicability.
*   Limited discussion of sensitivity to parameter settings and data quality.
*   Could benefit from comparison to other personalized screening strategies.

**Overall:**

Despite minor limitations, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of personalized medicine and decision-making under uncertainty. The technical rigor, empirical validation, and clear presentation make it a worthy addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
