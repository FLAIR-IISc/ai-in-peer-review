- Proposal: DPSCREEN — a decision-theoretic framework to dynamically personalize disease screening by minimizing weighted screening cost and delay-to-detection, with endogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent costs.  
- Model novelty: disease modeled as finite-state stochastic process with absorbing state; action-dependent inter-visit intervals and non-Markov/trajectory-dependent costs generalize beyond standard POMDP/POSMDP.  
- Theory: prove convex, piecewise-linear value function and derive a tractable PBVI-style approximation with trajectory-informed belief sampling and an explicit approximation bound.  
- Robustness: provide a theoretical robustness result to model-estimation error.  
- Empirical results: on a large breast-cancer cohort (n≈45k), DPSCREEN personalizes by Gail baseline risk and test history, cutting 28–68% of screenings at comparable expected detection delays vs annual/biennial schedules.  
- Relation to prior work: extends personalized screening and PBVI literature by addressing external-information arrivals, trajectory-dependent delays, and non-Markov disease models.  
- Quality assessment: technically sound formulation with clear Bellman equations and separation of approximation vs estimation errors; empirical gains are sizable but evaluation is model-based (learn-then-simulate), limiting causal validity.  
- Clarity: paper is well written and organized though notation is heavy; recommends a schematic for the external-information mechanism and more code/pseudocode for reproducibility.  
- Originality: novel integration of external triggers for arrivals, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and general non-Markov disease dynamics.  
- Significance: high potential impact for ML in health and sequential decision-making with clear policy relevance and extensibility.  
- Strengths: strong motivation and principled modeling, theoretical guarantees, large realistic case study, and dynamic personalization by features and history.  
- Weaknesses: relies on learned simulators—needs off-policy evaluation or sensitivity analyses; some theoretical assumptions (e.g., no false positives/negatives) are strong and need clarification; computational complexity concerns—need runtime/scale benchmarks and ablations.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel and general formulation, solid theory, meaningful empirical gains, and clear relevance to NeurIPS sequential decision-making.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited external/causal validation, strong theoretical assumptions, and insufficient evidence on scalability.
