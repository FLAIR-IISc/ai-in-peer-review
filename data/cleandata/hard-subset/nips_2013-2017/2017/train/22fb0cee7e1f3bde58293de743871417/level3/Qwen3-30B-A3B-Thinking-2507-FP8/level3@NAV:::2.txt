

This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing personalized screening policies by modeling disease as a finite-state stochastic process. The authors address the important problem of balancing screening frequency against detection delay, which has significant clinical implications. The core contribution is a computationally efficient approximate solution to the otherwise intractable optimal screening policy problem, framed as a POMDP with extensions for external information processes.

The paper is well-written and the proposed method is technically sound, with clear theoretical justification for the approximation error bounds. The experimental results on breast cancer data demonstrate promising reductions (28-68%) in screening frequency while maintaining similar detection delays compared to annual screening protocols. The authors effectively illustrate how the policy personalizes to patient features and dynamic history, which is a significant advancement over non-personalized approaches.

However, the paper has notable limitations. The core assumption that disease models are perfectly known is unrealistic in practice, where model inference from observational data is the primary challenge. The methodological novelty is limited as it builds on existing POMDP approaches with minor extensions. The experimental validation is insufficient - the comparison only to an annual baseline ignores more sophisticated alternatives, and there's no analysis of robustness to model misspecification or sensitivity to uncertainty in disease parameters.

While the problem is important and the technical execution is reasonable, the lack of practical validation and limited novelty make the contribution less compelling for NIPS. The paper would benefit significantly from addressing the model uncertainty issue and comparing against more relevant baselines (e.g., risk-stratified screening protocols already in use).

Strengths: Important problem addressed, well-written, promising results, technically sound approach. Weaknesses: Unrealistic model assumption, limited novelty, insufficient experimental validation, no robustness analysis.

Overall, this is a reasonable technical contribution to a relevant problem, but the lack of practical validation and limited novelty prevents it from being a strong candidate for NIPS. The work would be significantly strengthened by addressing the model uncertainty issue and providing more comprehensive experimental comparisons.
