## Review of DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for constructing personalized screening policies to minimize screening costs and delays in disease detection. The core idea is to model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and develop an approximately optimal policy, addressing a critical gap in existing literature that often relies on overly simplistic Markovian assumptions or focuses on population-level strategies. The work is well-motivated, recognizing the increasing availability of electronic health records and the need for individualized screening approaches.

The proposed algorithm is reasonable and well-developed, leveraging point-based value iteration to tackle the computational intractability of finding the truly optimal policy. The application to simulated breast cancer data demonstrates promising results, showing significant reductions in screening frequency (28-68%) while maintaining comparable delays to annual screening. The clarity of the paper is commendable; the authors clearly articulate the problem, their approach, and the experimental setup. The inclusion of a graphical model and detailed algorithmic descriptions in the supplementary materials further enhances understanding.

However, the paper suffers from limitations in originality and practical validation. While the approach is sound, the methodological novelty is incremental. The core contribution lies in the application of PBVI to a specific problem formulation, rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic breakthrough. More significantly, the reliance on the strong assumption that each patient’s disease model is *known* severely limits the practical relevance of the work. In reality, model inference is a major challenge, and the method’s performance under model misspecification remains unexplored. 

The experimental evaluation is also limited. Comparing against only a trivial annual-screening baseline provides insufficient evidence of the policy’s superiority. A more robust evaluation would involve comparisons to other personalized screening strategies and a thorough sensitivity analysis to assess the impact of uncertainties in the disease model parameters. The authors acknowledge this in their discussion of robustness, but further investigation is needed. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and growing research area (personalized screening).
* Presents a reasonable and well-developed algorithm.
* Demonstrates promising results in a simulated setting.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Limited methodological novelty.
* Relies on an unrealistic assumption of known disease models.
* Insufficient experimental validation (limited baseline comparison, lack of robustness analysis).
* The impact of the cost function on the policy is not fully explored.

**Overall:**

This is an interesting and reasonable piece of work, but it falls short of fully convincing due to its limitations in novelty and practical validation. While the theoretical framework is sound, the strong assumptions and limited experimental evaluation raise concerns about its real-world applicability. I recommend rejection with encouragement to address the identified weaknesses, particularly focusing on model inference and robustness analysis, before resubmission.
