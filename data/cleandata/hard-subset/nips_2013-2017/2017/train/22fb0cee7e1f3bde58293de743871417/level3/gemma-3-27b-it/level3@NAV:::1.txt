## Review of DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies across a broad class of diseases. The core contribution lies in formulating screening as a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) and developing a computationally tractable approach to approximate the optimal policy, considering both patient features and screening history. This work directly addresses a critical gap in existing literature, which often focuses on population-level or disease-specific screening strategies with limited personalization. The authors effectively situate their work within the context of existing decision-theoretic approaches to screening, highlighting the limitations of previous methods in handling the complexities of dynamic disease progression and patient-driven arrivals for testing.

The strength of this paper is multi-faceted. Technically, the paper is sound, providing a concise yet thorough mathematical exposition of the model and the proposed solution. The computational complexity analysis is valuable and acknowledges the inherent challenges of the problem. Furthermore, the validation on a large, real-world dataset (Athena Health, 45k patients) lends significant credibility to the findings. Demonstrating improvements – reductions in screening frequency without compromising detection delay – across multiple disease model types underscores the generalizability of the DPSCREEN approach. The personalization aspect, explicitly shown in the illustrative examples, is a key strength, reflecting a nuanced understanding of patient risk profiles.

However, a minor weakness lies in the assumption of a single absorbing disease state, which might be overly simplistic for some diseases with intermediate stages. While acknowledged, a more in-depth discussion of the impact of this simplification on the accuracy of the personalized policies would be beneficial. The reliance on the Gail model for initial risk assessment, while standard, introduces potential biases inherent in that model. Additionally, while the paper demonstrates significant reductions in screening frequency, the cost-benefit analysis could be further elaborated, perhaps including considerations of patient anxiety and the potential harms associated with false positives.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty & Significance:** DPSCREEN presents a genuinely novel approach to personalized screening, addressing a practically important problem.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The method is rigorously developed and mathematically well-founded.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Validation on a large, real-world dataset supports the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and methodology.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Simplifying Assumption:** The single absorbing state may limit applicability to certain diseases.
*   **Model Dependency:** Reliance on the Gail model introduces potential biases.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a clear technical contribution with substantial potential practical impact. The strengths significantly outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
