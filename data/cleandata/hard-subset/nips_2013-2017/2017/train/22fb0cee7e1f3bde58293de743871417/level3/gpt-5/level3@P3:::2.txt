Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for computing personalized screening schedules that minimize a weighted combination of testing cost and delay-to-detection. Disease progression is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, and the clinician’s actions (when to schedule next screening) interact with an “external information” process (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger earlier visits. The optimization departs from standard POMDPs because (i) decision epochs are endogenous (arrival depends on actions and exogenous signals) and (ii) the delay cost depends on the trajectory (incidence and detection times), not just the current hidden state. The authors derive a Bellman formulation, show a piecewise-linear convex value function, and develop a point-based approximate solution that samples likely trajectories and reachable beliefs to make computation tractable. They provide an approximation bound (in terms of belief sampling density) and a continuity result suggesting limited sensitivity to small model-estimation errors. Experiments using a breast cancer dataset (with a 2-state H/D model estimated from data and a simple self-exam signal) show that, at similar expected delay as annual screening, DPSCREEN reduces the expected number of screenings substantially.

This work connects to POMDP-based screening (e.g., Ayer et al. 2012; Maillart et al. 2008; Erenay et al. 2014) and to point-based value iteration (PBVI) methods. It argues greater generality by allowing non-Markov trajectory costs and endogenous arrivals, and it goes beyond clinical simulation studies that compare a fixed menu of schedules.

Strengths
- Timely and important topic: personalized screening with explicit cost–delay trade-offs is clinically relevant.
- Clear formulation that highlights departures from standard POMDPs (trajectory-dependent costs; endogenous decision epochs).
- Practical algorithmic design using trajectory/belief sampling with PBVI, with a clean convexity result and an approximation-error statement.
- Empirical illustration on breast cancer data shows notable reductions in screening frequency without increasing expected delay (relative to annual screening).
- Well written and organized; the methodology and assumptions are explicit.

Weaknesses
- The approach effectively assumes the patient’s disease/test model is known; in practice, model inference (and uncertainty about it) is the central challenge. The experiments do not assess robustness to model misspecification.
- Methodological novelty is limited: the algorithm is a PBVI-style approximation with a custom belief-construction heuristic; theoretical results (piecewise linearity, error bound) largely mirror known POMDP properties, and the bound depends on an uncalibrated density constant.
- Empirical evaluation is narrow: comparisons are primarily to an annual (and in appendix, biennial) baseline. No comparisons to stronger personalized baselines (e.g., existing POMDP-based screening, risk-density–based schedules, joint-model design policies), nor to simple risk-threshold heuristics learned from the same model.
- Some modeling assumptions are unrealistic or under-justified for practice (e.g., no false positives in some analyses; simplified two-state disease model; biopsy cost set to 1 like mammography).
- No sensitivity analysis to core uncertainties (transition hazards, test accuracies, self-exam properties), despite claiming model-estimation considerations.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation and PBVI adaptation are sound, and the convexity result is standard. The approximation bound is qualitative; the constant ζ and coverage of belief space are not operationalized, and there is no empirical assessment of suboptimality gaps.
- Empirical support: Results demonstrate internal consistency and plausible gains under the learned model. However, they hinge on model correctness, and there are no confidence intervals, subgroup analyses, or robustness checks.
- Completeness: The paper presents a full pipeline (modeling, solution, illustration), but practical validation is limited.

Clarity
- Very clearly written and well structured. The motivation for endogenous arrivals and trajectory costs is compelling. Notation is heavy but consistent; suggestions: briefly summarize Algorithm 1/2 in the main text rather than relegating details to the appendix, and report concrete values of T, K, |B̄|, and wall-clock runtimes to ground complexity claims.

Originality
- Conceptual framing of screening with endogenous arrivals and trajectory-based delay is a nice synthesis, but core algorithmic elements (PBVI, piecewise linearity) are not new. Relative to prior POMDP screening work, the main novelty is the generality claim and handling of external information; nonetheless, methodological contribution feels incremental.

Significance
- The topic is important; a scalable method for personalized screening could be impactful. However, without robustness to misspecification and stronger comparative baselines, it is hard to judge practical significance. The current evaluation stops short of what would persuade practitioners.

Suggestions for improvement
- Robustness to model uncertainty: conduct sensitivity analyses varying transition hazards, test sensitivity/specificity, self-exam parameters, and the assumption of no false positives/negatives; report how schedules and outcomes change. Consider Bayesian or distributionally robust POMDP variants to propagate uncertainty over model parameters into decisions.
- Stronger baselines: compare against (i) existing POMDP-based screening models instantiated to your setting, (ii) risk-stratified schedules based on Gail score and breast density, (iii) joint-model–based personalized intervals, and (iv) simple learned heuristics (e.g., thresholded posterior-risk rules).
- Ablations: quantify the marginal value of external information; analyze performance when self-exams are absent or noisy.
- Practical cost model: separate costs of mammography vs biopsy and include downstream harms (false positives, overdiagnosis) to better reflect clinical trade-offs.
- Report uncertainty (CIs) and subgroup fairness analyses (e.g., by age, density, race) given known calibration issues with risk models.
- Clarify computational footprint on realistic T and model sizes; release code if possible.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive are your policies to misspecification of ptr(x), pin(x), and test accuracies? Do schedules change materially under plausible ranges?
- Can your framework accommodate online learning of model parameters (e.g., Bayes-adaptive POMDP) to mitigate initial misspecification?
- How would the approach scale to richer state spaces (e.g., multi-stage preclinical states) and multiple tests with different costs?

Pro/Con list
Pros:
- Clear, well-motivated problem; reasonable, scalable method; promising reductions in screening at matched delay; solid exposition.

Cons:
- Limited methodological novelty; unrealistic assumption of known patient-level model; weak baselines; no robustness analysis; simplified cost/diagnostic model.

Overall evaluation
Interesting and reasonable work that addresses an important problem with a well-executed approximation, but limited novelty and, more importantly, insufficient practical validation and robustness analysis. I lean toward a weak reject, with the hope the authors expand empirical validation and robustness to strengthen the case.
