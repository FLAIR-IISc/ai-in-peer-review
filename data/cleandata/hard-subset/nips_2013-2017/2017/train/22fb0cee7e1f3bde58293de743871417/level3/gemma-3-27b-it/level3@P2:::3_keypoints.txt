- Presents DPSCREEN, a novel framework for personalized, dynamic screening policies balancing screening costs and delayed detection.  
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and includes external information influencing screening arrival.  
- Core contribution: a computationally tractable approximation to the optimal screening policy for an otherwise intractable problem.  
- Demonstrates effectiveness on a large breast cancer dataset with 28–68% reductions in screening frequency while keeping detection delays comparable.  
- Situates work relative to POMDPs, Bayesian design, simulation methods, controlled sensing, and dynamic Bayesian networks.  
- Strength: technically sound stochastic optimization formulation with a well-motivated PBVI approximation and error bounds (Proposition 1).  
- Strength: novel handling of non-Markovian disease processes and incorporation of external information for personalization.  
- Strength: strong empirical validation including performance across risk groups and cost functions.  
- Strength: generally clear writing and organization; robustness to model estimation error discussed.  
- Strength: significant potential impact in reducing costs, patient burden, and improving resource allocation; applicable beyond breast cancer.  
- Weakness: relies on assumptions (finite-state representation, independence of test outcomes/external info) whose violations need sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: computational complexity remains a concern for very large datasets or complex models; further optimization/parallelization may be required.  
- Weakness: brief description of parameter estimation (Gail model, MCMC); more detail and validation of estimates needed.  
- Weakness: limited scope of external information (only breast lump size); needs expansion to other factors (family history, lifestyle).  
- Weakness: missing Appendix B/graphical model in submission reduces clarity and should be included.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem with a novel, sound approach and compelling empirical results; aligns with precision medicine trends.  
- Arguments against acceptance: computational barriers, restrictive model assumptions, and insufficient parameter-estimation/missing appendix details.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions—include Appendix B, expand parameter-estimation details, and discuss sensitivity to model assumptions.  
- Overall rating: 7 (Strong Accept).
