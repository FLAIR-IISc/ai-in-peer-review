This paper presents DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing dynamic and personalized screening policies for diseases. The authors model disease progression as a general finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state. The goal is to find a policy that optimally trades off the frequency of screening (cost) with the delay in disease detection (cost). A novel aspect of the formulation is the inclusion of an external information process (e.g., patient self-examinations) that can trigger unscheduled visits to a clinician. As finding the exactly optimal policy is computationally intractable, the paper proposes a tractable approximation algorithm based on point-based value iteration (PBVI) over a carefully sampled set of belief states. This approach extends prior work on personalized screening (e.g., Ayer et al., Operations Research 2012), which was often restricted to Markovian disease models. The method is demonstrated on a large breast cancer dataset, where a disease model is first learned from the data. The resulting DPSCREEN policy is shown in simulation to achieve large reductions in the number of screenings compared to standard annual policies, while holding the expected detection delay constant.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a problem of high practical importance. Personalized screening has the potential to significantly reduce healthcare costs and the burden on patients (e.g., from radiation exposure or invasive tests) while improving health outcomes. This work provides a general and principled framework for achieving this goal.
2.  **Generality of the Model:** The proposed framework is quite general. By modeling the disease as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process, it moves beyond the restrictive Markovian assumptions of some previous works. The inclusion of unscheduled patient arrivals based on external information is a thoughtful and realistic feature that makes the model more applicable to real-world scenarios.
3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. The problem is formally defined, the proposed algorithm is well-developed, and the connection to the POMDP literature is made explicit. The illustrative experiments effectively demonstrate the nature of the learned personalized policy.
4.  **Promising Results:** The experimental results, though simulated on a learned model, are impressive. A 28-68% reduction in the number of screenings for the same detection delay is a significant improvement and strongly motivates the value of personalization.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Assumption of a Known Disease Model:** The primary weakness of the paper is its reliance on the assumption that an accurate disease progression model is known. The evaluation pipeline consists of learning a single model from data and then optimizing and evaluating the policy on that same model. This approach does not account for model uncertainty or misspecification, which is arguably the central challenge in applying such methods in practice. The robustness analysis in Section 3.3 is theoretical and rests on the assumption that model estimation errors are "sufficiently close," which is not empirically validated. A more convincing study would include a sensitivity analysis to assess how the policy's performance degrades when the true disease dynamics differ from the estimated model.
2.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** While the application is novel and important, the core methodology is an adaptation of point-based value iteration (PBVI), a well-established algorithm for approximately solving POMDPs. The main contribution is in the problem formulation and the adaptation of PBVI to this specific belief space and cost structure, rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic advance.
3.  **Experimental Baselines:** The experiments compare DPSCREEN only to fixed annual and biennial screening policies. While these are relevant clinical benchmarks, they are non-adaptive. A stronger evaluation would compare the proposed method against other data-driven or personalized screening algorithms from the literature to better situate its performance.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper addresses a significant real-world problem with a well-developed and general framework. It is clearly presented and the simulation results are compelling, highlighting the substantial potential benefits of personalized screening.
*   **Con:** The work's practical relevance is not fully established due to the strong assumption of a known disease model and the lack of experiments evaluating robustness to model misspecification. The methodological novelty is also incremental.

In summary, this is an interesting and well-executed paper on an important topic. The proposed framework is reasonable and the results are promising. However, the paper would be significantly stronger if it addressed the critical issue of model uncertainty more directly in its evaluation. While the current evaluation demonstrates the potential of the approach under ideal conditions, establishing its practical relevance requires a more thorough assessment of its sensitivity to the inevitable errors in model estimation.
