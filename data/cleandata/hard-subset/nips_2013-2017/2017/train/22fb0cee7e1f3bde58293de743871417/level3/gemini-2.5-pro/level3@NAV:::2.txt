This paper addresses the important problem of optimizing personalized screening policies for diseases. The authors model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and frame the challenge as a sequential decision-making problem, aiming to minimize a weighted sum of screening costs and detection delays. Due to the computational intractability of finding an exact solution, the paper proposes a computationally efficient approximate policy based on Point-Based Value Iteration. The approach is evaluated on a simulated breast cancer screening task, where it demonstrates a significant reduction in the number of screenings compared to a standard annual policy, while achieving a similar expected delay in detection. This work fits into a growing body of literature applying POMDP-like frameworks to medical decision-making.

The paper has several notable strengths. The problem of personalized screening is of high practical and societal significance, and the paper makes a clear case for its importance. The proposed algorithm is reasonable, well-motivated, and the overall paper is clearly written and well-structured. The experimental results, though on simulated data, are promising and effectively illustrate the potential benefits of personalizing screening schedules based on patient risk and history.

However, the paper also has significant weaknesses that limit its impact. The primary limitation is the strong and unrealistic assumption that each patient’s individual disease model is known beforehand. In practice, the main challenge is inferring these personalized models from sparse and noisy data, an issue this work circumvents entirely. This assumption undermines the practical relevance of the proposed method.

Furthermore, the methodological novelty is limited, as the core approach is an adaptation of existing POMDP approximation techniques. The experimental evaluation is also not sufficiently rigorous. The comparison is limited to a trivial annual screening baseline, and more sophisticated or existing personalized baselines are not considered. Critically, the experiments do not assess the policy's robustness to model misspecification. A sensitivity analysis demonstrating how performance degrades under uncertainty in the estimated disease model would be essential to convince the reader of the method’s utility in a real-world setting.

In summary, this is an interesting and well-written paper on an important problem. However, the lack of methodological novelty and, more importantly, the failure to address the critical challenge of model uncertainty and validation make the current contribution feel incomplete and of questionable practical significance.
