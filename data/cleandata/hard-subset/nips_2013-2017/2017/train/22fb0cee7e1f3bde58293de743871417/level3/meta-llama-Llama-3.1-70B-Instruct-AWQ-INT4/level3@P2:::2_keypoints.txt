- Proposes DPSCREEN: a method for personalized screening that balances screening cost against delay cost, modeling disease as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing state and incorporating external information-triggered visits.

- Main technical approach is a computationally efficient approximate solution using point-based value iteration (PBVI).

- Experiments on simulated breast cancer data show promising results.

- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and free of major errors.

- Major limitation: assumes each patient's disease model is known, which is unrealistic in practice.

- Methodological novelty is limited; the work builds on existing models and algorithms rather than introducing a major new technique.

- Experiments only compare to a trivial annual baseline and do not test robustness to model misspecification.

- Authors acknowledge the need to evaluate sensitivity to disease-model uncertainty but have not done so in this paper.

- Lack of practical validation and robustness analysis limits confidence in real-world effectiveness.

- Originality is modest, though the clear exposition is valuable for readers new to the topic.

- Significance is potentially high (reducing delays and costs in screening) but currently constrained by unrealistic assumptions and limited evaluation.

- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important problem, shows potential benefits, and is well-presented.

- Arguments against acceptance: unrealistic modeling assumptions, limited novelty, insufficient robustness and practical validation.

- Overall rating: 6/10; recommendation: accept with revisions pending further development and evaluation of model inference and robustness.
