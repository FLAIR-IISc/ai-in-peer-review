This paper tackles dynamic, personalized screening by modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and incorporating an exogenous “external information” process (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger early visits. The objective is to minimize a weighted sum of discounted screening costs and delay costs, where delay depends on the incidence and detection times. The authors formulate the problem as a belief-state control problem, derive a Bellman recursion, show the value function is convex and piecewise linear in the belief (as in POMDPs), and argue that exact solution via alpha-vectors is intractable. They propose DPSCREEN, a point-based value iteration (PBVI)–style approximation that samples likely state trajectories to construct a lower-dimensional set of reachable beliefs and performs value backups on these points. They provide a complexity bound and an approximation error bound in terms of the density of belief sampling, and a robustness result showing local constancy of optimal actions under small model estimation errors. Empirically, they estimate a two-state survival model for breast cancer from a large de-identified dataset (Athena Network) and evaluate policies by simulating from the learned model. Compared to annual screening (and with some discussion of biennial in the appendix), DPSCREEN achieves large reductions (28–68%) in the number of screens at comparable expected delays, adapts to risk features and test histories, and shows differing behaviors under linear vs. quadratic delay costs. The work relates to POMDP-based screening for breast and colon cancer, Bayesian design/joint models for personalized intervals, and medical simulation frameworks (e.g., MISCAN), while claiming broader generality (non-Markov disease, exogenous arrivals, trajectory-dependent delay) and a computationally tractable approximator.

Quality: The formulation is careful, the Bellman model is clear, and the extension of piecewise-linear convexity to this setting is plausible. The PBVI-style approximation is reasonable given the very high-dimensional belief over trajectories. The approximation bound is qualitative—depending on an unknown constant ζ and a sampling density metric—and rests on simplifying assumptions (e.g., no false positives/negatives) that are relaxed only in the empirical section. The robustness theorem (local constancy of optimal actions) is standard in spirit for POMDPs under unique optimality. Empirical validation uses a real dataset for model estimation but evaluates policies only on the learned model, so real-world counterfactuals are not assessed; this is common in this literature but limits practical conclusions. Baselines are limited (primarily annual; biennial is relegated to the supplement), and there is no comparison to prior adaptive screening policies.

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized. The model, belief updates, and action space are defined clearly. The role of the weight w and how it is tuned to meet a delay constraint is explained, though guidance on choosing w in practice would help. Some assumptions (e.g., no false positives in the theory vs. realistic sensitivities/specificities in experiments) should be reconciled more explicitly. A brief schematic of the belief sampling/backups (beyond the supplemental pseudo-code) would improve accessibility.

Originality: Conceptually, the work integrates several known components—belief-state control, PBVI, and model-based evaluation—but pushes them into a more general screening setting with endogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent delay. Theoretical properties (PWL, local constancy) are extensions of known POMDP results; the main novelty is problem formulation and an application-oriented approximation scheme with an error bound tailored to belief sampling over trajectories.

Significance: Personalized screening is important, and a general framework that can flexibly incorporate disease/test/external-information models is valuable. The reported reductions in screenings at comparable delays are promising. However, the lack of robust empirical validation and limited baselines reduce confidence in practical impact at this stage.

Empirical evaluation and baselines: The evaluation is model-based on simulated trajectories drawn from the estimated disease and external-information processes. This avoids counterfactual issues but inherits model misspecification risk. The comparison to annual screening is a weak baseline; biennial is standard in many countries. Prior adaptive methods (e.g., POMDP-based mammography scheduling, joint modeling approaches, ConfidentCare-style systems, risk/density-tailored intervals) should be included. Sensitivity analyses to model errors (transition rates, test accuracies, external-information noise and threshold) and to design choices (discount δ, maximum interval W, belief sampling K and B, and weight w) would strengthen claims of robustness.

Pros:
- Addresses a clinically important problem with a clean, generalizable formulation.
- Incorporates external information and trajectory-dependent delay costs not handled by prior POMDP screening work.
- Provides a tractable PBVI-style algorithm and a (qualitative) approximation bound.
- Uses a large real dataset for model estimation; shows clear personalization effects and substantial screening reductions for fixed delay.
- Clear writing and careful problem setup.

Cons:
- Methodological novelty is moderate; theory extends known POMDP properties and PBVI.
- Evaluation relies entirely on the learned model; no robustness to misspecification or counterfactual validation.
- Baselines are limited; no comparison to prior adaptive/personalized scheduling methods.
- Error bound depends on unknown constants and strong assumptions (e.g., perfect tests).
- Practical details (choice of w, patient adherence, subgroup fairness) are not explored.

Questions for the authors:
- How sensitive are the results to misspecification of ptr(x), pin(x), test sensitivities/specificities, and the external-information model (noise variance, threshold)?
- What values of K (trajectory samples) and B (belief points) were used, and what were the runtime/memory footprints? Any convergence diagnostics?
- How is w chosen operationally to satisfy a delay constraint, and how stable is the resulting policy across w?
- Can you compare to prior adaptive baselines (e.g., POMDP mammography, joint model tailoring, risk/density-based intervals, ConfidentCare)?

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add comprehensive sensitivity analyses and robustness checks; consider distributional robustness or Bayesian model averaging over disease/test models.
- Expand baselines to include biennial, risk- and density-tailored policies, and prior adaptive schedulers.
- Provide ablations (no external information; fixed intervals; bounded W) and subgroup analyses (age, density, family history) to assess equity and stability.
- Quantify the approximation bound empirically by varying belief-sampling density; report computational scaling.
- Clarify and reconcile theoretical assumptions (e.g., no false positives) with experimental settings.
- Release code and synthetic data generators to facilitate reproducibility.

Overall: Interesting and well-presented work with promising results and a reasonable approximation strategy, but limited methodological novelty and insufficient practical validation. Strengthening the empirical section with stronger baselines and robustness analyses would significantly increase the paper’s impact.
