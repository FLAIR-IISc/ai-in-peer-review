- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized screening that trades off screening frequency vs. detection delay.
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating patient features and dynamic history.
- Presents a computationally tractable method to construct approximately optimal screening policies that outperform current clinical practices.
- Generalizes prior work by handling arbitrary finite-state processes rather than restricting to Markov disease processes or stationary test models.
- Clearly distinguishes the approach from operations-research and medical simulation-based methods that compare fixed policy sets.
- Strong mathematical formulation with clear definitions of disease model, external information process, and screening policies.
- Provides computational complexity analysis and an approximation error bound (Proposition 1) establishing practicality.
- Validated on a large real-world breast cancer dataset (45k patients), showing 28–68% screening reductions at equal expected detection delay.
- Demonstrates flexibility across different delay-cost models (e.g., linear vs. quadratic) via additional analyses.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, with concise but complete mathematical exposition and reproducibility-focused detail.
- Figures effectively illustrate personalization and complement the text.
- Addresses robustness to model estimation errors (Theorem 1), important for real-world deployment.
- Original contribution through a generalizable framework and inclusion of external information processes (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger visits.
- Significant practical impact by reducing unnecessary screenings and improving healthcare resource allocation.
- Main limitation: empirical validation focused primarily on breast cancer; broader disease validation would strengthen generality.
- Minor weakness: some implementation details (e.g., specifics of Algorithm 1) could be elaborated.
