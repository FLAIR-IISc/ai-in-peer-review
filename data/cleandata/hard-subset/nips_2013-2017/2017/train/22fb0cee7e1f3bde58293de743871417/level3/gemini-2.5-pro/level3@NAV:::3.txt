This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing dynamic and personalized screening policies. The central idea is to formulate the problem as an optimization that balances screening costs with the cost of delayed disease detection. The framework is highly general, modeling disease as a finite-state stochastic process and uniquely incorporating external information, such as patient self-examinations, which can trigger unscheduled visits. Recognizing that the exact solution is computationally intractable, the authors develop a tractable approximation algorithm based on Point-Based Value Iteration. This work builds upon previous research in personalized screening (e.g., Ayer et al., Operations Research 2012) but generalizes it by moving beyond restrictive Markovian assumptions and adding a mechanism for patient-initiated appointments.

The paper has several compelling strengths.
**Significance:** The work addresses a problem of critical importance in healthcare. The potential to reduce the frequency of invasive and costly screenings without compromising patient outcomes is highly significant.
**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, providing a rigorous problem formulation, an appropriate approximation algorithm, and theoretical analysis of its performance bounds and robustness. The empirical evaluation is a key strength, using a large real-world dataset for breast cancer to demonstrate that DPSCREEN can reduce the number of screenings by 28-68% while maintaining the same expected detection delay as current clinical standards.
**Originality:** The proposed framework is novel in its generality and its inclusion of an external information process, which makes the model more realistic than prior work. This represents a clear and important advancement in the state of the art for medical decision-making.
**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and effectively communicates a complex model and solution approach.

While the contribution is strong, there are minor points for consideration. The practical application of this method hinges on the ability to accurately estimate the underlying disease progression model from observational data, which is a significant challenge in itself. A more detailed discussion on the potential difficulties and limitations of this estimation step would be beneficial. Additionally, while the authors provide the computational complexity, further discussion on the practical scalability of the algorithm to diseases with more states or longer time horizons would be welcome.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a high-impact, real-world problem with a novel, generalized framework.
*   Presents compelling empirical results on a large dataset, demonstrating significant improvements over the standard of care.
*   Technically sound, with theoretical grounding and a robust evaluation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Limited discussion on the practical challenges of learning the necessary disease models from real-world, often messy, observational data.

Overall, the paper's strengths far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a significant and well-executed contribution to an important application area for AI, and I strongly recommend its acceptance.
