This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a decision-theoretic framework for dynamically personalizing disease screening schedules by minimizing a weighted combination of screening cost and delay-to-detection cost. The disease evolves as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; patient arrivals are endogenous via external information (e.g., self-exam) as well as clinician-scheduled visits. The problem generalizes beyond standard POMDP/POSMDP settings due to trajectory-dependent costs and action-dependent inter-visit intervals. The authors prove a convex, piecewise-linear value function; derive a tractable PBVI-style approximation using trajectory-informed belief sampling with an explicit approximation bound; and show a robustness result to model-estimation error. On a large breast-cancer cohort (n≈45k), DPSCREEN personalizes by baseline risk (Gail model) and test history, achieving 28–68% fewer screenings at comparable expected delays to standard annual/biennial protocols. This relates to prior work in personalized screening (e.g., Ayer et al.; Maillart et al.; Rizopoulos et al.) and PBVI (Pineau et al.) while addressing limitations of Markov/stationary assumptions and fixed decision epochs.

Evaluation:
- Quality: The formulation is technically sound, with clear Bellman equations, structural results, and a sampling-based PBVI that is appropriate for the high-dimensional belief space. The theory carefully separates approximation and estimation errors. Empirical evaluation is thorough and shows sizable gains; however, outcome evaluation is model-based (learn-then-simulate), which limits causal validity.
- Clarity: Well written and organized; notation is heavy but largely necessary. A brief schematic of the external-information mechanism and action space would help. Reproducibility could be improved by releasing code or pseudo-code beyond the appendix.
- Originality: The integration of external information triggering endogenous arrivals, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and general (non-Markov) disease models is novel relative to prior POMDP-based screening work.
- Significance: High potential impact for ML-in-health and sequential decision-making, with clear policy relevance and extensibility to other diseases and cost structures.

Strengths:
- Strong problem motivation; principled modeling.
- Theoretical guarantees (piecewise-linear value, approximation bound, robustness).
- Large, realistic case study with substantial empirical improvements.
- Personalization by features and dynamic history.

Weaknesses:
- Empirical evaluation relies on learned simulators; consider off-policy evaluation or sensitivity analyses to misspecification.
- Some theoretical assumptions (e.g., no false positives/negatives in the bound) are strong; clarify practical implications.
- Computational complexity may be high; include runtime/scale benchmarks and ablations (e.g., K, B, action-space restrictions).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel, general formulation; solid theory; meaningful empirical gains; clear relevance to NeurIPS sequential decision-making.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited external/causal validation; strong assumptions in theory; scalability evidence could be stronger.
