Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a general framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening frequency against delay in disease detection. The disease is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, with an external information process (e.g., self-exam, comorbidities) that can trigger early arrivals. The clinician schedules the next screening based on test outcomes and external information. The problem departs from standard POMDP/POSMDP formulations because (i) the time to the next decision is endogenous (depends on action, external information, and state trajectory), and (ii) the delay cost depends on the incidence-detection lag along the entire state trajectory. The authors derive a dynamic programming formulation in belief space (beliefs over state trajectories and diagnosis status), show that the value function is convex and piecewise linear, and define the exact policy via alpha-vectors, which is computationally intractable. They then propose a tractable point-based scheme that samples likely trajectories and reachable beliefs, with a bound on approximation error based on sampling density, and a robustness result indicating local constancy of optimal actions under small model estimation error.

Empirically, using a large real-world dataset (45k women; 84k mammograms), they estimate a two-state disease model with self-examination as external information and demonstrate that DPSCREEN reduces screenings by 28–68% relative to current clinical schedules (annual/biennial) while matching the expected delay, with stronger gains when self-exams are incorporated. They also illustrate how the choice of delay cost (linear vs quadratic) shifts the policy’s operating regime.

Compared to prior screening work (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al. for POMDPs; Rizopoulos et al. for joint modeling; simulation programs such as MISCAN-COLON informing USPSTF guidelines), DPSCREEN relaxes strong Markov/stationarity assumptions, explicitly models external information and action-dependent timing, and optimizes beyond a fixed menu of policies.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated formulation that captures key realities of screening: personalization by features and history, external arrivals, and trajectory-based costs.
- Solid theoretical framing: belief-based Bellman equations for a non-standard setting; convex piecewise-linear value; alpha-vector construction; principled point-based approximation; tractable complexity characterization; approximation and robustness results.
- Strong empirical illustration on a large, real dataset with plausible parameterization and literature-grounded estimates; shows meaningful resource savings without increasing expected delay.
- Careful comparisons against clinical baselines; analysis with and without self-exams; exploration of different delay costs to reflect different diseases.
- Potential for practical impact, given widespread interest in risk- and history-adaptive screening.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Some assumptions are restrictive for practice: (i) no false positives (and no false negatives in the approximation bound) simplify diagnosis dynamics and may overstate benefits; consider relaxing to realistic test error models and quantifying impact. (ii) Equal unit cost for mammography and biopsy conflates distinct burdens; a sensitivity analysis with differentiated costs would be informative.
- The approximation bound uses Ω(B̄) with an unspecified ζ and depends on L1 coverage of the belief set; guidance on how to control Ω in practice (choice of K, B̄, horizon) and empirical convergence curves would strengthen the claims.
- Model-based evaluation risks misspecification bias. The robustness theorem is reassuring, but empirical sensitivity analyses (e.g., bootstrap over parameter posteriors; stress-tests with alternative survival/test models; calibration on held-out data) would improve credibility.
- The external information model (thresholded self-exam) may be simplistic; many guidelines do not recommend routine self-exams. The largest gains appear with self-exams; present more detailed results/ablation “without self-exams,” and discuss generalizability to other external signals (e.g., incidental imaging, comorbid events).
- Complexity expression contains repeated T factors; clarify scaling and provide wall-clock runtimes, K and |B̄[t]| used, and parallelization details.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally excellent, but the heavy trajectory-based notation may obscure intuition. A worked example for a small T illustrating belief updates and action selection would help. Define the set Z+ earlier and consistently. Clarify how the arrival threshold ỹ is set and whether the clinician observes Y values or only early arrival events.

Originality
- The extension of POMDP machinery to endogenous timing and trajectory-dependent costs, plus the integration of external information and personalized risk, is novel relative to prior screening and controlled sensing literature. The use of tailored point-based approximations for trajectory beliefs is also original.

Significance
- The potential reductions in screenings for the same expected delay are substantial. The framework is broadly applicable across diseases and cost structures. Releasing code and synthetic data, or stronger reproducibility artifacts, would enhance impact.

Pro/Con arguments
Pros
- Addresses an important, timely problem; broad applicability.
- Sound theory with practical approximation and nontrivial guarantees.
- Large-scale, realistic empirical illustration with clear gains.
- Provides personalization by both features and history; accommodates external info.

Cons
- Some practical assumptions (test errors, costs) are simplified.
- Evaluation relies on a single real disease model plus a synthetic cost variant; broader empirical diversity would help.
- Approximation/error constants and computational choices need clearer guidance and empirical calibration.
- Reproducibility limited by proprietary data; code availability not stated.

Questions for authors
- Can you report runtimes, K, |B̄[t]|, and sensitivity of performance to these choices?
- How do results change with realistic false positive/negative rates in both mammography and biopsy, and with distinct costs for each?
- Can you provide uncertainty bands for performance by propagating posterior parameter uncertainty?
- Beyond self-exams, can DPSCREEN incorporate other external signals (e.g., incidental findings), and do you have examples?
- How does the policy behave for longer horizons and maximum interval constraints W; any risk of excessively long gaps for very low-risk patients?

Overall recommendation
This is a strong and timely contribution with solid theory and promising empirical results. While some modeling simplifications and evaluation limitations remain, the paper advances the state of the art in personalized screening and should be of broad interest. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying computational details, relaxing/testing key assumptions (test errors, costs), and strengthening sensitivity analyses.

Reviewer confidence: High.
