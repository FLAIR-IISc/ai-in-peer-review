- DPSCREEN: a framework for personalized screening that trades off screening frequency against delay in disease detection.
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and incorporates external triggers (e.g., self-exams).
- Computes approximately optimal policies using Bayesian updating, Bellman equations, and point-based value iteration (PBVI) to handle intractability.
- Validated on a 45,000-patient Athena Health dataset, showing 28–68% reductions in screening frequency without compromising detection timeliness.
- Technical soundness: rigorous mathematical formulation and detailed derivations enable reproducibility.
- Clarity and organization: clearly written with supplementary materials for transparency.
- Originality: generalizes personalized screening beyond Markov/stationary assumptions and novel inclusion of external information.
- Significance: potential cost savings, improved outcomes, and adaptability to different diseases and delay-cost structures.
- Strong empirical validation across different disease/delay-cost scenarios (e.g., linear vs. quadratic).
- Computational complexity: approximate solution remains costly and may not scale to large state spaces or long horizons.
- Unrealistic assumptions: assumes no false positives/negatives and depends on accurately estimated disease progression models.
- Limited comparisons: does not benchmark against other advanced computational methods (e.g., Bayesian optimization, reinforcement learning).
- External-information modeling is simplistic and may not capture complex real-world patient behavior.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions focused on improving scalability and robustness.
