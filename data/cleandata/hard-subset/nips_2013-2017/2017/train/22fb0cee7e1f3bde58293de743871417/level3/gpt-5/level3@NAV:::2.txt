This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for computing dynamic, patient-specific screening policies that minimize a weighted sum of screening burden and detection delay. The disease is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, augmented by an external information process (e.g., self-exam signals) that can trigger early visits. The authors show the value function is piecewise-linear and convex in beliefs, and they develop a computationally efficient approximate policy via point-based value iteration that samples likely state trajectories and reachable beliefs. They give an approximation bound tied to belief sampling density and a stability result for small model perturbations. On simulated breast cancer data learned from EHR-derived risk factors, the method reduces expected screening counts substantially at comparable delays to an annual-screening baseline.

Relation to prior work: The paper sits at the intersection of POMDP-based screening (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al.), joint modeling/survival (Rizopoulos), and controlled sensing. Within NeurIPS, it connects to work on disease trajectories (Schulam et al.) and decision making with endogenous observations (Alaa et al.). The use of PBVI and piecewise-linear value properties are well known in POMDPs; the main claimed novelty is the general modeling of external information and trajectory-dependent costs.

Quality: The formulation is careful and the algorithmic development is reasonable. However, key assumptions limit practical validity: the approach presumes the patient-specific disease model is known and relies on strong conditions (e.g., perfect tests in parts of the theory). The approximation bound hides important constants and requires convex delay costs and bounded inter-screen intervals. Experiments compare only to annual screening and do not probe robustness to model misspecification or parameter uncertainty.

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized; the modeling choices and algorithms are clearly presented.

Originality: Methodological novelty is incremental; PBVI with belief sampling is standard, and the structural results mirror classical POMDP theory. The handling of external information is a useful but modest extension.

Significance: Personalized screening is important, and the empirical reductions are promising. Yet without stronger baselines and robustness analysis, practical impact is uncertain.

Pros:
- Timely problem; clear formulation; efficient approximate solver.
- Promising results on breast cancer simulations; readable paper.

Cons:
- Assumes known per-patient disease models; limited novelty.
- Evaluation limited to a trivial baseline; no sensitivity/robustness to misspecification.
- Theoretical guarantees rely on restrictive assumptions and opaque constants.

Suggestions: Compare against risk-based intervals (biennial/triennial), ConfidentCare and information-gain approaches; stress-test policy under perturbed hazards, sensitivities/specificities, and external-information models; quantify uncertainty (e.g., distributionally robust POMDP or Bayesian model averaging); report scalability/run-time and ablations on belief sampling; consider off-policy evaluation with uncertainty bounds. Overall, interesting and reasonable work, but additional novelty and practical validation are needed to fully convince.
