- Proposes DPSCREEN, a dynamic programming framework for personalized breast cancer screening that balances screening costs and delayed detection using individual history and external information.  
- Method is technically sound: rigorous formulation and a point-based value iteration approximation with a provable error bound (Proposition 1).  
- Novelty: uses a general stochastic disease model, relaxes Markovian/test-stationarity assumptions, and incorporates patient-driven arrivals and external signals.  
- Empirical results on a large dataset show substantial gains—28–68% reduction in screenings with comparable detection delay.  
- Sensitivity analysis (linear vs. quadratic delay costs) highlights the importance of cost-function choice.  
- Paper is very clear and well-presented, with effective figures (e.g., personalization graphic) and extensive supplementary material.  
- Robustness: theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) indicates small estimation errors do not erase policy benefits.  
- Model simplifications (single absorbing disease state, independence assumptions) could limit realism and should be discussed further.  
- Reliance on retrospective observational data risks selection bias despite model-learning efforts.  
- Computational complexity remains high despite approximations, potentially limiting scalability to larger populations or richer models.  
- Arguments for acceptance: clinically important problem, technically innovative solution, strong empirical evidence, and rigorous presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited generalizability due to model simplifications and practical implementation challenges from computational cost.  
- Overall: high-quality, impactful work with strengths outweighing weaknesses; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
