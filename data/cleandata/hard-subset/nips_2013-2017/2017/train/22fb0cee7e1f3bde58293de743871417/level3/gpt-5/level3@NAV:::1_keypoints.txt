- Introduces DPSCREEN: a general framework optimizing screening frequency vs detection delay across broad disease models.  
- Formulates screening as a belief-state control problem with endogenous arrivals from clinician tests and patient-initiated visits.  
- Extends prior POMDP work by handling non-Markov disease trajectories, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and exogenous information processes.  
- Proves piecewise-linear convexity of the value function and proposes a tractable PBVI-style approximation on a sampled reachable belief set.  
- Provides computational complexity characterization and an approximation error bound for the algorithm.  
- Argues robustness to model estimation error via local constancy of optimal actions.  
- Empirically validated on 45k Athena Health patients, achieving 28–68% fewer screens at comparable expected detection delay.  
- Demonstrates clear gains from personalization using both patient features and screening history.  
- Explores different delay-cost models, revealing disease-specific policy regimes.  
- Technical exposition is concise and careful with clear Bellman formulations and complexity/approximation analysis.  
- Caveat: some theoretical guarantees assume perfect tests (no FP/FN), while experiments use realistic sensitivities/specificities—impact on guarantees should be clarified.  
- Evaluation is model-based; would benefit from sensitivity analyses, calibration checks, and more robustness experiments.  
- Clarity suggestions: clarify scaling of belief space with trajectory samples K and reachable set size B, and report ablations on K/B/W versus accuracy/runtime; detail tuning of w for constrained objectives.  
- Originality: high—novel endogenous-arrival, trajectory-cost setting and sampled-trajectory belief construction.  
- Significance: strong practical relevance and likely to spur follow-on work.  
- Pros: principled general framework, solid theory, large-scale real-world validation, substantial efficiency gains, demonstrable personalization benefit.  
- Cons: theoretical guarantees rely on strong test assumptions, potential misspecification in model-based evaluation, and limited code/ablation/reproducibility details.
