

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the trade-off between screening frequency and delay in disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating both patient features (e.g., age, family history) and dynamic history (previous screening results, external information like self-examinations). The key contribution is a computationally tractable method for constructing approximately optimal screening policies that significantly outperform current clinical practices.

The paper situates itself well within the literature, acknowledging that prior work (e.g., [7], [9], [10]) is restricted to Markov disease processes and stationary test outcome models, while this work generalizes to arbitrary finite state stochastic processes. The authors effectively distinguish their approach from related works in both operations research (e.g., [42]) and medical literature (e.g., MISCAN-COLON [46]), which rely on simulation-based comparisons of fixed policy sets rather than optimal policy construction.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The mathematical formulation is thorough and well-justified, with clear definitions of the disease model, external information process, and screening policies. The authors provide a rigorous analysis of the computational complexity and derive a bound on the approximation error (Proposition 1), which is crucial for establishing the practicality of their approach. The validation on a large breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients from Athena Health Network) is compelling, showing 28-68% reductions in screenings while maintaining the same expected detection delays compared to current clinical policies. The additional analysis across different disease models (linear vs. quadratic delay costs) effectively demonstrates the framework's flexibility.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear, with a logical flow from problem formulation to solution to validation. The mathematical exposition is concise yet complete, providing sufficient detail for reproducibility. The figures (e.g., Figure 1 illustrating personalization) effectively complement the text. The authors also thoughtfully address the robustness to model estimation errors (Theorem 1), which is critical for real-world application.

The originality of the work is substantial. While previous works have considered personalized screening for specific diseases (e.g., breast cancer [7]), this paper develops a general framework applicable to a wide range of disease models. The incorporation of external information processes (e.g., self-examinations, comorbidities) that can trigger early patient visits is particularly novel and addresses a key limitation of existing approaches.

The significance of this work is clear. Healthcare systems face substantial costs from unnecessary screenings, and this framework offers a practical method to personalize screening policies without compromising detection quality. The demonstrated efficiency gains (28-68% reduction in screenings) have meaningful implications for healthcare resource allocation. The framework's ability to adapt to different disease models (e.g., linear vs. quadratic delay costs) further enhances its potential impact across various medical contexts.

One minor limitation is that the validation focuses primarily on breast cancer; while the authors demonstrate the framework's applicability to different disease models through synthetic examples, additional validation on other diseases would strengthen the case for broader applicability. However, this is a minor point given the thorough validation on a large real-world dataset and the clear theoretical foundation for generalization.

Strengths:
- Novel framework for personalized screening across multiple disease classes
- Strong theoretical foundation with approximation error bounds
- Comprehensive validation on large real-world dataset
- Clear demonstration of personalization benefits (28-68% screening reduction)
- Well-written and technically sound exposition
- Effective comparison with existing clinical policies

Weaknesses:
- Validation limited to breast cancer (though synthetic examples show applicability to other diseases)
- Some technical details (e.g., specific implementation of Algorithm 1) could be more elaborated

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the intersection of decision theory and healthcare. It addresses a meaningful problem with a technically sound solution that has clear practical implications. The work advances the state of the art in personalized screening by providing a general framework that incorporates both patient features and dynamic history, while offering substantial efficiency gains over current clinical practices. The paper is well-positioned to be of interest to both the machine learning and healthcare communities.

Arguments for acceptance: Strong technical contribution with clear practical impact, well-validated on a large real-world dataset, addresses a meaningful problem with a novel approach, and provides theoretical guarantees.

Arguments against acceptance: Limited validation on diseases other than breast cancer (though this is mitigated by the general framework and synthetic examples).

This paper meets all four criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission. It is technically sound, clearly written, original, and significant. The work represents an important step toward more efficient and personalized healthcare screening policies. I strongly recommend acceptance.
