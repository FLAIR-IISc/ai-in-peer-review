The paper "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening" proposes a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The authors model the disease as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and incorporate the possibility of external information, such as self-examination or discovery of co-morbidities, that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment.

The main idea of the paper is to develop a computationally efficient approximate solution to the problem of finding the optimal personalized screening policy. The authors propose a point-based value iteration (PBVI) algorithm to solve the problem, which is a reasonable and well-developed approach. The paper is well-written, and the results presented in the experiments on simulated breast cancer data are promising.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The method relies on the unrealistic assumption that each patient's disease model is known, whereas in practice, model inference is the main challenge. The methodological novelty of the paper is limited, as it builds upon existing work in the field of personalized screening. The experiments only compare the proposed algorithm to a trivial annual baseline and do not assess robustness to model misspecification.

To establish the practical relevance of the proposed algorithm, it is necessary to evaluate its sensitivity to uncertainties in the disease model. The authors acknowledge this limitation and mention that they plan to address it in future work. However, without this evaluation, it is difficult to fully convince readers of the algorithm's effectiveness in real-world scenarios.

In terms of originality, the paper does not present a significant departure from existing work in the field. The authors build upon existing models and algorithms, and the main contribution of the paper is the application of these models to a specific problem. However, the paper does provide a clear and well-written exposition of the problem and the proposed solution, which is useful for readers who are not familiar with the field.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important research area, namely personalized screening. The authors demonstrate the potential of their algorithm to reduce delays and costs in breast cancer screening, which is a significant public health problem. However, the paper's impact is limited by its lack of practical validation and its reliance on unrealistic assumptions.

In terms of quality, the paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide a clear exposition of the problem, the proposed solution, and the experimental results. The paper is free of major errors, and the references are properly cited.

Overall, the paper is an interesting and reasonable work that addresses an important research area. However, it lacks novelty and practical validation to fully convince readers of its effectiveness. With further development and evaluation, the proposed algorithm has the potential to make a significant impact in the field of personalized screening.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper addresses an important research area, namely personalized screening.
* The authors demonstrate the potential of their algorithm to reduce delays and costs in breast cancer screening.
* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The method relies on unrealistic assumptions about the disease model.
* The methodological novelty of the paper is limited.
* The experiments do not assess robustness to model misspecification.
* The paper lacks practical validation to establish its effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
