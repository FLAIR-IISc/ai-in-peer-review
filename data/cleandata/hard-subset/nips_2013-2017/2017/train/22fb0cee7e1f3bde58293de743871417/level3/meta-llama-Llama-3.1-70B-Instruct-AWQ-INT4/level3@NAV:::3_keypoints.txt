- Summary: Proposes a novel personalized screening policy that balances screening cost vs delay, models disease as a finite-state stochastic process with external information, and shows reduced screenings with maintained detection delay on breast cancer data.

- Strength — Well-written and empirically sound: Clear methodology and application to breast cancer.

- Strength — Significant improvement: Outperforms existing clinical policies by reducing screenings while keeping the same detection delay.

- Strength — Personalization: Policy adapts to individual medical history and external information.

- Weakness — Computational complexity: Exact computation of alpha-vector sets is intractable; an approximate algorithm is used.

- Weakness — Limited evaluation: Experiments are limited to breast cancer data and a single population.

- Weakness — Strong assumptions: Assumes linear delay cost and perfect test accuracy, which may not hold generally.

- Originality: Novel integration of external information and personalization into screening policy design.

- Significance: Potentially important for public health by reducing costs and preserving detection timeliness.

- Recommendation — Broader evaluation: Test methodology across other diseases and populations for generalizability.

- Recommendation — Relax assumptions: Allow non-linear delay costs and imperfect test outcomes to improve robustness.

- Recommendation — Computational analysis: Provide thorough complexity analysis and propose ways to reduce computational burden.

- Conclusion: Promising and impactful approach that requires further evaluation and refinement to demonstrate generalizability and robustness.
