- Proposes DPSCREEN: a framework to balance screening costs vs. delay costs using a finite-state stochastic disease model with external information triggers.
- Provides a computationally tractable method to construct approximately optimal screening policies with provable error bounds.
- Demonstrates 28–68% reduction in screening frequency on a large breast cancer dataset while maintaining equivalent expected detection delays.
- Extends POMDP-based screening work by removing Markovian/stationarity assumptions and incorporating external information processes.
- Distinguishes from controlled-sensing literature by handling non-stationary disease dynamics and more general models.
- Lemma 1: value function is convex and piecewise-linear, enabling alpha-vector policy representation.
- Proposition 1: gives a concrete approximation-error bound justifying practicality of the approach.
- Theorem 1: robustness analysis shows estimation errors in model parameters have minimal impact on policy quality.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, with comprehensive supplementary materials and informative figures.
- Novelty: general framework applicable to arbitrary finite-state stochastic processes (e.g., dynamic survival, first-hitting-time models).
- Empirical versatility: shown to work across different cost structures (linear vs. quadratic delay costs).
- Weakness: empirical evaluation is limited to breast cancer; more diseases needed to demonstrate general applicability.
- Weakness: paper does not clearly articulate its specific advances to sequential decision-making beyond the medical application for NIPS audiences.
- Weakness: lacks thorough discussion of clinical implementation challenges and patient compliance issues.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel generalization, strong theoretical foundation, significant empirical improvements, clarity, real-world importance.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited scope of empirical validation, insufficient NIPS-focused contributions, limited treatment of implementation hurdles.
- Overall recommendation: recommend acceptance for NIPS 2017.
