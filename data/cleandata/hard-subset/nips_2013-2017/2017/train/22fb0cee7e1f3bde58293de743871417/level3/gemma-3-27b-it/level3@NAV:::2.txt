## Review of DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

This paper presents a framework, DPSCREEN, for constructing personalized screening policies by modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process. The core idea – tailoring screening frequency to individual patient risk and screening history – is highly relevant and addresses a critical need in modern healthcare. The authors appropriately position their work within the context of existing decision-theoretic approaches to screening, highlighting the limitations of previous models regarding dynamic disease processes and the incorporation of external information. 

The proposed solution, an approximate dynamic programming approach, is reasonable given the inherent computational complexity of the problem. The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the model, algorithm, and results. The experimental validation using simulated breast cancer data demonstrates a promising reduction in screening frequency (28-68%) while maintaining comparable delays in disease detection, compared to an annual screening baseline. 

However, the paper suffers from limitations in both novelty and practical validation. The methodological novelty is incremental; the extension of POMDPs to accommodate the specific structure of the screening problem, while well-executed, doesn’t represent a paradigm shift. More significantly, the reliance on the assumption that each patient’s disease model is *known* is a major obstacle to real-world applicability. In practice, model inference is the dominant challenge, and the paper doesn’t adequately address this. The experiments only compare against a simplistic annual screening baseline; a comparison to more sophisticated, risk-stratified policies, or adaptive strategies would provide a more robust evaluation. 

Furthermore, the sensitivity of the proposed algorithm to uncertainties in the disease model – a crucial factor given the inherent variability in patient presentations and the limitations of observational data – remains unexplored. Assessing robustness to model misspecification is essential to establish practical relevance. A discussion of the limitations of the estimated model parameters, and their potential impact on policy recommendations, is also missing. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and clinically relevant problem.
* Technically sound and well-developed algorithm.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Promising initial results in a simulated setting.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Limited methodological novelty.
* Unrealistic assumption of known patient-specific disease models.
* Insufficient comparison to more sophisticated baselines.
* Lack of robustness analysis regarding model misspecification.



**Overall:** This is an interesting and reasonable piece of work, but it lacks the novelty and practical validation necessary to fully convince readers of its significant contribution. I recommend rejection in its current form, with encouragement to address the limitations outlined above, particularly focusing on model inference and robustness analysis. A more thorough evaluation against realistic, adaptive screening strategies would also strengthen the paper considerably.
