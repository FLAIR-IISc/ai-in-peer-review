- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized screening that trades off screening frequency against detection delay.  
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state using patient-specific features and dynamic history.  
- Handles external information and patient-triggered early visits in the screening decision model.  
- Main technical contribution: a tractable approximation of the optimal policy via point-based value iteration with provable error bounds.  
- Establishes Bellman formulation and proves the value function is convex and piecewise-linear (Lemma 1).  
- Develops the DPSCREEN algorithm that samples belief states to construct an approximately optimal policy with bounded error (Proposition 1).  
- Provides computational complexity analysis and concrete implementation details in the supplement.  
- Situates contribution clearly against prior work, showing broader modeling scope than stationary Markov or simulation-only approaches.  
- Empirical validation on 45,000 breast cancer patients shows 28–68% fewer screenings for the same expected detection delay.  
- Demonstrates personalization: policies adapt to ex ante risk, dynamic history, test results, and external shocks.  
- Analyzes impact of different delay cost structures (linear vs. quadratic) on screening regimes.  
- Addresses robustness to model estimation errors theoretically (Theorem 1) and validates across multiple disease model types.  
- Paper is well-written, organized, and sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.  
- Weaknesses: lacks discussion of practical clinical integration (e.g., EHR deployment), needs stronger empirical validation of the approximation bound across sampling densities, and focuses mainly on breast cancer without additional disease examples.  
- Overall significance is high: theoretically sound, practically implementable, substantial efficiency gains, and recommended for acceptance.
