

# Review: DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing optimal personalized screening policies across multiple disease classes. The authors address a critical gap in healthcare decision-making by developing a method that simultaneously incorporates patient-specific features (e.g., age, family history) and dynamic screening history to optimize the balance between screening frequency and disease detection delay. The paper makes a significant contribution by extending beyond previous work that typically assumed Markovian disease processes and stationary test outcome models, instead developing a general framework applicable to a wide range of disease models including dynamic survival processes and first hitting time models.

The authors provide a comprehensive mathematical formulation of the screening problem as a partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) with a general disease progression model. They rigorously prove that the value function is convex and piecewise linear, enabling the development of a computationally tractable approximation algorithm (Algorithm 1-2) that provides performance guarantees through approximation error bounds. This is a substantial technical advance over previous approaches that either assumed restrictive disease models or relied on simulation-based comparisons.

The paper's strengths are numerous. It is exceptionally well-written and technically sound, with thorough mathematical exposition that includes a complete computational complexity analysis. The authors clearly motivate the problem by situating it within the context of existing screening frameworks (both in operations research and medical literature), demonstrating how their approach overcomes key limitations of prior work. The validation on a large real-world dataset (45k patients from Athena Health) is particularly compelling, demonstrating 28-68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining equivalent disease detection delays compared to current clinical policies. The paper also validates across multiple disease model types, showing how the same framework can be applied to different diseases with varying delay cost structures (e.g., linear for breast cancer vs. quadratic for pancreatic cancer).

The paper's contribution is clearly significant. It addresses a fundamental healthcare challenge with substantial practical impact, offering a framework that could reduce healthcare costs while maintaining quality of care. The authors demonstrate the importance of personalization by showing how the constructed policy adapts to both patient-specific features and dynamic screening history, with Figure 1 providing an excellent graphical illustration of this personalization in action. The paper also carefully addresses the important issue of model estimation error and demonstrates that the proposed approach remains robust to reasonable estimation errors.

The only minor weakness is that the paper focuses primarily on breast cancer as a case study, though the authors do provide synthetic validation for different disease models. However, this limitation is well-justified given the need to demonstrate the method on a sufficiently large real dataset, and the synthetic validation across different cost structures adequately supports the broader applicability claim.

In terms of the NIPS evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical guarantees and thorough empirical validation.
- Clarity: The paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction.
- Originality: The paper makes a novel contribution by extending POMDP methods to the general personalized screening problem with external information processes.
- Significance: The work addresses an important healthcare problem with clear practical impact, demonstrated through significant efficiency gains in screening.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound contribution with strong empirical validation. It advances the state of the art in personalized healthcare decision-making with clear practical implications. The framework is generalizable to multiple disease classes and provides both theoretical guarantees and empirical evidence of its effectiveness.

Arguments against acceptance: None substantial. The paper's focus on breast cancer as a primary case study is justified by the need for a large real-world dataset, and the authors provide sufficient synthetic validation to demonstrate broader applicability.

This paper makes a clear technical contribution with potential for significant practical impact in healthcare. The methodological advances are substantial, the empirical validation is thorough, and the paper is well-written and technically sound. It represents a significant step forward in the application of decision-theoretic methods to personalized healthcare. I recommend acceptance.
