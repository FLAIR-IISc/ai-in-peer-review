- Proposal: DPSCREEN is a framework for dynamic, personalized screening that jointly minimizes screening burden and delay-to-detection with endogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent costs.  
- Modeling novelty: Decisions and costs depend on full state trajectories and exogenous information-driven arrivals, extending beyond standard POMDP/POSMDP assumptions.  
- Theoretical contributions: Bellman equations over trajectory beliefs, proof that the value function is convex and piecewise-linear in a high-dimensional belief space.  
- Algorithmic approach: A tailored point-based value iteration sampling likely physiological trajectories and reachable beliefs to make DP tractable.  
- Guarantees: Complexity characterization, an approximation-error bound tied to sampling density, and a robustness result showing local constancy of the optimal policy under small model perturbations.  
- Empirical results: On a large breast cancer dataset (45k women; 84k mammograms) DPSCREEN reduces expected screenings by 28–68% while matching delays of annual/biennial screening and personalizes to risk/history.  
- Sensitivity illustration: A synthetic study shows delay-cost function choice (linear vs quadratic) materially changes recommended screening intensity.  
- Relation to prior work: Extends OR/POMDP screening and Bayesian design methods by admitting non-Markov disease models, trajectory-dependent costs, and exogenous arrivals; goes beyond simulation-only guideline comparisons.  
- Strength: General, principled framework unifying multiple disease models, external information, and history-dependent personalization.  
- Strength: Strong technical development (formulation, piecewise linearity, tailored point-based DP, bounds, robustness).  
- Strength: Compelling empirical evidence on a large real dataset demonstrating meaningful burden reductions and personalization.  
- Strength: Clear positioning relative to OR and medical literature.  
- Weakness: Evaluation is model-based using outcomes simulated from an estimated model, limiting causal validity; recommends off-policy evaluation or sensitivity analyses.  
- Weakness: Error bound depends on strong assumptions (e.g., perfect tests, bounded intervals, large horizon) and introduces an abstract constant ζ without empirical calibration; recommends reporting performance vs sampling density and runtime/memory trade-offs.  
- Weakness: Computational scalability concerns due to worst-case complexity growth with horizon, observation/test spaces, belief points, and sampled trajectories; requests wall-clock timings and scaling plots.  
- Weakness: Limited baselines — comparisons only to annual/biennial schedules; suggests adding personalized baselines (risk/density-based intervals, ConfidentCare, info-gain designs).  
- Weakness: Heavy reliance on external information (self-exams); requests sensitivity analyses to self-exam parameters and subgroup analyses to avoid over-attributing gains.  
- Weakness: Practical deployment questions — guidance needed on choosing weight w, handling multiple tests/costs, and interpretability of recommended intervals.  
- Clarity: Generally clear and well organized, but notation-heavy sections should move key pseudo-code/graphical model to main text and add a small illustrative example.  
- Originality and significance: High originality (endogenous epochs, trajectory-dependent costs) and high potential impact if prospectively validated.  
- Acceptance pros: Novel general formulation, solid theory with tractable approximation, strong empirical results, and potential practical impact.  
- Acceptance cons: Model-based evaluation without causal validation, uncharacterized scalability and bound tightness, and sensitivity to external information and cost specification.  
- Questions for authors: Report runtime/memory/scaling with K and |B̄|; sensitivity to self-exam parameters and test accuracy; comparisons to risk/density-based and ConfidentCare baselines; and practical strategies for setting w or enforcing hard delay constraints.  
- Recommendation: Accept (high confidence).
