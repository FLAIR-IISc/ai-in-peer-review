**Paper Review: DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening**

**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The disease is modeled as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, and the method incorporates the possibility of external information that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their policy using breast cancer data and show that their personalized screening policies reduce the number of screenings performed while maintaining the same delay in detection of disease.

**Strengths**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of disease screening, which is the need for personalized screening policies that take into account individual patient characteristics and disease progression.
* The proposed methodology is novel and well-developed, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach.
* The use of breast cancer data to demonstrate the effectiveness of the policy is a strength, as it provides a concrete example of how the methodology can be applied in practice.
* The results are promising, showing significant reductions in the number of screenings performed while maintaining the same delay in detection of disease.

**Weaknesses**

* The method relies on the unrealistic assumption that each patient's disease model is known, whereas in practice model inference is the main challenge.
* The methodological novelty is limited, as the authors build on existing work in the field of partially observable Markov decision processes (POMDPs).
* The experiments only compare the proposed policy to a trivial annual baseline and do not assess robustness to model misspecification.
* The need to evaluate sensitivity to uncertainties in the disease model to establish practical relevance is not addressed.

**Originality**

* The paper proposes a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies, which is an original contribution to the field.
* The use of breast cancer data to demonstrate the effectiveness of the policy is a novel application of the methodology.

**Significance**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of disease screening, which is the need for personalized screening policies that take into account individual patient characteristics and disease progression.
* The results are promising, showing significant reductions in the number of screenings performed while maintaining the same delay in detection of disease.
* The paper has the potential to impact the field of disease screening, as personalized screening policies could lead to more effective and efficient disease detection.

**Quality**

* The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach.
* The claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results.
* The paper is a complete piece of work, and the authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work.

**Clarity**

* The paper is clearly written, and the authors provide a concise explanation of their approach.
* The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results.

**Recommendation**

* The paper is interesting and reasonable work, but it lacks novelty and practical validation to fully convince readers.
* I recommend acceptance of the paper, but with the condition that the authors address the limitations of the method, including the assumption that each patient's disease model is known, and provide more robust evaluation of the policy, including sensitivity to uncertainties in the disease model.
