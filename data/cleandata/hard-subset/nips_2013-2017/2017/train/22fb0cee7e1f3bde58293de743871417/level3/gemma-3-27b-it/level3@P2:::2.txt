This paper presents a framework for constructing personalized screening policies for diseases modeled as finite-state stochastic processes. The core idea is to balance the cost of frequent screenings against the cost of delayed disease detection, tailoring the policy to individual patient features and screening history. The authors address the computational intractability of finding the truly optimal policy by proposing a computationally tractable, approximately optimal construction, and demonstrate its effectiveness on a breast cancer dataset, achieving significant reductions in screening frequency while maintaining comparable detection delays compared to standard annual screening.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in screening and personalized medicine. The authors correctly position their work within the context of existing models for disease progression (e.g., Markov processes, survival analysis [7, 36]) and acknowledge limitations of prior work, such as restrictions to stationary processes or the inability to incorporate external information. The connection to controlled sensing [21, 34, 38] is also appropriately noted. This grounding in related literature demonstrates a clear understanding of the field.

The strengths of this paper lie in its formalization of a complex problem and the development of a tractable algorithmic approach. The model is general enough to accommodate a wide range of disease dynamics and incorporates both patient-specific risk factors and information gleaned from prior screenings. The theoretical guarantees on the approximation error (Proposition 1) are valuable, providing a bound on the performance loss incurred by using the approximate solution. The presentation is clear and well-organized, with a detailed description of the model and algorithm, and accompanying graphical illustrations (Figure 1). The illustrative experiments with breast cancer data are well-designed and demonstrate promising results, showing both personalization based on risk and history, and concrete reductions in screening frequency.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the proposed framework is theoretically sound, its practical relevance is somewhat limited by the strong assumption of known disease models. The authors acknowledge this (and address it partially in Section 3.3), but the reliance on accurate model parameter estimation is a significant hurdle in real-world applications. The experiment section lacks a more thorough sensitivity analysis to assess the robustness of the results to model misspecification, as noted in the key points. Furthermore, the comparison to a simple annual screening baseline is insufficient. More compelling evidence would be obtained by comparing against more sophisticated, established clinical policies, or by conducting simulations with more realistic patient populations and disease characteristics. The limited exploration of alternative cost functions (briefly touched upon in Section 4) could also be expanded upon to demonstrate the broader applicability of the framework.  The novelty of the methodological contributions is incremental; while the integration of various elements is well-executed, the individual components largely build on existing techniques.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in personalized medicine.
*   Presents a theoretically grounded and computationally tractable solution.
*   Demonstrates promising results on a real-world dataset.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Relies on strong assumptions regarding disease model knowledge.
*   Limited assessment of robustness to model misspecification.
*   Comparison to a simplistic baseline lacks convincing power.
*   Methodological novelty is incremental.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and technically sound paper that addresses an important problem. However, its practical impact is currently limited by the assumptions made about disease model knowledge and the lack of robust evaluation. While the results are promising, more extensive validation and sensitivity analysis are needed to fully convince the reader of the method's real-world applicability. I would rate this paper as a **weak accept**. Encouraging the authors to address the limitations regarding model uncertainty and to provide a more comprehensive evaluation would significantly strengthen the contribution. A minor revision addressing these points would be beneficial.
