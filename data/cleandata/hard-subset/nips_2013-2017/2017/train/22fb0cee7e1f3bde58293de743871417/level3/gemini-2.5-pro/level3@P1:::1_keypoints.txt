- Presents DPSCREEN, a general framework for dynamic, personalized screening that balances screening costs and delayed-detection costs using a non-Markovian finite-state disease model with an external information process.  
- Models screening as sequential decision-making under uncertainty and explicitly incorporates unscheduled patient-triggered visits (external information).  
- Uses a tractable PBVI-based approximation on sampled reachable belief points to handle the high-dimensional belief space.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees: approximation error bound (Proposition 1) and robustness to errors in the estimated disease model (Theorem 1).  
- Validated on a large real-world breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients), showing 28–68% reduction in expected screens without increasing detection delay.  
- Technical quality is high: rigorous formulation, principled approximation, and strong theoretical and empirical support.  
- Clarity is excellent: well-written, well-organized, and mathematically precise with helpful illustrations of personalization.  
- Original contributions include relaxing the Markov assumption, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and modeling endogenous unscheduled visits.  
- Significant potential impact: major clinical efficiency gains and applicability to various diseases and cost structures.  
- Weakness: lacks sensitivity analysis for fixed literature-derived model parameters used in the breast cancer experiment.  
- Weakness: practical computation times for experiments are not reported, limiting implementation guidance.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of discretization choices (e.g., discretizing continuous risk scores) and handling continuous/high-dimensional features.  
- Arguments for acceptance: high-impact problem, novel general framework, solid theory, clear writing, and strong real-data results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reviewer finds no major objections; identified weaknesses are minor and recommend addressing in revisions.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept — technically sound, original, significant, and well-validated.
