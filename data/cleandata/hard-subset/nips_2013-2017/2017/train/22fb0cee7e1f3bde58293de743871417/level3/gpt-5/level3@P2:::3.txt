Summary
This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a general framework for constructing dynamic, personalized screening policies that balance screening costs with delay-in-detection costs. Diseases are modeled as finite-state stochastic processes with an absorbing disease state; clinicians receive test outcomes at visits, while patients observe an external information process (e.g., self-exams, comorbidities) that can trigger unscheduled arrivals. The objective is to minimize a weighted sum of discounted screening costs and delay costs that depend on both incidence time and detection lag. The problem departs from standard POMDPs because (i) decision epochs are endogenous (determined by recommended follow-ups and external information), (ii) costs depend on trajectories, not just current state, and (iii) the disease process need not be Markovian or semi-Markov.

The paper shows that the value function remains convex and piecewise-linear in beliefs, enabling an alpha-vector characterization. Exact dynamic programming is intractable, so the authors adapt point-based value iteration by sampling a low-dimensional subspace of “typical” state trajectories and reachable beliefs under a mixture of random exploration and clinically motivated policies. They derive a bound on approximation error in terms of the sampling density of the belief simplex, and argue robustness to model estimation error via a local constancy result for the optimal action map.

Empirically, DPSCREEN is instantiated for breast cancer using a large observational dataset (45k women, 84k mammograms). The disease model is a two-state Markov process with risk features derived from the Gail model; self-exam signals follow a tumor growth model. Compared to annual screening (and in the supplement, biennial), the learned policies achieve similar expected detection delays with substantially fewer screenings: 28–45% reduction without self-exams and 57–68% with self-exams, with personalization by initial risk and dynamic history. A synthetic comparison shows that changing the delay cost from linear to quadratic makes the policy more aggressive, underscoring the importance of disease-specific costs.

Relation to prior work
- OR-based screening POMDPs (Ayer et al., Maillart; Erenay) assume Markov disease and stationary observation models; DPSCREEN allows general finite-state processes, exogenous external information, and trajectory-dependent costs.
- Bayesian design/joint modeling (Rizopoulos) optimizes information gain or prediction without endogenous arrivals or trajectory-based costs.
- Controlled sensing (Krishnamurthy, Sondik) focuses on stationary Markov processes and per-epoch costs; DPSCREEN departs via endogenous timing and trajectory costs.
- In NIPS/ML, the paper connects to PBVI (Pineau), decision making with endogenous info acquisition (Alaa), and disease trajectory modeling (Schulam), but contributes a new control formulation tailored to screening.

Quality
- Theory: The extension of piecewise-linear/convex value functions to this setting is sound and useful. The exact alpha-vector recursion is standard but infeasible; the PBVI-style approximation is appropriate. The approximation bound is intuitive but weakly informative without tighter control of the sampling-density constant ζ and relies on restrictive assumptions (notably perfect tests in the bound).
- Algorithms: Belief-space reduction via trajectory sampling and reachable-belief construction is well motivated; complexity and parallelization considerations are discussed.
- Empirics: The evaluation follows the accepted “learn model then simulate policies” practice for screening. Results are compelling in magnitude. However, sensitivity analyses to model misspecification/parameter uncertainty are limited, and there is no comparison to other optimized policies beyond guidelines.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. The modeling choices, belief updates, and Bellman equation are explained carefully, with details in the supplement. Some assumptions and notation could be clarified (see questions).

Originality
- Novel problem formulation combining endogenous arrivals, external information, and trajectory-dependent costs in a unified screening framework.
- Methodological contribution in adapting PBVI with trajectory-sampled belief spaces and providing an approximation guarantee in this nonstandard setting.
- Robustness result (local constancy of optimal actions) is a neat and practically relevant observation.

Significance
- If adopted, policies could reduce screenings substantially without increasing detection delays, an important clinical and economic impact.
- The framework is general and applicable to multiple diseases and alternative cost structures.
- The work should interest the NIPS community in RL/POMDPs, healthcare, and decision-making under uncertainty.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation that generalizes prior screening models.
- Solid theoretical underpinnings (value function structure; approximation and robustness statements).
- Strong empirical improvements on a large real-world dataset.
- Personalization demonstrated both by risk strata and dynamic test histories.
- Insightful illustration of how disease-specific cost functions shift policy regimes.

Weaknesses
- Approximation bound depends on strong assumptions (no false positives/negatives and bounded intervals) that do not hold in practice; its practical tightness is unclear.
- Limited sensitivity analyses to model misspecification and parameter uncertainty (e.g., tumor growth, self-exam noise/thresholds, test sensitivity/specificity).
- External information process is modeled and parameterized from literature but not validated on the dataset; results may be sensitive to the assumed y-threshold and noise.
- Comparisons are to guideline baselines; missing comparisons to other optimized screening models or heuristic policies learned from data.
- Computational scalability claims would benefit from concrete runtimes, number of belief points, and ablations (e.g., standard PBVI vs. proposed belief construction).

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify the diagnostic pathway and “no false positives” assumption: Z+ seems to include biopsy-confirmed positives only (z=3). Does the theoretical assumption in Proposition 1 require no false negatives at the biopsy stage as well?
- How sensitive are the results to the self-exam threshold ỹ and noise σ, and to the growth parameter ι? Can you report a sensitivity grid or CIs?
- What are the typical sizes of K and |B̄[t]| in the experiments, and the wall-clock/runtime/memory?
- Can you compare to an alternative optimized policy (e.g., a POMDP assuming Markov disease and no external info) to isolate the value of endogenous arrivals and trajectory costs?
- How is overfitting controlled in model estimation (train/validation/test split; posterior predictive checks)?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong and general formulation; clear novelty beyond existing screening POMDPs.
- Meaningful theoretical properties and a practical algorithmic approach.
- Large empirical gains on real data; clinically relevant.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical guarantees are limited by unrealistic assumptions in the bound.
- Sensitivity and comparative baselines could be strengthened.
- Computational performance details and ablations are sparse.

Recommendation
Lean accept. This is a well-executed, timely contribution at the intersection of POMDPs and healthcare with both methodological and applied impact. Strengthening sensitivity analyses and adding comparative baselines would further solidify the case.
