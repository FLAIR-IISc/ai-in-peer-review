- Proposes DPSCREEN: a framework that balances screening cost against detection delay using personalized screening policies.  
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and external triggers (e.g., self-exams).  
- Develops a computationally tractable approximate solution to an otherwise intractable optimal screening policy problem.  
- Validated on a breast cancer dataset showing 28–68% reduction in screenings while maintaining the same expected detection delay versus annual screening.  
- Extends prior POMDP-based screening work by allowing non-Markov disease dynamics and non-stationary test outcome models.  
- Relates to controlled sensing literature but generalizes beyond common structural assumptions like stationarity.  

Strengths:
- Well-written, clearly organized, and technically sound with precise mathematical formulation.  
- Theoretical contribution: proves the value function is convex and piecewise linear and provides an approximation-error bound.  
- Promising empirical results showing substantial screening reductions.  
- Personalization using patient features and dynamic test history is clinically intuitive and well explained.  

Weaknesses:
- Unrealistic assumption that the disease model is known; estimating it from observational data is not adequately addressed.  
- Experiments only compare to a trivial annual screening baseline, omitting more meaningful clinical baselines.  
- Lacks sensitivity analysis for robustness to model misspecification, critical for deployment.  
- Methodological novelty is limited—uses standard POMDP/point-based value iteration extended to a broader setting.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, relevant healthcare problem.  
- Provides a clear generalization of existing POMDP screening frameworks.  
- Demonstrates potential for reducing unnecessary screenings with promising numerical gains.  
- Technically rigorous and well-presented.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Practical relevance weakened by assumption of a known disease model.  
- Weak experimental comparisons lacking meaningful alternative baselines.  
- Does not adequately tackle the core challenge of model estimation from observational data.  
- Contribution is incremental rather than transformative.  

Overall assessment:
- Well-executed and technically solid but limited by insufficient attention to data-driven model estimation and weak empirical baselines; impact is reduced.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject (3).  

Suggested improvements:
- Provide a detailed discussion and method for estimating the disease model from observational data.  
- Compare against more sophisticated/risk-based screening baselines.  
- Conduct sensitivity analysis to assess robustness to model misspecification.  
- More clearly articulate methodological novelty beyond extending existing POMDP frameworks.
