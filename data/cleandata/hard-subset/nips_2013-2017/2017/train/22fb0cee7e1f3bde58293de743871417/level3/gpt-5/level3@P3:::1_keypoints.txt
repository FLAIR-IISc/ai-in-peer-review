- Proposes DPSCREEN: a dynamic, personalized screening framework choosing follow-up times to trade screening burden vs detection delay.
- Models disease as finite-state stochastic trajectories with an absorbing disease state and belief updates using patient features and history (tests, self-exams).
- Proves piecewise-linear convexity of the value function in beliefs and constructs an approximate policy via point-based value iteration (PBVI) on a sampled lower-dimensional belief space.
- Provides complexity analysis, an approximation-error bound that improves with belief-space coverage density, and a local robustness result to small model-estimation errors.
- Extends prior POMDP/screening work by allowing non-Markov/trajectory-dependent disease processes, external-triggered arrivals, and trajectory-dependent delay costs.
- Subsumes survival and first-hitting-time models and goes beyond simulation-only fixed-policy comparisons common in prior literature.
- Empirically instantiated for breast cancer on a large real-world Athena Health dataset (45k patients, ~84k mammograms).
- Demonstrates 28–68% reductions in screenings at comparable expected detection delay across scenarios (with/without self-exams, varying delay-cost shapes).
- Clear technical contribution with rigorous proofs, tailored PBVI algorithm, and theoretical guarantees on structure and approximation.
- Personalization is substantive: policies adapt to baseline risk (features/Gail score) and evolving evidence (test outcomes, self-exams), with stratified analyses.
- Strong empirical validation using realistic mammogram sensitivity/specificity and growth modeling choices.
- Robustness insight: optimal actions are locally invariant to small model perturbations, supporting practical model-based optimization.
- Broad applicability: framework applies to dynamic survival, hitting-time, and other finite-state trajectories and highlights sensitivity to delay-cost shapes.
- Well written, well situated in literature, with helpful graph-model perspective and supplementary algorithms aiding reproducibility.
- Evaluation depends on a learned disease model with no empirical off-policy or sensitivity analyses to model misspecification beyond theory.
- Limited generalizability: experiments focus on women aged 60–65 and a single disease; more diseases or semi-synthetic non-Markov studies would strengthen claims.
- Computational reporting is incomplete: empirical runtimes, scaling with trajectory samples K and reachable beliefs |B̄|, and action-space restriction impacts are not provided.
- Ablation needed to isolate personalization components (feature-only vs history-only vs combined) beyond risk stratification.
- Theoretical assumptions simplify diagnosis/tests (e.g., some analyses assume no false positives/negatives) but empirical model uses realistic error rates; reconciliation needed.
- Policy-selection could present a constrained optimization view or Pareto frontier (tests vs delay) instead of tuning weight w to match target delay.
- Fairness/ethics concerns: personalization may reduce screenings for low-risk groups; subgroup analyses (race, density) should be reported to assess equity.
- Paper is technically sound with rigorous derivations and realistic experiments, but would benefit from more empirical sensitivity and computational performance reporting.
- Mathematical exposition is concise and complete; minor clarity fixes include collecting assumptions, adding intuition for key theorems, and an illustrative toy example.
- High originality by incorporating external arrivals, trajectory-dependent costs, and PBVI over trajectory-belief space in screening.
- High significance given clinical relevance and large efficiency gains; framework likely to influence future research and practice.
- Pros for acceptance: principled framework, theoretical guarantees, strong real-world validation, practical algorithm, and demonstrated personalization benefits.
- Cons for acceptance: single-disease/age-range evaluation, limited empirical sensitivity/scaling results, simplifying theoretical assumptions vs clinical reality, and limited personalization ablations.
- Overall assessment: well-executed, impactful paper recommended for accept, with suggested additions on sensitivity, scalability, and broader validation.
