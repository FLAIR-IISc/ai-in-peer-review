The paper "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening" proposes a novel approach to constructing personalized screening policies for diseases, aiming to minimize screening costs and delays. The authors model the disease as a finite-state stochastic process and develop a computationally efficient approximate solution to find the optimal personalized screening policy.

The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the problem formulation, the proposed approach, and the experimental results. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method using a large breast cancer dataset and show that their policy reduces the number of screenings performed while maintaining the same delay in detection of disease compared to current clinical policies.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to address the challenges of personalized screening, including the need to account for individual patient features and dynamic history. The authors' use of a finite-state stochastic process to model the disease is a reasonable approach, and their development of a computationally efficient approximate solution is a significant contribution.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the main assumptions of the method is that the disease model is known, which may not be realistic in practice. The authors acknowledge this limitation and discuss the potential impact of estimation error on the policy. However, they do not provide a thorough evaluation of the robustness of their method to model misspecification.

Another limitation is that the experiments only compare the proposed policy to a trivial annual screening baseline. It would be more convincing to compare the policy to other existing methods, such as those proposed in the literature on personalized screening.

In terms of novelty, the paper builds upon existing work on personalized screening and dynamic programming. While the authors' approach is not entirely new, their application of these techniques to the problem of personalized screening is a significant contribution.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed approach. The experimental results are promising, but more work is needed to fully establish the practical relevance of the method. The authors should consider evaluating the sensitivity of their method to uncertainties in the disease model and comparing their policy to other existing methods.
