- Proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized screening policies balancing screening costs against detection delays.  
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and incorporates external information (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger early visits.  
- Exact optimal policy is intractable; authors use a tractable approximation based on point-based value iteration for POMDPs.  
- Extends standard POMDPs to handle delay costs that depend on state trajectories (not just current state) and to incorporate external information processes.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees bounding the approximation error of the proposed solution.  
- Demonstrates on breast cancer screening using a large Athena Health Network dataset.  
- Empirical result: personalized policies reduce screenings by 28–68% versus annual screening while maintaining similar expected detection delays.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly structured, and theoretically sound with careful modeling assumptions.  
- Experimental comparisons to clinical practice are appropriate, but baseline set is limited (only annual screening).  
- Originality: meaningful extension of POMDPs to this screening setting, but methodological novelty is limited since point-based VI is well-established.  
- Significance: potentially large practical impact by reducing unnecessary screenings and patient burden; importance of delay-cost choice is highlighted.  
- Major limitation: unrealistic assumption that disease model parameters are known; no thorough analysis of robustness to model misspecification or estimation error.  
- Evaluation gap: lacks comparisons to other personalized screening or state-of-the-art decision-theoretic approaches.  
- Recommendation: interesting, practically relevant work; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen robustness evaluation and better contextualize novelty.
