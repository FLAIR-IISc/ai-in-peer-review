- Proposes DPSCREEN, a dynamic personalized screening framework adapting to patient features, screening history, and external data.  
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and optimizes screening to balance cost and delay.  
- Extends prior decision-theoretic screening work by relaxing Markovian progression and stationary test-outcome assumptions.  
- Introduces a Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) approximation with a sampling strategy for high-dimensional belief spaces.  
- Empirical results on a large breast cancer dataset show 28–68% reduction in screening frequency while maintaining comparable delays.  
- Demonstrates personalization effectively (e.g., Figure 1) via adaptation to initial risk and subsequent test results.  
- Discusses robustness to model estimation error and the effects of different cost functions.  
- Needs clearer linkage between the abstract model and breast cancer implementation details (e.g., lump growth parameter choices).  
- Requires more sensitivity analysis on modeling assumptions, such as reliance on the Gail model.  
- Should include deeper comparisons with other personalized screening strategies beyond standard clinical policies.  
- Mathematical formulation may be difficult for readers outside ML/OR communities.  
- Dependence on specific modeling choices (e.g., tumor growth law) warrants further scrutiny.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper with significant impact; recommend acceptance pending minor revisions for clarity and assumption discussion.
