- Addresses personalized patient screening by modeling diseases as finite-state stochastic processes and proposing an approximate cost-delay minimizing solution.  
- Novel framework incorporates patient features, dynamic history, and external information (e.g., self-exams) to build personalized screening policies.  
- Algorithm is computationally efficient and comes with theoretical guarantees on approximation error.  
- Experiments on a breast cancer dataset show 28–68% reductions in screenings while maintaining comparable detection delays to current clinical policies.  
- Methodology is general and applicable to a wide range of diseases.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient technical detail for reproducibility.  
- Includes robustness analysis and sensitivity to model inaccuracies.  
- Assumes disease progression models are known a priori, which is unrealistic in practice and underexplored empirically.  
- Theoretical treatment of estimation error is present but practical implications are not thoroughly examined.  
- Methodological novelty is limited, largely adapting existing techniques like PBVI for screening.  
- Experimental baselines are trivial (e.g., annual screening); lacks comparison to state-of-the-art personalized screening methods.  
- Experiments do not sufficiently test robustness to significant model inaccuracies, critical for deployment.  
- Overall: promising and relevant contribution but limited by assumptions and novelty; acceptance recommended only if robustness and stronger baselines are addressed.
