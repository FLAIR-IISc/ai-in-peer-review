- Proposes DPSCREEN: a framework that balances screening costs against delay costs using a finite-state stochastic disease model with an absorbing disease state and external information processes.  
- Main contribution: a computationally tractable method to construct approximately optimal personalized screening policies with theoretical approximation error bounds.  
- Extends prior work (POMDP-based and joint longitudinal-survival models) by handling non-stationary disease progression and incorporating external information.  
- Develops a point-based value iteration algorithm with provable error bounds (Proposition 1).  
- Theoretical support: Lemma 1, Proposition 1, and Theorem 1 justify the approximation approach.  
- Empirical results on a large breast cancer dataset show 28–68% reductions in screenings while maintaining the same expected detection delay.  
- Provides analysis of how different delay cost structures (linear vs. quadratic) affect optimal screening policies.  
- Demonstrates personalization and practical improvements via clear examples and comparisons to current clinical practice.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written with precise problem formulation and reproducible algorithmic details.  
- Synthetic experiments indicate the framework’s broader applicability beyond breast cancer.  
- Primary weakness: experimental validation focused mainly on breast cancer as the single real-world case study.  
- Secondary concern: technical complexity may require careful implementation.  
- Significance: potential for substantial healthcare cost savings and reduced patient burden.  
- Recommendation: strong acceptance for NIPS.
