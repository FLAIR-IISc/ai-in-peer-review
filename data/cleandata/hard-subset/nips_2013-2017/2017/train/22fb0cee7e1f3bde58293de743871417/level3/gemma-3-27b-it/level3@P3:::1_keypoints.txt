- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework that dynamically personalizes screening frequency using patient features and screening history to balance detection delay and screening costs.  
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and uses Bayesian updating to refine beliefs about patient state.  
- Develops a computationally tractable algorithm that approximates the optimal policy with bounded error.  
- Validates method on a large real-world breast cancer dataset (Athena Health, 45k patients).  
- Demonstrates robustness to model estimation error and analyzes sensitivity to the choice of cost function.  
- Shows substantial empirical gains: 28–68% reductions in screenings while maintaining detection delay.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and provides a concise mathematical exposition.  
- Situates work well within decision theory, operations research, and medical screening literature.  
- Provides valuable computational complexity analysis and discusses practical tractability.  
- Novelty/significance: addresses dynamic personalized screening beyond static population approaches.  
- Technical rigor: clear formulation and justified approximation strategy acknowledging intractability of exact optimality.  
- Thoroughness: includes synthetic experiments across disease model types to augment real-data validation.  
- Weakness: finite-state disease model is a simplifying assumption that may not capture complex real-world dynamics.  
- Weakness: independence assumption between test results and external information given state/features may not hold in practice.  
- Weakness: computational complexity (O(T(B)^2 T^2 K |Y| |Z|)) could be prohibitive for large state spaces or long horizons.  
- Weakness: empirical validation is concentrated on breast cancer; broader disease evaluation would strengthen generalizability.  
- Weakness: Athena dataset reflects a specific population, so policy transferability to other populations is uncertain.  
- Acceptance arguments: significant technical contribution, strong empirical improvements, rigorous framework, and real-world validation.  
- Rejection concerns: model assumptions limit applicability, computational scalability challenges, and limited disease coverage in experiments.  
- Overall assessment: strong, technically rigorous, and impactful paper; recommended for acceptance despite noted limitations.
