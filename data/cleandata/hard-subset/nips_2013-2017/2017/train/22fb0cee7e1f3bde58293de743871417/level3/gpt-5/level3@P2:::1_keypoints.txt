- Proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized screening balancing screening frequency against detection delay using a finite-state disease model with external information.

- Models endogenous decision timing and trajectory-dependent delay costs, departing from standard POMDP/POSMDP frameworks.

- Derives belief-space dynamic programming, proving the value function is convex and piecewise-linear and characterizing exact policies via alpha-vectors.

- Shows exact alpha-vector policy is intractable and proposes a tractable point-based approximation sampling likely trajectories and reachable beliefs.

- Provides approximation-error bounds based on sampling density and a robustness result showing local constancy of optimal actions under small model errors.

- Empirical study on a large real-world dataset (45k women, 84k mammograms) with a two-state model demonstrates 28–68% screening reductions versus clinical schedules at matched expected delay.

- Incorporating self-exams yields stronger gains; choice of delay cost (linear vs quadratic) shifts the policy operating regime.

- Advances prior screening work by relaxing Markov/stationarity assumptions, modeling external arrivals and action-dependent timing, and optimizing beyond fixed policy menus.

- Strength: clear, well-motivated formulation capturing personalization, external arrivals, and trajectory-based costs.

- Strength: solid theoretical framing with principled point-based approximation and tractable complexity characterization.

- Strength: strong empirical illustration using large real data and literature-grounded parameter estimates.

- Strength: careful baseline comparisons, analyses with/without self-exams, and exploration of different delay costs.

- Strength: high potential practical impact given interest in risk- and history-adaptive screening.

- Weakness: restrictive assumptions—no false positives and absence of false negatives in approximation bounds simplify diagnosis dynamics.

- Weakness: conflating equal unit cost for mammography and biopsy overlooks distinct burdens; differentiated-cost sensitivity analysis needed.

- Weakness: approximation bound involves unspecified constants (Ω(B̄), ζ) and L1 coverage; lacks practical guidance on controlling sampling choices (K, B̄, horizon).

- Weakness: model-based evaluation risks misspecification bias; more empirical sensitivity analyses (bootstrap, stress-tests, held-out calibration) are needed.

- Weakness: external-information model (thresholded self-exam) may be simplistic and not generalize to settings without recommended self-exams.

- Weakness: complexity expression has repeated T factors; clarify scaling, report wall-clock runtimes, K, |B̄[t]|, and parallelization details.

- Clarity: heavy trajectory-based notation can obscure intuition; a small worked example illustrating belief updates/action selection would help and define Z+ earlier.

- Originality: novel extension of POMDP machinery to endogenous timing and trajectory-dependent costs plus tailored point-based approximations.

- Significance: substantial potential screening reductions for the same expected delay and broad applicability across diseases and cost structures.

- Pro: addresses an important problem with sound theory, practical approximation guarantees, and large-scale realistic empirical gains.

- Con: practical assumptions simplified; evaluation limited to a single real disease model and proprietary data; reproducibility/code release not stated.

- Questions for authors: report runtimes, K, |B̄[t]|, sensitivity to these choices, effects of realistic false positive/negative rates and distinct test costs, uncertainty bands from parameter posteriors, examples of other external signals, and behavior under longer horizons/maximum interval constraints.

- Overall recommendation: strong, timely contribution with solid theory and promising empirical results; lean toward acceptance pending clarification of computational details, relaxing/testing key assumptions, and stronger sensitivity analyses.
