Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing personalized screening policies across multiple diseases.

Formulates screening as a POMDP and develops a computationally tractable approximation for near-optimal policies using patient features and screening history.

Addresses a literature gap by moving beyond population-level or disease-specific screening to dynamic, personalized strategies.

Carefully situates the approach within decision-theoretic screening work and highlights prior limitations handling dynamic progression and patient-driven testing.

Technically sound with a concise, thorough mathematical exposition.

Includes computational complexity analysis that acknowledges inherent problem challenges.

Validated on a large real-world dataset (Athena Health, 45k patients), lending empirical credibility.

Demonstrates reductions in screening frequency without compromising detection delay across multiple disease models.

Shows clear personalization effects via illustrative examples reflecting different patient risk profiles.

Weakness: assumes a single absorbing disease state, which may be overly simplistic for diseases with intermediate stages.

Weakness: depends on the Gail model for initial risk assessment, introducing potential model biases.

Weakness: cost-benefit analysis is limited and could better address patient anxiety and harms from false positives.

Arguments for acceptance: novel and significant approach, technical rigor, strong empirical validation, and clear presentation.

Arguments against acceptance: simplifying single-state assumption and dependency on the Gail model.

Overall recommendation: strong paper with minor weaknesses; reviewer recommends acceptance.
