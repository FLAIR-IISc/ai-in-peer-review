- Proposes DPSCREEN to construct personalized screening policies by modeling disease as a finite-state stochastic process.  
- Tackles the clinically important trade-off between screening frequency and detection delay.  
- Core contribution: a computationally efficient approximate solution to an otherwise intractable optimal screening policy, framed as a POMDP with external information processes.  
- Method is technically sound with clear theoretical justification and approximation error bounds.  
- Experiments on breast cancer data report 28–68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining similar detection delays vs. annual screening.  
- Effectively demonstrates personalization to patient features and dynamic history, improving over non-personalized approaches.  
- Major limitation: assumes disease models are perfectly known, which is unrealistic—model inference from observational data is not addressed.  
- Limited methodological novelty: builds on existing POMDP approaches with only minor extensions.  
- Insufficient experimental validation: only compared to an annual baseline, lacking comparisons to more sophisticated or risk-stratified protocols.  
- No robustness or sensitivity analysis to model misspecification or uncertainty in disease parameters.  
- Recommendation: address model uncertainty and include more comprehensive baseline comparisons and robustness analyses.  
- Overall: reasonable technical contribution but limited novelty and practical validation reduce its strength for NIPS.
