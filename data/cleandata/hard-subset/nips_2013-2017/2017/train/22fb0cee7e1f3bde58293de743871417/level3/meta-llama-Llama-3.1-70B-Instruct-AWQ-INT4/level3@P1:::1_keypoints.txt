- Introduces DPSCREEN: a method for personalized screening that trades off screening cost vs delay, using patient history and features; models disease as a finite-state stochastic process with external arrivals; validated on 45k breast-cancer patients.  
- Positions DPSCREEN against POMDP/Bayesian methods, claiming greater flexibility and ability to incorporate external information without restrictive model assumptions.  

Strengths:
- Technical soundness: clear mathematical exposition and computational-complexity analysis.  
- Personalization: uses screening history and patient features to yield efficiency gains over existing policies.  
- Validation: demonstrated improvements on a large, real-world 45k-patient dataset.  
- Technical contribution: novel methodology with potential practical impact.  

Weaknesses:
- Computational complexity: worst-case runtime may be high, especially for large datasets.  
- Model assumptions: assumes the disease model is known, making performance sensitive to estimation error.  
- Limited generalizability: unclear how well the approach transfers to other diseases and settings.  

Originality:
- Novel integration of patient history and features into screening-policy construction, distinct from prior POMDP/Bayesian approaches.  

Significance:
- Potential to improve screening efficiency, health outcomes, and reduce healthcare costs.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel, well-motivated, technically sound, and validated on large real-world data with practical implications.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- High computational cost, dependence on known disease models, and uncertain generalizability.  

Overall recommendation:
- Strengths outweigh weaknesses; reviewer recommends acceptance.
