- Proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies balancing screening costs and delay-in-detection costs under general finite-state disease processes.  
- Models clinicians receiving test outcomes at visits while patients observe external signals that can trigger unscheduled arrivals.  
- Objective minimizes discounted sum of screening costs and trajectory-dependent delay costs; departs from standard POMDPs due to endogenous epochs, trajectory costs, and non-Markov disease processes.  
- Proves the value function is convex and piecewise-linear in beliefs, yielding an alpha-vector characterization.  
- Exact dynamic programming is infeasible, so authors adapt point-based value iteration (PBVI) by sampling “typical” trajectories and reachable beliefs.  
- Derives an approximation error bound based on belief-sampling density and provides a local constancy robustness result for the optimal action map.  
- Empirical instantiation for breast cancer uses a large observational dataset (45k women, 84k mammograms) with a two-state Markov disease model and self-exam tumor-growth signals.  
- Learned policies match detection delays of annual screening while reducing screenings by 28–45% without self-exams and 57–68% with self-exams, and personalize by baseline risk and history.  
- Synthetic experiment shows quadratic delay costs produce more aggressive policies, highlighting dependence on disease-specific cost structure.  
- Differs from prior OR-based screening POMDPs by allowing non-Markov disease dynamics, exogenous external information, and trajectory-dependent costs.  
- Distinct from Bayesian design/joint modeling by explicitly modeling endogenous arrivals and trajectory costs rather than optimizing prediction/information gain.  
- Extends controlled sensing and PBVI ideas from ML literature to a novel screening control formulation with endogenous timing.  
- Theoretical contribution: extending piecewise-linear/convex value functions to this nonstandard setting is sound but the exact recursion is infeasible.  
- Approximation bound is intuitive but limited by a poorly controlled sampling-density constant ζ and restrictive assumptions (notably perfect tests).  
- Algorithmic contribution: belief-space reduction via trajectory sampling and reachable-belief construction is well motivated, with complexity and parallelization discussed.  
- Empirical evaluation follows standard “learn model then simulate” practice and shows compelling gains, but lacks sensitivity analyses to model misspecification and uncertainty.  
- Clarity: paper is well written and organized, with careful explanations and supplementary details, though some assumptions and notation need clarification.  
- Originality: novel problem formulation combining endogenous arrivals, external info, and trajectory costs; methodological PBVI adaptation and a useful robustness observation.  
- Significance: potential for substantial screening reductions with clinical and economic impact; framework is generalizable to multiple diseases and cost structures.  
- Strengths: principled, general formulation; solid theoretical grounding; strong empirical improvements on real data; demonstrated personalization; insightful cost-structure analysis.  
- Weaknesses: approximation bound relies on unrealistic assumptions; limited sensitivity analyses and validation of external info process; missing comparisons to other optimized policies and ablation/runtime details.  
- Key questions: clarify the “no false positives”/diagnostic-pathway assumption; report sensitivity to self-exam threshold, noise, and growth parameter; provide K and |B̄[t]| sizes and runtimes; compare to alternative optimized policies; explain overfitting controls in model estimation.  
- Recommendation: lean accept, contingent on strengthening sensitivity analyses, comparative baselines, and computational/ablation details.
