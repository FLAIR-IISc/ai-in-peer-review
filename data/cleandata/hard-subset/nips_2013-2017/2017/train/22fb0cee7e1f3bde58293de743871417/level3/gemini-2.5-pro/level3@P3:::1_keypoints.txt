- Introduces DPSCREEN, a general framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off screening cost versus delayed detection cost.  
- Models disease progression as a general finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, covering many existing models.  
- Personalization uses two information sources: static patient features (prior risk) and dynamic patient history (past test outcomes).  
- Uniquely incorporates an external information process (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger unscheduled visits.  
- Allows delay costs to depend on the entire disease trajectory, not just the current state.  
- Formulates the problem as an intractable Bellman optimization and proposes a tractable PBVI-based approximation.  
- Provides theoretical contributions: an upper bound on approximation error (Proposition 1) and robustness analysis to model estimation errors (Theorem 1).  
- Situates work relative to prior POMDP and simulation approaches, highlighting greater generality and optimization over policy space.  
- Empirically validated on a large real-world dataset (45,000 patients from Athena Health) with transparent experimental setup.  
- Demonstrates substantial practical gains: 28–68% fewer screenings than annual policies for the same expected detection delay.  
- Paper quality is high: rigorous formulation, sound math, and well-motivated approximation.  
- Presentation is clear and well-written, with intuitive figures and logical flow.  
- Originality is strong: broader disease models, external information modeling, and trajectory-dependent costs are novel.  
- Significance is high: meaningful potential impact on public health and adaptable to other diseases.  
- Minor weaknesses: computational complexity depends heavily on sampled belief points and horizon—practical runtime details would help.  
- Minor theoretical caveat: Proposition 1 assumptions (e.g., perfect test accuracy, convex costs) may not fully hold; discussion of relaxed assumptions would be useful.  
- For acceptance: high-impact problem, novel general framework, solid theory, large-scale validation, and strong empirical improvements.  
- Against acceptance: no significant objections raised.
