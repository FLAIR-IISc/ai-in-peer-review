- Proposes DPSCREEN, a general decision-theoretic framework for dynamic, personalized disease screening balancing screening cost vs. detection delay.
- Models disease as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process, generalizing beyond Markovian assumptions common in prior work.
- Introduces an external information process to model events (e.g., self-exams) that trigger unscheduled visits, endogenizing decision times.
- Uses Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) over sampled belief points as a tractable approximation to the intractable optimal policy.
- Demonstrates on a large real-world breast cancer dataset, reducing screenings by 28–68% while maintaining the same expected detection delay.
- Strength: highly significant and impactful problem with potential to improve outcomes, reduce costs, and generalize to other diseases.
- Strength: original and novel modeling of external information and non-Markovian disease dynamics relative to prior POMDP screening work.
- Strength: technically sound formulation, theoretical analysis (approximation bound, robustness result), and strong empirical evaluation.
- Strength: exceptionally clear writing and intuitive figures showing personalization by static risk and dynamic history.
- Weakness: worst-case computational complexity is high; more practical scalability details (e.g., runtime) and limits are needed.
- Weakness: theoretical guarantees rely on strong assumptions (perfect tests, convex delay cost) that differ from real-world settings like mammography.
- Weakness: limited detail on disease model estimation and identifiability from observational data collected under suboptimal screening policies.
- For acceptance: addresses an important problem, offers a novel general framework with realistic external-information modeling, solid theory, and strong real-data results.
- Against acceptance: concerns about practical scalability to richer problems and reliance on simplifying assumptions for theoretical bounds.
- Overall: an excellent, influential contribution whose strengths (novelty and empirical impact) outweigh its noted limitations.
