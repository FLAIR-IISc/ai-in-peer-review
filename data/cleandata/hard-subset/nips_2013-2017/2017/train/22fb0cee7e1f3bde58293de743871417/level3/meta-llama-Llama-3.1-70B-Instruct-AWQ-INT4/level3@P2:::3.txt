This paper proposes a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The disease is modeled as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state. The authors incorporate the possibility of external information, such as self-examination or discovery of co-morbidities, that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment. The proposed approach is evaluated using a large breast cancer data set, and the results show significant improvement over existing clinical policies.

The main idea of the paper is to develop a dynamic personalized screening policy that takes into account the individual's medical history and external information. The authors use a finite state stochastic process to model the disease progression and incorporate the possibility of external information that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician. The proposed approach is based on a point-based value iteration algorithm, which is a variant of the standard value iteration algorithm used in Markov decision processes.

The paper is well written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed approach. The empirical results show significant improvement over existing methods, with a reduction of 28-68% in the number of screens performed while achieving the same expected delays in disease detection. The authors also provide a comparison with other relevant works in the field, highlighting the advantages of their approach.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to incorporate external information into the screening policy. This is a significant improvement over existing methods, which typically rely on a fixed screening schedule. The authors also provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed approach, making it easy to follow and understand.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the main limitations is the assumption that the disease progression follows a finite state stochastic process. While this assumption may be reasonable for some diseases, it may not be accurate for others. Additionally, the authors assume that the external information is independent of the past observations, which may not always be the case.
