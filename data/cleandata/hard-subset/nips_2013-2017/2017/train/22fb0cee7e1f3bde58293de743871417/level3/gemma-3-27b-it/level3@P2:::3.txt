This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for constructing personalized screening policies that dynamically adjust to patient features and screening history, aiming to balance the costs of screening and delayed disease detection. The approach models disease progression as a finite state stochastic process and incorporates external information influencing patient arrival for screening. The core contribution lies in providing a computationally tractable method for approximating the optimal screening policy, a challenge due to the inherent complexity of the problem. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method using a large breast cancer dataset, showcasing significant reductions in screening frequency while maintaining comparable detection delays compared to existing clinical practices.

The paper builds upon a rich body of work in screening methodologies, referencing key contributions in operations research and medical literature. It acknowledges limitations of previous approaches, such as their reliance on Markovian disease processes and stationary test outcome models, which do not always reflect real-world complexities.  The work correctly situates itself relative to POMDP-based approaches [7,9], Bayesian design [10], and simulation-based methods prevalent in the medical field, highlighting how DPSCREEN offers greater flexibility in modeling disease dynamics and incorporating diverse information sources. The connections drawn to controlled sensing [21,34,38] and dynamic Bayesian networks are also relevant and help contextualize the approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed methodology is grounded in a solid theoretical framework, effectively formulating the personalized screening problem as a stochastic optimization problem. The approximation algorithm (PBVI) is well-motivated and the bounding of the approximation error (Proposition 1) provides valuable insight into the quality of the solution. The authors acknowledge and address the computational intractability of the exact solution, offering a practical alternative.
* **Novelty & Originality:** The ability to handle non-Markovian disease processes and incorporate external information sets this work apart from many existing screening frameworks.  The personalization aspects, adapting to both patient features and screening history, represent a significant step toward more tailored and efficient screening strategies.
* **Empirical Validation:** The application to a large breast cancer dataset provides compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness. The reported reductions in screening frequency (28-68%) without compromising detection delays are substantial and demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach. The analysis comparing performance across risk groups and cost functions further strengthens the empirical validation.
* **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The model formulation is clearly explained, and the algorithm description is sufficiently detailed. The inclusion of a graphical model (mentioned in the text, though absent from the submitted materials) would further enhance clarity.  The discussion of robustness to model estimation error is a valuable addition.
* **Significance:** The potential impact of this work is significant. Reducing screening frequency while maintaining detection rates could lead to substantial cost savings, decreased patient burden (reducing invasive procedures and anxiety), and improved healthcare resource allocation. The methodology is broadly applicable to a range of diseases beyond breast cancer.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Model Assumptions:** While the framework is flexible, it still relies on several assumptions, such as the finite state representation of the disease process and the independence assumptions regarding test outcomes and external information. The sensitivity of the results to violations of these assumptions should be further investigated.
* **Computational Complexity:** Despite the approximation, the computational complexity remains a concern, especially for very large datasets or complex disease models. Further optimization of the algorithm or exploration of parallelization techniques might be necessary.
* **Parameter Estimation:** The description of the model estimation process using the Gail model and MCMC methods is somewhat brief. More details on the parameter estimation procedure, including validation of the estimated model parameters, would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of External Information:** The current implementation focuses on a single type of external information (breast lump size). Expanding the framework to incorporate other relevant external factors (e.g., family history updates, lifestyle changes) could further improve the personalization and accuracy of the screening policies.
* **Graphical Model Absence:** The text refers to a graphical model in the Appendix B of the Supplementary Materials, but this appendix was not provided with the submitted paper. Its inclusion would significantly improve understanding of the system.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in healthcare with a novel and technically sound approach.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a significant contribution to the field.
* The personalization aspects are particularly valuable and align with the growing trend toward precision medicine.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity could be a barrier to widespread adoption.
* The model assumptions may limit the applicability of the method to certain diseases or scenarios.
* The lack of detail regarding the parameter estimation process and the missing appendix limit full assessment of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance. The proposed DPSCREEN framework offers a promising approach to personalized screening, with the potential to significantly improve healthcare outcomes and reduce costs. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting the inclusion of the missing Appendix B (containing the graphical model) and a more detailed description of the parameter estimation procedure. A discussion regarding the sensitivity of results to the model assumptions would also be valuable.



**Rating:** 7 (Strong Accept)
