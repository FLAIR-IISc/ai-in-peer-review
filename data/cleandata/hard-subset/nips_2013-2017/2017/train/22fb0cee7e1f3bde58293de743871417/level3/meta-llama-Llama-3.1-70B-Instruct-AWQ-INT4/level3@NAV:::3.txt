**Review of DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening**

**Summary**

The paper proposes a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The disease is modeled as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state. The method incorporates the possibility of external information, such as self-examination or discovery of co-morbidities, that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their policy using breast cancer data and show that their personalized screening policies reduce the number of screenings performed while maintaining the same delay in detection of disease.

**Strengths**

1. **Well-written and empirically sound**: The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the methodology and its application to breast cancer screening.
2. **Significant improvement over existing methods**: The authors demonstrate that their personalized screening policies outperform existing clinical policies in terms of reducing the number of screenings while maintaining the same delay in detection of disease.
3. **Personalization**: The methodology allows for personalization of screening policies based on individual medical history and external information.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Computational complexity**: The algorithm to compute the sets of alpha vectors is computationally intractable, and the authors propose an approximate algorithm to compute an approximately optimal policy.
2. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their methodology using breast cancer data and do not provide a comprehensive evaluation of their methodology across different diseases and populations.
3. **Assumptions**: The authors make several assumptions, such as the cost of delay being linear and the test outcome being accurate, which may not hold in all cases.

**Originality**

The paper proposes a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balances the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The methodology is original in that it incorporates the possibility of external information and allows for personalization of screening policies based on individual medical history and external information.

**Significance**

The paper has significant implications for public health policy and practice. The authors demonstrate that their personalized screening policies can reduce the number of screenings performed while maintaining the same delay in detection of disease, which can lead to cost savings and improved health outcomes.

**Recommendations for improvement**

1. **Evaluate the methodology across different diseases and populations**: The authors should evaluate their methodology across different diseases and populations to demonstrate its generalizability.
2. **Relax assumptions**: The authors should relax their assumptions, such as the cost of delay being linear and the test outcome being accurate, to make their methodology more robust.
3. **Provide a comprehensive evaluation of the computational complexity**: The authors should provide a comprehensive evaluation of the computational complexity of their algorithm and propose methods to reduce it.

**Conclusion**

The paper proposes a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balances the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. While the methodology has significant implications for public health policy and practice, it requires further evaluation and refinement to demonstrate its generalizability and robustness.
