- Introduces a novel, tractable framework for personalized screening policies balancing screening cost and detection delay, validated on breast cancer (28–68% fewer screenings with similar delays).  
- Formulates the problem as a POMDP with rigorous Bellman equation derivation and theoretical guarantees on approximation error and robustness.  
- Generalizes prior models by handling non-Markovian disease progression, external information processes, and dynamic patient histories.  
- Demonstrates high practical significance by reducing screening frequency while maintaining detection timeliness, with applicability to other diseases.  
- Paper is well-written, organized, and uses real-world breast cancer data to strengthen practical relevance.  
- Scalability concerns: PBVI-based approximation may struggle with high-dimensional state spaces or long horizons; more discussion on computational efficiency is needed.  
- Evaluation is limited to breast cancer; additional experiments on diseases with different dynamics and cost structures would better support generalizability claims.  
- External information modeling is specific (e.g., lump size); clearer guidance is needed on adapting the framework to other signal types or less-defined external data.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a critical problem with a novel, technically robust solution and shows substantial empirical improvements.  
- Arguments against acceptance: computational feasibility for complex diseases/datasets is uncertain and empirical evaluation is narrow.  
- Overall recommendation: accept, with a suggestion to expand discussion on generalizability and computational considerations in the final version.
