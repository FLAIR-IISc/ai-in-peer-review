

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing dynamic personalized screening policies that balance screening frequency against detection delay across multiple disease classes. The authors model disease progression as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating both patient features and dynamic screening history, while accounting for external information (e.g., self-examinations) that may trigger early patient visits. This work significantly extends prior screening approaches (e.g., Ayer et al., 2014; Rizopoulos et al., 2013), which have been restricted to specific disease models with restrictive assumptions about disease progression and test outcomes.

The paper makes a strong technical contribution with several notable strengths. It provides a rigorous mathematical framework with thorough complexity analysis and a well-motivated approximation method using point-based value iteration, with provable error bounds (Proposition 1). The validation on a large real-world dataset (45,000 patients from Athena Health) demonstrates substantial efficiency gains (28-68% fewer screenings) while maintaining comparable disease detection delays. The paper effectively shows how personalization improves efficiency through clear comparisons between high-risk and low-risk patients, and demonstrates applicability across multiple disease model types through synthetic experiments.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The approximation error analysis and robustness analysis (Theorem 1) provide strong theoretical grounding, and the experimental results are thorough and convincing.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear organization and concise mathematical exposition. The technical content is accessible to readers familiar with POMDPs and decision theory, with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Originality: This represents a significant advance over prior work by developing a general framework applicable to diverse disease models, rather than being limited to specific diseases as previous methods.

Significance: The results demonstrate clear practical impact, with the potential to substantially reduce healthcare costs and patient burden through optimized screening policies. The framework's flexibility across disease types and validation on real-world data enhance its significance for healthcare decision-making.

The paper's main limitation is its primary validation on breast cancer data, though it does demonstrate applicability to other disease types through synthetic experiments. This does not detract from the paper's strong contribution.

Overall, DPSCREEN makes a compelling technical contribution with clear potential for practical impact in healthcare decision-making. It meets all criteria for a high-quality NIPS paper and represents a significant advance in personalized screening policy optimization.
