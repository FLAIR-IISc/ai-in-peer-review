The paper "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening" proposes a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The disease is modeled as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating the possibility of external information, such as self-examination or discovery of co-morbidities, that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment.

The authors provide a clear and concise summary of the main ideas in the paper, relating them to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and informative, making it easy for the reader to understand the proposed approach.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The proposed methodology is novel and addresses an important research area in personalized screening.
* The authors provide a computationally efficient approximate solution to the problem, which is a significant contribution.
* The experiments on simulated breast cancer data show promising results, with reduced delays at similar cost compared to an annual-screening baseline.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses:

* The method relies on the unrealistic assumption that each patient's disease model is known, whereas in practice, model inference is the main challenge.
* The methodological novelty is limited, as the authors build upon existing work in POMDPs and controlled sensing.
* The experiments only compare to a trivial annual baseline and do not assess robustness to model misspecification.
* There is a need to evaluate sensitivity to uncertainties in the disease model to establish practical relevance.

In terms of originality, the paper is a reasonable and well-developed contribution to the field of personalized screening. However, the methodological novelty is limited, and the paper does not fully convince the reader of its practical relevance.

The significance of the paper is high, as personalized screening is an important research area with significant potential impact on public health. The results are promising, and the proposed methodology has the potential to be applied to a wide range of diseases.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise summary of the main ideas. The proposed methodology is novel and addresses an important research area, but the methodological novelty is limited, and the paper does not fully convince the reader of its practical relevance.
