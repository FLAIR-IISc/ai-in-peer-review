- Summary: DPSCREEN computes personalized screening schedules minimizing weighted testing cost and delay using a finite-state disease model with endogenous decision epochs and trajectory-dependent delay; derives Bellman equations, shows piecewise-linear convexity, develops a PBVI-style approximation with a sampling-based bound, and demonstrates reduced screenings vs annual screening on breast cancer data.

- Strength: Timely and clinically relevant topic addressing personalized cost–delay trade-offs.

- Strength: Clear formulation highlighting departures from standard POMDPs (trajectory costs, endogenous arrivals).

- Strength: Practical algorithmic design (trajectory/belief sampling + PBVI), convexity result, and an approximation-error statement.

- Strength: Empirical illustration shows substantial screening reductions at similar expected delay.

- Strength: Well written, organized, and explicit about methodology and assumptions.

- Weakness: Assumes known patient-level disease/test model; experiments do not test robustness to model misspecification.

- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty—PBVI-style algorithm and theoretical results largely mirror known POMDP properties; bound depends on an uncalibrated density constant.

- Weakness: Narrow empirical evaluation with comparisons mainly to annual (and biennial) baselines and lacking stronger personalized baselines or simple learned heuristics.

- Weakness: Some modeling assumptions are unrealistic or under-justified (e.g., no false positives, simplified two-state model, biopsy cost equal to mammography).

- Weakness: No sensitivity analysis to core uncertainties (transition hazards, test accuracies, self-exam properties) despite claims about model estimation.

- Quality (technical): Formulation and PBVI adaptation are sound; convexity result is standard; approximation bound is qualitative and constants are not operationalized.

- Quality (empirical): Results are internally consistent but hinge on model correctness and lack confidence intervals, subgroup analyses, robustness checks, or suboptimality gap assessment.

- Completeness: Presents a full pipeline but practical validation and real-world readiness are limited.

- Clarity: Very clear writing and motivation; notation is heavy—recommend summarizing algorithms in main text and reporting T, K, |B̄|, and runtimes.

- Originality: Nice conceptual framing (endogenous arrivals, trajectory delay), but core algorithmic elements are incremental relative to prior POMDP screening work.

- Significance: Important problem with potential impact, but practical significance is uncertain without robustness and stronger comparative baselines.

- Suggestions: Conduct robustness/sensitivity analyses, consider Bayesian or distributionally robust variants, compare to stronger baselines (POMDPs, risk-stratified schedules, learned heuristics), run ablations on external info, refine cost models, report CIs and subgroup analyses, and clarify computational footprint/release code.

- Questions for authors: How sensitive are policies to misspecified ptr/pin and test accuracies? Can the framework support online learning/Bayes-adaptive POMDPs? How does it scale to richer state spaces and multiple tests?

- Pros: Clear motivation, scalable method, promising screening reductions, solid exposition.

- Cons: Limited novelty, unrealistic known-model assumption, weak baselines, no robustness analysis, simplified cost/diagnostic model.

- Overall evaluation: Interesting and reasonable work with a well-executed approximation but limited novelty and insufficient practical validation; reviewer leans toward weak reject pending expanded empirical validation and robustness analysis.
