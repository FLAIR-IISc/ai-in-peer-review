This paper presents a framework for constructing personalized screening policies, modeled as a finite-state stochastic process, aiming to minimize screening costs and delays. The core idea – adapting screening frequency based on individual patient features and screening history – is a valuable contribution to the field. The authors propose a computationally tractable approximate solution, demonstrating its efficacy on simulated breast cancer data by achieving reduced delays with comparable costs to annual screening.

The work builds upon existing research in screening optimization (e.g., [7, 9, 10]) by significantly broadening the applicability of these frameworks. Prior work often restricts itself to Markovian disease processes and stationary test outcome models. This paper’s strength lies in its ability to accommodate more general disease dynamics and the influence of external information (patient self-exams, comorbidities), a more realistic representation of clinical scenarios. The connection to controlled sensing [21, 34, 38] is also noted, highlighting the common thread of optimal information gathering.

**Strengths:**

* **Sound Methodology:** The modeling approach, framing the problem as a stochastic process and utilizing Bellman equations, is theoretically well-founded. The approximation algorithm (PBVI) addresses the computational intractability of the exact solution.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning. The included graphical model (mentioned in the text) is helpful for understanding the system’s components.
* **Promising Results:** The experiments on breast cancer data demonstrate a clear benefit – significant reductions in screening frequency without compromising delay – compared to a standard annual screening baseline. The personalization aspect, tailoring screening based on risk and history, is convincingly illustrated.
* **Robustness Discussion:** The inclusion of a discussion on robustness to estimation error and the proposition regarding the approximation error bound adds credibility to the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of existing techniques is well-executed, the methodological novelty is somewhat limited. The core ideas – dynamic programming and finite state modeling – are established within the POMDP and decision theory literature.
* **Baseline Comparison:** The primary comparison is to a trivial annual screening baseline. A more rigorous evaluation would involve comparing the proposed policy against more sophisticated, clinically-relevant screening strategies, or existing risk-based guidelines.
* **Model Dependence & Misspecification:** The method’s reliance on knowing each patient’s disease model is a significant limitation. The authors acknowledge this but the sensitivity analysis to model misspecification is not sufficiently explored. The effect of inaccuracies in estimating the disease progression parameters (pin(x), ptr(x)) could substantially impact performance.
* **Data Specificity:** The experiments are based on a single dataset (breast cancer). While a good illustrative example, generalizing the findings to other diseases requires further investigation. The applicability to diseases with significantly different characteristics and cost structures remains unclear.
* **External Information Simplification:** The modeling of external information (lump size, self-examination) appears somewhat simplified.  The assumption of independent observations conditional on state trajectory and features might not fully capture the complexities of patient behavior and self-perception.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The work addresses an important problem – personalized screening – with a well-defined mathematical framework.
* The results are promising and demonstrate a potential for improving screening efficiency.
* The paper is clearly written and contributes to the understanding of personalized healthcare optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The limited novelty and reliance on strong assumptions raise concerns about the practical applicability of the proposed method.
* The lack of comparison to more realistic baselines and thorough robustness analysis weakens the claims of superiority.
* The method’s heavy dependence on accurate disease model knowledge presents a significant barrier to implementation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an interesting and reasonable piece of work. The authors present a technically sound approach to a challenging problem. However, the lack of substantial novelty and practical validation, particularly concerning model misspecification, prevents it from being a definitively strong contribution. While the paper demonstrates potential, further work is needed to address the limitations and establish its relevance in real-world clinical settings.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more thorough discussion of model robustness, comparison to more relevant baselines, and exploration of the method’s applicability beyond breast cancer screening.
