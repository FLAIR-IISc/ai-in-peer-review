- Proposes DPSCREEN: dynamic, patient-specific screening policies minimizing weighted screening burden and detection delay.
- Models disease as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and an external information process that can trigger visits.
- Proves the value function is piecewise-linear and convex in belief space.
- Develops an efficient approximate policy using point-based value iteration (PBVI) that samples likely state trajectories and reachable beliefs.
- Provides an approximation bound tied to belief sampling density and a stability result for small model perturbations.
- Empirical evaluation on simulated breast-cancer data shows substantial reductions in expected screening counts at comparable delays to annual screening.
- Situates work relative to POMDP-based screening, joint modeling/survival, and controlled sensing; connects to recent NeurIPS disease-trajectory and endogenous-observation work.
- Notes PBVI and piecewise-linear/convex value properties are standard POMDP results; main novelty is modeling external information and trajectory-dependent costs.
- Quality: careful formulation and reasonable algorithmic development.
- Key theoretical limitations: assumes known per-patient disease models and uses strong conditions (e.g., perfect tests in parts of theory).
- Approximation bound issues: hides important constants and requires convex delay costs and bounded inter-screen intervals.
- Experimental limitations: only compared to an annual-screening baseline and lacks robustness/sensitivity analyses to model misspecification or parameter uncertainty.
- Clarity: paper is well written and organized.
- Originality: methodological novelty is incremental; handling of external information is useful but modest.
- Significance: personalized screening is important and results are promising, but practical impact is uncertain without stronger baselines and robustness checks.
- Pros: timely problem, clear formulation, efficient solver, promising simulated results, readable presentation.
- Cons: requires known patient models, limited novelty, limited evaluation and no sensitivity/robustness testing, theoretical guarantees rely on restrictive assumptions.
- Suggestions: compare to risk-based intervals, ConfidentCare, and information-gain methods; stress-test under perturbed hazards, test characteristics, and external-info models.
- Additional suggestions: quantify uncertainty via distributional robustness or Bayesian averaging, report scalability/run-time and ablations on belief sampling, and consider off-policy evaluation with uncertainty bounds.
