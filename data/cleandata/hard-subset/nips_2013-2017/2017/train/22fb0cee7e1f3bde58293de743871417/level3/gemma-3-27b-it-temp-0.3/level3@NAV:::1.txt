## Review of DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies across a wide range of diseases. The core contribution lies in its ability to dynamically adjust screening schedules based on both patient features and screening history, moving beyond static, one-size-fits-all approaches. The authors model disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and address the computational intractability of finding optimal policies with a tractable approximation algorithm. This work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and builds upon existing literature in decision theory, personalized medicine, and screening optimization. 

The paper effectively situates itself within the existing body of work, acknowledging limitations of previous approaches that rely on Markovian assumptions or focus on specific diseases. It distinguishes itself by offering a more general framework applicable to diverse disease models and incorporating external information (like patient self-exams). The mathematical exposition is concise and thorough, including a complexity analysis, which is crucial for understanding the scalability of the proposed method. The validation on a large, real-world dataset (Athena Health, 45k patients) is a significant strength, demonstrating the practical relevance of DPSCREEN. 

The results are compelling, showing substantial reductions in the number of screenings (28-68%) without compromising disease detection delay. The validation across multiple disease model types further strengthens the robustness of the approach. The personalization aspect is clearly demonstrated, with higher-risk patients and those with concerning test results receiving more frequent screenings. The discussion of robustness to model estimation error and the impact of different cost functions (linear vs. quadratic) adds valuable nuance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Introduces a general framework for personalized screening policies.
*   **Technical Soundness:** Rigorous mathematical formulation and complexity analysis.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Validated on a large real-world dataset with demonstrable improvements.
*   **Clarity:** Well-written and organized, making the complex concepts accessible.
*   **Significance:** Potential to improve screening efficiency and reduce healthcare costs.

**Weaknesses:**

*   While the computational complexity analysis is provided, a more detailed discussion of the practical limitations for very large datasets or complex disease models would be beneficial.
*   The assumption of a finite state stochastic process for disease progression, while general, might not be suitable for all diseases. Exploring the impact of this assumption would be valuable.
*   The paper focuses primarily on breast cancer. While the framework is presented as general, further examples with other diseases would strengthen the claims of broad applicability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant technical contribution with clear potential for practical impact.
*   The methodology is well-motivated, rigorously developed, and thoroughly validated.
*   The results demonstrate substantial improvements over existing screening policies.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   None that outweigh the strengths. While some limitations exist, they do not fundamentally undermine the value of the work.

**Overall:**

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a valuable contribution to the field of personalized medicine and offers a promising approach to optimizing screening strategies for a wide range of diseases. The clarity, rigor, and practical relevance of the work make it a strong fit for NIPS.
