This paper presents a novel and valuable approach to personalized breast cancer screening, addressing a critical gap in existing literature that often relies on population-level policies. The authors propose a dynamic programming framework (DPSCREEN) to optimize screening schedules by balancing the costs of screening and delayed detection, incorporating individual patient history, features, and external information (like self-exams). The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically demonstrated to significantly improve upon current clinical practices.

The work builds upon a rich history of research in screening optimization, particularly within operations research and medical decision-making. Prior work, such as Ayer et al. (2013) and Maillart et al. (2014), has explored POMDP-based approaches to mammography screening, but often with restrictive assumptions like Markovian disease progression and stationary test outcome models.  This paper distinctly addresses these limitations by allowing for more general disease models and incorporating dynamic external information. It also moves beyond simple comparisons to fixed policies (common in simulation-based approaches like those from the Cancer Intervention and Surveillance Modelling Network – see Alagoz et al., 2017) by formulating an optimization problem and providing a tractable, albeit approximate, solution. Related work in controlled sensing (Krishnamurthy, 2017) shares similarities in the sequential decision-making framework, but often focuses on scenarios with different informational constraints. The authors correctly position their work as extending beyond these prior efforts with its focus on personalized, dynamic policies within a complex medical context.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is rigorous, and the approximation method (point-based value iteration) is well-justified and provides a bound on the approximation error (Proposition 1). The authors acknowledge and address the computational intractability of finding the truly optimal policy, offering a practical alternative.
* **Novelty & Originality:** The combination of a general stochastic disease model, incorporation of external information, and dynamic policy optimization is novel. The authors effectively address limitations of prior approaches by relaxing Markovian assumptions and accounting for patient-driven arrivals.
* **Empirical Validation:** The application to a large breast cancer dataset demonstrates significant improvements over existing clinical policies (28-68% reduction in screenings with comparable delay). The sensitivity analysis regarding linear vs. quadratic delay costs (Figure 2) convincingly illustrates the importance of cost function selection.
* **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The notation is clear, and the key concepts are explained intuitively. The inclusion of a graphical representation of personalization (Figure 1) is particularly effective. The extensive supplementary material further strengthens the paper.
* **Robustness Analysis:** The discussion of robustness to estimation error (Theorem 1) is crucial for practical applicability, demonstrating that small errors in model estimation do not necessarily negate the benefits of the proposed policy.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Model Simplifications:** While the model is more general than many prior works, certain simplifications are made (e.g., single absorbing disease state, independence assumptions). These are acknowledged, but their potential impact on real-world performance could be further discussed.
* **Data Limitations:** The reliance on retrospective data introduces potential biases (e.g., selection bias in screening participation). While the authors address this through model learning, it’s important to acknowledge the inherent limitations of observational data.
* **Computational Cost:** Despite the approximation method, the computational complexity remains significant, potentially limiting scalability to very large patient populations or more complex disease models.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a clinically important problem with a technically innovative solution.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate substantial improvements over current practice.
* The work is well-written, clearly presented, and rigorously analyzed.
* The authors acknowledge limitations and discuss potential future directions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The model simplifications could limit the generalizability of the findings.
* The computational complexity might pose challenges for real-world implementation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of personalized medicine and screening optimization. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses, and the work has the potential to impact clinical practice. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Rating:** Strong Accept.
