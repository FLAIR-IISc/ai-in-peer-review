Proposes DPSCREEN: constructs personalized screening policies using a finite-state stochastic disease model and a computationally efficient approximate solver.  
Well-written paper with clear problem formulation, method description, and experimental setup.  
Demonstrated on a large breast cancer dataset, showing fewer screenings while maintaining detection delay versus current clinical policies.  
Strength: accounts for individual patient features and dynamic history in screening decisions.  
Strength: finite-state stochastic modeling and efficient approximation are reasonable and significant contributions.  
Main limitation: assumes the disease model is known, which may be unrealistic in practice.  
Paper lacks a thorough evaluation of robustness to disease-model misspecification or estimation error.  
Experiments only compare to a trivial annual screening baseline rather than existing personalized-screening methods.  
Novelty is incremental—builds on existing personalized screening and dynamic programming literature but applies these techniques fruitfully to screening.  
Quality: technically sound but weakened by the strong model-knowledge assumption and limited robustness analysis.  
Clarity: clear and concise writing, easy to follow.  
Significance: addresses an important problem with a promising approach, but practical relevance needs further validation.  
Suggested improvements: evaluate sensitivity to model uncertainty and compare to stronger baselines from the literature.
