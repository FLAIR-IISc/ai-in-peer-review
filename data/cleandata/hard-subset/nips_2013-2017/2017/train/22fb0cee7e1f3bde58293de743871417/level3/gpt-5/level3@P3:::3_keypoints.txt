- Proposes DPSCREEN, a dynamic personalized screening framework that schedules future screens to minimize weighted screening cost and delay-to-diagnosis using a finite-state disease trajectory model with external information-triggered arrivals.  
- Formulates a belief-state dynamic program over distributions on state trajectories and derives Bellman equations for trajectory-dependent costs and endogenous decision epochs.  
- Shows the value function is convex and piecewise-linear in beliefs (extending POMDP results) but exact computation is intractable.  
- Develops a PBVI-style approximation that samples likely physiological trajectories and constructs reachable beliefs under randomized/clinical policies.  
- Provides computational complexity characterization, an approximation error bound based on belief-space sampling density, and a robustness result to small model-estimation errors.  
- Empirically evaluates on a large de-identified breast cancer cohort (ages 60–65) using Gail risk, literature test characteristics, and a tumor-growth/self-exam model as external info.  
- Reports personalized intervals that reduce expected screens by 28–68% versus annual screening while matching expected detection delay.  
- Positions work relative to POMDP-based screening, Bayesian joint modeling, CISNET simulation, and controlled sensing, claiming broader modeling flexibility.  
- Strength: clear problem formulation capturing individual features, dynamic test history, and exogenous early-visit information.  
- Strength: methodologically novel extension of POMDP machinery to trajectory-dependent costs and endogenous decision epochs.  
- Strength: solid theoretical support (piecewise-linearity, approximation bound, robustness).  
- Strength: strong empirical gains and ablations demonstrating impact of self-exams and risk strata.  
- Strength: well written, well organized, and comprehensive related work.  
- Weakness: evaluation relies on a learned generative simulator — performance may be optimistic under model misspecification; limited sensitivity analyses in main text.  
- Weakness: approximation bound assumes perfect tests and bounded intervals, which real-world tests violate, limiting applicability of guarantees.  
- Weakness: external information model is stylized (monthly self-exam, simple threshold, parametric growth) with limited robustness exploration.  
- Weakness: outcome metrics focus on expected delay and screen counts, omitting stage at detection, interval cancers, false positives, overdiagnosis, and downstream costs.  
- Weakness: personalization in experiments is coarse (two Gail-risk groups) despite framework supporting richer features and multi-state disease.  
- Weakness: practical aspects (computational cost, scalability, code/data availability) are not fully detailed.  
- Clarity: generally well written; would benefit from core pseudo-code and clarification where “no false positives” applies.  
- Originality: high — novel integration of trajectory-dependent delay costs, external-info-driven arrivals, and belief-space DP with PBVI approximation.  
- Significance: high potential impact for personalized screening and sequential healthcare decision-making.  
- Author requests: clarify tuning of the weight w and provide Pareto curves with CIs for screens vs. delay.  
- Author requests: provide sensitivity analyses for alternative external information models and test sensitivity/specificity.  
- Author requests: extend theory to imperfect tests or empirically assess sensitivity to test errors.  
- Author requests: report computational runtimes, chosen B and K values, and scaling behavior for larger/more complex state spaces.  
- Author requests: report additional clinical metrics (stage proxy, interval cancer probability, false positive/biopsy rates) and subgroup analyses for fairness/safety.  
- Author requests: release code or pseudo-code and clarify whether policy class enforces maximum interval constraints.  
- Reasons to accept: novel, well-motivated formulation; solid theory; implementable algorithm; substantial empirical improvements; clear writing.  
- Reasons to reject: reliance on learned simulator and strong theoretical assumptions; narrow experimental scope and limited clinical outcome reporting.  
- Overall: strong, timely contribution; recommendation leans toward acceptance conditional on clarifying assumptions and adding robustness analyses.
