This paper introduces a novel methodology, DPSCREEN, for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The disease is modeled as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, allowing for a high degree of flexibility and generality. The authors incorporate the possibility of external information, such as self-examination or discovery of co-morbidities, that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with a clear and concise mathematical exposition. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of their approach and demonstrate its validity on a large real-world dataset of 45,000 patients from Athena Health. The results show that personalization yields efficiency gains over existing screening policies without delaying disease detection.

The approach is well-motivated and situates itself with pertinent related work in the field. The authors provide a clear technical contribution with potential practical impact, demonstrating improvements attributable to personalization across multiple disease model types.

The strengths of the paper include its ability to handle a wide range of disease models, its incorporation of external information, and its personalization of screening policies based on patient features and screening history. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of their approach and demonstrate its validity on a large real-world dataset.

The weaknesses of the paper include the potential for the approach to be computationally intensive, particularly for large datasets or complex disease models. Additionally, the authors assume that the model parameters are known, which may not always be the case in practice. However, the authors do provide a discussion of the impact of estimation error on the exactly optimal policy and demonstrate that the approximately optimal policy converges to the exactly optimal policy as the approximation error goes to zero.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field by introducing a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies. The approach is distinct from existing frameworks in the field, which often rely on more restrictive models of disease progression and do not incorporate external information.

In terms of significance, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field of healthcare by improving the efficiency and effectiveness of screening policies. The authors demonstrate that personalization yields efficiency gains over existing screening policies without delaying disease detection, which could lead to improved health outcomes and reduced healthcare costs.

Overall, this paper is a strong contribution to the field and demonstrates a clear technical contribution with potential practical impact. The authors provide a thorough analysis of their approach and demonstrate its validity on a large real-world dataset.
