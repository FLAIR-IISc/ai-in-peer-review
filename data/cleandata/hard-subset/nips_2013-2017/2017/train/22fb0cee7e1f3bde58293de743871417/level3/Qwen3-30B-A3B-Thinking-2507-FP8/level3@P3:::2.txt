

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

This paper proposes a framework for constructing personalized screening policies by modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state. The authors develop a computationally efficient approximate solution (DPSCREEN) to the problem of optimizing screening policies that balance screening costs against delays in disease detection. The method incorporates external information processes (e.g., patient self-examinations) and updates patient risk assessments dynamically based on test results. The authors evaluate their approach using a large breast cancer dataset, showing significant reductions (28-68%) in screening frequency compared to annual screening policies while maintaining similar expected detection delays.

The paper builds on standard POMDP techniques (specifically Point-Based Value Iteration) but extends them to a more general disease modeling framework than previous work. Previous screening approaches like Ayer et al. (2015) and Maillart et al. (2013) restricted disease models to Markov processes and stationary test outcome models, while this paper accommodates more complex disease progression models. The authors position their work as addressing limitations in both operations research screening frameworks and medical literature approaches.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a thorough problem formulation and clear theoretical analysis. The proof that the value function is convex and piecewise linear (Lemma 1) and the approximation error bound (Proposition 1) provide solid theoretical grounding. The experimental evaluation on breast cancer data is well-executed, showing meaningful reductions in screening frequency. However, the paper's primary weakness is its unrealistic assumption that the disease model is known a priori, while in practice, model inference from observational data is the main challenge. The paper briefly addresses estimation error (Section 3.3) but fails to evaluate sensitivity to model misspecification, which is critical for practical implementation.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The graphical illustrations (e.g., Figure 1) effectively demonstrate the personalization mechanism. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the problem formulation is comprehensive. The supplementary materials are well-structured and provide additional technical details.

**Originality:** The main contribution is the application of POMDP techniques to personalized screening in a more general setting than previous work. However, the core algorithm (PBVI) is not novel, and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from related work. The related work section (Section 5) adequately covers previous screening frameworks but could better emphasize how this work differs from existing POMDP-based approaches. The methodological novelty is limited, as the paper primarily applies established techniques to a new application domain.

**Significance:** Personalized screening is an important problem with significant potential impact on healthcare resource allocation. The results (28-68% reduction in screenings) are promising for practitioners, and the framework's generality across disease models is a positive aspect. However, the paper's significance is limited by its lack of practical validation. The experiments only compare to an annual screening baseline, not to more sophisticated clinical policies or alternative personalized approaches. The failure to assess robustness to model misspecification undermines the claim of practical relevance.

**Overall Assessment:** This is an interesting and well-executed paper that addresses an important problem in healthcare. The technical approach is sound, the writing is excellent, and the results are promising. However, the lack of novelty in the core methodology and the unrealistic assumption about known disease models significantly limit the paper's contribution. The authors would strengthen their work substantially by addressing the main practical challenge of model inference and evaluating robustness to model misspecification.

**Recommendation:** Accept. While the paper has significant limitations in novelty and practical validation, it represents a reasonable and well-executed application of existing techniques to an important healthcare problem. The results are compelling, and the framework has potential for future development. The authors should address the limitations mentioned above in future work, but the current work is valuable as a first step toward personalized screening policies.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Strong technical foundation with sound theoretical analysis
- Well-executed experiments showing meaningful improvements over baseline
- Clear, well-written presentation that would be accessible to the NIPS audience
- Addresses an important problem with potential real-world impact

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited methodological novelty (core algorithm is standard POMDP approximation)
- Unrealistic assumption about known disease models (neglects the main practical challenge)
- Experiments only compare to trivial annual baseline without robustness analysis
- Fails to address sensitivity to uncertainties in disease model parameters

The paper makes a reasonable contribution to the field, but its practical relevance would be significantly enhanced by addressing the model inference challenge and conducting more comprehensive validation. As it stands, it's a solid but not groundbreaking work in the context of NIPS.
