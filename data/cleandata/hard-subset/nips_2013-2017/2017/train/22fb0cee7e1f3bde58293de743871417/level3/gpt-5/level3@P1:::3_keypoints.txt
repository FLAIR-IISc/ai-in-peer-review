- Proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies minimizing weighted screening costs and detection delays.  
- Models disease as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and allows tests at clinician visits plus external-information-triggered arrivals.  
- Decision problem departs from standard POMDPs because decision times are endogenous and delay cost depends on full trajectories.  
- Formulates Bellman equations over beliefs on trajectory-label pairs and proves the value function is convex and piecewise-linear.  
- Exact alpha-vector computation is intractable; proposes a PBVI-style approximation sampling reachable beliefs via likely trajectories and action exploration.  
- Provides an approximation error bound depending on belief sampling density and a robustness result showing local constancy of optimal actions outside a lower-dimensional set.  
- Theoretical assumptions include no false positives/negatives in parts, bounded max screening interval, and large T, which may limit realism of guarantees.  
- Instantiates DPSCREEN for breast cancer using ~45k women (~84k mammograms), modeling disease as two-state with feature-dependent probabilities.  
- External information modeled as monthly self-exams driven by a tumor growth model plus noise; test sensitivity/specificity taken from literature.  
- Tunes Lagrangian weight to match baseline expected delay and reports large screening reductions: 57–68% with self-exams and 28–45% without, across risk strata, at comparable delays.  
- Shows policy differences under linear vs quadratic delay costs and personalization to baseline risk and dynamic histories.  
- Positions work relative to POMDP screening, Bayesian design, and simulation-based guideline development, highlighting novelty in trajectory-dependent costs and endogenous arrivals.  
- Theoretical contribution extends piecewise-linear convexity and alpha-vector recursion to a nonstandard setting; robustness/approximation results are incremental but useful.  
- Empirical study is substantial and clinically intuitive, but relies on a learned simulator and weight tuning; uncertainty quantification and sensitivity analyses are limited.  
- Manuscript is well written; modeling and deviations from POMDPs are clearly explained, with pseudocode/supplementary details referenced.  
- Requests clarification on consistency of “no false positives” with use of sensitivity/specificity, tuning of w, and exact computational settings (K, B, W, runtimes).  
- Strengths: flexible clinical formulation, sound dynamic programming treatment, tractable approximation with error bound, large realistic empirical gains, and persuasive personalization.  
- Weaknesses: strong theoretical assumptions, limited uncertainty estimates (CIs) and sensitivity analyses, narrow baseline comparisons, and missing computational/runtime details.  
- Suggested additions: relax/quantify theoretical assumptions, provide CIs and ablations (e.g., no history personalization), expand baselines (risk-based, ConfidentCare, RL/POMDP), report computational footprint, and discuss duality/tuning of w and generalization beyond two-state models.  
- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, principled general framework, theoretical structure plus practical algorithm, strong empirical improvements, and good writing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unrealistic theoretical assumptions, simulation-based evaluation with limited uncertainty and baselines, and insufficient reproducibility/implementation details.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept (high reviewer confidence).
