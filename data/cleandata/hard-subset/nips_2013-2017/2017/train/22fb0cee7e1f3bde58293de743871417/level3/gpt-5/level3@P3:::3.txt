Paper summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a dynamic, personalized screening framework that schedules future screening times to minimize a weighted combination of screening cost and delay-to-diagnosis cost. The disease is modeled as a general finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; the decision-maker also observes an external information process (e.g., patient self-exams or comorbidities) that can trigger unscheduled visits. The key challenge is that costs depend on the entire state trajectory (delay depends on incidence and detection times), decisions endogenously determine the next decision epoch (via recommended follow-up time plus possible early arrival from external information), and the process need not be Markov/stationary in the conventional sense used in screening OR models.

The authors formulate a belief-state dynamic program over distributions on state trajectories with a diagnosis label and derive Bellman equations. They show that the value function is convex and piecewise linear in beliefs (extending classic POMDP results to this setting) but exact computation is intractable. They therefore develop a point-based value iteration (PBVI)-style approximation that (i) samples likely physiological trajectories to form a reduced belief space and (ii) constructs reachable beliefs under randomized and clinically motivated policies. They provide a computational complexity characterization and prove an approximation error bound in terms of belief-space sampling density, along with a robustness result showing local constancy of optimal actions with respect to small model-estimation errors.

For evaluation, they estimate a simple two-state breast cancer model on a large de-identified dataset of women aged 60–65, using Gail risk scores as features, literature-based sensitivity/specificity, and a tumor growth/self-exam model as external information. DPSCREEN personalizes intervals by baseline risk and by test history/external events, and empirically reduces the expected number of screens by 28–68% vs. annual screening while matching expected detection delay; similar comparisons to biennial screening are reported in the supplement. The paper situates itself relative to POMDP-based screening (Ayer; Maillart), Bayesian/joint modeling (Rizopoulos), clinical simulation (CISNET), and controlled sensing, arguing broader modeling flexibility (trajectory-dependent costs, external info, endogenous arrivals) than prior work.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation that captures key practical factors: individual features, dynamic test history, and exogenous information leading to early visits.
- Methodologically novel extension of POMDP machinery to trajectory-dependent costs and endogenous decision epochs, with a constructive PBVI variant tailored to very high-dimensional belief spaces.
- Theoretical support: piecewise-linear convexity, an approximation bound under explicit assumptions, and a robustness result to estimation error.
- Strong empirical demonstration on a large breast cancer cohort with personalized policies that substantially reduce screening volume at matched expected delay; ablations with/without self-exams and across risk strata.
- Well written and organized; related work is comprehensive and positioning is clear.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Evaluation depends on a learned generative model rather than counterfactuals; performance may be optimistic if the model mis-specifies disease progression, adherence, or test characteristics. Sensitivity analyses are limited in the main text.
- The approximation bound assumes perfect tests (no false positives/negatives) and bounded intervals; real-world mammography/biopsy errors violate this, so theoretical guarantees may not carry directly to practice.
- External information is stylized (monthly self-exam, simple threshold, parametric tumor growth); robustness to these modeling choices is not deeply explored.
- Outcome metrics emphasize expected delay (unconditional and conditional on disease) and number of screens; harms/benefits such as stage at detection, interval cancers, false positives, overdiagnosis, and downstream costs are not modeled.
- Personalization is coarse in experiments (two risk groups using Gail risk) despite a framework that could handle richer feature sets and multi-state disease.
- Practical aspects—computational cost, scalability to richer state spaces, and code/data availability—are not fully detailed in the main paper.

Evaluation against review criteria:
- Quality: Technically sound formulation and careful derivations; the PBVI-style algorithm and error bound are appropriate given intractability. Empirical setup is reasonable but limited by reliance on a learned simulator and simplifying assumptions about testing and external information.
- Clarity: Generally well written and well structured. It would help to include core pseudo-code in the main text and to clarify where “no false positives” applies (final diagnosis vs. interim tests).
- Originality: High. The integration of trajectory-dependent delay costs, external information-driven endogenous arrivals, and belief-space DP with PBVI-style approximation is a novel combination beyond prior screening POMDPs and clinical simulation.
- Significance: High potential for impact in personalized screening and broader sequential decision-making in healthcare. The empirical gains are substantial; the framework is general enough to extend to other diseases and cost structures.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Please clarify the mapping from the weighted objective to the constrained formulation: how is w tuned and how sensitive are results to w? Can you provide Pareto curves (screens vs. delay) with CIs?
- How robust are the results to alternative external information models (e.g., different self-exam frequencies/thresholds, noise levels, or no self-exams)? Include sensitivity analyses.
- The approximation bound assumes perfect tests; can you extend the theory to imperfect but calibrated tests? Empirically, how do sensitivity/specificity variations affect policy performance?
- Provide computational runtimes, B and K values, and scaling behavior for larger/continuous state spaces or multi-state disease.
- Consider reporting more clinically relevant metrics (e.g., stage at detection proxy, interval cancer probability, false positive/biopsy rates) and subgroup analyses across race/ethnicity and comorbidities to assess fairness and safety.
- If possible, release code or pseudo-code in the main paper and clarify whether the policy class enforces maximum interval constraints in experiments.

Reasons to accept:
- Novel and well-motivated formulation addressing important limitations of prior screening models.
- Solid theoretical contributions and an implementable algorithm with clear personalization.
- Empirical improvements that are large and practically meaningful.
- Clear, careful writing and comprehensive related work.

Reasons to reject:
- Reliance on a learned simulator and strong assumptions (perfect diagnosis for bounds, stylized external info) limit real-world validity of claims.
- Experimental scope is narrow (single age band, coarse risk groups) with limited clinical outcome metrics reported.

Overall: A strong and timely contribution; I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying assumptions and adding robustness analyses.
