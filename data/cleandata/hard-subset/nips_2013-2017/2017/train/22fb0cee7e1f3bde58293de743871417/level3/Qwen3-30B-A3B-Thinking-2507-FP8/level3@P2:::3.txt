

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against delay costs in disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating external information processes (e.g., self-examinations, discovery of comorbidities) that can trigger early patient visits. The key theoretical contribution is a computationally tractable method to construct approximately optimal screening policies, with provable error bounds for the approximation. The authors demonstrate their approach on a large breast cancer dataset, showing significant reductions (28-68%) in screening frequency while maintaining equivalent expected detection delays compared to current clinical policies.

The paper relates well to previous work in several ways. It builds upon POMDP-based screening frameworks [7, 9] but extends them significantly by relaxing restrictive assumptions about disease processes (moving beyond Markovian assumptions) and incorporating external information processes. The authors correctly identify that existing works [7, 9, 10] assume stationary Markov test outcome models, which are not suitable for many diseases. They also distinguish their approach from controlled sensing literature [21, 34, 38] by noting that those works rely on structural assumptions (like stationarity) that are less applicable to personalized screening.

**Strengths:**
*Quality:* The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical contributions. Lemma 1 establishes that the value function is convex and piecewise linear, enabling the use of alpha vectors for policy construction. Proposition 1 provides a concrete bound on the approximation error, which is crucial for justifying the practicality of the approach. The robustness analysis (Theorem 1) demonstrates that estimation errors in model parameters have minimal impact on the resulting policy, which is an important practical consideration.

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The model formulation is clear and well-motivated, with careful definition of all components. Figure 1 provides an excellent graphical representation of how the policy personalizes to patients' features and history. The supplementary materials are comprehensive, with detailed algorithms and proofs.

*Originality:* The framework is genuinely novel in its generality. While previous work [7, 9] used POMDPs for screening, they were restricted to Markov disease processes. DPSCREEN's ability to handle arbitrary finite state stochastic processes (including dynamic survival models, first hitting time models, etc.) represents a significant advance. The incorporation of external information processes as an integral part of the decision-making framework is particularly innovative.

*Significance:* The empirical results are compelling. The 28-68% reduction in screenings while maintaining equivalent detection delays represents a substantial practical improvement over current clinical policies. The paper also demonstrates how the same framework can be applied across different diseases with different cost structures (linear vs. quadratic delay costs), highlighting the framework's versatility.

**Weaknesses:**
*Quality:* While the theoretical contribution is strong, the evaluation is limited to breast cancer. It would be valuable to see how the framework performs on other diseases with different progression patterns to better demonstrate its general applicability.

*Clarity:* The connection to NIPS-specific contributions could be strengthened. While the paper uses POMDP techniques, it doesn't clearly articulate how it advances the state of the art in sequential decision making beyond the medical application.

*Significance:* The clinical implementation challenges (e.g., how to integrate this framework into existing healthcare systems, patient compliance with personalized schedules) are not thoroughly discussed, which limits the immediate practical impact assessment.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel framework that generalizes previous screening approaches
- Strong theoretical foundation with provable error bounds
- Significant empirical improvements over current clinical policies (28-68% reduction in screenings)
- Well-written with clear organization and excellent supplementary materials
- Addresses a meaningful real-world problem with potential to reduce healthcare costs

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited to breast cancer application (though the framework is general)
- Doesn't sufficiently connect to NIPS-specific contributions in sequential decision making
- Implementation challenges in real-world clinical settings aren't adequately addressed

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to both the medical decision-making literature and the broader field of sequential decision making under uncertainty. The framework is theoretically sound, well-executed, and demonstrates substantial practical benefits. While the evaluation is limited to breast cancer, the framework's generality suggests it could be applied to many other screening scenarios. The paper meets all the quality, clarity, originality, and significance criteria for NIPS, and the empirical results are compelling.

I recommend acceptance for NIPS 2017.
