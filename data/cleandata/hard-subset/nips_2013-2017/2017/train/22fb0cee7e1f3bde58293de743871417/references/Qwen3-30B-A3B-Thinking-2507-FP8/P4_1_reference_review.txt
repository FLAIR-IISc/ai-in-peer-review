

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for developing personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against delay costs in disease detection. The authors formulate the problem as a general partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) that accommodates non-Markovian disease progression models, external information processes (e.g., patient self-examinations), and dynamic history. Unlike previous works that restrict to Markov disease processes [7,9] or stationary test outcome models, the proposed DPSCREEN framework handles more general disease dynamics. The authors develop a computationally feasible approximation method based on point-based value iteration with theoretical error bounds, and demonstrate significant improvements in breast cancer screening (28-68% reduction in screenings while maintaining detection delays) compared to current clinical policies.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides a rigorous mathematical formulation of the problem, with clear definitions of disease models, external information, and cost functions. The theoretical analysis is strong, including proofs of the value function's convexity and piecewise linearity, and a concrete error bound (Proposition 1) for the approximate policy. The proposed algorithm (DPSCREEN) is well-motivated and theoretically justified.

2. **Novelty**: The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by developing a framework that handles non-Markovian disease processes and incorporates external information processes (like patient self-examinations) that trigger early screenings. This is a clear advancement over previous POMDP-based screening approaches [7,9,10] that make restrictive assumptions about disease dynamics and test outcomes.

3. **Significance**: The experimental results demonstrate substantial practical benefits in a real healthcare setting. The 28-68% reduction in screenings while maintaining detection delays has meaningful implications for healthcare resource allocation and patient burden. The demonstration of disease-specific policy differences based on delay cost functions (linear vs. quadratic) further highlights the framework's flexibility.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical formulations are presented systematically, with appropriate explanations of the components. The experimental results are clearly presented with supporting tables and figures.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Comparison with Recent Methods**: While the paper cites relevant prior work, it doesn't thoroughly compare with the most recent methods in personalized screening. For instance, it doesn't address methods like "Disease Trajectory Maps" [Schulam et al., NIPS 2016] or "ConfidentCare" [Alaa et al., IEEE Transactions on Multimedia 2017] that also deal with personalized screening.

2. **Vague Error Bound**: Proposition 1 provides an error bound in terms of Ω(B̄), but doesn't provide practical guidance on how to choose the sampling density B̄ to achieve a specific error tolerance. The bound is theoretically sound but lacks concrete implementation details.

3. **Insufficient Dataset Details**: The paper mentions "a large breast cancer dataset" but doesn't provide critical details about the dataset size, characteristics, or how it was split for training and testing. This limits reproducibility.

4. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The experimental section doesn't include ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of different components of the proposed method (e.g., the impact of including external information versus not including it).

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing a more general framework for personalized screening that handles non-Markovian disease processes
- Strong theoretical foundation with clear error bounds
- Demonstrates substantial practical benefits in a real-world healthcare application
- Well-written and clearly organized
- Contributes to the growing literature on personalized healthcare decisions at NIPS

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- Limited comparison with the most recent methods in the literature
- Error bound lacks practical implementation guidance
- Insufficient dataset details for reproducibility
- Missing ablation studies to isolate the contribution of different components

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to personalized screening that advances the state of the art beyond previous POMDP-based frameworks. The method is well-motivated by real-world healthcare challenges, and the experimental results demonstrate significant practical benefits. While the paper has some weaknesses in comparison with the most recent methods and lacks some implementation details, these do not undermine the core contribution. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of healthcare decision making, and the proposed framework is general enough to be applicable to many different disease contexts.

The paper meets all four criteria for quality: it is technically sound with strong theoretical foundations, clearly written, novel in addressing a significant gap in the literature, and significant in its potential impact on healthcare resource allocation. The contribution is substantial and represents a clear advancement over previous work in this area.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS.
