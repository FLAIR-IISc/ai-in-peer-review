This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for personalized screening policies, addressing a critical need in disease diagnosis and treatment. The core idea is to develop policies that dynamically adjust screening frequency based on disease type, patient features, and screening history, moving beyond traditional, static approaches. The authors tackle significant challenges including non-stationary disease processes, unknown delay costs, external information impacting patient arrival, and computational intractability. They propose a computationally effective procedure with approximation error bounds, and demonstrate its efficacy using a breast cancer dataset and a synthetic exercise across different cost functions.

The paper builds upon existing work in screening (e.g., [7, 9]) by relaxing key assumptions such as Markovian disease processes and stationary test outcome models. It also connects to literature on POMDPs [18, 20] and controlled sensing [38], acknowledging limitations of existing methods when applied to this specific problem. The approach also considers and addresses the practical challenge of model estimation error, demonstrating robustness.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The development of personalized screening policies adaptable to both patient features and dynamic disease progression is a significant contribution. The framework’s generality—accommodating various disease models (survival, first hitting time) and external information—is a key strength.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed approach appears theoretically well-founded, with approximation error bounds (Proposition 1) and a robustness analysis regarding model estimation error (Theorem 1). The use of point-based value iteration (PBVI) and the developed algorithm for belief construction seem reasonable.
* **Practical Relevance:** The illustrative experiments with the breast cancer dataset demonstrate substantial reductions in screening frequency (28-68%) while maintaining comparable delay times, highlighting the potential clinical impact. The sensitivity analysis showcasing the influence of cost functions adds further practical insight.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The model formulation is detailed, though dense. The authors attempt to explain complex concepts clearly, and the use of a graphical model in the appendix is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors acknowledge computational challenges, the stated complexity of O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|) remains high. The parallelization potential is mentioned but not quantified. A more thorough discussion of scalability limitations would be beneficial.
* **Model Assumptions:** The model relies on several assumptions (e.g., independence of test results given state and features) that may not hold in real-world scenarios. The sensitivity of the policy to violations of these assumptions is not fully explored.
* **Dataset Details:** The description of the breast cancer dataset is brief. More detail about the data sources, potential biases, and the specific features used in the model would enhance reproducibility and evaluation.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The choice of parameters (e.g., ι = 0.9 for tumor growth, σ = 0.43 for self-examination variance) seems somewhat arbitrary. A justification for these values, or a sensitivity analysis exploring their impact on the policy, is warranted.
* **Limited Comparison:** While compared to current clinical practices, the comparison lacks consideration of other personalized screening strategies that might exist or be emerging.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in healthcare.
* The proposed framework is novel, technically sound, and demonstrates promising results.
* The work has the potential to significantly improve screening policies and patient outcomes.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The high computational complexity could limit the practical applicability of the method.
* The reliance on strong modeling assumptions requires further investigation of robustness.
* A more comprehensive comparison to alternative personalized screening strategies would strengthen the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of personalized medicine and screening. The novelty of the approach, coupled with its theoretical grounding and promising experimental results, outweigh the identified weaknesses. While the computational complexity and modeling assumptions warrant further investigation, the potential impact of this work is substantial. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding computational scalability and model robustness. Specifically, the authors should provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost in practical settings, discuss potential mitigation strategies, and explore the sensitivity of the results to variations in the key model assumptions.
