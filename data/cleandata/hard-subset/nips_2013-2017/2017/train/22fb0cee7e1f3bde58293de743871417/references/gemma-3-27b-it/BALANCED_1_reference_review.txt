This paper tackles the important problem of personalized screening policies, moving beyond one-size-fits-all approaches. The core idea is to develop a dynamic policy that adjusts screening frequency based on patient features, screening history, and disease progression, accounting for both the cost of screening and the cost of delayed detection. The authors present a computationally effective procedure, with bounds on approximation error, applicable to a general class of disease models.

The paper builds upon prior work in screening (e.g., [7, 9]) by relaxing restrictions to Markov processes and stationary test outcome models – a crucial step given the complexities of real-world disease progression. It also addresses challenges like unknown delay costs and patient arrival patterns. The connection to related areas like controlled sensing [21, 34, 38] and POMDPs [18, 19] is acknowledged, highlighting the novelty of their approach in handling the specific complexities of screening.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Generality:** The framework is a significant advancement, enabling personalized policies for a broad range of diseases and models, addressing limitations of existing methods.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The provision of approximation error bounds adds confidence in the practicality and reliability of the proposed approach.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to breast cancer screening, utilizing a real-world dataset, demonstrates the potential impact of the methodology. The substantial reductions in screening frequency (28-68%) while maintaining delay are compelling.
* **Comprehensive Modeling:** The inclusion of external information (self-exams) and consideration of different cost functions (linear, quadratic) enhance the realism and adaptability of the model.
* **Clear Personalization:** The illustrative examples effectively demonstrate how the policy adapts to individual patient risk profiles and test results.
* **Robustness Analysis:** The discussion of robustness to model estimation error provides valuable insight into the practical limitations and potential mitigation strategies.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors acknowledge and address the computational challenges, the worst-case complexity (O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)) may still be prohibitive for very large state spaces or long time horizons. Further investigation of scalable approximation techniques would be valuable.
* **Model Assumptions:** The assumption of no false positives (Section 2) is a simplification. While acknowledged, it's a significant limitation, as false positives are common in screening and impact patient anxiety and further testing.
* **Dataset Description:** The description of the Athena Health Network dataset is relatively brief. More details regarding data quality, potential biases, and the process of feature selection would strengthen the results.
* **Limited Benchmarking:** The comparison is primarily against annual screening. Comparing against other risk-adaptive strategies or more sophisticated clinical guidelines would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
* **Cost Function Justification:** While the rationale for exploring different cost functions is sound, a more detailed discussion of how these costs would be determined in practice for specific diseases would be beneficial.
* **Algorithm Presentation:** The pseudo-code in the Appendix is difficult to follow without extensive familiarity with POMDP algorithms. A higher-level explanation of the key steps and their intuition would improve accessibility.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a clinically important problem.
* The method offers significant potential for improving screening efficiency and reducing patient burden.
* The experimental results demonstrate substantial improvements over existing clinical practice.
* The authors address potential limitations and provide a clear roadmap for future research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity may limit the scalability of the approach.
* The model assumptions (e.g., no false positives) are simplifications that may impact the real-world performance.
* The presentation of the algorithms could be improved for broader accessibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of personalized screening. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor, and the promising experimental results outweigh the identified weaknesses. While the computational complexity and model assumptions are concerns, they are acknowledged by the authors and represent areas for future work. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** 

I recommend acceptance, contingent on the following minor revisions:

1. **Computational Complexity:** Expand the discussion of potential scalability improvements, such as parallelization or alternative approximation techniques.
2. **Model Assumptions:** Acknowledge the impact of the no-false-positive assumption and discuss potential strategies for incorporating false positives into the model.
3. **Dataset Details:** Provide a more detailed description of the Athena Health Network dataset, including data quality measures and feature selection criteria.
4. **Algorithm Clarity:** Enhance the explanation of the algorithms in the Appendix, providing a higher-level overview of the key steps.
5. **Benchmarking:** Consider including comparisons against other relevant risk-adaptive screening strategies.
