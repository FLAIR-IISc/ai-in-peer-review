This paper proposes a dynamic personalized screening policy, DPSCREEN, aiming to optimize the trade-off between screening costs and delays in disease detection. The authors address complexities inherent in real-world screening scenarios, including non-Markovian disease progression, external information influencing patient arrival, and computational intractability. They present a computationally efficient approximation algorithm and provide bounds on its performance. The core contribution lies in formulating a general framework capable of accommodating diverse disease models and tailoring screening schedules to individual patient characteristics and evolving risk profiles.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a critical problem with significant practical implications. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing screening models, which often rely on strong Markovian assumptions and static policies. The proposed approach, aiming for personalization and dynamic adaptation, represents a logical progression in the field. However, despite the breadth of the problem tackled and the technical sophistication of the proposed solution, the paper suffers from several weaknesses.

A primary concern is the level of empirical validation. While the authors apply DPSCREEN to a breast cancer dataset, the results, while promising (28-68% reduction in screening), lack a robust comparison to simpler, well-established, and clinically implemented screening policies. Demonstrating superiority *only* against "current clinical policies" which are often based on heuristics rather than optimization is insufficient. A more convincing evaluation would involve comparing DPSCREEN against, for instance, age-based guidelines with varying screening frequencies, or risk-stratification models commonly used in practice. The synthetic exercise across different delay cost functions is interesting but doesn't substitute for real-world validation.

Furthermore, the computational complexity, while acknowledged, remains a potential barrier. The stated complexity, O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|), is significant, and the discussion of parallelization feels cursory. A more detailed analysis of scalability and practical runtimes for realistic problem sizes (large patient populations, long time horizons) is crucial. The assumptions underlying the approximation error bound (Proposition 1) – specifically, convexity of the delay cost and accurate model estimation – are strong and potentially unrealistic in many applications. The claim of robustness (Theorem 1) regarding estimation error, while theoretically appealing, needs further justification; simply stating that the policy is “locally constant” isn't a strong enough guarantee.

The model itself, while general, introduces several simplifying assumptions. The independence assumptions concerning test results and external information, conditional on the state trajectory, are potentially restrictive. The formulation of the cost function, while reasonable, is not rigorously justified. The absence of false positives is also a notable simplification. While acknowledged, the reliance on pre-estimated hazard rates (e.g., Gail model for breast cancer) introduces potential biases. The paper lacks a thorough sensitivity analysis exploring how the performance of DPSCREEN is affected by inaccuracies in these underlying estimates.

Finally, the presentation, while generally clear, suffers from a degree of mathematical density. Some sections, particularly those describing the Bellman equations and the PBVI algorithm, are challenging to follow without a strong background in POMDPs. Increased clarity and more intuitive explanations would improve accessibility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in personalized medicine.
*   Presents a general framework capable of handling complex disease models and dynamic patient information.
*   Offers a computationally tractable approximation algorithm.
*   Demonstrates promising results on a real-world dataset.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficient empirical validation; lacks comparison to established clinical practices beyond broad statements of superiority.
*   Significant computational complexity that may limit practical applicability.
*   Strong assumptions underlying the theoretical guarantees.
*   Simplifying assumptions in the model that may restrict its realism.
*   Mathematical density and potential lack of clarity in presentation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a conceptually sound and technically sophisticated approach to personalized screening. However, the lack of comprehensive empirical validation, concerns about computational scalability, and reliance on simplifying assumptions outweigh its strengths. While the framework holds potential, it requires further refinement and more rigorous evaluation before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field. I recommend **rejection** at this time, but encourage the authors to address the outlined weaknesses and resubmit in the future. A stronger emphasis on empirical validation against established benchmarks and a more detailed analysis of the algorithm's scalability are essential.
