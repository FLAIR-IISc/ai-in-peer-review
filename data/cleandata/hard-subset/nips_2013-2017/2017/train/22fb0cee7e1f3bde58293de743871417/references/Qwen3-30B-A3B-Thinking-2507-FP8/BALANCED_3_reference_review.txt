

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

This paper addresses the important problem of developing personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against delays in disease detection. The authors propose DPSCREEN, a method that extends beyond previous Markov-based approaches to handle general disease progression models, incorporate external information (e.g., self-examinations), and personalize screening based on both patient features and dynamic history. The paper makes a significant contribution by modeling disease as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process (not restricted to Markov or semi-Markov), which encompasses many disease models in the literature. It builds on POMDP frameworks but addresses key limitations of existing approaches by handling state trajectory-dependent costs and incorporating non-stationary external information.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical formulation of the screening problem and proves key properties of the value function (Lemma 1). The approximation error bound (Proposition 1) is particularly valuable as it justifies the practical use of their computationally tractable approach.

2. **Practical Impact:** The breast cancer case study demonstrates compelling results: 28-68% reduction in screenings while maintaining comparable detection delays compared to current clinical policies. This is a significant practical contribution with clear potential for healthcare impact.

3. **Novelty:** The paper clearly articulates how it differs from prior work. Previous screening frameworks [7,9,10] rely on restrictive assumptions (Markov disease processes, stationary test outcomes), while DPSCREEN handles more general disease models and incorporates external information. The related work section effectively contextualizes their contribution.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized with clear mathematical notation. The graphical illustrations (Figure 1, Figure 2) effectively demonstrate the personalization aspects of the approach. The experimental design and metrics are well-justified.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Implementation Details:** The paper could provide more details about the practical implementation challenges, particularly regarding the estimation of disease progression models from observational data. The model estimation section (Section 4.2) is somewhat brief, and it's unclear how robust their method is to model misspecification in real-world settings.

2. **Model Complexity vs. Practicality:** The paper uses a two-state disease model (Healthy/Disease) for breast cancer, but in reality, breast cancer progression involves multiple intermediate stages (e.g., benign tumors, precancerous conditions). The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't explore how incorporating these stages might affect the screening policy.

3. **Ethical Considerations:** The paper doesn't address potential ethical implications of personalized screening policies, such as how to handle patients with higher risk scores who might be disadvantaged by the risk-based approach or how to ensure equitable access to screening.

4. **Comparison Depth:** While the paper compares with annual/biennial screening policies, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of how the delay cost might vary across different patient subgroups (e.g., age, comorbidities), which would strengthen the case for personalization.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work addresses an important healthcare problem with a theoretically sound, practical approach that significantly outperforms current clinical policies in the breast cancer case study. The methodological contributions extend beyond the specific healthcare application and could be valuable for other sequential decision problems with similar characteristics.

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. In the revised version, please expand the discussion of model estimation challenges and robustness to model misspecification. This would strengthen the practical relevance of the work.

2. Consider incorporating a more nuanced disease model (e.g., with multiple intermediate states) for breast cancer to better reflect clinical reality, and discuss how this would affect the screening policy.

3. Add a brief discussion of ethical considerations related to personalized screening policies, particularly regarding potential disparities in access or outcomes across different patient populations.

4. Include a more detailed analysis of how delay costs might vary across patient subgroups, which would strengthen the argument for personalization.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field with a novel approach that combines theoretical rigor with practical healthcare impact. The results are compelling, the methodology is sound, and the paper is well-written. With the suggested improvements, it would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
