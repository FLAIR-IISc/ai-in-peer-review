Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing dynamic, personalized screening policies that balance screening costs and delay-to-detection costs. The disease process is modeled as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; test outcomes and exogenous “external information” (e.g., self-exams, comorbidities) are incorporated, and patients can arrive either at scheduled times or earlier due to external information crossing a threshold. The decision variable is a recommendation plan that maps current observations to a follow-up date, so decision epochs are endogenous. The authors formulate a belief-state dynamic program and show the value function is convex piecewise linear (as in POMDPs), but exact solution is intractable. They propose a point-based value iteration scheme that samples “likely” disease trajectories and reachable beliefs, along with an approximation-error bound tied to the sampling density and a robustness result implying the optimal policy is locally constant in model–belief space away from a lower-dimensional set. Experiments on a large breast cancer dataset (Athena) illustrate personalization by risk and history, and report 28–68% fewer screenings than annual/biennial policies at similar expected delays; they also show different behavior under linear vs quadratic delay costs.

This work builds on screening POMDPs for breast/colon cancer (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Erenay et al.) and personalized survival/joint modeling (e.g., Rizopoulos). Compared to prior OR/medical simulation work, DPSCREEN emphasizes a more general disease model (beyond Markov/Semi-Markov), includes exogenous information-triggered arrivals, and treats delay costs that depend on the entire trajectory.

Strengths
- Clear and relevant problem with potentially high clinical impact; the decision-epoch endogenization via external information is a useful modeling advance over standard POMDP/POSMDP formulations.
- Sound DP formulation with a belief-state representation, and the piecewise-linear/convexity property appropriately extended from POMDPs.
- Practical algorithmic approach via point-based backups on sampled “likely” trajectories and reachable beliefs; personalization to both baseline risk and dynamic history is demonstrated.
- Attention to approximation error and model-estimation robustness is welcome; the local-constancy result matches intuition for finite-action piecewise-linear value functions.
- Empirical results suggest large test reductions at similar expected delays, and the linear vs quadratic delay-cost comparison is a nice illustration of disease-specific trade-offs.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Key theoretical guarantees rely on unrealistic assumptions (e.g., no false positives and no false negatives; bounded screening interval; large T). The approximation-error bound Ω(B̄) is presented at a high level with unclear definition/notation (e.g., the role of “B” vs b′) and no constants; its practical interpretability is limited. The robustness theorem is plausible but proof details are deferred.
- There are inconsistencies between assumptions and experiments. Early sections assume no false positives; Proposition 1 further assumes perfect tests; yet experiments use mammogram/biopsy sensitivity/specificity. It is unclear whether the guarantees transfer.
- Evaluation is model-based off-policy: the disease/test models are learned from observational data and policies are evaluated by simulation under the learned model. This risks misspecification and dynamic confounding (screening frequency depends on prior test outcomes and unobserved factors). No calibration/validation (e.g., split-sample, external validation, goodness-of-fit, predictive checks) is shown, nor are uncertainty quantification or sensitivity analyses reported.
- Limited baselines. Comparisons are mainly to annual/biennial schedules; more informative baselines would include previously published personalized policies (Ayer, Maillart) and recent data-driven systems (e.g., ConfidentCare), plus simple risk-based interval heuristics.
- Practical costs/harms are simplified. Setting the screening cost to 1 regardless of test type and ignoring false-positive harms/overdiagnosis undercuts clinical realism. The self-exam model relies on literature-tuned parameters and a hard threshold for arrival; adherence heterogeneity is not explored.

Clarity
- Notation and exposition have issues: typos, unclear definitions (e.g., Ω(B̄)), and heavy reliance on appendices for essential algorithms/proofs. Some formulas (e.g., C̃ cases) are hard to parse. It is not always clear what the time unit is or how T, W are chosen.
- The action space (recommendation plans τ) is infinite if follow-up can be any t; the practical discretization/constraints should be made explicit in the main text with consequences for complexity.

Originality
- The core algorithmic approach adapts PBVI to a large trajectory-belief space; the originality is moderate but meaningful in the integration of exogenous information and trajectory-dependent delay costs with endogenous decision epochs.
- Claims of generality beyond Markov/Semi-Markov are interesting but not demonstrated empirically (the breast cancer model used is two-state Markov).

Significance
- If validated, such policies could materially reduce screening burden while preserving timeliness. However, the current evidence is preliminary; stronger validation, broader baselines, and realistic cost/harms modeling are needed for high-impact conclusions.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Timely problem with potential impact; solid problem formulation and DP insights.
- Practical approximate algorithm with demonstrable personalization.
- Empirical results suggest large screening reductions.
- Inclusion of exogenous information-triggered arrivals is a nice modeling contribution.

Cons:
- Theoretical guarantees hinge on unrealistic assumptions; mismatch with experimental setup.
- Evaluation lacks rigorous validation, uncertainty quantification, and strong baselines; susceptible to model misspecification and confounding.
- Clarity issues and heavy dependence on appendices for key details.
- Clinical realism limited (costs, false positives/overdiagnosis, adherence).

Questions and suggestions for improvement
- Reconcile assumptions: provide guarantees under imperfect tests and report how approximation bounds degrade with sensitivity/specificity.
- Strengthen evaluation: train/validation splits, posterior predictive checks, calibration of incidence timing, sensitivity analyses to model misspecification and self-exam parameters, and CIs via bootstrap or Bayesian posterior predictive intervals.
- Add baselines: state-of-the-art personalized screening POMDPs, ConfidentCare, risk-dense interval heuristics, and ablations (no external info; no history personalization).
- Model clinical harms: incorporate false positives, biopsy harms, overdiagnosis, and test-specific costs; explore trade-offs beyond expected delay (e.g., late-stage detection probability).
- Clarify action space discretization, complexity in practice, and provide more algorithmic detail in the main paper.
- Improve notation and definitions (especially Ω(B̄)); fix typos and unify assumptions across sections.

Recommendation
Reject (encourage resubmission). The paper addresses an important problem with a thoughtful formulation and promising algorithmic ideas, but the current version falls short on theoretical rigor under realistic assumptions and on empirical validation with strong baselines and uncertainty quantification. Addressing the above concerns would make this a much stronger submission.
