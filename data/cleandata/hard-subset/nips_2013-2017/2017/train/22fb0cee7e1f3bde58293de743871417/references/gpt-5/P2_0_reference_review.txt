Summary and relation to prior work
The paper formulates dynamic, personalized screening as a sequential decision problem with: (i) a general finite-state disease process with an absorbing disease state, (ii) an external information process (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger early arrivals, (iii) tests with stochastic outcomes, and (iv) a cost that trades off discounted screening costs against delay-to-detection costs that depend on the future disease trajectory. The authors cast the problem as a belief-based dynamic program, prove piecewise-linear convexity of the value function in beliefs, show that exact dynamic programming is intractable, and propose a point-based value iteration (PBVI) approach (DPSCREEN) tailored to this setting by sampling likely disease trajectories and reachable beliefs. They give a bound on approximation error in terms of belief-sampling density under strong assumptions, and a local constancy result suggesting robustness of optimal actions to small model-estimation errors. They illustrate on a large breast-cancer dataset, showing large reductions in screening counts at matched expected delay.

Relative to prior screening work (Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Erenay et al.), the model allows non-Markov disease trajectories, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and an external-information-driven arrival process; relative to point-based POMDP methods (Pineau et al.) the algorithmic core is standard but adapted with a new belief-construction scheme. Compared to medical simulation frameworks (e.g., MISCAN-COLON), this work optimizes policies rather than comparing a fixed set.

Strengths
- Ambitious and timely problem formulation: integrates external information, dynamic personalization, and trajectory-based delay costs in a unified framework. This is an important setting for precision screening.
- Clear dynamic-programming perspective: the belief-state formulation and piecewise-linear convexity are well motivated and link to known POMDP structure.
- Practical approximate solution: a PBVI-style method with a domain-aware belief sampling procedure is plausible and implementable; complexity and parallelizability are discussed.
- Some theoretical support: a (coarse) approximation error bound tied to sampling density and a robustness argument (local constancy) provide intuition for when the approximate policy should be close to optimal.
- Empirical illustration at scale: using 45k patients to build a model and demonstrating substantial reductions in expected screening at matched delays is encouraging; inclusion/exclusion of self-exams shows the value of external information.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
- Novelty is modest on the algorithmic side. The core method is PBVI with tailored belief sampling. The main conceptual novelty is in the modeling assumptions (arrival driven by external information and action) rather than in new algorithmic techniques. The paper would be stronger with additional structural insights or guarantees specific to this endogenous-arrival setting beyond standard POMDP properties.
- Theory relies on strong, partially unrealistic assumptions. The approximation error bound assumes convex delay costs, perfect tests (no false positives/negatives), bounded screening intervals, and sufficiently large horizons. Yet the experiments explicitly use non-perfect test sensitivities/specificities. The gap between theoretical assumptions and practical setting limits the impact of the bound. The local-constancy theorem is plausible for piecewise-linear value functions but is stated at a high level; conditions and proof sketch are deferred, leaving unclear when model perturbations may cross decision boundaries (which can be common).
- Evaluation uses a learned model for both training and policy evaluation. While this follows some screening literature, it risks optimistic estimates. There is no out-of-sample policy evaluation with counterfactual estimators (e.g., off-policy evaluation, doubly robust methods) nor sensitivity analyses to model misspecification. The paper discusses robustness qualitatively but lacks empirical robustness tests (e.g., perturbing hazards/sensitivity/specificity; re-estimation under bootstrap; reporting variability).
- Limited baselines. Comparisons are to annual/biennial screening only. It would help to benchmark against prior POMDP-based screening policies (Ayer, Maillart) instantiated on the same learned model, heuristic risk-threshold policies, and ConfidentCare (Alaa et al.) to quantify gains due to endogenous arrival and trajectory-based costs.
- Claims of generality vs. instantiated model. Although the framework allows arbitrary finite-state disease processes and trajectory-based costs, the demonstration uses a simple 2-state Markov model with logistic hazards, which undermines the empirical case for handling non-Markov dynamics. Showing a non-Markov or semi-Markov example, or a richer state model (e.g., benign/risky/malignant) would strengthen the argument.
- Some modeling/clarity issues:
  - Earlier the paper assumes no false positives upon diagnosis (Z+ implies disease), whereas experiments use imperfect mammography/biopsy. Please reconcile.
  - Costing treats mammogram and biopsy as equal unit cost, which is not realistic and may bias policy conclusions; a sensitivity study with heterogeneous test costs would be informative.
  - Important implementation details are deferred: how K and B̄ are chosen; runtime and scalability on the breast-cancer instance; convergence diagnostics; selection of w to meet delay constraints.
  - Notation and a few equations could be tightened; the complexity expression O(T(B)2T2K|Y||Z|) is hard to parse.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation and DP are sound; the PWL convexity lemma is credible. The approximation analysis is directionally useful but depends on strong assumptions not met in experiments. Empirical evaluation is promising but limited by model-based evaluation and baseline choice.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized; however, the paper leans heavily on the supplement for key proofs/algorithms and could better reconcile assumptions across sections. More concrete algorithmic details and ablations would improve reproducibility and understanding.

Originality
- Medium. The modeling of endogenous arrival plus trajectory-based delay costs in a POMDP-like framework is a nice synthesis. The solution method largely adapts known PBVI ideas with a domain-specific belief sampling scheme.

Significance
- Potentially high for personalized screening if validated, but current evidence is preliminary due to model-based evaluation and limited baselines. Stronger empirical validation and robustness analysis would raise impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add empirical robustness: off-policy evaluation on held-out data; sensitivity to hazard/test-accuracy parameters; heterogeneous test costs.
- Compare against prior POMDP-based policies and heuristic risk-threshold schedules.
- Provide implementation details: K, |B̄| per t, runtime, memory, parallelization, and how w is tuned to meet delay constraints.
- Demonstrate a richer or non-Markov disease model to substantiate generality.
- Reconcile test-accuracy assumptions and revisit the theoretical bound under imperfect tests, if possible.

Pros
- Important problem; principled formulation
- Adapted PBVI with belief sampling; scalable in practice
- Some theoretical guidance
- Large-scale empirical illustration; clear personalization effects

Cons
- Limited algorithmic novelty; theory under strong assumptions
- Model-based evaluation without robust counterfactual checks
- Sparse baselines; simplified cost model
- Some inconsistencies/clarity issues

Recommendation
- Leaning toward rejection in current form. With stronger empirical validation, richer baselines, and clearer alignment between assumptions, theory, and experiments, this could become a solid contribution.
