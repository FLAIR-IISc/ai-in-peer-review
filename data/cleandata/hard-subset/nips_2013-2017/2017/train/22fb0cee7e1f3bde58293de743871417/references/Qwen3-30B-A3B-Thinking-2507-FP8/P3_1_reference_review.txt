

# Peer Review: DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the delay in disease detection. The authors address a critical gap in the literature by developing a framework that can handle general disease progression models (not restricted to Markov processes), incorporates external information (e.g., patient self-examinations), and personalizes screening based on patient features and dynamic history. The problem is formulated as an optimal control problem with a belief state, where the belief represents the probability distribution over disease state trajectories and diagnosis labels. The authors prove the value function is convex and piecewise linear (extending POMDP theory), develop a point-based value iteration approach to approximate the optimal policy, and provide theoretical bounds on the approximation error. They validate their approach using a breast cancer dataset, demonstrating 28-68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining equivalent detection delays compared to current clinical practices.

This work extends beyond previous POMDP-based screening approaches ([7], [9]) that assumed Markov disease processes and stationary test outcome models, and addresses limitations of simulation-based approaches used in medical literature ([44]-[48]) that only compare a fixed set of policies rather than finding optimal ones.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Problem Addressed**: The paper tackles a highly relevant and impactful healthcare problem with clear societal implications. Personalized screening has the potential to substantially reduce healthcare costs while maintaining detection quality.

2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The authors provide a rigorous mathematical foundation, proving the value function is convex and piecewise linear (Lemma 1), which is a key insight that enables their approximation algorithm. This extends standard POMDP theory to their specific problem setting.

3. **Novel Application of POMDP Techniques**: While point-based value iteration is not novel, its application to this specific healthcare problem with general disease models is novel and well-motivated. The authors clearly articulate why standard POMDP methods cannot be applied directly.

4. **Compelling Experimental Results**: The breast cancer case study demonstrates significant practical improvements over current clinical practices (28-68% reduction in screenings), which is impressive and well-documented. The ablation study showing the impact of self-examination (57-68% vs. 28-45% reduction) is particularly convincing.

5. **Theoretical Guarantees**: The paper provides meaningful theoretical bounds on the approximation error (Proposition 1), which is crucial for establishing the validity of their approach.

6. **Clear Presentation of Personalization Mechanism**: The graphical representation in Figure 1 effectively demonstrates how the policy personalizes to both patient features (ex ante risk) and dynamic history (test outcomes and external information).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Disease Modeling**: While the paper claims to handle "very general" disease models, the experimental validation is limited to a two-state Markov model for breast cancer. This contradicts the paper's claim of handling non-Markov disease processes. For example, the paper mentions pancreatic cancer as having a "very quick" survival decrease with delay (Section 4), but doesn't demonstrate their approach with a non-Markov disease model.

2. **Overly Simplistic Disease Model**: The breast cancer model (two states: Healthy/Disease) is quite simplistic for a complex disease like breast cancer, which typically involves multiple pre-cancerous stages (e.g., benign tumors, DCIS). This undermines the paper's claim of handling "very general" disease models.

3. **Theoretical Bound Limitations**: The approximation error bound (Proposition 1) depends on Ω(B̄), which is defined as "the density of sampling of the belief simplex." However, the paper doesn't provide guidance on how to choose an appropriate sampling density in practice, making the bound somewhat theoretical rather than practically useful.

4. **Lack of Comparison with Recent Deep RL Approaches**: The paper doesn't compare with recent deep reinforcement learning approaches for similar problems (e.g., [44], [50] which use deep RL for healthcare decision making). While the authors argue their approach is more general, they don't demonstrate why their method is superior to these more recent techniques.

5. **Computational Complexity Vagueness**: The complexity analysis (O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)) is not very informative for practical implementation. The paper doesn't provide any empirical evidence of computational time or scalability with problem size.

6. **Limited Discussion of Clinical Implementation**: The paper doesn't address practical barriers to implementing their approach in clinical settings, such as clinician acceptance, integration with electronic health records, or potential ethical considerations.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with a solid theoretical foundation and well-executed experiments. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results. However, the model simplifications (two-state Markov for breast cancer) and the limited demonstration of the "general" disease model capability weaken the overall contribution. The paper is complete as a scientific contribution, but the authors could have been more transparent about the limitations of their disease model.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The problem formulation is thorough, and the mathematical notation is consistent. However, some technical details (particularly regarding the belief representation and the connection between the disease state trajectories and the belief space) could be clarified with more intuitive explanations. The graphical model in Appendix B would be helpful to include in the main text.

## Originality Assessment

The paper's main contribution is the application of POMDP theory to a personalized screening problem with general disease models and external information, which is novel. However, the core algorithm (point-based value iteration) is not new to the POMDP community. The novelty lies in the problem formulation and the specific healthcare application. The paper adequately references related work in operations research, medical literature, and decision theory, though it could better distinguish itself from recent deep RL approaches for similar problems.

## Significance Assessment

The significance of this work is high due to the potential for substantial healthcare cost savings with maintained detection quality. The 28-68% reduction in screenings compared to current clinical practices is a compelling result that could have real-world impact. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the simplified disease model used in the experiments. If the approach were demonstrated with a more complex disease model, the significance would be even greater.

## Recommendations for Improvement

1. Extend the experimental validation to a non-Markov disease model (e.g., a three-state model for breast cancer with benign and malignant precancerous stages) to better demonstrate the "general" nature of their approach.

2. Provide more concrete guidance on choosing the sampling density Ω(B̄) for practical implementation.

3. Include a comparison with recent deep reinforcement learning approaches for personalized healthcare decision making.

4. Discuss potential barriers to clinical implementation and how they might be addressed.

5. Clarify the relationship between the two-state Markov model used in experiments and the "general" disease models claimed in the introduction.

## Final Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper, with the understanding that the authors should address the limitations mentioned above in the camera-ready version. The paper makes a significant contribution to the application of decision-theoretic methods in healthcare, with strong theoretical foundations and compelling empirical results. While the disease model simplifications are a limitation, the core methodology is novel and well-executed, and the practical impact demonstrated is substantial.

The paper aligns well with NIPS' focus on novel machine learning approaches with real-world impact, particularly in healthcare applications. It provides a theoretically grounded method for a challenging decision-making problem with clear practical benefits.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments**:
- Addresses a significant real-world healthcare problem with clear societal impact
- Provides a solid theoretical foundation extending POMDP theory
- Demonstrates substantial improvements (28-68%) over current clinical practices
- Includes meaningful theoretical bounds on approximation error
- Well-structured and clearly written

**Con Acceptance Arguments**:
- Limited disease model complexity undermines the "general" claim
- Simplistic two-state model for breast cancer doesn't reflect disease complexity
- Limited comparison with recent deep RL approaches for similar problems
- Theoretical approximation error bound lacks practical guidance

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of healthcare decision making with a well-executed methodological approach. The minor limitations noted do not significantly detract from the paper's value and could be addressed in the final version.
