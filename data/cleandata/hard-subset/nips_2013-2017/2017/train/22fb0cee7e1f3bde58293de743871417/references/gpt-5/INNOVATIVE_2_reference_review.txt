Summary
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a general framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off test cost against delay in disease detection. Unlike prior screening models that typically assume Markov disease dynamics, fixed decision epochs, and state-dependent costs, DPSCREEN allows: (i) arbitrary finite-state disease processes (including non-Markov, survival, first-hitting-time models), (ii) exogenous “external information” (e.g., self-exam signals) that triggers unscheduled arrivals, (iii) endogenous decision times that depend on actions and exogenous signals, and (iv) trajectory-dependent delay costs. The authors cast the problem in a belief space over full state trajectories and a diagnosis label; prove piecewise-linear convexity of the finite-horizon value function; and design a tractable, PBVI-style approximation that samples likely disease trajectories and reachable beliefs to construct alpha-vectors. They provide an approximation-error bound in terms of the density of sampled beliefs and a robustness result showing local invariance of optimal actions to small model-estimation errors. On a 45k-patient breast cancer dataset, they estimate a simple two-state disease model (with external self-exam signals), and report 28–68% reductions in screenings relative to annual/biennial clinical schedules for comparable expected detection delay. They also illustrate how delay cost shape (linear vs quadratic) shifts the operating regime.

Relation to prior work
The paper relates to POMDP/POSMDP planning and point-based methods, personalized screening POMDPs (Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Erenay et al.), Bayesian design approaches (Rizopoulos; Alaa “ConfidentCare”), and controlled sensing. The key claimed differences are allowing trajectory-dependent costs, non-Markov/non-stationary processes, and exogenous triggers for unscheduled visits, for which standard POMDP/POSMDP formulations are argued to be inadequate. The algorithmic core builds on PBVI, adapted to a belief space over sampled state-trajectories.

Strengths
- Ambitious, unified formulation that captures multiple practical aspects of screening rarely handled together (endogenous timing, exogenous info, trajectory-dependent costs).
- Clear theoretical insight that finite-horizon value remains piecewise-linear convex over a (very high-dimensional) belief space, enabling alpha-vector methods.
- Practical PBVI-style algorithm with reachability-driven belief sampling; approximation-error bound (albeit under assumptions).
- Compelling applied motivation; the empirical results suggest large cost reductions without sacrificing expected delay and demonstrate personalization across risk groups and dynamic histories.
- Useful discussion of how disease-specific cost functions shape optimal testing aggressiveness.

Weaknesses
- Some technical inconsistencies/clarity issues: the Bellman equation (Eq. 3) uses max with negative costs though the objective is a minimization problem; this needs correction/justification.
- The approximation bound requires strong assumptions (perfect testing, bounded intervals, large horizon) and uses an uncalibrated constant ζ; it is unclear how informative it is for realistic settings (where tests are imperfect—indeed used in experiments).
- The “robustness” theorem (local constancy of actions) is stated abstractly with limited intuition and no clear practical diagnostic for when model misspecification affects policy.
- The empirical study, while sizable, collapses the disease model to a two-state Markov process, undercutting the claimed generality. External information is stylized; parameterization borrows literature sensitivities/specificities and imposes tumor growth law; limited validation of model fit and sensitivity to misspecification.
- Baselines are limited to annual/biennial clinical policies; missing head-to-head comparisons with prior personalized POMDP screening work and other adaptive policies.
- Reproducibility is unclear (no code/implementation details beyond pseudo-code, no hyperparameter sensitivity, limited ablations).

Quality
- Theory seems plausible (piecewise-linear convexity on an augmented belief space), but key proofs are deferred, and there is a sign/optimization inconsistency in the Bellman equation that must be fixed.
- The approximation analysis gives a direction but rests on restrictive assumptions.
- Empirics show promising gains, but evaluation is policy-simulation-on-estimated-model; there is no counterfactual validation or comparison to learned baselines (e.g., POMDP solvers on the simplified model), and limited sensitivity analyses.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and motivated. However: fix the Bellman sign/min-max issue; tighten notation for beliefs over ST×{0,1}; clarify how “arrival” and “recommendation plan” interplay; define ζ and Ω(B̄) more concretely; reconcile “no false positives” in the model with imperfect tests in experiments. Some references are incomplete.

Originality
- The problem formulation (combining exogenous arrivals, endogenous timing, and trajectory-dependent costs with general disease processes) is bold and, to my knowledge, novel within ML/OR screening literature.
- The algorithmic contribution is an adaptation of PBVI with trajectory-belief sampling; conceptually natural but not radically new from a planning perspective. The novelty is more in the problem casting and demonstrating that POMDP machinery can be extended here.

Significance
- If adopted, this could meaningfully influence personalized screening policy design and spur ML work on planning with exogenous signals and trajectory-based costs. The breast cancer results, while model-based, suggest substantial savings. The general framework and insights about cost shaping are valuable.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Fix the Bellman min/max and cost sign; clarify the role of τ (recommendation plan) as the action.
- Provide a concrete, short proof sketch for Lemma 1 in the main text; explain why trajectory-dependent costs still yield PWLC.
- Reconcile test assumptions in theory vs experiments; if the bound can be extended to imperfect tests, state conditions.
- Add sensitivity analyses for model misspecification (e.g., vary sensitivity/specificity, growth parameters, external-signal noise) and report impact on policy and outcomes.
- Compare against prior personalized POMDP baselines (Ayer/Maillart) on the simplified Markov model; also include learned policies (e.g., standard PBVI on an augmented state).
- Report full trade-off curves and E[Δ|R,D] and stage-at-detection metrics; ablate external info to quantify its value.
- Provide implementation details and release code to improve reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, ambitious formulation capturing realistic screening complexities.
- Theoretical insight enabling alpha-vector planning despite trajectory costs.
- Practical algorithm with promising empirical gains on a large dataset.
- Timely and relevant to ML for health and sequential decision-making.

Cons for acceptance
- Technical clarity issues (Bellman sign; theoretical assumptions vs experiments).
- Approximation bound of limited practical bite; robustness result abstract.
- Empirics do not fully validate generality; limited baselines and sensitivity.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper advances a bold and novel direction; despite imperfections, its conceptual contribution and potential impact justify inclusion if the authors address the major clarity issues and strengthen empirical comparisons. Confidence: medium.
