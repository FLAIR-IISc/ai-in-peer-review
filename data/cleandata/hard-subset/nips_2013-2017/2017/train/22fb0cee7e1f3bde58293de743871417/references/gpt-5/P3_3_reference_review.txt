Summary
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a general framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off screening burden against delay in disease detection. The disease is modeled as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, augmented by an exogenous “external information” process (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger early arrivals. The objective combines discounted screening costs and a delay cost that depends on the incidence and detection times. The authors cast the problem as a belief-state decision process over distributions on full state trajectories (and a diagnosis label), derive Bellman equations, show the value function is convex and piecewise-linear, and construct exact alpha-vector recursions (intractable in general). They then develop a PBVI-style approximation tailored to the very high-dimensional belief over trajectories by (i) sampling likely physiological trajectories and (ii) constructing reachable beliefs under simulated policies (including random and clinical baselines). They provide a coarse bound on approximation error driven by belief-sampling density, and a robustness result arguing local constancy of optimal actions under small model mis-specification. Empirically, they instantiate the framework for breast cancer using a large de-identified cohort (age 60–65), estimate a two-state Markov disease model with Gail risk features and a simple lump growth model, and report 28–68% reductions in screenings versus annual/biennial policies at comparable expected delays; they also demonstrate how more convex delay costs induce more aggressive screening.

Relation to prior work
This work sits at the intersection of controlled sensing, POMDPs/POSMDPs, and medical screening optimization. Prior OR work (e.g., Ayer et al. OR 2012; Maillart et al. OR 2008; Erenay et al. MSOM 2011) typically assumes Markov disease processes and simpler observation/arrival models, and often optimizes fixed schedules or policies over predefined classes. Medical simulation efforts (e.g., MISCAN-COLON) compare hand-crafted schedules rather than optimizing over the policy space. Compared to risk-based heuristics and ConfidentCare-style decision systems, this paper’s main novelty is the explicit optimization over dynamic, history-dependent screening intervals in the presence of exogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent delay costs using a generalized belief-state DP. The PBVI adaptation to trajectory beliefs and the attempt at error control are also novel in this context.

Strengths
- Problem importance: Personalized screening with dynamic information is clinically relevant; the cost-delay tradeoff and incorporation of exogenous arrivals are compelling.
- Generality: The modeling framework accommodates non-Markov disease trajectories and exogenous information, and defines a value function with trajectory-dependent costs.
- Theory: Extends piecewise-linear/convex value results to this setting; provides alpha-vector recursions; gives an approximation-error bound tied to belief-sampling density; shows local constancy of optimal recommendations under small parameter perturbations.
- Algorithmic contribution: A practically minded PBVI variant that samples physiologically plausible trajectories and reachable beliefs using both random and clinical-policy rollouts.
- Empirical promise: On a large real dataset, reports substantial reductions in screenings without worsening expected delay; the quadratic-delay example is an informative case study.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical assumptions and consistency
- The paper alternates between assuming no false positives (and in the bound, even no false negatives) and using realistic mammography sensitivity/specificity with biopsy confirmation (e.g., z=2 implies mammogram positive, biopsy negative). This inconsistency affects both modeling and theoretical claims (e.g., Proposition 1).
- The delay cost treatment is inconsistent: the main formulation does not discount delay (only screening cost has δt), whereas the bound assumes C(td − tD; tD) = c(td − tD)δtD. Clarify and align assumptions.
- Equation (3) appears to mix signs and min/max: the objective is a minimization of costs, yet a max over τ with a leading negative sign is written. Please correct the Bellman equation and clarify whether V is a cost-to-go or negative value.
- Independence assumptions (current test independent of past given trajectory; external info independent of past given trajectory) may be violated in practice (e.g., behavioral feedback). Some discussion of robustness to these violations would help.

Theoretical contributions
- Lemma 1 and the alpha-vector construction plausibly carry over from POMDPs, but the proof sketches are deferred; since costs depend on trajectories, the reduction to a standard POMDP on an augmented state needs to be made explicit.
- Proposition 1 is high-level: the constant ζ is unspecified, the dependence on K (number of sampled trajectories) and B (belief points) is unclear, and the assumptions (perfect tests, bounded intervals, large T) are strong. A synthetic experiment quantifying error versus sampling density would strengthen this.
- Theorem 1 (local constancy) is a generic property of finite-action argmax policies under mild regularity; as stated it is useful but not especially strong. Clarify conditions and its practical implications (e.g., parameter ranges where decisions change).

Algorithmic scalability and reproducibility
- Belief over trajectories scales as |S|^T; while the sampling scheme is sensible, complexity remains high. Please report concrete runtimes, K, B, W, T, and parallelization details used in experiments.
- More details are needed to reproduce model estimation: priors for MCMC, convergence diagnostics, how Gail risk is integrated with logistic pin/ptr, and calibration of lump model parameters from sensitivity/specificity.

Empirical evaluation
- Validation relies on a learned generative model; there is no external validation of the model’s calibration (e.g., held-out Brier scores on incidence, calibration plots). Without that, policy improvements may be sensitive to misspecification.
- Comparisons are primarily to annual/biennial schedules. Please include comparisons to prior personalized policies (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al., ConfidentCare) and to simple reactive rules with exogenous arrivals, to isolate the benefit of the proposed optimization.
- Report uncertainty (CIs) for E[N], E[Δ], and E[Δ|D], and distributional metrics (e.g., tail delays, fraction exceeding clinical thresholds). Average delays can mask harmful long tails.
- Generalizability is unclear: cohort limited to ages 60–65; risk-grouping threshold at 5% is arbitrary; fairness and subgroup analyses (e.g., by density, family history) would be helpful.

Clarity
- The manuscript is dense and occasionally inconsistent (test accuracy, discounting, sign conventions). A clearer separation of the general framework, the assumptions used for theory, and those used in experiments would improve readability.
- Many crucial details are in the Supplement; ensure all proofs and algorithmic specifics are available.

Questions for the authors
- Please reconcile the assumptions on test accuracy between the theory and experiments, and clarify how biopsy is modeled.
- Provide explicit corrected Bellman equations (min vs. max; sign conventions) and the augmented-state reduction justifying piecewise linearity.
- Report computational budgets and sensitivity of results to K, B, W, and belief set construction.
- How calibrated is the learned disease model? Provide held-out predictive performance and sensitivity analyses to misspecification.
- Can you compare against existing personalized screening POMDP baselines and ConfidentCare?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a principled, general framework.
- Solid integration of exogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent costs into a belief-state DP.
- Practical PBVI adaptation and promising empirical results on real data.

Cons for acceptance
- Inconsistencies and strong assumptions in theory; coarse error bound without empirical validation.
- Limited baselines and lack of model calibration assessment.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues; missing quantitative runtime and sensitivity analyses.

Recommendation
Borderline. The paper is ambitious and potentially impactful, with a novel and thoughtful formulation and promising empirical findings. However, theoretical assumptions need to be aligned with the experimental setting, core equations clarified, and empirical validation strengthened (calibration, baselines, uncertainty). With revisions addressing these points, this could be a strong contribution.
