Summary
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off screening cost and delay-to-detection cost across heterogeneous patients and over time. The disease process is modeled very generally as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with a single absorbing disease state; observations include both clinician-administered tests and an external information process (e.g., self-exam) that can trigger unscheduled visits. Actions specify the next screening time (or diagnosis), and the objective is a weighted sum of discounted screening costs and delay costs. The authors derive Bellman equations on beliefs over state trajectories (rather than Markov states), prove convex piecewise-linear value functions (as in POMDPs), and propose a tractable approximation using a point-based value iteration (PBVI)-like method that samples likely trajectories and reachable beliefs. They provide an approximation bound tied to the density of sampled beliefs and a robustness result showing local constancy of optimal actions under small model-estimation perturbations. An application on a large de-identified breast cancer dataset suggests substantial reductions (28–68%) in screening counts at comparable expected delays versus annual/biennial schedules. A synthetic comparison with quadratic versus linear delay costs illustrates disease-specific policy aggressiveness.

Strengths
- Ambitious and relevant problem: unifying personalized, dynamic screening with external, endogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent delays goes beyond standard POMDP screening formulations.
- Conceptual generality: modeling beliefs over trajectories allows non-Markov disease processes and nonstationary observation models, enabling inclusion of external info that affects arrival.
- Theoretical structure: extension of POMDP convexity/piecewise linearity of the value function to this nonstandard setting, plus a PBVI-style algorithm tailored to extremely high-dimensional beliefs via trajectory sampling.
- Practical focus: a clear objective (minimize screens at fixed expected delay), use of real clinical data, and demonstration of personalization by risk group and time-varying history.
- Robustness intuition: local constancy of optimal actions outside a lower-dimensional set lends stability to small model-estimation errors.

Weaknesses and questions
Technical correctness and clarity
- Bellman sign and objective: Eq. (3) uses a maximization with a leading negative term for costs, while the problem is posed as minimization. Please clarify the sign convention and ensure consistency throughout. The definition of C̃ mixes immediate and terminal delay costs; a more formal derivation would help avoid double-counting.
- Belief space over trajectories: While expressive, the belief is over ST×{0,1}, whose dimension explodes in T. The approximation constructs beliefs over sampled trajectories S̃. What problem is actually being solved—an induced “projected POMDP” on S̃—or an importance-weighted approximation? Do the computed alpha-vectors define a valid policy for the original model, or only for the reduced model? This distinction matters for theoretical guarantees.
- Approximation bound: Proposition 1 assumes perfect tests (no false positives/negatives), convex delay c(d), bounded inter-screen interval, large T, and introduces ζ without a precise definition or derivation. In practice, tests are imperfect (indeed the application models mammogram sensitivity/specificity). Is there any bound under realistic error? How is ζ computed or bounded from model parameters? As stated, the bound risks being vacuous or inapplicable to the experiments.
- Robustness result: Theorem 1 (local constancy of τ*) is intuitive and likely true under uniqueness/continuity assumptions, but the statement is abstract (no conditions on policy class or regularity of the model-to-value map). It would be helpful to state precise conditions and provide at least a proof sketch, and to clarify whether the set E has Lebesgue measure zero in L.
- Independence assumptions: The model assumes Z(t) ⟂ Y(t) given the state trajectory and features, and conditional independence of current outcomes from past given the trajectory. Given that Y and Z are often co-determined by latent tumor biology and prior interventions, some discussion of the realism and the sensitivity to violations would be useful.

Empirical evaluation
- Baselines: Comparisons are only to annual/biennial schedules. Given prior POMDP-based personalized screening work (e.g., Ayer et al. 2012; Maillart et al. 2008), it would be important to implement at least one such baseline (even if those models are less expressive) on the same learned disease model to quantify DPSCREEN’s advantage beyond clinical heuristics.
- Generality vs. instantiation: The core methodological claim is general (non-Markov disease, nonstationary observations), but the main experiment uses a two-state Markov disease model. Showing one additional setting that leverages the non-Markov generality (e.g., semi-Markov progression or competing risks) would strengthen the significance.
- Outcome metrics: The objective aligns with expected delay and number of screens. However, clinical impact is mediated by stage at detection, overdiagnosis, false positives/biopsies, and quality-adjusted outcomes. You partially address this via E[Δ|R,D], but additional metrics (e.g., false positive rate, benign biopsy rate, stage distribution at detection) would make the case more compelling.
- Self-exam modeling: The large gains with self-exams rely on a monthly process with parameters calibrated from literature. Given the controversy around self-exams, sensitivity analyses over ι, σ, ỹ and the frequency of self-exams would help.
- Reproducibility and ablations: Key algorithmic choices (K, B, W, sampling policy mix, runtime, parallelization details) and sensitivity to these hyperparameters are not reported. An ablation of belief-construction strategies and K vs. performance would be valuable.

Positioning and related work
- The claim of “first such personalized screening policy in a very general setting” should be tempered and better positioned relative to: POMDP screening with personalized transitions; Bayesian design approaches; and recent decision-support systems (e.g., ConfidentCare). A direct empirical comparison to at least one is recommended.
- Some references are incomplete and a few statements seem inconsistent: earlier sections assume “no false positives” for diagnosis, whereas the application models imperfect mammography and relies on biopsy to confirm disease. Clarify that “no false positives” pertains to the diagnostic confirmation, not preliminary tests.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important societal problem with a principled sequential decision framework.
- Extends POMDP structure to a nonstandard, endogenous-arrival setting and proposes a practical approximation.
- Promising empirical reductions in screening burden at comparable delays.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical guarantees rely on restrictive assumptions and are not clearly connected to the experimental setup.
- Limited baselines; empirical validation does not yet demonstrate superiority over prior personalized screening models.
- Several clarity issues (Bellman sign, definition of ζ, formalism of reduced belief space) and missing implementation details.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper is thought-provoking and potentially impactful, but it needs stronger empirical comparisons, clarified and better-justified theoretical results (or at least alignment of assumptions with experiments), and more detail for reproducibility. Addressing the above issues could make it a strong submission to NeurIPS or a health/OR venue.
