This paper tackles a highly relevant and challenging problem: personalized screening policies. The core idea – dynamically adjusting screening frequency based on patient features, screening history, and disease model – is both intuitive and potentially impactful. The authors attempt a very general formulation, going beyond Markovian assumptions common in prior work, and address the computational intractability with a point-based value iteration approach. This ambition and breadth of scope are significant strengths.

The technical approach, while complex, appears sound. The formulation leveraging dynamic survival models and incorporating external information feels appropriately general. The attempt to bound the approximation error (Proposition 1) is commendable, even if the practical implications of that bound remain unclear without more detailed analysis. The breast cancer dataset application provides a concrete illustration, and the reported 28-68% reduction in screenings while maintaining delay is impressive. The exploration of different cost functions highlights the importance of tailoring the policy to the specific disease context.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of deep engagement with potential weaknesses and a somewhat superficial validation. While the authors acknowledge estimation error, the discussion feels cursory. How sensitive are the results to inaccuracies in the estimated disease model parameters? The robustness analysis feels incomplete. The dependence on the Gail model for risk assessment, while standard, should be discussed more critically - this model isn’t perfect. 

The core algorithm (DPSCREEN) is described in high-level terms, referring to appendices for details. This makes it difficult to assess the computational feasibility for truly large-scale applications. The complexity analysis mentions O(T(B)^2 T^2 K|Y||Z|), which is daunting, and more discussion on practical mitigation strategies would be valuable. While the PBVI approximation is reasonable, the choice of belief space sampling (Algorithm 1) feels somewhat ad-hoc; a more principled justification or comparison to other sampling strategies would strengthen the approach. 

The presentation, while generally clear, could be improved. The numerous equations and symbols, particularly in Section 2, make the paper dense and challenging to follow. More intuitive explanations and perhaps simpler notation could enhance readability. The connection between the theoretical results (Bellman equations, approximation bounds) and the practical implementation is not always immediately apparent.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper addresses a genuinely important and challenging problem with a novel, broadly applicable framework. It goes beyond existing approaches that rely on strong Markovian assumptions.
*   **Potential Impact:** Personalized screening has the potential to significantly improve healthcare efficiency and patient outcomes.
*   **Soundness:** The technical approach appears logically sound, and the initial results are promising.
*   **Boldness:** The authors tackle a complex problem with a general formulation, rather than focusing on a simplified case.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Incomplete Validation:** The validation relies on a single dataset and a relatively simple disease model. More comprehensive validation, including sensitivity analysis and comparisons to alternative approaches, is needed.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The computational complexity of the proposed algorithm is a concern.
*   **Lack of Detailed Analysis:** The discussion of robustness, estimation error, and algorithm details is superficial.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is dense and challenging to read in places.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its flaws, I lean towards acceptance. The paper presents a genuinely novel and potentially impactful approach to a critical problem. The ambition and breadth of scope are commendable. While the validation is incomplete and the computational complexity is a concern, these weaknesses are outweighed by the originality and potential significance of the work. The authors have identified an important research direction, and this paper represents a significant step forward. However, I strongly encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above in a revised version. Specifically, providing more detail about algorithm implementation and conducting more extensive validation is crucial.



**Rating:** 7/10 (Accept with minor revisions)
