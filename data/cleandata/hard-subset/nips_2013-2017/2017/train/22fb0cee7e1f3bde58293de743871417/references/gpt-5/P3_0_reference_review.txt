Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that minimize a weighted sum of screening and detection-delay costs. The disease process is modeled as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; patients generate external information (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger early arrivals; test outcomes are noisy; and the detection-delay cost depends on the incidence and detection times (trajectory-dependent). The authors formulate a belief-state dynamic program with a Bellman equation where the action is a recommendation plan mapping observed test outcomes to the next follow-up time, and arrivals are endogenous due to external information surpassing a threshold. Exact solution via alpha-vectors is intractable; they propose a point-based value iteration (PBVI) variant that samples likely disease trajectories and constructs reachable beliefs via random exploration and clinical heuristics. They prove the value function is piecewise-linear convex in belief, provide an approximation-error bound in terms of a belief-sampling density metric, and state a robustness result asserting local constancy of optimal actions to small model-estimation errors. On a large breast cancer dataset, they estimate a two-state Markov progression with external information modeled via a tumor growth law, and report 28–68% reductions in expected screenings compared to annual/biennial policies for matched expected delay.

The work relates to POMDP/POSMDP planning (Smallwood & Sondik; Pineau et al.; Krishnamurthy), OR screening models for breast/colon (Ayer et al.; Maillart et al.; Erenay et al.), risk-based survival/joint models (Rizopoulos), and medical simulation-based guideline work (CISNET, MISCAN). Compared to POMDP screening models, DPSCREEN departs by (i) trajectory-dependent costs, (ii) action-dependent and exogenous early arrivals via external information, and (iii) a generic disease process (at least in the formulation).

Strengths
- Problem importance: dynamic, personalized screening is high-impact; framing within belief-state control is appropriate.
- Modeling generality: accommodates trajectory-dependent delay cost and exogenous information-triggered arrivals, which are hard to encode in standard POMDPs.
- Algorithmic contribution: a PBVI-style approach tailored to high-dimensional belief over trajectories by sampling likely state-trajectories and reachable beliefs. This is a reasonable and pragmatic extension of point-based methods.
- Theoretical properties: piecewise-linear convexity of value function, an approximation-error upper bound linked to belief sampling density, and a robustness result on local constancy of optimal actions under small estimation errors.
- Empirical demonstration on a sizable breast cancer cohort with intuitive personalization behavior and large reductions in screening burden at matched expected delay.

Weaknesses and questions
- Generality vs. validation gap: While the formulation allows non-Markov disease processes and complex observation models, the empirical study uses a simple two-state Markov model. There is no demonstration that DPSCREEN scales or performs well when the disease process is non-Markovian or when observation models are non-stationary, which is central to the paper’s motivation.
- Approximation bound is weak/opaque: Ω(B̄) depends on a constant ζ defined as a “maximum expected one-step loss,” which is not specified or computable from data; W (max screening interval) and “T sufficiently large” are assumed. The bound gives limited actionable guidance for choosing K or B, and there is no empirical study of approximation error vs. sampling density.
- Robustness theorem (local constancy) is stated qualitatively: the set E is “closed lower-dimensional,” but measure-theoretic regularity, required continuity assumptions, and the probability measure on L are not explicit. The result mostly says “no ties implies stability,” which is standard, but without a proof sketch or conditions it adds little practical guidance for estimation error.
- Cost modeling/execution: The main objective uses discounting for screening costs (δ), and delay cost is allowed to depend on tD with discounting in Proposition 1, yet the experiments optimize “number of screens” with a linear, undiscounted delay cost. The reduction to a constrained problem via weight tuning presumes convexity of the Pareto frontier; no guarantees are provided, and no sensitivity curves (E[N] vs E[Δ]) with confidence intervals are shown.
- Test accuracy assumptions inconsistent across sections: The general model assumes no false positives at the “diagnosis” stage (Z+), which may be defensible if biopsy is treated as perfect. But Proposition 1 assumes no false negatives or positives, which is unrealistic; yet the empirical section uses literature sensitivity/specificity for mammography and biopsy. It is unclear which assumptions are used in the theoretical results vs. experiments.
- External information model is simplistic: A one-parameter “universal” tumor growth and a fixed monthly self-exam with a threshold-triggered arrival are strong assumptions, likely to drive the large reported savings. There is no sensitivity analysis to growth parameters, self-exam frequency/quality, or to removing early-arrival triggers.
- Evaluation lacks uncertainty quantification and external validation: Policies are evaluated on a learned model from observational data; no posterior predictive intervals, calibration checks, or robustness to model misspecification are reported. Given the heavy dependence on the model, this is a significant limitation.
- Computational aspects: The stated complexity O(T(B)^2 T^2 K|Y||Z|) is difficult to interpret and seems high. No wall-clock runtimes, scalability studies, or ablations (e.g., effect of K, B, action-space restrictions) are shown.
- Comparators: Empirical comparisons are to annual/biennial schedules. Missing are stronger learning-based baselines (e.g., hazard-threshold policies, Ayer/Maillart POMDP policies adapted to the same data, information-gain heuristics [10]).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and cites relevant OR/medical literature. However:
- Key elements (algorithms, proofs) are deferred to the supplement; at least high-level pseudocode and a brief proof sketch of Lemma 1’s extension would aid readability.
- Notational load is heavy; clarifying the relation between recommendation plans τ, actions, and arrivals earlier would help.
- Some assumptions and discrepancies (test accuracy, discounting) should be consolidated and clarified.

Originality
Formulating screening as a belief-MDP with trajectory-dependent costs and endogenous arrivals, and adapting PBVI with trajectory-belief sampling, is a reasonably novel combination for this application. The theoretical results extend known POMDP properties but are not deeply new. Relative to prior POMDP screening works, the allowance for external-information-triggered arrivals and trajectory-dependent costs is the main conceptual novelty.

Significance
If validated more thoroughly, DPSCREEN could influence how personalized screening schedules are designed. As presented, the empirical gains are promising but rely on strong modeling assumptions and lack uncertainty analysis. For NeurIPS, the methodological novelty and theoretical depth are moderate.

Pros for acceptance
- Important problem with societal impact.
- Sound formulation and practical PBVI adaptation.
- Clear qualitative personalization and large empirical gains.
- Bridges RL/POMDP methods with clinically relevant constraints (external info, delay costs).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical validation of the “general” setting; simple Markov disease model only.
- Weak/qualitative theory; approximation and robustness results provide little actionable guidance.
- Evaluation lacks baselines, sensitivity analyses, and uncertainty quantification.
- Scalability and runtime not demonstrated.

Suggestions
- Add sensitivity analyses (self-exam frequency/quality, tumor-growth parameters, test accuracy) and posterior uncertainty propagation to outcome metrics.
- Compare against stronger baselines, including adapted POMDP policies and hazard-threshold heuristics.
- Report runtime and scaling with K, B, and action-space constraints; ablate trajectory sampling choices.
- Clarify assumptions around false positives/negatives across theory and experiments; align cost models/discounting.
- Provide at least a proof sketch for Lemma 1 and more precise conditions for Theorem 1; make ζ computable or provide empirical approximation/error curves.

Overall
A thoughtful and potentially impactful application of belief-state planning to personalized screening, with reasonable algorithmic ideas but limited theoretical depth and empirical validation. I lean toward borderline reject for NeurIPS in its current form, but would be positive with stronger analysis and validation.
