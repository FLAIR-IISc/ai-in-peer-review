- Proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening that balances screening frequency against disease-detection delay with a general finite-state disease model and external information triggers.  
- Formal model supports non-Markov trajectory dependence, external arrivals (e.g., self-exams), multi-outcome tests, and disease-specific delay costs, minimizing a weighted sum of discounted screening and delay costs.  
- Derives a belief-state Bellman equation and shows finite-horizon value is convex and piecewise-linear in beliefs (POMDP-like).  
- Exact DP via alpha-vectors is intractable; authors propose a PBVI-style approximation using trajectory-driven belief sampling and reachable-belief construction.  
- Provide a qualitative approximation bound based on belief-sample density and a robustness result: optimal action map is locally constant away from a lower-dimensional exception set under continuity/uniqueness assumptions.  
- Experiments use a de-identified cohort (45k women, 60–65) with a two-state Markov cancer model, mammography test characteristics from literature, and a noisy self-exam growth model.  
- DPSCREEN reduces expected screenings substantially (28–45% without self-exams, 57–68% with self-exams) at similar expected delay and personalizes intervals by baseline risk and history.  
- Synthetic experiments with quadratic delay costs show more aggressive testing, illustrating importance of disease-specific cost modeling.  
- Advances prior work by handling external-arrival-triggered epochs and trajectory-dependent delay costs within a belief DP, extending PBVI sampling ideas to a larger belief space.  
- Strength: unified, ambitious formulation bridging OR, medical decision-making, and POMDP theory.  
- Strength: clear theoretical connection to POMDPs (piecewise-linear convex value) adapted to this setting.  
- Strength: practical PBVI-style approximation with problem-driven belief sampling and discussed computational/parallelization aspects.  
- Strength: appealing robustness intuition (local constancy) relevant for model-based policy evaluation.  
- Strength: empirical illustration on a large real dataset showing substantial screening reductions and personalization.  
- Strength: highlights disease-dependent delay costs, often overlooked in ML-for-health.  
- Weakness: approximation bound is qualitative, depends on strong assumptions (perfect tests, bounded intervals, large horizon), and constants/metrics are not computable in practice.  
- Weakness: Theorem 1 (local constancy) lacks a proof sketch and relies on nontrivial assumptions (uniqueness, continuity) that are hard to verify.  
- Weakness: Tension between “no false positives” assumption used for theory and empirical sensitivity/specificity in experiments; needs reconciliation and clarification of which assumptions apply to bounds.  
- Weakness: Entirely simulator-based evaluation using an estimated simulator—requires uncertainty quantification (bootstrap CIs), sensitivity analyses (self-exam and noise parameters), and ablation studies (belief-sampling size K and B effects).  
- Weakness: Objective primarily matches unconditional expected delay dominated by non-cases; distributional properties, tails, and time-to-stage detection are not analyzed but could impact clinical utility.  
- Clarity issues: dense presentation relying on appendices; key quantities (δ, T, W, typical K and B, runtime/memory) and notation inconsistencies should be moved/clarified in main text.  
- Clarification requested on whether POSMDP/state-augmentation reductions are possible and what specifically breaks in POSMDP reductions for their setup.  
- Originality: modeling composition (external arrivals + trajectory-dependent costs) is novel in belief-DP; algorithmic contribution is a tailored PBVI variant and could be characterized as incremental.  
- Significance: potential for impactful screening reductions, but empirical claims are weakened by simulator-based evaluation and use of a two-state Markov disease model.  
- Requested experiments/questions: report typical values of T, δ, K, |B̄[t]|, runtimes and scaling; sensitivity to self-exam model and cadence; bootstrap CIs and posterior uncertainty for key metrics; comparisons to prior POMDP mammography policies; and relaxing the perfect-test assumption in the bound.  
- Pros: principled framework, practical algorithm with promising large-dataset results, inclusion of external info and disease-specific costs, clear clinical motivation.  
- Cons: theory qualitative with strong assumptions, simulator-only evaluation with limited uncertainty quantification, modest algorithmic novelty vs PBVI, clarity and assumption inconsistencies.  
- Recommendation: borderline reject — promising and important problem but weak actionable theory and insufficient empirical rigor; would benefit from stronger experiments, clearer algorithmic novelty, and tightened presentation.
