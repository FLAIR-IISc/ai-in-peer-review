- Proposes a personalized screening framework that balances screening costs against detection delays and supports non-Markovian disease progression and external information.
- Shows the value function is convex and piecewise linear and provides theoretical error and robustness bounds (Lemma 1, Proposition 1, Theorem 1).
- Introduces DPSCREEN, a computationally efficient approximation using point-based value iteration with a belief sampling strategy.
- Empirical validation on a real breast cancer dataset (~45,000 patients) shows 28–68% reduction in screenings while maintaining equivalent detection delays.
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides rare error bounds for approximation and estimation.
- Evaluation is comprehensive with appropriate baselines, risk stratification, and ablation studies (including self-examinations).
- The framework is novel in handling non-Markovian disease models and incorporating external triggers like self-exams.
- DPSCREEN addresses high-dimensional belief space via a targeted belief sampling approach, making computation feasible.
- The approach has clear practical impact potential for reducing healthcare costs and patient burden and is broadly applicable beyond breast cancer.
- The paper extends POMDP theory to non-Markovian processes and general delay costs, a notable theoretical contribution.
- Paper is well-structured with clear notation and effective illustrative figures.
- Simplifies disease progression to two states (Healthy/Disease), which may miss important intermediate stages.
- Assumes perfect test accuracy (no false positives/negatives), limiting clinical realism and applicability.
- Dataset limited to ages 60–65, raising concerns about generalizability to other age groups.
- Could better differentiate technical/modeling/computational differences from closely related works (e.g., Ayer et al. 2010, Rizopoulos et al. 2012).
- Does not compare DPSCREEN to other approximate POMDP solvers (e.g., Monte Carlo planning) that might scale differently.
- Lacks discussion of practical implementation challenges in clinical settings and integration with health systems.
- Omits discussion of ethical implications such as access disparities and potential harms of personalized screening.
- Algorithm 1’s belief sampling procedure is under-specified (how trajectories are sampled and tied to the disease model).
- Computational complexity is stated but practical scaling and deployment implications are not fully discussed.
- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, strong theory, compelling empirical results, useful error bounds, and relevant to ML + healthcare.
- Arguments against acceptance: modeling simplifications, insufficient differentiation from prior POMDP work, missing implementation/ethical discussion, and limited dataset scope.
- Overall assessment: strong, theoretically sound contribution with compelling results; recommended for acceptance with minor revisions.
- Recommended revisions: clarify extension to multi-state disease models; address false positive/negative assumptions and required modifications; explicitly contrast technical differences with Ayer et al. and Rizopoulos et al.; add implementation and ethical discussion; provide concrete details on the belief sampling procedure in Algorithm 1.
