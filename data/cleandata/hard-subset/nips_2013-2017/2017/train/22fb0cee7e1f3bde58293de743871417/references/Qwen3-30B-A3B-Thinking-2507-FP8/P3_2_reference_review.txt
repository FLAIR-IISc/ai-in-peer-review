

# Peer Review: DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for personalized disease screening policies that balances screening costs against delay costs in disease detection. The authors develop a general mathematical model for disease progression (as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state), incorporate external information (such as patient self-examinations), and formulate the optimal screening policy as a dynamic decision problem. The key contribution is a computationally tractable method (DPSCREEN) that uses point-based value iteration on a belief space representation to approximate the optimal policy, with theoretical guarantees on approximation error. The authors demonstrate their approach on breast cancer screening data, showing significant reductions (28-68%) in screening frequency compared to current clinical practices while maintaining similar expected detection delays.

## Strengths

1. **Problem Relevance and Importance**: The paper addresses a critical healthcare problem with significant societal impact. Personalized screening is increasingly important as healthcare shifts toward precision medicine, and the paper correctly identifies the limitations of current clinical guidelines that treat all patients uniformly.

2. **Comprehensive Modeling**: The authors provide a well-structured model that generalizes beyond the restrictive assumptions of prior work (Markov disease processes, stationary test outcomes). The inclusion of external information (e.g., patient self-examinations) is particularly valuable for realistic implementation.

3. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper establishes a solid theoretical foundation with the Bellman equation formulation and proves key properties of the value function (convexity, piecewise linearity). The approximation error bound (Proposition 1) is a valuable contribution that provides justification for the computational approach.

4. **Promising Experimental Results**: The breast cancer case study demonstrates significant improvements over current clinical policies (28-68% reduction in screenings), which is compelling and relevant to healthcare practitioners.

5. **Clear Distinction from Related Work**: The paper effectively distinguishes its approach from prior work in operations research (e.g., [7], [9]) and medical literature (e.g., MISCAN-COLON model [46]), highlighting the limitations of existing frameworks.

## Weaknesses and Criticisms

1. **Overstated Computational Complexity Claims**: The paper claims the complexity is O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|), but this appears to be an oversimplification. The key issue is the belief space dimension, which is |ST × {0, 1}| = |S|^T × 2. For realistic disease models (even with |S|=2), this becomes 2^T, which is exponentially large in T. The paper's approach to "lower dimensional belief space" by sampling trajectories (Algorithm 1) is a good heuristic, but the paper fails to acknowledge that this may not scale to clinically relevant time horizons (e.g., T=10 years for cancer screening).

2. **Questionable Assumptions in Approximation Error Bound**: Proposition 1 relies on strong assumptions that may not hold in practice:
   - The delay cost is assumed to be linear (C(td - tD; tD) = c(td - tD)δtD), but the paper acknowledges in Section 4 that quadratic costs are more appropriate for some diseases (e.g., pancreatic cancer). 
   - The assumption of "no false positives and no false negatives" is highly unrealistic for medical screening (e.g., mammograms have known false positive rates).
   - The proof of Proposition 1 appears to rely on a specific cost function form that doesn't generalize to the more complex delay costs the paper acknowledges in Section 4.

3. **Insufficient Model Estimation Details**: The paper describes estimating the model parameters using the Gail model and MCMC, but lacks critical details:
   - How was the logistic function for pin(x) and ptran(x) chosen? What features were included in x?
   - What validation procedure was used to ensure the model was well-calibrated?
   - The paper claims "standard Markov Chain Monte Carlo methods" but provides no specifics about the implementation or convergence diagnostics.
   - Without these details, it's difficult to assess whether the model truly reflects the underlying disease process.

4. **Inadequate Baseline Comparison**: The paper compares with annual and biennial screening policies, but doesn't compare with more sophisticated personalized screening methods that might be in the literature. For example, [10] and [1] propose personalized screening models that the paper claims to generalize, but doesn't directly compare with these in the experimental section.

5. **Lack of Uncertainty Quantification**: The paper provides point estimates for the screening policies but doesn't quantify uncertainty in the predictions. In a healthcare setting, this is critical for clinical decision-making. The authors should consider providing confidence intervals or Bayesian uncertainty estimates for the recommended screening intervals.

6. **Limited Discussion of Practical Implementation**: The paper doesn't address key implementation challenges:
   - How would clinicians interact with this system? What would the user interface look like?
   - What are the ethical considerations of personalized screening (e.g., could this exacerbate health disparities for certain populations)?
   - How would the system handle missing data or incomplete patient histories?

7. **Overstated Impact Claims**: The claimed 28-68% reduction in screenings seems high without context. The paper doesn't clarify whether this reduction applies to the entire population or just to specific risk groups, and doesn't discuss potential trade-offs (e.g., could this lead to more late-stage diagnoses for some patients?).

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Reframe Computational Complexity**: The paper should be more transparent about the computational limitations. Consider adding a table showing estimated computation times for realistic parameter settings (e.g., T=10 years, |S|=3, K=1000 sampled trajectories).

2. **Strengthen Approximation Error Analysis**: Relax the assumptions in Proposition 1 to be more realistic. For example, consider how the error bound would change with non-zero false positive rates or with quadratic delay costs.

3. **Add More Model Estimation Details**: Include a subsection on model estimation with specific details about:
   - Feature selection for x (e.g., which variables from the Gail model were used)
   - Hyperparameter tuning procedures
   - Validation metrics (e.g., cross-validation AUC for disease prediction)

4. **Expand Baseline Comparison**: Add a comparison with at least one prior personalized screening method (e.g., [10]) to better demonstrate the incremental contribution.

5. **Include Uncertainty Estimates**: Add a figure showing confidence intervals for the recommended screening intervals, especially for high-risk patients.

6. **Address Implementation Challenges**: Add a short discussion section on practical implementation considerations, including ethical implications and potential barriers to adoption in clinical settings.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper that addresses an important problem with a theoretically sound approach. The core methodology is novel and the experimental results are promising. However, the paper has significant weaknesses in its computational complexity claims, approximation error analysis, and model estimation details that undermine the practicality and credibility of the proposed approach.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of personalized healthcare decision-making, but it needs to be more transparent about its limitations and provide more concrete details about how the approach would work in practice. With the suggested improvements, this could be a strong contribution to NIPS.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following conditions:

1. Address the computational complexity claims more honestly, acknowledging the exponential scaling of the belief space.
2. Strengthen the approximation error analysis to account for more realistic assumptions (false positives, quadratic costs).
3. Add more detail about the model estimation process, including validation procedures.
4. Expand the baseline comparison to include at least one prior personalized screening method.

Without these improvements, the paper's claims about the practical utility of the approach may be overstated. The paper would be significantly stronger with these revisions, and would make a meaningful contribution to the field of healthcare AI.

## Final Note

The paper has the potential to make a real impact in healthcare, but it needs to be more grounded in the practical realities of clinical implementation. The authors should be encouraged to address these concerns in a revised version, as this is a promising line of research that could benefit many patients if implemented correctly.
