Summary
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies. The state of disease is modeled as a hidden finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; external information (e.g., self-exam) can trigger unscheduled visits. The objective trades off discounted screening cost and delay cost, where delay depends on the detection lag and incidence time. The authors formulate a belief-state dynamic program and claim a piecewise-linear convex value function (as in POMDPs). Because the belief space is over full trajectories (very high dimensional), they propose a point-based approximation: sample likely state-trajectories, construct reachable beliefs under randomized policy exploration (and clinical heuristics), and apply PBVI-style backups to compute an approximate policy. They provide a qualitative robustness statement to model-estimation error and a bound of the approximation error in terms of the density of sampled beliefs. Experiments on a de-identified mammography dataset (n=45k, age 60–65) fit a two-state Markov disease model parameterized by Gail risk and compare DPSCREEN to annual/biennial screening, reporting 28–68% reductions in expected number of screens with matched expected detection delays. They also illustrate the effect of convex (quadratic) delay cost.

Relation to prior work
This sits at the intersection of POMDPs/POSMDPs, controlled sensing, and personalized screening. The piecewise-linear value-function property and PBVI approach are classical (e.g., Sondik; Pineau et al.). OR/medical literature has POMDP-based screening (Ayer et al.; Maillart et al.; Erenay et al.), dynamic survival models, and simulation-based policy evaluation (e.g., MISCAN-COLON). ConfidentCare and risk-based intervals also exist. The novelty claimed is handling external information that endogenizes arrival, belief over trajectories, and trajectory-dependent delay costs; however, many of these can be accommodated in augmented-state POMDPs. The paper’s theoretical and algorithmic pieces lean heavily on known POMDP ideas with adaptations.

Strengths
- Important, high-impact application; personalization over risk features and dynamic history is valuable.
- Unifies screening with external information and trajectory-dependent delay within a DP framework.
- Attempts to provide error control (approximation error bound; local constancy under small estimation error).
- Empirical results on a sizable real dataset; clear demonstration of policy personalization and disease-dependent costs.
- Practical focus on tractable approximation and parallelizability.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness/clarity:
- Bellman equation sign and optimization direction seem inconsistent with a cost-minimization objective: Eq. (3) maximizes while subtracting costs, but V is not explicitly defined as negative cost; the convention is not stated.
- C̃ definition (Eq. 2) is unclear and internally inconsistent. The “t=T, l=0” line imposes delay cost regardless of whether disease occurred; cases do not condition cleanly on disease incidence and detection status. No explicit discounting for future value is shown.
- The distribution Pr(z, y, τ̃ | b, τ, x) is central but not specified/derived; modeling τ̃ requires first-passage timing induced by the external process and policy threshold. The independence assumptions are terse; dependence on history via trajectory is not operationalized.
- Lemma 1 (piecewise linearity) extends classic results, but relies on an atypical action space (recommend next time) and trajectory-based costs; the mapping to a finite mixture of linear functions is asserted, not carefully shown in the main text.
- Proposition 1: the approximation bound is effectively vacuous. Ω(B̄) uses ζ, an unspecified “max expected loss per time slot,” and the expression maxt maxB minb∈B̄[t] ||b − b′||1 has undefined symbols (B and b′). No sample complexity relation (K, B̄ size) is provided.
- Theorem 1 (local constancy of τ*) is a generic “measure-zero” argument typical for argmax mappings; conditions for uniqueness, regularity, and implications for approximate policies are not made precise.
- Computational complexity O(T (B)2 T2 K |Y||Z|) is opaque (is it O(B^2 T^3 K |Y||Z|)?). Memory complexity is not discussed. The curse of dimensionality is not quantified despite a trajectory-based belief space.
- Assumptions conflict/oversimplify: early sections assume no false positives when z ∈ Z+ leads to diagnosis; experiments use a two-stage test where z=2 is mammogram positive but biopsy negative, and z=3 is definitive. If Z+={3}, biopsy is assumed perfect—biopsy sensitivity/specificity are not modeled or cited.

Empirics:
- Model fit and validation are under-specified: priors, convergence diagnostics for MCMC, calibration (e.g., Brier score, calibration plots), and held-out validation are missing.
- Gail-risk cut at 5% 5-year risk for 60–65 is unusual; justify and report distributional stats; typical clinical thresholds differ.
- External information model (self-exam) is very ad hoc: monthly exam, tumor growth g(t) with ι=0.9, σ and threshold ỹ taken from literature without unit calibration to this dataset; sensitivity analyses are absent.
- The objective is tuned to match the expected delay of annual/biennial screening by picking w post hoc; this is essentially goal-seeking and may obscure trade-offs. No confidence intervals, no bootstrapping, no statistical significance.
- Reporting focuses on E[Δ|R], which is dominated by non-diseased patients; E[Δ|R,D] is reported but without uncertainty. Clinical relevance (stage shift, mortality) is not assessed.
- Baselines limited to annual/biennial; no comparison to recent risk-based policies (e.g., ConfidentCare) or OR-POMDP policies under matched assumptions.
- Figures/tables referenced (Fig. 1–2, Tables 1–2) are missing here; reproducibility is limited (no code; dataset not public).

Presentation and formatting:
- Numerous typographical/formatting issues: stray “st Conference on NIPS 2017” in the intro; dangling footnote markers (“1”, “2”) and mismatched footnote text; inconsistent notation (pin vs pin, ptr vs ptran); undefined symbols (b′, B); inconsistent citation formatting (missing years/venues); ambiguous δ used for both screening discount and delay discount in Proposition 1; inconsistent capitalization and punctuation. These hinder readability and trust.

Originality and significance
- While the problem is significant, the core technical components largely adapt known POMDP concepts (piecewise linearity; PBVI) with a tailored belief-construction heuristic. The contribution would be stronger with a precise, novel theoretical treatment of the endogenous-arrival DP and a meaningful approximation guarantee. The empirical claims are potentially important but currently insufficiently validated.

Pros
- Timely and important application; principled DP framing.
- Incorporates external information and dynamic personalization.
- Attempts at theory and large-scale empirical illustration.

Cons
- Multiple technical ambiguities/errors (Bellman, costs, bounds).
- Approximation guarantee and robustness result are weak/informal.
- Empirical evaluation lacks calibration, baselines, uncertainty quantification, and sensitivity analyses.
- Many presentation/notation/reference issues.

Recommendation: Reject
Despite the interesting problem and promising direction, the paper in its current form has substantial technical and experimental shortcomings and pervasive clarity/formatting issues. I encourage the authors to (i) rigorously formalize the DP with correct signs and costs, (ii) provide a meaningful approximation/error analysis with defined constants and sample complexity, (iii) thoroughly validate the learned models and perform robust sensitivity analyses, (iv) compare against stronger baselines, and (v) fix the numerous presentation issues. Confidence: high.
