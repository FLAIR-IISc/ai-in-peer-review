

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

## Summary
This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a novel framework for dynamic personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against delay costs in disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a general finite-state stochastic process (not restricted to Markov processes), incorporate external information (such as patient self-examinations), and develop a computationally feasible approach to compute approximately optimal policies using point-based value iteration. The method is evaluated on a breast cancer dataset, demonstrating 28-68% reductions in screening frequency compared to current clinical policies while maintaining equivalent expected delay in disease detection.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper makes a technically sound contribution with rigorous theoretical foundations. The authors establish key properties of the value function (Lemma 1) and provide a meaningful approximation error bound (Proposition 1). The experimental evaluation is thorough, using a large real-world dataset with appropriate metrics. The comparison with clinical policies is well-executed and demonstrates clear practical improvements.

**Originality:** The work significantly advances beyond existing screening frameworks by handling non-Markov disease processes, incorporating external information, and providing a more general solution framework. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from prior work (Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Rizopoulos et al.) and demonstrate the limitations of those approaches through their theoretical analysis.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly significant in healthcare, with potential to reduce costs and patient burden. The demonstrated improvements (28-68% fewer screenings) represent a meaningful advance over current clinical practice. The paper also highlights the importance of disease-specific cost structures (linear vs. quadratic delay costs), which is a valuable insight for practitioners.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to solution to evaluation. The graphical representation in Figure 1 effectively illustrates the personalization aspect. The supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

**Computational Scalability:** While the paper provides a complexity analysis (O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)), it lacks concrete evidence of scalability to real-world problems with large time horizons and state spaces. The claim that parallelization "significantly reduces computation time" needs empirical support.

**Model Assumptions:** The assumption of no false positives in tests (e.g., mammograms) is unrealistic for clinical practice. The paper should address how the framework would handle false positives, which is a critical consideration in screening contexts.

**Model Estimation:** The use of logistic functions for pin(x) and ptran(x) may be too simplistic for complex real-world data. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of model estimation challenges and potential improvements (e.g., using more flexible machine learning approaches).

**Limited Disease Generalization:** While the paper mentions applicability to other diseases, the evaluation is limited to breast cancer. A brief exploration of how the approach would perform for diseases with more rapid progression (e.g., pancreatic cancer) would strengthen the significance argument.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Novel theoretical framework that extends beyond Markov assumptions to handle general disease processes
- Demonstrated significant practical improvements over current clinical policies (28-68% fewer screenings)
- Rigorous approximation error analysis and theoretical guarantees
- Well-executed experimental validation on a large real-world dataset

**Con:**
- Limited discussion of how to handle false positives in screening tests
- Model estimation approach may be too simplistic for complex real-world data
- Insufficient evidence of scalability to larger problems
- Evaluation limited to breast cancer without broader disease comparison

## Recommendations

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of personalized healthcare decision-making. To strengthen the work for publication, I recommend:

1. Address the false positive issue in the model by extending the framework to handle imperfect tests or including a sensitivity analysis.

2. Provide more detailed discussion of the model estimation process, including potential alternatives to the logistic function assumption.

3. Include a brief analysis of how the framework would perform for diseases with different progression characteristics (e.g., pancreatic cancer with rapid progression).

4. Add a scalability analysis showing runtime performance on progressively larger problem instances.

5. Clarify the relationship between the belief space construction (Algorithm 1) and standard point-based value iteration methods.

The paper represents a strong contribution that addresses an important healthcare problem with a theoretically sound approach. With the recommended revisions addressing the scalability and modeling assumptions, this work would make an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
