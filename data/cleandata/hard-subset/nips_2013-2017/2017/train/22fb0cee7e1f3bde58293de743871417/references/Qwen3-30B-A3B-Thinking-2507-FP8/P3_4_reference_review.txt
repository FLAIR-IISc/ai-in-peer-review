

# Peer Review: "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

This paper proposes a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against delay in disease detection. The authors address significant limitations in prior work by developing a general model that doesn't restrict disease progression to Markov or Semi-Markov processes and incorporates external information (e.g., patient self-examinations) that can trigger early visits. They develop an approximately optimal policy using a modified Point-Based Value Iteration approach and provide theoretical bounds on approximation error.

## Strengths

The paper makes a strong technical contribution by generalizing the disease modeling framework beyond restrictive assumptions in prior work. The authors' Lemma 1 (showing the value function is convex and piecewise linear) is a valuable theoretical insight that extends the POMDP literature. Their Proposition 1 providing an approximation error bound is particularly significant for justifying their practical approach.

The empirical validation on breast cancer data is compelling, demonstrating 28-68% reduction in screenings while maintaining expected detection delays compared to current clinical practices. The analysis of how different delay cost functions (linear vs quadratic) lead to different screening policies is insightful and highlights an important consideration for different diseases.

The paper's framework is truly general, encompassing many disease models in the literature (survival models, first hitting time models) without restrictive assumptions about stationarity or Markovian structure. This generality is a major advance over existing works like [7,9] that assume stationary Markov test outcome models.

## Weaknesses

The paper fails to sufficiently distinguish its approach from existing POMDP-based screening methods [7,9]. While the authors claim these prior works assume stationary Markov test outcome models, they don't clearly explain why their model is more general or how their approach differs in practice. A more detailed comparison with these specific methods would strengthen the contribution claim.

The model assumptions (e.g., no false positives/negatives) are overly simplistic for real-world clinical settings. The paper doesn't discuss how their approach would handle the common scenario of false positives/negatives in mammography, which would significantly impact the practical implementation. In reality, mammograms have false positive rates of 7-12%, and this would substantially affect the policy.

The computational complexity analysis is vague. The authors state the complexity as O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|), but they don't provide guidance on how this would scale with realistic problem sizes (e.g., T=100, K=1000). For a practical healthcare application, the algorithm would need to handle much larger time horizons than what might be feasible with this complexity.

The empirical section would be stronger with more nuanced comparisons. The paper compares only against annual screening, but clinical guidelines often recommend different screening intervals for different risk groups. A comparison against these more nuanced guidelines would better demonstrate the value of their personalized approach.

## Significance

The problem addressed has clear real-world impact in healthcare resource allocation. The 28-68% reduction in screenings while maintaining detection quality could lead to significant cost savings and reduced patient burden. The framework's generality means it could be applied to various diseases beyond breast cancer.

However, the paper doesn't sufficiently discuss practical barriers to implementation, such as the need for accurate disease progression models and the computational requirements of the proposed algorithm. These would be important considerations for healthcare practitioners.

## Comparison to Related Work

While Section 5 mentions related work, the comparison with [7,9] is superficial. The authors claim these works assume stationary Markov test outcome models, but don't demonstrate how their model differs in practice. A more detailed comparison would help establish the paper's novelty.

The paper's approach to handling high-dimensional belief spaces (Algorithm 1) is thoughtful, but it doesn't sufficiently address how their method compares to more recent advances in POMDP approximation, such as those in [40] or [41] that also focus on belief space sampling.

## Recommendation

**Accept.** This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field that is both theoretically rigorous and practically relevant. The empirical results are compelling, and the framework has clear potential for impact in personalized healthcare.

The main weaknesses (insufficient differentiation from existing POMDP methods, simplistic assumptions, vague scalability analysis) are important but do not undermine the paper's core contribution. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field of personalized healthcare decision-making.

**Key Improvements Needed:**
1. More detailed comparison with existing POMDP-based screening methods [7,9]
2. Discussion of how the approach would handle false positives/negatives in clinical practice
3. More nuanced empirical comparison with current clinical guidelines for different risk groups
4. Better explanation of computational scalability for realistic healthcare applications

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem with clear real-world impact. It advances both the theoretical understanding of decision-making under uncertainty and provides a practical framework for healthcare applications. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
