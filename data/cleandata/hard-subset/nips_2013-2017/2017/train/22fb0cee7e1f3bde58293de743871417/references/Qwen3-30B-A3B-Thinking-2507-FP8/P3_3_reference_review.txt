

# Peer Review: DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

This paper proposes a novel framework for dynamic personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against delay in disease detection. The authors address a significant gap in the literature by developing a general approach that accommodates non-Markovian disease progression models and incorporates external information (e.g., self-examinations), moving beyond the restrictive Markov assumptions of existing POMDP-based screening approaches.

## Quality and Technical Contribution

The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution by extending POMDP methodology to a more general disease modeling framework. The authors correctly identify limitations in existing works (e.g., [7], [9]) that assume Markov disease processes and stationary test outcome models. Their formulation of the problem as a POMDP with a non-Markovian disease process is technically correct, and the Bellman equation formulation is well-justified.

The key technical contribution is the DPSCREEN algorithm, which applies point-based value iteration to the high-dimensional belief space by sampling likely physiological trajectories. The theoretical bound on approximation error (Proposition 1) is a valuable contribution, though the bound is somewhat weak as it's defined in terms of sampling density without concrete guarantees for practical implementation.

However, the paper has several quality limitations:
1. The assumption of no false positives in test results is unrealistic for medical screening (e.g., mammograms have significant false positive rates).
2. The disease model restricts to a single absorbing disease state, ignoring multiple intermediate states or competing risks that are common in medical contexts.
3. The theoretical bounds don't address how the sampling density Ω(B̄) relates to practical error tolerances or computational requirements.

## Clarity and Presentation

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem formulation is precise, and the mathematical notation is consistent. The connection to POMDPs is well-explained, though the paper would benefit from stronger positioning within the broader context of reinforcement learning and decision-making under uncertainty, which is central to NIPS.

The empirical validation on breast cancer data is promising, showing 28-68% reductions in screenings while maintaining expected detection delays. However, the paper lacks comparison with more recent constrained POMDP approaches [19] that could provide a stronger baseline. The supplemental materials appear to contain substantial technical content, but the main paper doesn't sufficiently integrate these details.

## Originality

The paper's main novelty lies in its general disease model that accommodates non-Markovian progression and external information processes. While the core algorithm (point-based value iteration) is not new, the application to this specific healthcare context with the stated extensions is novel. 

That said, the contribution appears somewhat incremental given the well-established POMDP literature. The paper would have stronger originality if it had demonstrated how their approach fundamentally differs from recent constrained POMDP work [19] or other extensions to POMDPs for healthcare applications [40].

## Significance

The problem of personalized screening is highly significant for healthcare, with potential for substantial cost savings and improved patient outcomes. The empirical results on breast cancer data are promising, suggesting substantial reductions in unnecessary screenings. However, the significance is somewhat limited by:
1. Single-disease validation (breast cancer only)
2. Lack of comparison with alternative personalized screening approaches beyond clinical guidelines
3. Insufficient discussion of ethical considerations (e.g., could personalized screening inadvertently lead to under-screening of certain populations?)

## Strengths

- Clear problem formulation with strong motivation from healthcare practice
- Theoretical bounds on approximation error provide useful guarantees
- Real-world validation on a substantial dataset (45,000 patients)
- Good discussion of the relationship between delay cost function and screening policy (Figure 2)

## Weaknesses

- Unrealistic assumption of no false positives in test results
- Limited validation to a single disease (breast cancer) with minimal comparison to alternative methods
- Theoretical bounds on approximation error are weak and lack practical interpretation
- Does not address practical challenges of implementing personalized screening (e.g., missing data, heterogeneous populations)
- Insufficient discussion of how the method would scale to larger healthcare systems

## Recommendation

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the literature on personalized screening policies, with promising empirical results. However, the novelty is somewhat incremental given existing POMDP work, and the theoretical contribution is limited by weak approximation bounds. The unrealistic assumptions about test accuracy and lack of broader validation limit the paper's significance.

I recommend **weak acceptance** (marginally below the acceptance threshold for NIPS). The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Addressing the false positive assumption with realistic test error rates
2. Expanding validation to multiple disease models
3. Providing stronger theoretical guarantees for the approximation error
4. Comparing against more recent constrained POMDP approaches

The work has potential for impact in healthcare decision-making, but requires more substantial theoretical and empirical validation to meet NIPS's standards for technical novelty and significance. The current contribution represents a useful extension to existing methods but doesn't fundamentally advance the state-of-the-art in the way NIPS typically prioritizes.
