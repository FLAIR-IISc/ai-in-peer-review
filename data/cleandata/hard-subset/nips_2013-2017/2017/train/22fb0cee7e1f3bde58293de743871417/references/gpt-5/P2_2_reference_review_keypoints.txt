- Problem: Learn dynamic, personalized screening policies trading test burden vs detection delay using a general finite-state latent disease model with endogenous visit triggers.
- Model/approach: Casts screening as a partially observed control problem with trajectory-dependent delay costs and endogenous decision epochs.
- Theory: Shows value function is convex and piecewise-linear in trajectory beliefs and derives an exact alpha-vector DP (intractable).
- Algorithm: Proposes DPSCREEN, a PBVI-style approximation that samples likely disease trajectories and reachable beliefs.
- Approximation/robustness: Provides an approximation bound (under strong assumptions) and a local-constancy robustness result for optimal policies.
- Empirical case study: On 45k women with a two-state breast cancer model, reports 28–68% screening reduction at similar expected detection delays vs annual/biennial policies.
- Relation to prior work: Extends POMDP/POSMDP screening work by allowing non-Markov dynamics, endogenous arrivals, and trajectory-dependent delay costs.
- Novelty: Main novelty is integrating endogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent delay into a PBVI-like framework rather than new algorithmic primitives.
- Strength — importance: Addresses a timely, clinically meaningful problem and unifies multiple modeling elements.
- Strength — conceptual: Handles endogenous decision epochs and trajectory-dependent costs within a value-iteration framework.
- Strength — practical: DPSCREEN is intuitive, implementable, and demonstrated on a large dataset with plausible external signals.
- Strength — robustness: Local-constancy result offers some comfort about policy sensitivity to estimation errors.
- Weakness — theory: Theoretical contributions are largely standard for POMDPs and key proofs/claims are deferred or informal.
- Weakness — approximation: The approximation bound is informal, relies on strong assumptions (e.g., no false positives/negatives), and lacks sampling-complexity guarantees or clarity about its reference model.
- Weakness — belief sampling bias: Restricting belief support via trajectory sampling induces bias; the bound addresses sampled beliefs, not necessarily the original model’s optimal policy.
- Weakness — computation: Stated complexity is hard to interpret and lacks wall-clock runtimes or scaling experiments.
- Weakness — empirical rigor: Evaluation is model-based with many chosen parameters, no uncertainty quantification, sensitivity analyses, or CIs; tuning w may induce selection bias.
- Weakness — evaluation alternatives: Off-policy evaluation methods (e.g., IPS/DR) are not explored despite being relevant.
- Weakness — clarity: Core details, notation, and proofs are relegated to the supplement; beliefs over trajectories and some assumptions need clearer exposition.
- Weakness — significance limits: Case study uses a simple two-state Markov model, so claimed generality to non-Markov dynamics is not strongly demonstrated.
- Suggestions: Add pseudocode and implementation details, report runtimes/scaling, quantify uncertainty (CIs, bootstrap, sensitivity), compare stronger baselines, clarify approximation bound and sampling complexity, run ablations on K/B and external info, and discuss safety/fairness constraints.
- Pros for acceptance: Well-motivated problem, principled integration of external information and endogenous scheduling, promising large-dataset results of interest to sequential decision-making community.
- Cons for acceptance: Modest theoretical novelty, high-level/assumption-heavy guarantees, and insufficient empirical validation, uncertainty quantification, and reproducibility details.
- Overall recommendation: Borderline — lean weak reject unless authors strengthen comparisons, uncertainty analyses, computational/theoretical clarity, and main-text algorithmic details.
