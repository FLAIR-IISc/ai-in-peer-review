- DPSCREEN: a dynamic personalized screening framework that optimizes screening frequency vs. detection delay for non‑Markovian disease progression using point‑based value iteration and novel belief construction, with theoretical approximation bounds and breast cancer experiments showing large screening reductions at comparable detection delays.

- Extends prior personalized screening and POMDP work by handling non‑Markovian dynamics and incorporating external information, improving over fixed‑interval and Markovian‑assumption methods.

- Strength: Highly general framework accommodating varied disease models and external data.

- Strength: Solid theoretical foundation with approximation error bounds and robustness analysis.

- Strength: Real-world breast cancer results showing 28–68% reduction in screening frequency while maintaining similar detection delay.

- Strength: Personalizes screening policies based on patient risk profiles and dynamic test results.

- Strength: Explicitly acknowledges and addresses computational intractability of optimal policy calculation.

- Strength: Includes analysis of robustness to estimation error.

- Weakness: Computational complexity analysis is insufficiently detailed; complexity formula lacks intuitive breakdown and typical parameter ranges.

- Weakness: Model assumptions (tumor growth, hazard functions, independence) need further justification and assessment of violation impact.

- Weakness: Comparisons are limited (primarily to annual screening) and lack broader personalized‑screening baselines.

- Weakness: Key algorithmic details (e.g., belief construction) are underexplained in the main text and mostly in the supplement.

- Weakness: Presentation is dense and would benefit from more illustrative examples and visualizations.

- Overall: Strong, well‑motivated, theoretically sound paper with promising empirical results, though scalability and modeling assumptions need further consideration.

- For acceptance: novel approach to an important problem, theoretical guarantees, strong empirical improvements, and broad applicability.

- Against acceptance: concerns about scalability, restrictive model assumptions, and limited comparative evaluation.

- Rating/recommendation: Strong Accept (7/10); accept with revisions to address computational complexity and model assumptions.
