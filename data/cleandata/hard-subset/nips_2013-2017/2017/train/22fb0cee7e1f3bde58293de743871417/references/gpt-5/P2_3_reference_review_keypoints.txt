- Proposes DPSCREEN: a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies trading screening cost vs. delay-in-detection in a general finite-state, trajectory-dependent disease model.  
- Derives Bellman recursion in a belief space over full state trajectories and diagnosis labels, and proves piecewise-linear convexity of the value function (alpha-vector view).  
- Shows alpha-vector characterization of optimal policy but notes intractability for direct computation.  
- Proposes a tractable PBVI-style approximation sampling high-probability trajectories and reachable beliefs, with complexity and parallelization discussion.  
- Provides a high-level approximation error bound tied to belief-sampling density and a robustness/local-constancy result under small model error.  
- Empirical illustration on a large breast cancer dataset claims similar expected delay with 28–68% fewer screens versus annual/biennial schedules.  
- Connects to prior POMDP and dynamic survival/joint model work, arguing novelty in handling trajectory-dependent delay and exogenous arrivals.  
- Strength: ambitious, general formulation explicitly modeling trajectory-dependent delay, endogenous scheduling, and exogenous arrivals.  
- Strength: theoretical framing extends classic POMDP geometry to the trajectory-dependent setting with an explicit alpha-vector view.  
- Strength: practical PBVI adaptation for high-dimensional belief spaces and discussion of approximation guarantees and robustness.  
- Strength: real-data application showing personalized policies and substantial reported screening reductions.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation limited to annual/biennial baselines; lacks stronger competitive baselines like tuned POMDPs or risk-threshold policies.  
- Weakness: learned simulation model lacks sufficient validation/calibration, risking sensitivity of reported gains to model misspecification and parameter choices.  
- Weakness: approximation bound assumptions are strong and partly inconsistent with experiments (e.g., Proposition 1 assumes perfect tests while experiments include sensitivity/specificity).  
- Weakness: Theorem 1 is presented at a high level with uniqueness/continuity conditions but lacks detailed formal conditions or practical proof relevance.  
- Weakness: key design choices (K, B, W, δ, weight tuning) are not ablated; no runtime, memory, or scalability results reported.  
- Clarity: well organized overall but many algorithmic details/proofs deferred to supplement; some notation and assumption choices are unclear (e.g., Z+, external-info threshold).  
- Originality: conceptually novel extension to trajectory-dependent costs and PBVI via trajectory sampling, but several elements adapt known techniques and guarantees are high-level.  
- Significance: approach could be high-impact if validated, but current empirical and computational evidence is insufficient for strong claims.  
- Suggestions: add competitive baselines, runtime/scalability and ablation studies, reconcile and clarify assumptions (false positives/negatives, ζ), and validate learned models (calibration, sensitivity).  
- Suggestions: consider optimizing external-information thresholds, incorporate multiple tests with different harms/costs, and discuss fairness/utility trade-offs across risk groups.  
- Pros: important problem, principled framework, theoretical structure, and promising real-data illustration.  
- Cons: limited validation, restrictive/inconsistent theoretical assumptions, unclear scalability, and some clarity/novelty overstatements.  
- Recommendation: borderline reject pending stronger baselines, sensitivity analyses, runtime results, and tightened theory to align assumptions with experiments.
