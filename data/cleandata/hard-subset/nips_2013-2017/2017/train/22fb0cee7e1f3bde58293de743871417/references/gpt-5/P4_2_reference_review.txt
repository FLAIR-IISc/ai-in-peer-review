Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a general framework for computing dynamic, personalized screening policies that balance screening burden against delay in disease detection. Time is discrete; disease evolves as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; external information (e.g., self-exams, comorbidities) affects unscheduled arrivals; and test results are observed only at visits. The policy maps patient features and full observation histories to the next screening time or to a diagnosis decision. The objective minimizes a weighted sum of discounted screening costs and a trajectory-dependent delay cost that depends on incidence and detection times.

The authors formulate a belief-state dynamic program with a Bellman recursion over beliefs on entire disease trajectories and a diagnosis label, show the value function is piecewise-linear and convex in beliefs (extending POMDP results), and derive an exact but intractable α-vector algorithm. They then propose a tractable approximation: (i) sample K “likely” disease trajectories to induce a lower-dimensional belief space; (ii) construct sets of reachable beliefs via policy rollouts (including random actions and clinical heuristics); (iii) perform point-based value backups (PBVI) on this belief set. They provide a computational complexity discussion and a bound stating that the approximation error is upper-bounded by a term tied to the belief-sampling density under assumptions (convex delay cost, perfect diagnostic accuracy, bounded inter-screening interval).

A robustness result shows that the optimal recommendation mapping is locally constant off a lower-dimensional set in the joint space of models and beliefs, suggesting small parameter-estimation errors should not change optimal actions almost surely. Experiments on a large breast cancer dataset (Athena) estimate a two-state Markov disease model with patient-specific risk via the Gail model; self-exams are modeled with a parametric tumor growth model plus noise. Using literature-based sensitivity/specificity for mammography and biopsy, DPSCREEN yields 28–68% reductions in screenings versus annual/biennial schedules at matched expected delay, with more frequent testing for higher-risk patients and after adverse test results. A synthetic exercise contrasts linear versus quadratic delay costs, showing more aggressive schedules under convex costs.

Relative to prior work: OR/POMDP-based screening models (e.g., Ayer et al. for breast cancer, Maillart et al., Erenay et al.) typically assume Markov disease and stationary observation processes and fixed decision epochs; Bayesian design approaches (Rizopoulos et al.) optimize information rather than delay costs; simulation-based guideline development (e.g., MISCAN-COLON) compares fixed schedules. DPSCREEN aims to accommodate non-Markov disease models, trajectory-dependent costs, and endogenous arrivals, and uses PBVI-like ideas [18,19].

Strengths
- Generality of formulation: allows external information triggering early visits, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and non-Markov disease processes within a finite-state trajectory framework.
- Clear separation of exact α-vector formulation and practical PBVI-style approximation; identifies the core intractability and proposes a scalable heuristic.
- Theoretical properties: piecewise-linear structure of value function; a sampling-density-based approximation bound (albeit under strong assumptions); a robustness result on local constancy of optimal recommendations with respect to model perturbations.
- Personalized behavior demonstrated qualitatively (risk- and history-adaptive intervals) and quantitative reductions in screening volume at matched expected delay on a large dataset.
- Discussion of how disease-specific delay costs shape optimal policies (linear vs quadratic).

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality/technical soundness
- Sign/optimality direction: The Bellman equation (3) uses a maximization with negative costs in the first term and claims convex, piecewise-linear value functions (Lemma 1). For a cost-minimization POMDP, the value function is usually a pointwise minimum of linear functions (concave). The paper should reconcile the sign convention and convexity/concavity claim, or explicitly define rewards to justify the maximization and convexity.
- Assumptions in the approximation bound (Proposition 1) are strong and misaligned with experiments: perfect tests (no false positives/negatives) and bounded maximum screening interval. In practice, mammography and even biopsy are imperfect; the experiments explicitly use literature sensitivity/specificity. The bound’s applicability to the reported results is therefore unclear. The constant ζ is not characterized or made computable, limiting practical interpretability of the bound.
- External information process: the model section assumes finite-state Y, but the experiment uses a continuous tumor-size model with noise. The discretization required to fit the algorithm is not described, and its impact on performance and complexity is not analyzed.
- Estimation/evaluation: Policy performance is assessed via simulation from a learned model, which is standard in this literature, but no uncertainty quantification (CIs), calibration checks, or robustness analyses to model misspecification are provided. Given the centrality of model-based evaluation, sensitivity to alternative specifications (e.g., different growth models, priors, test characteristics) is important.
- Policy class: The patient arrival threshold ỹ is exogenous. Jointly optimizing the trigger along with screening schedules could materially affect performance.
- Reproducibility/details: Key algorithmic details (exact belief construction, discretizations, parameter posteriors, computational resources) are deferred to appendices; without them, it is hard to assess reproducibility and scalability. The complexity expression O(T (B)2 T^2 K |Y||Z|) would benefit from a derivation and concrete runtime/memory figures on the breast cancer study.
- Clinical realism: The objective treats all screening costs equally and assumes no false positives at the “diagnosis” step, downweights biopsy harms, and does not model overdiagnosis or stage at detection. These are important in breast cancer screening and may alter the trade-offs.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well structured but notation is heavy, with occasional inconsistencies (e.g., ptr vs ptran), and some typographical issues (e.g., minus sign in (3)). Clarifying the action space (recommendation plan τ), belief update with endogenous arrival times, and the relation between exact and approximate beliefs would help.
- The robustness theorem is stated at a high level; a brief intuition and conditions (e.g., transversality) would aid readers.

Originality
- The combination of trajectory-dependent delay costs, endogenous arrivals from external information, and PBVI with trajectory-sampled belief spaces is novel relative to prior screening POMDPs and simulation-based approaches. However, the algorithmic ideas are close to established PBVI/Monte Carlo POMDP methods; empirical comparisons to alternative approximate POMDP solvers (e.g., HSVI, Perseus) are missing.

Significance
- If the framework is made robust and computationally practical, it could influence personalized screening policy design across diseases. Current empirical evidence shows promise on one dataset but lacks uncertainty and sensitivity analyses, and the theoretical guarantees rest on assumptions not met in experiments.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, high-impact problem with a unified, general formulation.
- Extends POMDP-style analysis to a richer setting and provides an implementable approximation with some theoretical support.
- Demonstrates substantial screening reductions at matched delays on a large cohort; clear personalization behavior.
- Highlights the effect of disease-specific delay costs.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical inconsistencies/omissions (sign/convexity) and strong assumptions in bounds reduce confidence in guarantees.
- Evaluation lacks uncertainty quantification, model calibration checks, and sensitivity to misspecification; external information discretization unspecified.
- Clinical modeling simplifications (diagnostic accuracy, harm modeling) may overstate practical gains.
- No comparisons to strong algorithmic baselines from the POMDP literature.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the Bellman sign convention and convexity/concavity properties; ensure consistency throughout.
- Specify discretization of the external information process and analyze its impact.
- Provide sensitivity analyses (test characteristics, growth parameters, priors), confidence intervals, and calibration on held-out data.
- Compare to alternative approximate POMDP solvers on matched models; include ablations (no external info, different belief set construction).
- Discuss optimizing the arrival trigger ỹ and incorporating richer clinical costs (biopsy harms, overdiagnosis).
- Report runtime/memory and scalability empirics; define/estimate ζ in the approximation bound.

Overall recommendation
The paper presents a promising and potentially impactful framework with novel elements and encouraging empirical results, but has technical clarity issues and evaluation gaps. Strengthening the theoretical consistency and empirical rigor would substantially improve its suitability for NeurIPS.
