Summary
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for computing personalized, dynamic screening policies that trade off screening cost and delay-in-detection cost. The formal model allows: (i) a general finite-state disease process with an absorbing disease state, (ii) an external information process (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger unscheduled visits, and (iii) actions that set the next screening time. The objective is a weighted sum of discounted screening costs and trajectory-dependent delay costs. The authors derive a belief-state dynamic program with a piecewise-linear convex value function and show that exact alpha-vector computation is intractable. They therefore propose a point-based approximation tailored to high-dimensional beliefs by sampling “likely” disease trajectories and reachable beliefs, and they provide a qualitative bound relating approximation error to coverage of the sampled belief set. They also argue robustness to model estimation error via a local constancy result. Experiments on a large breast cancer dataset (Athena; 45k patients) suggest substantial expected reductions in number of screens (28–68%) at comparable expected detection delays to annual/biennial schedules, and a synthetic exercise contrasts linear vs quadratic delay costs.

Relation to prior work
This paper builds on POMDP/POSMDP ideas (alpha-vector value functions, PBVI) and personalized screening in OR/medical decision-making (e.g., Ayer et al. 2012; Maillart et al. 2008; Erenay et al. 2014; risk-based and density-based intervals). The main claimed differences are: (a) endogenous visit timing through external information and recommended follow-up time, (b) trajectory-dependent delay costs, and (c) general (non-Markov) disease models. The algorithmic core remains point-based backups on sampled beliefs.

Strengths
- Ambitious and practically important problem formulation that unifies external information, personalized features, and endogenous scheduling.
- Clear belief-state dynamic program and recognition of piecewise-linear convexity.
- Pragmatic approximate algorithm using belief sampling; conceptually simple and potentially portable.
- Large dataset and an intuitive policy-level metric (reductions in expected screens for fixed expected delay).
- Some robustness discussion and an error bound relating approximation to belief coverage.

Weaknesses
- Empirical validation is limited and potentially fragile: comparisons are only to annual/biennial schedules, not to strong established personalized baselines (e.g., POMDP-based mammography, risk/density-tailored intervals, ConfidentCare).
- The learned model is not rigorously validated (no held-out calibration, posterior predictive checks, uncertainty quantification), yet conclusions hinge entirely on model-based counterfactuals.
- Theoretical guarantees rely on restrictive/unrealistic assumptions (e.g., no false positives/negatives for the bound) and the bound is not quantified in practice (ζ and coverage not computed).
- Internal inconsistencies: the method section assumes no false positives, yet the experiment includes a positive-mammogram/negative-biopsy outcome; the general model allows non-Markov processes but experiments use a 2-state Markov model.
- Algorithmic novelty over standard PBVI is modest; the core contribution is the application-specific belief sampling, with no demonstrated dominance or ablation.
- Scalability and computational practicality are not demonstrated (no runtime, memory, K/B/T sizes, parallel speedups).
- Many references are incomplete, and some claims about limits of existing frameworks are asserted but not convincingly substantiated.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The DP formulation and piecewise-linearity are standard and seem plausible, but key details (e.g., the construction of Pr(z, y, τ̃ | b, τ, x), exact belief updates under endogenous arrivals, and action space cardinality) are deferred to the supplement; it is difficult to reproduce. The approximation error bound is high-level and depends on strong assumptions (no misclassification, bounded inter-screen interval, large T) that do not match the experimental setup. The robustness theorem is intuitive (argmax stability a.e.) but uninformative for finite-sample estimated models, and no sensitivity analyses are provided.
- Empirical support: The reported reductions are measured under the authors’ learned model; there is no real-world or semi-synthetic validation where ground truth policy performance is known; no CIs or uncertainty bands; no comparisons to strong baselines; no ablations (e.g., effect of external info, belief sampling choices, without model misspecification). This is insufficient for endorsing a novel screening policy.
- Reproducibility: Core algorithms are in the appendix; hyperparameters (K, number of belief points per time, W, T), convergence criteria, and run-times are missing.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, but dense. Important algorithmic details are deferred, and the narrative sometimes mixes general assumptions with experiment-specific choices (e.g., false positives). References are often incomplete. A tighter, self-contained algorithm description with complexity and implementation details would improve clarity.

Originality
The problem framing with endogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent delay costs is interesting, but the proposed solution largely adapts PBVI with heuristic belief sampling. The theoretical components (piecewise linearity; stability of argmax; coverage-style bounds) are known in spirit; the novelty lies more in problem instantiation than in algorithmic theory.

Significance
Personalized screening is impactful, but the current evidence is not strong enough to influence practice or the NeurIPS community. Without robust baselines, sensitivity analyses, and quantified uncertainty, the reported gains could be artifacts of model misspecification. The methodological advance over existing POMDP-based approaches is incremental.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide comparisons to strong baselines: (i) published POMDP policies for mammography, (ii) risk/density-based interval policies (e.g., Trentham-Dietz et al.), (iii) simple myopic thresholding on posterior risk, and (iv) ConfidentCare.
- Validate the learned model: train/test splits, calibration curves for incidence, posterior predictive checks, and uncertainty quantification; show that policy conclusions are robust to plausible model perturbations.
- Quantify Ω(B̄): report ζ estimates, belief coverage metrics, K and B, and simulation showing convergence of value/policy with increasing coverage. Include a small synthetic domain where exact optimal is computable to validate approximation quality.
- Reconcile assumptions: clarify false positive/negative assumptions in theory vs experiments. If false positives/negatives exist, adapt the bound accordingly.
- Report computational performance: wall-clock times, memory, number of belief points per time, parallelization gains.
- Clarify whether the proposed framework truly cannot be expressed as a (semi-)Markov POMDP with augmented state and options; if it can, articulate where your approach gains practically.
- Provide confidence intervals on E[N] and E[Δ], and subgroup analyses (e.g., by age, density) to assess fairness and robustness.

Pros
- Important application with a comprehensive formalization.
- Sensible adaptation of point-based methods to endogenous scheduling.
- Large dataset and some personalization evidence.
- Piecewise-linear DP and stability arguments are coherent.

Cons
- Weak empirical validation and missing strong baselines.
- Theoretical guarantees rely on unrealistic assumptions and are not quantified in practice.
- Limited algorithmic novelty over PBVI; unclear scalability.
- Clarity/reproducibility issues and inconsistent assumptions.

Recommendation
Reject. The problem is worthwhile, but the current submission lacks the rigorous empirical validation, baseline comparisons, and practically meaningful guarantees needed to support its claims at NeurIPS. Strengthening the experimental section with strong baselines, robust model validation, quantified bounds, and computational evidence could make this a compelling future submission.
