This paper presents a dynamic personalized screening policy, addressing a crucial problem with significant practical implications. The authors aim to optimize screening schedules by considering patient features, disease progression, and external information, moving beyond limitations of existing Markovian-based approaches. While the problem is well-motivated and the proposed approach addresses several complexities, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet collectively substantial, flaws in presentation, technical detail, and justification, leading to a recommendation for rejection.

The introduction adequately motivates the problem but is overly verbose and repetitive. The enumeration of challenges (1-4) feels somewhat disjointed and could be more tightly integrated into a cohesive narrative. The claim of providing "the first such personalized screening policies in a very general setting" is strong and requires more rigorous justification, especially given the breadth of existing literature in dynamic treatment regimes.

Section 2, detailing the model and problem formulation, is dense and relies heavily on mathematical notation without sufficient explanation. While the intention to provide generality is commendable, the lack of concrete examples hinders understanding. For instance, the explanation of the Cox Proportional Odds model as a specific case is insufficient for readers unfamiliar with survival analysis. The assumption of independence between current test results and past results *conditional on* state trajectory and features is a standard assumption, but its repeated mention feels redundant. The notation is also inconsistent; for example, the use of both ~s and s to represent states is confusing. The cost function is normalized to 1 without sufficient justification; while easily generalized, this choice lacks motivation.

Section 3, the core of the paper, is where the majority of concerns arise. The description of the belief update process is unclear. The construction of the belief space in Algorithm 1 is computationally expensive and requires a thorough complexity analysis *beyond* the provided worst-case bound. The choice of sampling trajectories and beliefs lacks a clear theoretical underpinning. Why is this approach more effective than alternative sampling strategies? The Lemma 1, stating the value function's convexity, is a standard result but not adequately attributed. The discussion of approximation error (Proposition 1) is weak. The bound Ω(B̄) is rather uninformative without a clear understanding of how B̄ is determined and what factors influence its magnitude. The robustness analysis in Section 3.3, while important, is superficially treated; Theorem 1 feels like a statement of the obvious and does not offer practical insights. The personalization illustration (Figure 1) is helpful but lacks quantitative analysis.

The illustrative experiments in Section 4, using breast cancer data, are a significant weakness. The description of the dataset and model estimation process is cursory. The justification for choosing the specific parameters (ι = 0.9, σ = 0.43, ỹ = 1) is inadequate. The comparison with current clinical policies is limited; a more comprehensive comparison with existing screening guidelines and their associated cost-effectiveness analyses is needed. Table 1 shows promising reductions in screening frequency, but the statistical significance of these results is not discussed. The analysis of different delay costs (Figure 2) is insightful but presented without proper statistical validation. The choice of linear versus quadratic delay costs is motivated, but lacks a rigorous justification tied to specific disease characteristics.

The related work section is incomplete and overlooks relevant contributions in areas such as reinforcement learning for healthcare and sequential decision-making under uncertainty. Several key papers are missing, limiting the context of the presented work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The problem addressed is important and practically relevant.
*   The authors attempt to address complexities ignored by existing approaches.
*   The illustrative results suggest potential benefits of personalized screening.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper is overly verbose and lacks clarity in several sections.
*   The technical details are often insufficiently explained and justified.
*   The computational complexity analysis is incomplete.
*   The experimental validation is weak and lacks statistical rigor.
*   The related work section is incomplete.
*   The numerous minor flaws, while individually small, collectively detract from the paper's overall quality.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting idea, but its execution falls short of NIPS standards. The lack of clarity, insufficient technical detail, weak experimental validation, and numerous minor flaws outweigh the potential significance of the work. Despite addressing a relevant problem, the current presentation is unlikely to have a substantial impact on the field. Therefore, I recommend rejection. A major revision addressing the concerns outlined above, with a significantly more rigorous analysis and clearer presentation, may be considered for future submission.
