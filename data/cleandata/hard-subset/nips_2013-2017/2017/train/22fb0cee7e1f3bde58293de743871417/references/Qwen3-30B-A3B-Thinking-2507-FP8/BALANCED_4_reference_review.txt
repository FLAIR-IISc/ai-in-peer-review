

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

This paper proposes a novel framework for personalized disease screening policies that accounts for disease progression models, patient features, dynamic history, and external information (e.g., self-examinations). The authors develop a general model where disease progression follows an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, extending beyond the Markov assumptions of prior work [7,9]. They address the computational intractability of the optimal policy by proposing a point-based value iteration approach (DPSCREEN) with theoretical approximation error bounds, validated on breast cancer screening data showing 28-68% reductions in screenings compared to clinical standards.

**Strengths:**

1. **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a meaningful contribution by generalizing screening models beyond the restrictive Markov assumptions of prior work [7,9,10]. The theoretical results (Lemma 1 and Proposition 1) extend POMDP theory to this more complex setting, providing a foundation for the computational approach.

2. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The evaluation on real-world breast cancer data demonstrates substantial practical impact, with clear evidence of reduced screening frequency (28-68%) while maintaining expected detection delays. The analysis of how different cost structures (linear vs. quadratic) affect screening policies is particularly insightful.

3. **Comprehensive Related Work:** The paper thoroughly contextualizes its contribution against both operations research frameworks [7,9,10] and medical literature [44-48], clearly articulating how the proposed approach overcomes limitations of prior work.

4. **Rigorous Theoretical Guarantees:** The approximation error bounds (Proposition 1) and robustness analysis (Theorem 1) provide important theoretical assurances for the practical implementation of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Computational Scalability Concerns:** While the paper claims tractability, the worst-case complexity (O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)) remains high, and it's unclear how well this scales to more complex disease models with larger state spaces. The paper would benefit from more detailed analysis of computational requirements for realistic scenarios.

2. **Simplified Disease Modeling:** The empirical evaluation uses a two-state disease model (Healthy/Disease), which may not capture the complexity of real disease progression (e.g., intermediate states like "Risky" mentioned in the paper). The authors should address how their framework would handle more complex disease models.

3. **Limited Comparison with Modern Methods:** The paper doesn't compare with recent deep reinforcement learning approaches that could potentially address similar problems. A comparison with state-of-the-art RL methods would strengthen the empirical evaluation.

4. **Lack of Clinical Implementation Details:** The paper doesn't address practical implementation challenges in real clinical settings (e.g., integration with electronic health records, clinician workflow, patient adherence to personalized schedules), which are crucial for real-world adoption.

5. **Data Limitations:** The dataset (45,000 patients) is relatively modest for the proposed approach, and the paper doesn't thoroughly address how the model might generalize to different populations or diseases beyond breast cancer.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically sound and practically significant contribution to personalized screening. The general framework and theoretical results are novel and address important limitations in the existing literature. The empirical results demonstrate clear potential for improving healthcare efficiency, which is highly relevant to the NeurIPS community's interest in real-world applications.

However, the computational complexity concerns and simplified disease modeling for the empirical evaluation limit the paper's immediate impact. The authors should address these limitations in a revised version to strengthen the contribution.

**Recommendation:** Accept. While not perfect, the paper makes a significant theoretical contribution with clear practical implications. The strengths substantially outweigh the weaknesses, and with minor revisions addressing the computational scalability and disease modeling limitations, this paper would be a strong contribution to NeurIPS. The proposed framework has the potential to significantly impact healthcare practice and aligns well with NeurIPS' interest in practical applications of machine learning.

**Suggested Improvements:**

1. Include a more detailed analysis of computational requirements for realistic problem sizes.
2. Extend the empirical evaluation to a more complex disease model (e.g., including intermediate "Risky" states).
3. Add a comparison with recent deep RL approaches for similar sequential decision-making problems.
4. Discuss practical implementation challenges in clinical settings more thoroughly.
5. Provide more details on how the model parameters were estimated from the breast cancer data.
