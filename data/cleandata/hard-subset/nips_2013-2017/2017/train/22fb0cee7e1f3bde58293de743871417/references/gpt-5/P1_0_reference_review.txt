Summary
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off screening costs and delay-to-detection costs across heterogeneous patients and over time. The disease is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; external information (e.g., self-exams, comorbidities) endogenously triggers visits, and test outcomes are conditionally independent given state trajectories and patient features. The objective is a weighted sum of discounted screening cost and delay cost that can depend on both the detection lag and time of incidence.

The problem is cast as a belief-state dynamic program with a nonstandard structure: action-dependent inter-visit times driven jointly by recommendations and an exogenous information process, and trajectory-dependent costs. The authors prove the value function is convex and piecewise linear and derive Bellman equations. Because exact dynamic programming is intractable, they develop a point-based value iteration (PBVI) variant tailored to ultra–high-dimensional beliefs: sample a set of likely physiological trajectories and then construct a set of reachable belief points under random and clinically motivated policies. They provide an approximation bound in terms of a belief-space sampling density metric and a robustness theorem stating that the optimal policy is locally constant in model-belief space outside a lower-dimensional set; combined with the approximation bound, this suggests stability to estimation error.

For evaluation, they estimate a simple two-state breast-cancer progression model on a de-identified dataset (45k patients, 84k mammograms), use Gail model features, literature-based test sensitivities/specificities, and a tumor growth model for external signals (self-exams). Policies are tuned to match the expected detection delay of current clinical practice (annual or biennial screening). DPSCREEN achieves 28–68% reductions in expected screening frequency at similar expected delays; it personalizes both by baseline risk and by dynamic test/external information history. A synthetic exercise shows policies differ markedly when the delay cost is quadratic vs linear.

Relation to prior work
Compared to POMDP-based screening for breast/colon cancer (Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Erenay et al.), DPSCREEN relaxes Markov and stationarity assumptions, allows external information to endogenously trigger visits, and considers trajectory-dependent delay costs. It is closer to controlled sensing in spirit but departs from the usual stationary Markov and epoch-cost assumptions. Unlike medical simulation-based guideline evaluations (e.g., MISCAN-COLON), DPSCREEN optimizes over a large policy space rather than comparing a fixed set.

Strengths
- Problem importance and relevance to NeurIPS: sequential decision-making under uncertainty with personalization and external information.
- General modeling framework: arbitrary finite-state disease process, external info process, action-dependent inter-visit times, trajectory-dependent costs.
- Theoretical properties: piecewise linear convexity of the value function; a PBVI-style algorithm adapted via trajectory sampling; an approximation guarantee tied to belief-space coverage; a robustness result showing local constancy of optimal actions to model perturbations.
- Empirical demonstration on a large real dataset with plausible data-driven modeling; substantial potential reductions in screenings at matched expected delay; clear personalization effects; informative analysis of different delay-cost shapes.
- Clarity of the high-level narrative and motivation for external information and dynamic personalization.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical:
- Approximation bound is high level: Ω(B̄) is defined via an unspecified constant ζ and the distance to the sampled belief set; key assumptions (no false positives/negatives, bounded screening interval, large T, convex c) are strong and partly inconsistent with experiments where mammograms have imperfect accuracy. It is unclear how the bound degrades under realistic error models.
- Theorem 1’s “locally constant” policy map is stated without conditions (e.g., uniqueness, regularity) beyond a uniqueness assumption; the nature of the exceptional set E and its measure properties are not detailed. Practical implications for finite-sample estimation error are not quantified.
- Equation (3) appears to use a negative sign on costs inside the maximization. If minimizing cost, either the sign or optimization direction needs clarification.
- The belief dimensionality reduction via sampling K trajectories induces model misspecification: beliefs place zero mass outside S̃. The impact of trajectory sampling K and reachable belief set size B on performance is not empirically ablated.
- Complexity expression O(T(B)2 T^2 K|Y||Z|) is opaque and not derived; no wall-clock runtimes or scaling plots.

Empirical:
- Evaluation is entirely model-based: policies are assessed on simulations from an estimated model. There is no off-policy evaluation or uncertainty quantification (e.g., bootstrap CIs, sensitivity analyses) for screening counts or delays.
- Baselines are limited to annual/biennial schedules. Comparisons to prior POMDP-based mammography policies or to heuristic risk-adaptive intervals are missing.
- Metrics: expected delay unconditional and conditional on disease are useful, but clinically salient endpoints (stage at detection, mortality proxies) are not reported. Expected delay can be dominated by the many non-cases.
- Dataset access and reproducibility: the dataset is proprietary; code and detailed hyperparameters/sampling settings are not provided.

Clarity/presentation:
- Several parts defer crucial details to the supplement; some references are incomplete; minor typos and formatting issues reduce readability.
- Assumptions on independence and “no false positives” are stated early but later relaxed in the empirical section; this inconsistency should be reconciled.

Originality
Moderate to high. The problem formulation and incorporation of external information with endogenous arrivals is novel for screening. The algorithm builds on PBVI but adapts it via trajectory-based belief compression for a nonstandard DP; the robustness/approximation claims are conceptually useful though technically light.

Significance
Potentially significant for personalized screening policy design and for ML-in-health sequential decision-making. The demonstrated reductions in screenings at matched delays are compelling, but the reliance on a learned simulator and absence of stronger baselines/uncertainty weaken the immediate practical impact.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify Eq. (3): are you maximizing negative costs or is the sign a typo?
- How sensitive are results to K (number of sampled trajectories), B (belief points), and the action space restriction W? Provide ablations and runtime.
- How are test inaccuracies handled in the theory vs practice? Can the approximation bound be extended to imperfect tests?
- Can you compare against prior POMDP-based mammography policies (e.g., Ayer, Maillart) under your estimated model?
- Provide uncertainty estimates for E[N], E[Δ], and an analysis of robustness to model misspecification (e.g., alternative hazard/logit forms, different self-exam parameters).
- What fraction of early arrivals are triggered by external information vs scheduled follow-ups under learned policies?

Suggestions for improvement
- Reconcile and clearly state assumptions on test accuracy and independence; specify Z+ precisely (biopsy positivity) to justify “no false positives.”
- Add ablation studies and runtime/scaling plots; quantify ζ or provide an upper bound in terms of problem parameters.
- Expand baselines and include off-policy evaluation or sensitivity bands.
- Report additional clinical endpoints and subgroup analyses.
- Improve reference completeness and fix formatting/typos; provide code or pseudo-code sufficient for reproduction.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and timely formulation with external information and endogenous visit times.
- Solid high-level theory and an implementable algorithmic framework.
- Promising empirical results suggesting large efficiency gains.
- Relevance and potential impact in ML for healthcare.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical guarantees are qualitative and rely on strong assumptions; limited quantitative guidance.
- Evaluation is simulator-based with limited baselines and no uncertainty quantification.
- Some clarity and consistency issues in the exposition and assumptions.
- Reproducibility concerns (data/code).

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper tackles an important problem with a thoughtful and reasonably novel approach and shows promising results. Strengthening theoretical clarity, empirical comparisons, and uncertainty analyses would make this a clear accept.
