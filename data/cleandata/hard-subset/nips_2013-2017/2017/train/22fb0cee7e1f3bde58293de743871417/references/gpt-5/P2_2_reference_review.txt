Summary
The paper addresses the problem of learning dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off test burden versus detection delay. It formulates a very general model: disease is an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; actions schedule the next follow-up date; arrivals can also be triggered endogenously by external information (e.g., self-exam lumps); test outcomes depend on latent trajectories and features. The authors cast the problem as a partially observed control problem with a nonstandard cost (delay depends on the incidence and detection times) and endogenous decision epochs. They show the value function is convex, piecewise-linear in a belief over state trajectories, derive an exact but intractable alpha-vector dynamic program, and propose an approximate PBVI-style method (DPSCREEN) that samples likely disease trajectories and reachable beliefs. They provide a bound on approximation error in terms of belief-sampling density (under strong assumptions) and a robustness result stating the optimal policy is locally constant in model-belief space almost everywhere. A case study on 45k women (Athena dataset) with a two-state breast cancer model and self-exam process suggests 28–68% expected reduction in screenings at similar expected detection delays relative to annual/biennial policies.

Relation to prior work
The work extends POMDP/POSMDP-based screening (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Erenay et al.) by allowing non-Markov latent disease models, external information that endogenously triggers visits, and delay costs that depend on trajectories rather than current state. The algorithmic approach builds on PBVI (Pineau et al.) but adapts belief construction to extremely high-dimensional beliefs over trajectories by sampling likely trajectories and reachable beliefs. It contrasts with medical simulation studies (e.g., MISCAN-COLON) that compare fixed policy sets without optimization, and with Bayesian design approaches that optimize information rather than delay-aware utility.

Strengths
- Problem importance and scope: Personalized screening with endogenous arrivals is timely and clinically meaningful. The framework unifies disparate modeling elements (disease dynamics, test processes, external information).
- Conceptual novelty: Endogenous decision epochs and trajectory-dependent costs are handled within a value-iteration framework; the belief over trajectories is a clear way to encode non-Markov dynamics.
- Practical algorithm: DPSCREEN adapts PBVI via trajectory sampling and reachable-belief construction, which is intuitive and implementable.
- Empirical illustration on a large dataset with plausible external information modeling; the reported reductions in screening load at maintained expected delays are promising and align with personalization goals.
- The robustness statement (local constancy of optimal actions away from a measure-zero set) gives some comfort about estimation error.

Weaknesses and detailed assessment
Quality/technical soundness:
- Theoretical contributions are mostly known or high-level. The piecewise-linear/convexity of V is standard in POMDPs; the local-constancy theorem is plausible but not detailed in the main text. The approximation bound is informal: Ω(B̄) depends on an unspecified ζ and assumes no false positives/negatives, convex delay, bounded screening interval, and large T. It neither quantifies sample complexity nor ties back to the original (unsampled) model rigorously.
- The belief compression by sampling a subset of trajectories induces bias; the bound appears to be about sampling density over beliefs encountered, not approximation to the original model’s optimal policy. Clarify whether the bound is w.r.t. the original model or the restricted-support model.
- Computational complexity is given as O(T (B)2 T^2 K|Y||Z|) but is hard to interpret and seems loose; wall-clock runtimes and scaling experiments are missing.
- Empirical evaluation is model-based and relies on several external parameter choices (mammogram sensitivity/specificity, self-exam process) with no uncertainty quantification. Tuning w to match the benchmark’s expected delay risks selection bias; report the sensitivity of outcomes to w and to model parameters. No CIs or statistical variability are reported.
- Off-policy evaluation alternatives (e.g., inverse propensity or doubly robust estimators) are not explored, though the authors note the challenge.

Clarity:
- The high-level problem and motivation are clear, and the relation to POMDPs is articulated. However, many core details (algorithms, proofs) are deferred to the supplement. Notation could be streamlined; beliefs over trajectories plus a diagnosis label are unusual and would benefit from a concise graphical explanation in the main text. The distinction between recommendation plan τ and policy could be clearer. Some assumptions (e.g., “no false positives” vs. experimental modeling of biopsies) seem inconsistent on first read; a clarifying paragraph would help.

Originality:
- The modeling of endogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent delay within a PBVI-like framework is a meaningful extension of prior screening POMDPs. However, the core algorithmic ingredients (PBVI, reachable-belief sampling, alpha-vector backups, piecewise linearity) are established; the novelty lies in their integration and application rather than in new theory or algorithms. The robustness theorem’s generality and proof details are not clear from the main text.

Significance:
- If validated, the approach could influence how we design risk- and history-adaptive screening schedules across diseases. The large dataset and the incorporation of self-exam signals increase practical relevance. That said, the case study ultimately uses a simple two-state Markov disease model; demonstrating benefits under more complex non-Markov dynamics would better showcase the generality claim. Lack of uncertainty quantification and external validation limits immediate impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide explicit algorithm pseudocode and key implementation details in the main text; report computational cost and scaling.
- Quantify uncertainty: report CIs via bootstrap over patients and parameter posteriors; perform sensitivity analyses over self-exam parameters and tumor growth model.
- Compare against strong baselines, including prior POMDP-based screening policies (e.g., Ayer et al.), information-gain designs, and heuristic adaptive schedules.
- Clarify the approximation bound: define ζ, state the bound w.r.t. the original model, and, if possible, give a sampling complexity statement.
- Add ablations: impact of trajectory sampling size K and belief set size B on performance; effect of excluding external information.
- Discuss ethical/safety constraints (e.g., minimum/maximum intervals), and fairness across subgroups.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, well-motivated problem with a coherent framework.
- Integrates external information and endogenous scheduling in a principled way.
- Promising empirical results on a sizable dataset; method is likely of interest to the NeurIPS community working on sequential decision-making in healthcare.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novel theory; approximation guarantees are high-level and rely on strong assumptions.
- Experimental validation lacks uncertainty quantification, sensitivity analyses, and comparisons to strong methodological baselines.
- Scalability and reproducibility details are insufficient in the main text.

Overall recommendation
Borderline. The paper is ambitious and practically relevant, with a solid modeling contribution and promising initial results, but the theoretical advances are modest and the empirical validation needs strengthening to meet NeurIPS standards. I lean weak reject unless the authors can add stronger comparisons, uncertainty analyses, and clearer algorithmic/theoretical details.
