Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off test frequency against delay in disease detection. The authors cast screening as a sequential decision problem with: (i) a very general disease process (finite state trajectory model with a single absorbing disease state), (ii) external information (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger unscheduled visits, (iii) test outcomes, and (iv) a delay cost that depends on the lag between incidence and detection (trajectory-dependent cost). They formulate a belief-state dynamic program with endogenous decision epochs, derive Bellman equations over beliefs defined on state trajectories, show the value function is piecewise-linear and convex, and note that exact alpha-vector computation is intractable. They then propose a PBVI-style approximation: sample likely physiological trajectories, build a set of reachable beliefs through randomized exploration (optionally seeded with clinical policies), and perform value backups on this reduced belief set. They give a complexity estimate, an approximation error bound in terms of the sampling density of the belief set, and a robustness result suggesting local constancy of optimal actions under small model estimation errors. Empirically, they estimate a simple two-state breast cancer model on Athena data (45k women, 60–65 years), incorporate a self-exam signal process based on a tumor growth model, and report that DPSCREEN can reduce screening counts by 28–68% while maintaining expected detection delay comparable to annual (or biennial) clinical policies. They also illustrate that using convex (quadratic) delay costs yields more aggressive screening, emphasizing disease-specific cost modeling.

This work builds on POMDPs and PBVI (e.g., Smallwood & Sondik; Pineau et al.), and relates to prior screening optimization in OR (Ayer et al.; Maillart et al.; Erenay et al.) and to Bayesian design/joint longitudinal-survival approaches (Rizopoulos; Alaa et al.). The paper’s main claimed novelty is handling non-Markov disease trajectories, endogenously triggered decision times, external information, and trajectory-dependent delay costs within a unified, approximately solved framework with an error bound.

Strengths
- Ambitious, general formulation that subsumes several disease and screening models (including dynamic survival and first-hitting-time models), and explicitly incorporates external information and endogenous arrivals.
- Sound use of belief-state DP theory; the piecewise-linear value function result extends known POMDP structure to this setting.
- Practical approximation via trajectory-informed PBVI with reachable-belief construction; conceptually sensible for extremely high-dimensional beliefs.
- Clear personalization story: policies adapt to both static features (e.g., Gail risk) and dynamic history (test outcomes, external signals).
- Empirical case study on a sizeable real dataset; reported reductions in screening volume at fixed expected delay are clinically meaningful.
- Error and robustness results: even if assumptions are strong, having a bound and a local-constancy argument is a useful step.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical assumptions limit the practical force of guarantees. The approximation error bound assumes perfect tests (no false positives/negatives) and depends on an unspecified constant ζ and an intractable coverage metric over the full belief simplex; as stated, the bound is hard to compute/verify and may not reflect realistic test noise used in experiments.
- The robustness theorem is high-level; the statement that optimal actions are locally constant outside a lower-dimensional set is plausible (given piecewise-linear value functions), but depends on uniqueness and regularity assumptions and lacks a clear, actionable stability radius or sensitivity bound with respect to estimation error.
- Empirical evaluation is narrow. The disease model used in experiments is a two-state Markov process (simpler than the general setting); external information is modeled via a specific growth law with parameters tuned from literature. There is no comparison to prior personalized screening methods (e.g., POMDP-based approaches by Ayer/Maillart or ConfidentCare), only to annual/biennial clinical heuristics.
- Outcome metrics are limited to expected delay and number of tests. Clinically salient measures (e.g., detection rates among cases, stage at detection, false positives, biopsy burden, overdiagnosis proxies) are not reported. Treating all tests as unit-cost ignores different harms/costs (e.g., biopsies).
- Potential bias in model estimation from observational data with informative screening schedules is not addressed. Interval censoring and dependence of test timing on risk/observations complicate unbiased estimation of incidence and transition probabilities.
- Clarity issues: multiple constructs (policy vs recommendation plan) and independence assumptions could be more precisely defined; complexity expressions and the construction of B̄ merit more detail. Important algorithms/figures/tables are deferred to the supplement.

Quality
Technically competent and mostly sound in formulation; however, the main theoretical guarantees rest on strong or hard-to-verify assumptions. The empirical section demonstrates promise but lacks strong baselines and broader clinical endpoints.

Clarity
Generally well-written, but key ideas (reachable-belief construction, action space, recommendation-plan vs policy, and computation/parallelization details) should be clearer in the main text. Some notation (e.g., z ∈ Z+ and “no false positives”) seems at odds with experimental modeling of mammography + biopsy; clarify what “diagnosis” event corresponds to.

Originality
The combination of trajectory-based costs, external information with endogenous arrivals, and a PBVI variant tailored to sampled plausible trajectories appears novel relative to the cited screening literature.

Significance
Potentially impactful for risk-based screening policy design. However, significance is tempered by the gap between the generality of the theory and the narrowness of the empirical validation and baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Theory: (i) Relax the perfect-test assumption in the approximation bound; derive bounds under bounded false positive/negative rates. (ii) Provide a computable surrogate for Ω(B̄) (e.g., empirical covering radius on the sampled belief manifold) and report it. (iii) Strengthen robustness by quantifying action stability margins with respect to model parameter perturbations.
- Empirics: (i) Compare against prior personalized screening methods (Ayer/Maillart POMDPs, ConfidentCare) and risk/density-based clinical strategies. (ii) Report additional endpoints: detection among cases, stage distribution, false positives, biopsy counts, and sensitivity analyses for cost weights and self-exam parameters. (iii) Address estimation under informative screening/interval censoring; consider joint models or IPCW approaches. (iv) Provide runtimes, scalability with K and |B̄|, and ablations on belief-sampling strategies and action-space constraints.
- Clarity: (i) Tighten the distinction between policy π and recommendation plan τ; (ii) specify Z+, diagnostic pathway, and reconcile “no false positives” with experimental modeling; (iii) move essential algorithmic details from the supplement to the main paper.

Pros for acceptance
- General, principled framework with a clear personalization mechanism.
- Meaningful empirical gains on real data.
- Bridges decision-theoretic planning and clinical screening with external information.

Cons for acceptance
- Guarantees hinge on strong assumptions; bounds not readily usable in practice.
- Limited empirical validation and lack of comparisons to strong personalized baselines.
- Important methodological details deferred; some modeling choices and estimation issues insufficiently justified.

Decision
Reject (borderline). The paper is promising and could be impactful, but needs stronger empirical comparisons, more clinically comprehensive evaluation, and more usable theoretical guarantees to meet the bar for NeurIPS. The suggested additions would significantly strengthen the contribution.
