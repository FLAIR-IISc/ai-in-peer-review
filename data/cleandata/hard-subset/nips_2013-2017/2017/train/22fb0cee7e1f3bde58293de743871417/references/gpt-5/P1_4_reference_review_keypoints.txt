- Proposes DPSCREEN: a dynamic, personalized screening framework balancing test costs and detection delay using trajectory-dependent delay costs and an external information process.  
- Models disease as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and exogenous signals that can trigger visits.  
- Formulates a belief-state dynamic program over distributions on state trajectories and diagnosis labels, proving the value function is convex and piecewise-linear (alpha-vectors).  
- Derives Bellman equations but exact solution is intractable, so proposes a PBVI variant that samples likely disease trajectories to reduce the belief space.  
- Provides an approximation bound linking error to belief-sampling density and a robustness theorem that optimal actions are locally constant under small model errors.  
- Empirical claim: on a de-identified breast cancer dataset (n≈45k), DPSCREEN can reduce expected screens by 28–68% while matching annual screening delay.  
- Shows different delay-cost functions (linear vs quadratic) yield different screening recommendations.  
- Relates to prior POMDP and screening work, highlighting novelty in trajectory-dependent costs, external information, and non-(semi-)Markov processes, though many aspects can be state-augmented in standard frameworks.  
- Theoretical contributions extend known POMDP properties but are largely expected; the approximation bound is qualitative, depends on opaque constants, and relies on restrictive assumptions.  
- Robustness result is standard for finite-action PL value functions and offers limited practical guidance.  
- Algorithmic details are deferred to the supplement, complexity is high, and the stated complexity bound needs clearer derivation and practical guidance.  
- Stability and sensitivity to choice of sampled trajectories and belief construction are not analyzed.  
- Empirics follow a model-then-simulate paradigm but use a simple 2-state Markov model, undermining claims about non-Markov handling.  
- Lacks model fit metrics (calibration, predictive accuracy), uncertainty quantification (CIs), off-policy evaluation, stronger baselines, and runtime/scalability reporting.  
- Tuning w to match annual delay risks post hoc selection; sensitivity analyses to w, test costs, and self-exam parameters are missing.  
- Paper is generally well organized but dense; notation for trajectory beliefs is heavy and some assumptions/reconcilations (e.g., Z+ false positives) are unclear.  
- Originality is moderate: a novel synthesis of ideas but incremental algorithmic contributions and extensions of known theory.  
- Potentially significant if robust and scalable, but current empirical and theoretical support is insufficient for strong clinical claims.  
- Pros: principled framework for screening, explicit incorporation of external info and trajectory costs, PBVI-style practical approach, promising empirical reductions.  
- Cons: loose approximation bound with opaque constants, weak experimental validation and missing baselines, overstated generality, strong/practical assumptions that may bias results, clarity and reference issues.  
- Key author questions: how are trajectories K and belief points B chosen; runtime/memory/parallelization details; sensitivity to self-exam model and w; comparisons to prior POMDP policies and heuristics; model calibration and uncertainty intervals; modeling overdiagnosis/competing risks/test-cost heterogeneity.  
- Suggestions: strengthen evaluation (fit, sensitivity, baselines, uncertainty), report computational performance and provide code, clarify relation to standard POMDP reductions, tighten approximation analysis, and demonstrate on richer disease models.  
- Recommendation: borderline reject — important and promising but theoretical guarantees are weak and empirical evidence is insufficient for acceptance.
