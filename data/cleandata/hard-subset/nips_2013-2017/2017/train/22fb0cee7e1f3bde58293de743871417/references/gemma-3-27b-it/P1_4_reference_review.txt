## Peer Review for DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for developing personalized screening policies that dynamically adapt to patient features, screening history, and external information. It addresses limitations of existing screening models by allowing for non-Markovian disease progression and test outcomes, incorporating external information, and tackling the computational intractability of optimal policy derivation. The authors propose a point-based value iteration approach to approximate the optimal policy and provide bounds on the approximation error. They demonstrate the efficacy of DPSCREEN using a breast cancer dataset, showing significant reductions in screening frequency while maintaining comparable detection delays, and illustrate the impact of cost function choice.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds upon existing research in personalized screening (Ayer et al., 2017; Maillart et al., 2017), POMDPs (Pineau et al., 2006), and dynamic treatment regimes (Rizopoulos et al., 2016). However, it significantly extends these areas by relaxing Markovian assumptions, handling external information, and providing a computationally tractable solution with theoretical guarantees. The authors rightly point out the limitations of prior work (e.g., requiring stationary processes, inability to handle external information) and position DPSCREEN as a more general and flexible approach.  Connections to controlled sensing (Krishnamurthy, 2017) are also acknowledged, highlighting the shared problem of optimal information acquisition.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality:** DPSCREEN offers a remarkably general framework applicable to a wide range of diseases and models.  The relaxation of Markovian assumptions is a major strength.
* **Practicality:** The proposed point-based value iteration algorithm provides a computationally feasible approach to solving a complex problem.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  Providing bounds on the approximation error lends credibility to the approach and quantifies the trade-off between accuracy and computational cost.
* **Empirical Validation:** The breast cancer dataset demonstration convincingly shows the benefits of personalized screening, with significant reductions in screening frequency.
* **Impact of Cost Function:**  The illustration of how different delay cost functions influence policy decisions is insightful and highlights the importance of careful cost modeling.
* **Clear Discussion of Limitations:** The authors transparently acknowledge the challenges of model estimation and discuss the impact of estimation errors.
* **Well-written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the methodology and results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the algorithm is presented, implementing DPSCREEN appears to be a significant undertaking, requiring substantial expertise in POMDPs and statistical modeling. The paper could benefit from more details on practical implementation considerations.
* **Model Assumptions:** The reliance on accurate estimation of model parameters (transition probabilities, hazard rates) is a potential weakness. While the authors address estimation error, the sensitivity of the results to these estimations isn't fully explored.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The primary empirical validation is limited to breast cancer.  While a comprehensive demonstration across all diseases is impractical, exploring a second disease with different characteristics would strengthen the claims.
* **External Information Model:** The model for external information (lump size) feels somewhat simplistic. More sophisticated models could potentially improve the policy's accuracy.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors discuss computational complexity, a more detailed analysis of scaling with problem size (number of states, time horizon) would be helpful.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  The paper is technically sound, with clear theoretical analysis and a well-defined algorithm.  The claims are generally well-supported, though further sensitivity analysis on model parameters would be beneficial.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but some sections (particularly the mathematical formulations) are dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
* **Originality:** The combination of a general framework, relaxation of Markovian assumptions, handling of external information, and a practical approximation algorithm represents a significant advance over existing work.
* **Significance:**  The potential impact of DPSCREEN is substantial. Personalized screening policies have the potential to improve healthcare outcomes while reducing costs and patient burden. The work addresses a practically important and challenging problem.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a crucial problem in healthcare with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* It provides a general framework that extends existing methods and addresses their limitations.
* The empirical results demonstrate the potential benefits of personalized screening.
* The inclusion of approximation error bounds adds to the paper's rigor.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The implementation complexity might limit the widespread adoption of the method.
* The reliance on accurate model estimation could be a practical barrier.
* The experimental validation is somewhat limited in scope.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe that this is a strong paper with significant potential. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field of personalized screening. While there are some weaknesses, they are outweighed by the strengths of the work.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. 

**Suggested Revisions:**

* Provide more details on the practical implementation of the algorithm, including any software tools or libraries that could be used.
*  Conduct a sensitivity analysis to assess the robustness of the results to variations in model parameters.
* Explore the application of DPSCREEN to a second disease (even a simplified one) to demonstrate its generality.
* Consider exploring more sophisticated models for external information.
* Include a more detailed analysis of the algorithm’s computational complexity and potential scalability improvements.
