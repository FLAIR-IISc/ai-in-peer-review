This paper proposes a dynamic personalized screening policy (DPSCREEN) that aims to balance the benefits and costs of screening for various diseases. The core idea is to tailor screening frequency based on patient features, disease type, and individual screening history, addressing limitations of existing approaches that often rely on Markovian assumptions and stationary test outcomes. The authors formulate this as a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) and develop a computationally efficient approximation algorithm with bounds on the approximation error. They demonstrate the effectiveness of DPSCREEN using a breast cancer dataset, showing significant reductions in screening frequency while maintaining comparable detection delays, and a synthetic analysis across diseases with different cost functions.

The paper builds upon existing work in screening optimization (e.g., [7, 9, 10]) by relaxing the Markovian and stationarity assumptions. It also connects to the broader literature on controlled sensing [21, 34, 38] and POMDPs [18, 19], but explicitly addresses challenges arising from the dynamic nature of disease progression, external information, and the computational intractability of finding optimal policies. The consideration of different delay cost functions is also a valuable extension.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach addresses a relevant and challenging problem – personalized screening – with a focus on generality and realism. The relaxation of Markovian assumptions and the incorporation of external information represent significant advancements.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The formulation as a POMDP is appropriate, and the development of an approximation algorithm with error bounds demonstrates a rigorous approach. The theoretical analysis, including Lemma 1 and Proposition 1, provides a foundation for understanding the performance of the algorithm.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear introduction of the problem, model, and proposed solution. The use of notation is consistent, and the key equations are well-explained. The inclusion of a graphical model (Appendix B) is helpful.
*   **Significance:** The potential impact of personalized screening on healthcare costs and patient outcomes is substantial. The demonstrated reductions in screening frequency without compromising detection delays are particularly promising. The analysis highlighting the importance of cost function selection further strengthens the contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** While the authors acknowledge the computational complexity, it remains a potential barrier to wider adoption. Further discussion of scalability and practical implementation considerations would be beneficial.  The statement regarding parallelization is good, but more specific details would be helpful.
*   **Model Assumptions:** The model still relies on several simplifying assumptions, such as the independence of test results given the state trajectory and patient features.  The sensitivity of the results to violations of these assumptions is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Data Limitations:** The use of a single dataset (breast cancer) limits the generalizability of the results. Applying the framework to other diseases and diverse populations would strengthen the findings.
*   **Parameter Estimation:** The description of parameter estimation is relatively brief. Providing more details on the MCMC methods used and the sensitivity of the results to estimation errors would enhance reproducibility.
*   **Comparison to existing literature:** While the authors acknowledge related work, the comparison could be made more concrete. A more detailed discussion of how DPSCREEN addresses specific limitations of previous approaches (beyond stating the relaxed assumptions) would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a clinically important problem.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a strong foundation for the proposed algorithm.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the potential benefits of personalized screening.
*   The work significantly extends the existing literature on screening optimization.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity may limit practical application.
*   The model relies on simplifying assumptions that could affect the accuracy of the results.
*   The generalization to other diseases is not thoroughly evaluated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of personalized healthcare. While there are some limitations, the novelty, technical soundness, and potential impact of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding computational complexity, model assumptions, and data limitations. Specifically, I suggest expanding the discussion of scalability, conducting a sensitivity analysis of the model assumptions, and exploring the application of the framework to additional datasets.
