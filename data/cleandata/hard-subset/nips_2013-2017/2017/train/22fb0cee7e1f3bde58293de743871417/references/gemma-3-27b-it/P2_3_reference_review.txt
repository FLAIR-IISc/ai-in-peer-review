## Review of DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for developing personalized screening policies that dynamically adjust to patient features, screening history, and external information. It tackles the challenges of optimizing screening frequency against detection delay, considering non-Markovian disease progression and computationally intractable policy optimization. The authors propose a point-based value iteration approach with a novel belief construction method and provide theoretical guarantees on approximation error. They demonstrate the effectiveness of DPSCREEN using a breast cancer dataset, showing significant reductions in screening frequency while maintaining detection delay comparable to current clinical practices.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon existing work in personalized screening (Ayer et al., 2017; Maillart et al., 2017; Erenay et al., 2017) and decision-making under uncertainty (POMDPs – Pineau et al., 2006). However, it distinguishes itself by addressing limitations of previous approaches.  Unlike many existing methods assuming Markovian disease processes (e.g., Cox proportional hazards models), DPSCREEN accommodates more general disease dynamics.  It also surpasses methods relying on fixed screening intervals or limited consideration of external information. The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of controlled sensing and highlight its advantages in dealing with non-stationary processes and complex cost structures.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The framework is highly general, accommodating various disease models and incorporating external information, making it applicable to a wide range of screening scenarios.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, including approximation error bounds and robustness analysis. This is crucial for justifying the practical applicability of the proposed algorithm.
* **Practical Demonstration:** The use of a real-world breast cancer dataset lends credibility to the approach.  The reported reduction in screening frequency (28-68%) is substantial and potentially impactful.
* **Personalization:** The method demonstrably personalizes screening policies based on patient risk profiles and dynamic test results, a key advantage over standard approaches.
* **Clear Identification of Challenges:** The paper explicitly acknowledges and addresses the computational challenges inherent in personalized screening, particularly the intractability of optimal policy calculation.
* **Robustness Analysis:** The inclusion of robustness analysis relating to estimation error provides practical insights.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors acknowledge computational complexity, a more detailed discussion of scaling with problem size (number of states, time horizon) is needed.  The claimed complexity O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|) isn't fully intuitive without further breakdown and discussion of typical parameter ranges.
* **Model Assumptions:** The reliance on specific assumptions regarding disease progression (e.g., tumor growth model, hazard functions) and external information (e.g. independence assumptions) needs further justification. The impact of violating these assumptions should be discussed.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison is primarily with annual screening.  A more comprehensive comparison with other personalized screening approaches (even if requiring simplification for implementation) would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Clarity of Algorithm Details:** While algorithms are included in the supplementary material, some steps (e.g., belief construction) could benefit from more detailed explanation within the main body of the paper.
* **Presentation:**  The paper is dense and could benefit from more illustrative examples and visualizations beyond Figure 1.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper addressing an important and challenging problem. The proposed framework is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and demonstrates promising results in a real-world application. While the computational complexity and model assumptions warrant further consideration, the potential benefits of DPSCREEN in terms of reducing screening burden and improving healthcare efficiency are significant.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a significant problem in healthcare decision-making with a novel and well-motivated approach.
* The theoretical guarantees on approximation error provide confidence in the practical applicability of the method.
* The experimental results demonstrate substantial improvements over existing clinical practices.
* The generality of the framework makes it applicable to a wide range of screening scenarios.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity remains a concern, particularly for large-scale problems.
* The model assumptions could limit the generalizability of the approach.
* The limited comparison with other personalized screening methods.

**Rating:**  Strong Accept (7/10)

**Recommendation:** I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2017. The strengths of the work significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and the contribution is likely to be valuable to the NIPS community. I suggest the authors address the concerns regarding computational complexity and model assumptions in a revised version.
