Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing dynamic, personalized screening policies that optimize a weighted trade-off between screening cost and delay-in-detection cost. The model allows for general (finite-state) disease processes with an absorbing disease state, external information (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger unscheduled visits, and test outcomes, all conditioned on patient features. The optimization is formulated via a belief-state dynamic program with path-dependent costs and endogenous decision epochs. The authors derive a piecewise-linear convex value function, present an exact alpha-vector dynamic program (intractable in general), and develop a tractable PBVI-style approximation that samples likely state trajectories and reachable beliefs. They provide an approximation-error bound tied to belief-sampling density and a robustness result stating local constancy of the optimal policy under small model perturbations. Empirically, using a large breast cancer cohort (N≈45k, ages 60–65), they estimate a two-state disease model with Gail-model features and compare DPSCREEN to current clinical policies (annual/biennial), showing large reductions (28–68%) in expected screenings for similar expected detection delays; they also illustrate policy adaptations under convex delay costs.

This work relates to OR/health screening POMDP models for breast/colon cancer [Ayer et al. OR; Maillart et al. OR; Erenay et al. MSOM], risk-based designs and joint models [Rizopoulos], and simulation-based guideline development (e.g., MISCAN-COLON). Relative to these, DPSCREEN aims to relax Markov/stationarity assumptions, incorporate endogenous arrivals via external information, and handle delay costs depending on state trajectories rather than only current state. Methodologically, it adapts PBVI [Pineau et al.] to a higher-dimensional belief over state-trajectories and introduces trajectory-guided belief sampling.

Strengths:
- Clear formalization of a challenging screening problem with endogenous arrivals and path-dependent costs not handled by standard POMDP/POSMDP formulations.
- Sound use of finite-horizon belief-state DP; establishes piecewise-linear convexity and an alpha-vector representation.
- Practical approximation via trajectory- and policy-guided belief sampling; conceptually reasonable in very high-dimensional belief spaces.
- Approximation error result tying performance to belief-sampling density; robustness argument on policy stability under small model estimation error.
- Empirical study on a large real-world dataset with feature-based personalization; demonstrates substantial test reductions at matched expected delay; illustrates sensitivity to convex delay costs.

Weaknesses:
- Several theoretical results rely on strong or simplifying assumptions: no false positives/negatives and bounded inter-screen interval for the error bound; absorbing disease state; independence structures for external info and tests. Practical realism and implications are not fully explored.
- Key algorithmic details (belief construction, action space restriction, parallelization) and complexity are insufficiently specified; the provided complexity expression is unclear and likely loose.
- Evaluation uses a two-state Markov disease model, undercutting claims of generality beyond Markov processes. No ablation showing benefits over standard Markov/POMDP baselines on the same model.
- Off-policy evaluation is entirely model-based; there is limited uncertainty quantification (e.g., parameter/posterior uncertainty, sensitivity to self-exam parameters, tumor growth model, and test error rates).
- Some notation inconsistencies and potential sign conventions (maximize negative costs) reduce clarity; several derivations relegated to the supplement.

Quality:
- Theory: The extension of piecewise-linear convex value functions to this setting appears sound. The PBVI-style approximation is standard in spirit; the novelty lies in belief support restriction via trajectory sampling. The approximation bound (Proposition 1) depends on a sampling-density term Ω(B̄) and a constant ζ, but ζ is not characterized, and the assumptions (perfect tests, convex costs, bounded interval, large T) limit practical interpretability. The robustness theorem’s local constancy statement is plausible (policies are piecewise constant on regions), but conditions ensuring a measure-zero boundary set should be clarified.
- Algorithms: The belief construction via sampling likely trajectories and reachable beliefs is reasonable, but the method risks bias if the belief support misses rare but consequential trajectories. Action space restriction (e.g., maximum inter-screen interval W) is critical for tractability; this needs explicit specification and analysis.
- Experiments: Model estimation via Gail features and MCMC is standard. The evaluation metric—matching expected delay to annual/biennial policies and reporting test reductions—is appropriate, but the results’ robustness to model misspecification and to the external information process (monthly self-exams) is not quantified. Confidence intervals, sensitivity analyses, and comparisons to simpler risk-stratified periodic policies and to prior POMDP screening methods would strengthen claims.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable and well-structured, with clear problem setup and motivation. However, several issues detract from clarity:
- Sign convention in the Bellman equation (maximizing negative costs) should be stated explicitly; reconcile with convex/concave statements.
- Notation inconsistencies (e.g., ptr vs ptran; Z+ vs diagnostic mapping) and minor typographical issues.
- Important algorithmic details and proofs are deferred; include concise descriptions in the main text to aid understanding.

Originality:
The combination of (i) endogenous arrivals driven by external information, (ii) path-dependent delay costs, and (iii) a PBVI variant with trajectory-guided belief support appears novel relative to prior screening and controlled sensing work. The error/robustness results, though high-level, add theoretical value.

Significance:
Personalized screening with the potential to substantially reduce testing while maintaining detection timeliness is important. The framework could be applied to multiple diseases and modalities. However, broader impact depends on stronger empirical validation, sensitivity/uncertainty analyses, and demonstration beyond a two-state Markov model.

Questions for the authors:
- Can you quantify ζ in Proposition 1 or provide empirical proxies? How sensitive is performance to belief sampling density and K?
- How is the action space (follow-up time) discretized and bounded (W)? What is the runtime and memory profile vs T, K, |Y|, |Z|?
- How robust are the results to test misclassification (false positives/negatives) and to self-exam frequency/specificity assumptions?
- Can you compare against risk-stratified periodic baselines and prior POMDP screening methods on your learned model?
- Please clarify the Bellman sign convention and the role of Z+ in ensuring “no false positives” at the diagnosis decision.

Suggestions:
- Add uncertainty quantification (bootstrap/MCMC draws of model parameters) and sensitivity analyses for key assumptions (self-exams, growth model, test accuracies).
- Provide ablations (without external info; different belief sampling strategies; effects of W) and more baselines.
- Clarify and tighten the complexity analysis and algorithmic details; include a schematic of Algorithm 1–2 in main text.
- Improve notation consistency and briefly outline proofs’ key ideas in the main paper.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important problem with a principled, general framework.
- Theoretical formulation and adaptation of PBVI are sound and plausibly novel for this setting.
- Empirical results suggest large practical gains.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Dependence on strong assumptions in theory; limited empirical robustness and uncertainty analysis.
- Instantiated experiments use a simple Markov model, weakening claims of generality.
- Missing comparisons to strong baselines and prior POMDP screening approaches; some clarity and detail issues remain.
