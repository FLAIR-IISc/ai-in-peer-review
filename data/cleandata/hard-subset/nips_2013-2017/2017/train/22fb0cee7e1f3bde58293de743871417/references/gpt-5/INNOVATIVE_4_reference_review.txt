Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a general framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off screening cost and detection delay. The model allows: (i) arbitrary finite-state disease trajectories with an absorbing disease state (not restricted to Markov or semi-Markov), (ii) external information that can trigger early visits (e.g., self-exams), (iii) observation processes whose distribution depends on the latent trajectory and patient features, and (iv) delay costs that depend on the time between incidence and detection (possibly non-linear). The authors formulate a belief-state dynamic program over distributions on full state trajectories and a diagnosis label, prove the value function is convex and piecewise linear (as in POMDPs), and show how to compute the exact policy via alpha-vectors (intractable in general).

They then develop a tractable PBVI-style approximation tailored to the extremely high-dimensional belief space: sample “typical” disease trajectories, construct the set of reachable beliefs under random and policy-guided exploration, and run point-based value backups. They give a complexity expression, an approximation-error bound in terms of sampling density under several assumptions, and a robustness result showing local constancy of the optimal policy with respect to small model-parameter perturbations. Empirically, they fit a two-state disease model for breast cancer on a 45k-patient dataset, incorporate self-exam signals, and demonstrate that DPSCREEN reduces expected tests by 28–68% while matching the expected detection delay of annual/biennial screening. They also show how changing the delay-cost function (linear vs quadratic) leads to different operating regimes.

Relative to prior OR and ML work on screening (Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Erenay et al.; Rizopoulos; controlled sensing; POMDPs), the paper is broader in modeling scope (endogenous arrivals via external information and trajectory-dependent delay cost) and proposes a PBVI variant designed for very large trajectory-based belief spaces. Related NeurIPS work on adaptive information acquisition and health decision support is cited.

Strengths
- Novelty and scope: A bold, unifying formulation that captures nonstationary disease/test processes, external information, and trajectory-based delay costs. This goes beyond standard POMDP screening models with fixed decision epochs and state-dependent costs.
- Methodological contribution: Adapting PBVI via trajectory sampling and reachable-belief construction is a sensible, practical innovation for otherwise intractable belief spaces. The piecewise-linear/convexity result anchors the approach theoretically.
- Theoretical support: An explicit approximation-error bound (albeit under strong assumptions) and a robustness result on policy stability with respect to model estimation error.
- Empirical relevance: A sizable real dataset; clear personalization effects; substantial reductions in screening burden at matched expected delays; sensitivity to disease-specific cost structure (linear vs quadratic).
- Clarity of the overall pipeline: Model specification, learning from observational data, policy construction, and evaluation are described coherently.

Weaknesses
- Theory assumptions vs reality: The approximation-error bound assumes no false positives and no false negatives and a bounded screening interval. These are unrealistic for many screening tests (though biopsy confirmation may mitigate false positives). The bound’s practical tightness is unclear, and it would help to discuss how violations affect guarantees.
- Evaluation breadth and baselines: The empirical model used in the breast-cancer study is two-state Markov—the very setting where prior POMDP screening models apply. A head-to-head comparison with those policies (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al.) is missing and would strengthen claims of improvement beyond clinical heuristics. Similarly, comparisons to recent risk-based or information-gain strategies would be informative.
- Model validation: It is not clear how well the learned disease model fits held-out data (calibration, discrimination), which is crucial since evaluation relies on the model to simulate counterfactual outcomes. Reporting goodness-of-fit metrics and sensitivity analyses would increase confidence.
- Some clarity issues: (i) The Bellman equation (3) uses a “max” with a negative sign in front of costs; the sign convention and objective (reward vs cost) should be made consistent. (ii) The text alternates between “no false positives” for diagnosis and realistic sensitivities/specificities for mammography; please clarify what constitutes “diagnosis” and where errors are allowed. (iii) Choice and tuning of ỹ (self-exam threshold) and weight w could be better justified; e.g., specify how w is tuned to match delays and whether the same w generalizes across subgroups.
- Reproducibility: Key details such as K (number of sampled trajectories), |B̄| per time, runtime, hardware, and sensitivity of results to K/|B̄| are not reported. Code/data availability is not indicated.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with appropriate use of belief-state DP and PBVI. Proof sketches are deferred; the main caveat is that theoretical guarantees rely on strong assumptions. The empirical study is careful within its scope but lacks competitive baselines and model validation metrics.

Clarity
Generally well-organized, though long and notation-heavy. Clarify the cost/reward sign, the diagnostic error assumptions, and the parameter tuning procedure. Adding a compact algorithm box in the main text (not only appendix) and a figure of the belief construction process would help.

Originality
High. The endogenization of arrival times via external information and trajectory-dependent costs in a generalized POMDP-like setting, plus the tailored PBVI over sampled trajectory-beliefs, is a meaningful advance over prior screening and controlled sensing formulations.

Significance
Potentially significant for ML-for-health and decision-making under uncertainty. Even if parts of the theory rest on idealizations, the framework can influence how personalized screening policies are designed and evaluated, and the empirical reductions in test burden are compelling.

Pros for acceptance
- Bold, general modeling that extends the screening literature
- Practical PBVI adaptation to very high-dimensional belief spaces
- Theoretical properties (piecewise linearity, approximation bound, robustness)
- Empirical evidence of large screening reductions with maintained delays
- Clear personalization and sensitivity to disease-specific cost structures

Cons for acceptance
- Strong theoretical assumptions; limited discussion of their practical impact
- Missing comparisons to state-of-the-art algorithmic baselines in the Markov case
- Lack of thorough model-fit validation and ablation on approximation hyperparameters
- Some clarity and reproducibility gaps

Suggestions for improvement
- Add head-to-head comparisons against POMDP-based screening baselines on the same data/model.
- Report held-out predictive performance of the learned disease model and sensitivity analyses to misspecification.
- Clarify diagnostic error assumptions and cost sign convention; detail w and ỹ tuning.
- Include runtime, K, |B̄|, and sensitivity of results to these choices; consider releasing code.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper introduces a substantial and novel direction with promising practical impact. While the empirical validation could be stronger and the theoretical assumptions are idealized, the conceptual advance and methodological ingenuity outweigh these limitations.
