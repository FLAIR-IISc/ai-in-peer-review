

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

This paper proposes a method for developing personalized screening policies using a generalized POMDP framework that accounts for disease progression, external information, and dynamic screening schedules. The authors frame personalized screening as an optimization problem balancing screening costs against delay costs in disease detection, and propose a "DPSCREEN" algorithm that uses point-based value iteration to approximate the optimal policy.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper builds upon standard POMDP techniques (notably Pineau et al. [18]) to address a healthcare application domain. While the problem of personalized screening has been studied in operations research (e.g., Ayer et al. [7], Maillart et al. [8]), the authors claim their approach is novel in handling "very general" disease models beyond Markov processes. However, they fail to adequately distinguish their method from existing POMDP-based screening approaches, which also aim to personalize screening policies. The core technical contribution appears to be applying standard POMDP techniques to a new application domain, not developing new theoretical methods.

## Strengths

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a solid mathematical formulation of the problem. The theoretical bound on approximation error (Proposition 1) provides a theoretically grounded justification for their approximate solution. The application to breast cancer screening with empirical results showing potential screening reductions (28-68%) is a strong practical motivation.

## Weaknesses

**Lack of Novelty and Proper Baseline Comparison**: The paper's main contribution is applying established POMDP techniques to a healthcare application. The authors cite Ayer et al. [7] as previous work using POMDPs for personalized screening, yet fail to demonstrate how their approach significantly differs or improves upon this existing work. Crucially, they don't compare against Ayer et al.'s method or other POMDP-based screening approaches in their experiments, making it impossible to assess whether their method actually provides meaningful improvements.

**Insufficient Experimental Validation**: The empirical results rely on a single dataset (breast cancer) and don't compare against established clinical policies beyond simple annual vs. biennial screening. The claim of "28-68% reduction in screening" lacks context: how does this compare to other methods that might already achieve similar results? The paper doesn't provide sufficient detail about the baseline policies used for comparison (e.g., what specific clinical guidelines they're comparing against), making it difficult to assess the claimed improvements.

**Limited Scope of Validation**: The paper claims to handle "very general" disease models but only tests on a simple two-state Markov model for breast cancer. They don't demonstrate how their method works for more complex disease models they claim to support (e.g., non-stationary processes, Semi-Markov processes mentioned in the introduction). The synthetic disease cost experiment in Figure 2 is superficial and doesn't validate the core method.

**Theoretical Contribution is Limited**: While they provide a bound on approximation error, this is a standard result for point-based value iteration methods (Pineau et al. [18]), not a novel theoretical contribution. The paper doesn't advance the underlying theory of POMDPs or provide new structural insights about the screening problem.

**Lack of Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address practical limitations of their approach, such as computational complexity for larger state spaces, sensitivity to model misspecification, or implementation challenges in clinical settings.

## Overall Assessment

The paper addresses an important healthcare problem but fails to provide sufficient evidence that their method represents a meaningful advance over existing approaches. The core methodology is a straightforward application of well-established POMDP techniques to a new domain, with minimal theoretical or empirical validation against relevant baselines. The claimed improvements (28-68% reduction in screenings) are impressive but lack proper context and validation against existing personalized screening approaches.

While the problem is significant and the application is relevant to healthcare, the paper doesn't demonstrate that their approach offers a meaningful contribution to the field. The lack of thorough comparison against existing methods, the limited scope of validation, and the minimal theoretical contribution make it difficult to justify the novelty and significance claims.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper presents an interesting application of standard POMDP techniques but fails to provide the necessary evidence of novelty and significance required for NIPS. The authors should substantially strengthen their comparison against existing methods (particularly Ayer et al. [7] and other POMDP-based screening approaches), provide more thorough validation across diverse disease models, and better articulate how their approach differs from and improves upon prior work before it would be suitable for publication at NIPS.
