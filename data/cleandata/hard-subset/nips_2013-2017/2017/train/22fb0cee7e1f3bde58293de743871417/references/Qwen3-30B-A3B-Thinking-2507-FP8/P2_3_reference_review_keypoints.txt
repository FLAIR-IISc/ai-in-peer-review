- Proposes DPSCREEN: a framework for personalized screening that balances screening costs against disease detection delays and generalizes beyond Markovian assumptions.  
- Models arbitrary finite-state stochastic disease progression with absorbing states and external triggers (e.g., self-exams).  
- Extends POMDP/point-based value iteration to this context and provides theoretical guarantees (Lemma 1: value function convex & piecewise linear; Proposition 1: approximation error bound).  
- Empirical evaluation on a real breast cancer dataset shows 28–68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining equivalent expected detection delays.  
- Analyzes robustness to estimation errors (Theorem 1) and acknowledges need to estimate disease models from observational data.  
- Clear and well-organized exposition with consistent notation, helpful figures and appendix; Algorithm 1 could use more explicit explanation and some medical terms may need clarification.  
- Highly original: relaxes restrictive assumptions of prior work (non‑Markovian processes) and shows different cost functions (linear vs. quadratic) produce different screening regimes.  
- Significant practical impact for ML and healthcare: potential cost reductions and broad applicability to multiple diseases (supported by synthetic experiments).  
- Strengths: addresses an important real-world problem, provides theoretical guarantees, strong empirical results, and thorough robustness evaluation.  
- Weaknesses: high computational complexity for large-scale deployment, assumes no false positives/negatives, limited validation beyond breast cancer, and limited discussion of ethical/disparity concerns.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to better discuss computational complexity and acknowledge ethical considerations.
