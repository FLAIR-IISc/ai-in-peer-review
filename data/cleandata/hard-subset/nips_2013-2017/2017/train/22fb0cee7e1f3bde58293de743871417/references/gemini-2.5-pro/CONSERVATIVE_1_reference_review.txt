### Summary
This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for generating dynamic, personalized screening policies for diseases. The authors formulate the problem as minimizing a weighted sum of screening costs and delay-in-detection costs. The key feature of the framework is its generality: it accommodates arbitrary finite-state stochastic processes for disease progression (not restricted to Markovian), incorporates external information that can trigger unscheduled appointments, and allows for flexible, trajectory-dependent cost functions. Recognizing that this general formulation is computationally intractable, the authors propose an approximate solution based on Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI). The main technical contribution appears to be a belief sampling procedure for the PBVI algorithm, designed to handle the high-dimensional belief space induced by conditioning on entire state trajectories. The method is evaluated by learning a two-state Markov model for breast cancer from a large real-world dataset and showing through simulation that the resulting policies can significantly reduce the number of screenings compared to standard annual/biennial policies while maintaining the same expected delay in detection.

### Strengths
1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem. The formulation is comprehensive, attempting to capture several real-world complexities of medical screening, such as patient self-examinations (external information) and non-Markovian disease progression, which are often abstracted away in prior work (e.g., [7], [9]).
2.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the model is presented with clarity. The authors do a good job of motivating the problem and articulating why standard POMDP/POSMDP frameworks are insufficient for their general formulation.
3.  **Experimental Results:** The results from the breast cancer case study are compelling on the surface. A 28-68% reduction in the number of required screenings is a substantial improvement over the fixed-interval clinical baselines and highlights the potential benefits of personalization.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Mismatch Between General Framework and Evaluation:** The primary weakness of this work lies in the significant disconnect between the proposed general, non-Markovian framework and the empirical validation. The main justification for the new framework is its ability to handle complex disease models beyond the Markovian assumption. However, the evaluation is performed on a simple two-state (Healthy, Disease) Markov model. This specific model likely falls within the scope of, or is a simple extension to, existing POMDP-based approaches for screening [7]. The paper does not provide a compelling reason why its complex, trajectory-based machinery was necessary for this specific problem. A crucial missing baseline is a comparison against established POMDP methods on the same learned model. Without this comparison, it is impossible to assess whether the proposed framework provides any practical benefit over simpler, established techniques.

2.  **Insufficient Validation of Robustness and Scalability:** The evaluation relies entirely on simulations from a single learned model. The performance of any such policy is critically dependent on the accuracy of this model. While Theorem 1 provides a theoretical argument for robustness to estimation error, this is an asymptotic result and offers no quantitative guarantees for a model learned from finite data. A sensitivity analysis examining how the policy's performance degrades with plausible model misspecification is needed to build confidence in the method. Furthermore, the computational complexity seems formidable. The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the parameters used in the experiments (e.g., the horizon T, the number of sampled trajectories K, the number of belief points B) and an analysis of how runtime scales with these parameters. This makes it difficult to judge the practicality of the approach for more complex models or longer time horizons.

3.  **Limited Empirical Scope:** The experiments are confined to a single disease (breast cancer) modeled in a very simple way. The framework's touted generality remains unproven. It would have been more convincing to see the method applied to a disease where non-Markovian dynamics are known to be important, or where the state space is richer than just Healthy/Disease. The synthetic experiment on cost functions is illustrative but does not substitute for a more thorough empirical validation of the core modeling assumptions.

4.  **Incremental Novelty of the Solution Method:** The proposed solution is an application of the well-established Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) algorithm. The novelty is claimed to be in the belief-space sampling strategy (Algorithm 1). This strategy—sampling likely trajectories and then exploring reachable beliefs—is a reasonable heuristic but may be viewed as an incremental adaptation rather than a fundamental algorithmic contribution.

### Detailed Comments

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its derivations. However, the experimental rigor is lacking. The claims of superiority are based on comparisons to non-adaptive clinical heuristics rather than state-of-the-art optimization-based methods. The central claim that a general, non-Markovian framework is needed is not substantiated by the experiments.

**Originality:** The originality lies primarily in the ambitious problem formulation. The solution technique is an adaptation of existing methods (PBVI) to this formulation. The work is clearly situated with respect to prior literature, but its marginal contribution over established POMDP methods is not clearly demonstrated in practice.

**Significance:** While the problem is highly significant, the significance of the proposed solution is questionable without stronger evidence. If the method provides no tangible benefit over simpler POMDP models for a wide class of real-world problems (like the one studied here), its impact will be limited. Practitioners are unlikely to adopt a highly complex and computationally intensive method if a simpler, established one performs just as well. The paper presents a framework that is theoretically appealing but has not been sufficiently validated to demonstrate its practical necessity or superiority.

### Recommendation
**Reject.**

While the paper addresses an important problem with a conceptually appealing general framework, the empirical evaluation fails to justify the need for the proposed complexity. By applying the method to a simple Markovian disease model and only comparing against fixed clinical policies, the authors miss the crucial comparison against established POMDP-based approaches. This omission makes it impossible to evaluate the practical contribution of the work. The paper feels incomplete—it presents a powerful engine but uses it to drive a very simple vehicle on a straight road, leaving the reader to wonder if a simpler engine would have sufficed. For the paper to be acceptable, it would need to either (a) provide a direct, quantitative comparison against relevant POMDP baselines on the current dataset, or (b) apply the framework to a problem where the non-Markovian and trajectory-dependent features are demonstrably essential and cannot be handled by prior methods.
