## Peer Review: DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for developing personalized screening policies that dynamically adjust to patient features, screening history, and external information, optimizing the trade-off between screening cost and delay in disease detection.  Unlike existing approaches which often rely on Markovian assumptions or stationary test outcome models, DPSCREEN handles general stochastic disease progression models and non-stationary information processes. The authors address the computational intractability of finding optimal policies by proposing an approximate solution with provable error bounds, and demonstrate its effectiveness using a breast cancer dataset, achieving significant reductions in screening frequency while maintaining comparable detection delays to current clinical practice.  The work also highlights the importance of cost function choice in tailoring screening regimes.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper positions itself well within the existing literature on screening (Wilson et al., 2016) and decision-making under uncertainty (e.g., POMDPs - Krishnamurthy, 2017). It clearly differentiates itself from prior work in personalized screening (Ayer et al., 2017; Maillart et al., 2017; Erenay et al., 2017; Rizopoulos et al., 2017) by relaxing the restrictive assumptions (Markovian processes, stationary outcomes) commonly found in those models. It also distinguishes itself from simulation-based approaches (e.g., MISCAN-COLON - Loeve et al., 2005) by providing a dynamic, policy-optimization framework.  The connection to controlled sensing (Smallwood & Sondik, 1996) is appropriately noted, but the authors rightly point out that the stationarity assumptions in that field limit its applicability to this personalized screening context.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality:**  The framework is exceptionally general, accommodating diverse disease models, non-stationary test outcomes, and external information. This broad applicability is a significant strength.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The authors provide a computational procedure for obtaining *approximately* optimal policies and, crucially, a bound on the approximation error (Proposition 1). This is a major contribution, as it provides a guarantee on the quality of the solution.
* **Practical Demonstration:** The application to breast cancer screening with a real-world dataset is compelling. The observed reductions in screening frequency (28-68%) while maintaining detection delays are substantial and practically relevant.
* **Personalization:** The policy demonstrably personalizes to both patient features (risk profiles) and dynamic history (test results), as illustrated in Figure 1, providing a more nuanced approach than standard protocols.
* **Cost Sensitivity:**  The analysis demonstrating the impact of different delay cost functions on policy regimes (Figure 2) emphasizes the importance of tailoring policies to disease-specific characteristics.
* **Robustness Analysis:** The inclusion of a theoretical analysis of the impact of model estimation error (Theorem 1) adds robustness to the method.



**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors acknowledge the computational complexity, the stated O(T(B)^2 T^2 K|Y||Z|) complexity still appears potentially prohibitive for very large state spaces or long time horizons, even with parallelization.  A more detailed discussion of scalability limitations and potential mitigations (e.g., function approximation) would be beneficial.
* **Model Assumptions:** Despite the generality of the *framework*, the breast cancer demonstration relies on specific assumptions about the disease model (two-state Markov) and the parameters governing lump growth and self-examination.  The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions should be further explored.
* **External Information Modeling:** The modeling of external information (lump size) seems simplistic. A more realistic model accounting for patient reporting biases, measurement errors, or varying sensitivity to lumps could improve the accuracy of the policy.
* **Lack of Comparison to Alternative Approximation Techniques:** The paper focuses on PBVI. A brief discussion of, and potentially a comparison to, other POMDP approximation techniques (e.g., Monte Carlo Tree Search) would strengthen the justification for the chosen approach.
* **Limited Discussion of Generalizability:** While the breast cancer example is strong, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of how the framework could be adapted to other diseases with different characteristics.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamentally important problem (personalized screening) with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* The generality of the framework and the inclusion of approximation error bounds are significant contributions.
* The practical demonstration with a real-world dataset is compelling and shows substantial potential benefits.
* The work is clearly written and well-motivated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity could limit the applicability of the method to certain problems.
* The reliance on specific model assumptions in the breast cancer demonstration warrants further investigation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential to impact the field of personalized medicine. The authors have developed a sophisticated and general framework for dynamic personalized screening that addresses key limitations of existing approaches.  While the computational complexity and model assumptions warrant further consideration, the theoretical rigor, practical demonstration, and clear presentation make this a valuable contribution to NIPS.

**Rating:**  **Strong Accept**

**Recommendation:**  I recommend accepting this paper for presentation at NIPS 2017.
