- Proposes DPSCREEN: a PBVI-style framework for dynamic personalized screening that trades screening costs vs. delay costs, models trajectory-dependent delay and external information.
- Models disease as arbitrary finite-state process with absorbing disease state; beliefs over full state trajectories; intractable exact solution so uses trajectory-sampling for belief reduction.
- Proves piecewise-linear convexity of the value function (POMDP-like) and gives a PBVI approximation with an approximation error bound under strong assumptions.
- Provides a robustness result that the optimal recommendation mapping is locally constant almost everywhere in model parameters.
- Case study on 45k-woman breast cancer dataset shows 28–68% expected screening reductions at matched detection delay, and policy sensitivity to delay-cost shape.
- Connects to POMDP planning and screening literature; novelty claimed in handling non-Markov processes, exogenous arrivals, and trajectory-dependent delay.
- Technical correctness largely sound, but sign convention in the Bellman equation is inconsistent with minimization objective and needs clarification.
- Approximation bound depends on unspecified constant ζ and unrealistic assumptions (no false positives/negatives, bounded gaps, convex delay), limiting interpretability.
- Robustness theorem is essentially generic for piecewise-linear value functions and does not quantify sensitivity or performance impact.
- Experiments use a simple 2-state Markov disease with literature sensitivities, which is far less general than the claimed model class.
- No comparisons to prior personalized screening methods (Ayer, Maillart, ConfidentCare) or advanced POMDP planners (SARSOP, HSVI, Perseus, POMCP).
- Lack of held-out predictive validation for incidence/detection dynamics and limited sensitivity analyses beyond self-exam inclusion.
- Procedure for tuning weight w to match target delay is unspecified and Pareto-frontier placement is not verified.
- Reproducibility issues: key estimation details, hyperparameters, and code are not provided.
- Clarity is good overall, but notation/ sign inconsistencies, unclear variable definitions in the Bellman equation, and mismatched assumptions (e.g., “no false positives” vs. using sensitivity/specificity in experiments) require fixes.
- Several bibliographic references are incomplete and need correction.
- Methodological novelty is modest—adaptation of existing PBVI/belief compression ideas rather than a fundamentally new planning algorithm.
- The approximation bound is novel in spirit but relies on unrealistic perfect-test assumptions and is not numerically instantiated.
- Potentially high practical impact if validated, but significance for NeurIPS is limited by modest novelty and weak empirical validation.
- Pros: important socially relevant problem, clear formalization including external information and trajectory-dependent delay, practical PBVI algorithm, large real-world dataset, and useful discussion on delay-cost effects.
- Cons: limited methodological novelty, unrealistic theoretical assumptions, weak experimental baselines and validation, empirical claim of generality not demonstrated, and notational/inconsistency issues.
- Suggested improvements: compare to personalized baselines and advanced POMDP planners, provide small-scale exact-solution experiments to assess approximation, perform predictive validation and sensitivity analyses, relax perfect-test assumptions in theory, detail w-tuning and show trade-off curves, fix clarity and references, and release code/data.
- Recommendation: borderline reject—important problem and thoughtful framework but needs stronger comparisons, validation, and theoretical grounding before acceptance.
