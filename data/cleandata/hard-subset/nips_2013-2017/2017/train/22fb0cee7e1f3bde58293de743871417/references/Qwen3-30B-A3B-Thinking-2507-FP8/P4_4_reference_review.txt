

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

This paper proposes a novel framework for developing personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against delays in disease detection. The authors formulate the problem as an optimal control problem where disease progression is modeled as a general finite state stochastic process (not restricted to Markovian or semi-Markovian processes as in prior work), incorporating external information (e.g., patient self-examinations) that can trigger earlier screenings. The authors develop a computationally efficient approximation method using point-based value iteration on a reduced belief space, with theoretical guarantees on approximation error.

The paper relates well to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. It extends the POMDP-based screening approaches of Ayer et al. (2013) and Maillart et al. (2013), which assumed Markov disease processes and stationary test outcome models. The authors correctly note that many disease processes (e.g., breast cancer, HIV, colon cancer) do not follow these restrictive assumptions. Their work also relates to controlled sensing literature (Krishnamurthy, 2017; Pineau et al., 2006) but addresses the more complex problem of personalized screening with external information and non-Markovian disease progression.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a theoretically grounded approach with a clear problem formulation, rigorous mathematical treatment, and well-supported claims. The authors derive a convexity property of the value function (Lemma 1) and provide an approximation error bound (Proposition 1), demonstrating careful attention to theoretical foundations. The computational complexity analysis is thorough, and the proposed approximation method (DPSCREEN) is shown to be tractable.

2. **Novelty:** The paper makes a significant contribution by developing a framework that handles general finite state stochastic disease processes (not restricted to Markov or semi-Markov assumptions), incorporates dynamic external information, and provides a computationally feasible solution. This addresses a key limitation in prior screening frameworks (Ayer et al., 2013; Maillart et al., 2013; Rizopoulos et al., 2014) that assumed restrictive disease models.

3. **Significance:** The experimental results on breast cancer data are compelling, showing 28-68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining the same expected delay in disease detection compared to current clinical policies. The synthetic exercise across diseases with different cost structures (linear vs. quadratic) demonstrates the framework's adaptability to different disease contexts.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with consistent notation and thorough explanations of the problem formulation. The supplementary materials provide additional mathematical details and algorithm descriptions that enhance reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Disease Modeling:** While the paper claims generality, it primarily demonstrates results on a two-state Markov model for breast cancer. The authors acknowledge that their model restricts to a single absorbing disease state (for expositional convenience), which may not capture complex disease trajectories with multiple intermediate states or competing risks.

2. **Simplified Test Outcome Model:** The paper assumes no false positives/negatives and a simplified test outcome model (Z = {∅, 1, 2, 3}), which may not reflect real-world diagnostic complexities. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, more discussion of how the framework would handle noisy or imperfect test results would strengthen the work.

3. **Validation of Model Estimation:** The model estimation procedure using observational data is described but lacks detailed validation of the estimated parameters. The authors assume the Gail model for risk scores, but it would be valuable to demonstrate how well their disease progression model fits the observational data.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper addresses a significant gap in personalized screening frameworks by handling general disease processes.
- It provides a theoretically sound, computationally tractable solution with error bounds.
- The results demonstrate substantial practical improvements over current clinical policies.
- The work has clear relevance to healthcare decision-making and personalized medicine.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The model simplifications (single absorbing state, no false positives) limit the applicability to some real-world screening contexts.
- The experimental validation is limited to breast cancer; more diverse disease examples would strengthen the claims of generality.
- The paper doesn't fully address how the framework would handle cases where disease progression models are highly complex or unknown.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of personalized decision-making under uncertainty, with clear technical merit and significant practical implications. While the model simplifications present some limitations, the framework's generality, theoretical grounding, and compelling empirical results outweigh these concerns. The paper advances the state of the art in personalized screening policy design and demonstrates clear significance for healthcare applications. The technical soundness, novelty, and significance of the work meet NIPS standards for high-quality contributions.
