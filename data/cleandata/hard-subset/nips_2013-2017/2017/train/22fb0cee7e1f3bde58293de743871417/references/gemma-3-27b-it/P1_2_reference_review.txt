## NIPS 2017 Review: DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for developing personalized screening policies that optimally balance the frequency of testing against the delay in disease detection. It tackles key challenges in personalized screening—handling non-stationary disease progression, incorporating external information, dealing with unknown delay costs, and computational intractability—by proposing a computationally effective procedure with bounded approximation error. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a breast cancer dataset, showing significant reductions in screening frequency while maintaining acceptable delay, and through synthetic experiments highlighting the impact of different cost structures.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The authors position their work within the broader context of screening optimization, referencing works in operations research, medical literature, and related machine learning fields. They clearly differentiate their approach from existing methods like those in [7, 9, 10], which often rely on restrictive assumptions (Markov processes, stationary test outcomes) that do not hold in many real-world scenarios.  The paper also acknowledges connections to controlled sensing [21, 34, 38] and POMDPs [18-20], but emphasizes how DPSCREEN extends these frameworks to handle the complexities of dynamic screening policies with state-trajectory dependent costs and arrival times. The related work section is comprehensive, and properly cites relevant contributions.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The framework addresses a significant gap in the literature by developing a flexible and generalizable approach to personalized screening, moving beyond restrictive assumptions.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a well-defined mathematical formulation of the problem and offers a bounded approximation error for the proposed solution.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to breast cancer screening demonstrates the potential for significant improvements in clinical practice, achieving substantial reductions in screening frequency without compromising detection delay.
* **Comprehensive Modeling:**  The inclusion of external information (self-exams) and the flexible delay cost function enhance the realism and applicability of the model.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly explains the core concepts, though some sections (especially regarding Lemma 1 and the Bellman equations) are dense and could benefit from further simplification.
* **Robustness Analysis:** The discussion of estimation error and the theorem establishing robustness to model misspecification adds credibility to the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors acknowledge the computational complexity, the scaling (O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)) may still be prohibitive for very large state spaces or long time horizons.  Further discussion of practical strategies for mitigating this complexity (beyond parallelization) would be beneficial.
* **Model Assumptions:** The two-state Markov model for disease progression, while simplifying, might be overly restrictive for some diseases. The authors acknowledge this but could expand on the potential for incorporating more complex disease models.
* **Data Dependence:** The performance of the model is highly dependent on the quality and availability of data for parameter estimation. The breast cancer example relies on the Gail model, which has its limitations.  A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of different estimation methods or data sources would be valuable.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the breast cancer example is compelling, extending the evaluation to other diseases or using synthetic data with varying characteristics could further demonstrate the generalizability of DPSCREEN.
* **Clarity of Algorithm Description**: Algorithms 1 and 2 are presented in the Appendix, which makes it difficult to understand the specifics of the proposed approach. A high-level overview of the key steps in the main body of the paper would improve readability.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for personalized screening.
* The approach has the potential to significantly improve clinical practice and reduce healthcare costs.
* The authors provide a rigorous analysis of the approximation error and robustness to model misspecification.
* The breast cancer example demonstrates the practical feasibility and effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity may limit the scalability of the approach.
* The model assumptions might be restrictive for some applications.
* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept** for NIPS. While there are some minor weaknesses, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and practical relevance of the work outweigh these concerns. The paper addresses a significant problem with a well-developed and promising solution. The authors clearly articulate the contributions of their work and provide a compelling demonstration of its potential.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above in a revised version would further strengthen the paper.  Specifically, clarifying the computational complexity and providing more details about the algorithms in the main text would be helpful.

**Confidence:** 8/10
