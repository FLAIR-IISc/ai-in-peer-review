Summary
This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for computing dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off screening costs against delay costs, accounting for patient heterogeneity, test outcomes over time, and exogenous “external information” (e.g., self-exams). The disease process is modeled as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; test outcomes and external information are conditionally independent given the (unobserved) state trajectory and features. The optimization objective is a weighted sum of discounted screening costs and delay costs that depend on both the incidence time and detection delay. The authors formulate a belief-state dynamic program and a Bellman equation where beliefs are over entire state trajectories and a diagnosis flag. Exact solution is intractable; they prove a POMDP-like piecewise-linear convexity of the value function and propose a point-based value iteration (PBVI) style approximation that (i) samples likely disease trajectories to define a reduced belief space and (ii) constructs reachable beliefs under random and guideline-based policies. They provide an approximation error bound tied to belief sampling density under strong assumptions (no false positives/negatives, bounded inter-screening window). They also give a robustness result arguing the optimal recommendation mapping is locally constant almost everywhere w.r.t. model parameters. A case study on a large de-identified breast cancer dataset (Athena, 45k women aged 60–65) shows that DPSCREEN can reduce expected screenings by 28–68% while matching the expected detection delay of annual/biennial policies, with or without self-exams. They further illustrate how different delay cost shapes (linear vs quadratic) change the aggressiveness of the optimal policy.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to POMDP planning (e.g., piecewise-linear value functions, PBVI) and healthcare screening models (e.g., POMDP-based mammography scheduling by Ayer et al., Maillart et al.; colon cancer by Erenay et al.; joint longitudinal-survival models by Rizopoulos, and guideline simulation efforts like MISCAN-COLON). The claimed novelty is to handle non-Markov disease/test processes and exogenous arrivals via external information, and to optimize trajectory-dependent delay costs. Within the POMDP literature, the approach resembles PBVI but with a trajectory-sampling–based belief reduction.

Quality
- Technical correctness: The formalization is mostly sound, and the convex piecewise-linear value function result follows standard POMDP arguments extended to beliefs over trajectories. However:
  - The Bellman equation uses a “maximize negative cost” form (V(b,t) = maxτ [−E C̃ + E V(...)]) while the objective is a minimization; please clarify the sign convention and ensure consistency across the paper.
  - The approximation bound Ω(B̄) depends on an unspecified constant ζ and requires very strong assumptions (no false positives and no false negatives, bounded screening gaps, convex delay structure). These assumptions are unrealistic for mammography and limit the interpretability of the bound. An empirical validation of approximation quality on small toy instances with exact solutions would help.
  - The robustness theorem (local constancy of optimal actions a.e.) is essentially a generic property of piecewise-linear value functions and does not quantify performance sensitivity; it is useful but limited.
- Experimental evaluation:
  - The empirical demonstration uses a 2-state Markov disease with logistic hazards and sensitivity/specificity from literature, plus a simple growth/self-exam model. This is much less general than the claimed model class. The main reduction claims are relative only to fixed periodic baselines; there is no comparison to prior personalized methods (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al., ConfidentCare (Alaa et al.), or information-gain–based adaptive schedules).
  - The approach relies on a model learned from observational data; however, there is no held-out predictive validation for incidence/detection dynamics and no sensitivity analysis beyond with/without self-exam. Since counterfactuals are unavailable, such checks are crucial.
  - It is unclear how w is tuned to match the target delay; please specify the procedure and verify that the resulting policies lie on the estimated Pareto frontier.
  - Reproducibility: key estimation details, exact hyperparameters, and code are not provided.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well organized, with appendices for proofs and algorithms. Some points need clarification:
- Clearly state the sign convention for costs vs. rewards in the Bellman equation and unify notation.
- The assumption “no false positives” appears early, while later the experiments rely on literature sensitivity/specificity; reconcile these and state what assumptions are used where (particularly in the bound vs. in practice).
- Define all variables in the Bellman equation, especially how Pr(z, y, τ̃ | b, τ, x) is constructed and how arrival processes induced by external information couple into planning.
- Several references are incomplete; please fix bibliographic details.

Originality
The problem setting—dynamic personalized screening with exogenous, patient-triggered arrivals and trajectory-dependent delay costs—is interesting and relevant. The algorithmic contribution is an adaptation of PBVI with trajectory-based belief reduction. This is a reasonable engineering of known POMDP ideas, but methodologically incremental relative to established point-based planning (e.g., Perseus, HSVI, SARSOP) and belief compression. The theoretical results (piecewise linearity and local constancy) are standard adaptations; the approximation bound is novel in spirit but rests on unrealistic assumptions.

Significance
If validated, the approach could inform individualized screening policies and reduce over-screening. The potential impact is high in healthcare operations, but the significance for the NeurIPS audience depends on methodological novelty and rigorous validation. As written, the empirical comparison set is limited, and the theoretical guarantees are weak under realistic noise.

Pros
- Addresses an important, socially relevant sequential decision problem with personalization.
- Clear problem formalization accommodating external information and trajectory-dependent delay.
- Practical PBVI-style algorithm with a thoughtful belief sampling strategy.
- Large real-world dataset; results suggest large reductions in screening burden at matched delays.
- Helpful discussion about how disease-specific delay costs affect policy aggressiveness.

Cons
- Methodological novelty over existing POMDP planning is modest; related POMDP algorithms (SARSOP, HSVI, Perseus, POMCP) are not cited or compared.
- Approximation bound requires unrealistic perfect-test assumptions; bound not instantiated numerically.
- Experiments lack comparisons to strong personalized baselines and lack rigorous model validation or sensitivity analyses.
- Claimed generality not demonstrated empirically (only 2-state Markov disease used).
- Notational/sign inconsistencies and some incomplete references.

Suggestions for improvement
- Compare against prior personalized screening policies (Ayer et al., Maillart et al., ConfidentCare) and advanced point-based POMDP planners (SARSOP, HSVI, Perseus, POMCP), or justify why they are unsuitable here.
- Provide small-scale instances where exact optimal policies are computable to assess solution quality and the effect of belief sampling density.
- Perform predictive validation and sensitivity analyses for the disease/test/external-information models; report robustness of outcomes to parameter uncertainty.
- Clarify the role of test imperfections and relax perfect-test assumptions in the theory if possible.
- Detail the tuning of w and show full trade-off curves.
- Improve clarity and fix references; consider releasing code and synthetic data.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem is important and the framework is thoughtfully constructed, but the methodological novelty is limited relative to existing POMDP planning work, and the empirical validation does not yet meet the bar for NeurIPS (missing strong baselines and robustness checks). Strengthening comparisons, validation, and theoretical assumptions would substantially improve the paper. Reviewer confidence: medium.
