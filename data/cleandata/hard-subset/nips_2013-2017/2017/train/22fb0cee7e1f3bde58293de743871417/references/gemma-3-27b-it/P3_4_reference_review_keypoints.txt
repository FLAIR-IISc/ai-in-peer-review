- Tackles personalized screening by dynamically adjusting screening frequency using patient features, screening history, and evolving disease-state beliefs.  
- Proposes DPSCREEN, a computationally tractable approximation algorithm with theoretical approximation-error bounds.  
- Demonstrates on a breast cancer dataset large reductions in screening frequency while maintaining comparable detection delays.  
- Positions work relative to POMDPs, controlled sensing, and joint longitudinal/time-to-event models, arguing they fail on trajectory-dependent delay costs and dynamic arrivals.  
- Emphasizes moving beyond Markovian and stationary-test-outcome assumptions common in prior work.  
- Comprehensive, mathematically rigorous problem formulation that incorporates external information and non-Markovian disease processes.  
- Computational tractability achieved via point-based value iteration and tailored belief-space construction.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees (Proposition 1) on approximation error.  
- Empirical validation against clinical policies is compelling and shows practical benefit.  
- Sensitivity analysis shows how different delay-cost structures (linear vs. quadratic) affect optimal policies.  
- Robustness analysis links model estimation error to approximation error (Theorem 1).  
- Limited description of the Athena Health Network dataset—needs more on data quality, biases, and missingness.  
- Relies on several modeling assumptions (e.g., conditional independence of tests, specific hazard forms) without sufficient discussion of misspecification impacts.  
- Algorithm appears complex to implement; more detailed pseudocode or a simplified implementation sketch is needed.  
- Scalability analysis is insufficient; parallelization is mentioned but larger-scale performance and complexity need deeper evaluation.  
- External information component (self-examination) is underexplored; broader incorporation of genetic/comorbidity data would strengthen the work.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem with a novel, theoretically grounded method that shows empirical gains and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical adoption may be hindered by algorithm complexity, dataset limitations, and simplifying assumptions that may not hold generally.  
- Overall assessment: strong contribution to personalized medicine and decision-making under uncertainty; strengths outweigh weaknesses.  
- Recommendation: add more dataset details, expand discussion of model assumptions, provide implementation pseudocode, and perform a more thorough scalability analysis.
