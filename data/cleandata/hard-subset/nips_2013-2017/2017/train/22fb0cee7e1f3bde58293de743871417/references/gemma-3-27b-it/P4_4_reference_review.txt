This paper proposes a dynamic personalized screening policy (DPSCREEN) for diseases, addressing limitations of existing approaches that often rely on Markovian assumptions and stationary test outcomes. The core idea is to optimally balance the frequency of testing with the delay in disease detection, personalizing this trade-off based on disease type, patient features, and screening history. The authors formulate this as a POMDP-like problem, but acknowledge the intractability of exact solutions and propose a point-based value iteration (PBVI) approximation algorithm with novel techniques for belief space construction. They validate the approach using a breast cancer dataset, demonstrating reductions in screening frequency while maintaining detection delays, and illustrate the impact of cost functions through a synthetic exercise.

The paper is well-motivated, tackling a significant problem in healthcare. The mathematical formulation is comprehensive, explicitly addressing complexities like non-Markovian disease progression, external information, and patient arrival patterns. The proposed solution, while relying on approximation, is justified with theoretical bounds on the approximation error. The application to breast cancer screening, using a real dataset, provides concrete evidence of the policy's effectiveness. The analysis of cost function impact is insightful.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The detailed description of the PBVI algorithm, while present in the supplementary material, makes the core algorithm opaque within the main text. The choice of specific parameters (e.g., ι = 0.9 for tumor growth) feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks rigorous justification, especially considering the sensitivity analysis is limited. The claim of incorporating "arbitrary" finite state stochastic processes for disease progression is somewhat overstated. The practical implementation details of the belief space construction, despite being crucial for performance, are not thoroughly explained. While the bound on approximation error is presented, the practical implications of this bound—how it translates to real-world performance—are unclear. The comparison to existing clinical policies is limited to annual vs. biennial screening. Exploring the performance against more sophisticated, risk-adjusted clinical guidelines would strengthen the evaluation. The discussion of robustness to model estimation error, while theoretically sound, doesn’t fully address the practical challenges of accurately estimating the model parameters from observational data. The writing style, while technically correct, is dense and could benefit from increased clarity and conciseness.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Addresses a complex problem with a novel framework that goes beyond standard Markovian assumptions.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is rigorous, and the approximation algorithm is supported by theoretical error bounds.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Demonstrated improvements in screening efficiency using a real-world dataset.
*   **Comprehensive Modeling:** Considers various factors impacting screening, including disease progression, patient features, and external information.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** Algorithm details are relegated to the supplement, and the writing can be dense.
*   **Parameter Justification:** Lack of rigorous justification for key parameter choices.
*   **Implementation Details:** Insufficient explanation of the belief space construction.
*   **Limited Benchmarking:** Comparison to current clinical practice could be more comprehensive.
*   **Practical Impact of Error Bound:** The theoretical error bound's practical implications are unclear.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant advancement in personalized screening policies.
*   The technical contributions are substantial and well-supported.
*   The empirical results demonstrate a clear benefit in terms of reduced screening frequency.
*   The work addresses a crucial problem with potential for broad impact on healthcare.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of clarity in the algorithm description and parameter justification hinders reproducibility and understanding.
*   The limited benchmarking and discussion of practical implementation challenges weaken the evaluation.
*   The theoretical results, while sound, may not translate directly to meaningful improvements in real-world scenarios.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of personalized medicine and screening policies. While there are weaknesses regarding clarity and practical considerations, the novelty and technical soundness of the approach are compelling. I believe the strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and thus recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity and provide more detail on the practical aspects of the proposed method. Specifically, authors should consider: 1) providing a high-level overview of the PBVI algorithm in the main text; 2) justifying the choice of key parameters; and 3) discussing the sensitivity of the results to model estimation error.
