- Proposes DPSCREEN: a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off test costs and delay-to-detection costs using belief-state planning.  
- Models allow arbitrary finite-state disease dynamics with an absorbing state, endogenous visit timing via external signals, trajectory-based delay costs, and a Bellman equation over trajectory beliefs.  
- Exact DP is intractable; authors adapt PBVI by sampling likely disease trajectories and reachable beliefs to approximate the value function.  
- Theoretical claims: piecewise-linear convexity of the value function, an approximation error bound tied to belief-space sampling density, and a robustness/local-constancy result for optimal actions under model perturbations.  
- Empirical application: two-state (healthy/diseased) model fit on a 45k-woman breast-cancer dataset with self-exam signals, showing 28–68% fewer screens while matching expected delay versus annual/biennial guidelines.  
- Convex (nonlinear) delay costs lead to more aggressive screening policies in experiments.  
- Builds on POMDP/PBVI literature and prior screening models; novelty is synthesizing trajectory-based costs and endogenous arrivals into a PBVI pipeline.  
- Strength: tackles an important practical problem and provides a clear methodological pipeline applicable to survival/first-hitting-time settings.  
- Strength: large real-world dataset and clinically relevant outcome metrics; promising empirical reductions in screening burden.  
- Strength: discusses robustness to model error and the effect of disease-dependent delay costs.  
- Weakness: theoretical guarantees rely on unrealistic assumptions (e.g., perfect tests, bounded inter-screen intervals) that conflict with experiments using imperfect tests.  
- Weakness: the approximation bound is qualitative (depends on unspecified constant ζ) and lacks finite-sample rates or practical guidance for sampling design.  
- Weakness: the “local constancy” robustness claim is essentially standard PWLC/alpha-vector behavior and adds limited novelty.  
- Weakness: approach inflates hidden state to trajectories, making belief space enormous; tractability depends on aggressive sampling but paper lacks ablations or diagnostics on sampling coverage and sensitivity to K or |B̄|.  
- Weakness: notation/sign inconsistencies in the Bellman formulation (min vs max) and limited derivation of key transition/arrival probabilities influenced by actions.  
- Weakness: empirical model is simple (two-state Markov, linear delay costs, fixed self-exam params) with no thorough model validation, uncertainty quantification, or counterfactual evaluation.  
- Weakness: inconsistent assumptions across sections about test errors (false positives/negatives) that need reconciliation.  
- Weakness: limited direct empirical comparisons to prior personalized screening or joint longitudinal-survival baselines beyond annual/biennial rules.  
- Quality: technically coherent at a high level, but proofs mirror standard POMDP results or rest on restrictive assumptions; empirical evaluation is promising but under-validated.  
- Clarity: generally readable but defers key derivations to the supplement; needs clarification on Bellman signs, probability constructions, complexity, and test-error assumptions.  
- Originality: moderate—useful synthesis of known ideas but incremental theoretical contributions.  
- Significance: potentially important for personalized screening but current empirical evidence insufficient for strong clinical claims.  
- Suggestions: resolve test-accuracy assumptions, relax Prop.1 for realistic sensitivity/specificity, and provide empirical/theoretical bounds under realistic noise.  
- Suggestions: add derivations for Eq.(3) and transition probabilities, fix sign/notation issues, and include a worked example.  
- Suggestions: include ablations on K and |B̄|, runtime/memory scaling, and policy stability vs sampling density; quantify Ω(B̄) empirically.  
- Suggestions: propagate parameter uncertainty via bootstrap or Bayesian posterior, report CIs, compare to prior POMDP screening baselines, validate on semi-synthetic data, and analyze subgroup/fairness impacts.  
- Recommendation: borderline reject—important problem and promising approach, but limited theoretical novelty and insufficient empirical rigor/validation.
