- Summary: DPSCREEN proposes dynamic personalized screening by modeling disease as a finite-state stochastic process, using beliefs over state trajectories, deriving Bellman equations and PWL value structure, and a PBVI-like approximation with bounds and robustness; applied to breast cancer data showing 28–68% fewer screens at similar delays.

- Strength: Tackles an ambitious, relevant problem unifying personalized dynamic screening with endogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent delays.

- Strength: Conceptually general—beliefs over trajectories allow non-Markov processes and nonstationary observations, incorporating external info like self-exams.

- Strength: Theoretically extends POMDP convexity/piecewise-linearity and proposes a PBVI-style algorithm for very high-dimensional beliefs via trajectory sampling.

- Strength: Practical focus with a clear objective, real clinical data, and demonstrated personalization by risk group and history.

- Strength: Provides robustness intuition—local constancy of optimal actions suggesting stability to small model errors.

- Weakness: Bellman sign and objective are inconsistent (maximization vs minimization) and C̃ mixes immediate/terminal delay costs risking double-counting; needs formal clarification.

- Weakness: Belief-space approximation over sampled trajectories is unclear—uncertain if solving a projected POMDP or importance-weighted approximation and whether alpha-vectors/policy apply to the original model.

- Weakness: Approximation bound (Prop.1) relies on perfect tests, convex delay, bounded intervals, large T, and undefined ζ; lacks applicability to realistic imperfect tests and no method to compute/bound ζ.

- Weakness: Robustness theorem is abstract without precise regularity/uniqueness conditions, proof sketch, or measure-zero clarification for set E.

- Weakness: Independence assumptions (Z ⟂ Y | trajectory; conditional independence over time) may be unrealistic; need discussion or sensitivity analysis for violations.

- Empirical weakness: Baselines limited to annual/biennial schedules; missing comparisons to prior POMDP-based personalized screening work.

- Empirical weakness: Main experiment uses a two-state Markov model despite claims of non-Markov generality; needs an experiment exploiting non-Markov aspects.

- Empirical weakness: Outcome metrics omit clinically relevant measures (false positives, benign biopsies, stage at detection, QALYs); current metrics insufficient.

- Empirical weakness: Large gains hinge on self-exam modeling with parameters from literature; requires sensitivity analyses over those parameters and exam frequency.

- Empirical weakness: Missing reproducibility details and ablations—hyperparameters (K,B,W), sampling strategy, runtime, parallelization, and sensitivity analyses not reported.

- Positioning weakness: Overclaims novelty; needs clearer placement relative to POMDP screening, Bayesian design, ConfidentCare, and at least one empirical comparison.

- Positioning weakness: Inconsistent statements about false positives—clarify that “no false positives” refers to diagnostic confirmation, not preliminary tests.

- Pro for acceptance: Addresses an important societal problem with a principled sequential decision framework and practical approximation.

- Pro for acceptance: Extends POMDP structure to endogenous-arrival settings with promising empirical reductions in screening burden.

- Con for acceptance: Theoretical guarantees depend on restrictive assumptions and are not aligned with experiments.

- Con for acceptance: Limited baselines and empirical validation do not yet show superiority over prior personalized screening models.

- Con for acceptance: Several clarity issues (Bellman sign, ζ definition, reduced belief formalism) and missing implementation details hinder evaluation.

- Recommendation: Weak reject — promising and thought-provoking but requires stronger empirical comparisons, clarified/justified theory aligned with experiments, and more reproducibility detail.
