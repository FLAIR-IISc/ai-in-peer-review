This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for dynamic personalized screening policies, aiming to balance the trade-off between screening costs and the delay in disease detection. The approach tackles several complexities inherent in real-world screening scenarios: non-Markovian disease progression, patient-specific risk factors, external information impacting arrival times, and computational intractability of optimal policy derivation. The authors propose a computationally efficient algorithm with approximation error bounds, demonstrating its efficacy through a breast cancer dataset and synthetic exercises across different cost functions.

The core contribution lies in extending existing screening models to accommodate greater flexibility in disease modeling and patient characteristics. Compared to prior work (e.g., [7, 9, 10]), which often rely on Markovian assumptions and stationary test outcome models, DPSCREEN’s generality is a significant strength.  The framework incorporates external information, which is a crucial aspect of real-world patient behavior often ignored in theoretical models, and provides a pathway to approximate optimality even for complex disease dynamics.  The demonstration on breast cancer screening, showcasing substantial reductions in screening frequency without compromising detection delay, is compelling. The exploration of different cost functions also highlights the importance of tailoring policies to the specific disease and clinical priorities.

**Strengths:**

*   **Generality:** The model is highly flexible, accommodating a wide range of disease progression models (dynamic survival, first-hitting time) and external information sources. This is a major step forward compared to the restrictive assumptions of earlier work.
*   **Personalization:** The approach explicitly incorporates patient features and dynamic test history to personalize screening schedules, leading to more efficient resource allocation. The presented results on risk-based screening intervals are particularly convincing.
*   **Computational Tractability:** Despite the complexity of the problem, the authors propose a computationally feasible algorithm (PBVI-based) with provable approximation error bounds.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The application to breast cancer screening, using a real-world dataset, demonstrates the potential impact of the framework in clinical practice. The comparison with existing clinical policies provides a valuable benchmark.
*   **Thoroughness:** The paper addresses important considerations like estimation error and provides a theoretical guarantee on the robustness of the proposed policies. The inclusion of different cost functions and the impact of self-examination enhance the completeness of the analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Model Complexity:** While generality is a strength, the richness of the model can also make it challenging to implement and interpret. The many parameters and assumptions require careful consideration and validation.
*   **Estimation Assumptions:** The model estimation process (using the Gail model and MCMC methods) relies on several assumptions about the underlying distributions and relationships. A sensitivity analysis evaluating the impact of these assumptions on the policy performance would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the breast cancer dataset is valuable, the results are somewhat limited by the characteristics of the data. Exploring the performance of the framework on other diseases and datasets would strengthen the findings.
*   **Clarity in Algorithm Presentation:**  Algorithm 1, concerning the construction of the belief space, could be clarified. The criteria for sampling trajectories and beliefs appear somewhat heuristic, and more detail would enhance understanding.  The connection between the sampling density (Ω(B̄)) and the practical performance of the algorithm is also not entirely clear.
*   **Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the authors mention computational complexity, a more detailed discussion of the practical limitations and scalability of the algorithm for large-scale datasets would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in healthcare decision-making with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The framework’s generality and personalization capabilities represent a substantial advancement over existing methods.
*   The empirical results on breast cancer screening are promising and demonstrate the potential for real-world impact.
*   The inclusion of approximation error bounds and robustness analysis adds to the rigor of the work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The model complexity and estimation assumptions require careful consideration and further validation.
*   The limited empirical validation raises questions about the generalizability of the findings.
*   Some aspects of the algorithm presentation could be clarified for better understanding.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential to advance the field of personalized screening. The proposed framework is technically sound, theoretically justified, and practically relevant. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the work clearly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should consider: (1) Providing a more detailed sensitivity analysis of the model assumptions. (2)  Expanding the empirical validation to include additional datasets and diseases. (3) Clarifying the sampling strategy in Algorithm 1 and discussing the practical implications of the sampling density. (4)  Elaborating on the computational cost and scalability of the algorithm for large datasets.
