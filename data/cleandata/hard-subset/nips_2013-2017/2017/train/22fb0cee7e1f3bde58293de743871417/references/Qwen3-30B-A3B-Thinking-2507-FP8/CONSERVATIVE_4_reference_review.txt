

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

This paper proposes a method called DPSCREEN for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against delay in disease detection. The authors claim to address limitations in existing approaches by handling non-stationary disease processes, incorporating external information (like patient self-examinations), and developing a computationally tractable algorithm for the optimal policy. The method is framed as a generalization of POMDPs (Partially Observable Markov Decision Processes) and is evaluated on breast cancer screening data.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper builds on standard POMDP formulations for screening problems, which have been explored in prior work such as Ayer et al. (2013) and Maillart et al. (2008). The authors claim their method is the first to handle non-stationary disease processes and incorporate external information, but they don't sufficiently demonstrate how their approach differs from standard POMDP methods that have been successfully applied to similar problems. The core algorithm (DPSCREEN) appears to be a standard point-based value iteration approach for POMDPs, which has been well-established in the literature for decades (e.g., Pineau et al., 2006, 2008).

## Strengths

1. The paper clearly defines the screening problem and provides a comprehensive mathematical formulation of the disease process, external information, and screening costs.

2. The model is well-motivated, and the authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches for handling non-stationary disease processes.

3. The paper provides a clear approximation error bound (Proposition 1), which is an important theoretical contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core contribution is essentially a standard application of POMDP techniques to a medical screening problem. The authors claim to handle non-stationary processes, but the evaluation is restricted to a two-state Markov model (breast cancer progression), which is stationary by definition. The paper fails to demonstrate how their method handles truly non-stationary processes as claimed in the introduction.

2. **Inadequate Baseline Comparison:** The evaluation compares against annual screening (a basic clinical policy), but fails to compare against state-of-the-art personalized screening methods. Previous work by Ayer et al. (2013) already developed personalized screening policies using POMDPs, and the authors do not compare against these methods. The claimed 28-68% reduction in screenings is impressive, but only meaningful if compared against current personalized approaches, not against basic annual screening.

3. **Overstated Claims:** The paper claims to be "the first such personalized screening policies in a very general setting" and "applicable to many disease models," but the evaluation is limited to breast cancer with a specific model. The synthetic exercise with different delay cost functions (linear vs. quadratic) is superficial and doesn't demonstrate the claimed generality.

4. **Insufficient Validation:** The paper doesn't address whether the proposed policy would be practical in clinical settings. For instance, it doesn't discuss how the policy would handle real-world constraints like patient adherence to screening schedules, implementation complexity, or how clinicians would interpret the personalized recommendations.

5. **Theoretical Contribution Lacking:** The approximation error bound (Proposition 1) is a standard result in POMDP literature and isn't novel. The paper fails to provide any new theoretical insights about the structure of the screening problem.

## Assessment Against Review Criteria

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound but makes unsubstantiated claims about novelty and generality. The experimental validation is insufficient as it doesn't compare against relevant baselines or demonstrate the claimed generality across different disease models.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with a comprehensive mathematical formulation. However, the lack of proper comparison against relevant prior work and the overstatement of novelty make it difficult to assess the actual contribution.

**Originality:** The contribution is not significantly novel. The method is a standard application of existing POMDP techniques to a medical problem. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how it differs from previous work on personalized screening using POMDPs.

**Significance:** The problem of personalized screening is important, but the paper doesn't demonstrate that their approach provides significant improvements over existing methods. The claimed reductions in screenings are impressive but not validated against state-of-the-art personalized screening policies.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the problem is important and the formulation is clear, the paper fails to demonstrate a meaningful contribution beyond standard POMDP applications. The claims of novelty and generality are overstated, and the evaluation doesn't properly validate these claims against relevant baselines. The paper would be better suited for a medical or operations research venue rather than NIPS, as it lacks a significant contribution to the machine learning or AI field.

The authors should either:
1) Provide a proper comparison against existing personalized screening methods using POMDPs (e.g., Ayer et al. 2013)
2) Demonstrate the method on a truly non-stationary disease model (not just a two-state Markov model)
3) Provide a more rigorous theoretical analysis of how their approach differs from standard POMDP methods

Without these improvements, the paper does not make a significant contribution to the field of machine learning or AI.
