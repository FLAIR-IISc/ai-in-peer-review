This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for personalized screening policies, aiming to optimize the trade-off between screening costs and delays in disease detection. The approach is significant because it tackles limitations of existing screening models by accommodating non-Markovian disease processes, dynamic test outcomes, external information, and computationally intractable optimization problems. The authors propose a computationally effective procedure with approximation error bounds and demonstrate its efficacy using a breast cancer dataset, achieving substantial reductions in screening frequency while maintaining detection delays comparable to current clinical practices.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the shortcomings of existing screening policies and outlining the complexities of developing personalized strategies. The model formulation is general, capable of encompassing various disease dynamics and incorporating external information—a notable strength. The proposed solution, combining point-based value iteration with tailored belief space construction, addresses the computational challenges effectively. The illustrative experiments on breast cancer are convincing, demonstrating substantial improvements over standard screening protocols. Furthermore, the discussion highlighting the impact of cost function selection on policy design adds valuable insight.

However, several aspects require attention. While the paper acknowledges the complexity of estimating model parameters from observational data, the specific estimation procedures used (described in the supplementary material) could benefit from more detailed justification and comparison with alternative approaches.  The assumption of no false positives simplifies the model; discussing the potential impact of incorporating false positives would strengthen the analysis. The discussion on robustness, while present, feels somewhat abstract. Providing concrete examples of how model misspecification might affect policy performance would be beneficial. 

The paper’s clarity is generally good, but some sections, particularly those detailing the mathematical formulations (e.g., Bellman equations, Lemma 1), are dense and could be made more accessible with additional explanations or intermediate steps. While the appendix provides details, integrating key equations and intuitions into the main text would enhance readability. The large number of citations, while comprehensive, sometimes interrupts the flow of the text.

The originality of the work lies in its comprehensive approach to personalized screening, unifying several crucial aspects often treated separately. This is not merely a combination of existing techniques; the development of the point-based value iteration algorithm tailored to this specific problem demonstrates a novel contribution.  The related work section is thorough, positioning the work effectively within the broader literature.

The significance of this work is considerable. Personalized screening has the potential to dramatically improve healthcare efficiency and patient outcomes. The presented framework offers a principled and flexible approach to designing such policies. The demonstrated reductions in screening frequency, while maintaining detection performance, suggest substantial cost savings and reduced patient burden. The sensitivity analysis regarding the cost function highlights the importance of tailoring screening strategies to specific diseases.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practically important problem with significant potential impact.
*   Presents a novel and technically sound framework for personalized screening.
*   Demonstrates compelling results using a real-world dataset.
*   Handles complexities of non-Markovian processes, external information, and computational challenges.
*   Provides theoretical justification for the approximation approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Parameter estimation details could be more thoroughly justified.
*   The assumption of no false positives is a simplification.
*   Robustness analysis could be more concrete.
*   Mathematical formulations could be more accessible.
*   The paper is dense and could benefit from clearer explanations in key sections.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of personalized screening. While some aspects could be refined, the novelty, technical soundness, and potential impact of the work are compelling. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the clarity and robustness concerns mentioned above. Specifically, I would suggest:

1.  Expanding the discussion of model parameter estimation, including a comparison with alternative methods.
2.  Adding a section discussing the potential impact of incorporating false positives into the model.
3.  Providing concrete examples illustrating the sensitivity of the policy to model misspecification.
4.  Rewriting certain sections to improve readability and accessibility, particularly those containing complex mathematical formulations.
