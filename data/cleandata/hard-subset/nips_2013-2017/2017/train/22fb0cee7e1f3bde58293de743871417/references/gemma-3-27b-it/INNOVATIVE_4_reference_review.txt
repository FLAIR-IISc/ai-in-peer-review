This paper tackles a crucial and challenging problem: personalized screening policies that dynamically adapt to patient features, test results, and external information. The core idea – formulating this as a POMDP-like problem and developing an approximately optimal solution via point-based value iteration – is genuinely novel and ambitious. While the methodological details are complex, the authors successfully navigate a difficult landscape, proposing a framework applicable to various disease models beyond the limitations of prior work which often rely on Markovian assumptions. The illustrative experiments with breast cancer data are compelling, demonstrating substantial reductions in screening frequency while maintaining equivalent detection delays. The exploration of cost function impacts is also a valuable addition.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach is a significant departure from existing screening frameworks, addressing limitations inherent in Markovian models and static policies. The ability to handle non-stationary processes and external information is a major advance.
*   **Generality:** The framework is remarkably general, accommodating diverse disease models (survival, first-hitting time) and cost structures.
*   **Practical Impact:** The demonstrated reduction in screening frequency (28-68%) with maintained detection delays is a compelling result with clear clinical relevance.
*   **Theoretical Grounding:**  The paper includes a discussion of approximation error bounds and robustness to model estimation errors, which is commendable, even if the bounds require specific assumptions.
*   **Personalization:** The clear demonstration of how the policy adapts to both patient risk factors and dynamic test results highlights the personalization aspect.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** The computational complexity, while acknowledged, remains a concern. While parallelization is mentioned, the scaling to very large patient populations and complex disease models isn’t fully addressed.
*   **Model Assumptions:** The reliance on accurate estimation of disease progression and test characteristics is critical. The paper touches on robustness, but a more detailed sensitivity analysis to estimation errors would be beneficial.
*   **External Information Modeling:**  The modeling of external information (lump size) seems somewhat simplified.  Real-world external information is often noisy and incomplete.
*   **Limited Validation:** While the breast cancer example is strong, broader validation across different disease types and datasets would increase confidence in the framework's generalizability.
*   **Clarity of Algorithm 1:** The description of Algorithm 1 (belief construction) could be more detailed, especially regarding the selection of 'typical' trajectories.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a fundamentally new approach to a critical problem.
*   The theoretical framework is well-developed and offers a path toward optimal policy construction.
*   The experimental results are promising and demonstrate substantial potential benefits.
*   The authors acknowledge and address limitations, indicating a realistic assessment of their work.
*   The generality of the approach makes it applicable to a wide range of healthcare scenarios.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity could limit practical application in real-world settings.
*   The reliance on accurate model estimation introduces potential vulnerabilities.
*   The limited validation, while strong in one domain, doesn’t fully establish generalizability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a highly innovative and potentially impactful contribution to the field of personalized medicine. While some methodological details require further refinement and the computational complexity is a concern, the novelty and significance of the work outweigh these weaknesses. The authors have tackled a truly difficult problem with a bold and insightful approach. I believe this paper represents a significant step forward and warrants acceptance, despite the areas for improvement. It sets a new direction for research in personalized screening and has the potential to improve healthcare outcomes substantially. I would rate it as a strong accept.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
