Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a general framework for dynamic, personalized medical screening under partial observability with endogenous visit times and trajectory-dependent costs. Key ingredients:
- Disease evolution: arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state (not restricted to Markov or semi-Markov).
- External information: a patient-level exogenous signal (e.g., self-exam) that can trigger early arrival, modeled as a stochastic process correlated with latent disease trajectory.
- Objective: trade off discounted screening cost against trajectory-dependent delay cost.
- Solution approach: formulate Bellman equations over belief distributions on state trajectories and diagnosis labels; prove the value function is piecewise-linear convex in beliefs; compute approximately optimal policies via a point-based value iteration (PBVI) variant that samples likely trajectories and reachable beliefs; provide an approximation bound (via a sampling-density measure) and a robustness result (optimal action is locally constant in model-belief space off a lower-dimensional set).

The empirical study learns a breast-cancer model from a large observational dataset (~45k patients, ~84k mammograms), incorporates a self-exam–based arrival process via a tumor growth model, and reports 28–68% expected reductions in number of screens versus annual screening for matched expected delays. A synthetic experiment illustrates how different delay-cost shapes (linear vs. quadratic) change the operating regime.

Compared to prior screening models in OR/medical literature (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Erenay et al., Rizopoulos et al.), DPSCREEN relaxes common assumptions (Markov/semi-Markov disease, stationary test processes, fixed decision epochs, state-only costs) and goes beyond simulation-based policy comparison by solving a dynamic program (approximately) over beliefs. Methodologically, it builds on POMDP ideas (PWLC value functions; PBVI) but adapts them to trajectory-based costs and endogenous decision times.

Strengths
- Originality: Poses a bold, generalized screening POMDP with trajectory-dependent delay costs and endogenous arrivals; the adaptation of PWLC structure and PBVI to this setting is novel and intellectually appealing.
- Theoretical backing: PWLC extension, an explicit PBVI-style algorithm with a principled belief/trajectory sampling scheme, an approximation bound tied to belief coverage, and a robustness argument that actions are locally constant except on a measure-zero set.
- Practical relevance and significance: Screening is societally important; the framework accommodates external information and different disease types/cost structures, providing a unifying view across conditions.
- Empirical illustration at scale: Large breast-cancer dataset; clear personalization behavior; sizable reductions in screening burden at fixed expected delay. The cost-shape sensitivity study is insightful for clinical policy design.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Validation scope: While the dataset is large, the evaluation relies on a learned generative model to assess policies (standard but limited). No uncertainty quantification, confidence intervals, or sensitivity analyses (e.g., to mis-specification of tumor growth/self-exam parameters) are reported. Comparisons are primarily to clinical heuristics (annual/biennial), not to prior personalized methods (e.g., Ayer/Maillart POMDPs or joint-model–based screening). Even a restricted comparison would strengthen claims.
- Assumptions in theory vs. practice: The approximation bound assumes perfect tests and convex delay costs, and uses a constant ζ whose magnitude and computability are unclear. In practice, tests are imperfect and the breast-cancer model uses mammogram sensitivity/specificity; bridging the gap (or empirically studying error vs. sampling density) would increase credibility.
- Clarity and completeness: Some notational and editorial issues (e.g., “st Conference … NIPS 2017” artifact), and several details deferred to the supplement (algorithms, proofs). The complexity expression O(T(B)2T2K|Y||Z|) is hard to parse (potential typo). The assumption “no false positives” appears earlier, yet experiments include specificity/sensitivity; if positivity requires biopsy confirmation this should be clarified to resolve the apparent mismatch.
- Modeling choices in the experiment: Although the framework supports non-Markov disease, the breast-cancer instantiation uses a two-state Markov model; this weakens the empirical demonstration of the claimed generality. The self-exam arrival model is stylized; parameter estimation from literature rather than data may bias conclusions.
- Reproducibility: No mention of code or model release.

Quality
Conceptually sound and generally careful; the DP formulation and PWLC property are appropriate, and the robustness theorem is consistent with PWLC structure. The approximation bound is helpful but rests on strong assumptions and an abstract constant; more empirical diagnostics (e.g., value residuals vs. belief coverage, K/B sensitivity) would improve rigor.

Clarity
Mostly clear, but dense. The paper would benefit from:
- Explicitly stating the action space restriction used in practice (e.g., max follow-up interval) and its effect on complexity.
- A concise schematic of the belief/trajectory sampling pipeline and how B̄ grows over iterations.
- Clearer reconciliation of test accuracy assumptions between theory and experiments.

Originality
High. The generalization from standard POMDP screening to trajectory-dependent costs and endogenous arrivals, plus the PBVI adaptation with sampling over likely trajectories, is a meaningful conceptual advance.

Significance
High potential impact. If adopted, DPSCREEN could materially reduce screening burden without sacrificing detection timeliness across diseases, and offers a platform to explore disease-specific cost structures.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines from prior personalized screening work on a common disease model, even if simplified, to contextualize gains beyond clinical heuristics.
- Provide uncertainty bars and sensitivity analyses (to learned hazard parameters, tumor growth/self-exam noise, weight w, action-space constraints).
- Report wall-clock time, parallelization details, and scaling with K and |B̄|.
- Empirically probe the approximation error: vary sampling density and show performance/value convergence.
- Clarify the test model: whether diagnosis requires biopsy positivity (hence effectively no false positives at the “diagnosis” decision).
- Fix typos and polish references.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning accept). The paper introduces a bold, general framework that extends the POMDP toolkit to a class of clinically important screening problems with endogenous information and trajectory-based costs, and it demonstrates promising benefits on a large dataset. Despite some evaluation and clarity gaps, the conceptual novelty and potential impact justify acceptance at NIPS.
