- Introduces DPSCREEN: a framework for personalized screening balancing screening costs and delayed detection using PBVI approximation with error bounds; validated on breast cancer showing fewer screenings with similar delays.

- Positions the work relative to POMDP/POSMDP and simulation-based medical approaches, highlighting ability to derive policies and citing relevant literature.

- Novelty/significance: personalizes screening using patient features and dynamic disease/test history.

- Generality: accommodates various disease models (dynamic survival, first-hitting time) and external information processes.

- Computational tractability: uses a practical PBVI approximation with provable error bounds.

- Empirical validation: breast cancer results show substantial screening reductions without compromising detection delays.

- Robustness: analyzes estimation error effects and provides theoretical guarantees.

- Clear motivation and framing in the introduction.

- Weakness: Requires significant model specification; assumptions (e.g., no false positives) may limit applicability and need sensitivity analysis.

- Weakness: Lacks detailed discussion of practical computational burden and scalability (states, patients, horizon).

- Weakness: Limited exploration of delay/cost functions; synthetic exercise too brief.

- Weakness: Empirical evaluation restricted to breast cancer; needs broader disease validation.

- Weakness: External information model (lump size) oversimplified compared to real-world self-exams.

- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, is theoretically sound and tractable, shows compelling empirical results, and is well-written.

- Arguments against acceptance: simplifying assumptions, potential scalability barriers, and single-disease evaluation.

- Overall assessment: strong, significant contribution; strengths outweigh weaknesses; recommend accept with minor revisions.

- Suggested improvements: add sensitivity analysis, provide empirical runtimes/scalability details, expand cost-function experiments, simulate another disease, and refine external information modeling.
