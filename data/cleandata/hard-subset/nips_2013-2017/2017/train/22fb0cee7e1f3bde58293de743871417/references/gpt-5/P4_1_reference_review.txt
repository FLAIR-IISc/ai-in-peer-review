Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for designing dynamic, personalized screening policies that balance screening costs and delay costs across diseases and patients, while allowing the policy to adapt over time to patient-specific history and exogenous information (e.g., self-exams). The disease process is modeled as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; test outcomes and external information are stochastic and may depend on disease trajectory and patient features. Policies map a patient’s feature vector and observed history to the next screening date or a diagnosis decision.

The authors formulate a belief-state dynamic program over distributions on state trajectories and a diagnosis label, show that the finite-horizon value function is convex and piecewise linear (as in POMDPs), and characterize the exactly optimal solution via alpha-vectors, which is intractable due to the belief space dimension |S|^T. They propose a tractable point-based method: sample “typical” disease trajectories, generate reachable beliefs via random and clinically motivated policy rollouts, and perform point-based value backups to compute an approximately optimal policy. They provide a qualitative performance bound: the suboptimality is upper-bounded by a term proportional to the maximum L1 distance from any reachable belief to the sampled belief set. They also show that the optimal action map is locally constant almost everywhere in model-parameter/belief space, implying robustness to small estimation errors.

Experiments instantiate a two-state breast cancer model on a de-identified dataset (45k women, 84k mammograms). Patient risk is computed via the Gail model; mammogram sensitivity/specificity are from prior work; external information is a self-exam “lump size” process following a growth model with noise. Policies are tuned to match the expected delay of current clinical practice (annual or biennial), then compared on expected number of tests and delays. DPSCREEN reportedly reduces screening by 28–68% while maintaining comparable expected delay; policies differ across risk groups and are more aggressive under convex (quadratic) delay costs.

The work relates to POMDP-based screening formulations (e.g., Ayer et al. for mammography; Maillart et al.; Erenay et al. for colonoscopy; Rizopoulos et al. for joint longitudinal–survival models), and to controlled sensing/POMDP structural results. Compared to prior screening models, DPSCREEN targets broader disease/test/external-information models and explicitly handles endogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent delay costs.

Strengths
- Generality of the formulation: allows non-Markovian disease processes (captured via trajectory beliefs), endogenous arrivals driven by both scheduled follow-ups and external signals, and delay costs that depend on incidence time and lag.
- Clear reduction to a belief-state DP with a piecewise-linear convex finite-horizon value function, enabling the use of alpha-vector methods.
- Practical approximation via trajectory- and belief-sampling, with PBVI-style backups; the approach is natural and scalable relative to the full problem.
- Robustness observation (local constancy of optimal actions) aligns with standard POMDP geometry and supports the claim that small parameter errors rarely flip decisions.
- Empirical illustration on a sizeable dataset with interpretable gains (large reductions in screenings under matched-delay constraints), and personalization by risk group and dynamic history.
- Sensible exploration of how the delay cost shape (linear vs quadratic) affects recommended screening frequency, highlighting disease-specific policy design.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality/technical soundness
- Approximation guarantee is high-level. The bound depends on Ω(B̄) with an unspecified constant ζ and no link between sample sizes (K, |B̄|) and Ω. No finite-sample guidance or empirical verification of convergence/stability is provided. Suggest quantifying ζ, relating K and |B̄| to covering numbers, or providing empirical error curves.
- Assumptions for Proposition 1 (no false positives and no false negatives, bounded inter-screening interval, large T) are restrictive and not aligned with the breast cancer experiment (which uses realistic sensitivity/specificity and includes mammogram false positives). Clarify which results hold under realistic error models or extend the bound accordingly.
- The Bellman equation includes Pr(z, y, τ̃ | b, τ, x) but the induced arrival-time distribution under endogenous arrivals is not fully specified; a formal derivation of the observation/arrival kernel, and how it depends on the recommendation plan and threshold-crossing external process, would improve rigor.
- Theorem 1 (local constancy) is intuitive but informal: the structure of model space M, continuity assumptions, and proof sketch are deferred. Adding a short, self-contained statement with conditions (e.g., finiteness and non-degeneracy of alpha-vector maxima) would strengthen the result.
- Complexity claims (O(T(B)2 T^2 K|Y||Z|)) lack derivation, and no runtime/memory results are reported. Include computational benchmarks and ablations (e.g., effect of K, |B̄|, action-space restrictions).

Clarity
- Notation and exposition are dense; some definitions (e.g., Z+, diagnosis rule vs. “no false positives”) could be clarified. Consider a clean, self-contained description of the generative model for arrivals/tests, and a small running example to illustrate belief updates.
- References are inconsistently formatted and some are incomplete.

Originality
- The core computational idea adapts PBVI to a trajectory-belief space via sampling, which is a reasonable extension rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic contribution. The novelty lies more in the general problem formulation (trajectory-dependent costs, endogenous arrivals, external info) than in algorithmic techniques.

Significance
- The empirical gains are promising but rely entirely on a learned generative model. No uncertainty quantification, sensitivity analyses, or validation against alternative disease models are provided. Include:
  - Confidence intervals over simulated performance via posterior sampling or bootstrap.
  - Sensitivity to key parameters (e.g., self-exam accuracy, growth rate ι, threshold ỹ).
  - Comparisons to stronger baselines (e.g., optimized threshold policies, existing risk- and density-based interval guidelines, or POMDPs tailored to two-state Markov models without trajectory beliefs).
  - Reporting of false positives/biopsies and harm-benefit metrics beyond delay and test count.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, practically relevant problem with a general and principled framework.
- Sound DP formulation with appropriate use of POMDP tools; logical robustness property.
- Practical approximation method with plausible scalability and notable empirical improvements.
- Highlights disease-specific tailoring via cost-shape, an important design dimension.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical guarantees are qualitative and rely on restrictive assumptions not met in experiments; no sample-complexity guidance.
- Endogenous arrival/observation model is underspecified in the main text; derivations are opaque.
- Experimental validation lacks uncertainty quantification, sensitivity analyses, and comparisons to strong baselines; claims rely on a single disease model fit from observational data.
- Limited evidence that the added generality is necessary for the instantiated experiment, which uses a simple two-state Markov model.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles a timely and impactful problem with a thoughtful, general formulation and a practical PBVI-style approximation, and presents encouraging empirical results. To meet NeurIPS standards, the authors should strengthen the technical development (clarify the observation/arrival model, tighten the approximation analysis with realistic test errors, and provide computational evidence) and deepen the empirical evaluation (uncertainty, sensitivity, and stronger baselines). Pending these, the contribution is promising but not yet fully substantiated.
