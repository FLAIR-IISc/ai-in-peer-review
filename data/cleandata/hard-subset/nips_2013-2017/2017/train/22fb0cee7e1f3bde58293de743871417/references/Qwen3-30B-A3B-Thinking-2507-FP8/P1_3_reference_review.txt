

# Peer Review: DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

**Summary:**
This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for developing personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against delays in disease detection. The authors address key challenges in personalized screening: (1) disease progression varies significantly across diseases (not restricted to Markov processes), (2) the cost of delay is unknown, (3) patients may arrive early based on external information, and (4) optimal policy computation is computationally intractable. The authors model disease progression as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporate external information (e.g., patient self-examinations), and develop a computationally efficient approximation algorithm based on point-based value iteration. Evaluated on a breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients), DPSCREEN achieves 28-68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining equivalent expected delays compared to current clinical policies. The approach extends beyond previous work [7,9,10] by not restricting to Markov disease processes or stationary test outcome models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant contribution by developing a framework that handles a much broader class of disease models than previous work. The theoretical foundation (Lemma 1, Proposition 1) provides strong justification for the approximation approach, with a provable error bound.

2. **Real-World Validation:** The breast cancer case study is comprehensive and well-executed, using a large real-world dataset with appropriate model estimation techniques (Gail model, MCMC). The results demonstrate substantial practical improvements over current clinical policies.

3. **Personalization:** The paper clearly demonstrates how the approach personalizes screening based on patient features (risk groups) and dynamic history (test results, external information), which is a major advancement over current one-size-fits-all clinical policies.

4. **Theoretical Guarantees:** The approximation error bound (Proposition 1) is particularly valuable for justifying the practical use of their approach and is well-supported by the theoretical analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Implementation Details:** While the algorithm is described, there's insufficient detail on how the belief space is reduced (Step 2 in Algorithm 1). The paper mentions "sampling trajectories that are more likely to occur," but without specific methods for identifying and prioritizing these trajectories, it's difficult to assess practical feasibility.

2. **Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The comparison with clinical policies is strong, but the paper doesn't sufficiently compare with recent advanced methods like ConfidentCare [ConfidentCare: A Clinical Decision Support System for Personalized Breast Cancer Screening] which also addresses personalized screening. A more thorough comparison would strengthen the contribution claim.

3. **Model Assumptions:** The paper makes simplifying assumptions (no false positives, linear delay costs) that may not hold in all real-world scenarios. While the synthetic exercise with quadratic costs partially addresses this, a more comprehensive discussion of how the approach would handle false positives or other deviations from assumptions would be valuable.

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis (Lemma 1, Proposition 1) provides strong theoretical grounding for the approximation approach, and the empirical validation on a substantial breast cancer dataset is convincing. The authors are honest about limitations and assumptions.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, though some technical sections (e.g., the Bellman equation formulation) might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with POMDPs. The graphical representations (Figure 1, Figure 2) are particularly helpful for understanding the personalization aspect. The supplementary materials provide necessary details for reproducibility.

**Originality:**
The work significantly extends previous screening policy approaches by handling non-Markovian disease processes and incorporating external information in a general way. While related to POMDP work [18,19], this is the first application to personalized screening with the specific challenges described. The combination of disease modeling with decision theory in this context represents a novel contribution to healthcare applications.

**Significance:**
This work has substantial potential impact in healthcare, where personalized screening could reduce costs and patient burden while maintaining or improving health outcomes. The 28-68% reduction in screenings is clinically meaningful and would have significant implications for healthcare systems. The approach could be extended to other diseases beyond breast cancer, as demonstrated in the synthetic exercise with different delay cost functions.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong technical contribution to personalized healthcare with clear theoretical foundations, practical implementation, and significant empirical results. The limitations discussed are reasonable and don't undermine the main contribution. The work is highly relevant to the NeurIPS community as it applies advanced decision theory to a significant real-world problem.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**
*Pro:* Significant technical contribution to a real-world healthcare problem, strong theoretical foundation, clear empirical validation showing substantial improvements over current practice.
*Con:* Some implementation details could be more specific, and the comparison with the most recent related work (ConfidentCare) could be expanded.

**Overall:**
DPSCREEN presents a compelling and well-executed approach to personalized screening that advances the state of the art in healthcare decision making. The method's ability to handle complex disease models while maintaining computational tractability is particularly impressive. The empirical results on breast cancer data provide strong evidence of the method's practical value, and the extension to other diseases via the synthetic exercise demonstrates the framework's generality. This work would be a valuable contribution to the NeurIPS community, bridging the gap between advanced decision theory and real-world healthcare applications.
