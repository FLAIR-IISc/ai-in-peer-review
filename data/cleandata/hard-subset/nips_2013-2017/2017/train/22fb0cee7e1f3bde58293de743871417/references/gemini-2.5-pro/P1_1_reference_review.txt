### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for deriving dynamic and personalized screening policies for diseases. The core idea is to formalize the screening problem as a sequential decision-making process that optimally trades off the cost of screening against the cost of delayed disease detection. The framework is highly general, accommodating non-Markovian disease progression models, non-stationary test outcomes, and external information (e.g., patient-reported symptoms) that can trigger unscheduled appointments.

The problem is formulated to minimize a weighted sum of discounted screening costs and a general delay cost function. Recognizing that an exact solution is computationally intractable, the authors propose an approximation algorithm based on Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI). A key contribution is a method for sampling belief points in the extremely high-dimensional belief space (over state trajectories) by first sampling likely trajectories and then exploring reachable beliefs. The paper provides theoretical support for this approximation, including a bound on the performance loss compared to the optimal policy and a robustness result showing that the optimal policy is stable under small errors in the underlying disease model estimation.

The framework's effectiveness is demonstrated using a large, real-world dataset for breast cancer screening. After learning a disease progression model, the authors show that DPSCREEN can reduce the number of screenings by 28-68% compared to standard annual screening policies while achieving the same expected delay in detection. The resulting policies are shown to be personalized to both static patient risk factors and the dynamic history of test results and patient-initiated visits.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles a problem of high societal importance—optimizing medical screening. The proposed framework is a significant step forward from previous work. By moving beyond restrictive assumptions like Markovian disease processes [7, 9] and simple cost structures [10], it offers a much more general and realistic model of the screening problem. The explicit modeling of patient-initiated visits based on external information is a particularly novel and practical contribution that reflects real-world clinical scenarios.

2.  **Technical Quality and Rigor:** The work is technically sound. The formulation as a sequential decision problem on a belief space over trajectories is appropriate and powerful. The choice of a PBVI-based approximation is well-motivated by the problem's intractability. The authors provide important theoretical guarantees: Proposition 1 offers a bound on the approximation error, linking performance to the quality of belief space sampling, and Theorem 1 provides a valuable robustness guarantee, showing that small errors in model estimation are unlikely to change the optimal policy. This combination of a practical algorithm with theoretical justification is a major strength.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental section is a highlight. The application to a large (45,000 patient) real-world breast cancer dataset convincingly demonstrates the practical value of the proposed method. The results are striking, showing a substantial reduction in the screening burden without compromising detection timeliness. The paper effectively illustrates the personalization aspect, showing how the policy adapts to both baseline risk and dynamic observations (Figure 1, Table 2). The synthetic experiment on linear vs. quadratic costs further underscores the framework's flexibility to adapt to the characteristics of different diseases.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly defined in Section 2, and the proposed approach is logically developed in Section 3. Figure 1 provides an excellent, intuitive explanation of how the policy personalizes recommendations based on both prior risk and new evidence. The relationship to and distinction from prior work is also clearly articulated.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Disconnect Between General Framework and Specific Implementation:** While the framework is lauded for its generality (e.g., handling non-Markovian models), the breast cancer application employs a relatively simple two-state discrete-time Markov model. This leaves the reader wondering about the practical challenges of applying DPSCREEN to the more complex disease models that the theory supports. Demonstrating the method on a non-Markovian model, even a synthetic one, would have made the claims of generality more compelling.

2.  **Computational Complexity and Scalability:** The worst-case complexity appears very high, with a strong dependence on the number of belief points (`B`) and the time horizon (`T`). The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the practical scalability. For instance, what were the values of `K` (number of trajectories) and `B` (number of belief points) used in the experiments? How long did the policy computation take? This information is crucial for assessing whether the method is practical for problems with longer time horizons or more complex state spaces.

3.  **Assumptions for Theoretical Results:** The assumptions underpinning Proposition 1, particularly that tests have no false negatives and the delay cost has a specific functional form, are quite strong and may not hold in many real-world settings. A discussion of the potential impact of relaxing these assumptions on the performance bound would be valuable.

4.  **Details on Belief Sampling:** The description of the belief point generation (Algorithm 1) is somewhat high-level in the main text. More detail on the "random exploration of the actions" and how clinical policies are used to guide belief generation would improve the clarity and reproducibility of this critical step in the algorithm.

### Overall Assessment and Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to both the machine learning community and the field of medical decision-making. It presents a novel, general, and powerful framework for a critical real-world problem. The work is technically strong, blending a practical approximation algorithm with solid theoretical grounding. The experimental validation on a large, real-world dataset is thorough and provides compelling evidence of the method's potential impact.

The weaknesses identified are largely areas for clarification or future work rather than fundamental flaws. The paper is well-written, original, and highly significant. It advances the state of the art in personalized screening and is likely to inspire follow-up research.

I strongly recommend acceptance.
