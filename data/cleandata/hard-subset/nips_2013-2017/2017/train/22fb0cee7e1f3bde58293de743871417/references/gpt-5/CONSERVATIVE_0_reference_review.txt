Summary
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework to compute dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off test costs and detection delays. The disease is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; external information (e.g., self-exam signals) can trigger unscheduled visits. The decision at each visit is a recommendation plan mapping observed test outcomes to the next screening time. The authors derive Bellman equations over beliefs on full state trajectories and show the value function is convex and piecewise-linear, enabling an alpha-vector representation. Because exact computation is intractable, they propose a PBVI-style approximation that samples likely state trajectories and reachable beliefs under simulated policies. They provide a complexity analysis and a loose approximation bound (Proposition 1) tied to belief sampling density under strong assumptions (e.g., perfect tests, bounded screening intervals). They also state a robustness result (Theorem 1) that optimal actions are locally constant in model-belief space except on a measure-zero set. Empirically, they estimate a simple two-state (H/D) Markov model for breast cancer on 45k patients, include a stylized self-exam signal model, and compare DPSCREEN against annual/biennial screening. They report 28–68% expected reductions in tests while matching the expected detection delay.

Strengths
- Tackles an important, clinically relevant problem of optimizing screening schedules and adheres to decision-theoretic principles.
- Incorporates exogenous, patient-driven information (self-exams) and endogenous scheduling decisions in a single framework.
- Clear articulation of the computational challenges and a reasonable PBVI-style workaround.
- Empirical study on a large dataset, with stratification by risk groups, and sensitivity to presence/absence of self-exams.
- The generality of the modeling (arbitrary finite-state trajectory process, trajectory-dependent delay costs) could be useful in principle.

Weaknesses
- Novelty and necessity of the general setup are not convincingly established. The experiments reduce to a two-state Markov model where standard POMDP formulations (or MDP with belief over H/D and elapsed time) and established solvers could apply. Claims that existing frameworks cannot handle trajectory-dependent costs or external information are unpersuasive; both can be encoded by state augmentation or POSMDP formulations.
- The approximation guarantee (Proposition 1) relies on unrealistic assumptions (no false positives/negatives, convex delay costs, bounded inter-screen intervals) and introduces an uncomputable constant ζ. It is not clear how to translate sampling density into practical error bars for the implemented algorithm.
- Theorem 1 (local constancy of optimal policies outside a lower-dimensional exceptional set) is a generic continuity/selection claim that offers little actionable robustness guidance in practice; proof details are deferred, and the result hinges on uniqueness and regularity assumptions.
- Evaluation relies on a model learned from observational data and then simulates outcomes under that same model. There is no off-policy evaluation, uncertainty quantification, or assessment under plausible misspecification. The breast cancer model is simplistic (two-state Markov, stylized self-exam growth law), and many parameters are taken from literature rather than fit/validated on this dataset. The reported large savings may be artifacts of the model rather than robust gains.
- Comparisons are limited to fixed-interval clinical policies. There is no comparison against prior personalized screening approaches (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Rizopoulos et al., ConfidentCare) or generic POMDP baselines, which is critical given the heavy reuse of POMDP/PBVI machinery.
- Cost modeling is coarse: counting each screening equally (biopsy vs mammogram) ignores known heterogeneity in harms/costs, and discounting is assumed but not justified clinically. Delay cost is set linear or quadratic without calibration; w is tuned post hoc to match benchmark delays.
- Technical clarity issues: the sign convention in the Bellman equation (maximize negative costs) is confusing; belief is over trajectory space ST, which explodes combinatorially, yet in practice the method samples K trajectories—this mismatch raises questions about consistency. The piecewise-linear value function claim outside standard POMDP assumptions needs more rigorous conditions.
- Reproducibility: proprietary dataset, key implementation details and code not provided; no runtime or scalability evidence beyond complexity formulae.

Quality
The paper is ambitious but currently weak in methodological validation. The theoretical parts are high-level and rest on strong idealizations; empirical support is entirely model-in-silico and lacks comparisons to established baselines and robustness checks. Important modeling choices (self-exam process, diagnostic accuracy) are simplified in ways that undermine conclusions.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, but several notational/definition inconsistencies (objective sign, belief space, action space definition as recommendation plans) hamper understanding. Key proofs and algorithms are in the supplement; more detail in the main text would help.

Originality
The combination of trajectory-based belief modeling with external information and scheduling as an action is interesting, but much of the computational approach is a variant of PBVI with heuristic belief sampling. Given prior POMDP-based screening work, the incremental novelty is modest unless the generality is demonstrated empirically (which it is not).

Significance
Potentially significant if validated rigorously and compared against strong baselines, but current evidence is insufficient to recommend clinical or methodological impact. The reported reductions in testing are promising but not trustworthy without robust evaluation.

Questions and suggestions
- Compare against POMDP baselines on the same (Markov) problem, including Ayer/Maillart-style models, and ConfidentCare/RL baselines.
- Provide sensitivity analyses to model misspecification (transition rates, test accuracy, self-exam parameters), and uncertainty bands on outcomes.
- Clarify the Bellman formulation (signs, costs) and give conditions under which the piecewise-linear property holds given trajectory-dependent costs and scheduling actions.
- Quantify ζ and Ω(B̄) in practice; report how K and |B̄| affect performance and runtime; include runtime/memory.
- Consider more realistic cost models separating mammogram/biopsy harms and false positives; report E[false positives], number of biopsies, stage at detection.
- Justify or calibrate delay cost functions to disease-specific outcomes (e.g., survival) rather than arbitrary linear/quadratic choices.

Pros
- Important problem with clear potential for impact.
- Unified framework including external information.
- Sensible approximate solution strategy; piecewise-linear structure leveraged.
- Large dataset; clear, interpretable reported metrics.

Cons
- Overstated novelty relative to established POMDP literature.
- Weak, assumption-heavy theory; no practical error control.
- Evaluation only in-silico under self-fitted model; no strong baselines.
- Simplified/uncalibrated cost and signal models; limited reproducibility.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The paper raises an interesting direction but falls short on methodological rigor and empirical validation against established baselines. Strengthening the theoretical guarantees under realistic assumptions, adding robust comparisons and sensitivity analyses, and demonstrating scalability and reproducibility would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
