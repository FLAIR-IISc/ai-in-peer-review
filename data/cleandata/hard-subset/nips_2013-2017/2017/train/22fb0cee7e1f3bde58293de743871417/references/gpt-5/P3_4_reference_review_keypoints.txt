- Proposes DPSCREEN: dynamic, personalized screening policies for general finite-state disease trajectories with endogenous arrivals and noisy tests.  
- Casts problem as a POMDP over entire state trajectories plus diagnosis label and derives belief-based Bellman equations.  
- Shows the value function is piecewise-linear and convex (PWL), analogous to classic POMDP results.  
- Introduces a PBVI-style approximation that samples likely trajectories to reduce belief dimensionality and explores reachable beliefs under heuristic policies.  
- Provides an approximation bound dependent on sampling density under assumptions (no FP/FN, convex delay cost, bounded screening interval).  
- Claims a robustness result that optimal actions are locally constant off a lower-dimensional boundary in model–belief space.  
- Empirical case study on a large de-identified breast cancer dataset with a 2-state Markov progression and self-exam model.  
- Reports 28–68% reduction in expected number of screens vs annual screening when tuning weight to match baseline expected delay.  
- Finds quadratic delay costs produce more aggressive screening policies in synthetic comparisons.  

- Technical concern: Bellman equation uses a maximization with negative costs while original problem is stated as a minimization — sign convention must be clarified or corrected.  
- Scalability concern: belief space over trajectory–label pairs is combinatorially large; tractability critically depends on sampling but sampling details are sparse.  
- Approximation bound issues: relies on no false positives/negatives and convex costs, introduces an unspecified constant ζ and unspecified sampling density, making the bound hard to interpret.  
- Robustness theorem is weak and does not establish practical robustness to model misspecification.  
- Modeling inconsistencies: Y is defined discrete but treated as continuous tumor size later; theory assumes no FP/FN but experiments use sensitivity/specificity.  

- Empirics limited: evaluation is fully model-based using a simple 2-state Markov model that does not test claimed generality for non-Markov/non-stationary processes.  
- Tuning concern: weight w is tuned to match baseline expected delay, which could constitute cherry-picking and influence reported gains.  
- Missing baselines: no comparisons to prior POMDP screening methods (Ayer et al. 2012, Maillart et al. 2008) or modern risk/density-based interval guidelines under the same fitted model.  
- Missing scalability/reporting: computational parameters (K, B, T), number of alpha-vectors, runtimes, and hardware are not reported.  

- Clarity needs: explicitly state objective sign in Bellman recursion and whether costs are converted to rewards.  
- Clarify reconciliation of discrete Y vs continuous lump model and specify the sufficient statistics of Y used by the arrival process.  
- State when FP/FN are allowed vs ruled out and how that affects value function and theoretical bounds.  
- Provide full details on trajectory sampling, belief exploration policies, number of sampled points, and complexity.  

- Originality: useful extension of screening POMDPs with endogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent costs, but PWL property and PBVI are known techniques combined here; claim of being “first” should be nuanced.  
- Significance: important and relevant problem with potential impact, but current theoretical guarantees and empirical validation are insufficient for top-conference standards.  

- Pros: principled sequential model for healthcare screening, incorporates external information, adapts PBVI to high-dimensional beliefs, and evaluates on a large real-world dataset with personalization effects.  
- Cons: Bellman sign/optimization mismatch, strong/unrealistic assumptions for theoretical results, limited empirical validation and baselines, and lack of runtime/reproducibility details.  

- Suggested fixes: clarify and correct Bellman recursion derivation; quantify approximation bound (estimate ζ, empirically report sampling coverage); relax no-FP/FN assumption.  
- Suggested experiments: ablations (with/without external info), sensitivity to sampling budgets and model misspecification, comparisons to prior POMDP and risk-based methods, and at least one non-Markov/non-stationary process.  
- Reproducibility suggestions: report K, B, T, alpha-vector counts, runtimes, hardware, and release code or detailed pseudocode.  

- Recommendation: Weak reject — promising direction but needs stronger technical clarity, tighter theoretical guarantees, and more rigorous empirical validation.
