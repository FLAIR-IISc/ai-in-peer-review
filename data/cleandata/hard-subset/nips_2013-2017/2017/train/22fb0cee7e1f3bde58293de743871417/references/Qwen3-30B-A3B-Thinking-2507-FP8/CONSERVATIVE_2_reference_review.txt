

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for personalized disease screening policies that accounts for disease progression models, patient features, and external information (e.g., self-examinations). The authors formulate the problem as a partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) with a non-standard delay cost function and propose an approximate solution using Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) with theoretical error bounds. They demonstrate their approach on breast cancer screening data, claiming 28-68% reductions in screening frequency compared to current clinical policies while maintaining similar expected detection delays.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important real-world problem with clear societal impact. The theoretical foundation of extending POMDPs to handle non-stationary disease processes and external information is conceptually sound. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical bound on approximation error (Proposition 1), which is a notable contribution to the theoretical understanding of their method. The application to breast cancer screening with a large dataset (45,000 patients) demonstrates practical relevance.

## Weaknesses
Despite the promising problem statement, the paper suffers from several significant methodological and empirical shortcomings that undermine its contribution:

1. **Insufficient validation against established baselines:** The paper compares only to current clinical policies (annual/biennial screening) rather than to existing computational methods for personalized screening. The related work section cites Ayer et al. [7] and Maillart et al. [8] who developed POMDP-based screening models, yet the authors fail to compare their approach to these established methods. Without such comparison, it's impossible to assess whether their method actually improves upon existing computational approaches.

2. **Overstated novelty claims:** The paper claims to be "the first such personalized screening policies in a very general setting," yet the related work section shows that existing POMDP-based screening methods [7,8,9] already address personalized screening with non-Markov disease processes. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how their formulation differs from these existing approaches in a way that justifies the novelty claim.

3. **Unrealistic assumptions:** The paper assumes "no false positives" for test results, which is a highly unrealistic assumption in medical screening. This assumption drastically simplifies the problem and likely inflates the claimed performance improvements. The paper doesn't provide any analysis of how their method would perform under more realistic conditions with false positives/negatives.

4. **Lack of ablation studies:** The paper doesn't conduct ablation studies to isolate the contribution of different components of their approach. For instance, it's unclear how much of the performance improvement comes from the personalized risk modeling versus the dynamic scheduling component.

5. **Questionable clinical significance:** The claimed 28-68% reduction in screening frequency is dramatic but lacks proper clinical context. The paper doesn't address whether reducing screening frequency would increase the risk of missed early detection for high-risk patients, which is a critical concern in clinical practice. The paper also doesn't validate whether the policies would be accepted by clinicians or patients.

6. **Limited generalizability:** The experiments are limited to breast cancer with a specific linear delay cost function. The paper claims their method is generalizable to "many disease models," but provides no evidence beyond the breast cancer case. The synthetic exercise with quadratic delay costs (Figure 2) is insufficient to demonstrate robustness across different disease contexts.

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a modest theoretical contribution to extending POMDP formulations for screening, but the empirical validation is inadequate to support the claimed significance. The paper fails to properly benchmark against existing computational approaches for personalized screening, which is a fundamental requirement for a machine learning paper. The clinical claims are overstated without sufficient validation of the real-world impact of the proposed policies.

While the problem of personalized screening is important and the theoretical framework is well-constructed, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to demonstrate that the proposed method represents a meaningful advancement over existing approaches. The authors' claims about performance improvements (28-68% reduction in screenings) are not supported by rigorous comparison to the state-of-the-art computational methods for personalized screening.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. The paper's main contribution appears to be a slightly modified application of existing POMDP techniques to a specific clinical problem, without sufficient empirical validation against established baselines. The claimed performance improvements are not convincingly demonstrated, and the paper fails to address critical limitations of the approach (particularly the unrealistic no-false-positives assumption). For a paper to be accepted at NIPS, it should demonstrate a clear, significant advancement over the state of the art with rigorous validation. This paper does not meet that standard.
