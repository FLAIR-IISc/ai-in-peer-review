## Peer Review for "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for developing personalized screening policies that optimally balance the costs of screening and delayed disease detection. The authors address key limitations of existing screening models by allowing for dynamic, patient-specific policies considering disease progression, external information, and patient arrival patterns. They propose a computationally tractable approximation algorithm with provable error bounds and demonstrate its effectiveness using a breast cancer dataset, showing significant reductions in screening frequency while maintaining comparable delay times compared to current clinical practices. The work also highlights the importance of cost function selection based on the specific disease characteristics.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of screening policies. They effectively distinguish themselves from prior work (e.g., [7, 9, 10]) which relies on Markovian disease processes and stationary test outcomes.  They acknowledge connections to related fields like controlled sensing [21, 34, 38] but emphasize the differences arising from non-Markovian dynamics, patient-specific costs, and the importance of external information. The paper builds upon POMDP frameworks [18, 19, 20] but explicitly addresses challenges inherent in personalized screening, particularly the dependency of delay costs on state trajectories and the non-fixed intervals between decision epochs.  The related work section is comprehensive and accurately reflects the state-of-the-art.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Generalizability:** The proposed framework is highly novel in its ability to handle complex disease dynamics, incorporate external information, and personalize screening based on both patient features and individual history. The model is remarkably general, encompassing a wide range of disease models.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The inclusion of approximation error bounds (Proposition 1) strengthens the theoretical grounding of the work.  Demonstrating the robustness to model estimation errors (Theorem 1) is also a significant contribution.
* **Practical Relevance:**  The application to breast cancer screening demonstrates the practical viability of the approach. The observed reductions in screening frequency (28-68%) without compromising delay times are highly significant.
* **Clear Presentation:** While the model is complex, the authors generally present it in a clear and organized manner. The use of a graphical model (mentioned in the text) is helpful.
* **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis highlighting the impact of the cost function on the resulting policy demonstrates a deep understanding of the trade-offs involved in screening strategies.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors acknowledge the computational challenges, the complexity (O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)) remains a concern, particularly for high-dimensional state spaces and long time horizons. More discussion on practical strategies for managing this complexity beyond parallelization would be valuable.  The impact of *K* (number of sampled trajectories) on the performance and computational cost isn't fully explored.
* **Model Assumptions:** The assumption of no false positives (Section 2) is a simplification. While acknowledged, it’s worth explicitly discussing the impact of relaxing this assumption and how it might be incorporated into the framework. Similarly, the independence assumptions regarding test outcomes and external information are strong and might not hold in real-world scenarios.
* **Dataset Specifics:**  The breast cancer dataset is limited to a specific age range (60-65).  Extending the analysis to a broader demographic and different stages of disease progression would strengthen the generalizability of the findings.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** A more thorough sensitivity analysis of the key parameters (e.g.,  ι in the tumor growth model, σ in the self-examination model) would be beneficial to assess the robustness of the results.  How do changes in these parameters affect the optimal policy?
* **Algorithm Details:** While Algorithms 1 & 2 are referenced in the appendix, a high-level description of their key steps within the main text would improve readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* This is a technically sound and original contribution that addresses a challenging and important problem in healthcare.
* The proposed framework offers a significant advancement over existing screening models by enabling truly personalized policies.
* The theoretical guarantees and empirical validation provide strong evidence of the effectiveness of the approach.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the key ideas and contributions.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity could limit the applicability of the method to certain problems.
* The model assumptions, while reasonable, may not hold in all scenarios.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The novelty, theoretical rigor, and practical relevance of the work outweigh the identified weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest:

1. **Expand discussion on computational complexity:**  Provide more insights into practical mitigation strategies.
2. **Address model assumptions:**  Discuss the potential impact of relaxing the assumption of no false positives.
3. **Briefly describe Algorithms 1 & 2** in the main text.
4. **Consider a sensitivity analysis** on key parameters.



**Confidence:** 8/10.  The paper is solid and well-presented, but the computational complexity remains a concern that warrants careful consideration.
