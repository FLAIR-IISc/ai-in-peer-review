### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel and general framework for deriving dynamic, personalized screening policies. The goal is to optimize the trade-off between the cost of screening (e.g., financial, health risks) and the cost of delayed disease detection. The problem is formulated as a generalized Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP). The framework is highly flexible, accommodating arbitrary finite-state, non-Markovian disease progression models, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and patient arrivals triggered by external information (e.g., self-examination), in addition to scheduled appointments.

The authors correctly note that standard POMDP solvers are not applicable due to these generalizations: (1) the time between decision epochs is not fixed or a simple function of the state, but depends on the chosen action and stochastic external events; (2) the costs are not memoryless but depend on the entire disease trajectory (specifically, the time of onset vs. time of detection). To solve this intractable problem, the authors propose an approximate solution based on Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI). A key part of their contribution is a two-step belief point sampling strategy tailored to the high-dimensional belief space: first sampling likely disease trajectories and then exploring reachable beliefs from this reduced space.

This work significantly generalizes previous POMDP-based screening models (e.g., Ayer et al., NIPS 2017; Erenay et al.), which typically assume Markovian disease progression and simpler cost structures. It also presents a more principled optimization approach compared to the simulation-based methods common in the medical literature (e.g., CISNET), which can only compare a small, pre-selected set of policies. The problem is related to controlled sensing, but the assumptions made in that field (e.g., stationarity) are often too restrictive for medical applications, a gap this paper aims to fill.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Generality:** The paper tackles a problem of high societal and clinical importance. The proposed framework is impressively general, relaxing many restrictive assumptions made in prior work. The ability to handle non-Markovian disease models, general delay cost functions, and patient-initiated visits makes the framework applicable to a wide range of diseases and realistic clinical scenarios. This represents a substantial advance in the state of the art for medical decision-making models.

2.  **Sound Technical Approach:** The formulation of the problem through Bellman equations is clear and correct. Recognizing the intractability, the adaptation of a PBVI-style algorithm is a practical and well-motivated choice. The proposed belief sampling heuristic is sensible, addressing the challenge of the enormous belief space by focusing on plausible trajectories and reachable belief states.

3.  **Compelling Experimental Validation:** The application to a large, real-world breast cancer dataset is a major strength. The results are striking, demonstrating that DPSCREEN can reduce the expected number of screenings by 28-68% compared to current clinical guidelines while maintaining the same expected delay in detection. This provides strong evidence for the practical utility of the approach. The paper further strengthens its evaluation by:
    *   Analyzing different patient risk groups, demonstrating personalization.
    *   Considering the impact of external information (self-exams).
    *   Using a synthetic experiment to show how the policy adapts to different disease characteristics (represented by linear vs. quadratic delay costs).

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the model is formally defined, and the proposed algorithm is explained logically. The graphical illustration of personalization in Figure 1 is particularly effective at conveying the intuition behind the dynamic policy.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Practical Scalability and Computational Cost:** The worst-case complexity of the algorithm is high, with quadratic dependence on the number of time steps (T) and belief points (B). While the authors mention parallelization and action space restriction as mitigation strategies, the paper would be stronger with a more detailed discussion of the computational resources required for the experiments presented. For instance, what were the values of K, B, and T? How long did policy computation take? This information is crucial for assessing the feasibility of applying DPSCREEN to problems with longer time horizons or more complex state/observation spaces.

2.  **Assumptions for Theoretical Results:** Proposition 1, which bounds the approximation error, relies on several strong assumptions, most notably that tests are perfectly accurate ("no false positives and no false negatives"). This is rarely true in practice, especially for screening tests like mammograms. While the authors are transparent about this assumption, it significantly limits the practical relevance of the theoretical guarantee. It would be helpful to explicitly state in the main text that this assumption is only for the theoretical result and was not used in the main experiments. Similarly, Theorem 1 provides a nice robustness result for the *exactly optimal* policy. Its connection to the computed *approximate* policy is somewhat informal. A more thorough discussion of the robustness of the practical algorithm to model estimation errors would be valuable.

3.  **Simplicity of the Experimental Disease Model:** The framework is motivated by its ability to handle general, non-Markovian disease processes. However, the breast cancer experiment employs a simple two-state (Healthy, Disease) Markov model. This creates a slight disconnect between the advertised power of the framework and its application. While this choice is pragmatic and aligns with previous literature, the experiments do not fully showcase the novel capabilities of the proposed method. The work could be strengthened by including an experiment (even a synthetic one) with a more complex, non-Markovian disease model (e.g., a semi-Markov model with multiple pre-cancerous states) to demonstrate that the computational machinery can indeed handle such cases.

4.  **Details of Model Estimation:** The process of learning the disease model from data is a critical precursor to the planning algorithm. The main paper provides a high-level overview, deferring details to the appendix. Given that the quality of the resulting policy is fundamentally dependent on the accuracy of the learned model, a bit more detail in the main text about the MCMC estimation process and sensitivity to modeling choices (e.g., the use of the Gail model) would be beneficial.

### Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the fields of machine learning for healthcare and sequential decision-making. The proposed DPSCREEN framework is novel, general, and addresses an important real-world problem with a principled and effective approach. The experimental results on a large clinical dataset are impressive and convincingly demonstrate the potential of personalized screening to reduce costs while maintaining clinical effectiveness. The weaknesses identified are largely related to the practicalities of implementation and the gap between theory and practice, but they do not undermine the core contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **High Significance:** Addresses a critical problem in healthcare with a method that could lead to substantial improvements over the standard of care.
*   **High Originality:** The problem formulation is a novel and meaningful generalization of standard POMDPs and existing screening models.
*   **Strong Quality:** The technical approach is sound, and the experimental evaluation is thorough and compelling, using a large real-world dataset.
*   **Good Clarity:** The paper is well-written and communicates its ideas effectively.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The practical scalability of the method for more complex models is not fully explored.
*   The theoretical guarantees rely on strong, unrealistic assumptions.
*   The main experiment uses a simpler model than the framework is capable of handling.

On balance, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. This paper presents a complete and impactful piece of research that is highly relevant to the NeurIPS community.

**Decision:** Accept.
