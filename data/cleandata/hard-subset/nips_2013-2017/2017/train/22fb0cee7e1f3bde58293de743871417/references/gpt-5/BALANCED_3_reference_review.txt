Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off screening costs versus detection delay. The disease process is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; external information (e.g., self-exam) can trigger unscheduled visits. The decision is when to schedule the next screening after each observation. The optimization problem is a finite-horizon partially observed decision problem where costs include screening and a trajectory-dependent delay cost. The authors show the value function is convex and piecewise-linear in belief (as in POMDPs), derive a Bellman recursion, and propose a point-based value iteration (PBVI) variant that operates on a reduced belief space constructed by sampling likely disease trajectories and reachable beliefs under a mix of exploratory and clinically motivated policies. They give an approximation-error bound in terms of the density of sampled beliefs and a robustness result showing local constancy of the optimal policy under small model perturbations.

Empirically, they fit a two-state Markov disease model for breast cancer using a large observational dataset (45k women; 84k mammograms), with self-exam modeled via a noisy tumor growth process. Policies are tuned to match the expected delay of benchmark clinical schedules (annual/biennial), and report 28–68% reductions in expected number of screenings at comparable expected delay. A synthetic comparison suggests that the convexity of delay costs (e.g., quadratic vs linear) materially changes the policy’s aggressiveness.

This work sits at the intersection of POMDP-based screening (e.g., Ayer et al. 2012; Maillart et al. 2008), dynamic survival modeling and Bayesian design for screening (e.g., Rizopoulos 2012; Alaa et al.), and controlled sensing (e.g., Krishnamurthy). Relative to prior applications, the paper emphasizes: (i) external information triggering endogenous decision epochs; (ii) trajectory-dependent costs; and (iii) a belief representation over disease trajectories, approximated via sampled trajectories.

Strengths
- Addresses a clinically important problem with a principled sequential decision framework that personalizes by features and dynamic history.
- Clear unification of external information arrivals with scheduling decisions—useful in practice.
- Theoretical properties: piecewise-linear convexity of V(b,t), a PBVI-style algorithm with a sampling-driven belief set, and an intuitive robustness claim (local constancy of the argmax policy).
- Large-scale application on real data; the reported reductions in screening load, if borne out under robust evaluation, could be impactful.
- Sensible exploration of how disease-specific delay costs change the optimal operating point.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- The “generality” claims are not reflected in experiments. The empirical model is two-state Markov with standard sensitivity/specificity, which prior POMDP screening works also handle; thus the added generality (non-Markov disease, trajectory-dependent costs) is not validated experimentally.
- The theoretical approximation bound (Proposition 1) is stated in terms of a belief-sampling density Ω(B̄) with a constant ζ but lacks a rigorous derivation and assumptions are strong (no false positives/negatives, bounded screening intervals). In practice, false positives/negatives exist and matter clinically.
- Evaluation relies on a model learned from observational data, then simulates counterfactual policies on that model. This is standard in the literature, but the paper does not provide uncertainty quantification (confidence intervals), calibration checks, or sensitivity analyses to model misspecification, which are crucial.
- Comparisons are limited to fixed clinical schedules. There is no comparison to prior personalized POMDP baselines (e.g., Ayer; Maillart) or risk-based clinical strategies (e.g., density/risk-tailored intervals), despite citing them.
- Cost modeling is simplistic: test cost is fixed “1” regardless of mammogram vs biopsy; harms from false positives, recalls, and overdiagnosis are not incorporated, while the method claims to reduce screenings at “same delay.”
- Some notational and conceptual inconsistencies: early sections assume no false positives, yet experiments include positive mammogram/negative biopsy (z=2). The Bellman equation (3) appears to maximize negative instantaneous costs (a minus sign in front of C̃), which should be clarified.

Clarity
- Core algorithmic details (belief construction, value backups) are deferred to the supplement; main text lacks pseudo-code, hyperparameters, and guidance on K, |B̄[t]|, and runtime/parallelization.
- The complexity expression O(T (B)2T2K|Y||Z|) is opaque; please clarify growth with T and K, and what “(B)2T2” denotes.
- Some references are incomplete.

Originality
- The PBVI approach, piecewise-linear value functions, and belief approximation are well-known in POMDPs. The contribution lies in adapting PBVI to beliefs over sampled trajectories and handling endogenous arrivals. However, much of the structure can be captured by augmenting states (e.g., time since onset) in a standard POMDP/SMDP. The originality is moderate unless stronger formal connections (or separation) are made.

Significance
- Potentially significant for clinical decision support, but current evidence is limited by evaluation breadth and lack of stronger baselines and uncertainty analyses. The claims of generality would be more convincing with a multi-state disease or a non-Markov trajectory example.

Questions for the authors
1. Can the proposed model be reduced to an equivalent (semi-)Markov POMDP by augmenting state with onset time and time since last test, thereby enabling standard PBVI without beliefs over full trajectories? If not, what formal barrier exists?
2. How sensitive are the results to misspecification of the tumor growth/self-exam model and to the Gail-based risk stratification? Please provide calibration plots and CI bands.
3. How are biopsies and recalls costed? Could you incorporate false-positive harms and overdiagnosis metrics?
4. Can you report runtime, K, |B̄[t]|, and wall-clock/parallelization details? How does performance scale with multi-state disease models?
5. Please compare against prior personalized screening policies (Ayer; Maillart) and recent risk-based clinical intervals (e.g., Trentham-Dietz), using your same evaluation protocol.

Pros for acceptance
- Important problem; principled sequential formulation with external information.
- PBVI-style method adapted to high-dimensional belief via trajectory sampling.
- Robustness intuition and an error bound (albeit under strong assumptions).
- Promising empirical reductions in screenings at matched delays.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to existing POMDP screening literature; claims about inapplicability of prior methods feel overstated.
- Empirical evaluation lacks strong baselines, uncertainty, and richer clinical endpoints.
- Theoretical assumptions (no FP/FN) and clarity gaps weaken credibility.
- Generality claims not validated by experiments beyond two-state Markov disease.

Recommendation: Weak reject
I see promise in the direction and applaud the integration of external info and personalization. To strengthen the paper: add rigorous comparisons to prior personalized baselines, incorporate realistic test harms, provide uncertainty/sensitivity analyses, clarify the algorithm and complexity, and either validate the general non-Markov/trajectory-cost setting empirically or temper claims. Even if rejected, I encourage a revision along these lines, as the topic is timely and relevant to the NeurIPS community.
