

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

This paper proposes a method called DPSCREEN for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against delay costs in disease detection. The authors claim their approach generalizes previous work by handling non-Markov disease processes and incorporating external information (like self-examinations), which they argue existing methods cannot do.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper formulates personalized screening as a partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) with a general disease model. The authors develop a point-based value iteration approach (DPSCREEN) to approximate the optimal policy, claiming it can handle arbitrary finite state stochastic disease processes, unlike previous work that assumed Markov or semi-Markov disease progression. They validate their approach on breast cancer data, claiming 28-68% reductions in screenings compared to current clinical practice while maintaining the same expected delays in detection.

The paper's theoretical framework builds upon standard POMDP techniques, particularly point-based value iteration (Pineau et al., 2006), which has been widely used in the literature. The authors' "generalization" to non-Markov processes appears to be a straightforward extension of existing POMDP methodology, not a significant theoretical advance. The claim that "existing frameworks cannot be used" is unsubstantiated, as Ayer et al. (2016) already used POMDPs for personalized mammography screening with a Markov disease model, which would handle the two-state Markov model they use for breast cancer.

## Strengths

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem formulation is mathematically rigorous, and the authors provide a theoretical bound on approximation error (Proposition 1), which is a standard result in POMDP literature. The clinical relevance of personalized screening is well-motivated, and the experimental results on a real breast cancer dataset provide some evidence of practical applicability.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty**: The core method (point-based value iteration) is well-established in POMDP literature. The authors claim their method handles "very general" disease models, but their breast cancer example uses a simple two-state Markov model (Healthy/Disease), which existing methods like Ayer et al. (2016) could handle. They don't demonstrate that non-Markov disease processes are necessary or beneficial for the specific diseases they study.

2. **Inadequate experimental validation**: The claimed 28-68% reduction in screenings lacks statistical significance testing. The paper compares against "current clinical practice" (annual screening) but doesn't compare against other personalized screening methods that might already exist. The lack of comparison with established POMDP-based screening approaches (e.g., Ayer et al.) makes their claims unsubstantiated.

3. **Safety concerns unaddressed**: The paper claims reduced screenings while maintaining "the same expected delays in disease detection" but fails to discuss potential downsides, such as increased missed diagnoses or mortality in high-risk patients. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the reduced screenings are safe for clinical implementation.

4. **Theoretical bound lacks clinical meaning**: Proposition 1 bounds the approximation error by Ω(B̄), a theoretical construct that has no clear interpretation in terms of clinical outcomes. The authors don't explain how the bound relates to practical screening decisions or how it compares to existing bounds in the POMDP literature.

5. **Insufficient model estimation details**: The disease model estimation process is superficially described (using the Gail model for risk scores), but the authors don't provide sufficient details on how they estimated the disease progression model from the observational data. The model estimation process appears to be a black box in their framework.

6. **Lack of robustness analysis**: The paper claims their method is robust to estimation errors (Theorem 1) but doesn't provide empirical evidence to support this claim. They don't test how their method performs when the disease model is misspecified.

## Overall Assessment

While the problem of personalized screening is important and relevant, this paper fails to provide sufficient evidence that their approach offers a meaningful advance over existing methods. The claimed novelty is overstated, the experimental validation is inadequate, and the paper doesn't adequately address safety concerns in clinical implementation. The core methodology is a standard POMDP technique, and the paper doesn't demonstrate that the "generalization" to non-Markov processes provides tangible benefits for the specific diseases they consider.

The paper would benefit from a more rigorous comparison against existing personalized screening methods, statistical significance testing for their results, and a thorough discussion of the clinical safety implications of reduced screenings. Without these, the claims of significant improvement (28-68% reduction in screenings) cannot be substantiated.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper makes strong claims about novelty and impact without providing sufficient evidence. The method is not sufficiently different from established POMDP approaches to warrant publication in NIPS, and the experimental validation does not meet the high standards required for the conference. The paper would be better suited for a medical or operations research venue where the clinical relevance is more directly addressed.
