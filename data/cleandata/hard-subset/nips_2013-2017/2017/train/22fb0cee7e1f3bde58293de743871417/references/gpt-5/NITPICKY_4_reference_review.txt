Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off screening frequency against delay in disease detection. The authors model disease as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, allow endogenous arrivals via external information (e.g., self-exams), and define a trajectory-dependent delay cost. They cast the problem in a belief-space dynamic program, derive a Bellman equation, show the value function is convex and piecewise linear, and propose a point-based value iteration (PBVI) scheme customized to very high-dimensional belief spaces by sampling likely disease trajectories and reachable beliefs. They give a loose approximation-error bound based on belief sampling density and a “robustness” result that the optimal action mapping is locally constant in model-belief space outside a lower-dimensional set. A case study on breast cancer suggests large reductions (28–68%) in expected screenings at matched expected delay, with and without self-exams.

This line of work is related to POMDP-based screening (Ayer et al., Maillart et al.), survival/joint modeling for personalized intervals (Rizopoulos), and controlled sensing. Compared to prior POMDP screening works, the paper emphasizes trajectory-dependent costs and an external information-triggered arrival process, and adopts PBVI-style approximations. The application echoes risk-based screening and decision-support systems (e.g., ConfidentCare by Alaa et al.), but the formalization is more general than many disease-specific simulators (e.g., MISCAN-COLON).

Strengths
- Ambitious, general formulation that explicitly includes external information-triggered arrivals and trajectory-dependent costs; this is a meaningful extension beyond many standard POMDP formulations used in screening.
- Clear articulation of the need to personalize by both static features and dynamic history.
- Use of belief-space DP and piecewise-linear convexity is appropriate; the high-level plan to adapt PBVI via trajectory sampling is sensible.
- Empirical case study on a large breast cancer dataset; the reported reductions in screening at matched expected delay (assuming the model) are practically interesting.
- The paper tries to address model misspecification and approximation error conceptually.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns

Quality (technical soundness, support for claims)
- Bellman/sign conventions: Eq. (3) maximizes the negative of immediate costs plus future value, but V is not clearly defined as (negative) cost-to-go. As written, maximizing −C̃ + E[V] contradicts the stated minimization objective unless V is a reward. Please clarify sign conventions and consistency between (1), (2), and (3).
- Arrival process and τ̃: The probability Pr(z, y, τ̃ | b, τ, x) is central but not formally defined. τ̃ should be the minimum between the scheduled follow-up and the first threshold-crossing of Y(t); derive its distribution and the dependence on τ, the disease trajectory, and noise. Without this, Eq. (3) is underspecified.
- Assumptions on test errors are inconsistent. Early text assumes “no false positives” for z ∈ Z+ (diagnosis), while later the approximation bound assumes “no false positives and no false negatives,” yet the empirical model uses published sensitivity/specificity (i.e., both error types exist). This mismatch undermines both theory and experiments; please reconcile and state exactly which z are in Z+ (is it only biopsy-positive?).
- Approximation error: Proposition 1 states the error ≤ Ω(B̄), where Ω itself is defined in terms of a constant ζ (“maximum expected loss in one time slot”) that is not specified nor bounded; dependence on T, discounting, W, and c(·) is not explicit. As stated, the bound risks being tautological and not actionable. Provide a concrete Lipschitz-type constant with explicit dependence on model parameters, or at least a verifiable bound for ζ.
- Theorem 1 (local constancy): The mapping τ∗ and domain/codomain are not cleanly defined (L×Z×T → T|Z|). Topology/measure for “with probability 1” is unspecified; the argument seems to rely on genericity/unique maximizer assumptions but details are absent. As a robustness statement it is too loose to guide practice.
- Computational complexity: O(T (B)2 T2 K |Y||Z|) is unclear (two T factors?), and the dependence on K and |Γ| induced by backups is not justified. Provide a derivation, define B precisely, and report runtime/parallelization results.
- Experiments: Tuning w to match the expected delay of annual screening raises concerns about overfitting. There is no train/test split, no external validation, no uncertainty intervals, and limited sensitivity analyses to model misspecification (e.g., alternative tumor growth, self-exam adherence, noise σ, threshold ỹ). The large claimed savings could be brittle.
- Model choices: The two-state Markov disease with logistic pin/ptr is simplistic for cancer progression; discretization of time and absorbing D make regression to the mean and lead-time biases likely. The monthly self-exam model (ι=0.9, σ=0.43, ỹ=1) and adherence assumptions seem ad hoc; their impacts should be quantified.
- Evaluation on observational data relies entirely on a learned model; there is no retrospective counterfactual validation (e.g., off-policy evaluation bounds) or calibration checks of predicted incidence/detection vs observed.

Clarity (writing/organization)
- Heavy reliance on appendices; the main paper is not self-contained. Key algorithmic details, figures, tables are missing from the body; Table 1/2 and Figure 1/2 are referenced but not shown.
- Numerous notational inconsistencies and typos: ptr(x) vs ptran(x), “Pr(s(t)=D, s(t′)≠D)=0” explanation, Z+, definition of H(t), “st Conference on NIPS 2017” artifact, footnote markers mis-placed.
- References are incomplete (missing years/venues/pages) and some are duplicated; ensure proper citation formatting.
- Graphical model is only in the appendix.

Originality
- The high-level idea of PBVI for personalized screening under POMDP-like uncertainty is not new; the novelty lies in (a) trajectory-dependent costs and (b) endogenous arrival via external information. While interesting, the theoretical contributions are modest and currently underspecified. Claims of “first such personalized screening policies in a very general setting” are too strong given related POMDP, survival/joint modeling, and decision-support literature (some cited).

Significance
- If validated and made robust, DPSCREEN could impact ML-for-health screening research. However, given the theoretical gaps and fragile empirical evaluation (model-only evaluation without uncertainty), practical significance is unclear.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem with a general formulation.
- Sensible adaptation of PBVI to high-dimensional beliefs via trajectory sampling.
- Encouraging empirical trends on a large dataset.
- Attempts to discuss approximation and estimation errors.

Cons
- Key modeling and theoretical details are missing or inconsistent (signs, arrivals, error models).
- Approximation and robustness results are too vague to be useful.
- Empirical evaluation lacks validation, uncertainty, and sensitivity analyses.
- Clarity and formatting issues throughout; main paper is not self-contained.

Recommendation: Reject

Suggestions to improve
- Formalize the arrival/τ̃ process and fix the reward/cost sign conventions in the Bellman equation. Precisely define Z+ and reconcile test error assumptions across theory and experiments.
- Provide a concrete, parameterized error bound (explicit ζ) and a proof sketch in the main paper; clarify Theorem 1 with proper topology/measure and mapping definitions.
- Detail Algorithm 1–2 in the main text, including choices of K, |B̄|, trajectory sampling strategy, and runtime; report sensitivity to K and |B̄|.
- Add rigorous validation: calibration of incidence/detection, uncertainty intervals via bootstrap/posterior sampling, sensitivity to self-exam parameters, cross-validation on held-out cohorts, and ablations (no external info, coarser time grid).
- Clean up notation, typos, and references; include key figures/tables in the main paper; temper novelty claims and better position against prior art.
