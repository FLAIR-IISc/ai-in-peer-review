- Summary: DPSCREEN is a new framework for personalized screening that optimally trades off testing frequency against detection delay, handles non-stationary progression and external signals, and offers a computationally effective procedure with bounded approximation error; evaluated on breast cancer data and synthetic experiments.

- Relation to prior work: Situates work within screening optimization, controlled sensing, and POMDP literature, highlighting it relaxes restrictive assumptions (e.g., Markov/stationary outcomes) and extends to state-trajectory dependent costs and arrival times; related work is comprehensively cited.

- Novelty: Fills a gap by providing a flexible, generalizable approach to personalized screening beyond prior restrictive models.

- Theoretical rigor: Provides a clear mathematical formulation and bounded approximation error for the proposed solution.

- Practical relevance: Breast cancer application shows substantial reductions in screening frequency while maintaining acceptable detection delays, indicating potential clinical impact.

- Comprehensive modeling: Incorporates external information (e.g., self-exams) and flexible delay cost functions to increase realism.

- Presentation clarity: Paper is generally well organized and clear, though Lemma 1 and Bellman equation sections are dense and could be simplified.

- Robustness analysis: Includes discussion of estimation error and a theorem on robustness to model misspecification, adding credibility.

- Computational complexity (weakness): Algorithmic scaling (O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)) may be prohibitive for large state spaces or long horizons; more mitigation strategies needed beyond parallelization.

- Model assumptions (weakness): Use of a two-state Markov disease model may be overly restrictive for some diseases; extension to richer models is needed.

- Data dependence (weakness): Performance hinges on data quality and parameter estimation (e.g., reliance on the Gail model); sensitivity analyses across estimation methods/data sources would help.

- Experimental scope (weakness): Evaluation is limited mainly to breast cancer; broader disease evaluations or varied synthetic data would better demonstrate generalizability.

- Algorithm clarity (weakness): Key algorithms are placed in the appendix, reducing readability; a high-level algorithm overview in the main text is recommended.

- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, theoretically sound, potential to improve clinical practice and reduce costs, rigorous approximation and robustness analysis, and practical demonstration.

- Arguments against acceptance: Scalability concerns, potentially restrictive modelling assumptions, and limited experimental breadth.

- Overall assessment: Strong accept—novelty, theoretical rigor, and practical relevance outweigh the listed weaknesses; suggest clarifying computational complexity and bringing algorithm details into the main text.

- Confidence: 8/10
