

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

## Summary
This paper proposes a method called DPSCREEN for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against delay in disease detection. The authors model the problem as a partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) with a novel belief structure to handle non-stationary disease processes, external information (e.g., patient self-examinations), and dynamic patient history. They claim their method is the first to handle these complexities in a general setting. The approach uses a modified point-based value iteration algorithm with a belief space constructed from sampled disease trajectories. The paper demonstrates the method using breast cancer screening data, claiming 28-68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining equivalent detection delays compared to current clinical practices.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in healthcare optimization with potential real-world impact. The problem formulation is well-motivated, and the authors correctly identify limitations in existing screening frameworks. The methodological approach of using a general disease model with external information is a reasonable extension of prior work in POMDPs for healthcare applications. The experimental results are promising and suggest significant potential benefits.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Technical Inconsistencies and Incorrect Claims
The paper makes several technical claims that are either incorrect or poorly justified:

- The paper states "the disease state trajectory is not restricted to a Markovian or Semi-Markovian process" (Section 2), but then uses a two-state Markov model for breast cancer in Section 4, contradicting the stated generality. The paper's own examples rely on Markov assumptions, undermining the claim of generality.

- The computational complexity analysis is incorrect. The paper claims "the worst case computation of the policy requires O(T(B)²T²K|Y||Z|) steps" but this doesn't match standard POMDP complexity analysis. The complexity should be exponential in the number of states (|S|), not linear in T, making the algorithm impractical for realistic disease models.

- Proposition 1 states "the approximation error is bounded above by Ω(B̄)" but provides no proof and defines Ω(B̄) ambiguously as "ζ maxt∈T maxBminb∈B̄[t] ||b− b′||1" without defining b'. This is mathematically unsound and cannot be used to justify the algorithm.

### 2. Missing Critical Details
The paper lacks essential details for reproducibility and scientific rigor:

- The paper refers to "Appendix A" for Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2 but these appendices are not included in the submission. This is a serious omission for a paper claiming to present a new algorithm.

- The experimental section doesn't describe data splitting (training/validation/test sets), making it impossible to assess whether the results are overfitted to the dataset.

- There are no statistical significance tests for the claimed 28-68% reduction in screenings. Without p-values or confidence intervals, these claims cannot be evaluated scientifically.

- The paper doesn't address the computational burden of the proposed method. For a real-world healthcare application, the algorithm must be computationally feasible, but the paper provides no runtime analysis for realistic problem sizes.

### 3. Inconsistent Modeling Assumptions
The paper contains multiple inconsistent modeling assumptions:

- The paper claims to handle "non-stationary stochastic processes" but then states "the test outcomes observed over time may follow a non-stationary stochastic process" (Section 1), yet the breast cancer model in Section 4 uses a stationary Markov process.

- The paper states "we assume that the current test result is independent of past test results, conditional on the state trajectory and patient features" (Section 2), but this is contradicted by the breast cancer model in Section 4, which uses a dynamic tumor growth model that inherently creates temporal dependencies.

### 4. Formatting and Presentation Issues
The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies that undermine its professionalism:

- Inconsistent mathematical notation (e.g., "tD" vs "t_D", "δt" vs "δ^t", "Z+" vs "Z+").
- Missing definitions for key terms (e.g., "external information" is not precisely defined).
- Inconsistent spacing around operators and in equations.
- The paper claims to use "a large breast cancer data set" but doesn't specify the exact size of the dataset used for training versus evaluation.

### 5. Related Work and Novelty Claims
The paper's novelty claims are overstated:

- The paper claims "this paper develops the first such personalized screening policies in a very general setting," but previous work by Ayer et al. (2014) and Maillart et al. (2013) already developed personalized POMDP-based screening models for breast cancer. The paper doesn't properly engage with these works.

- The paper cites Krishnamurthy (2016) for POMDP structural results but fails to acknowledge that the value function convexity result (Lemma 1) is a standard POMDP result, not novel.

## Conclusion
While the paper addresses an important problem with potential healthcare impact, it contains multiple serious technical flaws that undermine its scientific contribution. The claims about the generality of the model are contradicted by the specific Markov assumptions used in the examples. The computational complexity analysis is incorrect, and the approximation error bound is mathematically unsound. The paper lacks critical details for reproducibility and fails to properly engage with relevant prior work.

The paper would benefit from a complete revision to address these technical inconsistencies, provide proper mathematical justification for the claims, include the missing appendices, and properly contextualize the work within the existing literature. As submitted, the paper does not meet the standards for publication at NeurIPS due to these substantial technical shortcomings that prevent proper evaluation of the method's claims. I recommend rejection.
