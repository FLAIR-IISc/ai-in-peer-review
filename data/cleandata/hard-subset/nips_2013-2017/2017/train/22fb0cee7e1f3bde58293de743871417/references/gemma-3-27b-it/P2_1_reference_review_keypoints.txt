- Tackles personalized, dynamic screening policies beyond population-level approaches with DPSCREEN.
- DPSCREEN handles non-Markovian disease processes, external information, and computational intractability via an approximately optimal policy with error bounds.
- Empirical results on a breast cancer dataset show substantial screening reductions (28–68%) while maintaining similar detection delays.
- Important problem with high potential healthcare impact.
- Framework is general and not restricted to Markovian models or specific test characteristics.
- Strong theoretical contribution with approximation error bounds and robustness analysis; clear POMDP connection.
- Practical demonstration on real data convincingly supports effectiveness.
- Clear examples showing adaptation to patient risk and dynamic test results.
- Generally well-written introduction and mostly clear mathematical notation.
- Computational complexity and scalability concerns need more detailed discussion and concrete specifics about parallelization.
- Simplifying model assumptions (e.g., no false positives, specific functional forms) may limit realism; sensitivity to these assumptions is underexplored.
- Limited empirical comparison to other personalized screening algorithms or simpler personalization strategies.
- Algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 & 2) lack clarity—belief space construction, "random exploration," sample counts, and sampling criteria are vague.
- Impact of different delay cost functions is shown but requires a more systematic sensitivity analysis.
- Overall technically sound, novel, and significant work with practical promise.
- Reviewer recommends acceptance with minor revisions to address scalability, algorithmic clarity, and sensitivity analyses.
