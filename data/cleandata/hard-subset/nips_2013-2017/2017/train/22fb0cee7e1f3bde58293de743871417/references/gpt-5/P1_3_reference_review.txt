Summary
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a general framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that balance screening frequency against delay in disease detection. The formal model allows: (i) an arbitrary finite-state disease process with an absorbing disease state (not restricted to Markov or semi-Markov), (ii) external information that can trigger early arrivals (e.g., self-exams, comorbidities), (iii) observation models for clinical tests with multiple outcomes, and (iv) delay costs that depend on the time between incidence and detection and can vary by disease. The problem is cast as minimizing a weighted sum of discounted screening costs and delay costs.

Methodologically, the authors derive a belief-state Bellman equation and show (in analogy to POMDPs) that the finite-horizon value function is convex, piecewise-linear in beliefs. Exact dynamic programming via alpha-vectors is intractable, so they propose a point-based value iteration (PBVI)-style approximation: sample likely state trajectories to create a reduced belief subspace, construct reachable beliefs under exploratory and clinically inspired policies, and back up alpha-vectors on this set. They present a qualitative approximation bound in terms of the density of sampled beliefs and provide a robustness result: under continuity/uniqueness assumptions, the optimal policy mapping is locally constant away from a lower-dimensional set, implying that small model estimation errors rarely change optimal actions; together with the PBVI bound, this suggests stability of the approximate policy.

Experiments use a de-identified breast cancer cohort (45k women, age 60–65). A two-state Markov disease model is estimated from observational data: initial cancer risk and transition risk are logistic in the Gail risk score; mammography sensitivity/specificity are taken from prior work; external information is a self-exam lump-size process obeying a growth law with noise. The objective is tuned to match the expected delay of current clinical policies (annual or biennial), and the number of screenings is compared. DPSCREEN reduces expected screenings by 28–45% without self-exams and 57–68% with self-exams at similar expected delay, and personalizes intervals by baseline risk and recent test results. A synthetic comparison with quadratic delay costs shows more aggressive testing, highlighting disease-specific cost modeling.

Relation to prior work: The paper goes beyond earlier personalized screening models framed as POMDPs with Markov disease and stationary test processes (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al.) and beyond Bayesian design approaches that optimize information gain (Rizopoulos, Alaa) by handling external information-triggered arrivals and trajectory-dependent delay costs within a belief DP. Algorithmically it adapts PBVI ideas (Pineau et al.) to a larger belief space via trajectory-based belief sampling.

Strengths
- Ambitious, unified formulation that cleanly captures external arrivals and trajectory-dependent delay costs, bridging OR, medical decision-making, and POMDPs.
- Clear connection to POMDP theory (piecewise-linear convexity of finite-horizon value) extended to this setting.
- Practical, implementable approximation via PBVI with problem-driven belief sampling; computational complexity and parallelization possibilities are discussed.
- Robustness argument (local constancy of optimal action map) is intuitively appealing and relevant for model-based evaluation.
- Empirical illustration on a large real cohort; results suggest substantial reductions in screening while preserving average delay; personalization by risk and history is demonstrated.
- Discussion on disease-dependent delay costs is valuable and often overlooked in ML-for-health.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
Quality/soundness:
- Approximation bound is qualitative and relies on strong assumptions (e.g., perfect tests, bounded screening interval, large horizon). The constant ζ and the belief-density metric are not computable in practice, so the bound gives limited actionable guidance.
- Theorem 1 (local constancy) is stated at a high level (existence of a lower-dimensional exception set) with no proof sketch; conditions like uniqueness of the optimal solution and continuity of the distributions are nontrivial to verify here.
- There is tension between the “no false positives” assumption used earlier and the experimental section that uses empirical sensitivity/specificity; please reconcile assumptions, and clarify which are only for the bound.
- Evaluation is entirely model-based: policies are trained and evaluated on an estimated simulator created from observational data. While common in this domain, claims of large savings would benefit from uncertainty quantification (bootstrap CIs), sensitivity analyses (e.g., to self-exam parameters, noise variance, growth rate), and ablations (effect of the belief sampling size K and B on performance and runtime).
- The objective matches unconditional expected delay E[Δ|R], which is dominated by non-cases; though E[Δ|R,D] is also reported, distributional properties (tails) and time-to-stage detection are not analyzed. These could materially affect clinical utility despite similar means.

Clarity:
- The presentation is dense and relies heavily on appendices. Key quantities (δ, T, W, typical K and B, runtime/memory) should be stated in the main text. Some notation inconsistencies (pin vs pin, ptr vs ptran; m′ spacing) and typos should be fixed.
- The departure from standard POMDPs is asserted; however, one can often augment state (e.g., with last incidence time) to absorb trajectory-dependent costs. Clarify what breaks in POSMDP reductions in your setup (e.g., endogenous decision epochs due to external arrivals).

Originality:
- The modeling composition (external arrivals + trajectory-dependent costs) in a belief-DP is the main novelty; algorithmically, the approach is a PBVI variant with tailored belief sampling. This is reasonable but incremental on the planning side; make the sampling strategy and its empirical benefits more explicit and compare to standard PBVI baselines if possible.

Significance:
- If robust, the reported screening reductions at matched delay are impactful. However, given simulator-based evaluation and reliance on a two-state Markov disease (despite the generality claimed), the empirical evidence is not yet strong enough to establish clinical significance. Stronger comparative baselines (e.g., Ayer/Maillart policies, CISNET-style simulators) and sensitivity analyses would improve confidence.

Questions for authors
- What are typical values of T, δ, K, |B̄[t]|, and wall-clock runtimes? How does performance scale with these?
- How sensitive are results to the self-exam model (ι, σ, threshold ỹ) and to the assumed monthly cadence?
- Can you quantify uncertainty (CIs) for E[N], E[Δ|R], E[Δ|R,D] via bootstrap over patients and posterior samples of model parameters?
- How does DPSCREEN compare against prior POMDP policies for mammography (e.g., Ayer et al.) on your simulator?
- Can you relax the perfect-test assumption in the approximation bound?

Pros
- General, principled framework; nice theoretical connection to POMDPs.
- Practical algorithm with promising results on a large dataset.
- Thoughtful inclusion of external information and disease-specific costs.
- Clear clinical motivation.

Cons
- Theory is mostly qualitative with strong assumptions; limited practical guidance.
- Evaluation is simulator-based with limited uncertainty quantification and ablations.
- Algorithmic novelty is modest relative to PBVI.
- Clarity issues and some inconsistencies in assumptions.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem is important and the framework is promising, but the theoretical guarantees are weakly actionable, and the empirical evidence (entirely simulator-based, limited sensitivity analyses) does not yet support strong claims. With additional experimental rigor, clearer articulation of what is genuinely new algorithmically, and tightened presentation, this could be a strong submission.
