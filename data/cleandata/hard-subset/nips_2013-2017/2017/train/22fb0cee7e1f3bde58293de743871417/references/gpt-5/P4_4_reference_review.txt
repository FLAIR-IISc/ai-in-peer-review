Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that balance screening cost against delay-in-detection cost. The model allows (i) arbitrary finite-state disease processes with an absorbing disease state (beyond Markov/semi-Markov), (ii) exogenous “external information” (e.g., self-exam signals) that can trigger early arrivals, (iii) test outcomes dependent on latent disease trajectories and patient features, and (iv) delay costs that depend on the time lag between incidence and detection and on incidence time. The decision is a recommendation of the next screening time as a function of patient features and history.

The authors formulate a belief-state dynamic program with decision epochs occurring at screening/arrival times, derive Bellman equations with costs depending on trajectories, and show the value function is convex and piecewise linear in the belief (as in POMDPs). They present an exact alpha-vector solution concept that is intractable, and then an approximate, point-based value iteration (PBVI)-style algorithm that constructs a reduced belief set by sampling likely disease trajectories and reachable beliefs under randomized and policy-guided exploration. They provide (sketch-level) bounds on approximation error in terms of a belief-sampling density metric and argue robustness to model-estimation error via a local constancy result for the optimal policy with respect to model parameters.

Empirically, they estimate a two-state Markov disease model for breast cancer on a de-identified Athena dataset (45k patients, 84k mammograms), incorporate a simple tumor growth/self-exam model as external information, and compare DPSCREEN against clinical annual/biennial policies. They report 28–68% reductions in number of screens for similar expected detection delays; they also illustrate how convex (quadratic) delay costs yield more aggressive policies.

The work is related to POMDP-based screening models in operations research (e.g., Ayer et al. OR 2012; Maillart et al. OR 2008; Erenay et al. MSOM 2011) and dynamic designs (Rizopoulos Biostatistics 2010/2011). Unlike these, DPSCREEN claims to handle non-Markov disease evolution, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and exogenous information-triggered arrivals. Methodologically, it leverages standard POMDP properties (piecewise linear convex value functions, PBVI) but adapts belief construction and action spaces to the screening context.

Quality (technical soundness)
Strengths:
- The Bellman formulation and piecewise-linear convexity result are consistent with POMDP theory when costs are linear in trajectory indicators; the extension to trajectory-dependent delay costs is handled by augmenting the belief over trajectories.
- The PBVI-style approximation and trajectory-focused belief sampling are reasonable for managing very high-dimensional beliefs.
- The approximation-error statement, while high-level, aligns with known PBVI bounds that depend on belief coverage.
- The robustness result (local constancy of the policy off a measure-zero set) is plausible given finite alpha-vector partitions.

Weaknesses/clarifications needed:
- The approximation bound (Proposition 1) relies on strong assumptions (perfect tests, bounded screening interval, large T) and an unspecified constant ζ; a precise Lipschitz argument and concrete constants are not given. It is unclear how the bound degrades with noisy tests or unbounded intervals.
- The external information process and arrival modeling enlarge the action/observation space; the derivation of Pr(z, y, τ̃ | b, τ, x) and Bayesian updates is relegated to the supplement. A clearer specification of this kernel in the main text would aid reproducibility.
- The theorem on robustness (local constancy) is stated abstractly. Its practical implication (tolerance to parameter estimation error) would benefit from an empirical sensitivity analysis.
- The breast cancer evaluation is model-based: policy performance is assessed on simulated outcomes from the estimated model. This is standard in screening literature but risks bias if the model is misspecified. There is no held-out validation of predictive adequacy (e.g., calibration, discrimination) of the disease/self-exam models.
- The theory assumes no false positives; the empirical section uses sensitivity/specificity for mammography and biopsies. The mismatch between theoretical assumptions and practical test errors should be reconciled, or theory extended.

Clarity
Strengths:
- The problem formulation and motivation are clearly articulated. The personalization mechanism is explained with an illustrative example.
- The relation to standard POMDP tools and why standard POMDP/POSMDP formulations are insufficient here is discussed.

Weaknesses:
- Some notation inconsistencies (e.g., ptr vs ptran), editorial issues, and heavy reliance on the supplement for algorithms and proofs impede readability.
- Action space definition (recommendation plan τ) and constraints (e.g., bounded interval W) should be introduced earlier and more explicitly.

Originality
Strengths:
- The integration of trajectory-dependent delay costs, action-dependent inter-visit times influenced by exogenous information, and a general disease model within a POMDP-like framework is a novel formulation for screening.
- The belief construction via sampling likely trajectories and reachable beliefs tailored to the screening domain is a practical contribution.

Weaknesses:
- The approximate-solution machinery (PBVI, alpha-vectors, belief sampling) is largely adapted from known POMDP methods. The theoretical contributions (piecewise linearity, error bound, local constancy) are extensions of standard results; novelty lies more in application-specific modeling.
- Empirical validation leverages a simple two-state Markov model, so the claimed generality (non-Markov disease dynamics) is not demonstrated empirically.

Significance
Strengths:
- Personalized screening is a high-impact application. If robust, large reductions in unnecessary screening at fixed detection delay would be clinically meaningful.
- The framework is flexible and could be adapted to other diseases and cost structures.

Weaknesses:
- The main empirical claims rely on simulated counterfactuals from the same model used for policy optimization, without external validation or sensitivity analysis across alternative plausible models. This limits actionable significance.
- Comparisons are to fixed-interval clinical schedules; stronger baselines include state-of-the-art risk-adaptive strategies (e.g., ConfidentCare; density/risk-tailored intervals) and OR models with joint longitudinal-survival modeling.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated formulation capturing important aspects (external information, trajectory costs).
- Sound adaptation of POMDP tools to a challenging personalized screening setting.
- Promising empirical results indicating substantial screening reduction.
- Broad applicability and alignment with NIPS interests in sequential decision-making under uncertainty in healthcare.

Cons against acceptance
- Theoretical guarantees are high-level and rely on restrictive assumptions; key constants and conditions are unspecified.
- Empirical evaluation lacks model validation, off-policy evaluation robustness, and comparisons to strong adaptive baselines; generality claims are not stress-tested on non-Markov/non-stationary settings.
- Reproducibility is hindered by missing algorithmic details in the main text; clarity could be improved.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide explicit kernels for Pr(z, y, τ̃ | b, τ, x), full pseudocode, and complexity benchmarks; release code if possible.
- Tighten Proposition 1 with explicit constants and extend to noisy tests; clarify the role of W and δ.
- Add sensitivity analyses: vary disease/self-exam model parameters, test error rates, and show robustness of policy performance; validate the disease model on held-out data.
- Compare against risk-adaptive baselines (e.g., ConfidentCare; density/risk-tailored intervals; joint models) and report full Pareto frontiers (screens vs detection delay), including E[Δ|D].
- Demonstrate advantages on a non-Markov or non-stationary test process to substantiate generality claims.

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem with a thoughtful formulation and reasonable algorithmic approach, but stronger theoretical specificity and more rigorous empirical validation would significantly increase confidence in the claims.
