- Proposal: DPSCREEN — dynamic personalized screening framework trading screening cost vs delay-to-detection across heterogeneous patients.
- Model: disease as finite-state stochastic process with absorbing disease state; external info (self-exams, comorbidities) endogenously triggers visits; test outcomes conditionally independent given trajectories and features.
- Objective: weighted sum of discounted screening cost and delay cost that can depend on detection lag and time of incidence.
- Formulation: belief-state dynamic program with action-dependent inter-visit times and trajectory-dependent costs.
- Theory: value function is convex and piecewise linear; Bellman equations derived.
- Algorithm: PBVI-style variant using sampled physiological trajectories and reachable belief points to handle ultra–high-dimensional beliefs.
- Guarantees: approximation bound tied to belief-space sampling density and a robustness theorem that optimal policy is locally constant outside a lower-dimensional set.
- Empirical setup: two-state breast-cancer progression estimated on 45k patients (84k mammograms) using Gail features, literature test characteristics, and tumor-growth model for self-exams.
- Empirical result: DPSCREEN reduces expected screening frequency by 28–68% at similar expected detection delays and personalizes by baseline risk and dynamic history.
- Sensitivity note: synthetic experiment shows substantially different policies under quadratic vs linear delay costs.
- Novelty: integrates external information and endogenous arrivals into screening POMDPs, relaxing Markov/stationarity assumptions.
- Strength: important ML-in-health problem, general modeling framework, solid high-level theory, tailored algorithm, and promising data-driven results.
- Theoretical concern: approximation bound uses an unspecified constant ζ and strong assumptions (no false pos/neg, bounded intervals, large T, convex c) partly inconsistent with experiments.
- Robustness concern: “locally constant” policy result lacks detailed conditions and characterization of exceptional set E; finite-sample implications are unclear.
- Possible error: Eq. (3) appears to maximize negative costs — sign or optimization direction needs clarification.
- Model misspecification: trajectory sampling for belief compression forces zero mass outside sampled set; impact of K (trajectories) and B (belief points) not ablated empirically.
- Complexity/scaling concern: complexity expression O(T(B)2 T^2 K|Y||Z|) is opaque, not derived, and no runtimes or scaling plots are provided.
- Evaluation limitation: entirely simulator-based with no off-policy evaluation, bootstrap CIs, or sensitivity analyses.
- Baselines limitation: comparisons only to annual/biennial schedules; missing prior POMDP-based policies and heuristic risk-adaptive intervals.
- Metric limitation: reported expected delays may be dominated by non-cases; clinically salient endpoints (stage at detection, mortality proxies) not reported.
- Reproducibility issue: proprietary dataset, no code, and missing hyperparameter/sampling detail disclosures.
- Clarity issues: crucial details deferred to supplement; inconsistent assumptions on test accuracy; incomplete references and minor typos.
- Question for authors: clarify Eq. (3) sign and optimization direction.
- Question for authors: provide sensitivity/ablation for K, B, action space W, and runtime measurements.
- Question for authors: reconcile theoretical handling of imperfect tests with empirical settings and extend approximation bound if possible.
- Question for authors: compare against prior POMDP-based mammography policies under the estimated model.
- Question for authors: provide uncertainty estimates for E[N], E[Δ], and robustness analyses to model misspecification.
- Question for authors: report fraction of early arrivals triggered by external information vs scheduled follow-ups under learned policies.
- Suggested improvements: reconcile test-accuracy assumptions, add ablations and runtime plots, expand baselines and off-policy evaluation, report clinical endpoints and subgroup analyses, fix references/typos, and release code or detailed pseudo-code.
- Pros for acceptance: novel, timely formulation; implementable algorithm; promising empirical efficiency gains; high relevance to ML for healthcare.
- Cons for acceptance: qualitative guarantees under strong assumptions; simulator-only evaluation with limited baselines and no uncertainty quantification; exposition and reproducibility concerns.
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept — interesting and promising but needs stronger theoretical clarity, broader empirical comparisons, and uncertainty analyses.
