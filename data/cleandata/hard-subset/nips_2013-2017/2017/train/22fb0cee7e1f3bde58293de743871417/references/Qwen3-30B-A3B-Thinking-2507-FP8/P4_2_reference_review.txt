

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against delay in disease detection. The authors address key limitations of existing screening approaches, which typically assume Markov disease processes, stationary test outcome models, and ignore external information that may trigger early screening. Unlike previous works that use POMDPs for specific diseases like breast cancer (e.g., Ayer et al., 2012) or colon cancer (e.g., Erenay et al., 2015), DPSCREEN generalizes to arbitrary finite state stochastic processes for disease progression, incorporates external information processes (e.g., patient self-examinations), and handles delay costs that depend on the full state trajectory rather than just the current state.

The paper builds on POMDP theory but extends it to address the specific challenges of screening policies, particularly the non-stationary nature of disease progression and the trajectory-dependent delay costs. The authors relate their work to controlled sensing (Krishnamurthy, 2017) and medical literature (e.g., MISCAN-COLON model for colon cancer), highlighting how their approach overcomes the restrictive assumptions of these prior works.

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness (Quality):** The paper presents a mathematically rigorous formulation of the personalized screening problem as a constrained optimization task. The authors derive a Bellman equation that accounts for trajectory-dependent delay costs and develop a theoretically grounded approximation method (DPSCREEN) using point-based value iteration with a novel belief space reduction technique. The approximation error bound (Proposition 1) provides important theoretical guarantees that justify the practical approach. The robustness analysis (Theorem 1) demonstrates that estimation errors have minimal impact on the optimal policy, which is a significant strength.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized with clear definitions of the problem components (disease model, external information, screening policies). The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the graphical model in the supplementary materials aids understanding. The experimental section clearly demonstrates the practical value of the approach using breast cancer data, with well-defined metrics for comparison against clinical practices.

**Originality:** The work makes a clear contribution by generalizing screening policy models beyond the restrictive assumptions of prior work. The key innovations include: (1) modeling disease progression as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process (not limited to Markov or Semi-Markov), (2) incorporating external information processes that can trigger early screening, and (3) developing a computationally tractable approximation method with theoretical error bounds. The comparison with previous screening frameworks (Section 5) clearly establishes how this work differs from prior NIPS and medical literature.

**Significance:** The results demonstrate substantial practical impact: 28-68% reduction in screening frequency while maintaining equivalent disease detection delays compared to current clinical practices. The framework is generalizable across diseases (as shown with synthetic examples using linear vs. quadratic delay costs), suggesting potential broad applicability in healthcare decision-making. The method could directly inform clinical guidelines for personalized screening, making it highly relevant to healthcare practitioners.

## Weaknesses

**Computational Complexity:** While the paper provides complexity analysis (O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)), the practical computational requirements for large-scale implementation are not fully addressed. The parameter B (maximum number of belief points sampled) can become large, potentially limiting the method's applicability in real-world settings with high-dimensional feature spaces.

**Experimental Validation:** The breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients) is substantial, but the paper lacks a thorough analysis of how the model parameters (e.g., the Gail model risk scores) affect the personalized policies. More detailed analysis of how the method performs across different risk subgroups would strengthen the empirical contribution.

**Supplementary Material Dependency:** The paper relies heavily on supplementary materials for algorithm details (e.g., Algorithms 1-3) and implementation specifics. While this is common in NIPS papers, the main text could benefit from more concise presentation of the core algorithmic approach to enhance accessibility.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- Addresses a significant gap in the literature by generalizing screening policy models beyond restrictive assumptions
- Provides theoretically grounded approximation with error bounds
- Demonstrates substantial practical impact (28-68% reduction in screenings)
- Clear differentiation from related work in decision theory and medical literature
- Well-structured mathematical formulation with rigorous theoretical contributions

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- Computational complexity may limit real-world applicability without further optimization
- Some algorithmic details depend heavily on supplementary materials
- Limited analysis of how model parameter choices affect personalized policies

## Overall Assessment

DPSCREEN makes a strong technical contribution by extending POMDP-based approaches to handle the unique challenges of personalized screening policies. The work is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates significant practical impact. The theoretical bounds on approximation error and robustness analysis are particularly noteworthy contributions. While the computational complexity and some implementation details could be further clarified, these do not undermine the paper's core contributions.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), well-written and organized (Clarity), makes a clear contribution beyond previous work (Originality), and addresses a significant problem with potential real-world impact (Significance). The results on breast cancer data provide compelling evidence of the method's practical value.

I recommend acceptance as a strong contribution to the field of decision-making under uncertainty with clear applications to healthcare.
