

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against delay costs in disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a general finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating external information (e.g., self-examinations) that may trigger early patient visits. The approach uses point-based value iteration to approximate the optimal screening policy with theoretical guarantees on approximation error. The method is validated using breast cancer data, showing 28-68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining comparable disease detection delays.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a well-formulated problem and appropriate solution approach. The authors provide theoretical bounds on approximation error (Proposition 1) and demonstrate how model estimation errors impact policy performance (Theorem 1). The experimental design is reasonable, using a real breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients aged 60-65) to compare against current clinical practices. However, the assumption of no false positives in test outcomes (stated in Section 2) is strong and potentially limiting in practical applications. The computational complexity analysis (Section 3.2) is theoretically sound but lacks discussion of practical runtimes for large-scale implementation.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The problem formulation in Section 2 is precise and well-motivated, with appropriate mathematical notation. The distinction between the proposed approach and previous works (e.g., [7], [9], [10]) is clearly articulated. The experimental results are presented effectively through tables (Tables 1-2) and figures (Figure 1-2), with sufficient detail to understand the methodology and findings.

**Novelty:** The paper makes a significant contribution by developing a general framework for personalized screening that extends beyond previous approaches. Unlike prior works that assume specific disease models (e.g., Markov processes for disease progression and stationary test outcome models), the proposed method accommodates general finite state stochastic processes. The incorporation of external information that can trigger early screening appointments is novel. The theoretical guarantees on approximation error and the demonstration of how different delay cost structures (linear vs. quadratic) lead to different optimal policies are also novel contributions.

**Significance:** The work addresses an important healthcare problem with meaningful practical implications. The demonstrated 28-68% reduction in screenings while maintaining detection delays represents a substantial improvement over current clinical practices. The framework's generality allows application to various diseases, and the paper effectively shows how different diseases with different delay cost structures require different screening policies (Figure 2). This represents a meaningful advance over previous screening optimization approaches.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a clear technical contribution by extending personalized screening to a general disease model. The theoretical guarantees on approximation error provide important credibility. The real-world validation using breast cancer data demonstrates practical relevance. The comparison with clinical practices provides strong evidence of the method's value.

**Weaknesses:** The assumption of no false positives limits the method's applicability to real-world screening scenarios. The computational complexity discussion lacks practical implementation details (e.g., time to compute policies for large populations). The dataset is limited to 45,000 patients aged 60-65, potentially limiting generalizability to younger populations. The paper does not address potential ethical implications of personalized screening policies.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**

Pro:
- Addresses an important healthcare problem with a technically sound solution
- Novel general framework that extends beyond previous personalized screening models
- Strong theoretical guarantees on approximation error
- Demonstrates substantial improvements (28-68% reduction in screenings) using real-world data
- Clear and well-structured presentation

Con:
- Strong assumption of no false positives in test outcomes
- Limited discussion of practical computational complexity
- Dataset limited to specific demographic group
- No discussion of ethical implications

**Overall Assessment:** This paper makes a significant contribution to the field with a novel, theoretically grounded approach to personalized screening. The method addresses a meaningful healthcare problem with practical implications, and the experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over current clinical practices. While there are some limitations (particularly the no false positives assumption), these do not detract from the core technical contribution. The paper aligns well with NIPS' focus on rigorous, impactful machine learning research with real-world applications. The work represents a meaningful advancement in the field of personalized healthcare optimization.
