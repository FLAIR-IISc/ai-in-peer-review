- Proposes a novel personalized screening framework that trades screening costs against detection delay and permits external triggers for early visits.  
- Generalizes disease modeling beyond Markov/Semi-Markov assumptions, covering survival and first-hitting-time models.  
- Develops an approximately optimal policy via a modified Point-Based Value Iteration and provides theoretical approximation error bounds.  
- Proves the value function is convex and piecewise linear (Lemma 1), extending POMDP theory.  
- Empirical results on breast cancer data show 28–68% fewer screenings with maintained expected detection delays.  
- Shows different delay cost functions (linear vs quadratic) yield qualitatively different screening policies.  
- Insufficiently distinguishes and compares the approach to existing POMDP-based screening methods [7,9]; practical differences are unclear.  
- Unrealistic model assumptions (no false positives/negatives) ignore important clinical realities like mammography FP/FN rates.  
- Computational complexity analysis is vague and lacks guidance on scaling to realistic problem sizes (e.g., T=100, K=1000).  
- Empirical comparisons are limited (only annual screening); should compare to clinical guidelines for different risk groups.  
- Practical barriers—need for accurate disease-progression models and computational feasibility—are not adequately discussed.  
- Comparison to recent POMDP approximation work ([40],[41]) and belief-space sampling methods is superficial.  
- Recommendation: Accept — technically strong, practically relevant contribution despite noted weaknesses.  
- Key improvements requested: detailed comparisons with [7,9], discussion of FP/FN handling, nuanced empirical baselines, and clearer scalability analysis.
