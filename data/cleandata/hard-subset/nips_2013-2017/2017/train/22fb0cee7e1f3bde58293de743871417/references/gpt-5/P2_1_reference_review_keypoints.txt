- Proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening that trades off test burden and detection delay with a general (non-Markov) finite-state disease model.  
- Key modeling advances: trajectory-dependent delay costs, external information triggering unscheduled visits, and action-dependent inter-visit times.  
- Formulates a belief-state DP over distributions of entire state trajectories and a diagnosis flag, proving convexity and piecewise-linearity of the value function and deriving an exact (but intractable) alpha-vector recursion.  
- Proposes a PBVI-style approximation by sampling likely physiological trajectories and reachable beliefs, with a sampling-density error bound and a robustness/local-constancy result for policies.  
- Empirically fits a breast cancer model from large data and reports 28–68% reductions in expected screenings at matched expected delay versus annual/biennial guidelines.  
- Advances over prior work by relaxing Markov/stationarity assumptions, supporting external signals and trajectory-dependent costs, and removing fixed decision epochs.  
- Theoretical contributions are plausible but presented high-level; assumptions for error bounds (no FP/FN, convex costs, bounded intervals, large horizon) are restrictive and mismatch empirical setup.  
- The local constancy theorem is intuitive but stated abstractly and would benefit from citations to standard continuity results.  
- Algorithmic description is sensible for belief construction, but action space, pruning strategies, and practical implementation details are underspecified.  
- Complexity is discussed theoretically, but there is no empirical runtime, scalability, or pruning-performance evidence.  
- Empirics use a substantial dataset and standard counterfactual simulation protocol, with compelling screening reductions but many experimental details relegated to the supplement.  
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses, especially regarding model misspecification and external-information parameter sensitivity.  
- Presentation is well motivated but notation-heavy, with many derivations in the supplement and unclear separation of theoretical vs. experimental assumptions.  
- The combined setting (trajectory costs, endogenous arrivals, policy-dependent visit times) is novel; PBVI adaptation is reasonable though not deeply novel.  
- Potentially significant for clinical decision support if validated, with reusable framework beyond breast cancer.  
- Pros: principled framework for an important application, theoretical structure, implementable approximation, and strong empirical gains on real data.  
- Cons: theoretical assumptions diverge from empirical reality, key details in supplement, model-based evaluation only with limited baselines and no sensitivity/off-policy analyses, and clarity/reference issues.  
- Suggestions: reconcile theoretical assumptions with the empirical model, add proof sketches/citations for main lemmas/theorems, report runtimes and hyperparameters (K/B/T/W), detail pruning and scalability, perform sensitivity/misspecification ablations and stronger baseline comparisons, and release code/pseudocode.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept; promising and thoughtful but needs stronger empirical validation, clarity, and connection between theory and practice.  
- Reviewer confidence: moderate.
