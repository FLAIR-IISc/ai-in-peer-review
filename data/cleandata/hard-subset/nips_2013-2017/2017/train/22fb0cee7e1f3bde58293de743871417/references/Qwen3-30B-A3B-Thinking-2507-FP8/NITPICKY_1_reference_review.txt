

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

## Summary
This paper proposes a method for dynamic personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against delay in disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a general finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporate external information (e.g., self-examinations), and develop a computationally efficient approximate solution to the screening policy optimization problem. The method is evaluated on breast cancer screening data, claiming 28-68% reductions in screening frequency while maintaining detection delay.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important real-world problem with clear societal impact. The theoretical foundation using belief states and Bellman equations is sound, and the extension to non-Markovian disease processes represents a meaningful generalization over prior work that assumed Markovian disease evolution. The experimental results are promising, showing significant reductions in screening frequency compared to current clinical practices.

## Weaknesses

### Technical and Methodological Issues
1. **Inconsistent Model Specification**: The paper claims to handle "many disease models" including "dynamic survival models" and "first hitting time models," yet the experimental evaluation uses a two-state Markov model (S={H,D}), which is a highly specific case of the general model. This disconnect between the general claims and specific implementation weakens the contribution.

2. **Notational Confusion**: In Equation (3), the term "t+ τ̃" is ambiguous. If τ̃ represents time until next arrival, it should be t + τ̃, but this indexing implies the next time step could exceed the horizon T, creating potential inconsistencies in the value function.

3. **Definition of Approximation Error**: In Proposition 1, the approximation error bound is defined as Ω(B̄) = ζ maxt∈T maxBminb∈B̄[t] ||b− b' ||1, but b' is never defined. This is a critical omission that undermines the theoretical contribution.

4. **Computational Complexity**: The claimed complexity O(T (B)2T 2K|Y||Z|) contains multiple inconsistencies. The exponent "2T" is confusing (is it T squared or 2^T?), and the notation "B̄[t]" is not properly defined in the complexity expression.

### Clarity and Presentation Issues
1. **Typos and Formatting**: The paper contains several typos including "st Conference" (should be "The Conference"), inconsistent capitalization of "NIPS," and unclear references to "st" in the bibliography. These minor errors undermine the professionalism of the work.

2. **Ambiguous Terminology**: The paper uses "ex ante survival curve" without defining "ex ante" in this context. The term "risk assessment" is used without clear operationalization in the experiments.

3. **Missing Details**: The paper states they "divide the population into two risk groups" but does not explain how this division was determined (e.g., cutoff values, statistical significance). The comparison with clinical policies lacks statistical significance testing for the claimed reductions.

### Related Work and Originality
The paper claims to be "the first such personalized screening policies in a very general setting," but fails to adequately distinguish from prior work. While citing [7], [9], and [10] as using Markov disease processes, it doesn't clearly explain how their approach differs from these POMDP-based methods beyond the model assumptions. The related work section is insufficiently detailed to establish the novelty of the contribution.

### Experimental Validation
The breast cancer dataset of 45,000 patients is well-chosen, but the paper lacks critical details:
- No train-test split is described
- Statistical significance of performance improvements is not reported
- The assumption of no false positives (in test outcomes) is strong and not justified
- The model parameters are estimated using Gail model, but the paper doesn't explain how this affects the generalizability of the approach

## Recommendation
While the paper addresses an important problem and contains some technically sound elements, the numerous technical inconsistencies, unclear definitions, and insufficient experimental validation prevent it from meeting NIPS standards for quality and clarity. The paper's claims about generality are not supported by the specific implementation details provided, and the minor but numerous technical errors (especially in the theoretical framework) undermine the paper's credibility.

The paper's contribution appears to be a minor extension of existing POMDP methods to a specific medical domain, rather than a significant advancement in the field of sequential decision making. The claimed 28-68% reduction in screenings is promising, but the lack of statistical validation and methodological transparency prevents us from fully assessing this contribution.

I recommend rejection. The paper would benefit from significant revision to clarify the theoretical framework, provide more detailed experimental validation, and better contextualize the contribution relative to existing work in POMDPs and personalized screening.
