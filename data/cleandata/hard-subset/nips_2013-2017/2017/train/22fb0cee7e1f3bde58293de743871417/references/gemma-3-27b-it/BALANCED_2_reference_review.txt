This paper tackles the important problem of personalized screening policies, moving beyond one-size-fits-all approaches to account for disease progression, patient features, and external information. The core idea is to develop a computationally tractable method for approximately optimizing screening schedules, balancing the costs of screening with the costs of delayed detection. The authors address several challenges inherent in this problem, including non-Markovian disease processes, the need to reason about beliefs about disease state, and the impact of external information triggering unscheduled visits. The proposed DPSCREEN approach combines belief updating, point-based value iteration, and careful approximation to yield a practical algorithm. The application to breast cancer, leveraging a large dataset, is a strong point, demonstrating both personalization based on risk factors and dynamic adjustment based on test results, leading to significant reductions in screening frequency while maintaining comparable detection delays.

**Strengths:**

* **Problem Importance & Novelty:** Personalized screening is a crucial area, and the authors address a complex problem with a sophisticated framework. The ability to handle non-Markovian disease progression and external information represents a significant advancement over existing approaches.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method, DPSCREEN, appears technically sound. The use of point-based value iteration, combined with a careful analysis of approximation errors and robustness to estimation errors, is well-motivated. The derivation of bounds on approximation error (Proposition 1) is a particularly valuable contribution.
* **Comprehensive Modeling:** The model incorporates a wealth of relevant factors: patient features, disease progression, external information, test outcomes, and costs.  The mathematical formulation is thorough and well-defined.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The application to a real-world breast cancer dataset provides strong evidence of the efficacy of the approach. The reported reductions in screening frequency (28-68%) are substantial and practically significant.  The comparison against current clinical policies is insightful.
* **Clear Presentation of Personalization:** Figure 1 effectively illustrates how the policy adapts to different patient profiles and dynamic test results, demonstrating the personalization aspect.
* **Discussion of Disease Impact:** The synthetic exercise showing how the policy changes with different cost functions is valuable and highlights the importance of tailoring the approach to the specific disease.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors acknowledge the computational complexity and discuss potential parallelization, the scaling of the algorithm remains a concern, particularly for diseases with very large state spaces or long time horizons. More detailed analysis of the practical runtime performance on larger datasets would be beneficial.
* **Model Assumptions:** The model relies on several assumptions (e.g., independence of test results given the state trajectory) that may not hold in all real-world scenarios. A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to violations of these assumptions would strengthen the paper.
* **Estimation of Model Parameters:** The model requires estimation of various parameters (e.g., transition probabilities, hazard rates). The authors provide a brief overview of the estimation process but lack detail. A more thorough description of the estimation procedure, including potential biases and uncertainties, is needed.
* **Limited Benchmarking:** The comparison is primarily against annual screening.  Including comparisons with other established risk-based screening strategies, or more sophisticated adaptive policies, would provide a more complete evaluation.
* **Clarity of Algorithm 1:** Algorithm 1's description of belief space construction could be clearer. The process of sampling trajectories and constructing the reachable belief sets is not fully intuitive and could benefit from a more detailed explanation or illustration.
* **Lack of Generalizability Discussion:** While the breast cancer example is compelling, the authors could strengthen the paper by discussing the potential challenges and adaptations needed to apply DPSCREEN to other diseases with different characteristics.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critically important and challenging problem in healthcare.
* Presents a novel and technically sound approach with strong theoretical grounding.
* Demonstrates significant potential for improving screening efficiency and reducing healthcare costs.
* Empirically validated on a large, real-world dataset with promising results.
* Offers valuable insights into the trade-offs between screening frequency and detection delay.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity may limit scalability to more complex scenarios.
* Reliance on model assumptions and potential sensitivities to violations.
* Limited comparison to other state-of-the-art approaches.
* Algorithm description in some sections could be improved for clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of personalized medicine and decision-making under uncertainty. The proposed DPSCREEN approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. While there are some weaknesses, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the following minor revisions:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and potential strategies for scaling the algorithm.
2.  Elaborate on the sensitivity analysis with respect to the model assumptions.
3.  Expand the discussion of model parameter estimation, including details on potential biases and uncertainties.
4.  Include comparisons to other relevant risk-based screening strategies.
5.  Clarify the description of Algorithm 1, potentially with an illustrative example.
6.  Discuss the potential challenges and adaptations required to apply DPSCREEN to other diseases.
