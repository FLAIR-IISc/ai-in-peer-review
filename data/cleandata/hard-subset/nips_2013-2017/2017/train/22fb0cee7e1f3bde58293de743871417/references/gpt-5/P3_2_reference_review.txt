Summary
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for computing dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off test costs and delay-to-detection costs. The model allows: (i) arbitrary finite-state disease dynamics with an absorbing disease state, (ii) endogenous visit timing via external information (e.g., self-exam signals) that can trigger earlier screening, (iii) trajectory-based delay costs, and (iv) belief-state planning with a Bellman equation over a high-dimensional belief space on trajectories. Exact dynamic programming is intractable; the authors adapt point-based value iteration (PBVI) by sampling likely disease trajectories and reachable beliefs, and derive piecewise-linear convexity of the value function, an approximation error bound dependent on belief-space sampling density, and a robustness result stating local constancy of the optimal action with respect to model perturbations. On a large breast-cancer dataset (45k women aged 60–65), they estimate a simple two-state (H/D) model with self-exam signals, then show substantial reductions (28–68%) in the expected number of screens while matching expected delay compared to annual or biennial guidelines. They further illustrate that convex delay costs induce more aggressive screening.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on POMDP/POSMDP methods (Smallwood & Sondik; PBVI) and prior screening formulations in OR/medical decision making (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Erenay et al., Rizopoulos et al.). Compared to prior screening POMDPs that assume Markov disease and stationary observation models, the authors emphasize their generality (trajectory-dependent costs, external information affecting arrival times). The empirical evaluation, however, instantiates a standard two-state Markov model with conventional sensitivity/specificity and a simple external signal model. The analysis of alpha-vector structure and piecewise linearity closely mirrors standard POMDP results.

Strengths
- Ambitious, practically relevant problem: endogenizing visit timing and personalizing screening to static features and dynamic test histories.
- Methodological pipeline is clear: belief-state DP, PBVI with trajectory sampling, and policy extraction; the approach is broadly applicable to survival/first-hitting-time models.
- Empirical application on a large real-world dataset with reasonable calibration of some components, and clear clinical outcome metrics.
- The empirical finding—large test reductions without higher expected delay—is potentially impactful, especially when incorporating external information.
- Discussion of robustness to model estimation error and of the role of disease-dependent delay costs is useful.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical guarantees rely on unrealistic assumptions. Proposition 1 requires perfect tests (no false positives/negatives) and a bounded inter-screen interval; experiments use imperfect tests. The bound is qualitative (depends on an unspecified constant ζ) and lacks finite-sample rates or practical guidance for B̄ construction.
- The “local constancy” result (Theorem 1) is essentially a standard property of PWLC value functions/alpha-vector partitions: optimal actions are constant within polyhedral regions except on measure-zero boundaries. Framed as robustness, it adds little beyond known POMDP structure, and the uniqueness assumption conflicts with the existence of a nontrivial exception set.
- Generality vs practicality: the main novelty is handling trajectory-based costs and endogenous arrivals by inflating the hidden state to trajectories. This makes the belief space enormous; tractability hinges entirely on aggressive sampling of ST. While PBVI is a reasonable choice, the paper lacks ablations or diagnostics that show coverage/quality of sampled beliefs, sensitivity to K, B̄, or runtime/memory scaling with T, |Z|, |Y|.
- Bellman equation/sign conventions and clarity: Eq. (3) maximizes negative costs; earlier the objective is minimization. There is some sign/notation inconsistency and limited derivation of Pr(z, y, τ̃ | b, τ, x), which is central because actions affect inter-visit times and external information processes.
- Model-data mismatch and validation: The empirical disease model is a simple two-state Markov process parametrized by Gail risk; delay costs are linear; and self-exam parameters are fixed from literature. There is no thorough model validation (posterior predictive checks, calibration plots, sensitivity to misspecification, or uncertainty in downstream policy evaluation), no counterfactual evaluation, and no confidence intervals on reported improvements. This weakens the strength of claims about real-world gains.
- Assumptions contradict across sections: early sections assume no false positives; the empirical model includes mammogram false positives but only diagnoses when both mammogram and biopsy are positive; Prop. 1 further assumes no false negatives. These inconsistencies should be resolved.
- Related work positioning could be more balanced: prior OR/medical screening models with external signals or joint longitudinal-survival modeling are acknowledged, but direct empirical comparisons to those baselines (beyond “annual/biennial”) are absent.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level (casting as a POMDP with endogenous arrivals via action-dependent inter-visit times and trajectory-based costs). However, proofs and guarantees either mirror standard POMDP properties or hinge on restrictive assumptions. Empirical evaluation is promising but lacks rigorous validation and uncertainty quantification.

Clarity
Readable overall, but some key definitions and derivations are deferred to the supplement. Clarify the Bellman sign convention, the construction of Pr(z, y, τ̃ | b, τ, x), the complexity expression, and the exact test error assumptions across theory and experiments. Including a small worked example would help.

Originality
Moderate. The combination of trajectory-based costs, endogenous arrivals, and PBVI for personalized screening is a useful synthesis, though each component builds on known ideas. Theoretical contributions are incremental.

Significance
Potentially significant given the importance of personalized screening, but the current empirical evidence is not yet sufficiently convincing for strong clinical claims. As a methodological contribution, it could influence follow-up work on personalized surveillance policies.

Suggestions for improvement
- Resolve test accuracy assumptions; relax Prop. 1 to handle realistic sensitivity/specificity or provide an empirical bound.
- Provide a detailed derivation of Eq. (3) and the transition/arrival probability model; correct sign inconsistencies.
- Add ablations: vary K and |B̄|; report runtime and memory; show policy stability vs sampling density; quantify Ω(B̄) in practice.
- Include uncertainty: bootstrap or Bayesian posterior over model parameters and propagate to policy outcomes; report CIs.
- Compare against prior personalized screening POMDPs (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al.) and information-gain policies.
- Validate on semi-synthetic ground truth where the disease process is known, to isolate optimization benefits from model fit.
- Discuss fairness and subgroup performance (beyond two risk groups), and harms like false positives/biopsy rates.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem is important and the approach is promising, but theoretical novelty is limited and the empirical evaluation lacks rigor needed to substantiate strong claims. With stronger validation, clearer theory under realistic assumptions, and comparative baselines, this could become a solid contribution.

Pros
- Addresses an impactful real problem with a unified decision-theoretic framework.
- Clear personalization to static and dynamic patient information.
- Promising empirical reductions in screening burden.

Cons
- Guarantees rely on unrealistic assumptions; novelty of theory is limited.
- Insufficient empirical validation and uncertainty quantification.
- Clarity and internal consistency issues in the core Bellman formulation and assumptions.
