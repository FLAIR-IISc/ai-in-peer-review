

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against the delay in disease detection. The authors formulate the problem as a partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) with a novel belief space that accommodates non-stationary disease processes, external information, and dynamic patient histories. They develop an approximate solution using point-based value iteration with a modified belief space construction, and validate their approach using breast cancer data, reporting 28-68% reductions in screenings while maintaining the same expected delay in detection.

## Quality
The paper contains several technical inconsistencies that undermine its claims. In the Bellman equation (3), the delay cost function C̃(~s, t, z, l) is incorrectly defined: for t < T, l = 0, z ∈ Z+, it uses wC(t - tD; tD), but the delay should be based on detection time (td), not current time (t). This misdefinition invalidates the subsequent analysis. The paper claims "the cost of delay is 0 if disease never occurs or occurs only at time t = T," but if disease occurs at time T, detection would occur at td ≥ T, making the delay cost C(td - T; T), which is not necessarily zero.

The approximation error bound in Proposition 1 is misstated: Ω(B̄) = ζ maxt∈T maxB minb∈B̄[t] ||b - b' ||1 is not properly defined (what is b'?), and the paper fails to provide the proof. The claim that "the test outcome is accurate, i.e. no false positives and no false negatives" (Proposition 1) contradicts the breast cancer model description where z=3 represents "both mammogram and biopsy is positive," implying false positives are possible.

The computational complexity analysis contains an error: O(T (B)2T 2K|Y||Z|) is likely incorrect as the complexity should depend exponentially on T, not linearly. The paper also fails to provide any sensitivity analysis showing how the policy changes with estimation error, despite claiming to address this in Section 3.3.

## Clarity
The paper suffers from inconsistent notation and undefined terms. The symbol "tD" for incidence time is used without clear definition, and the distinction between "tD" (incidence time) and "td" (detection time) is confusingly maintained. The paper uses both "~s" and "s" for state trajectories without explanation. The notation "H = ⋃T t=0H(t)" uses inconsistent capitalization (H vs H(t)).

The breast cancer model description contains unexplained notation: "Y(t) = g(t) + (t)" fails to define "(t)". The claim "each woman has one self-examination per month [25][26]" is not supported by the cited references. The paper also inconsistently refers to "ex ante survival curve" without defining it in the main text (Figure 1).

The results in Table 1 lack clarity: the paper states "expected reduction in number of screens is 57-68% (depending on risk group)" but doesn't specify which risk group achieves which percentage, making the results difficult to interpret.

## Originality
While the paper claims to be "the first such personalized screening policies in a very general setting," it fails to clearly articulate how its approach differs from previous works. The authors cite [7], [9], and [10] as previous POMDP-based screening models that assume stationary Markov processes, but they don't sufficiently explain how their method overcomes these limitations. The proposed belief space construction (Algorithm 1) appears to be a standard modification of point-based value iteration rather than a novel contribution.

The paper's claim of addressing "all of these problems" (listed in the introduction) is overstated. For instance, the paper does not provide a new method for handling non-stationary disease processes beyond what is already possible in standard POMDPs.

## Significance
The claimed 28-68% reduction in screenings is promising, but the paper lacks statistical significance testing for these results. The paper states "the policy we construct leads to large reductions (28-68%) in screening" in the abstract but reports "57-68% reduction" in the results, creating an inconsistency. The paper also fails to discuss computational scalability to larger populations or more complex disease models, which is crucial for real-world implementation.

The paper's claim that "the regime of operation (frequency of tests vs expected delay in detection) for the policies for the two costs are significantly different" is not adequately supported by the limited analysis in Figure 2. The paper does not provide a quantitative measure of this difference.

## Conclusion
The paper presents an interesting application of POMDP methods to personalized screening, but suffers from numerous technical inconsistencies, unclear definitions, and unsupported claims. The methodological flaws undermine the validity of the results, and the paper fails to clearly articulate its novel contributions relative to existing work. While the potential impact of personalized screening policies is significant, the paper's execution does not meet NIPS standards for technical rigor.

The paper should be rejected due to substantial technical flaws that prevent the reader from verifying the claims. The authors should address these issues before reconsideration.
