Summary
This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off screening costs and delay-in-detection costs. The model: (i) represents disease as a finite-state hidden process with an absorbing disease state, (ii) allows non-Markov state trajectories, (iii) incorporates exogenous “external information” (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger early arrivals, and (iv) accounts for test outcomes. The optimization problem minimizes a weighted sum of discounted screening costs and delay costs; beliefs are over entire trajectories and a diagnosis label. The authors derive a Bellman equation and show the value function is convex, piecewise-linear in belief (alpha-vectors). Because exact solution is intractable, they propose a point-based value iteration (PBVI) variant that samples likely state-trajectories and reachable beliefs. They provide an approximation-error bound in terms of a sampling-density metric Ω(B̄) and a qualitative robustness result stating local constancy of the optimal policy w.r.t. model/belief perturbations off a lower-dimensional exceptional set. Experiments on a de-identified breast cancer dataset (age 60–65) estimate a 2-state Markov disease model (Gail score features), add a self-exam signal via a tumor growth law, and compare against annual/biennial screening by tuning the weight to match expected delay; they report 28–68% fewer screens at similar expected delays and show how convex delay costs (quadratic) lead to more aggressive testing.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on POMDP/POSMDP methods (Sondik; PBVI; controlled sensing) and prior personalized screening models (Ayer et al.; Maillart et al.; Erenay et al.; Rizopoulos et al.). Authors claim novelty in handling non-Markov disease processes, trajectory-dependent costs, and endogenous/early arrivals via external information. The piecewise-linear convex value-function result extends standard POMDP theory to their setting. The algorithmic approach adapts PBVI with trajectory-belief sampling.

Strengths
- Ambitious generality: unifies diverse disease/test/external-information models, including dynamic arrivals and trajectory-dependent costs.
- Clear conceptual motivation for personalization and modeling external information.
- Theoretical pieces: convex piecewise-linear value function; an approximation-error statement; qualitative robustness claim on policy stability to estimation error.
- Practical orientation: end-to-end illustration on breast cancer with a tunable trade-off; shows impact of cost shape (linear vs quadratic delay).

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness/clarity
- Bellman/sign convention: Eq. (3) maximizes −E[C̃]+E[V], while the problem is posed as a minimization. If V is “negative total cost,” this must be stated and used consistently. As written, the sign/use of C̃, V is confusing.
- Immediate cost C̃ (Eq. 2) mixes cases and timing: charging the entire delay at detection time and otherwise at T is acceptable, but the case distinctions are not mutually exclusive; the t = T, l = 0 case overlaps with other branches. Please provide a precise, non-overlapping definition and justify that delay cost is charged exactly once.
- Complexity claims are unclear: “worst-case computation of the first term is |S|T” (is this |S|^T?) and the overall complexity O(T (B)2 T^2 K |Y||Z|) is dimensionally awkward (T multiplied twice). Please derive a clean bound with explicit dependence on |S|, |Z|, |Y|, K, horizon, and action space size.
- Approximation bound: Ω(B̄) = ζ maxt maxBminb∈B̄[t] ||b − b′||1 is ill-defined (what is “maxB”? what is b′?). ζ is an unspecified constant (“max expected loss in one time slot”), making the bound non-operational. Assumptions for Prop. 1 include “no false positives and no false negatives,” which contradicts earlier “no false positives” only; also unrealistic for experiments with mammography/biospy (z=2 is a false-positive mammogram). The bound’s applicability to the experimental setting is unclear.
- Robustness Theorem 1: claims local constancy of the optimal recommendation mapping off a lower-dimensional set. This is nontrivial and surprising for POMDP-like problems where optimal policies can change abruptly. No proof sketch or citation is provided; assumptions (e.g., regularity/uniqueness) and topology on L are vague. As stated, it’s hard to assess correctness or usefulness.
- Independence assumptions: test outcomes independent of external information given state and features may be too strong when the external signal (lump size) correlates with imaging quality beyond state; at minimum, discuss plausibility and potential bias.

Model/algorithmic details
- Belief representation is over ST × {0,1}, which is enormous; the trajectory sampling K is crucial. There is no guidance on K, coverage, or how to assess sampling density Ω(B̄) in practice. No ablation on K/B shows the trade-off.
- Construction of Pr(z,y,τ̃ | b,τ,x): τ̃ depends on threshold crossings and scheduled times; details are deferred to appendices not provided. Without a fully specified generative model, Eq. (3) is not reproducible.
- Action space: recommendation plan τ: Z → T is large; any constraints (e.g., max interval W) materially affect tractability and performance. This is only briefly mentioned; no sensitivity is shown.

Empirical evaluation
- Experimental scope is narrow relative to claims: despite advertising general non-Markov models, experiments use a simple 2-state, stationary Markov model with fixed sensitivity/specificity from literature, and a stylized self-exam process. No real-world labels or time-to-detection data are evaluated; everything is via a learned/specified generative model.
- Tuning w to match expected delay of annual/biennial policies biases the comparison in DPSCREEN’s favor (minimize screens under a fixed delay level). Report the full Pareto frontier (E[N], E[Δ]) with confidence intervals, and compare frontiers.
- No uncertainty quantification: no CIs over E[N], E[Δ], no sensitivity to priors, MCMC convergence diagnostics, or misspecification of self-exam parameters (ι, σ, ỹ).
- Reproducibility gaps: key algorithmic pseudocode, graphical model, proofs, and the tables/figures referenced are in appendices not available here. Without them, it’s difficult to verify claims or reproduce results.

Presentation and references
- Numerous formatting/citation issues (e.g., incomplete references, duplicates, “[32]” cited but not in list; “st Conference on NIPS” artifact). Table 1, 2, Figure 1, 2 are referenced but not shown.
- Overclaims: “first such personalized screening policy in a very general setting” seems too broad given prior POMDP screening (Ayer, Maillart) and controlled sensing literature; a more nuanced positioning is needed.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem with a principled sequential decision-making framework.
- Incorporates external information and trajectory-dependent delay costs.
- Extends piecewise-linear value-function logic; attempts an approximation/theory story.
- Empirical illustration suggests substantial reductions in screening burden at similar delays.

Cons
- Multiple technical ambiguities and potential inconsistencies (objective/Bellman signs, C̃ cases, complexity, approximation bound assumptions).
- Theoretical results are stated without adequate detail or convincing proofs/citations.
- Evaluation relies on a simplistic model and tuning to match baselines; lacks uncertainty analysis and sensitivity studies.
- Reproducibility and clarity issues; heavy reliance on unavailable appendices; many formatting/reference errors.

Questions for authors
1) Please clarify Eq. (2)-(3) (signs, non-overlapping cases) and provide a clean derivation of the Bellman recursion and complexity.
2) Define Ω(B̄) precisely, specify ζ, and reconcile assumptions (false positives/negatives) with experiments.
3) Provide a proof sketch or precise citation for Theorem 1, including assumptions and topology.
4) Detail the construction of Pr(z,y,τ̃ | b,τ,x) and the action space constraints; show sensitivity to K, B, W.
5) Provide full Pareto frontiers with CIs and sensitivity analyses (e.g., to self-exam parameters, model misspecification).

Recommendation: Reject
While the problem and high-level approach are compelling, the submission in its current form has too many technical ambiguities, missing details, and presentation issues to meet NeurIPS standards. With corrected theory, clearer algorithms, rigorous empirical evaluation (with uncertainty/sensitivity), and improved clarity, this work could be impactful. My decision is driven by the accumulation of smaller issues that collectively undermine confidence.
