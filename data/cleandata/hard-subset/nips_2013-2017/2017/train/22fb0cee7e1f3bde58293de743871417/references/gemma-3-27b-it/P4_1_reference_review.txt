This paper addresses the challenging problem of personalized screening policies, aiming to balance the benefits of early detection against the costs and risks of screening procedures. The core idea is to develop a computationally tractable framework that accounts for disease-specific progression, patient features, and dynamic test results. The authors formulate this as a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) but recognize and overcome the limitations of standard POMDP approaches due to the complexities of disease progression and patient arrival patterns. They propose a point-based value iteration (PBVI) approximation, tailored for this setting with a novel belief space construction method. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of DPSCREEN on a breast cancer dataset, showcasing reductions in screening frequency while maintaining delay performance, and illustrates the impact of cost function choice through synthetic experiments.

The strengths of this paper are significant. The problem addressed is both important and challenging, with direct relevance to clinical practice. The proposed methodology represents a substantial advance in the field, moving beyond traditional approaches that rely on simplifying assumptions about disease progression and patient behavior. The theoretical results, including the approximation error bound (Proposition 1) and robustness analysis (Theorem 1), provide a solid foundation for the method. The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating the potential of DPSCREEN to improve screening efficiency. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches and position their work within the broader context of decision-making under uncertainty. The detailed discussion of computational complexity and the proposal for parallelization are valuable. The inclusion of a graphical model (mentioned in the text, referenced in the supplement) is helpful for understanding the framework.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge the computational challenges, a more detailed discussion of scalability and practical implementation considerations would be beneficial. The model’s assumptions, particularly regarding the independence of test results given the state trajectory and features, could be further scrutinized. The parameter estimation process, while described, could be expanded upon, including details on sensitivity analysis and validation of the estimated parameters. The choice of specific functional forms for hazard rates and cost functions feels somewhat arbitrary; justification for these choices, or exploration of alternative forms, would strengthen the analysis. The supplementary material is extensive, and some key details are relegated there, making it harder to grasp the full picture from the main text alone. While the clinical relevance is established, a deeper discussion of how DPSCREEN would integrate into existing clinical workflows would be valuable. The performance improvements (28-68% reduction) sound promising but are presented as relative reductions without clear absolute numbers of tests, making the practical impact less immediately apparent.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach tackles a complex problem with a novel formulation and solution methodology that extends existing POMDP techniques.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical results, including approximation error bounds and robustness analysis, provide strong guarantees for the method’s performance.
*   **Significance:** The potential to improve screening efficiency and reduce healthcare costs is significant. The framework is generalizable to other diseases beyond breast cancer.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, although some details are moved to the supplement. The problem is clearly articulated and the proposed solution is well-explained.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** Despite discussion of parallelization, the computational cost remains a concern for large-scale applications.
*   **Model Assumptions:** The independence assumptions may not hold in all real-world scenarios.
*   **Implementation Details:** A more detailed discussion of practical implementation considerations and integration with existing clinical workflows is needed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of personalized screening. The novelty, technical soundness, and potential impact of the proposed methodology outweigh the identified weaknesses. While some aspects could be further elaborated, the paper presents a compelling and well-supported argument for its acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding computational scalability, model assumptions, and implementation details.
