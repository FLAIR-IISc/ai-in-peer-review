

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework for constructing personalized disease screening policies that accounts for disease progression models, patient features, and dynamic external information. The authors address a significant gap in existing screening literature by moving beyond restrictive Markov assumptions to develop a general approach applicable to various disease models. The core innovation lies in formulating the screening problem as a POMDP extension that handles non-stationary disease processes, incorporates external information (e.g., self-examinations), and personalizes screening based on both static patient features and dynamic history.

The paper's strongest contribution is its conceptual boldness in generalizing screening policy design. Previous works [7, 9, 10] are limited to Markov disease processes and stationary test outcome models, while this paper provides a framework that accommodates dynamic survival models, first hitting time models, and non-stationary external information. The authors effectively demonstrate that their approach can handle the complexity of real-world screening scenarios that other methods cannot.

Methodologically, the paper presents a computationally tractable solution through a point-based value iteration approach (DPSCREEN) that provides theoretical bounds on approximation error. The theoretical contribution (Lemma 1 and Proposition 1) establishes that the value function is convex and piecewise linear, enabling efficient approximation. The experimental validation using breast cancer data shows compelling results: DPSCREEN reduces screening frequency by 28-68% while maintaining equivalent detection delays compared to current clinical policies. The personalization aspect is particularly impressive, showing how the same patient's screening schedule adjusts dynamically based on test results and external information.

The paper's strengths lie in its novel conceptual framework, theoretical grounding, and practical demonstration of significant improvements over clinical standards. The comparison with related work in Section 5 is thorough and effectively highlights the paper's contributions. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from existing methods in both assumptions and capabilities.

However, the paper has some notable limitations. The experimental validation is confined to breast cancer with a two-state Markov disease model (despite claiming generality), which somewhat undermines the "very general setting" claim. The real-world dataset (45,000 patients) is limited in scope, and the paper doesn't sufficiently address how the approach would scale to more complex disease models with higher-dimensional state spaces. The assumption of no false positives in test outcomes is clinically unrealistic for mammography.

The paper's most significant contribution is conceptual rather than technical—it establishes a new direction for personalized screening policy design. This aligns perfectly with NIPS's preference for novel concepts over incremental methodological improvements. The authors have clearly identified a meaningful problem in healthcare decision-making and proposed a fundamentally different approach to solving it.

While the implementation details could be more thoroughly validated across multiple disease types, the paper's core contribution—establishing a general framework for personalized screening that handles complex disease dynamics and external information—represents a significant conceptual advance. The 28-68% reduction in screenings demonstrates tangible impact, and the theoretical bounds on approximation error provide important guarantees.

In summary, DPSCREEN introduces a novel conceptual framework that significantly extends the state of the art in personalized screening policy design. The authors have successfully demonstrated its potential to substantially reduce unnecessary screenings while maintaining detection quality. The paper's bold conceptual approach and significant practical implications outweigh the limitations in experimental scope.

I recommend acceptance. The paper's novel approach to a meaningful problem in healthcare decision-making represents a significant contribution to the field, and the authors have provided a theoretically grounded framework with practical validation. This work opens up new directions for research in personalized healthcare decision-making that could have substantial real-world impact.
