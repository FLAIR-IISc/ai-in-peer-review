- Paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies balancing testing cost vs trajectory-dependent detection delay using beliefs over state trajectories.
- Models disease as an arbitrary finite-state process with absorbing disease state and allows external information (e.g., self-exams) to trigger unscheduled visits.
- Formulates a Bellman equation, proves value function is convex and piecewise-linear in beliefs, and notes exact alpha-vector solution is intractable.
- Proposes a PBVI-style approximation: sample likely trajectories, construct reachable belief set under exploration/clinical policies, and perform point-based backups.
- Provides an approximation error bound under strong assumptions and a robustness result on local constancy of optimal actions to small model errors.
- Large-scale breast cancer application (45k patients) using a two-state Markov model with self-exams shows 28–68% screening reductions at matched expected detection delay.
- Synthetic experiment shows quadratic delay costs induce more aggressive testing than linear costs.
- Extends prior POMDP/surveillance work by handling endogenous arrivals, trajectory-dependent costs, and beliefs over trajectories (non-Markov/test processes).
- Strength: addresses a practically important problem with a flexible, unified model including external info and trajectory costs.
- Strength: clear POMDP connection with implementable PBVI-style algorithm for high-dimensional trajectory beliefs.
- Strength: provides theoretical approximation/robustness statements and interpretable, personalized policies with substantial screening reductions in experiments.
- Weakness: approximation bound relies on unrealistic assumptions (perfect tests, bounded intervals, large T, specific cost form) making practical relevance unclear.
- Weakness: robustness result does not imply performance robustness; no empirical sensitivity to model misspecification reported.
- Weakness: evaluation is entirely model-based with limited model validation, no uncertainty quantification (CIs), and minimal sensitivity analyses for key parameters.
- Weakness: computational details (K, B, runtime, convergence, sampling ablations) are underreported and complexity bound is coarse.
- Weakness: simplistic modeling choices (two-state Markov, independence assumptions) may not capture breast cancer complexity.
- Clarity issues: heavy/inconsistent notation, unclear sign conventions in Bellman equation, C̃ usage, role of diagnosis label l, and whether arrival threshold is optimized.
- Presentation issue: important proofs and algorithmic details are deferred to supplement; main text lacks concise algorithmic description and belief construction clarity.
- Originality: interesting idea to use beliefs over trajectories, but core algorithm is a PBVI variant and theoretical results largely extend known POMDP properties.
- Significance: approach could impact personalized screening if validated, but current model dependence and lack of uncertainty/sensitivity analysis limit practical impact.
- Suggested improvements: strengthen empirical validation (model fit, calibration, CIs, sensitivity analyses, ablations), clarify technical exposition, and report computational diagnostics.
- Suggested improvements: relax/discuss theoretical assumptions (include imperfect tests/overdiagnosis) or provide empirical error–performance curves.
- Suggested improvements: compare to more baselines (risk/density-tailored guidelines, recent ML systems) and discuss ethical/operational considerations (adherence, resources, interpretability).
- Pros for acceptance: important application, flexible model for external info/nonstandard costs, promising empirical results and personalization potential.
- Cons for acceptance: unrealistic theoretical assumptions, model-dependent evaluation with insufficient validation, incomplete algorithmic/computational characterization.
- Recommendation: borderline reject; promising but needs stronger empirical validation, clearer exposition, and more realistic theoretical support to meet conference standards.
