Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework to compute dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off screening cost against delay-in-detection cost. The setting is deliberately general: a finite-state disease process with an absorbing disease state, hidden true state, external information (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger unscheduled arrivals, and test outcomes with given conditional distributions. The objective is a weighted sum of discounted screening cost and delay cost that depends on the entire trajectory (incidence and detection times). The authors derive Bellman equations in a belief space over full state-trajectories and a diagnosis label, prove piecewise-linear convexity of the value function (generalizing classic POMDP results), and show how an optimal policy could be characterized via alpha-vectors but is intractable. They then propose a tractable point-based value iteration (PBVI)-style approximation that samples “typical” trajectories and reachable beliefs, provide a high-level bound on approximation error as a function of belief-sampling density, and a robustness result suggesting local constancy of the optimal policy under small model estimation error. An empirical illustration on a large breast cancer dataset is used to learn model parameters and compare against annual/biennial policies; DPSCREEN purportedly achieves similar expected delay with 28–68% fewer screens. A synthetic comparison illustrates how convex delay costs make policies more aggressive.

This connects to OR/ML work on screening via POMDPs (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al.) and dynamic survival/joint models (Rizopoulos et al.), and to PBVI (Pineau et al.). The paper argues prior POMDP approaches assume Markovian disease/test models and costs that depend only on current state, whereas DPSCREEN handles trajectory-dependent delay and exogenous arrivals.

Strengths
- Ambitious, general problem formulation that explicitly models trajectory-dependent delay, endogenous scheduling, and exogenous arrivals through external information.
- Theoretical framing: Bellman recursion in an unusual belief space and a piecewise-linear convexity result extending POMDP structure; explicit alpha-vector view.
- Practical approximation via PBVI adapted to high-dimensional belief spaces by sampling high-probability trajectories and reachable beliefs; complexity and parallelization discussion.
- Approximation guarantee tied to belief coverage and a robustness argument for model misspecification.
- Application to a large real dataset; results suggest sizable reductions in screening frequency at comparable expected delays; clear personalization to risk and dynamic history.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation

Quality
- The empirical evaluation is limited: comparisons are only to annual/biennial schedules. Stronger baselines are missing (e.g., best-tuned Markov/semi-Markov POMDP policies, risk-threshold policies, heuristic schedules triggered by risk or test history), undermining claims of superiority beyond simple clinical heuristics.
- The simulation-based evaluation relies on a learned model; while standard in this literature, the paper provides limited validation/calibration evidence for the disease and self-exam models (e.g., goodness-of-fit, posterior predictive checks, sensitivity analyses). Reported gains could be sensitive to model misspecification, discount factor, and the chosen threshold for external information.
- Assumptions used in the approximation bound are strong and partly inconsistent with the empirical model: Proposition 1 assumes perfect tests (no false positives/negatives), yet experiments explicitly use sensitivity/specificity; it also assumes a bounded maximum screening interval and convex delay cost forms. The constant ζ is not characterized, limiting interpretability of the bound.
- Theorem 1 (local constancy of the optimal policy) rests on uniqueness and continuity conditions but is presented at a high level; without more formal conditions or proofs, its practical value is limited.
- Important design choices (e.g., sampling K, belief set size B, weight tuning, δ, maximum interval W) are not ablated; no runtime or scalability results are reported.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized, but many essential algorithmic details and proofs are deferred to the supplement. Some notation choices could be clearer (e.g., clarify Z+ in experiments; align “no false positives” assumption with mammogram/biopsy modeling). The arrival model sets the external-information threshold ỹ from literature rather than optimizing it, but that choice and its implications are easy to miss.
- Minor issues: occasional typos (“st Conference …”), and some repetition.

Originality
- The problem setting (trajectory-dependent delay cost, endogenous inter-visit intervals with exogenous arrivals) and the extension of piecewise-linear convexity to this setting are conceptually novel. The PBVI adaptation via sampling over trajectory space is a reasonable and original engineering of known techniques.
- That said, several elements are adaptations of known ideas (alpha-vectors/PBVI, belief sampling), and the theoretical guarantees are fairly high-level and rely on restrictive assumptions.

Significance
- If validated and made practical, the approach could have high impact for personalized screening policies across diseases. The reported reductions in screening burden at comparable delays are compelling.
- However, the current empirical evidence is not sufficient for strong claims: lack of competitive baselines and absence of sensitivity/risk analyses limit confidence. The computational demands and scalability to richer disease models are unclear.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide competitive baselines beyond annual/biennial (e.g., risk-stratified periodic schedules, prior POMDP methods adapted as closely as possible, threshold policies on posterior risk).
- Report runtime, memory, and convergence behavior; ablate K, B, W, δ, and the belief-construction strategy; show how performance scales with sampling density, corroborating Proposition 1.
- Clarify and reconcile assumptions: when are false positives/negatives allowed? Define Z+ explicitly in the breast cancer model. Characterize ζ or give practical upper bounds.
- Validate the learned model: calibration curves, posterior predictive checks, sensitivity to tumor growth parameters and self-exam noise/threshold; consider optimizing ỹ as part of the policy.
- Discuss how to incorporate multiple tests with different costs and false-positive rates (biopsy harm), and fairness/utility trade-offs across risk groups.

Pros
- Addresses an important, high-impact problem with a general, principled framework.
- Theoretical structure (Bellman equation and value function geometry) and an approximation algorithm tailored to the setting.
- Empirical illustration on large real data with promising gains; personalization to risk and history is evident.

Cons
- Limited empirical validation and weak baselines; potential sensitivity to model misspecification.
- Theoretical guarantees are high-level and rely on restrictive or inconsistent assumptions relative to experiments.
- Missing scalability/runtime evidence; some clarity issues and overstatement of novelty.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem is important and the framework is promising, but the empirical validation and theoretical guarantees need strengthening, and assumptions should be clarified and aligned with experiments. With stronger baselines, sensitivity analyses, runtime results, and tightened theory, this could be a solid NeurIPS contribution.
