Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that balance screening costs against delay-in-detection costs. The model allows: (i) non-Markov disease processes over a finite state trajectory space with an absorbing disease state, (ii) external information (e.g., self-examination) that can trigger early arrival, (iii) test outcomes with imperfect accuracy, and (iv) decision epochs that are endogenous (the next screening time is the action). The problem is cast via beliefs over state trajectories and a diagnosis label, a Bellman equation is derived, and the value function is shown to be convex and piecewise linear (as in POMDPs). Because exact solution is intractable, the authors adapt point-based value iteration (PBVI), building a reduced belief set by sampling plausible physiological trajectories and reachable beliefs. They give a bound on approximation error in terms of the density of sampled beliefs under strong assumptions. They also show a robustness result: the optimal policy is locally constant in model parameters except on a lower-dimensional set. Empirically, they use a large breast cancer dataset (45k women, 84k mammograms) and a two-state Markov disease model with external information from a tumor growth model. They report 28–68% reductions in expected number of screenings while matching the expected detection delay of annual/biennial clinical policies; they also illustrate how delay cost shape (linear vs quadratic) shifts operating regimes.

The work relates to prior POMDP-based screening (e.g., Ayer et al.; Maillart et al.), Bayesian design/joint modeling approaches (Rizopoulos et al.), and controlled sensing. The stated distinctions are the general trajectory-based disease model, explicit external information, and endogenized screening intervals; the algorithmic core borrows from PBVI.

Strengths
- Ambitious, unified formulation that attempts to capture important practical aspects (personalization by features and history, external information, endogenous follow-up times).
- Clear articulation of why off-the-shelf POMDP/POSMDP formulations do not directly apply; the belief-based dynamic program and piecewise-linear convexity are well-motivated.
- Practical approximate solution via PBVI-style backups and belief set construction tailored to likely trajectories; computational parallelizability is noted.
- Attempt to quantify approximation error and discuss robustness to parameter estimation.
- Large real dataset; empirical results suggest substantial screening reductions at similar mean delays; exploration of different delay cost shapes is insightful.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality and validation
- Evaluation relies entirely on a model learned from observational data and then simulates counterfactuals. This is standard in clinical simulation, but from a machine learning standpoint the causal identifiability assumptions are untested. There is no uncertainty quantification on policy value, no sensitivity analysis to model misspecification, and no out-of-sample predictive checks linking the learned disease model to held-out observed outcomes.
- Theoretical guarantees hinge on assumptions that do not match experiments. The approximation bound assumes perfect tests (no false positives/negatives), bounded screening gaps, and convex delay costs of a specific form. In the empirical section, mammography has imperfect sensitivity/specificity and there are biopsies; the no-false-positive assumption is relaxed via a two-stage test, but then the theory no longer applies as stated. The bound is also non-operational: Ω involves an unknown constant ζ and the sampling density; no computed bound is reported.
- The “robustness” theorem (local constancy of optimal actions) is intuitive for piecewise-linear value functions but provides little actionable guidance; behavior can be unstable near decision boundaries, which is precisely when estimation error matters. No empirical study of stability is provided.

Methodological soundness
- The disease model used in experiments is a simple two-state Markov model with logistic transition, and external information is injected via a tumor growth model with fixed parameters from literature. This does not demonstrate the purported generality to non-Markov processes. It is unclear whether the self-exam process is supported by the dataset; if simulated, that should be made explicit and its calibration validated.
- Complexity and scalability are not empirically characterized. Theoretical complexity is high; no wall-clock times, K, B, T, or hardware are reported. Without runtime and scaling studies, practicality is uncertain.
- The Bellman equation has sign and max/min inconsistencies (equation (3) uses a maximization with a negative immediate cost term). The objective is to minimize cost; clarity in the dynamic programming derivation is needed.
- The construction of the belief set is critical to performance, yet choices (number of trajectories K, selection policies, sensitivity to these choices) are not systematically studied. No ablations are shown (e.g., effect of including clinical policies in belief construction).
- Outcome metrics are limited. Matching the expected delay of annual/biennial screening is a weak criterion; maximum delay, distribution of delays among those who develop cancer, and safety-related metrics (missed diagnoses, time to detection conditional on disease) are not reported. Grouping into two risk strata underutilizes personalization and hides variability.

Clarity
- The paper is dense and occasionally sloppy (e.g., typographical remnants; ambiguous notation). Key proofs are deferred to the supplement; more intuition is needed in the main text for Lemma 1’s extension beyond standard POMDPs.
- Dataset description lacks details on how ground-truth incidence times are inferred, how censoring is handled, and how mammography sensitivity/specificity from literature are reconciled with the no-false-positive assumption in the model.

Originality
- The formulation mixes known ingredients (belief-state DP, PBVI) with application-specific modeling choices. The claim of being the “first” to provide personalized screening in a very general setting seems overstated given prior POMDP and Bayesian design works; the novelty lies more in problem packaging and inclusion of external information and endogenous timing than in algorithmic advances.

Significance
- The problem is important, and the potential impact is high. However, given the heavy reliance on simulated components and the gap between theory and the empirical setup, the evidence is not yet convincing enough for NeurIPS standards.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide runtime, K/B/T values, and scaling with T and feature dimensionality. How large can K be before performance plateaus?
- Validate the learned disease model: predictive checks on held-out data, calibration of time-to-event distributions, and sensitivity analyses to alternative specifications (e.g., semi-Markov, different priors).
- Quantify uncertainty in policy value (e.g., via bootstrap over model parameters) and sensitivity to self-exam parameters (ι, σ, ỹ).
- Report richer safety metrics: percentiles of delay among cases, maximum allowed delay constraints, false negative rates.
- Clarify how incidence times are inferred and how the expected delay under annual screening is computed without access to counterfactuals.
- Reconcile theoretical assumptions with the empirical setting, or restate the guarantees under more realistic test accuracy assumptions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a principled DP framework and an implementable approximation.
- Large dataset; initial results suggest substantial reductions in screening burden.
- The inclusion of external information and endogenous timing is a useful addition to the literature.

Arguments against acceptance
- Evaluation is model-driven with no robust validation or uncertainty quantification; potential circularity in comparing to clinical baselines.
- Theory–practice mismatch and non-operational bounds.
- Lack of scalability evidence and ablation studies; clarity issues.

Recommendation
At this stage, I do not recommend acceptance. The methodological framing is interesting, but stronger and more rigorous empirical validation (with uncertainty quantification), clearer alignment between theory and experiments, and a more careful comparison to established baselines are needed.
