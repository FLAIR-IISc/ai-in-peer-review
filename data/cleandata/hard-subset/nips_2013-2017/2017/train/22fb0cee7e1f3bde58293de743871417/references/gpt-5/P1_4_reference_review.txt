Summary and contributions
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized disease screening policies that balance test costs against detection delay. It models disease as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and allows an exogenous “external information” process (e.g., self-exam lump size) that can trigger early visits. The objective minimizes a weighted sum of discounted screening costs and trajectory-dependent delay costs. The authors formulate a belief-state dynamic program over distributions on state trajectories and diagnosis labels, show the value function is convex and piecewise-linear (via alpha-vectors), and derive Bellman equations. Because exact computation is intractable, they propose a point-based value iteration (PBVI) variant that samples likely disease trajectories to reduce the belief space, constructs reachable beliefs under random and clinically-inspired policies, and computes an approximately optimal policy. They give a bound linking approximation error to the density of sampled beliefs and prove a robustness statement: optimal actions are locally constant with respect to small model estimation errors almost everywhere. Using a large de-identified breast cancer dataset (n≈45k, 84k mammograms), they estimate a simple 2-state model (Gail-based features, monthly self-exam, mammography sensitivity/specificity from literature) and report that DPSCREEN reduces the expected number of screens by 28–68% while maintaining the same expected delay as annual screening. They also illustrate how different delay-cost functions (linear vs quadratic) lead to different recommended regimes.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on POMDP-based screening (Ayer et al. 2012; Maillart et al. 2008; Erenay et al. 2014), dynamic survival/joint models, and controlled sensing. Novel aspects emphasized are: (i) trajectory-dependent delay costs, (ii) an external information process that endogenizes arrivals, and (iii) disease/test processes not restricted to (semi-)Markov assumptions. While these can often be embedded into standard POMDP/POSMDP frameworks by augmenting the state (e.g., time-since-onset, last test time, hazard parameters), the paper’s explicit construction over trajectory beliefs and the way arrivals depend jointly on actions and exogenous signals are useful perspectives. The algorithmic core (PBVI with belief sampling) follows established techniques, adapted to the high-dimensional belief representation.

Quality
- Theory: The piecewise-linear/convex value function result extends known POMDP properties; it is technically sound but largely expected. The approximation bound is qualitative: error ≤ Ω(B̄), a function of belief-sampling density times an unspecified constant ζ, under restrictive assumptions (perfect diagnostic test for Z+, bounded screening spacing W, convex delay cost, large T). No nontrivial rate or guarantee on covering B̄ is provided, and because B̄ is itself induced by exploratory rollouts, there is potential circularity. The robustness theorem (optimal action locally constant a.e. in model-belief space) aligns with standard results for finite-action PL value functions; it is reassuring but weak for guiding practice.
- Algorithm: The main ideas are plausible, but key details are deferred to the supplement (Algorithms 1–3). Complexity is high, and the stated bound O(T(B)2T2K|Y||Z|) needs clearer derivation and practical guidance on K, B, and action-space pruning. The method’s stability with respect to the choice of sampled trajectories and belief set construction is not analyzed.
- Empirics: The evaluation follows the “learn model, then simulate policies” paradigm common in screening literature. However, the disease model used in experiments is a simple 2-state Markov process, which undercuts the claim of handling non-Markov/nonstationary dynamics. There is limited information on model fit (train/test split, calibration, predictive accuracy), no uncertainty quantification on outcomes (e.g., CIs), and no off-policy evaluation against observational data. Tuning w post hoc to match annual screening delay is reasonable for constrained optimization but risks cherry-picking; sensitivity to w, test costs, and self-exam parameters should be reported. Baselines beyond annual/biennial (e.g., hazard-threshold heuristics, existing POMDP policies) are missing. Runtime and scalability on the real dataset are not reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized but dense. Core definitions and the Bellman equation are clear; however, the belief over trajectories is heavy notation-wise, and several assumptions (e.g., “no false positives” for Z+ vs the mammogram/biopsy pathway) should be reconciled explicitly. Critical algorithmic details and proofs are in the supplement. Some references appear incomplete, and figures/tables are referenced but not shown here.

Originality
Moderate. The combination of (i) external information triggering early visits, (ii) trajectory-dependent costs, and (iii) a PBVI variant over sampled trajectory-belief spaces is a novel synthesis within screening. Several theoretical claims extend known POMDP properties rather than introduce fundamentally new results.

Significance
Potentially significant for personalized screening if robust and scalable. However, the current experimental evidence relies on a simplified disease model and model-based simulation without robust validation, limiting claims of clinical impact. As an ML contribution, the algorithmic novelty is incremental.

Pros
- Addresses an important societal problem with a principled sequential decision framework.
- Incorporates external information and trajectory-dependent costs explicitly.
- Provides a practical PBVI-style approach and some theoretical reassurance (PL value function, robustness).
- Empirical results suggest large screening reductions at equal expected delay.

Cons
- Approximation bound is loose and depends on opaque constants and sampling choices.
- Experimental evaluation lacks predictive validation, uncertainty quantification, stronger baselines, and runtime/scalability reporting.
- Claims of generality vs prior POMDP work are somewhat overstated; many elements can be handled via state augmentation.
- Practical assumptions (perfect diagnosis for Z+, monthly self-exam, fixed screening cost) and absence of overdiagnosis/competing risks may bias results.
- Some clarity issues and incomplete references.

Questions for authors
1. How are K (trajectories) and B (belief points) selected in practice? What are runtime, memory, and parallelization details on the breast cancer experiment?
2. How sensitive are results to the self-exam model (ι, σ, threshold) and to w? Please include ablations.
3. Can you compare against prior POMDP-based policies (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al.) and simple hazard-threshold heuristics?
4. Do you have calibration metrics for the estimated disease model and uncertainty intervals for reported reductions?
5. How would you model overdiagnosis, competing mortality, and heterogeneous test costs within DPSCREEN?

Suggestions
- Strengthen evaluation with model fit assessment, sensitivity analyses, additional baselines, and uncertainty quantification.
- Report computational performance and resource use; include code if possible.
- Clarify the relation to standard POMDP formulations (show explicit reductions) and better justify the claimed intractability in those.
- Tighten the approximation analysis (e.g., covering numbers or Lipschitz constants) and discuss how to ensure adequate belief coverage.
- Expand to richer disease models (semi-Markov, multi-state) to demonstrate the promised generality.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem is important and the framework promising, but the current theoretical guarantees are weak and the empirical evidence is not yet strong enough for NeurIPS standards. Strengthening the experiments and sharpening the analysis could make this a compelling submission.
