- Addresses an important healthcare problem by proposing personalized screening policies that balance screening and delay costs.  
- Presents a general disease progression model incorporating external information (e.g., self-exams) and formulates screening as a dynamic decision problem.  
- Introduces DPSCREEN: a point-based value iteration method on belief space with theoretical approximation guarantees.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical results on breast cancer data, showing 28–68% reduction in screenings while maintaining similar detection delays.  
- Clearly differentiates the approach from prior operations research and medical models.  

- Computational complexity claims are overstated; belief space dimension is exponential in horizon T and may not scale to realistic timeframes.  
- Proposition 1’s approximation error bound relies on unrealistic assumptions (linear delay costs, no false positives/negatives) that limit applicability.  
- Model estimation lacks critical details: choice of logistic functions, feature set, MCMC implementation, convergence diagnostics, and calibration/validation procedures.  
- Baseline comparison is inadequate—omits direct comparisons to existing personalized screening methods in the literature.  
- No uncertainty quantification for policy recommendations; results are presented only as point estimates.  
- Practical implementation issues are under-discussed, including clinician interaction, ethical concerns, handling missing data, and potential health disparities.  
- Impact claims (28–68% reduction) are possibly overstated without clarity on population scope or trade-offs like late-stage diagnoses.  

- Reframe and clarify computational limits with empirical runtimes for realistic settings (e.g., T=10, |S|=3).  
- Relax or generalize assumptions in the error analysis to include false positives/negatives and non-linear delay costs.  
- Add detailed model estimation and validation procedures (features used, hyperparameter tuning, MCMC specifics, calibration metrics).  
- Include comparisons to at least one prior personalized screening method to demonstrate incremental benefit.  
- Provide uncertainty estimates (confidence intervals or Bayesian credible intervals) for recommended screening intervals.  
- Add discussion of implementation, ethical considerations, and handling of incomplete patient data.  

- Overall: novel, theoretically grounded, and promising, but practical credibility is undermined by computational, theoretical, and validation gaps.  
- Recommendation: weak accept conditional on addressing complexity transparency, strengthening error analysis, adding model and validation details, and expanding baseline comparisons.
