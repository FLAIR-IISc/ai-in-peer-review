Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off testing cost against delay in disease detection. The state is a hidden disease process (very generally modeled as an arbitrary finite-state process with an absorbing disease state), augmented by an external information process (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger unscheduled visits. The authors formulate a Bellman equation over beliefs defined on full state trajectories and a diagnosis flag, show the value function is convex/piecewise linear, and discuss an exact α-vector dynamic program that is intractable. They then propose a PBVI-style approximation that samples “likely” state trajectories to form a reduced belief space, performs point-based backups, and returns an approximately optimal policy. They provide a loose bound on approximation error as a function of belief coverage and a robustness statement asserting local constancy of optimal actions under small model estimation error. Experiments on a large breast cancer dataset (Athena) suggest substantial reductions in number of screens at similar expected detection delay versus annual/biennial schedules. They also show a synthetic comparison under linear vs quadratic delay costs.

This work connects most directly to POMDP/POSMDP literature and prior screening models in OR/medical decision making (e.g., Ayer et al. and Maillart et al. for mammography; Erenay et al. for colonoscopy; Bayesian design approaches), and to controlled sensing. The claim is that DPSCREEN handles non-Markovian disease/test processes and endogenous arrivals due to external information, which are challenging in standard POMDP settings.

Strengths
- Ambitious generalization: Formalizes screening with endogenous arrivals and path-dependent delay costs, beyond common Markov/stationary assumptions.
- Theoretical structure: Extends the piecewise-linear convex value-function characterization to this setting.
- Practical approximation: PBVI-style algorithm tailored via trajectory sampling and reachable-belief construction; discussion of computational complexity and potential parallelism.
- Attempts at rigor: Provides an approximation error bound (albeit qualitative) and a robustness result (local constancy of optimal actions).
- Empirical scale: Uses a large dataset and demonstrates personalization by patient risk and test history; includes a sensitivity illustration for different delay cost shapes.

Weaknesses
Methodological soundness and validation
- Evaluation on a learned generative model only. Policies are assessed under the same model estimated from observational data, which risks severe model-induced optimism. There is no off-policy evaluation, external validation, or uncertainty quantification. Reported reductions in screening could disappear under mild model misspecification.
- Tuning to match baseline delay. The weight w is tuned post hoc to yield the same expected delay as the clinical policy, then screens are counted. Without reporting full Pareto curves with uncertainty and holding w fixed across policies, the comparison is difficult to interpret.
- Metrics: Emphasis on unconditional expected delay E[Δ|R], which is dominated by non-cases; the clinically salient metric is delay among cases E[Δ|R,D], stage at detection, recall/biopsy rates, and false positive harms. These are not thoroughly analyzed with confidence intervals.
- Inconsistent/idealized assumptions: Early sections assume no false positives for diagnostic outcomes, which is unrealistic and conflicts with later use of mammography sensitivity/specificity. External information is assumed conditionally independent given state trajectory; real-world behaviors and comorbidities can violate this.

Theory
- Approximation bound is weak and non-operational. The constant ζ and Ω(B̄) (max distance to sampled beliefs) are not characterized quantitatively, and in high dimensions coverage is hard to guarantee. No finite-sample robustness or Lipschitz continuity of the value function/policy is provided.
- Robustness theorem (local constancy) is a generic topological property and does not yield actionable bounds on performance degradation under estimation error.
- Scalability remains unclear. Beliefs over trajectories have astronomical dimension; the proposed sampling of K trajectories and reachable beliefs is heuristic. There are no convergence or sensitivity studies vs K, |B̄|, or horizon T.

Claims vs implementation
- The framework is advertised as very general (non-Markovian, non-stationary), but the main experiment uses a two-state Markov model with simple extrinsic signal dynamics, undermining the generality claim.
- The “first such personalized screening policies in a very general setting” claim is overstated given earlier POMDP-based personalized screening and risk-based scheduling (e.g., Ayer, Maillart, ConfidentCare, and related OR/medical literature).

Clarity and reproducibility
- Many key details and all proofs are deferred to the supplement. Important constructs (e.g., definition/choice of ζ, W, reachable-belief generation, and the exact backup equations) should be in the main text for reproducibility.
- Notation is heavy and occasionally inconsistent (false-positive assumption vs later sensitivity/specificity). Pseudocode of the main algorithms should be included in the paper.

Significance
The problem is important and the high-level formulation is interesting. However, the paper stops short of providing either (i) strong theoretical guarantees that are usable in practice or (ii) rigorous empirical validation beyond an internally-evaluated model. Given the sensitivity of clinical screening, stronger evidence and baselines are needed to justify the reported gains.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide robust, model-agnostic evaluation: off-policy evaluation with uncertainty (e.g., doubly robust estimators), external validation, and sensitivity to misspecification of disease and external information models.
- Report full trade-off curves with confidence intervals; fix w across methods and compare to strong baselines (existing POMDP screening models, hazard-threshold policies, risk-band policies like ConfidentCare).
- Incorporate realistic test errors and downstream harms (false positives, recalls, biopsies) and report stage-at-detection.
- Quantify approximation quality: ablation on K and |B̄|, coverage diagnostics, and empirical error vs exact solutions on small problems; provide computable bounds or Lipschitz-style stability results.
- Move core algorithmic details to the main paper; clarify assumptions; avoid overstated novelty claims.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem; neat formalization with endogenous arrivals.
- Extension of POMDP structural result; practical PBVI-style approach.
- Large dataset; plausible personalization behavior; sensitivity to cost shape.

Cons
- Validation entirely on a learned model; no causal/off-policy evaluation or uncertainty.
- Approximation/robustness theory weak and non-operational.
- Key assumptions/claims are inconsistent or unrealistic; generality not reflected in experiments.
- Reproducibility and clarity issues; limited comparison to strong baselines.

Recommendation
Reject. While the topic is compelling, the methodological validation and theoretical guarantees are not yet at the level needed for NeurIPS, and the empirical claims rely on a potentially optimistic evaluation pipeline. Strengthening the evaluation and providing more actionable theory would substantially improve the paper. Confidence: high.
