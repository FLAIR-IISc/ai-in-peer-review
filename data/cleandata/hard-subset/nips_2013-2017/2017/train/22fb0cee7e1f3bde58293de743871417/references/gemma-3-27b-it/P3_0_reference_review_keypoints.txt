- Proposal: DPSCREEN — a framework to design personalized screening policies that trade off testing costs against delayed detection costs.  
- Technical scope: Handles non-Markovian disease processes, external signals, patient arrivals, and offers a tractable approximate solution with guarantees.  
- Empirical claim: Applied to a breast cancer dataset, DPSCREEN reduces screening frequency (28–68%) while maintaining comparable detection delays.  
- Positioning: Correctly differentiated from prior screening and POMDP work, addressing limitations of Markovian and simulation-only approaches.  
- Novelty: Extends controlled sensing ideas to non-stationary, complex-cost screening settings where prior methods do not apply.  
- Strength — Generality: Supports a wide class of disease models and multiple information sources for broad applicability.  
- Strength — Tractability: Introduces a PBVI approximation algorithm with provable error bounds (Proposition 1).  
- Strength — Personalization: Incorporates patient features and history to produce individualized screening recommendations.  
- Strength — Real-world relevance: Demonstrated on a large breast cancer dataset with substantial reductions in screening burden.  
- Strength — Trade-off analysis: Synthetic experiments clarify how different delay-cost functions change optimal policies.  
- Strength — Robustness: Provides analysis on model estimation error and a theorem on policy stability.  
- Weakness — Method complexity: Algorithmic approach remains complex and computationally demanding; runtime expression is opaque without concrete parameter values.  
- Weakness — Modeling assumptions: Relies on conditional independence of test results and a single absorbing disease state, which may limit realism.  
- Weakness — Limited empirical comparisons: Lacks rigorous comparisons to other personalized or heuristic screening strategies.  
- Weakness — Parameter tuning: Paper gives little practical guidance on choosing objective weights and disease-model parameters.  
- Weakness — External-info model choice: The stochastic model for external signals (e.g., lump size) seems arbitrary and could use alternatives or data-driven justification.  
- For acceptance: Addresses an important problem with a general, theoretically grounded method and promising empirical results.  
- Against acceptance: Concerns about scalability, simplifying assumptions, and incomplete empirical evaluation could limit impact.  
- Overall: Strong contribution with caveats around complexity and assumptions; recommendation is accept with minor revisions.  
- Required minor revisions: clarify runtime with typical parameter values; add sensitivity analysis for key assumptions; compare against simple personalized heuristics; provide guidance on parameter tuning.
