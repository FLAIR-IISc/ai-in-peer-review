Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for computing dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off screening costs against delay-in-detection costs. The model allows: (i) arbitrary finite-state disease dynamics with an absorbing disease state (not restricted to Markov/Semi-Markov), (ii) an external information process (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger early arrivals, (iii) test outcomes with general observation models, and (iv) a delay cost that depends on the full disease trajectory (incidence and detection times). The decision problem is cast as a belief-state dynamic program with beliefs over entire state trajectories. The value function is shown to be convex and piecewise-linear in beliefs, enabling an alpha-vector representation. Because exact computation is intractable, the paper proposes a PBVI-style approximation that samples likely state trajectories and reachable beliefs and provides an upper bound on the approximation error in terms of a sampling-density metric. A robustness result shows local constancy of optimal actions w.r.t. small model estimation errors. Experiments on a large breast-cancer dataset (Athena; 45k patients) estimate a two-state Markov disease model parameterized by Gail risk, include a self-exam external signal via a lump-growth model, and report 28–68% reductions in expected number of screenings relative to annual/biennial policies while matching expected detection delays. A synthetic exercise shows how the choice of delay cost (linear vs quadratic) changes policy aggressiveness.

The work relates to OR screening models (e.g., Ayer et al. 2012; Maillart et al. 2008; Erenay et al. 2014) and to controlled sensing/POMDPs (Smallwood & Sondik; Pineau et al.; Krishnamurthy). It also connects to medical simulation-based policy evaluation (e.g., MISCAN-COLON) and to personalization efforts (e.g., risk-based guidelines, joint models). Compared to prior POMDP screening models, the paper emphasizes non-stationary, trajectory-dependent costs and endogenous arrivals driven by external information.

Strengths
- General problem formulation that cleanly captures trajectory-dependent delay costs and externally triggered arrivals.
- Solid DP foundation with a piecewise-linear-convex value function and alpha-vector characterization adapted beyond standard POMDP assumptions.
- Practical approximate solution via PBVI with a principled belief set construction; explicit complexity accounting and a (albeit coarse) approximation error bound.
- Robustness argument (local constancy of optimal actions) provides insight into sensitivity to model estimation.
- Application to a sizable, real dataset; personalization emerges naturally via both static risk (Gail) and dynamic history (test/external signals).
- Clear empirical message: potential to reduce screening burden without increasing expected delays, and demonstration that cost specification materially changes policy regimes.

Weaknesses
- The key approximation bound (Prop. 1) assumes perfect tests (no false positives/negatives) and bounded inter-screen intervals, diverging from the model used in experiments and from clinical reality; the bound’s practical informativeness is limited and lacks rates in terms of K, |B̄|, or T.
- Despite claims of generality beyond Markov models, experiments instantiate a simple two-state Markov disease process; there is no empirical demonstration on a non-Markov or semi-Markov disease to substantiate the generality.
- Evaluation relies on model-based simulation learned from observational data with minimal uncertainty quantification; no confidence intervals, calibration checks, or sensitivity analyses to key assumptions (e.g., self-exam parameters, growth dynamics, δ, w).
- The decision metric “expected delay” is dominated by individuals without cancer; although E[Δ|R,D] is mentioned, concrete numbers and harms (false positives, unnecessary biopsies) are not fully incorporated in the objective (cost of a biopsy set equal to a screening).
- Important implementation details are missing: δ, K, |B̄[t]|, W, runtime, convergence criteria, and how w is tuned to meet constraints. Reproducibility is uncertain.
- Theorem 1’s statement and implications are plausible but the practical consequence for approximate policies is not quantified; the constant ζ in Ω(·) is unspecified.
- External information process is stylized (single threshold ỹ); learning the threshold from data or modeling richer symptom dynamics would strengthen realism.

Quality
Technically competent with correct use of POMDP machinery and a reasonable approximate solution scheme. However, theoretical guarantees hinge on unrealistic sensing assumptions, and empirical validation lacks uncertainty quantification and comprehensive harm-benefit analysis.

Clarity
Generally well written but notation heavy. Key algorithmic and experimental details are deferred and some are missing (parameters, tables, figures). Provide explicit values, tables, and runtime analyses in the main paper. Some references are incomplete.

Originality
The integration of endogenous arrivals, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and belief over state trajectories within a POMDP-like alpha-vector framework, plus a PBVI adaptation, is a notable combination. However, components build on established POMDP/PBVI ideas.

Significance
Personalized screening is consequential. If validated with robust evaluation, the approach could impact practice and inspire further work at the intersection of POMDPs and healthcare. Present evidence is promising but not yet definitive.

Questions and suggestions
- Report E[Δ|R,D] with CIs, plus false positive, recall, and biopsy rates. Explore overdiagnosis and patient-level harms beyond a unit screening cost.
- Provide sensitivity to model mis-specification: vary pin/ptr, the growth parameters, self-exam sensitivity/specificity, and the arrival threshold ỹ; include calibration plots.
- Relax the perfect-test assumption in theory; provide a bound that degrades with FP/FN rates or show empirically how performance changes with realistic test characteristics.
- Demonstrate on a non-Markov or semi-Markov disease process to validate generality (even synthetic).
- Detail PBVI design: K, |B̄[t]|, selection heuristics, W, δ, weight tuning procedure, runtime, and scalability; include ablations on belief set construction strategies.
- Consider off-policy evaluation techniques to complement model-based evaluation.
- Learn or adapt the external-information arrival model (e.g., threshold ỹ) from data.
- Release code and, if possible, a synthetic benchmark.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem with a principled, general framework.
- Sound DP foundation and a workable approximation.
- Persuasive qualitative personalization and sizable reductions in screening load.
- Discussion of cost-function choice and its policy implications.

Cons
- Theoretical guarantees rely on unrealistic sensing assumptions; bounds not actionable.
- Evaluation lacks uncertainty quantification and comprehensive harm/benefit metrics.
- Generality claims not empirically demonstrated beyond Markov models.
- Missing experimental/algorithmic details limit reproducibility.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline)
The paper is promising and potentially impactful, but the current submission falls short on empirical rigor (uncertainty, harms), theoretical realism (test assumptions), and demonstration of claimed generality. Addressing the above points would make it a strong candidate.
