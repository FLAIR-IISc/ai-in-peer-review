- Summary: Proposes DPSCREEN, a dynamic personalized screening framework that adapts schedules using patient features, history, and non‑Markovian disease progression, balancing screening cost and detection delay.  
- Method/Contribution: Introduces a tractable approximation algorithm with theoretical approximation error bounds and robustness to estimation error.  
- Empirical Results: Demonstrates on a breast cancer dataset (Athena Health Network) large reductions in screening frequency (28–68%) with comparable detection delays.  
- Relation to Prior Work: Correctly situates the work against Markovian/static policy literature and dynamic survival/Bayesian design, claiming broader generality for non‑Markovian processes.  
- Connection to Controlled Sensing: Notes similarities to controlled sensing but emphasizes distinct challenges due to disease progression modeling.  
- Clinical Context: References and aligns with medical screening guideline literature to justify practical relevance.  

Strengths:
- Generality: Handles diverse disease models (dynamic survival, first hitting time) and multiple information sources.  
- Theoretical Foundations: Provides approximation error bounds (Proposition 1) and robustness analysis for estimation error (Theorem 1).  
- Practical Demonstration: Real‑world dataset evaluation lends credibility and shows clinically meaningful gains.  
- Personalization: Policies adapt to patient features and dynamic history, illustrated empirically.  
- Cost Sensitivity: Analyses how delay cost functions shape optimal policies.  
- Clear Formulation: Problem is mathematically well‑posed and clearly presented.  

Weaknesses:
- Computational Complexity: Approximation algorithm complexity (O(T(B)^2 T^2 K |Y| |Z|)) may be prohibitive for large state spaces or long horizons; scalability discussion is limited.  
- Finite State Assumption: Discretizing disease and external information into finite states oversimplifies continuous real‑world processes; impact not fully explored.  
- Parameter Estimation Details: Sparse detail on parameter estimation (Gail model, MCMC), choice of parameters (ι=0.9, σ=0.43) lacks strong justification and sensitivity analysis.  
- Limited Baselines: Comparisons exclude other personalized screening approaches, weakening empirical claims.  
- Algorithm 1 Clarity: Belief construction and efficient sampling/coverage of the reachable belief set B̄ are not clearly or explicitly described.  

Arguments for Acceptance:
- Novelty: Presents a genuinely novel, dynamic personalized screening framework.  
- Significance: Potential to reduce healthcare costs and improve outcomes.  
- Soundness: Theoretical results and experiments support validity.  
- Impact: Could influence future screening program design and clinical decision support.  

Arguments Against Acceptance:
- Scalability Concerns: Algorithmic complexity may restrict real‑world applicability.  
- Modeling Simplifications: Key assumptions (finite states, independence) may not hold broadly.  

Overall Assessment: Strong, theoretically grounded, and practically relevant contribution to personalized screening that outweighs weaknesses, which are addressable.  
Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  

Requested Revisions (minor):
- Provide a more detailed discussion of computational scalability and limits.  
- Address the implications of the finite state space assumption and possible mitigations.  
- Elaborate parameter estimation procedures and include sensitivity analysis for key parameters.  
- Expand empirical comparisons to include other personalized screening methods.  
- Clarify Algorithm 1, specifically how the belief set B̄ is constructed and sampled efficiently.
