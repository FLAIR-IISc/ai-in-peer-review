This paper tackles the important problem of personalized screening policies, moving beyond static approaches to consider individual patient features, disease progression, and external information. The core idea of formulating this as a dynamic decision problem and attempting to solve it, even approximately, is valuable. However, despite the significance of the problem, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor – yet pervasive – issues that collectively detract from its overall impact and raise concerns about the rigor of the work.

The introduction sets a good stage but is excessively verbose. The repeated emphasis on the limitations of existing work feels repetitive and could be more concisely stated. Several citations appear somewhat dated given the rapid evolution of the field (e.g., [18]-[21]). The discussion of computational intractability is valid, but the subsequent claim of “addressing all of these problems” feels overstated, as the proposed approximation approach is not fully justified in its ability to overcome all hurdles.

Section 2, while laying out the model, is plagued by notational inconsistencies and a lack of clarity. For instance, the use of both ~s and s to represent state trajectories is confusing. The justification for assuming the current test result is independent of past results *conditional* on the state trajectory seems weak; real-world tests often have carry-over effects or provide cumulative evidence. The repeated assertion that assumptions are “standard” doesn’t excuse a lack of detailed justification. The formulation of the screening policy π is adequate but lacks formal definition of the action space. The description of the cost function is clear, but the assertion that it can be “easily generalized” is not elaborated.

Section 3 is the most problematic. The description of the Beliefs and Bellman equations is dense and difficult to follow. The leap from stating the intractability of exact solution to the proposed Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) feels abrupt. While PBVI is a reasonable approach, the justification for *how* it’s adapted to this specific problem—which deviates significantly from standard POMDPs—is weak. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 & 2 in the supplementary material) are brief and lack sufficient detail to ensure reproducibility. The computational complexity analysis is presented without a clear discussion of the practical implications – is it tractable for realistic problem sizes? The discussion of approximation error is framed in terms of Proposition 1, but the assumptions underlying the proposition (e.g., convex delay cost) limit its applicability. Theorem 1, regarding robustness to estimation error, feels almost trivial given the assumption of a “sufficiently close” estimate and doesn’t offer concrete guidance on how to assess this closeness. The personalization example (Figure 1) is helpful for illustrating the concept, but lacks quantitative details.

Section 4, the illustrative experiments, suffers from a lack of baselines. Comparing against only “current clinical policies” is insufficient. There are other existing screening strategies (even if not fully personalized) that could serve as more challenging benchmarks. The dataset description is sparse; crucial details about data quality, missing values, and potential biases are omitted. The model estimation process relies on several assumptions (e.g., logistic functions, MCMC estimation) that are not fully justified. The claim of “significant” differences in policies based on cost function in Figure 2 feels exaggerated without statistical significance testing.

The related works section is adequate but could be more comprehensive. The discussion of controlled sensing feels somewhat tangential.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The problem addressed is important and relevant to the NIPS community.
*   The attempt to formulate the problem as a dynamic decision problem is a step forward.
*   The illustrative experiments demonstrate the potential of the approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Numerous minor technical flaws and notational inconsistencies.
*   Lack of clarity and detail in the method description, hindering reproducibility.
*   Insufficient justification for the adaptation of PBVI to this specific problem.
*   Weak experimental evaluation with inadequate baselines and limited statistical analysis.
*   Overstated claims and lack of rigorous proof.
*   Pervasive lack of detail and justification for key assumptions.



**Overall Assessment:**

Reject. While the paper addresses an important problem, the multitude of minor flaws, the lack of clarity, and the insufficient experimental evaluation outweigh its strengths. The work feels incomplete and lacks the rigor expected of a NIPS publication. Significant revisions addressing these concerns would be necessary for reconsideration. The authors need to meticulously address each of the points raised above, providing more detailed explanations, justifications, and experimental validation. I am concerned that these are not merely stylistic issues, but indicators of a lack of thoroughness in the research process.
