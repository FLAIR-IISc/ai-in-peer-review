- Proposes DPSCREEN: a dynamic personalized screening framework balancing screening costs against detection delay.  
- Extends POMDP methodology to non‑Markovian disease progression and external information (e.g., self‑exams).  
- Formulates problem as a POMDP with a non‑Markovian disease process and a justified Bellman equation.  
- Introduces DPSCREEN algorithm using point‑based value iteration by sampling likely physiological trajectories.  
- Provides a theoretical approximation bound (Proposition 1) but the bound is weak and defined in terms of sampling density.  
- Assumes no false positives in test results, which is unrealistic for many medical tests (e.g., mammograms).  
- Disease model limited to a single absorbing disease state, ignoring intermediate states and competing risks.  
- Theoretical bounds do not relate sampling density Ω(B̄) to practical error tolerances or computational costs.  
- Paper is well‑written and organized with consistent notation; POMDP connection is clear.  
- Would benefit from stronger positioning within broader reinforcement learning and decision‑making literature.  
- Empirical results on breast cancer data are promising (28–68% screening reductions with maintained detection delay).  
- Lacks comparisons to recent constrained POMDP baselines [19] and other modern personalized screening approaches.  
- Main paper under‑utilizes substantial technical content placed in the supplement.  
- Originality stems from the non‑Markovian disease model and external info integration; core algorithmic approach is incremental.  
- Significance limited by single‑disease validation (breast cancer only) and lack of ethical analysis (risk of under‑screening vulnerable groups).  
- Strengths: clear formulation, theoretical error bounds, real‑world validation on 45k patients, and good analysis of delay cost effects.  
- Weaknesses: unrealistic test accuracy assumption, narrow validation, weak/practically uninterpretable bounds, and missing implementation/scalability considerations.  
- Recommendation: weak accept (marginal) — useful extension but incremental and needing stronger theory and broader empirical/ethical validation.  
- Suggested improvements: model realistic false positives, validate on multiple diseases, strengthen approximation guarantees, and compare to constrained POMDP approaches.
