Summary
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for computing dynamic, personalized screening policies that jointly optimize screening frequency and detection delay. The disease process is modeled as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, external information arrives via a process Y (e.g., self-exams), and clinical test outcomes Z are observed only upon visits. The objective is a weighted sum of discounted screening costs and delay costs that depend on the full disease trajectory. The authors argue that standard POMDP/POSMDP formulations are inadequate because (i) decision epochs are endogenous (driven by policy and external information) and (ii) costs depend on the state trajectory, not just the current state. They propose a belief-state formulation over entire state trajectories, establish that the value function is piecewise-linear and convex, and then develop a point-based approximation using sampled trajectories and reachable beliefs. They provide an approximation error bound in terms of belief-sampling density and a generic “almost everywhere” robustness claim to model estimation error. Experiments on a de-identified breast cancer dataset (Athena) report substantial reductions (28–68%) in the expected number of screens at matched expected delay compared to annual/biennial guidelines; they also demonstrate personalization by risk group and discuss the effect of different delay cost functions.

Strengths
- Ambitious, general formulation that attempts to unify heterogeneous disease progression models, external information, and trajectory-dependent costs.
- Clear articulation of why personalization should depend on both static features and dynamic history; the framework naturally incorporates both.
- Use of point-based value iteration ideas adapted to the high-dimensional belief setting via trajectory sampling is reasonable and practical in spirit.
- The piecewise-linear/convexity result and the alpha-vector construction connect to established POMDP theory.
- Application to breast cancer with external self-examination information is clinically relevant; the idea of tuning w to match a delay constraint is pragmatic.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Modeling claims vs. POMDP/POSMDP: The assertion that standard frameworks cannot model action-dependent inter-visit times and trajectory-dependent costs is overstated. Action-dependent sojourn distributions are standard in POSMDPs, and trajectory-dependent costs can often be handled by augmenting the (partially observed) state with sufficient summary variables (e.g., time since incidence), at least in important special cases. Please precisely delineate the gap and discuss reductions to known models.
- Belief space over full trajectories ST×{0,1}: Exact beliefs are exponential in T. The approximation restricts to K sampled trajectories but the paper does not specify how Pr(z,y,τ̃|b,τ,x) and Bayesian updates are computed under this restriction without bias. Are beliefs projected onto the span of sampled trajectories? How is normalization handled when true probability mass lies off-support?
- Approximation error (Proposition 1): The bound Ω(B̄)=ζ·(max over t of nearest-neighbor L1 distances) is stated without a concrete derivation of ζ, and it relies on strong assumptions that contradict parts of the model and experiments: no false positives/negatives, bounded screening interval W<T, and a particular discounted linear delay form. Earlier, the model allows z=2 (positive mammogram, negative biopsy), while Proposition 1 assumes perfect tests. The paper should reconcile these inconsistencies and either prove a bound under the actual experimental assumptions or clearly separate theory and practice.
- Robustness to estimation error (Theorem 1): The “locally constant a.e.” mapping claim is weak (generic continuity plus uniqueness) and does not quantify performance robustness. Moreover, convergence of the approximate policy to the true optimal one as Ω→0 is asserted, but the paper does not show that the proposed sampling scheme actually drives Ω→0 or controls it in practice.
- Computational complexity: The expression O(T (B)2 T 2 K |Y||Z|) is unclear (contains T twice, units unclear). Please provide precise time and memory complexity in terms of K, |Z|, |Y|, B̄ size, and horizon, and report wall-clock times and hardware for experiments. Parallelization claims should be substantiated.
- Evaluation methodology: Policies are evaluated on the same learned generative model used for design. There is no out-of-sample validation, posterior predictive checking, or sensitivity analysis to key parameters (e.g., test sensitivity/specificity, self-exam parameters ι, σ, threshold ỹ). The large reported savings (28–68%) could be fragile to misspecification. Provide uncertainty (e.g., bootstrap or posterior draws) and sensitivity curves.
- Tuning w to match expected delay: Selecting w post hoc to match a benchmark delay risks cherry-picking. A constrained optimization formulation or a sweep of w with Pareto frontiers and confidence bands would be more principled.
- Breast cancer model: The two-state Markov model with logistic pin(x), ptr(x) is simplistic relative to known non-stationarities (age effects, competing risks, preclinical dwell times). Please justify or compare to hazard-based models learned from the same data. Also clarify how Gail risk enters both pin and ptr and whether double-counting occurs.
- External information model: The lump growth and self-exam process is assumed with fixed parameters from literature and monthly self-exams. This is a strong behavioral assumption; its impact should be probed. Early arrivals are exogenous; heterogeneity in self-exam adherence is not modeled.
- Reproducibility and clarity: Many essential details (algorithms, proofs, data prep, hyperparameters K, B, W, priors, MCMC settings) are deferred to the supplement. Figures/tables referenced (Table 1/2, Figure 1/2) are not included. Reference formatting is incomplete and inconsistent; typos abound (e.g., “st Conference…” footer, ptr vs ptran). The paper in current form is hard to verify.

Quality
Partial. The theoretical claims rely on restrictive assumptions and lack tight, usable bounds for the implemented setting. The experimental evaluation is promising but not statistically rigorous, and critical details are missing.

Clarity
Moderate to low. The manuscript has many notation inconsistencies, missing tables/figures in the main paper, and heavy reliance on the supplement for core methods.

Originality
Moderate. The generality claimed is ambitious, but several aspects can be reframed within existing POMDP/SMDP variants. The trajectory-belief and sampling approach is a notable twist, though.

Significance
Potentially high if substantiated, given the importance of personalized screening; however, the current evidence is insufficient for a strong NeurIPS claim.

Specific suggestions
- Precisely formalize how your model differs from POSMDP/POMDP with action-dependent durations and whether a state augmentation could reduce your setting to known forms.
- Provide full algorithmic details in the main paper (at least high-level pseudocode), rigorous complexity, and ablations on K and B̄.
- Tighten Proposition 1 to the experimental assumptions or present a separate bound for imperfect tests; define and bound ζ explicitly.
- Add comprehensive sensitivity analyses and uncertainty quantification; evaluate on posterior predictive datasets; include CIs.
- Report run times, memory, and number of alpha vectors; visualize Pareto frontiers E[N] vs E[Δ].
- Clean up references and formatting; fix typos and notation (pin/ptran vs ptr), and include all referenced tables/figures.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a unified and flexible framework.
- Introduces a practical sampling-based PBVI variant tailored to dynamic screening.
- Promising empirical improvements and clear personalization behavior.

Cons for acceptance
- Overstated novelty relative to existing POMDP/POSMDP literature.
- Incomplete and sometimes inconsistent theory; weak approximation and robustness guarantees under actual experimental assumptions.
- Evaluation lacks uncertainty quantification and sensitivity; potential overfitting to a learned model.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues; missing experimental details and poor formatting.

Recommendation
Reject (weak). The ideas are interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper requires substantial theoretical tightening, rigorous and transparent experiments, and improved clarity before being ready for NeurIPS.
