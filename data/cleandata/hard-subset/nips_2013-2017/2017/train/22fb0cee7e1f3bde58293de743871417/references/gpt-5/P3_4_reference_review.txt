Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for designing dynamic, personalized screening policies that balance test burden and detection delay under very general disease and observation models. The disease is an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; external information (e.g., self-exam) can trigger unscheduled arrivals; tests are noisy. The objective is a weighted sum of discounted screening cost and delay cost, where delay can depend on both lag and incidence time. The authors cast the problem as a partially observed control problem with beliefs defined over entire state trajectories (plus a diagnosis label), derive Bellman equations, and show the value function is convex piecewise-linear in beliefs, akin to standard POMDPs. Because the belief space is enormous, they propose a point-based value iteration (PBVI) variant that: (i) samples likely disease trajectories to define a lower-dimensional belief subspace, (ii) explores reachable beliefs under random and clinically-inspired policies, and (iii) performs point-based backups to compute an approximate policy. They state a bound that the approximation error is controlled by the sampling density of the belief simplex under assumptions (no FP/FN, convex delay cost, bounded screening interval). They further give a robustness result claiming local constancy of optimal actions in model–belief space except on a lower-dimensional set. A case study on a de-identified breast cancer dataset (~45k women, ~84k mammograms) fits a 2-state Markov progression with Gail risk covariates and a self-exam lump growth model. With an appropriately tuned weight to match baseline expected delay, DPSCREEN purportedly reduces the expected number of screens by 28–68% vs annual screening, with and without self-examination. A synthetic comparison shows that quadratic delay costs yield more aggressive policies.

Quality
- Technical formulation: The problem is carefully motivated and the graphical model and belief-based Bellman recursion are natural generalizations of POMDPs to endogenous arrival and trajectory-dependent costs. The piecewise-linear-convex value function follows the classic Sondik result.
- However, there are several technical concerns:
  - The Bellman equation (3) uses a maximization with negative costs, while the overall problem (1) is a minimization. If the authors intentionally flipped sign, this should be stated explicitly; otherwise it is a correctness issue.
  - The belief space is over trajectory–label pairs, which is combinatorially large; tractability hinges on the sampling procedure. The approximation bound (Proposition 1) assumes no false positives/negatives and convex delay cost and introduces an unspecified constant ζ; without quantifying ζ or the sampling density, the bound is difficult to interpret or use.
  - The robustness theorem (“locally constant” optimal policy off a measure-zero boundary) is intuitive but very weak; practical robustness to model misspecification is not established.
  - Some modeling inconsistencies: Y is defined as a finite-state process, but later modeled as a continuous tumor size with thresholding; the main model assumes no false positives, whereas the empirical section explicitly uses reported sensitivity/specificity of mammography and biopsy.
- Empirics: The empirical study is sizeable and well-motivated, but the evaluation is entirely model-based. The disease model used in experiments is a simple 2-state Markov chain; thus the empirical section does not stress-test the claimed generality (non-Markov, non-stationary outcomes). The weight w is tuned to match baseline expected delay, which risks cherry-picking; there is no comparison against prior POMDP screening policies (e.g., Ayer et al. OR 2012; Maillart et al. OR 2008) under the same fitted model, or against recent risk- and density-based interval guidelines. Computational scalability (K, B, T, runtimes) is not reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well organized, with helpful intuition for personalization and external information. That said, several definitions and assumptions need tightening for reproducibility:
- Clarify the objective sign in the Bellman recursion, and whether costs are negated to convert to a reward-maximization form.
- Reconcile the finite/discrete Y assumption with the continuous lump model; specify what sufficient statistics of Y enter the arrival process (e.g., crossing probability).
- State explicitly when false positives/negatives are allowed (empirics) vs ruled out (theory), and how that affects the value function and bounds.
- Provide more details on trajectory sampling, belief exploration policies, and the number of sampled points; include complexity and runtime in experiments.

Originality
The problem setting—endogenous arrival via external information and trajectory-dependent delay costs—is a useful generalization of prior screening POMDPs. The PWL property is known; the approximate solution is a PBVI variant with sampling restricted to likely trajectories. The novelty lies mainly in combining these pieces for screening with exogenous arrivals and in arguing approximation quality under sampling. Related OR and medical simulation work is reasonably cited, but direct comparison to constrained POMDPs and existing personalized screening POMDPs is limited. The claim of “first such personalized screening policies in a very general setting” should be nuanced in light of prior POMDP-based screening and risk-based interval work.

Significance
Personalized, dynamic screening is important and squarely within NeurIPS’s interest in sequential decision-making under uncertainty in healthcare. If made more rigorous and empirically validated against strong baselines, this line of work could influence practice and spur follow-on research. As written, the theoretical guarantees are weakly informative, and the empirical gains may partly reflect modeling and tuning choices; nonetheless the direction is promising.

Pros
- Addresses an impactful healthcare decision problem with a principled sequential model.
- Incorporates external information and endogenous arrivals.
- Piecewise-linear value characterization and PBVI-style algorithm adapted to high-dimensional beliefs.
- Large real-world dataset; clear personalization effects; exploration of disease-dependent cost shapes.

Cons
- Bellman sign/optimization mismatch and several modeling inconsistencies.
- Approximation and robustness results rely on strong/unrealistic assumptions and lack usable constants.
- Empirical validation limited to a simple Markov model; no comparisons to prior POMDP screening methods or modern risk-based guidelines.
- No runtime/scalability evidence; reproducibility details are sparse.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix and clarify the Bellman recursion and objective sign; provide a self-contained derivation.
- Quantify the approximation bound (estimate ζ, report Ω(B̄) empirically) and relax the no-FP/FN assumption.
- Provide ablations: with/without external information; different sampling budgets; sensitivity to model misspecification.
- Compare against Ayer et al. (2012), Maillart et al. (2008), and recent risk/density-based interval strategies under the same fitted model.
- Report computational resources, K, B, T, number of alpha-vectors, and runtimes.
- Demonstrate at least one non-Markov or non-stationary process to showcase generality.
- Release code or detailed pseudocode sufficient for reproduction.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The problem is important and the proposed framework is promising, but technical clarity, theoretical guarantees, and empirical validation need strengthening to meet NeurIPS standards. With the above revisions, this work could become a solid contribution. Reviewer confidence: medium-high.
