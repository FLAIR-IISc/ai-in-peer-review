

# Peer Review: DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel framework for developing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the delay in disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a general finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating external information (such as patient self-examinations) that can trigger early visits. They formulate the problem as a partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) and develop a computationally tractable algorithm (DPSCREEN) based on point-based value iteration (PBVI) to approximate the optimal policy. The authors evaluate their approach on breast cancer screening data, demonstrating significant reductions (28-68%) in screening frequency while maintaining the same expected delay in disease detection compared to current clinical practices.

The paper builds on the POMDP literature (e.g., [18], [39]) but extends it to a more general setting that accommodates non-Markovian disease progression models and incorporates external information processes. It addresses limitations of previous personalized screening approaches (e.g., [7], [9], [10]) that assume Markovian disease processes and stationary test outcome models.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending POMDP methods to handle general disease progression models with absorbing states, incorporating external information, and addressing the computational intractability of the exact solution. The characterization of the value function as convex and piecewise linear (Lemma 1) is a valuable theoretical insight.

2. **Practical Impact**: The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating substantial reductions in screening frequency (28-68%) while maintaining the same expected delay in disease detection. This has clear potential to improve healthcare resource allocation and reduce patient burden.

3. **Methodological Rigor**: The authors provide a detailed theoretical analysis of the approximation error (Proposition 1) and discuss robustness to model estimation errors (Theorem 1), which is crucial for real-world implementation.

4. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper provides a thorough and well-structured problem formulation that clearly articulates the challenges of personalized screening, distinguishing their approach from previous work.

5. **Comprehensive Related Work**: The related work section effectively contextualizes the paper within both the decision theory literature (POMDPs) and medical screening literature, highlighting the limitations of prior approaches.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated Originality**: The claim that this is "the first such personalized screening policies in a very general setting" is problematic. While the paper extends previous work, the related work section (Section 5) cites several papers that already address personalized screening with similar or more general models ([7], [9], [10]). The paper should more clearly articulate how their approach differs from these prior works, particularly in terms of the specific restrictions they relax.

2. **Simplified Disease Model**: The breast cancer model uses a two-state Markov process (Healthy/Disease), which is a significant simplification of actual breast cancer progression. Real breast cancer involves multiple intermediate stages (e.g., benign lumps, ductal carcinoma in situ), which the paper acknowledges but does not adequately model. This limits the applicability of their results.

3. **Unrealistic Assumptions**: The paper assumes no false positives (Section 2), which is highly unrealistic for mammography and other screening tests. This assumption greatly simplifies the model but undermines the practical relevance of the results.

4. **Limited Experimental Validation**: The experiments are limited to breast cancer, with no evaluation across multiple disease types. The synthetic exercise in Figure 2 (comparing linear vs. quadratic delay costs) is interesting but insufficient to demonstrate generalizability across different disease models.

5. **Theoretical Gap in Approximation Error Analysis**: Proposition 1 claims the approximation error is bounded by Ω(B̄), but the derivation is not sufficiently rigorous. The definition of Ω(B̄) as "the density of sampling of the belief simplex" is vague, and the proof is relegated to supplementary materials without sufficient detail. Without seeing the proof, it's difficult to assess the validity of this bound.

6. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper compares against annual and biennial screening policies but does not compare against other advanced personalized screening approaches (e.g., [10], [9]) that might be more relevant benchmarks.

## Specific Recommendations

1. **Clarify the Contribution**: The authors should more precisely articulate how their approach differs from prior work, particularly in terms of the specific restrictions they relax. A table comparing the assumptions of previous approaches versus their method would be helpful.

2. **Address False Positives**: The paper should address the impact of false positives on their model and results. This could be done by extending the model to include false positive rates or by discussing how their approach would need to be modified to account for them.

3. **Expand Experimental Validation**: The authors should include experiments with more realistic disease models (e.g., multi-state models for breast cancer) and potentially evaluate on additional disease types to demonstrate broader applicability.

4. **Strengthen Theoretical Analysis**: The authors should provide more detail on the proof of Proposition 1 in the main paper or in the supplementary materials. They should also discuss the practical implications of the approximation error bound.

5. **Improve Clarity on Belief Space**: The belief space is defined as |ST × {0, 1}| dimensions, but the paper doesn't clearly explain why this is necessary or how it differs from standard POMDP belief spaces.

## Overall Assessment

This paper addresses an important real-world problem with significant potential impact on healthcare. The theoretical framework is well-motivated and builds on solid foundations in decision theory. The experimental results are promising, demonstrating substantial reductions in screening frequency.

However, the paper's claims of novelty are overstated, and the theoretical analysis of the approximation error is insufficiently rigorous. The unrealistic assumptions (no false positives) and simplified disease model limit the practical applicability of the results. The paper would be significantly stronger with more detailed theoretical justification, more realistic modeling assumptions, and broader experimental validation.

## Recommendation

**Weak Accept**

This is a strong paper with significant potential impact, but it has several important weaknesses that need to be addressed before it can be accepted. The claims of novelty need to be refined, the theoretical analysis needs to be strengthened, and the experimental validation needs to be expanded to include more realistic modeling assumptions and disease types.

If the authors address the concerns raised in this review (particularly regarding the novelty claim, the theoretical analysis of approximation error, and the unrealistic assumptions), this paper would be an excellent contribution to the field. As it stands, it's a good paper that needs some important revisions before it can be accepted.
