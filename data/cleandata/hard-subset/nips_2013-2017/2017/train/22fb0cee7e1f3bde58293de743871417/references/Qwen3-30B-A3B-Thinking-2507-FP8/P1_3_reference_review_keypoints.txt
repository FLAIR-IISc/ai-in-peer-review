- Summary: DPSCREEN is a framework for personalized screening that models non‑Markov disease progression, incorporates external information, and uses a point‑based approximation to balance screening costs vs detection delays; validated on 45k breast cancer patients with 28–68% fewer screenings at equivalent delays.

- Strength 1 (Technical): Handles a much broader class of disease models than prior work and provides provable approximation error bounds (Lemma 1, Proposition 1).

- Strength 2 (Real-world validation): Large breast cancer case study using Gail model + MCMC shows substantial practical improvements over current clinical policies.

- Strength 3 (Personalization): Adapts screening to patient features and dynamic history (tests, external info), improving over one‑size‑fits‑all policies.

- Strength 4 (Theoretical guarantees): Provides a useful approximation error bound that justifies practical use.

- Weakness 1 (Implementation): Insufficient detail on belief‑space reduction and how likely trajectories are sampled/prioritized in Algorithm 1.

- Weakness 2 (Baselines): Does not sufficiently compare to recent personalized screening systems (e.g., ConfidentCare).

- Weakness 3 (Assumptions): Simplifying assumptions (no false positives, linear delay costs) may limit real‑world applicability; only limited exploration of alternatives.

- Quality: Technically sound with strong theory and convincing empirical evidence; limitations are acknowledged.

- Clarity: Generally well written with helpful figures, though some POMDP technical sections may be challenging for non‑experts; supplementary materials aid reproducibility.

- Originality: Novel extension of screening policy methods to non‑Markovian disease processes and external information; a new application of these decision‑theoretic tools.

- Significance: Potentially high healthcare impact with clinically meaningful screening reductions and extensibility to other diseases.

- Recommendation: Accept — strong technical contribution, theoretical foundation, and empirical validation; noted limitations do not undermine the main contribution.

- Pro/Con summary: Pro — significant technical and empirical advances in personalized healthcare; Con — need for more implementation detail and stronger comparison to recent related work.
