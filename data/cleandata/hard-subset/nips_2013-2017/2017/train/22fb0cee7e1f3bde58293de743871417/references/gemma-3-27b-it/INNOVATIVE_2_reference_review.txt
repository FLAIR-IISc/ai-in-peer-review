This paper tackles the crucial problem of personalized screening policies, moving beyond one-size-fits-all approaches. The core idea – developing policies that dynamically adjust screening frequency based on patient features, screening history, and disease models – is highly novel and potentially impactful. The authors attempt to address several significant challenges in this domain, including non-Markovian disease progression, delayed cost of non-screening, external information, and computational intractability. The formulation is rigorous and the attempt to provide theoretical bounds on approximation error is commendable.

The primary strength of this work lies in its ambition and generality. Unlike prior work focusing on specific diseases and restrictive assumptions (Markov processes, stationary test outcomes), this framework aims for broad applicability. The inclusion of external information, like patient self-exams, is a realistic and important addition. The breast cancer example provides a concrete illustration, and the reported reduction in screening frequency (28-68%) while maintaining delay is very promising. The synthetic exercise highlighting the impact of cost function choice is insightful.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of detailed validation and a somewhat hand-wavy treatment of computational complexities. While the authors acknowledge the computational intractability and propose an approximation algorithm, the description of the point-based value iteration and belief space construction (Algorithm 1) feels high-level. The statement about complexity (O(T(B)^2 T^2 K|Y||Z|)) doesn't offer much practical guidance.  The assumption of no false positives, while stated, feels limiting, and the extension to more realistic scenarios isn't discussed. The robustness analysis, while theoretically appealing (Theorem 1), lacks concrete demonstration of sensitivity to model estimation error. The reliance on the Gail model for risk assessment, while standard, represents an external dependency that could limit generalizability. The appendix references are crucial, but a more detailed exposition of key algorithmic steps *within* the main text would improve clarity. 

The discussion of related work is thorough but could be more critical. The authors accurately point out limitations of existing frameworks but don't fully engage with potentially relevant advances in related areas like Bayesian optimization or adaptive experimental design, which might offer alternative approaches to policy learning.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The framework is a significant departure from existing personalized screening approaches, offering a more general and flexible solution.
*   **Significance:** Personalized screening is a critical area with high potential for improving healthcare outcomes and reducing costs. A successful general framework could have a wide impact.
*   **Theoretical grounding:**  The attempt to provide approximation error bounds, even if not fully tight, demonstrates a commitment to rigorous analysis.
*   **Promising results:** The initial results on breast cancer data are encouraging, suggesting that substantial improvements in screening efficiency are possible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Computational complexity:** The lack of detailed computational analysis and practical scalability discussion raises concerns about real-world applicability.
*   **Limited validation:** The empirical evaluation is restricted to a single dataset and disease. More extensive validation would strengthen the claims.
*   **Algorithmic detail:**  The high-level description of key algorithmic components hinders understanding and reproducibility.
*   **Simplifying assumptions:** The assumption of no false positives and the reliance on a specific risk model limit the generality of the approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations, I lean towards acceptance. The paper presents a genuinely novel and potentially significant contribution to the field of personalized screening. The strengths – particularly the generality and theoretical grounding – outweigh the weaknesses. The authors are tackling a very difficult problem, and a perfectly polished solution is not expected at this stage. I believe the ideas presented are compelling enough to warrant inclusion in NIPS, and the feedback from reviewers could guide future work to address the remaining challenges.  I would encourage the authors to focus on providing more detailed algorithmic descriptions, conducting more extensive validation, and addressing the computational complexity concerns in a revised version.



**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions).
