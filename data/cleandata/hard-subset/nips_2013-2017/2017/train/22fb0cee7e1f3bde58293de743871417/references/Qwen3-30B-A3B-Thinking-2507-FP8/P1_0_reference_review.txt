

# Peer Review: DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses the problem of developing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in disease detection. The authors propose a general framework called DPSCREEN that can handle arbitrary finite state stochastic disease processes (not limited to Markov processes), incorporate external information (e.g., patient self-examinations), and dynamically personalize screening schedules based on patient features and history.

The key contributions include:
1. A formal model of personalized screening that generalizes previous work by relaxing assumptions about disease progression models
2. A computationally efficient approximation algorithm using point-based value iteration to handle the high-dimensional belief space
3. Theoretical guarantees on approximation error
4. Evaluation on a breast cancer dataset showing significant improvements (28-68% reduction in screenings) over current clinical policies while maintaining the same expected delay in detection

The paper distinguishes itself from previous works by not restricting to Markov disease processes or stationary test outcome models, which is a significant limitation in existing screening frameworks. The authors also demonstrate how different cost functions (linear vs. quadratic delay costs) lead to substantially different screening policies, highlighting the importance of choosing appropriate cost structures for specific diseases.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides a well-structured mathematical framework with clear definitions and proofs. The key Lemma 1 showing the convexity of the value function is particularly important for making the problem tractable.

2. **Novel Approach**: The DPSCREEN framework is genuinely novel in its ability to handle arbitrary finite state stochastic disease processes without restrictive Markov assumptions, which addresses a significant limitation in the literature.

3. **Compelling Results**: The experimental results are impressive, showing substantial reductions in screening frequency (28-68%) while maintaining equivalent detection delays. The demonstration of personalization (different screening schedules for different risk groups) is particularly convincing.

4. **Theoretical Guarantees**: The paper provides a bound on the approximation error (Proposition 1), which is crucial for justifying the practical use of the approximate policy.

5. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper carefully delineates the differences between their approach and previous work, making the contribution clear and well-justified.

6. **Real-World Impact**: The application to breast cancer screening has significant practical implications for healthcare resource allocation, which is a strong point for a NeurIPS submission.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Detail on Model Estimation**: The paper mentions using observational data to estimate the disease model but provides insufficient detail about the estimation process. The supplementary materials are referenced extensively, but the main paper should include more information about how they estimated the disease progression model from the observational data.

2. **Simplified Test Model**: The paper assumes no false positives or negatives in test results, which is a strong simplification that may not hold in real-world breast cancer screening (where mammograms can have false positives).

3. **External Information Model**: The model for external information (breast lumps) is simplified and may not capture the full complexity of patient self-examinations in practice.

4. **Computational Complexity**: While the paper provides a complexity analysis, it may be overly optimistic for large-scale real-world applications. The paper doesn't discuss how the method would scale to more complex diseases with larger state spaces.

5. **Limited Comparison to Machine Learning Approaches**: The paper compares primarily to clinical policies but doesn't compare to other machine learning or decision-theoretic approaches that could potentially address similar problems.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical foundations are strong, and the experimental results are compelling. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the need for model estimation from observational data) and provide appropriate caveats. The paper is a complete piece of work rather than a position paper, with a clear methodology, theoretical analysis, and experimental validation.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to solution to evaluation. The mathematical notation is well-defined, and the core ideas are explained with concrete examples. The graphical representation of personalization (Figure 1) is particularly helpful. The paper would benefit from a slightly more detailed description of the model estimation process in the main text rather than relying on supplementary materials.

## Originality Assessment

This work is significantly different from previous NIPS papers on similar topics. While there have been previous works on POMDP-based screening (e.g., Ayer et al., "A POMDP approach to personalize mammography screening"), those works assume Markov disease processes and stationary test outcome models, which is a key limitation that this paper addresses. The paper also differs from other relevant works by not restricting to a specific disease model or assuming stationary test outcomes. The approach to handle the high-dimensional belief space using point-based value iteration is novel for this application domain.

## Significance Assessment

The problem of personalized screening is highly significant for healthcare, with the potential to improve patient outcomes while reducing healthcare costs. The paper demonstrates a meaningful improvement over current clinical practices (28-68% reduction in screenings), which is substantial for a healthcare application. The framework could be applied to many diseases beyond breast cancer, making it broadly applicable. The demonstration of how different cost functions (linear vs. quadratic) lead to different screening policies is particularly valuable for practitioners.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper. It addresses an important real-world problem with a theoretically sound approach that advances the state of the art in personalized screening. The results are compelling, and the paper makes clear contributions over previous work.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Provide more detail about the model estimation process from observational data in the main paper (not just in supplementary materials).

2. Discuss the implications of the no false positives/negatives assumption for real-world applications.

3. Consider adding a comparison to other machine learning-based approaches for screening, even if only as a brief discussion.

4. Clarify the computational complexity for larger-scale applications (e.g., how the method would scale to more complex diseases).

5. Consider discussing how the framework might handle multiple diseases or competing risks, which is an important extension.

## Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Addresses a significant real-world problem with meaningful healthcare impact
- Provides a theoretically sound and novel approach that generalizes previous work
- Demonstrates substantial improvements over current clinical practices
- Includes theoretical guarantees on approximation error
- Well-structured and clearly written
- Makes a clear contribution to the field beyond previous NIPS papers

**Arguments for Rejection:**
- Limited detail on model estimation process
- Simplified assumptions about test accuracy
- No comparison to other machine learning approaches

Overall, the strengths of this paper substantially outweigh the weaknesses, and it represents a strong contribution to the field of decision theory with important healthcare applications.
