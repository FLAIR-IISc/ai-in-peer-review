### Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

**Summary:**
This paper proposes a general and powerful framework called DPSCREEN for computing personalized, dynamic screening policies for diseases. The authors model the problem as a sequential decision-making process under uncertainty, aiming to minimize a weighted sum of screening costs and costs associated with delayed disease detection. The key contributions are threefold: 1) A highly general problem formulation that accommodates non-Markovian disease progression models, trajectory-dependent costs, and unscheduled patient arrivals triggered by external information (e.g., self-exams). This formulation is more expressive than prior work based on standard POMDPs. 2) A computationally tractable, approximate solution method based on Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI). The authors cleverly adapt PBVI to the extremely high-dimensional belief space (distributions over entire disease trajectories) by first sampling a set of representative trajectories to create a lower-dimensional projection, and then sampling reachable belief points within that space. 3) An empirical demonstration using a large breast cancer dataset, showing that DPSCREEN can reduce the number of screenings by 28-68% compared to standard annual screening, while maintaining the same expected delay in detection.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The problem formulation is a significant advance over the state-of-the-art in personalized screening. Previous POMDP-based approaches (e.g., Ayer et al., 2007) are limited to Markovian disease models and state-dependent costs. By defining the state space over entire trajectories (`~s`) and allowing trajectory-dependent costs (`C(td - tD, tD)`), the framework can capture complex disease dynamics and more realistic cost structures (e.g., where the harm of a delay depends on when the disease began). The inclusion of an external information process that can trigger unscheduled visits is also a novel and practically important feature. This generality makes the framework a powerful tool for a wide range of medical screening problems beyond the specific examples shown.

2.  **Methodological Quality:** The proposed solution is technically sound and well-motivated. Recognizing the intractability of solving the Bellman equations over the full belief space of trajectories, the authors propose a principled approximation. The two-stage sampling procedure (first sampling trajectories, then sampling beliefs) is an intelligent way to manage the curse of dimensionality and is a valuable contribution to the application of approximate dynamic programming in complex domains. The paper provides theoretical justification for this approach with an approximation error bound (Proposition 1) and a robustness analysis regarding model estimation errors (Theorem 1), which adds significant rigor.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. The use of a large, real-world breast cancer dataset grounds the work in a high-impact application. The results are impressive, demonstrating substantial reductions in screening frequency without compromising on detection delay. The paper effectively showcases the "personalized" aspect of the policy by comparing recommendations for high-risk vs. low-risk patients (Table 2) and by illustrating how the policy adapts to a patient's history (Figure 1). Furthermore, the synthetic experiment comparing linear vs. quadratic delay costs effectively highlights the framework's flexibility and the importance of choosing a cost function appropriate for the specific disease.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**

1.  **Disconnect Between General Model and Specific Experiment:** The primary strength of the paper is the generality of the disease model, which is not restricted to be Markovian. However, the breast cancer experiment employs a simple two-state (Healthy, Disease) Markov model. While this is a reasonable model for the application, it does not fully showcase the power of the proposed framework over a standard POMDP formulation (which could also handle the external information process with some state augmentation). The paper would be significantly strengthened by either:
    *   Explicitly justifying why a simple Markov model was sufficient and discussing the potential gains of a more complex model for breast cancer.
    *   Including a synthetic experiment with a non-Markovian or semi-Markovian disease process to concretely demonstrate the unique capabilities of the proposed general framework.

2.  **Scalability and Computational Cost:** The paper provides the computational complexity but lacks a practical discussion of it. The PBVI algorithm can be computationally intensive, and its performance depends heavily on the number of sampled trajectories (`K`) and belief points (`B`). Could the authors provide the values of `K` and `B` used in the experiments, and the approximate time required to compute the policies? Understanding the scalability is crucial for assessing the practical applicability of this method to problems with larger state spaces or longer time horizons.

3.  **Clarity on Model Estimation:** The model estimation process is described briefly, with details deferred to the appendix. A key aspect is estimating the transition probabilities `pin(x)` and `ptran(x)`. It would be helpful to clarify if the MCMC estimation was performed on the same dataset used for the policy evaluation. If so, were standard practices like cross-validation used to avoid overfitting and ensure the performance results are generalizable? While Theorem 1 provides a theoretical guarantee of robustness, a discussion of the practical impact of uncertainty in the estimated parameters on policy performance would be valuable.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to both the machine learning community (in applied approximate dynamic programming) and the medical decision-making field. The problem formulation is novel and general, the proposed solution is technically sound and well-justified, and the experimental results are compelling. The work clearly advances the state of the art in developing personalized screening policies. The weaknesses are relatively minor and mostly relate to opportunities for making the paper even stronger. The work is well-written, clearly organized, and positions itself effectively against related literature. The ideas presented are likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers. I strongly recommend acceptance.
