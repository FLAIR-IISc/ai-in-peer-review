Summary and relation to prior work
The paper formulates dynamic, personalized screening as a sequential decision problem with (i) a general, finite-state disease process with an absorbing disease state, potentially non-Markov and time-inhomogeneous; (ii) exogenous, patient-side “external information” (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger unscheduled arrivals; (iii) action-dependent time between decision epochs (next recommended screen); and (iv) costs that couple across time via delay from disease incidence to detection. The objective is to minimize a weighted sum of discounted screening cost and expected delay cost. The authors derive a belief-state formulation, prove the value function is convex and piecewise-linear in the belief (as in POMDPs), and describe an exact alpha-vector recursion which is intractable. They then propose DPSCREEN, a point-based value iteration (PBVI) approach that samples likely state trajectories to define a tractable lower-dimensional belief subset, performs backups on these beliefs, and outputs an approximately optimal policy. They provide a sampling-density type bound on the approximation error and a “local constancy” result showing the optimal action is stable to small model estimation errors away from a lower-dimensional set.

Empirically, using a large breast cancer dataset (Athena; 45k women, 84k mammograms), they estimate a two-state (H/D) model parameterized by Gail risk, literature-based test sensitivity/specificity, and a simple tumor growth/self-exam model. They compare DPSCREEN to annual (and in appendix, biennial) screening, tuning the weight to match benchmark expected delays. They report 28–68% fewer screenings at comparable expected delay, and show personalization across risk strata and differences under linear vs. quadratic delay costs.

Relative to prior work: Operations research screening models based on POMDPs (e.g., Ayer et al. 2012; Maillart et al. 2008) typically assume Markov disease and stationary observation models with fixed decision epochs. Bayesian design/joint-model approaches (e.g., Rizopoulos 2016; ConfidentCare) focus on information gain or particular disease/test models. The present work aims to generalize the process and cost structure, handle exogenous arrivals, and compute policies rather than compare a fixed slate.

Strengths
- General model: Allows non-Markov disease trajectories and exogenous information that induces endogenous decision times, and handles trajectory-dependent delay costs.
- Theoretical structure: Extends POMDP piecewise-linear convexity to this setting and derives an alpha-vector dynamic program. The “local constancy” result formalizes robustness of optimal actions to small model estimation errors.
- Practical algorithm: A PBVI variant that samples likely trajectories to reduce the belief dimension addresses the core combinatorial explosion from beliefs over trajectories.
- Empirical illustration at scale: Uses a large dataset and compares against widely used clinical schedules; shows substantial reductions in tests at fixed expected delay, and clear personalization by risk and history.
- Sensible disease-specific analysis: Demonstrates impact of cost shape (linear vs. quadratic) on policy aggressiveness.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Assumptions vs. experiments: The formalism assumes no false positives when z ∈ Z+ triggers diagnosis. In experiments, z includes mammogram+/biopsy– outcomes (false positives). Clarify how Z+ is defined and how false positives/negatives are handled throughout the theory, Bellman equation, and cost accounting.
- Sign and objective consistency: Equation (3) uses a “max” over τ with a negative cost term; but the problem is stated as cost minimization. Please reconcile sign conventions and confirm the value function definition.
- Approximation error bound: Ω(B̄) uses a constant ζ, the “maximum expected loss in one slot,” but ζ is not precisely defined or bounded in terms of model primitives. The bound is only over the sampled-belief simplex; discuss how error behaves when true beliefs fall outside the convex hull of sampled trajectories, especially when the true process is non-Markov and K is limited.
- Estimation error and evaluation: The robustness result is qualitative (piecewise-constant regions). There is no finite-sample or PAC-style bound relating parameter estimation error to performance loss of the learned policy. Given model-based evaluation (no counterfactuals), sensitivity analyses (e.g., posterior predictive checks, bootstrap CIs for E[N], E[Δ], and robustness to self-exam parameters and growth rates) would strengthen claims.
- Computational detail: The stated complexity O(T(B)2T2K|Y||Z|) is unusual; provide a derivation, typical values for K and B, wall-clock runtimes, and parallelization gains. Discuss scalability with longer horizons and richer test menus.
- Relation to POSMDPs/options/continuous-time: Prior work on POMDPs with action-dependent sojourns (e.g., POSMDPs, options) may handle endogenous decision spacing. More explicit positioning would help. Also discuss whether trajectory-dependent delay costs can be captured via augmented hidden state (e.g., time-since-onset) and whether this reduces to a high-dimensional but Markov POMDP.
- Practical deployment: The weight tuning to match expected delay of current practice depends on the calibrated model; practitioners might prefer constraints (e.g., chance constraints on delay). Clarify how threshold ỹ for self-exam arrival is chosen and whether it is part of the design.
- Clarity: Some notation (C̃ cases) and definitions (diagnosis label transitions, belief updates with arrivals) would benefit from a concise, self-contained algorithmic outline in the main text. Several references are incomplete.

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality: The modeling choices are reasonable; the belief-state DP is sound in spirit, and the PBVI approach is appropriate. However, theoretical guarantees are high-level: the approximation bound depends on an unspecified Lipschitz-like constant and sampling coverage; robustness to estimation is qualitative. Empirical evaluation is model-based without uncertainty quantification.
- Clarity: Generally clear, but some inconsistencies (no-false-positives vs. experiments; sign in Bellman), heavy reliance on appendix for key algorithms/proofs, and some notational density.
- Originality: The combination of trajectory-dependent costs, exogenous arrivals, and general disease evolution in a belief-DP with a tailored PBVI and error bound is novel within screening. The work builds on classic POMDP structure but applies it in a less restrictive setting than prior screening models.
- Significance: If validated and robustly calibrated, the approach could materially influence screening intervals and reduce resource use. The general framework could transfer to other diseases with different delay cost shapes. Stronger validation would increase impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, practical problem with a principled sequential decision framework.
- Novel handling of endogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent costs.
- Demonstrates large, policy-relevant gains under a realistic dataset.
- Provides some theoretical structure (piecewise linearity; approximation and robustness statements).

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical guarantees are not fully quantified; key constants and coverage conditions are vague.
- Model-based evaluation lacks uncertainty/sensitivity analysis and external validation.
- Some assumptions and notational inconsistencies need clarification.
- Limited empirical comparison to stronger baselines (e.g., ConfidentCare, joint-model policies).

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Z+, false positives/negatives, and ensure consistency between theory and experiments.
- Fix the objective/sign in the Bellman equation and provide a succinct main-text algorithm.
- Provide finite-sample performance bounds linking parameter estimation error to policy regret, or at least detailed sensitivity analyses with CIs.
- Report computational complexity empirically and discuss scalability.
- Expand related work to POSMDPs/options and clarify how/why those frameworks are insufficient here.
- Consider constrained formulations directly (e.g., Lagrangian/C-POMDP) and robust evaluation.
