**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for creating dynamic, personalized screening policies for diseases. The authors formulate the problem as a generalized Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP), where the objective is to minimize a weighted sum of screening costs and delay-in-detection costs. The framework is designed to be highly general, accommodating arbitrary finite-state disease processes (not necessarily Markovian), patient-specific features, and external information (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger unscheduled visits. Due to the computational intractability of solving this problem exactly (the belief space is over entire state trajectories), the authors propose an approximate solution based on Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI). The key innovation here is a two-step sampling process to make PBVI tractable: first sampling a set of likely disease trajectories and then sampling a set of reachable beliefs within this reduced space. The authors provide a theoretical bound on the approximation error and a robustness result concerning model estimation errors. They demonstrate their approach on a large, real-world breast cancer dataset, showing that DPSCREEN can reduce the number of screenings by 28-68% compared to standard annual clinical guidelines, while achieving a similar expected delay in detection.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The problem of optimizing personalized screening schedules is of high practical importance in healthcare. Moving beyond one-size-fits-all policies towards data-driven, personalized approaches has the potential for significant societal and economic impact.

2.  **General Formulation:** The proposed framework is impressively general. By not restricting the disease model to be Markovian and by explicitly modeling trajectory-dependent delay costs and external information sources, the formulation is more faithful to the complexity of real-world clinical scenarios than previous POMDP-based approaches (e.g., Ayer et al., Operations Research 2015).

3.  **Empirical Validation on Real Data:** The use of a large dataset of 45,000 patients for model estimation and evaluation is a major strength. It grounds the work in a concrete and challenging real-world application, lending credibility to the results.

4.  **Promising Results:** The reported reductions in screening frequency are substantial. A method that can maintain clinical outcomes (delay in detection) while significantly reducing the costs and burdens of screening is a compelling contribution.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Disconnect Between General Framework and Specific Implementation:** The paper's primary conceptual contribution is a framework for *arbitrary* stochastic processes. However, the experimental validation relies on a simple, two-state (Healthy/Disease) discrete-time Markov model. This raises the question of whether the full, complex machinery of trajectory-based belief updates is necessary for the problem instance actually solved. The benefits of the general framework over more established, simpler POMDP models are asserted but not empirically demonstrated.

2.  **Weak Baselines:** The primary benchmarks are fixed clinical policies (annual and biennial screening). While these are the standard of care, they are not state-of-the-art *computational* methods for this type of optimization problem. A more rigorous evaluation would compare DPSCREEN against the established POMDP models cited in the paper (e.g., Ayer et al., Erenay et al.). Without this comparison, it is unclear if the performance gains come from the novel aspects of the DPSCREEN formulation or simply from using any optimization-based policy over a fixed heuristic. The paper claims existing frameworks cannot solve their setup, but it would have been more convincing to show that a standard POMDP formulation using the same estimated two-state Markov model yields substantially worse results.

3.  **Practicality of Theoretical Guarantees:** Proposition 1 provides an approximation error bound, which is standard for PBVI-style algorithms. However, this bound depends on the sampling density `Ω(B̄)` and a constant `ζ` representing maximum single-step loss. Such bounds are often loose in practice and provide little guidance on how to set the sampling parameters (K, B) to achieve a desired level of performance. Similarly, Theorem 1 establishes local robustness to model estimation error but does not quantify how "close" the model estimate must be to the true model for the policy to remain optimal. The theoretical contributions, while present, feel more like formal exercises than tools that provide practical insight or confidence in the method.

4.  **Computational Complexity and Scalability:** The worst-case complexity is high, and the paper only briefly mentions that it can be parallelized. Crucial details regarding the computational cost are missing. What were the run times for the experiments? What were the values of K (number of sampled trajectories) and B (number of belief points) used? How does the performance and runtime scale with these parameters? Without this information, it is difficult to assess the practicality of this method for more complex models (e.g., with more states or a longer time horizon T).

5.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The belief sampling procedure in Algorithm 1 is a critical component of the method but is described at a high level. The construction of the reachable belief set B̄ relies on "random exploration" and actions from "clinical policies." The exact nature of this exploration and how these policies are chosen and simulated is underspecified, which would make reproduction difficult.

**Conclusion**

This paper addresses an important and challenging problem with a novel and general formulation. The application to a large, real-world breast cancer dataset is commendable, and the results are promising. However, the work is weakened by a significant gap between the sophistication of the proposed framework and the simplicity of the model used in the experiments. More importantly, the evaluation is conducted against non-adaptive clinical heuristics rather than against established optimization-based methods from the literature, making it difficult to isolate the contribution of the proposed innovations. While the idea is interesting, the paper would be much stronger with a more thorough and convincing validation that demonstrates the necessity and superiority of this new, complex approach over existing, well-understood methods on a comparable problem formulation.
