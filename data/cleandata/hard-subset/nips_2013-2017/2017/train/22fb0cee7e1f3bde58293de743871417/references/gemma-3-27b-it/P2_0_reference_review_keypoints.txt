- Proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic personalized screening that adapts to patient features, history, and external info to trade off screening frequency vs detection delay.
- Provides a computationally tractable PBVI-based algorithm with bounded approximation error for non-Markovian/stochastic disease models.
- Empirical results on breast cancer data show large reductions in screening frequency with comparable detection delays; policies vary with cost function (linear vs quadratic).
- Extends prior personalized screening work by relaxing Markovian assumptions and incorporating external information.
- Differentiates from simulation-based medical literature by offering an optimization-based approach that explores a broader policy space.
- Connects to controlled sensing literature while highlighting different assumptions and objectives.
- Strength: very general model handling dynamic survival and first-hitting-time disease processes and external covariates.
- Strength: rigorous theoretical analysis with approximation error bounds supporting the algorithm.
- Strength: PBVI approximation makes the approach computationally practical.
- Strength: convincing empirical validation and informative comparisons to existing clinical policies.
- Strength: sensitivity analysis shows impact of cost function choice on policy design.
- Strength: clear illustrative example of personalization based on patient risk and history.
- Weakness: model complexity and dense stochastic/POMDP notation may hinder accessibility.
- Weakness: relies on assumptions (e.g., conditional independence of tests, accurate outcomes) without full robustness analysis to violations.
- Weakness: limited details on data preprocessing and handling of missing data in the empirical study.
- Weakness: lacks discussion of sensitivity to algorithm parameters (e.g., number of sampled trajectories).
- Weakness: comparisons focus on screenings and delay; lacks a cost analysis including real screening/biopsy costs.
- Qualitative: technically sound, well-presented experiments, but some equations and notation could be made more readable.
- Overall judgment: strong accept with minor revisions—recommend addressing assumptions, algorithm parameter sensitivity, and data/ cost-analysis details.
