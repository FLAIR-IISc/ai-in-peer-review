Summary
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a general framework for designing dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off the cost of testing against the (trajectory-dependent) cost of delayed disease detection. The disease is modeled as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; external information (e.g., self-exams) can trigger unscheduled visits. The decision-maker maintains beliefs over entire state trajectories and diagnosis status, updates via Bayes’ rule at arrivals, and selects the next follow-up time via a recommendation plan. The authors formulate a Bellman equation and show that the value function is convex and piecewise linear in beliefs (as in POMDPs). An exact solution via alpha-vectors is intractable, so they propose a point-based value iteration (PBVI)-style approximation: sample a subset of likely state trajectories and construct a reachable set of beliefs under exploration and clinically motivated policies; do point-based backups to yield an approximate policy. They provide a bound on approximation error in terms of the sampling density of the belief simplex (under strong assumptions) and a robustness result stating local constancy of the optimal action under small model estimation errors. Experiments on a large breast cancer dataset (45k patients) fit a simple two-state Markov disease model with self-exam external information, and report 28–68% reductions in number of screenings at matched expected detection delay vs annual/biennial clinical policies. A synthetic exercise illustrates that convex (quadratic) delay costs produce more aggressive testing.

Relation to prior work
This work is closely related to POMDP-based screening/surveillance (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Erenay et al.) and point-based POMDP methods (Pineau et al.). It differs by accommodating (i) endogenous arrival times driven by external information and scheduled follow-ups, (ii) trajectory-dependent costs (delay since incidence), and (iii) potentially non-Markov disease/test processes by operating over beliefs on trajectories. Compared to Bayesian design approaches for biomarker monitoring (Rizopoulos; Verdinelli et al.), the focus here is on optimizing detection delay vs testing frequency rather than information gain. The evaluation follows the common “learn generative model then simulate” paradigm used in medical screening simulation (CISNET, USPSTF modeling).

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important problem (personalized, dynamic screening) with a unified, flexible model that includes external information and trajectory-based costs.
- Clear connection to POMDP theory; establishes piecewise linearity and provides an implementable PBVI-style algorithm tailored to the high-dimensional belief over trajectories.
- Provides an (albeit stylized) approximation error bound and a robustness result regarding stability of optimal actions to model estimation errors.
- Large-scale application to breast cancer screening, with interpretable personalization (risk- and history-adaptive intervals) and substantial reductions in screening load while maintaining expected detection delay.
- Useful illustration that the choice of delay cost (linear vs. quadratic) materially changes optimal testing regimes.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- The approximation error bound (Proposition 1) relies on strong assumptions: perfect tests (no false positives/negatives), bounded screening intervals, large T, and a specific convex discounted delay form. These assumptions are unrealistic in cancer screening; the bound’s practical relevance is unclear. No finite-sample constants or data-driven diagnostics are provided to relate Ω(B̄) to performance.
- The robustness result (Theorem 1) shows local constancy of actions but does not translate to performance robustness; small changes in model could preserve actions yet alter delays/costs materially. No empirical sensitivity to model misspecification is reported.
- Experimental evaluation is entirely model-based (policy evaluated on a model estimated from observational data with many fixed parameters drawn from literature). There is limited validation of model fit, calibration, or predictive accuracy; no uncertainty quantification (CIs) on the reported gains; and minimal sensitivity analysis (e.g., to self-exam parameters, growth rate, mammography sensitivity/specificity, risk model).
- Computational aspects are underreported: no details on K, B, runtime, convergence criteria, or ablations of the trajectory/belief sampling strategy. The stated complexity bound is coarse and lacks guidance for practical settings.
- Some modeling choices are simplistic (two-state Markov disease, independence assumptions) relative to known complexities of breast cancer progression and test dependencies.

Clarity
- The notation is heavy and sometimes inconsistent (e.g., signs in Bellman equation (3) appear to maximize negative costs; C̃ definition and its usage could be clarified). The role of the diagnosis label l in belief updates should be more explicit. The arrival threshold ỹ is exogenous; clarify whether it is optimized or fixed and how it is set.
- Important proofs and algorithmic details are deferred to the supplement; the main text could benefit from a concise but complete description of Algorithms 1–2 and the belief construction procedure.

Originality
- The idea to work over beliefs on trajectories to accommodate trajectory-dependent costs and endogenous arrivals is interesting. However, the core algorithm is a PBVI variant with tailored belief sampling; the theoretical results largely extend known POMDP properties. Novelty is moderate but credible given the problem setting.

Significance
- If validated, the approach could materially influence personalized screening policy design. The demonstrated reductions in screening burden at matched expected delay are promising, but given model dependence and lack of uncertainty/sensitivity analysis, the practical impact is not yet definitive.

Detailed suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen empirical validation: report model fit (e.g., calibration plots, predictive checks), uncertainty on outcomes, and extensive sensitivity analyses to key parameters (self-exams, test accuracies, growth model, risk model). Include ablations: without external information; with Markov POMDP baseline; with simpler heuristics (risk-stratified annual/biennial).
- Clarify and correct technical exposition: consistent sign conventions in the Bellman equation; explicit derivation of piecewise linearity in the nonstandard setting; precise definition and computation of Ω(B̄); report K, B, coverage diagnostics for belief sampling; runtime and scalability; adherence/noncompliance modeling and its effects.
- Relax or explicitly discuss the gap between theoretical assumptions and practice (imperfect tests, overdiagnosis, biopsy costs). Consider incorporating false negatives/positives into the bound or providing empirical error–performance curves.
- Compare against additional baselines from risk-based screening guidelines (e.g., density- and risk-tailored intervals) and recent ML systems (e.g., ConfidentCare) under a common evaluation framework.
- Discuss ethical/operational issues: patient adherence, varying resource constraints, and clinical interpretability of recommended intervals.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, timely application with a principled sequential decision framework.
- Introduces a flexible model accommodating external info and nonstandard costs.
- Promising empirical results and a clear path to personalization.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical guarantees rely on unrealistic assumptions; limited practical interpretability.
- Evaluation is model-dependent with insufficient validation and sensitivity analysis.
- Algorithmic details and computational characterization are incomplete.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem and approach are compelling, and the preliminary results are promising, but the current submission needs stronger empirical validation, clearer/rigorous exposition, and more realistic theoretical support to meet NeurIPS standards. With the suggested improvements, this work could become a strong contribution.
