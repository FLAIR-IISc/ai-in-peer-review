- Proposes DPSCREEN: a belief-state framework for personalized screening minimizing weighted screening and detection-delay costs with trajectory-dependent delay and endogenous early arrivals.
- Models disease as a finite-state stochastic process with noisy tests and external information triggering arrivals; formulates a belief-state dynamic program with recommendation plans as actions.
- Exact alpha-vector solution is intractable; authors propose a PBVI-style algorithm that samples likely state trajectories and reachable beliefs using random exploration and clinical heuristics.
- Theoretical contributions: value function is piecewise-linear convex; an approximation-error bound tied to belief-sampling density; and a robustness result asserting local constancy of optimal actions to small estimation errors.
- Empirical claim: on a large breast cancer cohort (two-state Markov fit + tumor-growth external info) DPSCREEN reduces expected screenings by 28–68% at matched expected delay.
- Strength: tackles an important, high-impact problem and frames it appropriately in belief-state control.
- Strength: modeling generality to incorporate trajectory-dependent costs and exogenous information-triggered arrivals beyond standard POMDP encodings.
- Strength: pragmatic PBVI adaptation to high-dimensional trajectory beliefs by sampling likely trajectories.
- Strength: provides theoretical properties (PL convexity, error bound, robustness) that extend POMDP results.
- Strength: empirical personalization behavior is intuitive and shows large screening reductions.
- Weakness: formulation claims generality but experiments use a simple two-state Markov model, leaving non-Markov scalability untested.
- Weakness: approximation bound is opaque and non-actionable—depends on an unspecified constant ζ and assumptions like large T and W.
- Weakness: robustness theorem is qualitative and lacks explicit measure-theoretic/continuity conditions or practical guidance.
- Weakness: cost modeling discrepancies—discounting in theory vs. undiscounted empirical optimization and unstated convexity of Pareto frontier for weight tuning.
- Weakness: inconsistent test-accuracy assumptions across theory and experiments (e.g., propositions assume no errors vs. empirical sensitivity/specificity).
- Weakness: external-information model (universal tumor growth + fixed self-exam threshold) is simplistic and likely drives large reported gains without sensitivity analysis.
- Weakness: evaluation lacks uncertainty quantification, posterior predictive checks, calibration, or robustness to model misspecification.
- Weakness: computational complexity claim is hard to interpret; no runtimes, scalability studies, or ablation of key hyperparameters (K, B) are provided.
- Weakness: empirical comparators are weak (annual/biennial); missing stronger baselines like adapted POMDPs, hazard-threshold policies, or information-gain heuristics.
- Clarity: well written overall but key algorithms/proofs deferred to supplement; heavy notation and unclear relation between recommendation plans, actions, and arrivals.
- Originality: novel combination of belief-MDP with trajectory-dependent costs and endogenous arrivals; theoretical extensions are modest over existing POMDP work.
- Significance: potential high impact if validated, but current empirical and theoretical limitations temper significance for a top ML venue.
- Pros for acceptance: important problem, practical PBVI adaptation, promising empirical gains, bridges RL/POMDP and clinical constraints.
- Cons for acceptance: limited empirical validation of general setting, weak/qualitative theory, insufficient baselines and sensitivity analyses, and unproven scalability.
- Suggestions: add sensitivity analyses (growth, self-exam freq/quality, test accuracy), uncertainty propagation, compare stronger baselines, report runtimes and ablations, clarify theoretical assumptions, and provide proof sketches or computable ζ.
