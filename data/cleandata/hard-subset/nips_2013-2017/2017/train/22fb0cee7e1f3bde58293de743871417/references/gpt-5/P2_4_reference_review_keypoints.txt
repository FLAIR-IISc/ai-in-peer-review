- Proposes DPSCREEN: dynamic, personalized screening policies trading test costs vs detection delay using a trajectory-based finite-state disease model with external information arrivals.
- Objective minimizes a weighted sum of discounted screening costs and a general path-dependent delay cost based on incidence-detection lag and incidence time.
- Formulates Bellman equations over beliefs on state trajectories and diagnosis status; proves value function is convex piecewise-linear and exact alpha-vector solution is intractable.
- Proposes a tractable PBVI-style approximation: sample likely trajectories to reduce belief space, perform point-based backups, and output a policy.
- Gives an approximation-error upper bound tied to sampled belief density under strong assumptions and a robustness result that optimal actions are locally constant in model space except on a lower-dimensional set.
- Empirically fits a simple two-state breast-cancer model on a large de-identified dataset (≈45k patients, 84k mammograms) using Gail risk and a tumor-growth self-exam signal.
- DPSCREEN reduces expected tests by 28–68% at matched expected delay (via tuning weight w), with more aggressive policies when delay cost is quadratic.
- Positions work relative to prior POMDP and Bayesian/joint-model screening literature, claiming more general disease/test/external-info processes and path-dependent costs.
- Technical soundness: modeling and belief-state formulation are principled but extremely high-dimensional; PBVI approximation is reasonable and well-motivated.
- Some notational confusion (sign convention in Eq. (3)) that should be clarified to avoid misunderstandings.
- Theoretical support: piecewise-linearity follows POMDP results; approximation and robustness propositions rely on strong assumptions and have opaque definitions and limited finite-sample guarantees.
- Empirical support: case study is compelling and shows personalization benefits, but evaluation tests policies under the same learned model without out-of-sample validation or sensitivity analyses.
- Comparisons are limited—matching expected delay by tuning w shows Pareto dominance but stronger baselines (optimized periodic schedules, POMDP policies) and full frontier plots are missing.
- Clarity: generally readable and organized, but key definitions (Ω(B̄)), sign conventions, and complexity expressions need tightening and explanation.
- Originality: novel combination of trajectory-based disease modeling, path-dependent delay costs, external information processes, and sampled-belief PBVI; use of PBVI and piecewise-linear value functions is standard.
- Significance: potentially high impact with substantial test reductions, but practical adoption requires stronger validation under model uncertainty and scalability analysis.
- Pros: addresses important problem, extends prior models, provides implementable approximation with rationale and some guarantees, and demonstrates large-scale empirical personalization.
- Cons: theoretical guarantees are high-level and assumption-heavy; evaluation lacks robustness checks and external validation; several strong simplifying modeling assumptions are untested.
- Suggestions: clarify Eq. (3) and Ω(B̄), provide proof sketches, add sensitivity/out-of-sample analyses, compare to stronger baselines, show Pareto frontiers, report computational scaling, and release code or detailed pseudo-code.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, novel, and impactful with promising empirical results, but weakened by limited robustness checks and high-level theoretical claims—leaning toward a weak accept pending stronger validation.
