Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a general framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that balance screening cost with delay-in-detection cost. The disease process is modeled as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, allowing for non-Markov dynamics and for external information (e.g., self-exam or comorbidity cues) that can trigger unscheduled visits. The decision variable at each visit is a recommendation plan mapping the observed test outcome to a next screening time. The authors cast the problem as a belief-state dynamic program with trajectory-dependent costs, show that the value function is convex and piecewise linear (as in POMDPs), derive intractability of the exact solution, and propose a point-based value iteration (PBVI)–style approximation that samples likely trajectories and reachable beliefs to construct alpha-vectors. They provide a bound on the approximation error that scales with the sampling density (under strong assumptions) and argue qualitative robustness to model-estimation error via a “locally constant almost everywhere” optimal policy mapping. An illustration using a large de-identified breast cancer dataset (45k patients, 84k mammograms) suggests 28–68% fewer screens than annual/biennial schedules while maintaining expected detection delay; they also show how different delay-cost shapes (linear vs. quadratic) materially shift policy aggressiveness.

This sits at the intersection of POMDPs for medical screening (e.g., Ayer et al. 2012; Maillart et al. 2008; Erenay et al. 2014) and simulation-based policy evaluation adopted by clinical guideline bodies (e.g., MISCAN-COLON). Compared to prior OR/medical works, DPSCREEN aims for broader model class (non-Markovity, trajectory-dependent costs, external arrivals) and data-driven personalization. Methodologically it builds on point-based POMDP methods (e.g., Pineau et al.) and constrained POMDPs (Kim et al.), adapting belief construction to very high-dimensional trajectory beliefs.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The Bellman formulation and piecewise-linear convexity are consistent with POMDP theory. The approximate solution via trajectory-sampled PBVI is reasonable. However, the approximation error bound (Proposition 1) rests on restrictive assumptions (no false positives/negatives, bounded inter-screening interval, large T), and ζ is not operationally specified; Ω(B̄) is not computed in experiments. The robustness result (Theorem 1) is intuitively plausible (argmax is piecewise constant) but is high-level; its practical implications for the approximate policy are limited without quantitative sensitivity bounds.
- Empirical evaluation: The breast cancer case study is valuable, but evaluation is entirely model-based: parameters are estimated from observational data, and policies are then assessed via simulation on that estimated model. This avoids counterfactual issues but risks optimistic conclusions if misspecification exists. There is no off-policy evaluation (e.g., inverse propensity weighting or doubly robust estimators), no held-out calibration/validation check on temporal outcomes (e.g., detection-time calibration), and no uncertainty quantification (CIs) for the reported reductions. Tuning the weight w to match the baseline delay is pragmatic but may inflate apparent gains relative to benchmarks.
- Practical realism: The analysis assumes “no false positives” (and, in the bound, no false negatives) while simultaneously modeling mammogram sensitivity/specificity; this is contradictory and unrealistic. Adherence/compliance and missed screenings are not modeled. External information is simplified to a threshold on a parametric lump-growth model; sensitivity to this modeling choice is not shown.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-structured, with a clear problem statement and connection to related work. However, several crucial algorithmic details are deferred to the supplement (construction of B̄, backup computations, complexity derivations), which makes reproducibility difficult. Some assumptions (e.g., “no false positives” vs. inclusion of imperfect tests) need clarification. The notation around beliefs over full trajectories can be heavy; a sketch of how general trajectory costs reduce to augmented-state POMDPs would help the reader.

Originality:
The formulation with trajectory-dependent delay costs and endogenous arrivals triggered by external information is novel and relevant. The piecewise-linear result is an extension of known POMDP properties. The approximate method is an adaptation of PBVI; the main originality lies in belief set construction via trajectory sampling and policy-informed exploration for an unusually large belief space. Claims that standard POMDP/POSMDP frameworks are inapplicable seem somewhat overstated (many of the issues could be handled by state augmentation and semi-Markov actions), but the proposed unifying framework is nevertheless interesting.

Significance:
Screening policy design is impactful. If the reported reductions in screening burden at comparable delays hold under more robust evaluation, this would be significant for clinical practice. The framework could, in principle, generalize to other diseases and to different delay-cost shapes. However, the current empirical support relies on a single disease, a simplified two-state Markov model (reducing the claimed generality), and model-based evaluation lacking uncertainty quantification. The theoretical bounds, as stated, offer limited practical guidance.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a principled, general framework.
- Incorporates external information and dynamic personalization beyond prior screening POMDPs.
- Sensible PBVI-based approximation with a clear engineering recipe for belief construction.
- Large, realistic case study; results suggest sizable potential reductions in screening.
- Discussion of how disease-specific delay costs alter policy regimes.

Cons for acceptance:
- Approximation error bound and robustness result are high-level and rely on unrealistic assumptions; no empirical validation of bound tightness.
- Evaluation is entirely model-based, with no off-policy causal evaluation, no uncertainty quantification, and limited sensitivity analyses.
- Experimental model is a simple two-state Markov process, undercutting the paper’s emphasis on non-Markov, trajectory-dependent generality.
- Some unrealistic/contradictory assumptions (no false positives/negatives vs. imperfect tests); adherence and operational constraints not modeled.
- Limited comparison to constrained-POMDP baselines and modern off-policy RL for healthcare.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify and reconcile test accuracy assumptions; incorporate imperfect tests into the theory (bounds) and experiments.
- Provide uncertainty estimates (bootstrap or Bayesian posterior predictive CIs) for key metrics and conduct sensitivity analyses to model misspecification (e.g., varying tumor growth parameters, self-exam sensitivity/specificity, adherence).
- Add off-policy evaluation on observational data or at least validate simulation fidelity via held-out temporal outcomes (e.g., time-to-diagnosis calibration).
- Compare against constrained-POMDP methods (e.g., Kim et al.) and simpler heuristics (risk-threshold schedules) under identical models.
- Expand personalization beyond two risk bins; show individualized policies and fairness across subgroups (e.g., age, density).
- Detail belief set construction and backup algorithms in the main text, report computational costs, and release code/data if possible.
- Discuss extension to multi-test action sets and variable costs.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper tackles an important problem with a promising framework, but the current theoretical guarantees are limited by strong assumptions and the empirical support relies on model-based evaluation without uncertainty or robustness analyses. Strengthening evaluation and clarifying assumptions would make this a strong candidate for a future submission.
