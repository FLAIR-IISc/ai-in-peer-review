Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off test costs against delay in disease detection. The disease is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, augmented by an “external information” process (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger unscheduled arrivals. The objective minimizes a weighted sum of discounted screening costs and a general delay cost that depends on the incidence-detection lag and the incidence time. The authors formulate Bellman equations over beliefs defined on state trajectories and diagnosis status, show the value function is convex piecewise-linear, and derive an intractable exact alpha-vector solution. They then propose a tractable point-based approximation: sample likely trajectories to construct a reduced belief space, perform point-based value backups, and output a policy. They give an approximation-error upper bound in terms of the density of sampled beliefs (under strong assumptions) and a robustness result that optimal actions are locally constant in model space except on a lower-dimensional set, implying small estimation errors rarely change optimal actions.

Empirically, they estimate a simple two-state model for breast cancer using a large de-identified dataset (Athena Health Network, ~45k patients, 84k mammograms), use Gail risk as features, include a tumor growth-based self-exam signal model, and compare DPSCREEN to annual/biennial screening. For a fixed expected delay (matched by tuning the weight w), DPSCREEN reduces expected tests by 28–68% (depending on risk group and whether self-exams are modeled). They also illustrate that changing the delay cost from linear to quadratic makes the policy more aggressive, highlighting disease-specific cost modeling. The work is positioned relative to POMDP-based screening (Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Erenay et al.) and Bayesian design/joint models (Rizopoulos), arguing DPSCREEN handles more general disease/test/external-info processes and path-dependent costs.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The modeling is carefully constructed, and the belief-state formulation over trajectory-label pairs is principled albeit very high dimensional. The Bellman equation and the piecewise-linear property are consistent with POMDP theory; converting a minimization to a maximization via negative stage costs is standard, but the sign convention in Eq. (3) could be clarified to avoid confusion. The exact solution is intractable, and the proposed PBVI-style approximation is reasonable and well-motivated.
- Theoretical support: Lemma 1 (piecewise linearity) follows known POMDP results. The approximation error proposition is qualitative and relies on strong assumptions (no false positives/negatives, convex delay, bounded screening interval), with a somewhat opaque definition of Ω(B̄) and no finite-sample rates; nonetheless, it provides a rationale for the belief-sampling strategy. The robustness theorem (policy locally constant in model space off a lower-dimensional set) is plausible given finite alpha sets, but again proof details are in the supplement.
- Empirical support: The case study is compelling and demonstrates personalization and the benefit-cost trade-off. However, the evaluation uses a model learned from observational data and then evaluates policies under that same model. There is no out-of-sample validation, posterior predictive checks, or sensitivity analysis to key assumptions (e.g., sensitivity/specificity, self-exam parameters, growth rate), limiting claims of real-world performance. The comparison to clinical policies is via matched expected delay by tuning w, effectively showing that DPSCREEN sits on or dominates a Pareto frontier; this is useful but should be accompanied by full frontier plots and stronger baselines (e.g., optimized periodic schedules, heuristic risk-based intervals, or existing POMDP policies instantiated on the same model).

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized and readable, with clear motivation and a consistent narrative from model to algorithm to application. Important algorithmic details and proofs are deferred to the supplement; readers would benefit from tighter definitions in the main text (e.g., precise definition of Ω(B̄), clarifying the sign convention in Eq. (3), and making explicit when “no false positives” applies to the final diagnostic pathway vs. intermediate tests). The complexity expression O(T(B)2 T2 K|Y||Z|) could be unpacked, as the double T factors and the role of B warrant explanation.

Originality
- The combination of features—general trajectory-based disease model with path-dependent delay costs, an external information process that endogenizes arrival, and a PBVI variant driven by sampled trajectory beliefs—goes beyond prior POMDP screening formulations that assume Markov disease/test processes and fixed decision epochs. While the use of PBVI and piecewise-linear value functions is standard, applying them to a belief space over trajectories plus diagnosis status is novel. The robustness/approximation statements are helpful, albeit high level. The empirical application at scale is a useful contribution to the health ML literature.

Significance
- Screening policy design is impactful. If validated, the reported 28–68% reduction in expected screenings at matched delays is significant. The framework also clarifies how disease-specific delay costs change optimal screening behavior. Practitioners could build on this to develop adaptive screening tools. That said, practical adoption requires stronger validation under model uncertainty and more realistic sensing assumptions, and the computational approach may face scaling challenges as T or model complexity grows.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, societally relevant problem with a principled sequential decision framework.
- Formulates a more general model than prior screening POMDPs (external info, path-dependent costs).
- Provides an implementable approximation with a rationale and some guarantees.
- Empirical study on a large real-world dataset; clear personalization effects; strong reductions in screening counts at matched delay.
- Useful insight that disease-specific delay costs materially affect policy regimes.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical guarantees are high-level and rely on restrictive assumptions; no quantitative convergence rates or finite-sample guidance.
- Evaluation relies on a learned model and tests policies within that model; limited robustness checks, no external validation, and few head-to-head comparisons with optimized baselines.
- Some modeling assumptions are strong/simplifying (two-state disease, diagnosing on biopsy with “no false positives,” fixed sensitivity/specificity, specific self-exam model); their impact is not assessed.
- Notational/definition clarity issues (Eq. (3) sign, Ω(B̄) definition, complexity expression).

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Eq. (3) (min vs. max, sign of stage cost) and formalize Ω(B̄) with precise domain and constants; provide a sketch of the proof and discuss the strength of assumptions.
- Add sensitivity analyses to test robustness to sensitivity/specificity, self-exam parameters, tumor growth rate, and misspecified disease dynamics; include out-of-sample evaluation or posterior predictive checks.
- Compare against stronger baselines: optimized periodic schedules matched to the same learned model, existing POMDP-based policies on the same data/model, and ablations of the belief sampling strategy.
- Provide Pareto frontiers (E[N] vs E[Δ]) for all methods to visualize dominance; report computational costs and scaling with K, B, and T.
- Release code or detailed pseudo-code and parameter estimates sufficient for reproduction.

Overall assessment
The paper meets the NeurIPS criteria reasonably well: it is technically sound and novel enough for the venue, clearly written with a meaningful application, and potentially impactful. The main weaknesses are the limited empirical robustness and the high-level nature of theoretical guarantees. With stronger validation and clearer exposition of the theory, this would be a strong paper. As is, I lean toward a weak accept for its principled formulation and promising empirical evidence on an important problem.
