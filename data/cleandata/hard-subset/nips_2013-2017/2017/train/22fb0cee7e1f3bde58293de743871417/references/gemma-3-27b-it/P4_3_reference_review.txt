This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a dynamic personalized screening policy framework aiming to balance the benefits and costs of screening across diverse diseases and patient characteristics. The core idea is to develop a policy that considers disease type, patient features, and screening history to optimize testing frequency. The approach addresses challenges in existing screening models by allowing for non-Markovian disease progression, incorporating external information, handling patient arrival dynamics, and providing computational tractability via approximation techniques. The authors demonstrate the framework's effectiveness using breast cancer data, showing potential reductions in screening frequency while maintaining detection delays.

The paper is well-motivated and tackles a significant problem in healthcare. The generalization of existing screening models to non-Markovian processes and inclusion of external information represent valuable contributions. The development of an approximately optimal policy and bounding the approximation error are crucial for practical applicability. The application to breast cancer with a real-world dataset lends credibility. The discussion of the impact of different cost functions (linear vs. quadratic) highlights an important consideration often overlooked. The comparison to current clinical practice and the demonstration of reduced screening frequency are compelling.

However, several weaknesses need addressing. The model complexity, while a strength in terms of generality, makes it difficult to fully grasp the assumptions and limitations without significant effort. The notation is dense and requires careful reading. While the authors acknowledge computational challenges, the practical feasibility of applying DPSCREEN to very high-dimensional patient datasets isn't fully addressed. The reliance on specific parameters (e.g., ι = 0.9 for tumor growth) is not thoroughly justified and may limit the generalizability of the findings. The analysis could be strengthened by including sensitivity analyses to assess the impact of varying these parameters. While the approximation error bound is provided, a more intuitive understanding of its implications for different scenarios would be beneficial. The description of the observational data and estimation procedures (Appendix G) would benefit from more detail. Further, the connection to the broader literature on sequential decision making under uncertainty, particularly beyond POMDPs, could be strengthened. The presentation of results in tables 1 and 2 could be made clearer with more contextual information.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The framework extends existing screening models to handle more realistic and complex scenarios, including non-Markovian disease progression and external information.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The development of an approximately optimal policy with error bounds provides a rigorous foundation for the approach.
*   **Potential Impact:** The demonstration of reduced screening frequency while maintaining detection delays suggests a significant potential for improving healthcare efficiency and patient outcomes.
*   **Real-world application:** Applying the framework to breast cancer data enhances its practical relevance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Complexity:** The model's complexity and dense notation may hinder understanding and adoption.
*   **Practical feasibility:** The scalability of the approach to very large datasets is not fully addressed.
*   **Parameter sensitivity:** The reliance on specific parameter values requires further justification and sensitivity analysis.
*   **Limited comparison:** The comparison to other personalized screening approaches could be more extensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant advancement in personalized screening policy development. While the complexity of the model and some limitations in practical feasibility exist, the novelty, technical soundness, and potential impact of the work outweigh these concerns. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the challenges in this area and propose a well-motivated and rigorously developed solution. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns regarding clarity, parameter sensitivity, and scalability in a revised version. A more thorough discussion on the computational resources needed for real-world deployment would also be beneficial.
