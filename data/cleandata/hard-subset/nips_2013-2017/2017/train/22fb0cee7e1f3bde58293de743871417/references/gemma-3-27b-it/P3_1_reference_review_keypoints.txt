- Novel framework (DPSCREEN) for personalized screening that balances test frequency vs. detection delay and handles non‑Markov disease progression.  
- Incorporates external information (e.g., patient self‑exams) and addresses computational intractability of optimal policies.  
- Uses a point‑based value iteration (PBVI) with a novel belief‑space construction and provides approximation error bounds.  
- Empirical validation on a breast cancer dataset shows large reductions in screening frequency with comparable detection delays.  
- Highlights the critical role of cost‑function choice in screening policy design.  
- Well‑written, logically organized paper with effective visual illustration of personalization.  
- Includes robustness analysis addressing the impact of parameter estimation error.  
- Framework is very general and can accommodate a wide range of disease models and external signals.  
- Tackles computational tractability with a practical PBVI approach tailored to the problem.  
- Theoretical guarantees (approximation bounds) add rigor often missing in similar work.  
- Complexity of implementation appears substantial; scalability to very high‑dimensional state spaces is a concern.  
- Relies on modeling assumptions (e.g., conditional independence of test results, discrete time) without deep sensitivity analysis.  
- Empirical dataset is limited (ages 60–65), raising questions about generalizability to other demographics and diseases.  
- Insufficient detail on parameter estimation (MCMC methods, convergence diagnostics) and justification for chosen hyperparameters (ι and ỹ).  
- Lacks comparison to alternative POMDP approximation methods (e.g., Monte Carlo Tree Search) as benchmarks.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a critical problem with a novel, general, theoretically sound, and empirically supported approach.  
- Arguments against acceptance: computational/scalability concerns, reliance on assumptions, and limited empirical scope; needs more implementation/comparison details.  
- Overall assessment: significant contribution; recommend accept with minor revisions.  
- Suggested revisions: report runtime and scalability strategies, expand discussion of model assumptions and sensitivity, compare to other POMDP approximation techniques, give detailed parameter estimation and convergence diagnostics, and discuss dataset limitations and broader applicability.
