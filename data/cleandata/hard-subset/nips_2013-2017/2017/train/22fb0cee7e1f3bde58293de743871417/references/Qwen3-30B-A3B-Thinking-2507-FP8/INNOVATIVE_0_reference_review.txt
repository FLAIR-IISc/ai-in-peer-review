

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

This paper introduces a novel framework for dynamic personalized screening policies that significantly extends beyond current approaches in medical decision-making. The authors address a critical gap in the literature by developing a method that can handle non-stationary disease processes, incorporate external information (like patient self-examinations), and personalize screening based on both static patient features and dynamic history. Unlike previous works that restrict to Markov disease processes and stationary test outcome models [7,9], the authors model the disease as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, making their approach applicable to a wide range of diseases.

The core contribution is the DPSCREEN algorithm, which uses a point-based value iteration approach to construct approximately optimal screening policies. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the approximation error (Proposition 1), which is particularly valuable given the computational intractability of the exact solution. The paper demonstrates significant practical improvements over current clinical practices: for breast cancer screening, their method reduces the number of screenings by 28-68% while maintaining the same expected delay in disease detection.

The novelty of this work is substantial. While previous works like [7,9] developed personalized screening models based on POMDPs, they imposed restrictive Markov assumptions that don't hold for many diseases. The authors' framework is the first to handle non-stationary disease processes and external information that can trigger early patient visits. This represents a significant conceptual leap beyond the state of the art in medical decision theory.

The paper's significance is clear. Screening represents a major healthcare cost with significant patient burden, and current clinical policies are largely one-size-fits-all. The authors demonstrate that personalized screening can dramatically reduce unnecessary screenings without compromising detection quality. Their synthetic exercise across different disease models (linear vs. quadratic delay costs) further highlights how the cost structure fundamentally shapes optimal screening policies.

The experimental validation is strong for the problem domain, using a real-world breast cancer dataset of 45,000 patients. The comparison against current clinical policies (annual screening) is well-motivated and shows compelling results. The ablation study on the impact of self-examinations (which are common but often ignored in screening models) provides valuable practical insights.

Some minor weaknesses exist: the paper could better clarify why their approximation is necessary beyond just computational tractability (since the authors acknowledge the exact solution is intractable). The theoretical analysis of the approximation error (Proposition 1) is somewhat abstract and could be strengthened with more concrete examples. The clinical relevance could also be enhanced by discussing the potential barriers to implementation in real-world healthcare systems.

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold conceptual advances, I believe these minor weaknesses are outweighed by the paper's significant contributions. The authors have developed a genuinely new framework that addresses a fundamental limitation in current medical decision-making approaches. Their method is both theoretically sound and practically impactful, with clear potential for application across multiple diseases beyond breast cancer.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with sufficient technical detail to be reproducible. The related work section effectively positions their contribution against existing approaches, highlighting the specific gaps their work fills.

I believe this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field. It introduces a novel conceptual framework that addresses a long-standing limitation in personalized screening models, provides rigorous theoretical foundations, and demonstrates significant practical improvements over current clinical practices. The work represents a bold step forward in applying decision-theoretic methods to personalized healthcare, and it opens up new research directions for handling complex, dynamic medical decision problems.

The paper is clearly within NIPS scope as it develops a novel algorithmic approach to a significant problem using principled machine learning and decision theory methods. The work bridges machine learning, operations research, and medical decision-making in a way that would be of interest to the NIPS community.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Strengths:**
- Novel framework that handles non-stationary disease processes and external information
- Theoretical guarantees on approximation error
- Strong empirical results showing significant practical improvements
- Clear demonstration of how the method differs from prior work
- Applicable to multiple disease domains beyond breast cancer

**Weaknesses:**
- Slightly abstract approximation error analysis
- Limited discussion of implementation challenges in real healthcare systems
- Could better motivate why approximation is necessary beyond computational tractability

This work represents a significant advance that would be of great interest to the NIPS community, with clear potential for impact in healthcare decision-making. I believe it should be accepted.
