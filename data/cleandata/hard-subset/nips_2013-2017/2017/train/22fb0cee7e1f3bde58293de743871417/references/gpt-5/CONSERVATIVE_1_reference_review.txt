Summary
The paper formulates dynamic, personalized screening as a partially observable decision problem with exogenous information and endogenously determined follow-up times. The state is an unobserved disease trajectory with an absorbing disease state; observations come from clinical tests and external signals (e.g., self-exams). The objective trades off discounted screening cost and delay cost. The authors show the value function is convex, piecewise linear over beliefs, and propose an approximate solution via a PBVI-style algorithm that samples likely state trajectories and reachable beliefs (DPSCREEN). They provide a high-level approximation bound in terms of a belief-sampling density metric and a robustness result about local constancy of the optimal action w.r.t. small model estimation errors. Empirically, they fit a simple two-state breast cancer model on a large observational dataset, then simulate policy performance, reporting large reductions in expected number of screens versus clinical annual/biennial policies at matched expected detection delays. They also show qualitatively different policies under linear vs. quadratic delay costs.

Strengths
- Ambitious and timely formulation aiming for individualized, history-dependent screening policies that incorporate external arrivals.
- Sound use of POMDP concepts (belief-state formulation, piecewise linearity of value) and an approximation scheme inspired by PBVI adapted to high-dimensional belief supports.
- Addresses an important application area with potentially significant societal impact; aligns with decision-making under uncertainty.
- Attempts to discuss approximation and estimation error, and explores disease-dependent cost structures.
- Large dataset and a clear policy objective (reduce screens while keeping delay), with stratification by risk group.

Weaknesses
Technical rigor and assumptions:
- Several key theoretical claims rest on unrealistic or restrictive assumptions. The approximation bound assumes no false positives or false negatives and a bounded inter-screening window; these do not hold in practice for mammography/biopsy. The constant ζ in Ω(B̄) is undefined and the bound is not actionable.
- The “robustness” Theorem 1 (local constancy of optimal actions off measure-zero boundaries) is a generic property of argmax problems under continuity and uniqueness, offering little insight into model misspecification impact in this setting. No quantitative sensitivity bounds are provided.
- The paper claims to handle very general non-Markov disease and observation processes, yet the empirical model reverts to a two-state Markov process with fixed literature-based sensitivities/specificities. There is a mismatch between the general theory and the instantiated model.

Empirical validation:
- The evaluation relies entirely on a learned generative model to simulate counterfactuals. There is no off-policy evaluation (e.g., inverse propensity weighting, doubly robust estimators) or sensitivity analysis to model misspecification. Given known fragility of model-based policy evaluation, the large reported gains are not convincing without robustness checks.
- The external information (self-exam) process is hand-specified via a growth model and calibrated from literature rather than the dataset; its validity and impact are not empirically validated.
- Baselines are limited to annual and biennial screening. There is no comparison to prior personalized decision methods (e.g., POMDP-based policies in Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Erenay et al., ConfidentCare), nor to simpler heuristics (e.g., myopic, risk-threshold policies, QMDP). Consequently, it is unclear that DPSCREEN advances the state of the art on this application.
- Important practical aspects are abstracted away: equal cost for mammogram vs. biopsy; ignoring harms of false positives/recalls and overdiagnosis; assuming “no false positives” in parts of the theory while acknowledging positive mammograms with negative biopsies in the model.
- No runtime or scalability results are reported, despite a potentially heavy algorithm (belief dimension ∼ |S|^T, complexity expression unclear and likely mis-specified). No ablations on K, |B̄|, or action space constraints.

Clarity and reproducibility:
- Key algorithms, proofs, and figures/tables are deferred to the supplement; some placeholders/typos remain (e.g., “st Conference …”). The complexity expression O(T(B)^2 T^2 K|Y||Z|) is not explained. Numerical results (Tables/Figures) are not shown in the main text; parameter estimation details and code availability are missing.

Originality
- The high-level mapping—using a POMDP/SMDP with waiting time as an action and exogenous observations—is not fundamentally new; exogenous arrivals and time between decisions have been treated in POSMDPs/SMDPs. The PBVI-based approximation with sampled belief supports is standard. The novelty claim rests on allowing trajectory-dependent delay costs and external signals; however, this is not empirically demonstrated against strong prior personalized screening frameworks, and parts of the claimed generality are not exercised in experiments.

Significance
- The problem is important, and if validated rigorously, the approach could influence screening policy design. However, given the reliance on a learned model with limited calibration, lack of robust off-policy evaluation, and constrained baselines, the current evidence does not convincingly demonstrate superiority or practical readiness.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide rigorous off-policy evaluation on the observational cohort (e.g., DR/OPE methods), plus extensive sensitivity analyses to model misspecification (transition hazards, test accuracy, self-exam parameters, delay cost form).
- Relax the no-FP/FN assumption in the theory and deliver bounds with interpretable constants, or position the bound as heuristic and focus on empirical validation.
- Report detailed runtime/scalability, ablation over K and |B̄|, and the effect of action space constraints.
- Calibrate mammogram/biopsy test characteristics from the data; include harms and costs beyond test count (false positives, recalls, overdiagnosis).
- Compare against prior POMDP-based screening policies and strong heuristic baselines on the same learned model.
- Improve clarity and self-containedness of the main paper; include key tables/figures and algorithmic details.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an impactful problem with a principled decision-theoretic framework.
- Incorporates external information and personalized, dynamic scheduling.
- Potentially large practical benefits if validated.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical contributions are incremental and rely on unrealistic assumptions; the key robustness result is weak.
- Empirical evaluation is model-based without robust OPE/sensitivity; baselines are limited; practical costs/harms are simplified.
- Mismatch between claimed generality and the instantiated experimental model; lack of runtime/scaling evidence.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues.

Recommendation
Given the current methodological gaps and insufficiently rigorous empirical validation against established baselines, I cannot recommend acceptance at NIPS. The work would benefit from stronger theoretical guarantees under realistic assumptions and substantially more robust empirical evaluation.
