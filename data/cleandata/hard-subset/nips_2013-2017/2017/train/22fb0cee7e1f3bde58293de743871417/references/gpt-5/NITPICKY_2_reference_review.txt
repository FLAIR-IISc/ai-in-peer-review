Summary
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a personalized screening framework that balances testing costs with delay-in-detection costs. Time is discrete and finite; disease is a hidden finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; external information (e.g., self-exam signals) influences unscheduled arrivals; and screening actions set contingent follow-up times via a recommendation plan. The authors derive a Bellman equation over beliefs defined on trajectory-label pairs, show the value function is convex piecewise-linear (as in POMDPs), and outline an intractable exact alpha-vector solution. They propose a PBVI-style approximation that (i) samples a reduced set of trajectories and (ii) builds a reachable belief set via random exploration and clinical policies, then computes alpha-vectors on these points. They claim an approximation error bound based on the sampling density of the belief simplex and a robustness result stating that the optimal action is locally constant in model space outside a measure-zero set. Experiments on a de-identified breast cancer dataset (45k patients, 84k mammograms) suggest large reductions in expected number of screenings (28–68%) at comparable expected delay vs annual/biennial screening. They also show that convex delay costs (quadratic) yield more aggressive testing.

Strengths
- Ambitious and general formulation that explicitly models endogenous arrivals via external information and allows non-Markov disease processes (via beliefs over trajectories).
- Correctly identifies that delay costs depend on state trajectories, not only current state, and that standard POSMDP assumptions do not apply directly.
- The piecewise-linear convexity of the value function is well-aligned with POMDP theory, enabling alpha-vector approximations.
- The idea to sample likely trajectories and reachable beliefs to tame the curse of dimensionality is sensible in this domain.
- Real clinical dataset and an attempt to quantify reductions in screening burden while maintaining expected delay.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns

Quality
- Modeling inconsistencies:
  - The general setup assumes finite Y, yet the breast cancer model uses a continuous self-exam signal Y(t)=g(t)+noise. It is unclear whether Y is discretized, and how this affects Pr(z,y,τ̃|b,τ,x) and the Bayes updates.
  - Early, the paper assumes “no false positives” upon diagnosis (z∈Z+). Later, the approximation error analysis assumes no false positives and no false negatives, which is unrealistic for mammography/biopsy and contradicts the experimental model that includes z=2 (biopsy negative). Please reconcile detection events (is Z+={3} only?) and clarify whether perfect detection is assumed in theory and violated in experiments.
  - Beliefs are defined over ST×{0,1}. Including l (diagnosis status) doubles dimension but is unnecessary if diagnosis terminates episodes; this complicates notation without clear benefit.
  - The delay cost is later assumed to be C(td−tD,tD)=c(td−tD)δtD; earlier delay cost had no discounting, only screening costs were discounted. This change is introduced only in the approximation bound section; justify/clarify.

- Algorithmic novelty: The approximation is essentially PBVI on sampled beliefs. There is no comparison to state-of-the-art POMDP solvers (SARSOP, HSVI2, PERSEUS, POMCP), nor discussion of why trajectory-based sampling is preferable in this setting. The claimed complexity O(T(B)2T2K|Y||Z|) is opaque (e.g., two T factors). Without runtimes, K, |B̄[t]|, or parallelization details, practicality is hard to judge.

- Theoretical results:
  - Lemma 1 follows standard POMDP arguments; fine. Proposition 1 (approximation error ≤ Ω(B̄)) is underspecified: Ω uses ζ as “maximum expected loss per time slot” without a formula; the “max_B min_b∈B̄[t] ||b−b′||1” is ambiguous (what is B? all beliefs or reachable ones?), and the norm Lipschitz constant depends on costs, observation models, and horizon. As stated, the bound is hard to interpret or use.
  - The robustness Theorem 1 is nonrigorous: it assumes uniqueness of the optimal solution for every model and then asserts local constancy outside a lower-dimensional set, but does not specify topology, measure, or a distribution over models (the phrase “with probability 1” is ill-defined here). A proof sketch or precise conditions are needed.

- Empirics:
  - The evaluation uses a two-state Markov disease model, undermining the claimed generality. No ablations to show benefit from handling external information vs standard POMDPs.
  - Key experimental details are missing: horizon T, time discretization, K (number of trajectories), sizes of B̄[t], run time, convergence/stopping criteria, chosen w per group, and sensitivity to w. It is unclear how often “unscheduled” arrivals occur under the self-exam process in the learned model.
  - No uncertainty quantification (confidence intervals), calibration checks, or sensitivity analyses (e.g., to self-exam sensitivity/specificity, noise σ, threshold ỹ). The large reported savings (up to 68%) may be sensitive to modeling assumptions.
  - No comparisons to learned personalized policies from the literature (Ayer et al., Maillart et al., ConfidentCare), or to modern POMDP methods. Baselines are limited to annual/biennial heuristics.

Clarity
- The exposition mixes general and application-specific assumptions without clear demarcation. The definition of Pr(z,y,τ̃|b,τ,x) and the belief update across endogenous arrival times is central but not formalized in the main text.
- Several notational slips (e.g., “st Conference…”, footnote dangling “1”, duplicate/incomplete references), and inconsistent citation formatting impede readability.
- Figures/tables referenced (Fig. 1–2, Tables 1–2) are not shown; the reader cannot verify claims.

Originality
- The core novelty is limited: using trajectory sampling to reduce an ST belief space for PBVI is a natural extension of point-based methods. Claims of being the “first” to develop personalized policies in a very general setting seem overstated relative to extensive POMDP literature (SARSOP, POMCP) and health screening POMDP works.
- Endogenous arrivals via external information are interesting, but the formulation is essentially a POMDP with action-dependent inter-visit times; related POSMDP/semi-Markov POMDP literature could be cited and contrasted more precisely.

Significance
- The problem is important; personalization can meaningfully reduce screening burden. However, given methodological issues, lack of comparison to strong baselines, and insufficient experimental detail, the current evidence does not convincingly establish state-of-the-art performance or robustness.

Pros
- Addresses a clinically relevant, high-impact decision-making problem.
- Unifies screening, delay costs, and exogenous information in a single framework.
- Leverages POMDP structure (piecewise-linear convexity) to justify approximation.
- Real dataset; attempts to personalize by risk group and dynamic history.

Cons
- Numerous modeling inconsistencies and underspecified components (Y’s domain, detection assumptions, cost/discounters).
- Limited algorithmic novelty; missing comparisons to modern POMDP solvers and prior personalized screening methods.
- Theoretical bounds and robustness claims are vague, with undefined constants/sets.
- Experimental section lacks critical details, uncertainty quantification, and sensitivity analyses.
- Significant clarity and formatting issues; references are incomplete/inconsistent.

Suggestions for improvement
- Precisely formalize the arrival process, observation model, and belief updates; reconcile finite vs continuous Y.
- Clarify detection assumptions; if Z+={3}, state so; avoid perfect detection assumptions in bounds if experiments violate them.
- Provide explicit expressions for ζ and Ω(B̄); specify the belief sets over which distances are computed; add a proof sketch.
- Compare against SARSOP/HSVI2/PERSEUS/POMCP and prior screening POMDPs; report runtimes and scalability.
- Add sensitivity analyses (self-exam parameters, test accuracies, delay cost forms) and CIs; report dataset prevalence and censoring handling.
- Clean up references and formatting; provide sufficient implementation details (T, discretization, K, |B̄[t]|, w tuning).

Recommendation: Reject (promising direction but not yet ready for NeurIPS due to technical and experimental shortcomings).

Confidence: High.
