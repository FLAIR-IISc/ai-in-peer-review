This paper tackles the crucial problem of personalized screening policies, moving beyond traditional one-size-fits-all approaches. The core idea – to dynamically adjust screening frequency based on patient features, screening history, and disease progression – is both important and challenging. The authors successfully formulate this as a dynamic programming problem and propose a computationally tractable approximation, DPSCREEN, addressing key difficulties like non-Markovian disease processes and external information. The work is well-motivated, clearly articulates the challenges, and demonstrates the potential of personalized screening through a breast cancer example.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Problem:** Personalized screening is a clinically relevant and impactful area. The paper addresses a critical need for optimizing screening strategies.
* **General Framework:** The model is remarkably general, accommodating various disease dynamics (survival models, first hitting times) and external information. This broad applicability is a major strength.
* **Computational Tractability:**  The development of the PBVI-based approximation (DPSCREEN) is a key contribution, making the problem solvable in practice. The error bound provides some theoretical guarantees.
* **Empirical Validation:** The application to a real-world breast cancer dataset and comparison with existing clinical policies provides strong evidence of the effectiveness of DPSCREEN, showing substantial reductions in screening frequency without compromising detection delay.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, despite the technical complexity. The inclusion of a graphical model is helpful.
* **Robustness Analysis:** The discussion of estimation error and its impact on the policy is a valuable addition, increasing confidence in the practical viability of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** While the model is general, it relies on several assumptions (e.g., accurate test results, independence of test results given state and features) which may not hold in reality. The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions deserves further investigation.
* **Complexity of Implementation:**  While the algorithm is tractable, implementing it and estimating the model parameters in practice could be computationally demanding and require substantial expertise.
* **Model Estimation Details:** While the paper mentions using the Gail model and MCMC for parameter estimation, the details are somewhat limited. A more thorough discussion of the model fitting process, including validation and sensitivity analysis, would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are primarily focused on breast cancer. While insightful, extending the evaluation to other diseases and cost structures would strengthen the generalizability claims. The synthetic exercise highlights this need.
* **External Information Simplification:** The modeling of external information (e.g., self-exams) appears somewhat simplistic. The assumption of independence given state trajectory and features might be too strong.
* **Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The related work section could be expanded to include more recent advances in personalized medicine and dynamic treatment regimes, particularly in areas beyond screening. Works leveraging causal inference for treatment personalization, for example, could be discussed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a highly significant and clinically relevant problem.
* The proposed framework is novel, general, and theoretically sound.
* The computational approach is practical and demonstrates substantial improvements over existing methods.
* The empirical results are compelling and provide strong evidence of the effectiveness of DPSCREEN.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The assumptions underlying the model may be overly restrictive.
* The implementation and parameter estimation could be challenging in real-world scenarios.
* The scope of the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of personalized screening. While there are some weaknesses, the strengths of the work outweigh them. The paper is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. The proposed framework has the potential to improve healthcare outcomes by optimizing screening strategies.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting the paper, contingent on the authors addressing the following minor revisions:

1. **Sensitivity Analysis:**  Perform a sensitivity analysis to assess the robustness of the results to violations of key assumptions (e.g., test accuracy, independence assumptions).
2. **Model Estimation Details:** Provide a more detailed description of the model estimation process, including validation techniques and a discussion of potential biases.
3. **Expand Related Work:** Expand the related work section to include more recent advances in personalized medicine and dynamic treatment regimes.
4. **Address Simplification of External Information:** Acknowledge the limitations of the simplified modeling of external information and discuss potential avenues for improvement.
5. **Consider Expanding Evaluation:** Consider briefly discussing potential extensions to other diseases or including a small-scale evaluation on a different dataset.
