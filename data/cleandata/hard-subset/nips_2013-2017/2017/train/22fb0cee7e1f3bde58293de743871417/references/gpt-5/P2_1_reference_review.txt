Summary
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a general framework to compute dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off test burden against delay in disease detection. The key modeling advances are: (i) a very general disease process (finite state with an absorbing disease state) that need not be Markov; (ii) explicit incorporation of exogenous “external information” that can trigger unscheduled arrivals; (iii) trajectory-dependent delay costs; and (iv) action-dependent inter-visit times. The authors formulate a belief-state dynamic program where beliefs are distributions over entire state trajectories and a diagnosis flag, show the value function is convex and piecewise-linear in beliefs (extending classic POMDP results), and derive an exact alpha-vector recursion that is intractable. They then propose a PBVI-style approximation that samples likely physiological trajectories and reachable beliefs, give a complexity discussion, and prove a bound showing the approximation error scales with a measure of belief-space sampling density. A robustness result argues that optimal policies are locally constant in model parameters (so small estimation error yields the same actions), and that the approximate policy converges as sampling density increases. Empirically, they estimate a breast cancer model from a large dataset, and show sizable reductions (28–68%) in expected screenings while matching the expected delay of annual or biennial guidelines. They also examine the impact of different delay-cost shapes.

Relation to prior work
Compared to OR/POMDP-based screening works (Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Erenay et al.), DPSCREEN relaxes Markov/stationarity assumptions, supports external signals and trajectory-dependent costs, and does not restrict to fixed decision epochs. Compared to information-gain and joint-modeling approaches (Rizopoulos et al.), the objective here directly optimizes a screening–delay tradeoff under more general dynamics. The piecewise-linear/alpha-vector machinery borrows from POMDPs, but the belief space over trajectories and endogenous arrivals depart from standard formulations. The robustness argument is in the spirit of Berge’s maximum theorem.

Quality
- Theory: The extension of the piecewise-linear property to the trajectory-belief setting is plausible; an outline in the main text would help. The PBVI variant and the sampling-density error bound are reasonable but appear high-level; the bound assumes no false positives/negatives, convex delay cost, bounded screening interval, and large horizon—assumptions that are restrictive and not aligned with the empirical setup (where test sensitivity/specificity are finite). The local constancy theorem is intuitive but stated abstractly; citing standard parametric continuity results would strengthen it.
- Algorithms: The belief construction via sampling likely trajectories and reachable beliefs is sensible; however, the action space and practical pruning strategies are not fully specified in the main text. Complexity is discussed but without empirical runtime or scalability evidence.
- Empirics: The dataset is substantial, and the evaluation protocol (fit a model, then simulate counterfactual policies) is standard for screening. The reported reductions in screening frequency at matched expected delay are compelling. However, many experimental details (tables, figures, hyperparameters K/B/T/W, code, runtime) are in the supplement; ablations and sensitivity to model misspecification are limited. External information is modeled via a tumor growth law; sensitivity to those parameters is only briefly mentioned.

Clarity
The paper is generally well motivated and organized, but is notation-heavy and pushes most derivations and algorithmic details to the supplement. A clearer separation of assumptions used for theory vs. those used in experiments would avoid confusion. Some references are incomplete and prose could be tightened.

Originality
The setting combining trajectory-dependent costs, endogenous arrivals from external signals, and policy-dependent inter-visit times is novel relative to classic POMDP screening. The PBVI variant over sampled trajectory-belief subsets is a reasonable adaptation. The approximation/robustness statements are useful but not deeply novel.

Significance
Personalized screening is important and timely. If validated further, DPSCREEN could influence clinical decision support by systematically exploiting patient features and dynamic histories. The empirical gains reported are sizable, though derived under model-based evaluation. The framework is potentially reusable beyond breast cancer.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, high-impact application with a principled decision-theoretic framework.
- Generalizes prior screening models to handle external information and trajectory-dependent delay costs.
- Provides theoretical structure (piecewise linearity), an implementable approximation, and an error bound.
- Demonstrates large practical gains on a substantial real dataset; explores effect of different delay costs.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical assumptions (e.g., no FP/FN) diverge from the empirical setting, limiting the practical relevance of the bounds.
- Many critical details and results are deferred to the supplement; limited transparency on computational performance and implementation choices.
- Evaluation is purely model-based; no off-policy evaluation or sensitivity analyses to model misspecification, nor comparisons to stronger risk-based heuristic baselines beyond annual/biennial.
- Clarity and referencing issues (incomplete citations, some typos).

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Clarify and reconcile the assumptions used in Proposition 1 with the empirical mammography model that includes imperfect sensitivity/specificity.
- Provide proof sketches or clear citations for Lemma 1 and Theorem 1 (e.g., Berge’s maximum theorem) in the main text.
- Report runtime, number of belief points, K, B, T, W, and pruning strategies; include a scalability experiment.
- Add sensitivity analyses to model misspecification and to external-information parameters; ablate the contributions of personalization by features vs. history vs. external info.
- Compare against stronger adaptive baselines (e.g., density/risk-guideline-based intervals, learned heuristics) and report subgroup analyses.
- Release code or pseudocode sufficient for reproduction.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper tackles an important problem with a thoughtful generalization of POMDP methods, provides some theoretical grounding, and shows promising empirical gains. Strengthening the connection between assumptions and practice, improving clarity, and expanding evaluation would solidify the contribution.

Reviewer confidence: moderate.
