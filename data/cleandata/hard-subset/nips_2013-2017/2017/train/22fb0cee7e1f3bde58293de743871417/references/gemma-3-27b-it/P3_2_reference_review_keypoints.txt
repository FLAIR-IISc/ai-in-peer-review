- Proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamically personalized screening that adapts frequency based on patient features, history, and evolving disease progression.  
- Handles non-Markovian disease processes, delayed costs, external information, and computational tractability challenges.  
- Core contribution: a computationally effective PBVI-based procedure to approximate optimal policies with provable approximation error bounds.  
- Provides robustness analysis to estimation error (Theorem 1).  
- Demonstrates substantial reductions in screening frequency with comparable detection delays on a breast cancer dataset.  
- Synthetic experiments illustrate the effects of different cost structures.  
- Extends prior screening optimization work by supporting general stochastic processes beyond Markovian assumptions.  
- Produces optimized policies (vs. simulation-only evaluation) and relates to controlled sensing and POMDP literature while highlighting key differences.  
- Strength: very general modeling framework covering dynamic survival and first-hitting time models and multiple information sources.  
- Strength: theoretical rigor via approximation bounds and robustness analysis.  
- Strength: practical algorithmic approach making the problem tractable for real applications.  
- Strength: clear writing, helpful diagrams, and logical organization.  
- Weakness: computational complexity may remain high (O(T(B)2T2K|Y||Z|), concerns about scalability to large state spaces and long horizons).  
- Weakness: reliance on specific model assumptions in the breast cancer application; limited sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: dataset is de-identified with limited features; richer patient/genetic data might change results.  
- Weakness: linear delay cost used in the application is a simplification and not fully justified.  
- Weakness: limited comparison to other advanced baselines (e.g., risk-based screening using Gail model alone).  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, provides a novel/theoretically sound framework, and shows promising real-data results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: potential scalability issues, dependence on modeling assumptions, and limited baseline comparisons.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper, recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Revision requests: add a detailed scalability discussion and optimization strategies, perform sensitivity analyses on model and cost choices, include additional baselines (e.g., Gail-based risk screening), and better justify the linear delay cost.
