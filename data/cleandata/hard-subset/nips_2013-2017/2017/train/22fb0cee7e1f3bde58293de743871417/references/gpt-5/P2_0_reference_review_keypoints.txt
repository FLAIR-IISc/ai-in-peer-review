- Formulates personalized dynamic screening as a belief-based DP with finite-state disease, external-information arrivals, stochastic tests, and trajectory-dependent delay costs.
- Proves piecewise-linear convexity of the value function, shows exact DP is intractable, and proposes a PBVI-style algorithm (DPSCREEN) using sampled trajectories and reachable beliefs.
- Provides an approximation error bound tied to belief-sampling density under strong assumptions and a local-constancy robustness result.
- Empirical illustration on a 45k-patient breast-cancer dataset shows large reductions in screening counts at matched expected delay; demonstrates value of including self-exams.
- Strength: ambitious, timely integration of external information, personalization, and trajectory-based delay costs in a unified framework.
- Strength: clear DP/POMDP perspective with well-motivated belief-state formulation and convexity link to known structure.
- Strength: practical, implementable PBVI adaptation with domain-aware belief sampling; discusses complexity and parallelizability.
- Strength: some theoretical support (coarse error bound and robustness intuition).
- Strength: large-scale empirical demonstration indicating promising personalization gains.
- Weakness: modest algorithmic novelty—mostly standard PBVI adapted with tailored belief sampling, lacking new structural results specific to endogenous arrivals.
- Weakness: theoretical results rely on strong/unrealistic assumptions (convex delay costs, perfect tests, bounded intervals, large horizons) that contradict experimental settings.
- Weakness: local-constancy result is stated high-level with deferred conditions/proof, leaving unclear when model perturbations cross decision boundaries.
- Weakness: evaluation is model-based (learned model used for training and evaluation), risking optimistic performance estimates without off-policy/counterfactual validation or robustness checks.
- Weakness: limited baselines—only annual/biennial comparisons; missing prior POMDP policies, heuristics, and relevant methods like ConfidentCare.
- Weakness: empirical claim of generality undermined by using a simple 2-state Markov model; no non-Markov or richer-state demonstrations.
- Weakness: modeling and clarity issues—assumed no false positives on diagnosis vs. experiments with imperfect tests; equal unit costs for mammogram/biopsy; deferred implementation details (K, |B̄|, runtime, convergence, w tuning); hard-to-parse complexity notation.
- Quality: formulation and DP are sound and PWL convexity credible, but approximation analysis depends on unmet assumptions; empirical evaluation promising but limited.
- Clarity: generally well written but over-relies on the supplement for key proofs/algorithms and inconsistently reconciles assumptions across sections.
- Originality: medium—nice synthesis of endogenous arrivals and trajectory costs in a POMDP-like model, but solution largely adapts known PBVI ideas.
- Significance: potentially high for personalized screening if validated, but current evidence is preliminary due to model-based evaluation and sparse baselines.
- Suggested improvements: add empirical robustness (off-policy evaluation, sensitivity to hazards/test-accuracy, bootstrap re-estimation), include heterogeneous test costs, and report variability.
- Suggested improvements: compare against prior POMDP-based policies, heuristic risk-threshold schedules, and ConfidentCare on the same model.
- Suggested improvements: provide implementation details (K, |B̄| per t, runtime, memory, parallelization, convergence diagnostics, how w is tuned).
- Suggested improvements: demonstrate a richer or non-Markov disease model to substantiate claimed generality and reconcile test-accuracy assumptions with theory.
- Recommendation: leaning toward rejection in current form; acceptability depends on stronger empirical validation, richer baselines, and clearer alignment between assumptions, theory, and experiments.
