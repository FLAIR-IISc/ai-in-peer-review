

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

This paper proposes a framework for personalized screening policies that balances screening costs against delay costs in disease detection. The authors formulate the problem as a general partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) with a finite state disease model, incorporating external information (e.g., self-examinations) and non-Markovian disease progression. They develop a point-based value iteration approach (DPSCREEN) for approximating the optimal policy with theoretical error bounds and demonstrate the method on breast cancer screening data.

## Technical Strengths

The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by extending POMDPs to handle disease trajectories rather than just current states, which is a significant departure from existing screening models that assume Markovian disease progression. The theoretical analysis of the value function (Lemma 1) and the approximation error bound (Proposition 1) are technically sound and well-motivated. The experimental results on breast cancer data show promising reductions in screening frequency (28-68%) while maintaining similar detection delays compared to standard clinical practices.

## Technical Weaknesses

Despite the theoretical contributions, the paper contains several critical technical inconsistencies that undermine its claims:

1. The paper claims to handle "arbitrary finite state stochastic processes" but the model description (Section 2) and experimental validation (Section 4) both rely on Markovian disease processes. The breast cancer example uses a two-state Markov model (Healthy/Disease), contradicting the paper's central claim of handling non-Markovian processes. The authors should clarify how their method handles truly non-Markovian processes.

2. The definition of Ω(B̄) in Proposition 1 is incomplete: "Ω(B̄) = ζ maxt∈T maxBminb∈B̄[t] ||b− b ′ ||1" - the symbol b' is undefined, creating confusion about the sampling density measure.

3. The computational complexity analysis (Section 3.2) states "O(T(B)2T2K|Y||Z|)" but this appears to be incorrect. Standard point-based value iteration complexity is O(|B| * |A| * |S|), not O(T(B)2T2K|Y||Z|). The extra factor of 2T is unexplained and likely a typo.

4. The paper claims to handle "non-stationary" processes, but the breast cancer model assumes stationary transition probabilities (logistic functions of features). The authors provide no evidence that their method works for genuinely non-stationary disease processes.

5. The robustness analysis (Section 3.3) makes a strong claim that "with probability 1, if the model estimate m̂ and the true model m' are sufficiently close, then the actions recommended by the exactly optimal policies for both models are identical," but this claim lacks proof or reference in the main text.

## Clarity and Formatting Issues

The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies that undermine its professionalism:

1. Inconsistent use of "Supplementary Material" vs "Supplementary Materials" (Appendix D-F vs Appendix A).

2. Inconsistent notation: "Pr(s(t) = D, s(t′) 6= D)" should be "s(t') ≠ D" (with space between ≠ and D).

3. Inconsistent use of thousands separators: "45, 000 patients" (with space) vs "84,000 mammograms" (without space).

4. The phrase "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)" contains an unclear "st" prefix.

5. The notation "Y (t) = g(t) + (t)" uses (t) for noise, which is confusing and should use a different symbol (e.g., ε(t)).

## Significance and Impact

While the potential impact on personalized medicine is significant, the paper fails to adequately demonstrate this. The experimental validation is limited to one disease (breast cancer) with a two-state Markov model, contradicting the paper's claims about handling general disease processes. The paper doesn't discuss how the approach would generalize to other diseases with different progression patterns (e.g., pancreatic cancer with rapid decline in survival probability).

The paper also lacks discussion of clinical feasibility: How would clinicians implement these personalized screening policies? What would the user interface look like? Without addressing these practical considerations, the significance of the work is diminished.

## Related Work

The related work section (Section 5) correctly identifies limitations of existing approaches but doesn't sufficiently differentiate the current work from similar POMDP-based screening methods (e.g., Ayer et al. [7]). The paper claims to be "the first such personalized screening policies in a very general setting," but the experimental validation doesn't support this claim given the limited scope of their disease model.

## Conclusion

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to personalized screening, the technical inconsistencies, overstatements of capabilities, and insufficient experimental validation undermine its contribution. The claims about handling arbitrary disease processes are contradicted by the Markovian disease model used in the experiments. The paper contains multiple technical errors that would make it difficult for readers to properly understand and implement the proposed method.

The paper would benefit from: (1) clarifying the scope of disease processes it can handle, (2) correcting the technical inconsistencies in the theoretical analysis, (3) providing more comprehensive experimental validation across different disease models, and (4) addressing clinical feasibility concerns.

Given these significant technical issues and the paper's failure to adequately support its central claims, I recommend rejection. The paper's current form does not meet NIPS standards for technical rigor and clarity, despite the potentially valuable framework it attempts to present.
