- Paper introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized screening that trades off screening costs and detection delays using an optimal-control/POMDP-like formulation with non-Markovian disease models and external information.
- Proposal includes a point-based value iteration approximation with theoretical approximation-error guarantees.
- Empirical validation on a large breast cancer dataset shows 28–68% reduction in screenings while maintaining expected detection delay.

Strengths — Quality:
- Technically sound model addressing limitations of Markov-only and stationary-test-outcome assumptions.
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear statements and proofs (e.g., convexity lemma, approximation error bounds).
- Thorough discussion of prior-work limitations and how DPSCREEN overcomes them.
- Well-designed experiments using realistic data and appropriate metrics.
- Comprehensive treatment of both approximation and estimation error sources.

Strengths — Originality:
- Novel handling of non-Markovian disease progression in a decision-theoretic screening framework.
- Innovative incorporation of external information (e.g., self-exams) affecting scheduling decisions.
- General method applicable to multiple disease models (dynamic survival, first hitting time, etc.).
- Clear comparison to prior POMDP/Bayesian decision-making work showing substantive extensions.

Strengths — Significance:
- Addresses a high-impact real-world problem with potential to reduce healthcare costs while preserving outcomes.
- Substantial empirical screening reductions that could influence clinical practice.
- Framework enables personalization based on patient features and history.
- Analysis of different delay cost choices highlights practical implications for policy design.

Weaknesses — Clarity:
- Dense mathematical notation; some sections (Bellman equations) need more intuitive exposition or examples.
- Connection between theory and implementation (Algorithms 1–2) is somewhat opaque; belief-space reduction needs concrete examples.
- Computational-complexity expression is vague; lacks practical runtime estimates for realistic sizes.

Weaknesses — Quality:
- Experiments limited to breast cancer; generalization to other disease types is not demonstrated.
- No comparison to more recent approaches (e.g., deep RL methods for medical decision-making).
- Clinical claim about personalization could use more supporting evidence beyond the single case study.

Overall assessment:
- Strong, novel, and technically rigorous contribution with compelling empirical results and clear practical relevance.

Recommendation:
- Recommend acceptance; minor clarifications and broader validation would strengthen the paper.

Suggested improvements:
- Provide more intuitive explanations and examples for belief-space reduction.
- Include a brief comparison to recent deep reinforcement learning approaches.
- Discuss generalization potential to other diseases beyond breast cancer.
