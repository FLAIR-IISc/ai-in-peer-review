Summary
This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a general framework for dynamic, personalized disease screening under partial observability with endogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent delay costs. The authors model disease as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, allow exogenous “external information” (e.g., self-exam) that can trigger early arrivals, and define costs as a weighted sum of discounted screening costs and detection delay costs that depend on the full disease trajectory (not just current state). They derive a Bellman equation over beliefs defined on state trajectories plus a diagnosis label, show the value function is piecewise-linear and convex (as in POMDPs), and argue that exact dynamic programming is intractable. They then develop a PBVI-style approximation that (i) samples physiologically plausible trajectories and (ii) constructs a set of reachable beliefs under plausible policies, performing point-based backups to obtain an approximately optimal policy. They provide a bound on approximation error in terms of the density of belief sampling and a robustness result showing local constancy of the optimal policy under small model-parameter perturbations. Empirically, using a large breast-cancer screening dataset (Athena; ~45K patients, ~84K mammograms), they fit a two-state Markov disease model with external info from a lump-growth/self-exam model and show that DPSCREEN can reduce screenings by 28–68% versus annual screening while maintaining similar expected detection delay; they also illustrate how the shape of delay costs (linear vs quadratic) shifts the operating regime.

Relation to prior work
Compared to prior personalized screening formulations (e.g., POMDPs for mammography and colon cancer: Ayer et al., Maillart et al., Erenay et al.), this work relaxes important modeling restrictions: disease evolution need not be Markov, arrival times are endogenous via actions and external signals, and costs depend on trajectory-level detection delay rather than instantaneous state misclassification. It also contrasts with Bayesian-design/joint modeling approaches (e.g., Rizopoulos) that target information gain or simplified misdetection costs, and with medical simulation guidelines that compare fixed policy sets (e.g., USPSTF, MISCAN-COLON). Methodologically, the alpha-vector structure and PBVI approximation follow classical POMDP results, but the belief is over trajectories and the action is a recommendation plan mapping observations to next visit times, making standard POSMDP/POMDP machinery nontrivial to deploy.

Strengths
- Bold and timely problem formulation that integrates endogenous arrivals, external information, and trajectory-dependent delay costs into a single decision-theoretic framework.
- Extension of POMDP value-function structure (piecewise-linear convexity) to this richer setting; clear articulation of why standard POMDP/POSMDP formulations do not apply out of the box.
- Practical, scalable approximation (trajectory- and policy-guided belief sampling) with an explicit approximation-error bound and a robustness argument w.r.t. model estimation error.
- Clinically meaningful experiments on a sizable real dataset, with large reported reductions in testing at matched expected delay, and a persuasive demonstration that the choice of delay cost (linear vs quadratic) materially changes the optimal testing aggressiveness.
- Strong personalization narrative: schedules adapt to patient features and evolving test/external signals.

Weaknesses
- Demonstration undercuts the claimed generality: experiments use a two-state Markov disease model. It would strengthen the case to show at least one non-Markov/semi-Markov or competing-risk example, or a setting where external information materially changes arrivals versus a standard POMDP baseline.
- Assumption inconsistencies: earlier parts assume no false positives (and in Proposition 1 both no false positives/negatives), while the empirical section uses realistic mammogram sensitivity/specificity (and includes z=2 biopsy-negative outcomes). The validity and scope of the approximation bound under realistic test error need clarification.
- Evaluation is model-based: policies are evaluated on the learned model rather than via off-policy evaluation or robust sensitivity analyses. No uncertainty quantification (CIs) on the reported reductions. The weight w is tuned to match clinical delay—this is reasonable, but readers would benefit from a sensitivity sweep of w, and a direct comparison to a POMDP baseline that ignores external info.
- Scalability and reproducibility details are thin: no runtimes, memory, or ablations on K, |B̄|, W, or action-space restrictions. The complexity expression suggests potentially high cost; practical settings and parallelization gains should be quantified.
- External information/self-exam model (thresholded “universal tumor growth” plus noise) is simplistic; the choice of parameters and threshold ỹ should be stress-tested, and the clinical impact of self-exam false alarms considered. The age-60–65 cohort may limit generality.

Quality
Technically competent and mostly sound. The structural results and approximation scheme are plausible; the approximation bound is in line with PBVI-style Lipschitz arguments, albeit under restrictive assumptions. Experimental claims are promising but would benefit from stronger baselines, sensitivity analyses, and clearer statistical reporting.

Clarity
Generally well-organized, but there are typos, incomplete references, and occasional imprecision (e.g., test error assumptions). Algorithmic details are deferred to the supplement; a higher-level sketch in the main text would help readers. Explicitly stating all assumptions where each theorem applies would avoid confusion.

Originality
High on problem formulation and integration of elements (endogenous arrivals, external info, trajectory-dependent costs). Algorithmically, the approach is an adaptation of PBVI with tailored belief construction; novelty lies more in how it’s applied than in new core algorithms.

Significance
Potentially high: a unifying, decision-theoretic approach to personalized screening that could influence both ML-for-health and operations/clinical policy. If validated more broadly and with stronger baselines, this could be impactful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Resolve and clearly state the measurement-error assumptions; extend the approximation/error analysis to noisy tests if possible.
- Add a baseline POMDP/POSMDP comparison (e.g., Markov disease without external info) to quantify the benefit of the proposed generalization and the external info stream.
- Provide runtime/memory and ablations (K, |B̄|, W) and a sensitivity analysis over w, self-exam parameters, and model misspecification.
- Include uncertainty estimates (bootstrap or Bayesian posterior predictive) for key metrics; report patient-level heterogeneity.
- If feasible, showcase at least one non-Markov or competing-risk disease model to demonstrate generality.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, comprehensive formulation; theoretically grounded; clinically relevant.
- Practical algorithm with error/robustness guarantees.
- Strong empirical gains on a large real dataset.
- Opens a new direction for ML-driven screening policy optimization beyond fixed simulations.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical validation of the claimed generality; weak baselines; evaluation entirely model-based.
- Some assumption inconsistencies and clarity issues.
- Algorithmic novelty is incremental relative to PBVI.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper introduces an important, ambitious direction with a principled formulation and promising results. Strengthening empirical validation and clarifying assumptions would make it a solid accept.
