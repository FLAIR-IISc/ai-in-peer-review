Summary
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a general framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off screening cost with delay-in-detection cost. The disease is modeled as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state. Key novelties include: (i) decisions are not at fixed intervals—next screening times are actions, and patient arrivals are endogenous (triggered by planned follow-ups or external information such as self-exams); (ii) costs depend on the disease trajectory (delay since incidence), not just current state; and (iii) external information processes can be correlated with the disease trajectory.

The authors formulate a Bellman equation over beliefs that are distributions on state trajectories and a diagnosis flag. They show the value function is convex and piecewise linear (as in POMDPs), but exact dynamic programming is intractable due to the extremely high-dimensional belief space. They propose a point-based approximation that samples likely state trajectories and reachable beliefs under random exploration and plausible clinical policies. They provide a (somewhat high-level) bound on approximation error via a sampling-density metric and a robustness result that the optimal action map is locally constant off a lower-dimensional set, implying small model estimation error typically leaves actions unchanged.

An application to breast cancer (45k patients) uses a two-state Markov disease model parameterized by Gail risk scores, includes an external self-exam signal via a tumor-growth model, and compares against annual screening. The method purports to reduce the expected number of screenings by 28–68% while maintaining the same expected detection delay; it also shows how different delay cost structures (linear vs quadratic) qualitatively change the recommended policies.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned against POMDP-based personalized screening (e.g., Ayer et al., Maillart et al.) and Bayesian design/joint models for dynamic screening (e.g., Rizopoulos et al.), highlighting that those assume Markov/semi-Markov dynamics, stationary observation models, fixed decision epochs, or trajectory-independent costs. This work extends beyond those constraints by allowing trajectory-dependent costs, endogenous arrival times with external information, and very general disease processes. It also relates to controlled sensing, but here the action is scheduling a next visit rather than choosing among sensors.

Strengths
- Novel problem formulation: Endogenous decision epochs driven by both actions and an external info process, and trajectory-dependent delay costs, go beyond standard POMDP/POSMDP assumptions.
- Generality: The framework encompasses survival models, first-hitting-time models, and competing risks with minimal structural assumptions.
- Approximate solution method: Adaptation of point-based value iteration using sampled trajectories and reachable beliefs is sensible and practically motivated.
- Theoretical support: Piecewise-linear/convex value functions, an approximation bound tied to belief sampling density, and a robustness result regarding action stability to estimation error.
- Impactful application: Demonstrates sizable reductions in screenings for breast cancer while matching expected delay; analysis also shows the impact of different delay cost functions.

Weaknesses
- Technical details and assumptions: The approximation bound relies on strong assumptions (e.g., perfect tests, bounded screening interval, convex delay costs) and an opaque constant ζ; it is unclear how tight or informative the bound is in realistic settings with imperfect tests.
- Belief space and sampling: The practicality of sampling “typical” trajectories and constructing reachable beliefs is crucial but under-specified. There is limited sensitivity analysis on K, B, and action restrictions, and limited reporting on runtime/compute.
- Evaluation limitations: Policy evaluation depends on a learned generative model; no off-policy evaluation or uncertainty quantification around reported gains. Matching expected delay via weight tuning could mask clinically relevant tradeoffs (e.g., tail delays among those who develop disease).
- Clarity and consistency: Early sections assume no false positives (and the bound assumes perfect accuracy), whereas the empirical section incorporates imperfect sensitivity/specificity at the mammogram stage with biopsy confirmation. The reconciliation is not fully transparent.
- Comparisons: Empirical baselines are limited to clinical heuristics; no comparison to prior POMDP screening approaches under the same disease model, nor to modern OPE/dynamic treatment approaches.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, with reasonable DP formulation and an adaptation of PBVI. The theoretical pieces are supportive but not fully compelling for practice due to strong assumptions and limited actionable bounds. Experimental analysis is suggestive but would benefit from stronger evaluation methodology and ablations.

Clarity
Generally clear, but the exposition is dense and relies heavily on the supplement for algorithmic and proof details. Clarify the diagnostics/test accuracy assumptions and provide more concrete algorithmic settings and computational performance.

Originality
High. The combination of trajectory-dependent costs, action-dependent decision epochs with external information, and a general disease model in a screening context is a substantial step beyond prior POMDP screening papers.

Significance
Potentially high. If validated and made practical, DPSCREEN could meaningfully influence personalized screening across diseases. The breast cancer case study is compelling, though evaluation rigor should be strengthened.

Pro acceptance
- Bold, general framework that relaxes restrictive assumptions in prior screening models.
- Clear path to practical approximate solutions and personalization.
- Theoretical underpinnings and robustness intuition.
- Potentially large real-world impact.

Con acceptance
- Approximation bound and robustness theorem are not tightly actionable; rely on strong assumptions.
- Evaluation hinges on a learned model without robust OPE/uncertainty; limited ablations and baselines.
- Some ambiguity around test accuracy assumptions and practical implementability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide sensitivity analyses on the sampling design (K, B), action space restrictions, and runtime.
- Include off-policy evaluation or robust model-based evaluation with uncertainty quantification.
- Compare against prior POMDP screening methods under a matched disease model.
- Clarify assumptions about false positives/negatives across theory and experiments.
- Release code and details necessary for reproducibility.

Recommendation
Accept (borderline). Despite evaluation limitations, the paper introduces a genuinely novel and ambitious formulation for personalized screening with a plausible approximate solution. The conceptual leap and potential impact justify acceptance, provided the authors strengthen clarity and empirical evaluation in the camera-ready.

Confidence
Medium.
