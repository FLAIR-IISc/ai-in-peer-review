- Proposes DPSCREEN: a general belief-state DP framework for dynamic, personalized screening with trajectory-dependent delay costs and exogenous arrivals.  
- Casts problem as belief MDP over full state trajectories, proves value is convex and piecewise-linear, gives alpha-vector recursions (exact but intractable).  
- Introduces a PBVI-style approximation by sampling physiological trajectories and reachable beliefs under simulated policies.  
- Provides a coarse approximation-error bound tied to belief-sampling density and a robustness (local constancy) result under small model misspecification.  
- Empirically instantiates framework for breast cancer (ages 60–65) and reports 28–68% screening reductions versus annual/biennial policies at similar expected delays.  
- Novelty: explicit optimization over dynamic, history-dependent screening intervals with exogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent costs; PBVI adaptation for trajectory beliefs.  
- Strength: clinically relevant problem formulation combining screening burden and delay tradeoffs with exogenous information.  
- Strength: modeling generality allowing non-Markov trajectories and trajectory-dependent costs.  
- Strength: theoretical extension of POMDP piecewise-linear/convex value results to this setting.  
- Strength: practical PBVI algorithm that samples physiologically plausible trajectories and reachable beliefs.  
- Strength: promising empirical reductions in screenings on a large real dataset and illustrative quadratic-delay case study.  
- Concern: inconsistent assumptions about test accuracy—paper alternates between no false positives/negatives and realistic mammography sensitivity/specificity, undermining modeling and theoretical claims.  
- Concern: inconsistent treatment of delay costs and discounting between main formulation and the approximation bound.  
- Concern: Bellman equation (Eq. 3) has sign/min/max inconsistencies; unclear whether V is cost-to-go or negative value.  
- Concern: independence assumptions (tests and external info independent of past given trajectory) may be unrealistic; robustness to violations not discussed.  
- Concern: proofs deferred and reduction to a standard POMDP on augmented trajectories not made explicit despite trajectory-dependent costs.  
- Concern: Proposition 1’s error bound is high-level and relies on strong assumptions (perfect tests, bounded intervals, large T); dependence on K and B is unclear and unvalidated empirically.  
- Concern: Theorem 1 (local constancy) is generic and not strongly informative without clarified conditions and practical parameter ranges.  
- Concern: scalability—trajectory beliefs scale as |S|^T; need concrete runtimes and values for K, B, W, T, and parallelization details.  
- Concern: reproducibility—missing details on MCMC priors, convergence diagnostics, Gail risk integration, and calibration of lump model parameters.  
- Concern: empirical validation relies on learned generative model without held-out calibration metrics (e.g., Brier score, calibration plots).  
- Concern: limited baselines—comparisons only to annual/biennial schedules; should include prior personalized policies and reactive rules to isolate benefits.  
- Concern: missing uncertainty reporting—need CIs for E[N], E[Δ], E[Δ|D] and distributional/tail metrics to detect harmful delays.  
- Concern: generalizability unclear—cohort restricted to ages 60–65, arbitrary 5% risk threshold, and no subgroup/fairness analyses.  
- Concern: manuscript clarity—dense and inconsistent presentation; crucial details relegated to Supplement; proofs and algorithmic specifics should be included.  
- Key questions for authors: reconcile test-accuracy assumptions and biopsy modeling; provide corrected Bellman equations and augmented-state reduction; report computational budgets and sensitivity to K,B,W; provide held-out calibration and misspecification analyses; compare to existing personalized POMDP baselines and ConfidentCare.  
- Pros for acceptance: important problem, principled general framework, integration of exogenous arrivals and trajectory costs, practical PBVI adaptation, promising real-data results.  
- Cons for acceptance: theoretical-experimental inconsistencies, coarse unvalidated error bound, limited baselines, lack of calibration and runtime/sensitivity analyses, and reproducibility/clarity issues.  
- Recommendation: Borderline—promising and potentially impactful but requires alignment of theoretical assumptions with experiments, equation clarifications, and stronger empirical validation.
