- DPSCREEN: a framework for dynamic personalized screening that adapts to patient features, screening history, and external information while addressing non-stationary progression, delayed costs, and computational challenges.  
- Extends prior work by relaxing Markovian assumptions and supporting dynamic external information, unlike fixed-interval or disease-specific models.  
- Highly general: accommodates multiple disease models (survival, first-hitting time) and varied external data sources.  
- Dynamic personalization: policies adjust over time based on patient features and history, shown in the breast cancer example.  
- Uses Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) for computational tractability with a provable bounded approximation error (Proposition 1).  
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with robustness guarantees (Lemma 1, Theorem 1) to support the method.  
- Empirical results on a breast cancer dataset show 28–68% reduction in screening frequency without increasing detection delay.  
- Paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear mathematical notation.  
- Model complexity could hinder practical adoption and make implementation/parameterization for new diseases burdensome.  
- Relies on assumptions (e.g., no false positives, conditional independence) that may not hold; sensitivity analysis is limited.  
- Experimental scope is narrow (primarily breast cancer); broader validation on other diseases/datasets is needed.  
- PBVI algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 & 2) are brief and need more detail for reproducibility.  
- Lacks a detailed discussion of computational runtime and costs across datasets and problem sizes.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant advance, theoretical soundness, strong empirical gains, and potential high impact on healthcare decision-making.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical adoption concerns due to complexity and untested assumptions/limited experiments.  
- Overall assessment: strong contribution that merits acceptance; strengths outweigh the noted weaknesses.  
- Quality: excellent; Clarity: good but algorithmic details could improve; Originality & Significance: excellent.  
- Recommendation: Accept.
