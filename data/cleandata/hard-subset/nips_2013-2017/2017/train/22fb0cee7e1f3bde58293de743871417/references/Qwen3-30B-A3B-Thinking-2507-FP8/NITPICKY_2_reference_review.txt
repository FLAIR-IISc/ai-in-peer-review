

# Review of "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening"

This paper proposes a novel approach to personalized screening policies that dynamically balances screening costs against delay in disease detection. The authors model the problem as a partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) with a general disease progression model that accommodates non-stationary processes and external information. They develop a Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) approach to compute an approximately optimal policy with theoretical error bounds.

**Strengths:** The paper addresses a significant real-world problem with clear societal impact. The technical contributions are substantial, particularly Lemma 1 proving the value function is convex and piecewise linear, and Proposition 1 providing a meaningful approximation error bound. The experimental results on breast cancer data demonstrate substantial improvements (28-68% reduction in screenings) over current clinical practices while maintaining equivalent detection delays.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Formatting and presentation errors:** The paper contains multiple distracting typos, including "st Conference" (should be "the Conference"), inconsistent notation (Y(t) vs Y (t)), and incomplete reference formatting (e.g., "Annals of internal medicine,, , ,"). These undermine the paper's professional presentation.

2. **Technical inconsistencies:** The computational complexity claim "O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)" appears to contain a typo (repeated T^2). The approximation error bound in Proposition 1 is stated as "bounded above by Ω(B̄)" but Ω(B̄) is defined as "ζ maxt∈T maxBminb∈B̄[t] ||b− b′ ||1" without defining b′. The paper claims "no false positives" for test outcomes, which is unrealistic for medical screening (e.g., mammograms have false positives).

3. **Insufficient methodological justification:** The authors claim to be "the first such personalized screening policies in a very general setting" but fail to adequately differentiate their approach from prior work [7,9,10] that also uses POMDPs. The paper states "The maximum screening interval is bounded by W < T" without explaining why this restriction is necessary or how it affects the solution's generality.

4. **Experimental shortcomings:** The paper claims to use "a large breast cancer data set" but provides no details about the dataset size, structure, or preprocessing. The comparison in Table 1 lacks statistical significance testing for the reported improvements. The risk group division (5% threshold) is arbitrary without justification.

5. **Incomplete theoretical development:** The proof of Lemma 1 is referenced to "Appendix D-F" but these sections are not included in the provided excerpt. The paper makes a strong claim that "existing works on screening [7][9] are restricted to Markov disease processes" but fails to show why their model is truly more general than the models in these references.

**Critical evaluation:**

While the paper addresses an important problem with potentially significant clinical impact, the numerous technical inconsistencies and formatting errors, combined with insufficient methodological justification, significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The claim of being "the first" in a very general setting appears overstated given the lack of clear differentiation from prior work. The approximation error bound in Proposition 1 is theoretically interesting but lacks concrete implementation details that would allow reproducibility.

The paper's presentation would benefit from substantial polishing. The numerous typos and inconsistent notation (particularly the repeated "st Conference" error) suggest a lack of careful proofreading. The experimental section requires more methodological detail to be convincing.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses a meaningful problem with potentially valuable contributions, the numerous technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and insufficient methodological justification outweigh the paper's strengths. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to address these issues before it could be considered for publication at NIPS. The current version fails to meet the high standards of technical rigor expected at NIPS, particularly regarding clarity, completeness, and proper differentiation from prior work.

The paper's claims about being the first to address this problem in a "very general setting" require stronger justification, and the theoretical contributions would be more convincing with a more detailed presentation of the proofs and a clearer explanation of how the method differs from existing approaches. The experimental results, while promising, lack sufficient detail to assess their validity.
