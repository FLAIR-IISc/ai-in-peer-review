- Proposes DPSCREEN: a framework for dynamic personalized screening balancing screening costs and delay costs with a general finite-state disease progression model and external information.  
- Extends POMDP methods to handle trajectory-dependent delay costs and provides an efficient algorithm with approximation guarantees; validated on breast cancer data showing 28–68% fewer screenings for equivalent delay.

- Strength (Quality): Theoretical framework handles disease progression, external info, and screening decisions coherently.  
- Strength (Quality): Provides meaningful theoretical guarantees (Proposition 1) on approximation error using POMDP theory.  
- Strength (Quality): Thorough experimental validation on a large real-world breast cancer dataset with compelling reductions in screening frequency.  
- Strength (Quality): Addresses practical limitations like estimation error and discusses appropriate delay cost choices.

- Strength (Clarity): Problem formulation and components are detailed and precise.  
- Strength (Clarity): Consistent, well-explained mathematical notation.  
- Strength (Clarity): Effective figures and comparative tables aid understanding.  
- Strength (Clarity): Appendix includes sufficient replication details and algorithm pseudocode.

- Strength (Originality): Generalizes disease progression beyond Markov/Semi-Markov models.  
- Strength (Originality): Incorporates external information (e.g., self-exams) into screening decisions.  
- Strength (Originality): Novel DPSCREEN algorithm adapting point-based value iteration for high-dimensional belief spaces.  
- Strength (Originality): Rigorous theoretical analysis of approximation error.

- Strength (Significance): Potential for substantial cost savings and reduced patient burden (28–68% fewer screenings).  
- Strength (Significance): Framework generalizes to multiple diseases, increasing practical impact.  
- Strength (Significance): Provides an implementable framework relevant to clinical practice.  
- Strength (Significance): Bridges ML methods with a critical healthcare application.

- Weakness (Quality): Assumes no false positives in tests, which simplifies real-world testing and may limit applicability.  
- Weakness (Quality): Validation dataset limited to ages 60–65, raising generalizability concerns.  
- Weakness (Quality): Proposition 1’s assumptions (convex delay cost, no false positives, bounded interval) may not hold broadly.

- Weakness (Clarity): Belief space construction (Algorithm 1) lacks an intuitive explanation for sampling-based construction.  
- Weakness (Clarity): Relationship between the general disease model and the breast cancer implementation is not made explicit.  
- Weakness (Clarity): Insufficient explanation of why an absorbing disease state captures full progression including intermediate risky states.

- Weakness (Originality): Needs clearer distinction from standard POMDP approaches beyond screening application.  
- Weakness (Originality): Could better contextualize connections to recent POMDP work in ML literature.

- Weakness (Significance): Should discuss integration into clinical decision support systems to strengthen practical impact.  
- Weakness (Significance): Lacks discussion of implementation barriers in healthcare (regulatory, clinician acceptance).

- Overall assessment: Strong, well-executed, and significant contribution with sound theory, solid experiments, and clear practical relevance.  
- Recommendation: Accept.

- Suggestion: Extend framework to handle false positives in test outcomes.  
- Suggestion: Discuss generalizability to other age groups and populations beyond 60–65.  
- Suggestion: Add discussion of clinical implementation challenges and solutions.  
- Suggestion: Clarify how the general disease model maps to the specific breast cancer model in experiments.  
- Suggestion: Briefly compare and position work relative to recent NIPS/ML POMDP literature.
