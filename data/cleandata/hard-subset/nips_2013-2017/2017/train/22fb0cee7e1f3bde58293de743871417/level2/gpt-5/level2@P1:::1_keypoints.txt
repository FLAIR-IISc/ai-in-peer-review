- Proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening using finite-state disease trajectories, external information, and a cost trade-off between screening and delay-to-detection.  
- Casts the problem as a belief-state dynamic program with recommendation-plan actions and adapts PBVI via trajectory-based belief sampling to obtain approximate policies.  
- Empirical instantiation on a large breast-cancer dataset shows 28–68% fewer screens than annual/biennial schedules at matched expected detection delay, and personalization by risk/history.

- Extends prior work by allowing general (non-Markov) trajectory models, trajectory-dependent delay costs, and exogenous external information; builds on POMDP/PBVI but for endogenous arrivals and recommendation-plan actions.  
- Properly connects to controlled sensing and clinical simulation literature while claiming broader model class coverage.

- Strength: realistic problem formulation integrating features, history, and external info with clear cost objective.  
- Strength: solid DP formulation, adapted PBVI, and established value-function structure.  
- Strength: provides an approximation bound and a robustness/local-constancy argument for model error.  
- Strength: convincing empirical illustration on a sizable real dataset with clear personalization effects.  
- Strength: good discussion of how delay-cost shape influences policy aggressiveness.

- Main theoretical bound relies on strong assumptions (perfect tests, bounded intervals, convex delay cost) that do not hold in experiments; applicability needs clarification or a more general bound.  
- Key reduction (trajectory belief sampling) is underspecified: missing concrete K, |B̄[t]|, T, runtime, hardware, and ablation studies on sampling parameters.  
- Evaluation depends on model-based simulation from observational data; lacks empirical sensitivity analyses (bootstrap CIs, calibration, alternative hazard models, stress tests).  
- External-information parameters (self-exam threshold and noise) are taken from literature but sensitivity to these and comparisons to “no external info” are insufficiently explored.  
- Claiming “first framework” is too strong given concurrent personalized screening/scheduling work; should soften and clarify differences.  
- Constrained objective (match expected delay) is implemented by tuning weight w, but tuning procedure, convergence, and guarantees are not detailed.

- Mathematical formulation and adaptation of POMDP results are sound and well-motivated; proofs are provided in supplement.  
- Approximation and robustness claims are plausible but limited by restrictive assumptions and need stronger empirical validation.

- Paper is overall clear and organized but needs more experimental details (T, δ, K, |B̄[t]|, W, number of sweeps, wall-clock time) and precise reporting (definitions, CIs, handling of false positives/negatives).  
- Recommend adding pseudo-code for belief update with external information and stating assumptions/intuition of the local-constancy theorem in the main text.

- Originality: high for framing trajectory-based screening with external info and adapting PBVI to sampled trajectories; theoretical elements are adaptations but applied to a novel problem class.  
- Significance: potentially high for personalized screening if reproducibility and broader validation are improved.

- Questions for authors: report K, |B̄[t]|, T, δ, W, runtime, and memory used in experiments.  
- How was w tuned to meet delay constraints, was bisection used, and how sensitive is policy to w?  
- Provide bootstrap CIs for E[N] and E[Δ], and calibration/validation of estimated hazards.  
- How sensitive are results to self-exam parameters (σ, ỹ) and to excluding external information?  
- Can Proposition 1 be extended to imperfect tests, or can empirical approximation gaps be reported on held-out simulations?

- Minor: fix reference formatting and completeness.  
- Minor: include pseudo-code for belief update to aid reproducibility.  
- Minor: state assumptions/intuition of the local-constancy theorem in main text.

- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem with a principled, general framework, nontrivial POMDP adaptation, clear personalization effects, and substantial empirical gains.  
- Arguments against acceptance: theoretical bounds mismatch experimental assumptions, insufficient reproducibility/computational details, and limited sensitivity/uncertainty analysis.

- Overall recommendation: borderline accept pending stronger empirical validation, clearer computational reporting, and clarification/extension of theoretical guarantees.
