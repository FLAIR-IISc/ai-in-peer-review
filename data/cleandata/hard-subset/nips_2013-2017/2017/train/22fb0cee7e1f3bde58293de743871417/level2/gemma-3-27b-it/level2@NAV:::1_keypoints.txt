- Proposes DPSCREEN, a novel framework modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process to optimize personalized screening schedules.

- Leverages patient features and screening history, addressing limitations of restrictive Markovian assumptions in prior work.

- Uses point-based value iteration for a computationally tractable solution to the dynamic screening problem.

- Framework is disease-agnostic and broadly applicable, increasing potential practical impact.

- Empirical results on a large breast cancer dataset show 28–68% reductions in screening frequency with comparable delay times to clinical practice.

- Incorporates external information (e.g., self-exams) and analyzes robustness to model estimation errors.

- Provides insight into how different cost functions affect resulting screening policies, stressing careful cost modeling.

- Computational complexity discussion is present but lacks detailed breakdown and scalability analysis for higher-dimensional state spaces and larger datasets.

- Parameter estimation via MCMC is noted, but convergence diagnostics and sensitivity analyses are not sufficiently reported.

- The assumption of no false positives in test outcomes is limiting and its impact is not explored.

- Presentation is fragmented by frequent references to the Appendix (Algorithm 1, Theorem 1), which hinders readability.

- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, offers a generalizable approach, shows strong real-world results, and acknowledges limitations.

- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient computational and parameter-estimation detail, limiting assumption about false positives, and fragmented presentation.

- Overall recommendation: strong paper worth acceptance contingent on addressing computational detail, estimation transparency, false-positive assumptions, and presentation clarity.
