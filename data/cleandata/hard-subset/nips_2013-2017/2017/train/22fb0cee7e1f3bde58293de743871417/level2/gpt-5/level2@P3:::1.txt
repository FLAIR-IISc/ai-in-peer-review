Summary
The paper introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off screening frequency against delay in disease detection. Disease evolution is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, and screening times are action-dependent and endogenous due to an external information process (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger unscheduled visits. The objective combines discounted screening cost and trajectory-dependent delay cost. Exact optimization is intractable; the work formulates Bellman equations, proves the value function is convex piecewise-linear in beliefs, and proposes a tractable point-based value iteration (PBVI) variant that samples likely state trajectories and reachable beliefs. A bound relates approximation error to sampling density, and a robustness result shows local constancy of optimal actions with respect to small model-parameter perturbations. On a breast cancer dataset (45k women), learned two-state models and a self-exam process are used to construct policies that reduce the number of screens by 28–68% versus annual screening while maintaining the same expected detection delay, with personalization by risk and history. An illustration also contrasts linear vs quadratic delay costs.

Relation to prior work
The formulation extends POMDP ideas (Smallwood & Sondik; Pineau et al.) by allowing: (i) trajectory-dependent delay costs; (ii) action- and state-dependent time between decisions via patient-initiated arrivals; and (iii) non-Markov disease processes in principle. Prior screening works in OR and biostatistics typically assume Markov or semi-Markov disease/test processes and/or compare a fixed set of policies via simulation. The proposed PBVI-style approach adapts point-based planning to a high-dimensional belief over state trajectories by sampling likely trajectories and reachable beliefs.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The dynamic program and Bellman equations are well-motivated. The piecewise-linear/convexity result follows known POMDP structure adapted to trajectory beliefs. The PBVI variant is reasonable; the approximation error bound (via sampling density) and robustness theorem add useful theoretical support, though both rely on assumptions (accurate tests; bounded intervals; convex delay costs) that should be clearly delimited in the main text.
- Empirical evaluation: The model-based evaluation is standard for screening (learn model, evaluate policies via simulation), with a real dataset, personalization by risk, assessment with/without external information, and clear metrics. However, validation of model fit and sensitivity to misspecification could be expanded.

Clarity
- The problem, components (states, external information, arrivals, costs), and algorithms are clearly laid out. Proofs and pseudocode are delegated to the supplement.
- There is a potential inconsistency: the main text assumes no false positives for diagnosis, while experiments incorporate mammogram sensitivity/specificity and even z=2 (positive mammogram, negative biopsy). Clarifying the detection pipeline and where “no false positives” applies would improve coherence.
- The choice of w and δ and how constraints are enforced via tuning are explained but could be more explicit.

Originality
- The combination of trajectory-dependent costs, endogenous arrival times, and PBVI with trajectory/reachable-belief sampling appears novel in the screening context. The theoretical robustness/approximation results are also original and relevant.

Significance
- Screening is an important and active area, and a generalizable decision-theoretic framework could influence both ML and health operations research. The reported reductions in screening volume at fixed delay are substantial. The approach should be of interest to the NeurIPS community working on sequential decision-making, healthcare ML, and POMDPs.

Strengths
- Well-motivated formulation addressing key gaps in prior screening models (external info, endogenous timing, trajectory costs).
- Solid DP formulation with piecewise-linear value characterization.
- Practical, scalable approximation via PBVI with targeted belief construction.
- Theoretical guarantees: sampling-density-based error bound and local action robustness.
- Empirical study with real data; clear personalization effects and large practical gains; analysis of different delay cost shapes.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Assumption mismatches: “no false positives” vs experimental pipeline with imperfect tests; Proposition 1 also assumes no false negatives. Realistic diagnostic cascades should be consistently modeled.
- Generality vs practicality: while the model allows arbitrary finite-state disease processes, experiments use a two-state Markov model; scalability and performance for richer state spaces remain untested.
- Limited baselines: comparisons are primarily to annual/biennial schedules. Missing comparisons to prior POMDP-based screening (e.g., Ayer et al.) or strong heuristics (myopic thresholds, information-gain policies).
- Model validation: limited discussion of goodness-of-fit, posterior predictive checks, and sensitivity to parameter uncertainty. The robustness theorem is qualitative; empirical robustness (e.g., stress tests) would strengthen claims.
- Operational assumptions: perfect adherence to recommended schedules and self-exam behavior, and fixed thresholds for self-exam-triggered visits, may not hold in practice.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a principled, general framework and demonstrable benefits.
- Novel methodological contributions within the POMDP literature adapted to healthcare.
- Theoretical guarantees plus practical algorithmic design.
- Clear evidence of personalization and substantial resource savings at fixed detection delay.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incomplete reconciliation of diagnostic error modeling and theoretical assumptions.
- Limited comparative baselines and ablation analyses; evidence mainly relative to simple schedules.
- Unclear scalability to more complex disease/test models despite claims of generality.
- Insufficient empirical assessment of robustness to model misspecification.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Reconcile the “no false positives/negatives” assumptions used in theory with the experimental diagnostic pipeline (mammogram + biopsy with stated sensitivity/specificity). Clearly state where each assumption applies, and, if possible, relax Proposition 1 to allow realistic test errors or discuss how to extend the bound.
- Add at least one stronger baseline: a POMDP-based policy under Markov assumptions (e.g., Ayer et al.) and/or a myopic or information-gain heuristic, to situate gains beyond annual/biennial schedules.
- Provide model validation: posterior predictive checks for incidence and detection timing; sensitivity analyses to key parameters (transition hazards, self-exam threshold and noise, δ, and w).

Nice-to-have:
- Report computational costs (wall-clock times, K and |B̄[t]|) and scaling behavior; include parallelization details.
- Explore adherence uncertainty (missed appointments; variable self-exam adherence).
- Release code and anonymized parameter estimates to facilitate reproducibility.

Overall assessment
A strong, well-crafted contribution at the intersection of POMDPs and personalized healthcare. With clarification of test-error assumptions and stronger comparative/sensitivity analyses, the work would be a compelling addition to NeurIPS. Recommended for acceptance conditional on addressing essential points.
