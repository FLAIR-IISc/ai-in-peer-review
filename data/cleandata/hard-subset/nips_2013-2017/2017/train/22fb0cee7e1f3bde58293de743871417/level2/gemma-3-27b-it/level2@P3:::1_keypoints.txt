- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized screening balancing screening frequency and detection delay.  
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and incorporates external information.  
- Aims for computationally tractable, approximately optimal screening policies.  
- Strong literature grounding across medical decision-making, operations research, and machine learning.  
- Clearly positions contribution by highlighting limits of Markovian, stationary-outcome, and prior approaches.  
- Connects to POMDPs, survival models, and controlled sensing literature.  
- General framework applicable beyond Markovian disease models.  
- Inclusion of external signals (e.g., self-exams) adds realism missing in prior work.  
- Develops a computationally tractable approximation algorithm with theoretical approximation-error guarantees.  
- Empirical breast cancer experiment shows large reductions in screening frequency with comparable detection delays.  
- Insightful discussion on how cost function choices change policy regimes.  
- Model estimation description (MCMC) is cursory; needs more details, validation, and sensitivity analysis.  
- Assumes no false positives in tests—a strong, potentially unrealistic assumption that should be explored.  
- Complexity analysis mentions parallelization but lacks empirical quantification of speedup or runtime.  
- Robustness/theorem is theoretical; concrete examples showing policy changes under estimation error are missing.  
- Personalization claims rely on visuals; quantitative metrics (e.g., entropy/variance of schedules) are needed.  
- Notation is dense; earlier abbreviation definitions and clearer Appendix organization would improve readability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, generality, theoretical soundness, and supportive empirical validation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited estimation/validation detail, strong assumptions, lack of quantitative personalization analysis, and dense notation.  
- Overall assessment: significant contribution with strengths outweighing weaknesses; recommend qualified acceptance pending minor revisions.
