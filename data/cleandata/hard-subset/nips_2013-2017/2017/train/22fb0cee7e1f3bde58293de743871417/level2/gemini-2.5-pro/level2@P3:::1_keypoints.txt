- Introduces DPSCREEN, a general framework for dynamic, personalized disease screening balancing screening costs and detection delay.
- Models disease as a general finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, avoiding restrictive Markovian assumptions.
- Incorporates an external information process (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger unscheduled clinical visits.
- Exact optimization is intractable; the paper proposes an approximate, computationally feasible solution.
- Adapts Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) with trajectory sampling and reachable belief exploration to manage a high-dimensional belief space.
- Provides a theoretical approximation error bound and analyzes robustness to disease model estimation errors.
- Evaluated on a large real-world breast cancer dataset, showing 28–68% reduction in screenings while maintaining comparable detection delay.
- Strength: high societal and clinical significance with broad generality beyond prior personalized screening models.
- Strength: technically sound formulation and well-motivated PBVI adaptation with formal theoretical support (Proposition 1, Theorem 1).
- Strength: thorough experimental validation demonstrating substantial practical benefits and meaningful personalization analyses.
- Weakness: need clearer intuition and justification for the belief space reduction via trajectory sampling and why it yields tractable, relevant belief points.
- Weakness: assumes no false positives in diagnosis; should discuss extensions to handle diagnostic uncertainty (e.g., “suspected disease” states).
- Weakness: lacks empirical sensitivity analysis showing how estimation errors in model parameters affect the learned policy.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, offers a novel general framework with principled approximation and strong empirical results.
- Arguments against acceptance: only minor concerns on clarity and sensitivity analysis that do not undermine core contributions.
