- Introduces DPSCREEN: dynamic, personalized screening policies trading screening frequency against detection delay using a finite-state disease model with endogenous, action-dependent screening times.
- Models external information (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger unscheduled visits and uses a cost objective combining discounted screening cost and trajectory-dependent delay cost.
- Exact optimization is intractable; derives Bellman equations and proves the value function is convex piecewise-linear in trajectory beliefs.
- Proposes a tractable PBVI variant that samples likely state trajectories and reachable beliefs to approximate the value function.
- Provides an approximation error bound based on sampling density and a robustness result showing local constancy of optimal actions under small parameter perturbations.
- Empirical evaluation on a 45k-woman breast cancer dataset using two-state models shows 28–68% reduction in screenings versus annual screening at equal expected detection delay, with personalization by risk and history.
- Also illustrates effects of linear versus quadratic delay cost functions.
- Extends prior POMDP-based screening work by allowing trajectory-dependent costs, endogenous inter-decision times, and non-Markov disease processes in principle.
- Technical soundness: DP and Bellman formulation are well-motivated; piecewise-linear/convexity result adapts POMDP structure correctly.
- The PBVI variant is reasonable and supported by theoretical bounds, though assumptions (accurate tests, bounded intervals, convex delay costs) need clearer delimitation.
- Empirical approach (learn model, simulate policies) is standard and uses real data, but model-fit validation and sensitivity to misspecification are limited.
- Clarity is generally good; problem components and algorithms are clearly explained, with proofs/pseudocode in the supplement.
- Inconsistency: main text assumes no false positives while experiments use imperfect mammogram sensitivity/specificity, which should be clarified.
- Explanation of tuning parameters (w and δ) and constraint enforcement could be more explicit.
- Originality: novel combination of trajectory-dependent costs, endogenous arrivals, and PBVI with trajectory/reachable-belief sampling in screening context.
- Significance: relevant to ML and health operations research with potentially substantial resource savings and personalization benefits.
- Strengths: well-motivated formulation, solid DP theory, practical PBVI approximation, theoretical guarantees, and strong empirical gains on real data.
- Weaknesses: theoretical assumptions conflict with experimental diagnostic pipeline; experiments use only two-state Markov models despite claimed generality.
- Missing baselines: comparisons limited to annual/biennial schedules, lacking POMDP-based or strong heuristic baselines.
- Incomplete model validation: limited goodness-of-fit checks, posterior predictive checks, and empirical robustness analyses.
- Operational assumptions (perfect adherence, fixed self-exam thresholds) may not hold in practice.
- Essential suggestions: reconcile theory assumptions about test errors with experiments, add stronger baselines (POMDP/myopic/information-gain), and provide model validation and sensitivity analyses.
- Nice-to-have suggestions: report computational costs and scaling, explore adherence uncertainty, and release code and parameter estimates.
- Overall assessment: strong, well-crafted contribution at the POMDP–healthcare intersection; recommend acceptance conditional on addressing essential clarifications and stronger empirical analyses.
