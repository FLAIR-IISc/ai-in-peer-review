- Proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized screening balancing screening cost against delay cost using a finite-state stochastic disease model with an absorbing disease state.  
- Incorporates external information that can trigger unscheduled patient arrivals to clinicians.  
- Presentation is clear, well-motivated, and accessible with a good balance of technical detail and high-level explanation.  
- Uses a large breast cancer dataset for empirical evaluation, showing 28–68% reduction in screens for the same expected detection delay.  
- Main contribution is a computationally tractable construction of an approximately optimal policy.  
- Introduces a point-based value iteration (PBVI) approximation algorithm to compute the policy.  
- Provides theoretical analysis and discussion of robustness to estimation errors.  
- Compares the approach to existing decision-theory and operations-research frameworks, highlighting their limitations for this problem.  
- Strengths: clear problem statement, well-explained method, strong empirical evaluation, and good organization.  
- Limitation: assumes known model parameters; robustness to estimation error needs further work.  
- Limitation: evaluation focused on breast cancer, so generalizability to other diseases is unclear.  
- Limitation: lacks detailed comparison to other personalized screening models (e.g., Bayesian design or controlled sensing).  
- Scores: Quality 9/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 9/10.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address estimation robustness and include more comparisons to alternative personalized screening models.
