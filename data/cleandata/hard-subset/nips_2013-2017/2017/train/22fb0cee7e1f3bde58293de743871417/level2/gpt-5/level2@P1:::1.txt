Summary
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a general framework to design dynamic, personalized screening policies for disease detection. The disease is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; test outcomes and an external information process (e.g., self-exams) are incorporated, and the policy trades off screening cost against delay-to-detection cost. The problem is cast as a belief-state dynamic program with a recommendation plan action (mapping current test outcome to next screening time). Exact optimization is intractable; the authors adapt point-based value iteration (PBVI) by sampling likely state trajectories and reachable beliefs, yielding an approximately optimal policy with an error bound tied to a sampling-density metric. Robustness to model estimation error is argued via a local constancy result for the optimal policy. Using a large breast cancer dataset (Athena; ~45k women, 84k mammograms), DPSCREEN is instantiated with a two-state disease model, monthly self-exam external information, and test models drawn from the literature. Relative to annual or biennial screening policies, DPSCREEN reduces the expected number of screens by 28–68% while matching expected detection delay; it also adapts screening frequency to patient risk and test history.

Relation to prior work
Compared to earlier personalized screening models in operations research [7,9,10], DPSCREEN relaxes key assumptions: it allows general finite-state trajectory models (beyond Markov/semi-Markov), trajectory-dependent delay costs, and exogenous external information that can trigger early arrival. The approach draws on POMDP theory—piecewise-linear convex value functions [39], PBVI [18]—but extends it to an endogenous-arrival setting where costs depend on trajectories and the action is a recommendation plan. The medical literature typically compares a fixed set of schedules via simulation (e.g., MISCAN-COLON [46]); DPSCREEN instead optimizes over a rich policy class. The connection to controlled sensing/sensor scheduling [21,34,38] is appropriate; DPSCREEN differs by handling non-Markov disease trajectories and trajectory-dependent delay costs.

Strengths
- Formulates a realistic screening problem that integrates patient features, history, and external information, with a clear objective capturing cost-delay trade-offs.
- Solid DP formulation and adaptation of PBVI with a tailored belief sampling strategy; value function structure is established.
- Approximation framework includes a (admittedly coarse) bound and a robustness argument for model estimation error.
- Empirical illustration on a sizable real dataset; comparisons to clinical baselines (annual/biennial) show substantial screening reductions at matched expected delays.
- Clear demonstration of personalization to risk and history; thoughtful discussion of how delay cost shape affects policy aggressiveness.

Weaknesses and concerns
- The approximation error bound (Proposition 1) relies on strong assumptions (no false positives/negatives, bounded screening interval, convex delay cost). Experiments relax these (mammogram sensitivity/specificity < 1), so the theoretical bound does not apply to the implemented setting. Please clarify applicability and provide a more general bound or empirical approximation-error diagnostics.
- The belief space is over trajectories ST × {0,1}; the sampling-based reduction is key but specifics are sparse. The complexity expression is large; actual K, |B̄[t]|, T, and runtime/hardware are not reported. Reproducibility would benefit from concrete settings and ablations (e.g., effect of K, W).
- Policy evaluation is via model-based simulation using parameters learned from observational data. While standard in this literature, there is risk of model misspecification. Beyond the local constancy result, there is no empirical sensitivity analysis (e.g., bootstrap CIs, held-out calibration, posterior predictive checks, alternative hazard models, misspecification stress tests).
- External information process is influential; however, its parameters (self-exam threshold ỹ and noise σ) are set from literature sensitivity/specificity. It would help to show how performance depends on these and to compare against “no external info” more fully (beyond a single line in tables).
- The “first framework” claim seems strong given concurrent personalized screening/scheduling work (e.g., information-gain approaches [10], ConfidentCare), though DPSCREEN is broader in model class. Consider softening and sharpening the differentiation.
- The constrained formulation (match expected delay, minimize screenings) is implemented by tuning w in the weighted problem. Please detail the tuning procedure, convergence criteria, and whether guarantees exist for meeting constraints.

Quality
The mathematical formulation is sound; the use of recommendation plans and the value recursion are clearly motivated. The piecewise-linear convexity result is standard but nontrivial to adapt; the paper provides a consistent narrative (proofs in supplement). The approximation and robustness claims are plausible but limited by assumptions; empirical validation is convincing but could be strengthened through additional diagnostics and sensitivity analyses.

Clarity
Overall clear and well-organized. Suggestions:
- Specify T and δ in experiments; report K, |B̄[t]|, W, number of value-iteration sweeps, and wall-clock time.
- Detail how belief points are generated (policy set used for exploration, number of trajectories per policy).
- Provide exact definitions and values for E[Δ|R] and E[Δ|R,D] in tables, with CIs.
- Clarify diagnosis assumptions: earlier text assumes no false positives; experiments use biopsy-confirmed positives—state explicitly how false negatives/positives at mammography and biopsy are handled.

Originality
High in framing a general trajectory-based screening problem with external information and trajectory-based delay costs, and in adapting PBVI with trajectory-sampled beliefs. The theoretical pieces are mostly adaptations of known POMDP results, but applied in a novel problem class.

Significance
Potentially significant for personalized screening and adaptive testing in healthcare. If the approach is made reproducible and the empirical gains hold under broader validation, it could influence how screening intervals are designed and evaluated.

Questions for the authors
1. What were K, |B̄[t]|, T, δ, and W in the breast cancer experiments? What were runtime and memory requirements?
2. How was w tuned to meet the delay constraint? Did you use a bisection on w, and how sensitive is the resulting policy to w?
3. Can you provide bootstrapped confidence intervals for E[N] and E[Δ], and calibration plots for the estimated hazards?
4. How sensitive are results to the self-exam parameters (σ, ỹ) and to excluding external information?
5. Can Proposition 1 be extended to allow imperfect tests, or can you report empirical approximation gaps (e.g., bound surrogates) on held-out simulations?

Minor comments
- Please ensure all references are complete and consistently formatted.
- Consider including a brief pseudo-code snippet for belief update with external information to aid reproducibility.
- The “local constancy” theorem: briefly state assumptions and intuition in the main text.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a principled, general framework.
- Nontrivial adaptation of POMDP machinery; clear personalization effects and substantial empirical gains.
- Broad applicability beyond breast cancer.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical approximation bound does not match experimental assumptions; limited robustness validation.
- Reproducibility and computational details are insufficiently documented.
- Evaluation relies on a learned model without extensive sensitivity/uncertainty analysis.

Overall
Borderline accept. The problem and approach are compelling and likely to interest the NeurIPS community. Strengthening empirical validation, reporting computational details, and clarifying the scope of the theoretical guarantees would significantly improve the paper.
