This paper proposes a novel methodology for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The disease is modeled as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state. The method incorporates the possibility of external information, such as self-examination or discovery of co-morbidities, that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment.

The paper's main contributions are:

1. A general framework for constructing personalized screening policies that can be applied to a wide range of diseases.
2. A computationally effective procedure for solving for an approximately optimal policy, which is shown to be effective in reducing the number of screenings while maintaining the same delay in detection of disease.
3. A demonstration of the effectiveness of the proposed policy using a large breast cancer data set.

The paper's strengths include:

1. A clear and well-motivated problem statement, which highlights the importance of personalized screening policies in reducing the cost of screening while maintaining the same delay in detection of disease.
2. A rigorous and well-executed technical development, which provides a solid foundation for the proposed methodology.
3. A thorough evaluation of the proposed policy using a large breast cancer data set, which demonstrates its effectiveness in reducing the number of screenings while maintaining the same delay in detection of disease.

The paper's weaknesses include:

1. The assumption of a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, which may not be realistic for all diseases.
2. The use of a simplified model for the external information process, which may not capture the complexity of real-world external information.
3. The lack of a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, which may be important for large-scale applications.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of personalized screening policies, and its results have the potential to impact clinical practice. However, further research is needed to address the limitations of the proposed methodology and to explore its applicability to other diseases.
