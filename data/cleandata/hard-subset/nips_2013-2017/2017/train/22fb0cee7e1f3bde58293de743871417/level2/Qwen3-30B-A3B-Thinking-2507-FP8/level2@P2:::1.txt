

This paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing dynamic personalized screening policies that balance screening frequency against delay in disease detection. The authors model disease as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing state, incorporate external information (e.g., self-examinations), and develop a computationally tractable method to construct approximately optimal screening policies using modified POMDP techniques. The method is evaluated on breast cancer data, demonstrating significant reductions (28-68%) in screening frequency compared to clinical policies while maintaining equivalent detection delays.

Strengths:
The paper addresses an important clinical problem with clear potential impact on healthcare resource allocation. The framework is notably general, accommodating various disease models (Markov, survival, Semi-Markov) without restrictive assumptions. The authors provide a strong theoretical foundation with proof of convexity of the value function (Lemma 1) and approximation error bounds (Proposition 1). The experimental results are compelling and well-justified, with clear demonstration of personalization based on patient features and history. The paper properly contextualizes the work against prior literature, highlighting limitations of existing POMDP-based screening approaches that assume Markov disease processes.

Weaknesses:
The paper's assumption of no false positives/negatives in tests (in Proposition 1 and experimental setup) is unrealistic for medical testing and may overstate the practical benefits. The authors should address how their framework would handle test inaccuracies, which is critical for clinical adoption. The paper lacks sufficient detail on how external information (e.g., self-examinations) would be implemented in practice. For instance, it doesn't explain how the threshold ỹ for self-examination would be determined or how variability in patient self-examination behavior would be modeled. The computational complexity (O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)) appears high, and the authors don't provide concrete examples of computational requirements for real-world implementation, making it unclear how scalable the approach would be for large healthcare systems. While the paper claims to be the first general framework for personalized screening, it misses relevant work like the "ConfidentCare" system [50], which also uses a decision-theoretic approach for breast cancer screening. The generalizability to other diseases is not thoroughly demonstrated beyond the synthetic exercise with different cost functions; a more comprehensive analysis of how the framework would adapt to different disease progression models would strengthen the claim of generality.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with clear exposition of the mathematical framework. However, the unrealistic test accuracy assumption and lack of practical implementation details for external information are significant limitations that need addressing. The computational complexity concerns also warrant more discussion.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that makes a valuable technical advance in personalized healthcare. The framework is novel, the theoretical foundation is solid, and the experimental results are compelling. With revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding test accuracy assumptions, implementation details for external information, and scalability analysis, this paper would make an excellent contribution to NIPS. The significance of the work is substantial, as it could lead to more efficient and personalized screening practices that reduce unnecessary procedures while maintaining detection efficacy.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it presents a novel computational approach to a challenging problem with clear theoretical and practical implications. The technical contributions are significant and well-motivated, making it a strong candidate for acceptance with minor revisions.
