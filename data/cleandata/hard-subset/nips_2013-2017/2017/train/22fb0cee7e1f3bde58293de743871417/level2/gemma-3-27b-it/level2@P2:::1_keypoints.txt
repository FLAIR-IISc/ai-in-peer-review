- Proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for personalized screening that balances screening frequency against detection delay.  
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and uses external information plus test results to adapt screening schedules.  
- Develops a computationally tractable approximation to otherwise intractable optimal policies and provides bounds on approximation error.  
- Demonstrates empirical gains on a breast cancer dataset: significant screening reductions with comparable detection delays to clinical policies.  
- Well-motivated; moves beyond one-size-fits-all screening protocols.  
- General formulation supporting various disease models and incorporation of patient features and dynamic history.  
- Key technical contributions: tractable approximation algorithm and robustness analysis to estimation error.  
- Empirical results are promising and show substantial improvement over current clinical practice.  
- Includes useful discussion on the impact of different delay cost functions.  
- Extends POMDP and screening literature by addressing non-stationarity and including external information.  
- Strength: connects method to clinical practice through comparisons with existing policies.  
- Weakness: presentation is dense with heavy notation, reducing clarity and readability.  
- Weakness: finite-state assumption for disease and external processes may limit applicability and needs more discussion.  
- Weakness: reliance on the Gail model for initial risk estimates could introduce bias; sensitivity analysis to other models is needed.  
- Weakness: computational complexity analysis is high-level and lacks concrete empirical runtimes for realistic datasets.  
- Weakness: theoretical guarantees require assumptions (e.g., convex delay cost) that may not hold in practice.  
- Weakness: treatment of self-examination in simulations appears ad-hoc and needs stronger justification of parameter choices.  
- Specific clarity issues: abrupt jump from Lemma 1 to the PBVI approximation; need clearer explanation of value function properties exploited.  
- Specific clarity issue: connection between Proposition 1 and practical algorithm tuning (e.g., number of sampled trajectories) is unclear.  
- Acceptance arguments: important problem, novel/theoretically sound framework, promising real-data results, improvements over clinical policies, robustness guarantees.  
- Rejection arguments: dense presentation, limited applicability from finite-state assumption, lack of runtime evidence, sensitivity to initial risk model, stringent assumptions for error bounds.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with major revisions to improve clarity, provide empirical runtime data, and more thoroughly discuss model limitations.
