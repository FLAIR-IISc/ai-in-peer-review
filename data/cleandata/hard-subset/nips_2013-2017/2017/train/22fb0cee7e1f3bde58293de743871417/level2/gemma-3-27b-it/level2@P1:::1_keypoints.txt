- Introduces DPSCREEN, a novel framework modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process to optimize personalized screening schedules using patient features, history, and external information.

- Well-motivated problem tackling personalization in screening and addressing a critical need in disease diagnosis and treatment.

- Model formulation is comprehensive, incorporating disease state, external information, patient arrival, and test outcomes.

- Emphasizes generality, aiming to apply to a wide class of disease models (dynamic survival, first hitting time).

- Addresses computational intractability by constructing an approximately optimal policy and providing a bound on approximation error.

- Empirical validation on breast cancer data shows large reductions in screening frequency while maintaining comparable detection delays to clinical practice.

- Comparison against existing clinical policies is well-defined and highlights improved efficiency.

- Theoretical contribution: provision of approximation error bounds increases rigor and trustworthiness.

- Writing is clear and the personalization discussion with examples aids readability and understanding.

- Model complexity and multiple simplifying assumptions (e.g., conditional independence, limited state space) are insufficiently justified.

- Lacks a sensitivity analysis to assess how assumptions affect results.

- Computational complexity remains substantial (O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)), raising scalability concerns for large datasets or complex models.

- External information model (patient self-exam) is simplistic; modeling lump growth as zero-mean white noise may be unrealistic.

- Choice of a linear delay cost function is not rigorously justified and alternative cost functions are only briefly explored.

- Comparisons to other personalized screening approaches are limited and could be expanded.

- Arguments for acceptance: significant problem, novel and theoretically sound framework, convincing empirical evidence, and clear exposition.

- Arguments against acceptance: need for further justification of assumptions, sensitivity analysis, computational scalability, and refinement of external information modeling.

- Overall: Strong, impactful paper recommended for acceptance conditional on addressing model assumptions, providing sensitivity analyses, detailing computational complexity, and exploring scalability improvements.
