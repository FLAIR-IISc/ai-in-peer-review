This paper introduces a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening frequency and delay in disease detection. The work tackles a complex problem with significant practical implications, particularly in diseases like breast cancer where early detection is crucial. The approach models disease progression as a finite state stochastic process, incorporates external information, and aims to provide a computationally tractable, approximately optimal policy.

The paper demonstrates a strong understanding of relevant literature across several fields – medical decision-making, operations research, and machine learning. It effectively positions its contribution by highlighting limitations in existing approaches, specifically their reliance on Markovian assumptions, stationary test outcomes, and inability to incorporate external information or handle computational intractability. The connection to prior work on POMDPs, survival models, and controlled sensing is clearly articulated.

A key strength of the work lies in its generality. The framework is applicable to a wide range of disease models, not just those restricted to Markovian processes. The inclusion of external information, such as patient self-examination, adds a layer of realism often missing in existing models. The development of a computationally tractable approximation algorithm is also a significant accomplishment, allowing application to practical scenarios. The theoretical guarantee on the approximation error, though relying on assumptions, provides confidence in the policy’s quality. The illustrative experiment using a large breast cancer dataset is well-executed, demonstrating substantial reductions in screening frequency while maintaining comparable delays in detection compared to existing clinical policies. The discussion of how the cost function impacts policy regimes is insightful. 

However, the paper could be strengthened in several areas. While the authors acknowledge the need for model estimation, the description of the estimation procedure using the breast cancer dataset feels somewhat cursory. More details on the MCMC methods used, validation of the estimated parameters, and sensitivity analyses to estimation error would be beneficial.  The assumption of no false positives in the test outcome seems strong and might not hold in all real-world scenarios. Exploring the impact of false positives on the policy would be valuable. The complexity analysis mentions parallelization but doesn't quantify the speedup achieved through parallel processing. Providing empirical results on computational performance would be helpful. 

The discussion on robustness and the impact of estimation error, while theoretically sound (Theorem 1), could be more concrete.  Providing specific examples of how the policy changes with different levels of estimation error would be insightful.  The section on “Personalization” primarily relies on visual representation. While the figures are illustrative, a quantitative analysis demonstrating the degree of personalization (e.g., using metrics like entropy or variance of screening frequency across patient groups) would be more compelling. 

Finally, the notation is sometimes dense and could benefit from simplification. Defining abbreviations earlier in the text would improve readability.  The organization of the Appendix could be improved for easier navigation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The framework addresses a practically important problem with a novel approach that extends existing methodologies.
*   Generality: The applicability to a broad class of disease models and the incorporation of external information are significant advantages.
*   Theoretical Soundness: The approximation guarantees and robustness analysis provide a strong theoretical foundation.
*   Empirical Validation: The results on the breast cancer dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed policy.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited detail on model estimation and validation.
*   Strong assumptions (e.g., no false positives) that may limit real-world applicability.
*   Lack of quantitative analysis of personalization.
*   Dense notation and potentially confusing organization of supplemental material.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of personalized screening. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the work has the potential to impact clinical practice.  Addressing the suggested improvements, particularly regarding model estimation details and quantitative analysis of personalization, would further strengthen the paper.  A qualified acceptance is recommended, contingent on addressing these minor concerns.
