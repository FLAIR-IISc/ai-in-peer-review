- Proposes DPSCREEN: a framework for dynamic, personalized screening that trades off test frequency and detection delay using a belief-state DP with endogenous decision epochs and trajectory-dependent delay costs.  
- Shows the DP value function is convex and piecewise-linear, but exact alpha-vector solutions are intractable; develops a point-based PBVI-style approximation sampling likely trajectories and beliefs.  
- Provides a bound on approximation error as a function of sampling density and a robustness result showing local constancy of optimal actions under small model perturbations.  
- Empirical evaluation on a large breast cancer (Athena) dataset using a two-state Markov disease model and self-exam model shows 28–68% fewer screenings at comparable expected delay versus annual/biennial screening.  
- Demonstrates how different delay cost shapes (linear vs quadratic) affect screening aggressiveness and provides qualitative insights on personalization to risk/history.  
- Claims novelty in handling more general disease/test/external-information models, endogenous arrivals, and trajectory-dependent delay costs compared to prior POMDP and simulation-based screening work.  
- Strength: addresses an important practical problem with a principled sequential-decision approach accommodating external information and non-Markov trajectories.  
- Strength: clear separation of modeling, optimization, approximation, and evaluation; appropriate use of belief-structure and PBVI-style methods.  
- Strength: intuitive, computationally practical belief-construction heuristic via sampling plausible trajectories and reachable beliefs.  
- Strength: large real-data illustration with clinically relevant baselines and notable screening reductions.  
- Strength: robustness theorem provides conceptual reassurance about action stability to small model changes.  
- Weakness: theoretical error-bound assumptions are strong (no misclassification, bounded intervals, large horizon, convex delay form) and misaligned with empirical use of imperfect tests.  
- Weakness: bound depends on unspecified constants (ζ, Ω(B̄)), making practical interpretation/use difficult.  
- Weakness: empirical experiments use a simple two-state Markov model and threshold self-exam model, not stress-testing non-Markov/high-dimensional dynamics the paper claims to handle.  
- Weakness: coarse personalization in experiments (two Gail-risk bins); unclear how per-patient features x scale in belief sampling and policy construction.  
- Weakness: limited ablations/sensitivity analyses on the value of external information, self-exam threshold/noise, K, |B̄|, W, and model misspecification.  
- Weakness: validation of learned models is limited — no clear train/test split, calibration checks, uncertainty quantification, or off-policy evaluation safeguards.  
- Weakness: computational practicality claims lack runtime, memory, convergence diagnostics, and sensitivity studies to algorithmic hyperparameters.  
- Weakness: some modeling inconsistencies regarding test accuracy and how diagnostic certainty/misclassification are handled in the DP.  
- Quality: technically plausible at modeling/approximation level but theoretical guarantees hinge on restrictive assumptions and deferred algorithmic details/proofs.  
- Clarity: generally well written but heavy notation and many algorithms in the appendix; inconsistent treatment of test accuracy and limited hyperparameter guidance.  
- Originality: moderately high — integrating endogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent delay costs into a POMDP-like screening framework and engineering PBVI with trajectory belief sampling is novel for this area.  
- Significance: potentially significant for personalized screening and controlled sensing, though generality claims need broader empirical validation.  
- Suggested improvements: add ablations (with/without external info), sensitivity analyses, relax/no-misclassification assumption or separate diagnostic modeling, clarify per-feature policy handling, provide computational benchmarks, compare to recent personalized systems, and release code/pseudocode.  
- Acceptance arguments: important problem, principled framework, promising empirical savings, and novel integration of external information and trajectory costs.  
- Rejection arguments: theoretical guarantees rely on unrealistic assumptions, empirical validation is limited, scalability and sensitivity underexplored, and some modeling/clarity issues remain.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept; Reviewer confidence: Medium.
