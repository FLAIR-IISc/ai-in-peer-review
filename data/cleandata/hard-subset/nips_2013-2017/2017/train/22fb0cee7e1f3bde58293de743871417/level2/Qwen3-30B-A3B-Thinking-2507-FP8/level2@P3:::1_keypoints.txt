- Proposes a framework for personalized screening policies that trade off screening frequency against delay in disease detection.  
- Models disease as a finite-state stochastic process with a single absorbing disease state and includes external information (e.g., patient self-exams) that can trigger earlier visits.  
- Exact optimal policy is computationally intractable; authors develop a point-based value iteration approximation with theoretical error bounds.  
- Empirical evaluation on a breast cancer dataset shows 28–68% reduction in screening frequency while maintaining the same expected detection delay as current clinical policies.  
- Framework is general and applies to a wide class of disease models, not limited to Markov assumptions used in prior work.  
- Derives theoretical properties of the value function and provides a bound on approximation error, strengthening the technical contribution.  
- Experimental design and metrics are well-chosen and demonstrate substantial improvements over clinical practice.  
- Theoretical development is rigorous and supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility.  
- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, with consistent notation and an effective graphical illustration of personalization.  
- Originality is high: accommodates non-Markovian disease dynamics and incorporates external information processes.  
- Significance is substantial: potential to reduce screening costs while preserving detection quality; highlights impact of different cost functions.  
- Limitation: restriction to a single absorbing disease state may limit applicability to diseases with multiple progression paths or competing risks.  
- Computational complexity analysis (O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)) is somewhat vague and needs more intuition about real-world scaling.  
- Lacks detailed discussion of practical implementation challenges in healthcare settings and model estimation issues.  
- Evaluation is limited to breast cancer; additional disease examples would better demonstrate broader applicability.
