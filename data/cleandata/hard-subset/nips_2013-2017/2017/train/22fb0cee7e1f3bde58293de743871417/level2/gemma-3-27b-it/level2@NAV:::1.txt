## Review of DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for constructing personalized screening policies for a broad class of disease models. The core idea – modeling disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process and optimizing screening schedules based on patient features and screening history – is a valuable contribution. The authors address a critical gap in existing screening literature, which often relies on restrictive Markovian assumptions and doesn't fully account for dynamic patient information. The problem is well-motivated, with a clear explanation of its complexity, and the proposed approach offers a computationally tractable solution leveraging point-based value iteration.

The strengths of the paper lie in its generality and practical relevance. The framework isn't tied to a specific disease, making it broadly applicable. The demonstration using a large breast cancer dataset is compelling, showing significant reductions in screening frequency (28-68%) while maintaining comparable delay times compared to clinical practices. The consideration of external information (self-exams) and the discussion of robustness to model estimation errors further enhance the practicality of the work. The analysis demonstrating the impact of different cost functions on the resulting policies is insightful, highlighting the importance of careful cost modeling.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the computational complexity is discussed, a more detailed breakdown, especially regarding scalability to higher dimensional state spaces and larger datasets, would be beneficial. The estimation of model parameters (pin(x) and ptran(x)) using MCMC is mentioned, but further details on convergence diagnostics and sensitivity analysis would increase confidence in the results.  The assumption of no false positives in the test outcomes feels limiting, and exploring its impact would be worthwhile.  Finally, the presentation could be streamlined; the inclusion of multiple sections referencing the Appendix (e.g., Algorithm 1, Theorem 1) breaks the flow and hinders readability.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and practically relevant problem in personalized medicine.
*   Offers a novel and generalizable framework for dynamic screening policies.
*   Demonstrates promising results on a real-world dataset, showing substantial improvements over existing methods.
*   Acknowledges limitations and discusses robustness to model uncertainty.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Computational complexity details could be more thorough.
*   Parameter estimation details are limited, raising concerns about reproducibility.
*   The assumption of no false positives may limit applicability.
*   The presentation is somewhat fragmented due to heavy reliance on the appendix.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field. Despite the minor weaknesses, the novelty, generality, and practical relevance of DPSCREEN outweigh its shortcomings. I recommend acceptance, with a request for the authors to address the suggested improvements during a potential revision phase. The potential impact of this work on improving healthcare outcomes is significant.
