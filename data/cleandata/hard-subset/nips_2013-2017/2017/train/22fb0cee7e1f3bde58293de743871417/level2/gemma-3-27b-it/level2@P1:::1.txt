This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for constructing personalized screening policies, addressing a critical need in disease diagnosis and treatment. The core idea – modeling disease progression as a finite state stochastic process and optimizing screening schedules considering patient features, screening history, and potential external information – is well-motivated and tackles a complex problem. The approach is particularly strong in its generality, aiming to be applicable to a broad class of disease models, and its consideration of computational tractability.

The paper is well-structured, starting with a clear introduction that highlights the limitations of existing screening policies and motivating the need for personalization. The model formulation is comprehensive, accounting for disease state, external information, patient arrival, and test outcomes. The proposed approach tackles the computational intractability by constructing an approximately optimal policy, and importantly, provides a bound on the approximation error. The illustrative experiments on breast cancer data are convincing, demonstrating significant reductions in screening frequency while maintaining comparable delays in detection compared to current clinical practices. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Significance:** The framework represents a significant advancement in personalized screening, moving beyond population-based strategies. Addressing the computational challenges inherent in optimal policy construction is a key contribution.
* **Generality:** The model's applicability to a wide range of diseases and disease models (dynamic survival, first hitting time) is a notable strength.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The provision of approximation error bounds adds to the rigor and trustworthiness of the proposed approach.
* **Empirical Validation:** The results on the breast cancer dataset are compelling, showcasing the practical benefits of the personalized policies.  The comparison against existing clinical policies is well-defined and highlights the potential for improved efficiency.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the complex concepts and mathematical formulations.  The discussion of personalization with examples is particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Model Complexity & Assumptions:** While the model's generality is a strength, it also introduces complexity. The numerous assumptions (e.g., independence of test results given state and features, limited state space) are not fully justified or discussed in terms of their potential impact on the results.  A sensitivity analysis evaluating the impact of these assumptions would strengthen the work.
* **Computational Cost:** Although the paper addresses tractability, the computational complexity remains substantial (O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)). This might limit the applicability of the method to very large datasets or complex disease models. Further investigation into scalability and potential optimizations would be valuable.
* **External Information Model:** The model for external information (patient self-examination) seems relatively simplistic. The assumption of a zero-mean white noise process for the lump size growth might not accurately reflect real-world scenarios. Exploring more sophisticated models for external information could improve the policy’s performance.
* **Cost Function:** The choice of a linear delay cost function, while reasonable, is not rigorously justified. The impact of different cost functions (e.g., quadratic) on the policy is explored briefly, but a more comprehensive analysis could be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison:** While comparisons with existing policies are made, expanding the comparison to other personalized screening approaches (if any) would further highlight the advantages of DPSCREEN.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a significant and challenging problem in healthcare.
* The proposed framework is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated.
* The results demonstrate the potential for substantial improvements in screening efficiency.
* The clarity of the writing and organization make the paper accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The model complexity and underlying assumptions require further justification and sensitivity analysis.
* The computational cost remains a concern for large-scale applications.
* The external information model could benefit from further refinement.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential impact.  The proposed DPSCREEN framework offers a valuable contribution to the field of personalized screening. While some weaknesses remain, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding model assumptions and computational cost in a revised version. Specifically, I suggest adding a discussion of the sensitivity of the results to the model assumptions, providing a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity, and potentially exploring methods for improving scalability.
