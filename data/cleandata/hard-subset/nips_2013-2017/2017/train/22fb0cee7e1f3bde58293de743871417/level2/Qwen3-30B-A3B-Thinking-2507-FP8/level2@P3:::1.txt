

The paper proposes a framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance screening frequency against delay in disease detection. The authors model the disease as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating external information (such as patient self-examinations) that can trigger earlier visits to the clinician. Recognizing that computing the exact optimal policy is computationally intractable, the authors develop a point-based value iteration approach to construct an approximately optimal policy with theoretical error bounds. Evaluated on a breast cancer dataset, the approach reduces screening frequency by 28-68% while maintaining the same expected delay in disease detection compared to current clinical policies.

The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a general framework applicable to a wide class of disease models, unlike previous works that rely on specific assumptions (e.g., Markov disease processes). The authors derive theoretical properties of the value function and provide a bound on the approximation error, which strengthens the technical contribution. The experimental evaluation on breast cancer data is well-designed, with clear metrics showing substantial improvements over clinical practice.

Quality is strong: The theoretical development is sound, with rigorous derivation of the value function properties and error bounds. The experimental design appropriately addresses the challenges of evaluating screening policies using observational data. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., the single absorbing disease state restriction) and discusses robustness to model estimation errors. The supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Clarity is excellent: The paper is well-organized with clear explanations of the mathematical framework. The graphical representation of personalization (Figure 1) effectively illustrates how the policy adapts to patient features and history. The notation is consistent and well-defined throughout.

Originality is high: The framework extends beyond previous work by accommodating non-Markovian disease processes and incorporating external information. Previous approaches (e.g., [7], [9], [10]) are restricted to specific disease models, while this work provides a more general approach applicable to many disease models including dynamic survival processes. The incorporation of external information processes (e.g., patient self-examinations) into the screening policy represents a meaningful extension.

Significance is substantial: The results demonstrate clear practical benefits, with potential to significantly reduce screening costs while maintaining detection quality. The paper shows how different cost functions (linear vs. quadratic) lead to different screening policies, highlighting the importance of appropriate cost function selection for different diseases. This work could inform clinical guidelines and improve resource allocation in healthcare systems.

A minor limitation is the restriction to a single absorbing disease state, which the authors acknowledge as an exposition convenience. While the paper addresses this limitation in the discussion, it would strengthen the work to provide more concrete discussion of how this limitation might impact application to diseases with multiple progression paths or competing risks.

The computational complexity analysis (O(T(B)^2T^2K|Y||Z|)) is somewhat vague; providing more intuition for how this scales with realistic parameters would improve clarity. The paper mentions model estimation challenges but doesn't discuss practical implementation considerations for healthcare settings, which would strengthen the significance argument.

The evaluation focuses exclusively on breast cancer; including results from another disease example would better demonstrate the framework's broader applicability. The paper mentions this as future work but would benefit from some discussion of how the framework might adapt to different disease characteristics.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses an important practical problem with significant theoretical and empirical merit. The framework provides a promising path toward personalized screening policies that could substantially improve healthcare resource allocation while maintaining detection quality. The paper's strengths in theoretical development, empirical validation, and practical significance outweigh the minor limitations noted.
