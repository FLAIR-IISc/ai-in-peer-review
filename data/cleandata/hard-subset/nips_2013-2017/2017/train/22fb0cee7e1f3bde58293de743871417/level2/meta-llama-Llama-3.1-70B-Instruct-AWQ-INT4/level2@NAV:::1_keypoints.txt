- Introduces DPSCREEN, a method for personalized screening that trades off screening cost against delay cost in disease detection.  
- Models disease as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and allows external information (e.g., self-exam, comorbidities) to trigger visits.  
- Develops a computationally efficient procedure to compute an approximately optimal screening policy applicable to many disease models.  
- Provides theoretical bounds on the approximation error from using the approximate policy versus the exact optimal policy.  
- Validates the approach on a large breast cancer dataset, showing large reductions in screenings while maintaining detection delay.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and contains a thorough literature review that clarifies limitations of prior work.  
- Methodology is sound and empirical results are impressive.  
- Strength: novel framework for balancing screening cost and detection delay.  
- Strength: provision of approximation-error bounds.  
- Strength: real-world demonstration on extensive breast cancer data.  
- Strength: comprehensive review and clear exposition.  
- Weakness: methodological complexity may impede practical implementation.  
- Weakness: assumes model parameters are known, which may be unrealistic in practice.  
- Weakness: primary focus on breast cancer limits demonstrated generalizability to other diseases.  
- Suggestion: include more implementation details (software, languages, practical steps).  
- Suggestion: address parameter-uncertainty and provide guidance on estimating model parameters.  
- Suggestion: extend experiments to other diseases to demonstrate generalizability.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions; overall rating 8/10.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, sound methodology with strong empirical results on real data.  
- Rejection arguments: complexity, unrealistic parameter assumptions, and limited scope.
