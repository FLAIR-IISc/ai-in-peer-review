- Presents DPSCREEN, a general framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies modeled as a generalized POMDP.  
- Key novelty: handles arbitrary finite-state stochastic disease processes, trajectory-dependent costs, and external information triggers.  
- Uses Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) with representative trajectory sampling to obtain a tractable approximate solution.  
- Extends and generalizes prior POMDP screening work (e.g., Ayer et al. 2011) by removing Markov and state-cost restrictions.  
- Theoretical contributions include an approximation error bound (Proposition 1) and robustness analysis to model estimation error (Theorem 1).  
- Empirical results on a large real-world breast cancer dataset show 28–68% fewer screenings vs. annual screening while maintaining expected detection delay.  
- Strength: high originality and significance—more realistic modeling of disease screening leading to potential cost and burden reductions.  
- Strength: technically sound formulation and principled approximation approach with theoretical guarantees.  
- Strength: paper is very clear, well-organized, and includes intuitive illustrations of personalization.  
- Strength: strong empirical evaluation using learned disease models and appropriate clinical baselines.  
- Weakness: reliance on sampling K trajectories—sensitivity to K and risk of missing rare critical trajectories needs discussion and ablation.  
- Weakness: practical computational cost unclear—actual run-times for experiments should be reported.  
- Weakness: Proposition 1 assumes convex delay cost; need comment on restrictiveness and behavior with non-convex costs (e.g., threshold/sigmoidal).  
- Overall: excellent, high-impact paper with minor clarifications requested; recommendation: accept.
