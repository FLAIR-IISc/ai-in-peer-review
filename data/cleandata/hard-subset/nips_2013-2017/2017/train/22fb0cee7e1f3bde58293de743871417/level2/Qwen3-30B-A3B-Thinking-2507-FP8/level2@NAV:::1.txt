

This paper presents a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that dynamically adapt to patient features and historical screening data. The main claim is that the authors have developed the first computationally tractable method for constructing approximately optimal screening policies for a broad class of disease models, which incorporate external information (e.g., self-examinations) and patient history.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by modeling disease as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing state, and developing a value iteration-based approach (DPSCREEN) with provable approximation error bounds. The experimental validation on a large breast cancer dataset (45,000 patients) demonstrates significant reductions (28-68%) in screening frequency while maintaining equivalent expected delay in disease detection compared to current clinical policies. The results are well-supported by statistical evidence and comparative analysis against standard practices.

Strengths include the comprehensive theoretical foundation, clear demonstration of practical utility with real-world data, and thoughtful analysis of how the policy personalizes to different patient risk profiles. The authors provide a useful comparison of linear vs. quadratic delay cost functions, highlighting how policy recommendations vary based on disease characteristics.

A notable limitation is the assumption of a single absorbing disease state, which may not capture all disease progression patterns (though the authors acknowledge this as a simplification). While the framework is demonstrated on breast cancer, broader validation across diverse disease models would strengthen generalizability claims.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient technical detail for reproducibility. The related work section thoroughly contextualizes the contribution against previous approaches, highlighting the novelty in relaxing restrictive assumptions about disease models.

This work makes a significant contribution to the application of sequential decision making in healthcare. The technical quality is high, the significance is clear through substantial reductions in unnecessary screenings, and the approach advances the state of the art in personalized screening. The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
