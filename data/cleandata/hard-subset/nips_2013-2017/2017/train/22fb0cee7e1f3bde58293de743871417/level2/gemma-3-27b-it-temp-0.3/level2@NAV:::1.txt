## Review of DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening

This paper presents a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for constructing personalized screening policies that dynamically adjust to patient features and screening history. The core contribution lies in developing a computationally tractable approach to optimize screening schedules for diseases modeled as finite-state stochastic processes, incorporating external information and acknowledging the inherent difficulty of balancing detection delay and screening frequency. The work addresses a critical gap in existing literature, which often relies on restrictive assumptions like Markovian disease progression and stationary test outcomes.

The authors clearly articulate the problem and its complexities, drawing connections to relevant work in decision theory, operations research, and medical literature. The proposed solution leverages point-based value iteration to approximate the optimal policy, and importantly, provides a bound on the approximation error. The application to breast cancer screening, using a large dataset, demonstrates the potential of DPSCREEN to reduce screening frequency (28-68%) while maintaining equivalent detection delays compared to current clinical practices. The analysis of different cost functions (linear vs. quadratic) highlights the impact of disease characteristics on optimal policy design.

A key strength is the acknowledgement of estimation error in the disease model and the demonstration that the policy remains robust under reasonable estimation inaccuracies. The personalization aspect, illustrated with a clear example, convincingly shows how the policy adapts to individual risk profiles and test results. The paper is well-written and logically structured, with a thorough discussion of related work.

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost associated with the point-based value iteration, particularly as it scales with the size of the state space. While the authors mention parallelization, a more concrete analysis of runtime performance on the breast cancer dataset would be valuable. Furthermore, the assumption of a single absorbing disease state, while simplifying the model, might limit its applicability to certain diseases. Exploring extensions to multi-state models could be a direction for future work. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and practically relevant problem in personalized medicine.
*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for dynamic screening policy optimization.
*   Demonstrates promising results on a real-world dataset, showing substantial improvements over existing practices.
*   Acknowledges and addresses potential limitations, such as estimation error.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Computational complexity could be a barrier to application for very large state spaces.
*   The single absorbing state assumption may limit the model's generality.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The proposed framework is a valuable contribution to the field of personalized screening and offers a promising avenue for improving healthcare outcomes. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised regarding computational cost and model generality. The authors should be encouraged to resubmit a revised version.
