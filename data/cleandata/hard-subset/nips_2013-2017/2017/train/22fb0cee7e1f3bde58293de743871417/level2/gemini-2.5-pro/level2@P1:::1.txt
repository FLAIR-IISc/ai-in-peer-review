This paper presents DPSCREEN, a novel and general framework for constructing dynamic, personalized screening policies for diseases. The authors model the problem as a generalized Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP). A key contribution is the generality of the formulation, which can accommodate arbitrary finite-state stochastic disease processes (not limited to Markovian dynamics), trajectory-dependent costs that reflect the delay in detection, and an external information process (e.g., patient self-examination) that can trigger unscheduled clinical visits. Recognizing that solving this problem exactly is computationally intractable, the paper proposes a tractable, approximately optimal solution based on Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI). The PBVI algorithm is adapted to the high-dimensional belief space by sampling a set of representative disease trajectories and then exploring the space of reachable beliefs under various policies. This work builds upon, and significantly generalizes, prior research that applied POMDPs to screening (e.g., Ayer et al., Operations Research 2011), which were typically restricted to Markovian models with state-dependent costs. The authors demonstrate their framework's effectiveness on a large, real-world breast cancer dataset, showing that their derived policies can reduce the number of screenings by 28-68% compared to standard annual screening, while maintaining the same expected delay in detection.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of personalized screening. The primary strength is the generality of the proposed framework. By moving beyond the restrictive Markovian assumptions of prior work and incorporating features like trajectory-dependent costs and external information triggers, the authors have developed a model that can more realistically capture the complexities of many disease screening scenarios. The experimental results are highly significant, suggesting that the proposed policies could lead to substantial reductions in healthcare costs and patient burden without compromising clinical outcomes. This work represents a clear advance in the state-of-the-art for applying decision-theoretic methods to medicine.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the screening problem as a generalized POMDP is appropriate and well-defined. The use of a PBVI algorithm to find an approximate solution is a well-motivated and principled approach to handling the intractability of the exact solution. The theoretical contributions, including the bound on the approximation error (Proposition 1) and the robustness analysis with respect to model estimation error (Theorem 1), add rigor and credibility to the proposed method.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, clearly defining their model and assumptions, and logically presenting their proposed solution. The distinction between the exact (intractable) and approximate (tractable) solutions is laid out clearly. Figure 1 provides an excellent and intuitive illustration of how the policy personalizes to both static features and dynamic history.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is strong and convincing. The use of a large, real-world dataset for breast cancer screening makes the results compelling. The authors follow a standard and appropriate evaluation methodology: learning a disease model from the data and then evaluating the policy's performance on that model. The comparisons to established clinical baselines (annual screening) are well-chosen and effectively highlight the benefits of the DPSCREEN policy.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Scalability and Sensitivity of Trajectory Sampling:** The core of the approximation method relies on sampling K physiological trajectories to reduce the dimensionality of the belief space. The performance of the resulting policy likely depends on this sample. Could the authors provide some discussion on the sensitivity of the policy's performance to the number of sampled trajectories, K? It would be valuable to understand if there is a risk of missing rare but critical trajectories and how one might choose K in practice. An ablation study showing performance vs. K in the experimental section would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Practical Computational Cost:** While the paper provides a worst-case complexity analysis, it would be very helpful to report the actual run-times for the experiments conducted. This would give readers a more concrete sense of the computational resources required to apply this method to new problems, which may involve more states, a longer time horizon, or a larger action space.

3.  **Convexity Assumption for Delay Cost:** Proposition 1, which bounds the approximation error, assumes that the delay cost function `c(d)` is convex. The experiments use a linear cost, which satisfies this. Could the authors comment on how restrictive this assumption is? Are there important diseases or clinical scenarios where a non-convex cost function (e.g., a sigmoidal cost where small delays have little impact but there is a sharp penalty after a certain threshold) would be more appropriate, and how might the approach be affected in such cases?

**Summary of pros and cons:**

*   **Pros:**
    *   Highly novel and general framework for personalized screening, significantly advancing beyond prior work.
    *   Technically sound formulation and principled approximation algorithm with theoretical support.
    *   Extremely promising empirical results on a large real-world dataset, demonstrating significant practical value.
    *   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

*   **Cons:**
    *   Some questions remain about the practical scalability and sensitivity of the trajectory sampling approach.
    *   The paper would benefit from reporting concrete computational times for the experiments.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to both the machine learning and computational health domains. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which can be addressed with additional clarification. The work is original, technically strong, clearly presented, and has the potential for high impact. I strongly recommend acceptance.
