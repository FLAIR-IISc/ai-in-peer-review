The paper "DPSCREEN: Dynamic Personalized Screening" proposes a novel framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease. The authors model the disease as an arbitrary finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and incorporate the possibility of external information that may trigger arrival of the patient to the clinician in advance of a scheduled screening appointment.

The authors provide a clear and concise summary of the problem, and their proposed approach is well-motivated and well-explained. The use of a large breast cancer data set to illustrate the proposed personalized screening policy is a strength of the paper. The results show that the constructed policy leads to large reductions (28-68%) in the number of screens performed while achieving the same expected delays in disease detection.

The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise. The authors provide a good balance of technical details and high-level explanations, making the paper accessible to a broad audience.

The main contribution of the paper is the development of a computationally tractable construction of an approximately optimal policy for personalized screening. The authors provide a clear explanation of the challenges in solving the problem exactly and propose a point-based value iteration (PBVI) approximation algorithm to compute an approximately optimal policy.

The paper also discusses the robustness of the proposed approach to estimation errors and provides a theoretical analysis of the impact of estimation errors on the exactly optimal policy.

The authors compare their approach to existing frameworks in decision theory and operations research, highlighting the limitations of these frameworks in addressing the personalized screening problem.

The paper has several strengths, including:

* A clear and concise summary of the problem and proposed approach
* A well-motivated and well-explained proposed approach
* A strong empirical evaluation using a large breast cancer data set
* A good balance of technical details and high-level explanations

However, the paper also has some limitations:

* The authors assume that the model parameters are known, which may not be the case in practice. While they provide a discussion on the robustness of the proposed approach to estimation errors, more work is needed to address this issue.
* The paper focuses on breast cancer, and it is unclear how the proposed approach would generalize to other diseases.
* The authors do not provide a detailed comparison of their approach to other personalized screening models, such as those based on Bayesian design or controlled sensing.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of personalized screening and provides a promising approach for balancing the cost of screening against the cost of delay in detection of disease.
