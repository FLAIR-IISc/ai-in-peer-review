Summary
The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off test frequency and delay in disease detection. The disease is modeled as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state; an “external information” process (e.g., self-exam signals) can trigger earlier-than-scheduled visits. The decision variable is the next screening time given patient features and history. The authors formulate a belief-state dynamic program whose value function is shown to be convex and piecewise linear (as in POMDPs), but the decision epochs are endogenous due to arrivals, and costs depend on entire trajectories via delay. Exact solution via alpha-vectors is intractable; they develop a point-based approximation that samples likely state trajectories and reachable beliefs, and provide a bound on the approximation error as a function of the sampling density. They also provide a robustness result showing local constancy of the optimal action under small model perturbations. Using a large breast cancer dataset (Athena), they estimate a two-state Markov disease model and an external lump-growth/self-exam model, and show substantially fewer screenings (28–68%) at comparable expected delay relative to annual or biennial screening. They illustrate how costs (linear vs quadratic) lead to different screening aggressiveness.

Relation to prior work
This work relates to POMDP-based screening (Ayer et al., Maillart et al.), Bayesian design approaches (Rizopoulos et al.; Alaa et al.), and simulation-based policy comparisons used in medical guideline development (CISNET, MISCAN-COLON). The main claimed differences are: (i) more general disease/test/external information models (not restricted to stationary Markov or i.i.d. outcomes), (ii) endogenous arrivals via external information, (iii) delay costs that depend on trajectory rather than current state. The POMDP structural result (piecewise linear convexity) is standard but its extension to the present setting (endogenous decision epochs and trajectory-dependent costs) is nontrivial. Compared to medical simulations, DPSCREEN optimizes over a large policy class rather than comparing a fixed menu.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important problem (personalized screening) with a principled sequential decision framework that accommodates external information and non-Markov disease trajectories.
- Clear separation of modeling, optimization, approximation, and evaluation; value function structure and PBVI-style approximation are appropriate choices.
- The belief-construction heuristic (sampling plausible trajectories and reachable beliefs) is intuitive and computationally practical.
- Empirical illustration on a large real dataset with clinically relevant baselines; sizable reductions in screenings at matched expected delay.
- Useful qualitative insights (e.g., sensitivity to delay cost shape; policy personalization to risk and history).
- Robustness theorem about local constancy of the optimal action under small model perturbations is conceptually reassuring.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Key theoretical assumptions for the approximation error bound (no false positives/negatives; bounded max screening interval; large horizon; convex delay of a specific form) are strong and misaligned with the empirical model, where mammography has imperfect sensitivity/specificity and biopsies can produce discordant outcomes. The bound depends on an unspecified constant ζ and a sampling-density metric Ω(B̄); as stated, it is difficult to interpret or use in practice.
- The empirical demonstration uses a simple two-state Markov disease model and a threshold self-exam model; the paper claims wide generality, but experiments do not stress-test the method on non-Markov or higher-dimensional state processes, or alternative external information dynamics.
- Personalization to patient features is coarse in the experiments (two risk groups based on Gail risk). It is unclear whether the algorithm scales to per-patient-feature policies or how x is handled during belief sampling across a heterogeneous cohort.
- The endogeneity of arrivals is central, but evaluation does not include ablations showing the incremental value of incorporating external information versus not (beyond a brief comparison), nor a sensitivity analysis to the self-exam threshold ỹ and noise σ.
- Validation of the learned disease/exam models is limited. There is no clear train/test split, calibration checks, or uncertainty quantification; policy performance is evaluated on the learned model, which can be optimistic without off-policy evaluation safeguards.
- Computational practicality is only sketched (complexity expression and parallelizability). There are no runtime, memory, or convergence diagnostics, nor sensitivity to K, |B̄|, or maximum interval constraints W.
- Some modeling choices are inconsistent: earlier, the model allows no false positives; later, the experimental Z includes “mammogram positive, biopsy negative,” and sensitivity/specificity are used. Clarify when diagnostic certainty is achieved and how misclassification is handled in the DP.

Quality
Technically sound overall at the modeling/approximation level, but the main bound relies on restrictive assumptions and introduces constants that limit practical value. The algorithmic description is plausible but key algorithmic details, proofs, and complexity reductions are deferred to the supplement. Empirical support is promising but would benefit from stronger validation and sensitivity analyses.

Clarity
Generally well written, though notation is heavy and the main algorithms are in the appendix. The role of the recommendation plan τ versus the policy, and the handling of external arrivals, are conceptually clear. Some inconsistencies around test accuracy and diagnosis should be resolved. More explicit guidance on hyperparameters (K, |B̄|, W) and implementation choices would aid reproducibility.

Originality
Moderately high. Bringing endogenous arrivals and trajectory-dependent delay costs into a POMDP-like screening framework, and engineering a PBVI variant with belief sampling over state-trajectories, are novel for this application area.

Significance
Potentially significant for personalized screening and controlled sensing with endogenous observations. The demonstrated savings at matched delay are compelling, though the generality claims would be stronger with broader empirical tests and more robust validation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations: with/without external information; varying K, |B̄|, W; effect sizes on performance and runtime.
- Add sensitivity analyses to model misspecification (e.g., bootstrap over parameter draws), self-exam threshold/noise, and delay cost shapes; report uncertainty bands.
- Align theory and practice by relaxing the “no misclassification” assumption in bounds, or clearly separating diagnostic testing from screening with realistic false positive/negative modeling.
- Clarify how x is used in policy construction: per-feature policies vs risk bins; report results at finer granularity if feasible.
- Report computational benchmarks (runtime, memory, parallelization) and convergence behavior.
- Compare against recent personalized screening systems (e.g., ConfidentCare) beyond annual/biennial schedules, and against existing POMDP-based policies on their respective models.
- Release code or detailed pseudocode with parameter settings to enhance reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Important problem with a principled, general framework and a practically motivated algorithm.
- Promising empirical results demonstrating substantial resource savings without sacrificing timeliness.
- Novel integration of external information and trajectory-dependent costs.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical guarantees rely on unrealistic assumptions and are not empirically instantiated.
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope and validation rigor; scalability and sensitivity are underexplored.
- Some modeling inconsistencies and clarity issues.

Recommendation: Weak accept
