- Introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing personalized screening policies that trade off screening costs against delay in disease detection.  
- Models disease progression as a finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state and incorporates external signals (e.g., self-exams) that can trigger visits.  
- Main technical contribution: a computationally tractable method to construct approximately optimal screening policies with provable bounds on approximation error.  
- Theoretical results include proof that the value function is convex and piecewise-linear and a bound on approximation error (Proposition 1).  
- Empirical demonstration on a large breast cancer dataset showing 28–68% reductions in screenings versus current clinical policies while maintaining expected detection delay.  
- Generalizes prior personalized screening work by allowing dynamic survival models, non-stationary test outcomes, and external information processes.  
- Well-organized, clearly written paper with sufficient supplementary material for reproducibility.  
- Strong experimental validation: realistic breast cancer progression modeling, parameter validation against clinical literature, and meaningful comparisons to annual screening.  
- Shows that the choice of delay cost function (linear vs. quadratic) materially changes optimal screening regimes—important practical insight.  
- Main limitations: assumption of no false positives in tests, potential computational challenges at very large scales, and empirical evaluation limited to breast cancer.  
- Addresses review criteria well: high quality, clarity, originality, and significant potential impact on healthcare and ML research.  
- Recommendation: accept; strong candidate for NIPS 2017 with meaningful theoretical and practical contributions.
