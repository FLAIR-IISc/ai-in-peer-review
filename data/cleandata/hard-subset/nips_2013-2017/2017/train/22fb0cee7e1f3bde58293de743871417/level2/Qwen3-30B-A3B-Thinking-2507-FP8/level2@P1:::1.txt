

This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a framework for constructing personalized screening policies that balance the cost of screenings against the delay in disease detection. The authors model disease progression as a finite state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state, incorporating external information (e.g., patient self-examinations) that can trigger early visits to clinicians. The key contribution is a computationally tractable method to construct approximately optimal screening policies, with bounds on the approximation error. The authors demonstrate their approach using a large breast cancer dataset, showing significant reductions (28-68%) in screenings compared to current clinical policies while maintaining the same expected delay in disease detection.

The paper makes a significant contribution by generalizing previous work on personalized screening. As noted in Section 5, existing approaches [7,9,10] make restrictive assumptions about disease progression (e.g., requiring Markov processes), test outcome models (e.g., stationary i.i.d. processes), and the absence of external information. DPSCREEN's framework is more general, accommodating dynamic survival models, non-stationary test outcomes, and external information processes. The authors provide theoretical justification for their approach, including a proof that the value function is convex and piecewise linear, and a bound on the approximation error (Proposition 1).

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical development to empirical evaluation. The supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The experimental evaluation is particularly strong: the authors carefully model breast cancer progression using real-world data, validate their model parameters against clinical literature, and conduct meaningful comparisons against current clinical practices (annual screening). The results are compelling, with substantial reductions in screening frequency without compromising detection delays.

The paper also makes an important contribution by demonstrating how the choice of delay cost function (linear vs. quadratic) affects screening policies, showing that the optimal policy regime varies significantly with the cost structure. This insight is valuable for practitioners designing screening programs for different diseases with varying consequences of delayed detection.

The main limitations of the paper are: (1) The assumption of no false positives in test outcomes (stated in Section 2) may limit applicability to real-world medical tests, though the authors acknowledge this limitation. (2) The computational complexity of the approach, while theoretically tractable, might still present challenges for very large-scale implementations. (3) The empirical evaluation is limited to breast cancer; while the framework is general, more diverse disease examples would strengthen the argument for broader applicability.

The paper addresses all four NIPS review criteria effectively. The quality is high, with sound theoretical foundations and thorough experimental validation. The clarity is excellent, with well-structured writing and helpful figures. The originality is strong, as the framework significantly extends previous work on personalized screening. The significance is substantial, with clear potential for improving healthcare efficiency and outcomes.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a meaningful contribution to the intersection of healthcare and machine learning, addressing an important practical problem with a theoretically grounded and empirically validated approach. The paper is well-written, the claims are well-supported, and the results are compelling. The authors have clearly demonstrated the value of their approach through careful modeling and meaningful comparisons to current clinical practices.

The paper is a strong candidate for NIPS 2017. It advances the state of the art in personalized healthcare decision-making, with clear implications for both theoretical research and practical applications. The authors have carefully considered the limitations of their approach and provided a robust framework that can be adapted to various diseases and screening contexts.
