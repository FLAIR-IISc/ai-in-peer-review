Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes DPSCREEN, a framework for dynamic, personalized screening policies that trade off testing frequency against detection delay. Diseases are modeled as finite-state stochastic processes with an absorbing disease state, and patient-initiated early arrivals are captured via an external information process (e.g., self-exams). The optimization is a nonstandard POMDP-like problem with trajectory-dependent costs and endogenous decision epochs. The authors prove the value function is convex piecewise linear, derive an intractable exact solution via alpha-vectors, and introduce a tractable PBVI-style approximation based on sampling “typical” state trajectories to reduce belief dimensionality. They provide an approximation-error bound under strong assumptions and a robustness result showing local constancy of optimal actions under small model-estimation error. On a large breast-cancer dataset (Athena), DPSCREEN reportedly reduces tests by 28–68% versus annual/biennial screening while maintaining expected detection delay. The work builds on PBVI (Pineau et al.), controlled sensing (Krishnamurthy), and prior screening POMDPs (Ayer; Maillart) but aims for broader disease/test processes and trajectory-dependent delay costs; it relates to NIPS work on adaptive information acquisition (Alaa et al.) and disease trajectories.

Strengths:
- Ambitious, general modeling that explicitly accounts for external information and trajectory-dependent costs.
- Clear formulation of a challenging decision problem outside standard POMDP templates; extension of piecewise-linear value structure.
- Practical approximation with a principled belief-set construction; parallelizable.
- Illustrative application with sizable reported reductions in testing; personalization demonstrated across risk groups and histories.
- Discussion of different delay-cost shapes and their policy implications.

Weaknesses:
- Empirical evaluation relies on a learned simulator from observational data with no counterfactual validation; limited sensitivity analysis to misspecification despite a robustness theorem.
- Theoretical guarantees require unrealistic assumptions (no false positives/negatives, bounded intervals) and do not quantify constants; practical relevance of the bound is unclear.
- Limited baselines: no comparison to constrained-POMDP solvers, myopic/threshold/index heuristics, or learning-based off-policy policy optimization.
- Key estimation and implementation details are pushed to the supplement; reproducibility (code/data) not addressed; computational costs and scalability not benchmarked.
- “First framework” claim seems strong given prior POMDP screening and controlled-sensing literature; related work could be tightened with more recent RL/OPE methods.

Suggestions:
- Add off-policy evaluation (IPS/DR) and extensive sensitivity analyses (test characteristics, external-information noise, hazard misspecification).
- Compare against strong baselines (e.g., constrained PBVI, Whittle-style indices, myopic/threshold policies).
- Report runtime, scaling with T, K, and ablations on belief-set construction.
- Incorporate realistic test errors in theory or quantify empirical impact; clarify limits of assumptions.
- Release code and parameter estimates; moderate novelty claims.

Decision: Weak accept. The problem is important, the formulation and algorithmic pipeline are thoughtful, and the empirical gains are promising, though stronger validation and baselines would substantially strengthen the contribution.
