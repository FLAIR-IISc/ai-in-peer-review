- Introduces DPSCREEN, a general framework for computing approximately optimal, personalized, dynamic disease screening policies.
- Models disease progression as a general finite-state stochastic process and adds an external information process (e.g., self-exams) triggering unscheduled visits.
- Objective: minimize a weighted sum of screening costs and delay-in-detection costs.
- Exact solution is intractable; proposes a tractable approximation using Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) over sampled belief states.
- Extends prior POMDP-based screening models by relaxing Markovian assumptions and modeling realistic patient arrival processes.
- Strong empirical validation on a large real-world breast cancer dataset showing 28–68% fewer screenings for the same expected detection delay.
- Provides theoretical analysis on approximation error and robustness to model estimation errors.
- Paper is original, significant, technically sound, and clearly written.
- Weakness: main experiment uses a standard two-state Markov model; lacks demonstration on more complex non-Markovian models to validate generality.
- Weakness: reproducibility concerns—missing PBVI implementation details like number of belief points sampled and computational runtime.
