This paper proposes a novel framework, DPSCREEN, for constructing personalized screening policies, balancing screening frequency against the delay in disease detection. The core idea is to model disease progression as a finite state stochastic process and incorporate both external information and test results to dynamically adjust screening schedules based on individual patient characteristics and history. The authors address the computational intractability of finding optimal policies by developing a computationally tractable approximation and providing bounds on the approximation error. They illustrate the approach with a breast cancer dataset, demonstrating significant reductions in screening frequency while maintaining comparable detection delays compared to existing clinical policies.

The paper is well-motivated and tackles an important problem – moving beyond “one-size-fits-all” screening protocols. The formulation is general, allowing for various disease models and incorporating both patient features and dynamic history. The technical contributions, specifically the development of a tractable approximation algorithm and the robustness analysis concerning estimation error, are valuable.  The empirical results on the breast cancer dataset are promising, showing substantial improvements over current clinical practice. The inclusion of a discussion on the impact of different delay cost functions is insightful. The paper builds upon and extends existing work in POMDPs and screening frameworks by addressing limitations related to stationary assumptions and the inclusion of external information.  The connection to established clinical practice through comparison to existing policies is also a strength.

However, there are several weaknesses. First, the presentation is dense and the notation is heavy. While the technical details are necessary, improving clarity and readability would significantly benefit the paper. The explanations of some technical aspects, like the belief update process, could be more intuitive. Second, the assumption of a finite state space for both the disease process and the external information process might be limiting for some applications. While acknowledged, the potential impact of this simplification needs further discussion. Third, the reliance on the Gail model for initial risk estimation introduces potential biases inherent in that model. A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of different risk prediction models would be valuable.  Fourth, the computational complexity analysis is high-level and doesn't provide concrete performance estimates for realistic dataset sizes. Providing empirical runtimes would strengthen the argument for tractability. Fifth, while the theoretical guarantees on approximation error are valuable, the assumptions required for those guarantees to hold (e.g., convex delay cost) may not always be met in practice. Finally, the treatment of the self-examination component feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more detailed justification for the specific parameters used in the simulation is warranted.

Specifically, the jump between Lemma 1 and the PBVI approximation feels abrupt. A more detailed explanation of how the properties of the value function are exploited in the PBVI algorithm would improve understanding.  Similarly, the connection between Proposition 1 and practical implications for algorithm parameter tuning (e.g., the number of sampled trajectories) could be made clearer. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and practically relevant problem.
*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound framework.
*   Demonstrates promising empirical results on a real dataset.
*   Offers a significant improvement over existing clinical policies.
*   Provides robustness guarantees under model estimation error.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Dense and difficult to read due to heavy notation and technical detail.
*   Assumptions of finite state spaces may limit applicability.
*   Computational complexity analysis lacks concrete runtime estimates.
*   Sensitivity to initial risk assessment model.
*   Assumptions for approximation error bounds may not always hold.



**Overall assessment:**  I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. Despite the weaknesses, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and promising empirical results outweigh the drawbacks.  I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the clarity and computational complexity concerns, and providing a more thorough discussion of the limitations related to the model assumptions. I would suggest major revisions to address the clarity issues and include more empirical runtime data.
