This paper introduces DPSCREEN, a novel and general framework for computing approximately optimal, personalized, and dynamic screening policies for diseases. The authors model disease progression as a general finite-state stochastic process and uniquely incorporate an "external information process" (e.g., patient self-examination) that can trigger unscheduled clinician visits. The objective is to minimize a weighted sum of screening costs and delay-in-detection costs. Recognizing that an exact solution is computationally intractable, the paper proposes a tractable approximation based on Point-Based Value Iteration (PBVI) over a sampled set of belief states. This work significantly extends prior POMDP-based screening models (e.g., Ayer et al., Operations Research 2012) by relaxing restrictive Markovian assumptions and introducing a more realistic model of patient arrival.

The primary strengths of this paper are its originality, significance, and strong empirical validation.
**Pros for acceptance:**
1.  **Significance and Originality:** The problem of optimizing screening is of immense real-world importance. The proposed framework is a significant methodological advance, particularly the formal inclusion of an external information process that alters the timing between decision epochs, a feature absent in prior art.
2.  **Quality:** The approach is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by experiments on a large, real-world breast cancer dataset. The results, showing a 28-68% reduction in the number of screenings while maintaining the same expected detection delay as clinical standards, are impressive and demonstrate the practical value of the method. The inclusion of theoretical analysis on approximation error and robustness to model estimation errors further strengthens the paper.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem, the model, and the proposed solution.

The paper has a few minor weaknesses that could be addressed to improve it further.
**Cons for acceptance:**
1.  **Experimental Validation of Generality:** While the framework is presented as being applicable to a large class of disease models, the main experiment utilizes a standard two-state Markov model. The paper would be more compelling if it also demonstrated its method on a more complex, non-Markovian disease model (even a synthetic one) to better showcase the power of its general formulation.
2.  **Reproducibility:** The main text lacks some implementation details for the PBVI algorithm, such as the number of belief points sampled or the computational time required. Including these would enhance reproducibility and provide insight into the practical scalability of the approach.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to the application of AI in healthcare. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are primarily suggestions for strengthening the experimental section. The work is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers.
