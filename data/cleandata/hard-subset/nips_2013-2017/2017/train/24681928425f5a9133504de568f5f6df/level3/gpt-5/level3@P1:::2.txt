This paper proposes a cognitively motivated model of question asking that treats questions as executable programs defined by a hand-crafted, domain-general lambda-calculus/LISP-like language and a PCFG. Given a partially revealed “Battleship”-style board, the model samples candidate programs/questions, evaluates their usefulness via expected information gain (EIG) over a Bayesian hypothesis space of board configurations, and scores them with a log-linear energy that trades off informativeness, complexity (−log PCFG probability), and answer-type preferences. The approach is fit to a dataset of 605 human questions (from 40 participants across 18 contexts) previously collected in this domain; human questions were mapped into the program language. Lesioned variants (removing EIG, complexity, or answer-type features) are compared under leave-one-out cross-validation across contexts. Quantitatively, the full model attains the best held-out log-likelihood, with complexity a particularly important predictor; qualitatively, it generates novel, human-like questions not in the training set. The work connects to active learning (beyond label queries), formal semantics of questions (questions as partitions/programs), program induction, and recent program-based QA.

Quality. The technical core is sound and well motivated. Modeling questions as programs affords compositionality, answer-type polymorphism, and executability over possible worlds, enabling principled EIG computations. The probabilistic grammar provides a useful prior and a natural complexity measure that captures human preferences for concise questions. The learning setup (log-linear energy with features), training via importance sampling, leave-one-out evaluation, and ablations are appropriate. Some choices warrant closer scrutiny: (i) the mapping from natural language to programs is manual, leaving the NL-interface as future work; (ii) inference relies on large-scale sampling and an approximate normalizer, with Z bootstraps partially addressing uncertainty; (iii) the hypothesis space is domain-specific and two contexts were dropped due to computational load; (iv) correlations between model energy and human frequencies vary across contexts, and the information-agnostic model achieves slightly higher average rank correlation despite lower log-likelihood—this metric divergence merits discussion. Nonetheless, the main claims are supported: both informativeness and complexity matter, with complexity critical.

Clarity. The paper is clearly written, with concrete examples showing how questions map to programs, how EIG is computed, and how the grammar composes richer queries (e.g., sets, map, lambda). The evaluation protocol and ablations are described plainly. It would help to summarize the grammar’s core rules and answer-type signatures in the main text (not just the supplement), and to report variance/uncertainty on correlation metrics for completeness.

Originality. Framing question asking as program generation is a novel and timely synthesis across cognitive science, formal semantics, and ML. While prior work uses programs for answering questions or visual reasoning, this paper targets question generation and shows predictive alignment with human behavior. The explicit utility–cost trade-off (EIG vs. complexity) advances understanding of why informativeness alone often fails to predict human queries.

Significance. For cognitive science and human-centered AI, the contribution is notable: it offers a generative, predictive account of open-ended question asking and demonstrates creative generalization beyond the training set. For the NeurIPS audience, significance is mixed: the approach is demonstrated in a small, hand-engineered domain with heavy computational demands, and without an end-to-end natural language interface. If the authors can argue more convincingly for scalability or demonstrate transfer to richer domains (e.g., visual scenes, knowledge graphs, or task-oriented dialog with learned semantic parsers), the potential impact on active learning and interactive ML could be substantial.

Strengths
- Elegant integration of compositional semantics, executable programs, and Bayesian EIG within a learned log-linear model over a PCFG.
- Empirically grounded in human data; ablations show that complexity, informativeness, and answer-type preferences all contribute (with complexity most critical).
- Demonstrates generative creativity: novel, plausible questions emerge that align with human intuitions.
- Clear exposition and thoughtful connection to prior work in active learning, program induction, and formal semantics.

Weaknesses
- Scalability and generalization: restricted to a small Battleship domain; two contexts dropped; inference is computationally heavy.
- Dependence on hand-designed grammar and manual NL-to-program mapping; unclear how much knowledge engineering is required in new domains.
- Evaluation metrics partly diverge (log-likelihood vs. rank correlation); limited statistical testing on ablations beyond bootstrap comparisons of Z.
- Utility model limited to entropy reduction; the complexity penalty is grammar-based and may conflate length, prior mass, and design choices.

Suggestions and minor comments
- Provide nested model comparisons and paired statistical tests for ablation differences (e.g., likelihood-ratio tests or Bayesian model comparison), and CIs for rank correlations.
- Analyze sensitivity to the grammar prior q(x) and to alternative complexity measures (e.g., token length, AST depth, or cognitive cost proxies).
- Report computational costs (runtime, sample counts per context), and discuss strategies for scaling EIG over large hypothesis spaces (e.g., amortized inference, variational approximations, or pruning).
- Clarify the discrepancy between correlation and likelihood results; add per-context analyses and error breakdowns.
- Strengthen links to sequential question-asking and Questions-under-Discussion frameworks; discuss multi-turn planning and non-myopic utilities beyond single-shot EIG (the paper cites Roberts; connecting to dialog planning work would help).
- Outline a concrete path to a learned NL interface (e.g., compositional semantic parsing) and demonstrate transfer to a second domain to probe generality.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, well-motivated framework that advances computational understanding of human question asking.
- Solid empirical evidence that balancing informativeness and complexity predicts human behavior.
- Clear potential to inspire more flexible active learning and interactive ML.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited scope and heavy reliance on engineered representations; unclear scalability beyond the toy domain.
- Approximate evaluation with some metric inconsistencies; missing stronger statistical comparisons.
- No end-to-end NL component, which may limit practical impact for NeurIPS audiences.

Overall, this is a thoughtful and original contribution at the intersection of ML and cognitive science. With stronger evidence of scalability and broader applicability, it would be highly compelling for the NeurIPS community.
