Summary and relation to prior work
This paper models human question-asking as program generation in a small “Battleship” grid domain. Questions are represented in a typed lambda-calculus/LISP-like language; a hand-designed PCFG defines the hypothesis space and provides a structural prior. A log-linear model scores candidate questions via a linear combination of context-sensitive expected information gain (EIG), a complexity prior (length/−log PCFG probability), and answer-type preferences (Boolean/Number/Color/Location), with an additional relevance filter. Parameters are learned to predict which questions humans ask in particular contexts. Evaluation uses leave-one-out cross-validation over contexts, comparing the full model to lesioned variants (no EIG, no complexity, no type features). Quantitatively, all terms matter and complexity is the dominant predictor; qualitatively, the model generates plausible novel questions.

The work builds on active learning, program induction, and the “language of thought” tradition, and relates to formal semantics of questions (e.g., partition semantics) and pragmatics. It contrasts with goal-oriented or end-to-end neural dialog systems and VQG, which either restrict question types or require large datasets. It also complements work in visual reasoning as program execution and cognitive models of question usefulness (e.g., Hawkins et al., RSA-inspired approaches).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is clear and principled. Using a PCFG prior and a log-linear objective over EIG, complexity, and type is well-motivated. Inference uses importance sampling over programs; optimization matches feature expectations. Model ablations and LOO-CV are appropriate for this dataset.
- Empirical support: The main quantitative claim—complexity is a stronger predictor than informativeness alone—appears robust: the complexity-agnostic lesion performs far worse; correlations between model energies and human frequencies are substantial; EIG alone correlates poorly with human behavior. The qualitative samples are diverse and reasonable.
- Caveats: Computation is heavy (sampling 150k programs, long optimization), with approximated normalizers and expectations. Significance is assessed via bootstrap over Z; more rigorous paired comparisons on held-out contexts would strengthen claims. The domain is narrow and PCFG is hand-crafted; generalization beyond Battleship is untested.

Clarity
The paper is well-written and organized. The grammar, typing, and features are described clearly, and the cognitive assumptions are explicit. Providing the dataset link helps reproducibility; releasing code, grammar, and trained parameters would further aid replication.

Originality
Novel and timely combination of a PCFG prior over question programs with utility-based selection, bridging formal semantics, cognitive modeling, and program synthesis. Compared to prior ML work (VQG, dialog systems), the emphasis on compositional meaning, goal-directed informativeness, and human-like complexity tradeoffs is distinctive.

Significance
- Cognitive science: Advances understanding of self-directed learning and why informativeness alone does not predict human questions; demonstrates a computable balance between EIG and succinctness.
- ML/NIPS audience: Suggests a path toward richer active querying than label requests and integrates program synthesis with information-theoretic utilities. However, current scope is limited; broader ML impact hinges on scalability, domain transfer, and interfacing with natural language.

Strengths
- Innovative integration of PCFG-based compositional semantics with utility-theoretic selection.
- Clear modeling of K-ary answer types and type-sensitive priors.
- Thorough ablations showing non-negligible roles for informativeness, complexity, and type, with complexity dominant.
- Qualitative generation of novel, plausible questions.
- Careful, readable presentation and appropriate evaluation for the task.

Weaknesses
- Scalability: Heavy sampling and hand-engineered grammar demonstrated only in a small, low-dimensional domain; unclear generalization to larger state/action spaces or to natural language inputs/outputs.
- Statistical testing: Lesioned-model comparisons would benefit from nested model tests on per-context log-likelihoods.
- Engineering dependence: Substantial domain knowledge encoded in the grammar; cost to port to new domains is unknown.

Suggestions and next steps
- Demonstrate scalability or generality: apply the method to at least one additional domain with a larger hypothesis space, or to multi-turn tasks (e.g., 20 Questions, simple visual scenes), or show a learned/neural prior that replaces parts of the hand-engineered PCFG.
- Computational improvements: explore neural-guided program priors, amortized EIG predictors, MCTS/planning over program expansions, or variational bounds on EIG to reduce sampling cost.
- Natural language interface: couple to a semantic parser (e.g., “overnight” learning or neural semantic parsing) to map between NL and programs, and test end-to-end prediction of human questions.
- Statistical comparisons: report paired, per-context differences in test log-likelihood between full and lesioned models with non-parametric tests (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank), or likelihood-ratio tests when feasible; consider hierarchical models over contexts.
- Analyze complexity: decompose “complexity” into length vs. −log PCFG probability and check which component drives predictions; test whether a simple length prior suffices.

Minor comments
- Please perform nested model comparisons on lesioned-model log-likelihoods to assess statistical significance across held-out contexts (paired bootstrap, signed-rank tests, or Bayesian model comparison).
- Consider connecting to Questions Under Discussion frameworks for sequential question-asking, e.g., Roberts (1996/2012) and Ginzburg (2012), to discuss discourse structure and multi-turn extensions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, principled contribution at the intersection of ML, formal semantics, and cognitive modeling.
- Solid empirical evidence that complexity, not just informativeness, drives human question choice.
- Generates novel, human-like questions; strong qualitative appeal.
- Well written and likely to stimulate follow-up work on richer active querying.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited scalability and narrow domain reduce immediate impact for the broader NIPS audience.
- Heavy reliance on hand-crafted grammar; no demonstration of cross-domain generalization.
- Statistical comparisons could be strengthened.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. Strong conceptual contribution and solid evidence within scope; acceptance would be easier to justify if accompanied by a clearer scaling story or a second domain demonstrating broader applicability.
