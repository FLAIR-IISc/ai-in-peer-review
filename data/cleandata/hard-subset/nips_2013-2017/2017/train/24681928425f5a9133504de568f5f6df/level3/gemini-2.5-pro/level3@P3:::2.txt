This paper presents a novel computational model of human question-asking, framing the problem as one of probabilistic program generation. The authors develop a system that can predict and generate human-like questions in a "Battleship"-style grid-world game. The core idea is to represent questions as programs in a language based on lambda calculus, which are generated by a hand-designed Probabilistic Context-Free Grammar (PCFG). The model defines a probability distribution over the space of possible questions, formalized as a log-linear model. The "goodness" of a question is a weighted combination of its expected information gain (EIG), its complexity (approximated by the negative log probability under the PCFG), and its answer type (e.g., Boolean, Number).

The model is evaluated against a dataset of human questions collected in prior work. The human-generated natural language questions were hand-coded into the formal language defined by the grammar. Using leave-one-out cross-validation, the full model is compared against lesioned versions that lack key components (informativeness, complexity, or answer type features). The main quantitative result is that the full model provides the best fit to the human data, and that while all components contribute, question complexity is the most important predictor of human behavior. Qualitatively, the model is shown to generate novel, plausible, and contextually relevant questions that were not observed in the human data. This work connects to prior research in active learning, program synthesis, and computational cognitive science, offering a more structured and compositional approach to query generation than is typical in mainstream active learning.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Quality:** The paper’s main strength lies in its innovative and elegant synthesis of ideas from formal linguistics (compositional semantics), computer science (program synthesis), and cognitive science (modeling self-directed learning). Framing question-asking as probabilistic program generation is a powerful and principled approach that provides a "language of thought" for inquiry. The technical execution is sound, and the connection between the grammar, the probabilistic model, and the cognitive theory is well-articulated.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, explaining the different components of their model (the grammar, the feature space, the optimization), and presenting their results. The inclusion of concrete examples of questions and their program representations is very helpful for the reader.
*   **Evaluation:** The evaluation is appropriate and convincing for this type of work. The quantitative comparison of lesioned models provides strong evidence for the central claim that human question-asking balances informativeness with complexity. The qualitative results, which demonstrate the model's ability to generate creative and useful new questions, effectively showcase the generative power of the framework.
*   **Significance for Cognitive Science:** The work makes a significant contribution to computational cognitive science. It provides a formal, predictive model of open-ended question-asking, a core human capacity. The finding that complexity is a dominant factor, and that informativeness alone is a poor predictor, offers a compelling computational explanation for a key aspect of human inquiry and self-directed learning.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The primary weakness of this work is its limited scalability and questions about its broader significance for the NIPS community.

*   **Scalability and Generalization:** The approach is demonstrated in a small, low-dimensional, and highly structured grid world. The grammar is hand-designed for this specific domain, and the inference procedure (approximating the partition function over all possible programs) appears to be computationally intensive even in this constrained setting. This raises serious doubts about whether the framework, in its current form, can scale to more complex, high-dimensional, or open-ended problems that are the focus of modern machine learning. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the path towards generalization. A key next step would be for the authors to either demonstrate the method in a more complex domain or provide a compelling argument for specific classes of problems (perhaps in scientific discovery or interactive debugging) where the high initial cost of knowledge engineering is justified and where this approach could yield novel insights or capabilities beyond current ML methods.

**Detailed Comments:**

1.  To strengthen the quantitative claims, the authors should consider more formal statistical comparisons of their lesioned models. While the bootstrapped results are informative, a standard approach would be to perform nested model comparisons (e.g., via likelihood-ratio tests) on the log-likelihoods. This would allow for formal claims about the statistical significance of adding each set of features (informativeness, complexity, answer type) to the model.

2.  The paper could benefit from connecting to related work in formal semantics and pragmatics on the structure of inquiry in discourse. In particular, the "Questions under Discussion" (QUD) framework (e.g., Roberts, 2012) offers a formal model of how larger goals and the existing conversational context constrain which questions are relevant to ask. This framework seems highly compatible with the paper's goal-directed approach and could provide additional theoretical grounding for future work on modeling sequences of questions.

In summary, this is a high-quality, original, and thought-provoking paper that makes a clear contribution to the cognitive science of question-asking. The framework is elegant and the results are convincing within the chosen domain. The main reservation is its scalability and immediate relevance to broader, large-scale NIPS problems. However, the novelty of the approach and its potential to inspire new directions in building more structured and human-like active learning agents make it a valuable contribution to the conference.
