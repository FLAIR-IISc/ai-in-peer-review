- Introduces a computational framework for modeling human question asking in an information-seeking game (Battleship).
- Represents questions as formal programs that execute on a world state to yield answers.
- Proposes a probabilistic model trained on human question data to predict and generate questions.
- Model incorporates informativeness, complexity, and answer-type features and outperforms ablated variants.
- Builds on active learning, dialog systems, and visual question generation, emphasizing goal-directed, context-sensitive queries.
- Frames questions compositionally, drawing on linguistics and cognitive science ideas.
- Strength: clear problem formulation and principled, compositional framework.
- Strength: empirical evaluation shows predictive and generative capability; ablation study highlights feature importance.
- Contribution: introduces a novel dataset for human question asking in this task.
- Weakness: task is narrow and constrained (Battleship), limiting generalizability to real-world questioning.
- Weakness: core ideas (formalization, informativeness/complexity importance) are not highly novel; grammar feels somewhat ad-hoc.
- Weakness: reliance on a log-linear model may inadequately capture complex feature interactions.
- Weakness: quantitative evaluation uses Spearman correlation on sparse data, reducing interpretability.
- Weakness: qualitative evaluation of generated questions is subjective.
- Pro-accept arguments: novel computational model, empirical support, dataset, clear writing.
- Anti-accept arguments: oversimplified task, limited novelty, results likely driven by domain constraints, limited NIPS impact.
- Overall assessment: reviewer leans weak accept—technically sound and clear but limited in scope and novelty for NIPS.
