## Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper proposes a framework for modeling human question asking by treating questions as formal programs with compositional semantics, evaluated within the context of the game Battleship. The authors utilize a log-linear model with features capturing expected information gain, answer type (Boolean vs. numerical), and relevance to the game board to predict human question choices. The motivation, framed as an approach to active learning beyond simple label requests, is reasonable.

The work builds upon existing approaches in active learning [21], computational linguistics [8, 18], and cognitive science regarding compositional representations [10, 4]. It differs from recent deep learning approaches to question generation [16, 20] by focusing on goal-directed questioning in a well-defined, unambiguous domain and explicitly modeling questions as executable programs. The authors leverage a novel dataset of human questions from a Battleship game [19] to train and evaluate their model. 

The strengths of the paper lie in its clear articulation of the problem, the well-defined formalization of questions as programs, and the reasonably thorough exploration of feature space. The quantitative results, demonstrating the importance of both informativeness and complexity, are interesting. The ability to generate *novel* questions, while qualitative, is a promising step.

However, the core ideas feel incremental and the task itself is overly simplified. The Battleship domain is highly constrained, and the unambiguous nature of the game significantly limits the complexity of the questions considered. The grammar, while providing a compositional framework, is rather basic. Moreover, the reliance on manually engineered features, despite the use of a log-linear model, feels somewhat dated in the current landscape of end-to-end deep learning approaches. The paper demonstrates the feasibility of the approach within this limited setting, but doesn’t convincingly argue for its scalability or broader applicability.

Ultimately, while technically sound and clearly written, I do not believe this paper will be of significant interest to the NIPS community. The problem is too narrow, the approach too straightforward, and the results not sufficiently impactful to warrant a presentation at this venue.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
* Clear and well-motivated problem statement.
* Novel application of program synthesis to question generation.
* Demonstrates the importance of both informativeness and complexity.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
* Overly simplistic task and domain.
* Incremental contribution to the field.
* Reliance on manually engineered features.
* Limited scalability and generalizability.
