- Presents a novel model framing human question asking as probabilistic program generation in a compositional lambda-calculus-like language.  
- Represents questions as executable programs and scores them with a log-linear model combining expected information gain (EIG), complexity, and answer type.  
- Original and significant: integrates active learning, compositional semantics, and cognitive probabilistic modeling; first formal predictive model of open-ended human question asking.  
- Key empirical finding: question complexity strongly predicts human behavior; EIG alone is a poor predictor.  
- Clear, well-written paper with sound probabilistic modeling and appropriate approximate inference (importance sampling).  
- Thorough evaluation: leave-one-out cross-validation, lesion studies showing contributions of informativeness, complexity, and answer type, plus qualitative generative examples.  
- Main weakness: uncertain scalability and computational cost of inference beyond the small Battleship domain.  
- Domain-specificity concern: reliance on a hand-crafted grammar implies significant engineering to port to new domains.  
- Suggestion: perform formal statistical tests (e.g., likelihood-ratio tests) to quantify significance of lesioned model differences.  
- Suggestion: connect to formal semantics/pragmatics literature (e.g., Questions under Discussion / QUD) to enrich related work.  
- Suggestion: discuss avenues for scalability (learning grammar components, more efficient inference, and target real-world problem classes).  
- Overall judgement: excellent, original contribution with strong cognitive impact but practical generalizability and scalability remain open concerns.
