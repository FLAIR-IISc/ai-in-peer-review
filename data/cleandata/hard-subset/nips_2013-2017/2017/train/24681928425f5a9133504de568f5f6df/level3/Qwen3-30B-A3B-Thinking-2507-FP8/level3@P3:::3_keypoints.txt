- Proposes a cognitive model where a question's unnormalized log-probability is a weighted sum of EIG (informativeness), complexity (length), answer type, and relevance.
- Represents questions as programs that, when executed on a possible world state, produce answers.
- Evaluates the model in the Battleship domain, predicting human questions and generating novel ones.
- Key contributions: formal question-as-program framework, probabilistic model predicting human choices, and generation of novel human-like questions.
- Extends prior work by modeling open-ended, compositional natural-language questions rather than simple label requests.
- Differs from neural question-generation approaches by requiring minimal training data and generalizing to novel scenarios.
- Strong technical quality: well-designed experiment, clear evaluation, reproducible code, and thorough cross-validation of model variants.
- Evidence for EIG’s role is uncertain — bootstrap indicates a 19% chance the log-likelihood result is wrong, weakening claims about informativeness vs complexity.
- Missing important related work citations (Hawkins et al. 2015; Roberts 1996 on QUD).
- Exceptionally clear and well-organized exposition; grammar, program representation, and probabilistic model are accessible and reproducible.
- Demonstrates compositionality is essential for human-like question productivity (~15% of human questions were novel).
- Model successfully predicts question frequencies and generates human-like example questions (e.g., “Are all the ships horizontal?”).
- Claims about how people balance informativeness and complexity are not strongly supported; lesioned models without EIG correlate with human judgments similarly.
- Limited discussion of domain-specific versus domain-general aspects of the grammar.
- Significance: meaningful step toward AI systems that generate creative, context-sensitive, human-like questions.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to add missing related work and clarify uncertainty around EIG’s role.
