The paper "Question Asking as Program Generation" presents a novel approach to modeling human question asking as a program generation task. The authors propose a probabilistic model that treats questions as formal programs that, when executed on the state of the world, output an answer. The model is evaluated on a data set of human questions asked in a Battleship-like game, and the results show that the model can predict the distribution of human questions and generate novel, human-like questions.

The paper makes several key contributions to the field of artificial intelligence and cognitive science. Firstly, it provides a new perspective on question asking as a program generation task, which can be seen as a fundamental aspect of human intelligence. Secondly, it presents a probabilistic model that can capture the complexity and creativity of human question asking, which is a significant improvement over existing models that rely on simple information gain or heuristic-based approaches. Finally, the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the model in a real-world scenario, which is a crucial step towards developing more advanced artificial intelligence systems that can interact with humans in a more natural and intelligent way.

The strengths of the paper include its innovative integration of linguistic and cognitive theory, clear writing, and appropriate quantitative and qualitative evaluation. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the data and the model's performance, which helps to build confidence in the results. The paper also makes a significant cognitive-science contribution by advancing our understanding of self-directed learning and explaining why informativeness alone poorly predicts human question-asking.

However, the paper also has some limitations. One of the main concerns is scalability, as the approach is computationally strained and demonstrated only in a small, low-dimensional domain. This raises doubts about the model's ability to generalize to broader machine learning and NIPS-relevant problems. To address this limitation, the authors could consider demonstrating or arguing for scenarios where the method scales or yields novel insights for a broader audience.

In terms of future work, the authors could explore several directions. One possibility is to extend the model to more complex domains, such as natural language processing or computer vision, where the model's ability to generate creative and informative questions could be particularly valuable. Another direction is to investigate the model's performance in sequential question-asking scenarios, where the model's ability to adapt to changing context and generate follow-up questions could be critical.

Minor comments on the paper include the suggestion to perform nested model comparisons on lesioned-model log-likelihoods to test statistical significance. Additionally, the authors could consider related formal semantics/pragmatics work on sequential question-asking, such as Questions under Discussion, which could provide further insights into the model's performance and limitations.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of artificial intelligence and cognitive science, and its findings have the potential to impact a wide range of applications, from natural language processing to computer vision and robotics.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and innovative approach to modeling human question asking as a program generation task.
* The model is evaluated on a real-world data set and demonstrates strong performance in predicting human questions and generating novel, human-like questions.
* The paper makes a significant cognitive-science contribution by advancing our understanding of self-directed learning and explaining why informativeness alone poorly predicts human question-asking.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the data and the model's performance, which helps to build confidence in the results.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The approach is computationally strained and demonstrated only in a small, low-dimensional domain, which raises doubts about the model's ability to generalize to broader machine learning and NIPS-relevant problems.
* The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the model can be scaled up to more complex domains or how it can be adapted to different applications.
* The model's performance in sequential question-asking scenarios is not thoroughly evaluated, which is a critical aspect of human question asking.

Rating: 8/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns about scalability and provide a clearer explanation of how the model can be adapted to different applications. Additionally, the authors should consider performing nested model comparisons on lesioned-model log-likelihoods to test statistical significance.
