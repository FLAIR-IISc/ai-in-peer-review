Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a probabilistic model of question generation that treats questions as executable programs. Questions are drawn from a compositional grammar (lambda/LISP-like) and assigned probabilities via a log-linear model whose energy is a weighted sum of features: informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (−log prior under the grammar as a length/conciseness proxy), answer type (Boolean/Number/Color/Location), and a relevance filter that excludes world-independent programs. The model is instantiated in a Battleship-like domain with a Bayesian ideal observer for EIG, and trained to match human question frequencies using importance-sampled maximum likelihood. Held-out evaluations compare the full model to lesioned variants (no EIG, no complexity, no type priors).

This work connects program induction/semantic parsing (e.g., Wang et al., Johnson et al., Lake et al.), active learning (Settles), and linguistic/cognitive theories of questions (e.g., partition semantics; Groenendijk & Stokhof) and QUD/pragmatics (Roberts). It is closely related to cognitive models of goal-directed question asking (e.g., Hawkins et al.), while contributing a concrete synthesis-based generator over a rich, compositional hypothesis space.

Strengths
- Clear formalization: questions-as-programs enables compositionality, executability, and evaluation of informativeness over possible worlds.
- Principled modeling: integrates a Bayesian ideal observer (for EIG) with a grammar-based prior to capture complexity and base rate preferences over answer types.
- Strong qualitative results: produces novel, interpretable, context-sensitive questions that go beyond training instances.
- Careful comparison to lesioned baselines: demonstrates the necessity of a complexity preference (complexity-agnostic model performs poorly); relevance and type priors also help.
- Reproducibility: the grammar, datasets, and inference scheme are described in sufficient detail, and the optimization procedure is straightforward.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Ambiguity about the role of informativeness: While the full model achieves higher held-out log-likelihoods, the information-agnostic variant correlates about as well with human frequencies (Spearman ≈ 0.65 vs. 0.64). Bootstrap analysis indicates the full model beats the information-agnostic model in 81% of samples, leaving a nontrivial 19% chance the advantage reverses. Together with sparse counts, this weakens the claim that humans balance informativeness and complexity; the current evidence most strongly supports a complexity bias plus type/relevance preferences.
  • Suggestion: Provide additional metrics (e.g., top-k recall/precision for most frequent questions, calibration of predicted frequency mass) and statistical tests on per-context differences to isolate the contribution of EIG.
- Dependence on grammar prior: The complexity feature is −log q(x) under a uniform PCFG over rules. This couples “complexity” to specific grammar design choices and symbol factorizations.
  • Suggestion: Evaluate alternative complexity measures (program length, AST node count, MDL under different priors) and show robustness. Sensitivity analysis would strengthen claims about human conciseness.
- Limited generality claims: The model is demonstrated in a single, knowledge-rich domain, and two contexts were dropped for computational reasons.
  • Suggestion: Add a second domain (even a simpler toy domain) or a transfer experiment (reuse much of the grammar) to support generality claims; report results including the challenging contexts with approximate EIG (e.g., Monte Carlo over hypotheses).
- Natural language interface: The approach models semantic forms, not end-to-end question generation. While acknowledged, this limits immediate applicability to NLP/VQG.
  • Suggestion: Include a proof-of-concept semantic parser/generator mapping between natural language and programs for a subset of constructions, or evaluate with human judgments on the fluency/appropriateness of sampled questions.
- Related work positioning: The paper cites relevant linguistic and cognitive work (e.g., Roberts; Hawkins et al.) but could more explicitly contrast its predictive goals, learning setup, and innovations relative to prior goal-based models of question selection and semantic-program approaches in QA/VQA.
  • Suggestion: Add a structured comparison clarifying: (i) novelty of program synthesis over questions vs. selection from templates; (ii) learning from sparse human data vs. large supervised datasets; (iii) predictive vs. normative evaluation.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The formalization, likelihood, gradient, and importance-sampling estimates are standard; EIG is implemented with a Bayesian ideal observer over a well-defined hypothesis space. The main caveat is the reliance on approximations (Z and expectations) and the sensitivity of results to sparse frequency data; these are acknowledged and partially mitigated via bootstrapping.

Clarity
Well-written and well-organized. The grammar, features, optimization, and evaluation are described clearly, with helpful examples. A short appendix on complexity sensitivity and additional evaluation metrics would further improve clarity around key claims.

Originality
Modeling question asking as program generation with a learned log-linear model over informative, concise programs is a novel and compelling combination. Relative to prior work in active learning, VQG, and question pragmatics, the compositional synthesis and predictive evaluation on open-ended human questions are new contributions.

Significance
The ability to generate rich, compositional, context-sensitive questions is valuable for both AI (active querying, interactive agents) and cognitive science (mechanistic accounts of inquiry). The demonstrated generation of creative, human-like questions in a nontrivial domain is a meaningful step. Claims about human balancing of informativeness vs. complexity are suggestive but not yet conclusive.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are conclusions to the chosen grammar and the uniform rewrite prior? Have you tried alternative priors or length penalties?
- Can you report per-context improvements attributable to EIG vs. complexity, and test whether EIG helps specifically in contexts where top informative questions are long?
- Could approximate EIG (e.g., variational or sampling over hypotheses) make the dropped contexts tractable?
- How were semantic equivalences determined beyond MI thresholds; did small program rewrites lead to multiple near-duplicates?

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, principled framework; compositional, executable representation of questions.
- Demonstrated predictive power and qualitative creativity; meaningful baselines/ablations.
- Bridges AI and cognitive modeling; likely to inspire follow-up work on interactive learning.

Arguments against acceptance
- Evidence for the informativeness component’s contribution is mixed; correlations do not improve, and LL advantage is not decisive.
- Complexity measure is entwined with a hand-designed grammar; robustness not demonstrated.
- Single-domain evaluation with some contexts dropped; end-to-end language interface absent.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The core idea and demonstrated generation are strong and of broad interest; strengthening the empirical case for informativeness and robustness of the complexity prior would elevate the contribution.
