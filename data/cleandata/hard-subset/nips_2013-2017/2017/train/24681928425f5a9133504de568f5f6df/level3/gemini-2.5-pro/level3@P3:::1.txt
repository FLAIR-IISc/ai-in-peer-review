This paper presents a computational model of human question-asking, framing it as a program generation problem. The authors test their model on a dataset of human-generated questions in a Battleship-like game, where the goal is to resolve ambiguity about hidden objects on a grid. The core idea is to represent questions as programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. A probabilistic log-linear model is defined over the space of possible programs, predicting which questions humans are likely to ask in a given game context. The model's features include the expected information gain (EIG) of the question, its complexity (derived from a probabilistic context-free grammar), its answer type (e.g., Boolean, numerical), and a basic relevance filter. The authors demonstrate through ablation studies that a combination of these features, particularly informativeness and complexity, is necessary to best predict the distribution of human questions.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The motivation to extend active learning beyond simple label queries to richer, more structured questions is compelling and represents an important direction for AI. The framing of questions as programs is an elegant way to capture the compositional and generative nature of human inquiry.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The proposed model is technically sound. The use of a log-linear model to combine features is a standard and reasonable approach. The experimental setup, including the leave-one-out cross-validation and the ablation studies, is clean and provides clear support for the claim that both informativeness and complexity are crucial for modeling the data. The qualitative examples of novel questions generated by the model are also illustrative.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** While the application is novel, the core technical ideas are not. The representation of concepts as programs has been extensively explored in the computational cognitive science literature (e.g., by Goodman, Tenenbaum, Lake), and the use of Expected Information Gain is a cornerstone of Bayesian active learning and experimental design. This work essentially combines these two well-established ideas and applies them to a new, but highly simplified, domain. The contribution feels more like a demonstration of existing principles in a new context rather than the introduction of a new machine learning method or a fundamental insight.

2.  **Simplicity of the Task and Domain:** The Battleship game is a toy problem. It is a small, discrete, fully-observable (given queries) world with unambiguous logic. This extreme simplification sidesteps the most challenging aspects of question-asking and natural language understanding, such as grounding language in noisy perceptual input, handling semantic ambiguity, or reasoning over large, unstructured knowledge bases. The hand-crafted grammar, which is sufficient to cover all questions in this limited domain, is a major limitation. It is unclear how this approach could scale to more complex, realistic scenarios without significant, domain-specific feature engineering for the grammar and semantics.

3.  **Obviousness of the Findings:** The main conclusion—that people balance a desire for informative questions with a preference for simple ones—is intuitive and widely assumed in models of rational communication and inquiry. While the paper provides empirical validation for this trade-off in this specific task, it does not offer a surprising or particularly deep insight. The features themselves (EIG, complexity, answer type) are the most straightforward and obvious ones to consider for this problem.

**Fit for the NIPS Community:**

My primary concern is the paper's fit for the NIPS audience. The work feels much more aligned with the computational cognitive science community, where the primary goal is to build computational models that explain human behavior. For a top-tier machine learning conference like NIPS, the expectation is typically for contributions that introduce novel algorithms, provide significant theoretical advances, or demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on challenging, large-scale problems. This paper does not meet that bar. The learning component is a simple log-linear model over hand-crafted features, and the "program generation" aspect relies on a manually specified grammar rather than a learned synthesis procedure. While the motivation is couched in terms of active learning, the work does not advance the state of active learning methodology itself.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

**Pros:**
*   Well-written paper with a clear and reasonable motivation.
*   Elegant problem formulation (questions as programs).
*   Technically sound experiments that support the main claims.

**Cons:**
*   Limited technical novelty; combines existing ideas from program induction and active learning.
*   The task and domain are extremely simple and constrained, limiting the generalizability of the findings.
*   The primary conclusions about the trade-off between informativeness and complexity are not surprising.
*   The work is a better fit for a cognitive science venue than for NIPS, as its main contribution is in modeling human data in a toy domain rather than advancing machine learning.

In conclusion, this is a solid piece of work that successfully models human question-asking in a constrained environment. However, due to its limited technical novelty and the simplicity of the domain, I do not believe its contribution is significant enough to be of broad interest to the NIPS community.
