This paper presents a novel approach to modeling human question-asking abilities in a computational framework. The authors propose a probabilistic model that generates questions as formal programs, which are evaluated over possible worlds to output an answer. The model is trained on a dataset of human-generated questions in a "Battleship" grid domain and demonstrates the ability to predict human question-asking behavior and generate novel, plausible questions.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning. The introduction provides a thorough background on the importance of question-asking in human learning and the limitations of current active learning algorithms. The authors also provide a clear explanation of their approach, which is grounded in linguistic and cognitive theories.

The model is evaluated using a combination of quantitative and qualitative methods. The authors use leave-one-out cross-validation to compare the performance of different model variants, and the results show that the full model outperforms the alternatives. The model is also able to generate novel questions that are not present in the training data, demonstrating its ability to generalize.

One of the strengths of the paper is its innovative integration of linguistic and cognitive theories. The authors draw on a range of disciplines, including linguistics, cognitive science, and computer science, to develop a comprehensive model of human question-asking. The paper also makes a significant contribution to the field of cognitive science, advancing our understanding of self-directed learning and the role of question-asking in human cognition.

However, there are some limitations to the approach. The model is computationally intensive and is only demonstrated in a small, low-dimensional domain. It is unclear how the model would scale to more complex domains or larger datasets. Additionally, the model relies on a hand-designed grammar, which may not be generalizable to other domains.

To address these limitations, the authors could consider demonstrating the scalability of their approach or arguing for scenarios where the method would yield novel insights for a broader audience. Additionally, the authors could explore ways to automate the process of grammar design, making the model more applicable to a wider range of domains.

In terms of minor comments, the authors could consider performing nested model comparisons on lesioned-model log-likelihoods to test statistical significance. Additionally, the authors may want to consider related formal semantics/pragmatics work on sequential question-asking, such as the "Questions under Discussion" framework.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and innovative approach to modeling human question-asking abilities. While there are some limitations to the approach, the authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the underlying cognitive and linguistic theories and provide a comprehensive evaluation of their model.
