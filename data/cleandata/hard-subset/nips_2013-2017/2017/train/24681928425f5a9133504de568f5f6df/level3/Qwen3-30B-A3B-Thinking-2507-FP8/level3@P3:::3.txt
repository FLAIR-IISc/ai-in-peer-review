

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper proposes a cognitive model where the unnormalized log-probability of a question is a weighted sum of informativeness (EIG), complexity (length), answer type, and relevance (belief-state vs world-independent). The authors model questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer. They evaluate the model in the Battleship domain, where humans ask questions to locate ships on a grid. The paper makes several key contributions: a formal framework for modeling question asking as program generation, a probabilistic model that predicts human question choices, and demonstration of the model's ability to generate novel human-like questions.

The paper relates to prior work by building on the idea of questions as programs (e.g., Lake et al. 2015, Goodman et al. 2016) while extending beyond previous work in two important ways. Unlike previous approaches that focus on simple label requests or stereotyped questions (e.g., "Is it a person?"), this work models open-ended natural language questions in a domain requiring compositional reasoning. It also differs from neural network approaches to question generation (e.g., Mostafazadeh et al. 2016) that require large datasets of image-question pairs, as the authors' model can generate meaningful questions in novel scenarios with minimal training.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a well-designed experiment and clear evaluation methodology. The model is reproducible (with code available on GitHub), and the authors provide thorough analysis of model variants through cross-validation. However, the evidence for the role of EIG is uncertain due to noisy data (19% bootstrap chance the log-likelihood result is wrong), which weakens their main claim about how people balance informativeness and complexity. The paper also misses citing important related work: Hawkins et al. (2015) developed a similar goal-based question model, and Roberts (1996) established the QUD framework for question asking.

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, providing sufficient detail for expert reproducibility. The explanation of the grammar, program representation, and probabilistic model is particularly accessible. The authors effectively demonstrate that compositionality is essential for modeling human question asking productivity (about 15% of human questions were novel across contexts).

The main contribution of this work is demonstrating generation of interesting compositional questions for a non-trivial domain, making the approach useful for AI and cognitive modeling. The model successfully predicts question frequencies and generates novel human-like questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?"). However, the claims about revealing how people balance informativeness and complexity are not strongly supported, as the full model only outperforms lesioned versions on log-likelihood, while models without EIG correlate with human judgments just as well.

Strengths:
- Novel framework modeling questions as programs
- Demonstrates generation of novel, human-like questions
- Clear technical presentation and reproducible code
- Well-designed evaluation with cross-validation
- Shows compositionality is essential for human-like question asking

Weaknesses:
- Uncertain evidence for EIG's role (19% bootstrap error chance)
- Missing key related work (Hawkins 2015, Roberts 1996)
- Claims about balancing informativeness/complexity not strongly supported
- Limited discussion of domain-specific versus domain-general aspects of the grammar

The significance of this work lies in its contribution to modeling open-ended human question asking, which has implications for AI systems that need to interact with humans in natural language. While the paper doesn't fully achieve its goal of revealing how people balance informativeness and complexity, it makes a meaningful step toward more human-like question asking in machines by demonstrating how to generate creative, context-sensitive questions in a non-trivial domain.

In conclusion, this is a technically sound, well-written paper that makes a useful contribution to the field. While the evidence for EIG's role is weaker than claimed, and the paper misses some key related work, the main contribution of demonstrating compositional question generation in a non-trivial domain is significant. The paper advances the state of the art in modeling human question asking by providing a framework that can generate novel, human-like questions rather than merely reusing existing ones.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the missing related work and clarify the uncertainty around EIG's role. The paper's contribution to modeling question asking as program generation is valuable for both cognitive science and AI research.
