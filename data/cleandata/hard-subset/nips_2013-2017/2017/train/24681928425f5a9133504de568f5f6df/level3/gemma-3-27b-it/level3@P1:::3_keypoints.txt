- Proposes modeling questions as executable programs that trade off informativeness and complexity.
- Implements the model in a Battleship-style game to predict human question choices and generate novel questions.
- Positions work beyond traditional active learning and canned dialog systems toward flexible, context-aware queries.
- Connects to formal semantics (Groenendijk & Stokhof) and program induction (Lake et al.) as theoretical foundations.
- Fails to sufficiently discuss related work on goal-directed question asking (e.g., Hawkins et al., QUD by Roberts).
- Technical approach is sound: probabilistic grammar defines question space and a log-linear model scores features like EIG, complexity, answer type, and relevance.
- Experimental setup uses a published human question dataset (Gureckis et al., 2017) and is clearly described for reproducibility.
- Demonstrates ability to generate plausible, compositional novel questions—a promising step for proactive information-gathering agents.
- Full model outperforms ablated variants on held-out log-likelihood, showing benefit of combined features.
- Practical significance of improvements is limited: moderate correlation with human frequencies and notable uncertainty in results.
- Bootstrap analysis indicates a 19% chance the reported importance of EIG is incorrect, weakening claims about informativeness.
- Claim that the work reveals how people balance informativeness and complexity is overstated given limited statistical support.
- Reliance on the Battleship domain raises questions about generalizability despite compositional grammar design.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to add missing related work and temper claims about uncovering human cognitive mechanisms.
