- Proposes a cognitive model framing questions as programs executed on possible worlds, with question probability a weighted sum of EIG, complexity, answer type, and relevance.  
- Evaluated in a Battleship domain; model predicts human question patterns and generates novel human-like questions.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-written, and reproducible.  
- Important contribution: demonstrates compositional question generation in a non-trivial domain, advancing cognitive modeling and AI.  
- Claim that the model reveals how people balance informativeness and complexity is not strongly supported.  
- Full model outperforms lesioned versions on log-likelihood, but correlation with human question frequencies is noisy (avg Spearman 0.64).  
- Bootstrap estimates show a 19% chance the EIG advantage is spurious; models without EIG correlate as well with human judgments.  
- Missing critical prior work: Hawkins (2015) on goal-based question models and Roberts (1996) on the QUD framework.  
- Methodology and statistical evaluation are appropriate, but result interpretation is somewhat overstated.  
- Exceptionally clear and well-organized with sufficient detail for reproducibility.  
- Novelty: program-based question representation is new for modeling human question asking, though informativeness has precedents.  
- Significance: enables more human-like question generation with applications in educational technology and cognitive modeling, promising for richer human–AI interaction.
