- Proposes a cognitive model that treats questions as formal programs executed on the world state to produce answers.  
- Defines a probability distribution over a complex, compositional program space, favoring concise programs that aid learning.  
- Evaluated by modeling the open-ended questions humans ask when learning about an ambiguous game situation.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-written, and experiments are reproducible.  
- Main contribution: generates interesting compositional questions in a non-trivial domain, useful for AI and cognitive modeling.  
- Provides a thorough analysis of results, including model comparisons and a limitations discussion.  
- Claims about how people balance informativeness and complexity are not strongly supported by the data.  
- Correlation results are noisy due to sparse data; bootstrap estimates indicate a 19% chance the log-likelihood result is wrong, making EIG evidence uncertain.  
- Missing citation/discussion of related prior work (Hawkins 2015; Roberts 1996 QUD framework).  
- Well-organized and clear presentation with well-explained technical details and concise results.  
- Originality: novel combination of familiar techniques—using a probabilistic generative model to predict human question choices.  
- Significance: addresses a hard problem more comprehensively than prior work and has potential practical value.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, thorough analysis, technical soundness, clarity, and reproducibility.  
- Arguments against acceptance: weak empirical support for central claims, noisy/statistically uncertain results, and omissions in related work.
