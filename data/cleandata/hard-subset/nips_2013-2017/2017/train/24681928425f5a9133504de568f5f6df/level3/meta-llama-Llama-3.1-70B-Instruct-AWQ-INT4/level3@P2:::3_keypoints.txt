- Introduces a cognitive model that treats questions as executable programs producing answers from world states.  
- Proposes a probabilistic generative model predicting which questions people ask based on weighted features (informativeness, complexity, answer type, relevance).  
- Evaluated on human question data from an information-search game; full model outperforms lesioned versions by held-out log-likelihood.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-written, and reproducible with clear model, grammar, and optimization descriptions.  
- Results and implications are clearly presented and discussed.  
- Strength: generates interesting compositional questions in a non-trivial domain, useful for AI and cognitive modeling.  
- Good literature review covering active learning, dialog systems, and cognitive question-asking models.  
- Limitation: claims about how people balance informativeness and complexity are not strongly supported by the experimental evidence.  
- Correlation results are noisy due to sparse data; bootstrap indicates a 19% chance the log-likelihood result is incorrect, so EIG’s role is uncertain.  
- Missing related prior work (Hawkins 2015 on goal-based question models and Roberts 1996 QUD framework), weakening the literature positioning.  
- Originality: novel framing of question asking as program generation and a unique probabilistic predictive approach.  
- Significance: provides a predictive model of open-ended human question asking with potential cross-domain applications.  
- Recommendation: overall positive—accept with suggestion to address limitations and related-work omissions in future revisions.
