- Presents a novel cognitive model that treats questions as executable programs and defines a probabilistic distribution over a compositional program space favoring concise, context-useful programs.  
- Evaluates the model on human-generated open-ended questions in an ambiguous game domain.  
- Model predicts human question asking and can generate novel, creative questions not seen in training.  
- Comparison of model variants shows both question informativeness and complexity influence human-like question generation.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-written, and reproducible with clear descriptions of the generative model, grammar, and optimization.  
- Results and implications are presented clearly and discussed thoroughly.  
- Key strength: generates interesting compositional questions in a non-trivial domain, offering a new perspective on question asking for AI and cognitive modeling.  
- Emphasizes the theoretical importance of balancing informativeness and complexity when modeling question asking.  
- Limitation: claim that people balance informativeness and complexity is not strongly supported by the experiments.  
- Evidence for EIG’s role is uncertain due to noisy correlation results and sparse data.  
- Bootstrap analysis indicates a 19% chance the log-likelihood result is incorrect, weakening statistical confidence.  
- Paper omits some relevant prior work (Hawkins 2015; Roberts 1996) that should be discussed.  
- Overall assessment: significant and promising contribution to cognitive modeling and AI.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel approach, clear exposition, reproducible results, and potential broad impact; rejection arguments: missing related work and noisy/insufficient evidence for key claims.
