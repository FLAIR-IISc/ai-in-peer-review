- Models human question-asking as probabilistic program generation using a typed lambda-calculus/LISP PCFG and a log-linear energy with EIG, complexity, and answer-type features plus relevance filtering.
- Evaluated with importance sampling over programs and LOOCV on a Battleship-style dataset; full model predicts held-out human question distributions better than lesioned variants and generates novel plausible questions.
- Connects to formal semantics/pragmatics, program-based cognitive models, and visual/question generation work, focusing on goal-driven question production in a micro-world.
- Technically sound formulation and appropriate LOOCV evaluation for small datasets.
- Inference/optimization is heavy and approximate (importance sampling, approximate partition function); sensitivity to proposal distribution, convergence diagnostics, and seed variance are missing.
- The complexity prior (−log q(x)) is coupled to the proposal grammar; robustness to alternative complexity measures is not shown.
- Statistical comparison uses bootstrapping of Z, but per-context paired tests (e.g., Wilcoxon on held-out LLs) would give clearer significance.
- Paper is well written; grammar, typing, and features are clearly described and qualitative samples help.
- Clarifications needed: reconcile similar Spearman correlations between full and info-agnostic models, specify mutual-information equivalence threshold, and provide learning/convergence plots.
- Should summarize domain-specific vs. domain-general grammar productions for portability.
- Novel idea: probabilistic program synthesis for question asking with learned informativeness–complexity trade-offs is original and appealing.
- Strong cognitive contribution: explains human question distributions and why EIG alone fails by quantifying complexity trade-offs.
- Limited practical ML/NeurIPS impact due to small scale and domain specificity; broader demos or NL integration would increase significance.
- Strengths: compositional executable semantics, clear probabilistic formulation, complexity as a key predictor, quantitative and qualitative results, strong literature grounding.
- Weaknesses: scalability issues, hand-engineered grammar and assumed NL-to-program mapping, limited baselines and human evaluation, complexity metric tied to proposal grammar.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, principled approach with strong evidence for informativeness–complexity trade-off and clear exposition.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited domain/scalability, insufficient statistical robustness and baseline comparisons, unclear path to broader ML applications.
- Suggested improvements: per-context paired significance tests, robustness checks replacing −log q(x) with length/AST/MDL or learned PCFG, add EIG-with-cost and non-compositional baselines.
- Further suggestions: report optimization diagnostics and coverage, explore alternative utility functions, integrate or evaluate a learned semantic parser, and assess human ratings of generated questions.
- Connect explicitly to sequential/discourse pragmatics (QUD) and consider extending to multi-step question sequences.
- Overall assessment: creative, well-executed cognitive model with strong originality and clarity but moderate concerns about scalability, evaluation robustness, and breadth for the ML audience.
