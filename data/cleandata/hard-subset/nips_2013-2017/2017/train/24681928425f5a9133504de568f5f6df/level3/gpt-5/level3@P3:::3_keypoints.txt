- Proposal: Treat questions as programs over possible worlds with a probabilistic model scoring features (EIG, complexity via PCFG, answer type, relevance) and sampling from a compositional program space.
- Evaluation: Tested in a Battleship-style domain using leave-one-context-out fits; full model improves held-out log-likelihood and generates novel compositional questions.
- EIG result: Informativeness (EIG) contribution is equivocal—removing EIG leaves correlations with human question frequencies essentially unchanged.
- Relation to prior work: Connects to active learning, program induction/LoT, semantic parsing, and goal-based/question-under-discussion (QUD) accounts but needs clearer contrast, especially on compositionality vs fixed question spaces.
- Technical strength: Solid probabilistic formulation, coherent grammar, and tractable approximate inference.
- Reproducibility: Code and dataset available; grammar covers human questions and optimization details are thorough.
- Compositional generalization: Convincingly generates plausible, novel human-like questions in a non-trivial domain.
- Empirical evaluation: Uses held-out likelihoods, rank correlations, and ablations; complexity feature clearly important.
- Weak evidence for EIG: Bootstrap and correlated metrics show EIG’s incremental contribution is small and unstable (≈19% chance advantage may not hold).
- Overstated claims: Paper’s strong claims about humans trading EIG against complexity are not fully supported by the results.
- Data limitations: Sparsity (many singleton questions) reduces power to separate features and increases sensitivity to approximation error in Z and EIG.
- Relevance feature issue: The relevance indicator is ad hoc rather than a principled prior and may double-count with the grammar prior.
- Theoretical integration: Limited engagement with Hawkins (2015) and Roberts (1996); would benefit from mapping program partitions to QUDs and clarifying differences.
- Suggested uncertainty analyses: Recommend PSIS-LOO, AIS/SMC for normalizers, and bootstrap/Bayesian bootstrap for correlations.
- Suggested analyses: Control for complexity/answer type with matched comparisons, report per-context deltas where EIG helps, and provide uncertainty for Z/EIG estimates.
- Model extensions: Fold relevance into a structured prior or restrict grammar, explore hierarchical participant variability and richer cost functions, and test beyond Battleship.
- Acceptance pros: Elegant, compositional framework; strong engineering and reproducibility; advances beyond fixed-question-space models.
- Acceptance cons: Weak empirical support for the informativeness-complexity trade-off, single-domain focus, and ad hoc relevance filtering.
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept—technically strong and promising, but should temper EIG claims and deepen theoretical and empirical analyses.
