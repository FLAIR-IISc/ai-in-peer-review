- Models human question-asking as program generation in a small Battleship grid using typed lambda-calculus and a hand-designed PCFG prior.
- Uses a log-linear model combining context-sensitive expected information gain (EIG), a complexity prior (length/−log PCFG prob), answer-type preferences, and a relevance filter; parameters are learned.
- Evaluation via leave-one-out over contexts and lesioned variants shows complexity is the dominant predictor; model produces plausible novel questions.
- Connects to active learning, program induction, language-of-thought, formal semantics, and contrasts with neural dialog/VQG work that requires large datasets.
- Technical soundness: clear, principled formulation; PCFG prior and log-linear objective are well-motivated; inference via importance sampling; appropriate ablations and LOO-CV.
- Key empirical finding: complexity predicts human question choice better than informativeness alone; EIG alone correlates poorly with human behavior.
- Qualitative results demonstrate diverse and reasonable generated questions.
- Major caveat: heavy computation (≈150k sampled programs), approximated normalizers/expectations, and narrow, hand-crafted domain limits generalization.
- Clarity: well-written, clear description of grammar/typing/features; dataset linked; code/grammar/parameters release would aid replication.
- Originality: novel and timely integration of PCFG-based compositional semantics with utility-theoretic selection across ML, semantics, and cognitive modeling.
- Cognitive significance: advances understanding of self-directed learning and the tradeoff between EIG and succinctness in human question-asking.
- ML significance: suggests richer active querying via program synthesis and information-theoretic utilities but impact depends on scalability and domain transfer.
- Strengths: innovative integration, type-sensitive priors, thorough ablations showing roles for EIG/complexity/type, and strong qualitative generation.
- Weaknesses: scalability concerns, need for stronger statistical testing, and substantial engineering in the hand-crafted grammar.
- Suggestion: demonstrate scalability/generalization (additional domain, multi-turn tasks, or learned/neural prior replacing PCFG).
- Suggestion (compute): explore neural-guided priors, amortized EIG predictors, MCTS/planning, or variational bounds to reduce sampling cost.
- Suggestion (NL): couple to a semantic parser to map between natural language and programs and test end-to-end human question prediction.
- Suggestion (stats): report paired per-context log-likelihood differences with non-parametric tests (e.g., Wilcoxon) or hierarchical modeling for significance.
- Suggestion (complexity analysis): decompose complexity into length vs. −log PCFG probability to identify the driving component.
- Minor comments: perform nested model comparisons on lesioned-model log-likelihoods for significance; relate to Questions Under Discussion and discourse frameworks for multi-turn extensions.
- Arguments for acceptance: clear, principled contribution; solid evidence that complexity drives human question choice; generates human-like questions; likely to stimulate follow-ups.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited scalability, narrow domain, heavy hand-crafted grammar, and statistical comparisons could be strengthened.
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept; stronger acceptance case with clearer scaling story or demonstration on a second domain.
