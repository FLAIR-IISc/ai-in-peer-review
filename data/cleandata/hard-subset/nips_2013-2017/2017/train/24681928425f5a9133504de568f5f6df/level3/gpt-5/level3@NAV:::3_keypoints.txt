- Proposal: Treat questions as executable programs scored by an energy model combining informativeness (EIG), complexity (PCFG/description length), answer type, and a relevance filter; implemented in a Battleship-like domain to predict and generate compositional questions.  
- Key advance: a compositional, executable question representation paired with a probabilistic generator over a rich hypothesis space.  
- Technical quality: modeling and inference are careful and reproducible (importance sampling for Z, gradient ascent); held-out log-likelihood favors the full model, especially when complexity is included.  
- Evidence for EIG weak: models without EIG match human frequencies nearly as well, correlations are noisy from sparse counts, and bootstrap shows a 19% chance the full model’s advantage is illusory—claims about balancing informativeness and complexity overreach.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and transparent; clarify how answer-type priors and the relevance filter interact with EIG and report uncertainty on correlation estimates.  
- Originality: learning a distribution over programmatic questions in a nontrivial domain is novel; should more directly engage goal-based/QUD literature (Hawkins et al. 2015; Roberts 1996).  
- Significance: generating compositional, useful questions is valuable for AI and cognitive modeling, but broader impact requires evaluations in additional domains and comparisons to neural QG baselines.  
- Pros: strong, reproducible modeling; novel compositional question generator with compelling qualitative outputs; complexity and type features yield solid predictive gains.  
- Cons: weak/noisy evidence for EIG’s contribution; limited domain and sparse data; incomplete engagement with QUD and prior goal-based models.  
- Suggestions: add stronger baselines (e.g., neural QG), quantify uncertainty more thoroughly, test in another domain, and explicitly relate the program-based objective to QUD/goal structures.
