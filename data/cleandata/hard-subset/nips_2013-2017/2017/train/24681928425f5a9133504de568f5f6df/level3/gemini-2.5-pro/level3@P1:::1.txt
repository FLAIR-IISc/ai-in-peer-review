### Review

**Summary of the paper**

This paper presents a computational model of human question-asking, framing the problem as one of program generation. The authors propose that humans generate questions by composing conceptual building blocks into programs that, when executed on a world state, return an answer. The work is evaluated in the context of a "Battleship"-like game where human participants were asked to generate natural language questions to resolve ambiguity about the game board's configuration.

The core of the proposed model is a probabilistic log-linear model defined over a space of programs. This program space is generated by a hand-crafted, LISP-like compositional grammar. The model scores potential questions (programs) based on a weighted combination of features. The primary features are the Expected Information Gain (EIG) of the question, which captures its utility in reducing uncertainty, and its complexity, measured by the log probability of the program under a probabilistic context-free grammar. Additional features account for the question's answer type (e.g., Boolean, Number) and its relevance to the game. The model's parameters are fit to a dataset of human-generated questions, and its performance is evaluated on its ability to predict the distribution of questions in held-out contexts and to generate novel, human-like questions. The authors compare their full model to several ablated versions, concluding that both informativeness (EIG) and complexity are crucial for explaining human question-asking behavior.

This work positions itself as an advancement over traditional active learning, which typically involves simple label queries, and data-driven dialogue systems, which often lack goal-directedness and compositional creativity. It draws inspiration from cognitive science literature on compositional "language of thought" models and formal semantics.

**Strengths**

1.  **Elegant Formulation:** The central idea of modeling questions as programs is an elegant and powerful abstraction. It provides a natural way to capture the compositional, computable, and generative nature of human inquiry. This framing offers a compelling path toward building active learning agents capable of asking richer, more structured questions.

2.  **Clear Motivation:** The paper is well-motivated by the limitations of current active learning and dialogue systems. The goal of moving beyond simple label requests towards more human-like, information-seeking behavior is an important and challenging one for the AI and machine learning communities.

3.  **Sound Methodology and Analysis:** The authors conduct a systematic evaluation of their hypothesis. The use of a probabilistic model with interpretable features, combined with ablation studies (lesioned models), provides clear evidence for the importance of both informativeness and complexity in predicting human behavior. The qualitative results, showing the model's ability to generate novel and sensible questions, are also convincing.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Originality of Technical Components:** While the application to question generation is novel, the core technical components of the model are well-established. The use of Expected Information Gain for query selection is a cornerstone of Bayesian active learning. Representing meaning compositionally via a formal grammar is a classic approach in semantics (e.g., Montagovian semantics). Combining these elements in a log-linear model is a standard technique. The contribution is thus more in the novel combination and application of these ideas rather than in the development of a new machine learning technique.

2.  **Simplicity of the Task Domain:** The primary weakness of the paper is the highly constrained and simplistic nature of the "Battleship" domain. The world state is discrete, low-dimensional, and logically unambiguous. This removes many of the core difficulties that are of central interest to the NIPS community, such as learning from high-dimensional perceptual data, handling linguistic ambiguity, or scaling to open-ended, real-world problems. The hand-crafted grammar is sufficient for this toy domain, but its reliance on significant domain engineering is a major bottleneck for applying this method to more complex, realistic scenarios. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but it significantly curtails the paper's potential impact.

3.  **Limited Significance for the NIPS Community:** Given the simplicity of the task and the reliance on established techniques, the paper feels more aligned with the computational cognitive science community than with the machine learning community represented at NIPS. While the long-term vision is relevant, the current work is a proof-of-concept in a controlled environment. The NIPS community typically expects contributions that either introduce novel methods or demonstrate significant performance improvements on challenging, large-scale tasks. This paper does not clearly meet either of those bars. The work sidesteps the difficult problem of parsing natural language into the formal program representation, instead relying on manual translation, further distancing it from practical application.

**Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation, model, and experiments are described with great clarity, making the work easy to follow and understand.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents an elegant and compelling computational framework for a complex cognitive ability (question asking).
*   Provides a strong proof-of-concept that bridges active learning with compositional models of language and thought.
*   The results are clear, and the ablation study convincingly demonstrates that both informativeness and complexity are key factors.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The technical approach combines well-known ideas (EIG, compositional grammar, log-linear models) in a straightforward manner, limiting its technical novelty.
*   The evaluation is performed on a simple, "toy" domain that is not representative of the complex, large-scale problems typically addressed at NIPS.
*   The reliance on a hand-crafted grammar and manual translation of questions limits the generalizability and practical significance of the work for the broader machine learning community.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper presents a clear and well-executed study of human question-asking, framed as probabilistic program induction. The work is methodologically sound and provides interesting insights. However, its contribution to the core machine learning community is limited by the lack of technical novelty and the simplicity of the experimental domain. While this is a solid piece of work that may be a strong contribution to a cognitive science venue, its limited scope and significance make it a borderline case for NIPS.
