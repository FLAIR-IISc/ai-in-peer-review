- Presents a novel computational model framing human question asking as probabilistic program generation, with questions represented as compositional programs.
- Defines a log-linear probability over program space where features include Expected Information Gain (EIG), program complexity, answer type, and task relevance.
- Applied to a Battleship-like game to predict open-ended natural language questions humans ask to resolve ambiguity about hidden ships.
- Connects active learning, program synthesis, and cognitive science, moving beyond simple label queries to rich compositional question generation.
- Quality: technically sound and elegant; log-linear model with importance sampling is appropriate and authors report bootstrap uncertainty.
- Main weakness: evidence for the central claim that people balance informativeness and complexity is weak and statistically uncertain.
- Full model beats an information-agnostic lesion in only 81% of bootstrap samples, leaving a 19% chance the advantage is noise.
- Contradictory result: the information-agnostic model has a slightly higher Spearman correlation with human question frequencies (0.65 vs. 0.64).
- Clarity: exceptionally well-written, clear motivation and model description; qualitative examples effectively illustrate the approach; dataset provided for reproducibility.
- Originality: significant conceptual advance framing goal-directed question asking as program generation; novel application of program synthesis to question generation.
- Literature gap: overlooks relevant prior work (e.g., Hawkins et al., 2015) and missing connection to linguistic frameworks like Roberts' QUD (1996).
- Significance: strong proof-of-concept demonstrating generation of rich, compositional, and novel questions with implications for AI and cognitive science.
- Acceptance arguments: novel framework, creative unseen-question generation, technical soundness, clarity, reproducibility, and conceptual importance.
- Rejection arguments: weak experimental support for the informativeness-vs-complexity claim and missing key citations in related fields.
