This paper presents a computational model of human question asking, framing the problem as one of probabilistic program generation. The authors develop their model in the context of a "Battleship"-like grid-world game where humans were previously recorded asking open-ended questions to resolve ambiguity. The core idea is to represent questions as programs in a compositional language based on lambda calculus, which are generated by a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG). The model defines a log-linear probability distribution over these programs, where the score of a question is a weighted combination of its Expected Information Gain (EIG), its complexity (negative log probability under the PCFG), and its answer type (e.g., Boolean, Number). The model is trained and evaluated on a dataset of human questions that have been hand-coded into the programmatic representation. The main findings are that a full model including features for informativeness, complexity, and answer type best predicts human question frequencies in a leave-one-out cross-validation setup. Notably, question complexity is found to be a particularly strong predictor, explaining why humans do not simply ask the most informative questions. Qualitatively, the model is shown to generate novel, plausible, and contextually relevant questions.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The paper has several significant strengths, making it a valuable contribution.

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution is the novel and compelling framework of treating question asking as program synthesis. This is a significant departure from and improvement upon standard active learning paradigms, which are typically restricted to simple label queries. It also moves beyond recent deep learning approaches to question generation, which are often data-hungry and less able to capture the goal-directed, compositional nature of human inquiry in novel situations. The work provides a strong bridge between cognitive science, formal linguistics, and machine learning, offering a concrete computational model for a fundamental aspect of intelligence.

*   **Quality:** The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. The combination of a generative grammar (defining the space of possible thoughts/questions) with a probabilistic model that scores questions based on both utility (EIG) and cognitive cost (complexity) is elegant and principled. The evaluation is thorough, combining quantitative model comparison via held-out log-likelihood with qualitative analysis of newly generated questions. The ablation study (lesioned models) clearly demonstrates the importance of each component of the model, and the finding that informativeness alone is a poor predictor of human questions—and that complexity is crucial—is an important result for both cognitive science and AI.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the motivation, the formal model, the experimental setup, and the results. The examples provided throughout the text are helpful for building intuition.

The primary weakness of the work concerns its scalability and its immediate significance for the broader machine learning community.

*   **Scalability and Generalization:** The model is demonstrated in a small, discrete, and well-defined "Battleship" domain. The question grammar is hand-designed for this specific domain, and it is unclear how this approach would scale to more complex, high-dimensional, or open-ended real-world problems. The inference procedure, which relies on importance sampling over a vast space of potential programs, seems computationally intensive and may become intractable in more complex settings. While the paper makes a strong contribution to computational cognitive science, its direct applicability to larger-scale problems of interest to the NIPS community is not yet established. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but the paper would be stronger if it discussed potential paths toward scaling, for instance by learning the grammar from data or by developing more efficient inference methods.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance**

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a highly original and thought-provoking paradigm for active learning and goal-directed inquiry. It is technically solid, clearly presented, and supported by rigorous experiments on human data. The core idea of "question asking as program generation" has the potential to be very influential.
*   **Con:** The primary concern is the limited scope and scalability of the demonstrated approach. The reliance on a hand-engineered grammar in a toy domain raises questions about its broader utility for the machine learning community.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

1.  The model comparisons in Table 1 are based on approximate log-likelihoods. To strengthen the claim that the full model is superior, it would be beneficial to conduct formal statistical tests. Given that the models are nested, the authors could use a likelihood-ratio test to assess whether the inclusion of each set of features (informativeness, complexity, type) leads to a statistically significant improvement in model fit over the lesioned version. This would provide more rigorous evidence than the reported bootstrap percentages.

2.  The work connects well to program induction and cognitive science. The authors might also find fruitful connections in the formal semantics/pragmatics literature on discourse, particularly the "Questions under Discussion" (QUD) framework (e.g., Roberts, 2012, "Information structure in discourse"). This framework models dialogue as a process of cooperatively answering a stack of implicit questions to solve a larger problem. It seems like a natural theoretical extension for this work, especially for modeling sequences of questions rather than single, isolated ones.

In summary, this is a high-quality, original, and well-executed paper that makes a clear contribution at the intersection of machine learning, cognitive science, and linguistics. While its immediate scalability is a valid concern, the novelty and conceptual elegance of the framework are significant. The paper presents a new way of thinking about active information gathering that is likely to inspire future research. It is a strong paper that would be of interest to the NIPS audience.
