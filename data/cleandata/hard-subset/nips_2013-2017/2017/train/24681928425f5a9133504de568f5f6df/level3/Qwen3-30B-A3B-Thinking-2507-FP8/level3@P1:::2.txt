

This paper presents a novel computational framework for modeling human question-asking as program generation in a cognitive science context. The authors treat questions as formal programs that, when executed on a world state, output an answer, thereby recasting question asking as program synthesis. The model specifies a probability distribution over a compositional program space using a PCFG with features capturing question informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (via PCFG probability), and answer type. Evaluated on a dataset of 605 natural language questions from a Battleship game context, the model successfully predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, plausible questions not present in training.

The paper makes a significant contribution to cognitive science by advancing our understanding of self-directed learning. Previous work on active learning has focused on simple label requests, while dialog systems typically use canned questions or require large datasets for open-ended questioning. This paper bridges a critical gap by providing the first predictive model of human question-asking that accounts for the productive and context-sensitive nature of human questioning. The authors demonstrate that humans balance informativeness with complexity and answer type preferences, explaining why pure informativeness metrics fail to predict human question choices.

The work excels in quality, with a technically sound approach grounded in cognitive science and linguistics. The model's components—compositionality, computability, and the probabilistic generative framework—are well-motivated and rigorously evaluated. The authors conducted thorough model comparisons using leave-one-out cross-validation, showing that all key features (informativeness, complexity, answer type) contribute non-negligibly to predicting human question choices, with complexity emerging as the dominant predictor. The qualitative evaluation of novel question generation (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") demonstrates the model's creative capacity to produce human-like questions.

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, with a compelling narrative that connects linguistic theory to cognitive modeling. The grammar design, feature engineering, and evaluation methods are thoroughly explained, making the work accessible to both cognitive scientists and machine learning researchers. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, including the domain-specific nature of their grammar and computational scalability concerns.

The work demonstrates strong originality by formalizing question asking as program generation, a perspective that distinguishes it from previous approaches in active learning and question answering. While prior work has modeled questions as programs for question answering [12, 24], this paper uniquely applies the framework to question asking, providing a new theoretical lens for understanding human cognition. The connection to compositionality in linguistics [10] and the "language of thought" [3] provides a principled foundation that goes beyond recent neural approaches to question generation [16, 23].

The significance of this work is substantial for cognitive science, as it provides a computational account of how humans balance multiple constraints in question asking. For machine learning, it opens new avenues for developing more human-like active learning systems that can ask rich, context-sensitive questions rather than limited label requests. However, the paper's current scope is limited to a small Battleship domain, raising questions about scalability to more complex, real-world settings relevant to NIPS.

The main limitation is the lack of demonstration of scalability beyond the specific domain. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, a more compelling argument for broader applicability would strengthen the case for NIPS acceptance. The authors should address whether the grammar can be generalized to other domains with minimal engineering or whether the approach would require domain-specific customization, and how the computational cost would scale with more complex domains.

Minor improvements would include performing nested model comparisons to establish statistical significance of the full model's advantage over lesioned variants (addressing the key point about statistical testing), and considering related work on sequential question-asking in formal semantics (e.g., "Questions under Discussion" [18]), which could enrich the theoretical framing.

Overall, this is a high-quality, original contribution that advances both cognitive science and the technical modeling of question-asking. The paper successfully demonstrates how a formal computational approach can illuminate cognitive processes, while also providing a foundation for future work in human-like machine question-asking. The authors have made a compelling case for the importance of complexity and answer type in human question generation, explaining a key phenomenon in self-directed learning. While the domain specificity is a limitation, the methodological framework is robust and could be extended to more complex scenarios with appropriate domain adaptation. The work deserves acceptance for its theoretical contribution and methodological innovation.
