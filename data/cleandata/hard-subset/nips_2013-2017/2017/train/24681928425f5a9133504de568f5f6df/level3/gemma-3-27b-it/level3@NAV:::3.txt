## Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper presents a novel cognitive model for question generation, framing questions as formal programs with a probability distribution governed by informativeness (EIG), complexity (length), answer type, and relevance. The model is implemented and evaluated within the Battleship domain, aiming to predict human question-asking behavior. The authors demonstrate that their full model, incorporating all features, outperforms lesioned versions in predicting human questions based on log-likelihood. 

The approach builds upon the idea of active learning, moving beyond simple label requests to more complex, natural language-like inquiries. It connects to prior work in cognitive science concerning compositional semantics (Fodor, 1998) and the use of programs to model concepts (Lake et al., 2015), as well as computational linguistics’ efforts in semantic parsing (Wang et al., 2015). However, the paper doesn’t fully address related work in goal-directed dialogue systems (Bordes et al., 2017) or earlier goal-based question models like Hawkins (2015) and the QuD framework (Roberts, 1996) as noted in the submission details.  

A significant strength is the technical soundness and clarity of the presentation. The grammar for program generation and the probabilistic model are well-defined, and the experimental setup appears robust, with code available for reproducibility.  The demonstration of generating novel, plausible questions is a valuable contribution, hinting at the potential for this approach to aid AI systems in formulating informative queries.  

However, the claim that the model reveals *how* humans balance informativeness and complexity is not strongly supported. While the full model performs best, the correlation with human judgments isn't substantially improved over the model *without* EIG, and the authors acknowledge noisy data and a substantial bootstrap error rate (19%) regarding the EIG’s significance. This suggests that other factors, or perhaps the interplay between features, are at play and not adequately captured. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates a novel and technically sound approach to question generation as program synthesis.
*   Successful generation of novel, plausible questions in a non-trivial domain.
*   Clear presentation and reproducibility.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Claims about revealing human cognitive processes are not conclusively supported by results.
*   Missing relevant related work (Hawkins 2015, Roberts 1996).
*   Evidence for the importance of EIG is uncertain due to data sparsity and bootstrap results.



**Overall:** Despite some limitations, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field.  The approach offers a promising framework for building AI systems capable of more sophisticated question asking. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the missing related work and temper the claims regarding insights into human cognition.
