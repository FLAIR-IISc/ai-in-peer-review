

This paper introduces a novel computational framework for modeling human question-asking by treating questions as executable programs that map world states to answers. The authors formalize questions as programs in a lambda-calculus-based language, where program compositionality and computability enable modeling of complex, context-sensitive queries. The approach uses a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) with features for expected information gain (EIG), program complexity (via grammar log-probability), answer type, and relevance. Evaluated on a human question dataset from a Battleship-style game (605 questions across 18 contexts), the model predicts human question frequencies (Spearman’s ρ=0.64) and generates novel, plausible questions not in the training set.  

**Strengths:**  
The work makes a significant contribution to cognitive science by explaining why informativeness alone fails to predict human questions—people balance informativeness with complexity and answer-type preferences. This aligns with prior work on self-directed learning [Gureckis & Markant, 2015] but advances it through a formal, predictive model. The integration of linguistic compositionality [Jacobson, 2010] and cognitive pragmatics [Hawkins et al., 2015] is elegant and well-justified. The evaluation is thorough: quantitative (log-likelihood comparisons across model variants) and qualitative (novel question generation). The clarity of the writing and the well-structured model description (e.g., grammar rules, feature design) make the approach highly accessible.  

**Weaknesses:**  
The primary limitation is scalability. The model is demonstrated only on a constrained Battleship domain with a small hypothesis space (3 ships, 6×6 grid), raising questions about applicability to broader ML problems. The computational cost is high (150k samples per gradient step), and the grammar requires substantial domain-specific engineering (e.g., ship-size predicates). While the authors note domain-general constructs (e.g., set operations), the lack of analysis on scalability or transfer to larger domains weakens the significance for NIPS. Additionally, the statistical comparison of model variants lacks nested model tests (e.g., likelihood ratio tests) for the log-likelihood differences reported, though the bootstrap results are suggestive.  

**Originality:**  
The paper is highly original. It differs from prior work by treating questions as executable programs (vs. neural networks [Mostafazadeh et al., 2016] or canned dialog systems [Bordes et al., 2016]), and it provides the first predictive model of *open-ended* human question-asking. The use of program synthesis for cognitive modeling is fresh, and the compositionality perspective distinguishes it from related formal semantics work (e.g., [Groenendijk & Stokhof, 1991]).  

**Significance:**  
The cognitive science contribution is strong: it explains a key aspect of human learning (question-asking tradeoffs) with a computationally precise model. However, the significance for ML/NIPS is partially limited by the narrow domain. While the approach *could* inform future active learning systems, the paper does not demonstrate how it scales to high-dimensional or real-world problems (e.g., vision or language tasks). The authors suggest extending to "different domains" but provide no evidence of feasibility. A stronger case would address *why* this framework offers advantages over neural baselines in larger settings.  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
*Pro:*  
- First formal model of human question-asking as program generation, with cognitive validity.  
- Clear demonstration of compositionality and the complexity-informativeness tradeoff.  
- Strong quantitative and qualitative evaluation (novel question generation).  
- Well-motivated integration of linguistics, cognitive science, and ML.  

*Con:*  
- Domain limited to Battleship (small state space); scalability unaddressed.  
- Computational burden (150k samples/iteration) and domain-specific grammar design.  
- Missing rigorous statistical tests for model comparisons.  
- Limited discussion of how this advances *ML* beyond cognitive science.  

**Recommendation:**  
I recommend *acceptance* with minor revisions. The paper is technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), and highly original (Originality) for its cognitive science contribution. While the scalability limitation is notable, NIPS values foundational cognitive insights that may later inform ML. The authors should:  
1) Add a brief discussion of scalability challenges and potential pathways (e.g., using neural grammars [Wang et al., 2015]).  
2) Include nested model comparisons (e.g., likelihood ratio tests) for the log-likelihood differences.  
3) Cite related work on sequential question-asking (e.g., "Questions under Discussion" [Roberts, 1996]) to strengthen the theoretical grounding.  

This work is a compelling step toward modeling human-like question-asking, and its cognitive insights are valuable for NIPS. With minor revisions to address scalability and statistical rigor, it will be a strong contribution.
