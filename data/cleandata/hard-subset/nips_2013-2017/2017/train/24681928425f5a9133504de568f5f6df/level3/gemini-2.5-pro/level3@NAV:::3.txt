This paper introduces a computational model of human question asking, framing the task as probabilistic program generation. The core idea is that the utility of a question can be represented as a log-linear combination of features, including its informativeness (Expected Information Gain, EIG), complexity (program length), answer type, and relevance to the task. The authors implement this model in a "Battleship" game domain, evaluating its ability to predict the open-ended questions humans ask to identify hidden ships. The work connects concepts from active learning with the "language of thought" hypothesis in cognitive science, proposing a formal, compositional approach to inquiry.

The primary strength of this paper is its novel and powerful framework for generating rich, compositional questions. This is a significant step beyond the simple label requests common in most active learning literature. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The demonstration that the model can generate creative and contextually useful questions not seen in the training data is a compelling result, highlighting the approach's value for both building more sophisticated AI agents and as a formal model of human cognition.

However, the paper’s main weakness is that its central claim—that it reveals how people balance informativeness and complexity—is not strongly supported by the results. While the full model outperforms lesioned variants in terms of log-likelihood, the evidence for the specific contribution of the EIG feature is tenuous. The authors report that a model *without* EIG correlates with human question frequency almost as well as the full model. Moreover, their bootstrap analysis indicates a 19% chance that the log-likelihood advantage is spurious. This ambiguity significantly weakens the cognitive claims.

Furthermore, the paper overlooks key related literature. The model of goal-directed questioning by Hawkins et al. (2015) is highly relevant, as is the influential Question Under Discussion (QUD) framework in linguistics (Roberts, 1996), which formalizes how questions guide information-seeking.

In conclusion, this work presents an original and significant technical contribution by formalizing question-asking as program synthesis. While the empirical support for its specific cognitive claims is weak, the quality and novelty of the generative framework itself merit publication. I recommend acceptance, provided the authors revise the manuscript to moderate their claims about the role of EIG and situate their work with respect to the missing prior literature.
