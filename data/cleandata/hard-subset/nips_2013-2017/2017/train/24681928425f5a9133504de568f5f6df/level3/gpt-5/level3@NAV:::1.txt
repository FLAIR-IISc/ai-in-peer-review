This paper models question asking as program generation: questions are lambda-calculus/LISP programs with compositional (Montagovian) semantics executed on possible Battleship boards. A PCFG defines a space of executable queries; a small log-linear model over simple features—expected information gain (EIG), length/complexity, answer type (Boolean vs. numeric), and an indicator that the question refers to board features—assigns probabilities to programs. Parameters are learned by maximizing the likelihood of human questions from a prior dataset; evaluation uses leave-one-context-out prediction and qualitative sampling of “novel” questions. The work connects to active learning (Settles), program induction/semantic parsing (Wang et al.; Johnson et al.; Lake et al.), and pragmatic/partition-based accounts of questions (Groenendijk & Stokhof; Hawkins et al.) and contrasts with VQG/sequence models (Mostafazadeh et al.).

Quality: Technically sound within the toy domain. The inference (importance sampling for Z and feature expectations) is reasonable; ablations show complexity and EIG both matter. However, the hypothesis space, grammar, and likelihood are heavily engineered to the Battleship task; EIG relies on exact world models and one-step utility, sidestepping challenges central to real AL/interactive learning.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The grammar, features, and optimization are described sufficiently to reproduce results; the dataset linkage helps. Some details (proposal distribution calibration, convergence diagnostics, sensitivity to grammar priors) could be expanded.

Originality: The core ideas—compositional question meaning, EIG as utility, log-linear selection—are well known. The contribution is a modest integration and application to a highly constrained, logically unambiguous setting. Compared with prior NeurIPS work on program synthesis and pragmatic inference, novelty is limited.

Significance: Predicting question distributions in a toy game and sampling clever but hand-engineered questions is unlikely to interest the NeurIPS community. There is no demonstration that the method scales, interfaces with natural language, or improves downstream learning performance relative to strong baselines.

Pros:
- Clear, interpretable formalization; careful ablations.
- Bridges active learning and compositional semantics; reproducible setup.
- Interesting cognitive modeling perspective.

Cons:
- Narrow, hand-crafted domain; limited generality.
- Minimal novelty relative to prior program-induction and pragmatic/QUD literature.
- No natural language interface or scalable evaluation; unclear ML impact.

Recommendation: Reject. To strengthen for ML venues, demonstrate scaling beyond Battleship, integrate an NL front-end, compare against neural/question-generation baselines, and show gains in an actual active learning loop.
