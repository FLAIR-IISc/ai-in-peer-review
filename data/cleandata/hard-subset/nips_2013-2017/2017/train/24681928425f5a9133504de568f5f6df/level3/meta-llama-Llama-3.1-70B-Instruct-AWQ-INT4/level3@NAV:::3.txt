This paper proposes a cognitive model that treats questions as formal programs that, when executed on the state of the world, output an answer. The model specifies a probability distribution over a complex, compositional space of programs, favoring concise programs that help the agent learn in the current context. The authors evaluate their approach by modeling the types of open-ended questions generated by humans who were attempting to learn about an ambiguous situation in a game.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and reproducible. The main contribution of the paper is demonstrating the generation of interesting compositional questions for a non-trivial domain, making the approach useful for AI and cognitive modeling. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the results, including a comparison of different models and a discussion of the limitations of their approach.

However, the paper has some limitations. The claims about revealing how people balance informativeness and complexity are not strongly supported by the experimental results. The correlation results are noisy due to sparse data, and the bootstrap estimates give a 19% chance that the log-likelihood result is wrong, so the evidence for the role of Expected Information Gain (EIG) is uncertain. Additionally, the paper is missing related prior work, such as Hawkins 2015 (similar goal-based question model) and Roberts 1996 (QUD framework).

In terms of quality, the paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the problem and the proposed approach, and the technical details are well-explained. The results are presented in a clear and concise manner, and the discussion is thorough and well-reasoned.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a new approach to modeling question generation, which is a novel combination of familiar techniques. The use of a probabilistic generative model to predict which questions people will ask is a new approach to this problem, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the results.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The authors provide a more comprehensive and nuanced understanding of human question generation, and the approach has the potential to be useful for AI and cognitive modeling.
