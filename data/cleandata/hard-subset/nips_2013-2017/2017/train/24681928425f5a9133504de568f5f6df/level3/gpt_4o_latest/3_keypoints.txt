- Paper presents a cognitive model treating questions as compositional programs evaluated over possible world states, tested in the Battleship domain to predict human questions.

- Strength: Technically rigorous probabilistic model with a clear optimization framework and grammar-based representation of questions.

- Strength: Methodology is well-documented and reproducible, with supplementary materials (grammar rules, examples).

- Strength: Novel compositional approach improves on simpler active learning/question-generation systems.

- Strength: Model can generate novel, human-like questions, suggesting applications in tutoring systems and conversational agents.

- Weakness: Human data are sparse and noisy (many questions asked once), weakening evaluation robustness and claims about informativeness vs complexity.

- Weakness: Important related work (e.g., Hawkins 2015, Roberts 1996) is omitted, reducing contextual grounding.

- Weakness: Some grammar elements are Battleship-specific, leaving scalability and generalizability to other domains unclear.

- Weakness: Cognitive-science claims about how humans balance informativeness and complexity are underdeveloped and unconvincing.

- For acceptance: Introduces a novel, technically sound framework for modeling human question-asking behavior.

- For acceptance: Compositional approach has significant potential for advancing cognitive science and AI applications.

- For acceptance: Clear, reproducible methodology with promising results generating human-like questions.

- Against acceptance: Cognitive claims are weak due to sparse data and limited evidence.

- Against acceptance: Omission of key related work undermines originality and situational awareness.

- Against acceptance: Domain-specific design raises concerns about generalizability.

- Recommendation: Conditional acceptance contingent on addressing missing related work, clarifying cognitive implications, and discussing how the model could generalize to other domains.
