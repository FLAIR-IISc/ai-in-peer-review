- Novel framing: models question asking as program generation with a probabilistic model treating questions as executable programs.
- Evaluation: tested on a Battleship-like human-question dataset and can predict human question distributions and generate novel, human-like questions.
- Contribution: introduces a new perspective on question asking as a fundamental program-generation task in intelligence.
- Contribution: presents a probabilistic model that captures complexity and creativity beyond simple information-gain heuristics.
- Contribution: demonstrates effectiveness in a real-world scenario, advancing AI systems that interact more naturally with humans.
- Strength: innovative integration of linguistic and cognitive theory with clear writing and appropriate quantitative and qualitative evaluation.
- Strength: thorough data and model analysis, providing confidence in results and cognitive-science insight into self-directed learning.
- Main limitation: scalability concerns—computationally strained and demonstrated only in a small, low-dimensional domain, raising generalization doubts.
- Requested fix: authors should demonstrate or argue how the method scales or yields broader insights applicable to larger ML/NIPS problems.
- Future work: extend to complex domains (NLP, computer vision) and investigate sequential question-asking and follow-up adaptation.
- Minor comments: perform nested model comparisons on lesioned-model log-likelihoods for significance; consider related semantics/pragmatics work (e.g., Questions under Discussion).

- Arguments against acceptance: unclear scalability/generalization, lack of clear scaling strategy, insufficient evaluation of sequential question-asking.
