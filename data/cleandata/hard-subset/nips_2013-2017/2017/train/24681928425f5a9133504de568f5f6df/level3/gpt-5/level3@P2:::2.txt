Summary and relation to prior work
This paper models human question-asking as program generation. Questions are represented as executable, typed lambda-calculus/LISP programs defined by a hand-crafted probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG). The model assigns probabilities to questions via a log-linear energy over features: expected information gain (EIG) under a Bayesian ideal observer, a complexity prior (−log PCFG probability), and answer-type preferences (Boolean/Number/Color/Location), with an additional “relevance” filter. Parameters are fit by approximate maximum likelihood using importance sampling over programs; evaluation uses leave-one-context-out cross-validation on a Battleship-style information-search dataset [19], where people asked one-word-answerable questions to reduce uncertainty. Quantitatively, the full model outperforms lesioned variants (removing informativeness, complexity, or type features) in predicting held-out human question distributions; qualitatively, it samples novel, plausible questions not seen in the data. The results support a cognitive account in which people trade off informativeness and complexity, explaining why EIG alone poorly predicts frequencies.

The work connects to formal semantics of questions as partitions over worlds [6] and to pragmatics/QUD traditions [18, Roberts 1996], to program-based approaches in cognitive science [5, 17] and semantic parsing for QA [24, 12]. It is complementary to visual question generation (e.g., [16, 23]) and goal-oriented dialog (e.g., [25, 1]) but focuses on intentional, utility-driven question formation in a micro-world.

Quality
- Technically sound overall. The formulation as a log-linear model with EIG, complexity, and type features is clear, and the evaluation protocol (LOOCV across contexts) is appropriate for small datasets.
- Inference/optimization is heavy and approximate (importance sampling over a large compositional space, approximate Z). The paper provides reasonable details (150k sampled programs; 100k iterations; bootstrap over Z). It would help to report sensitivity to the proposal distribution, convergence diagnostics, and variance across random seeds.
- The design choice fcomp(x)=−log q(x) couples the complexity prior to the proposal grammar; robustness to alternative complexity measures (e.g., raw length, AST size, MDL) should be shown.
- The statistical comparison has promising bootstrap evidence, but a per-context paired analysis (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank on held-out log-likelihoods) would provide a clearer significance test across contexts than bootstrapping Z alone.

Clarity
- The paper is well written and organized. The grammar, typing, and feature set are described clearly. It is helpful that the authors include qualitative samples and correlations.
- A few clarifications would help: reconcile the near-equal average Spearman correlations for the full vs information-agnostic models (0.64 vs 0.65) with the log-likelihood ranking; specify the mutual-information threshold used to define “equivalent” questions; and provide learning curves or convergence plots.
- Minor: briefly summarize which domain-specific productions were required and which are domain-general to make portability clearer.

Originality
- The core idea—treating question asking as probabilistic program synthesis with utility and compositional priors—is novel and intellectually appealing, bridging formal semantics, cognitive modeling, and active learning.
- Prior work models question meaning as partitions and question answering as program execution, but the generative, goal-driven question production in a typed program space with learned trade-offs appears new.

Significance
- Cognitive science contribution is strong: the model predicts human question distributions and explains the failure of informativeness alone by quantifying a complexity trade-off.
- For the NeurIPS audience, impact is promising but currently limited by scale and domain specificity. Demonstrations outside a small micro-world, or integration with learned semantic parsing/NL interfaces, would broaden significance for ML and dialog systems.

Strengths
- Elegant, compositional representation with executable semantics.
- Clear probabilistic formulation that jointly captures informativeness, complexity, and type preferences; complexity emerges as a key predictor.
- Both quantitative prediction and qualitative generation (including novel, clever questions).
- Situates well in formal semantics/pragmatics and program-induction literatures.

Weaknesses
- Scalability: importance sampling over a large program space with hand-designed PCFG is computationally strained; two contexts and some rules were dropped.
- Domain engineering: the grammar and typing include task-specific constructs; the NL-to-program mapping is assumed rather than learned.
- Evaluation breadth: limited baselines; reliance on correlations and approximate LL; no human evaluation of generated questions.
- Complexity metric is tied to the proposal grammar; robustness not fully established.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, principled approach that advances computational accounts of question asking.
- Strong evidence that combining informativeness and complexity explains human behavior.
- Clear writing, thoughtful connections to prior work, and reproducible formulation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited domain and computational scalability, with unclear path to broader ML/NIPS applications in this submission.
- Evaluation lacks stronger statistical tests across contexts and robustness checks for complexity and informativeness features.
- Missing comparisons to additional baselines (e.g., EIG with explicit length cost, learned PCFG priors, or simple neural baselines conditioned on context).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide per-context paired significance tests of held-out log-likelihoods across models (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank; report effect sizes and CIs).
- Test robustness of the complexity feature: replace −log q(x) with (i) program length, (ii) AST node count, (iii) MDL-style code length; and/or learn PCFG rule probabilities from data.
- Add an EIG-with-cost baseline (EIG − λ·length), and a non-compositional baseline (e.g., context-conditioned n-gram/character LMs) to further support the compositional prior’s added value.
- Report optimization diagnostics: sensitivity to sample size (150k), question-length cap, number of iterations, and different random seeds; include coverage metrics for the sampled program set.
- Extend the informativity objective with task-relevant utilities (value of information under 0–1 identification loss), and explore alternative utilities (e.g., expected error reduction).
- Ground the model in natural language by integrating a learned semantic parser (e.g., [24]) or by evaluating human ratings of generated questions for usefulness, clarity, and answerability.
- Connect more explicitly to sequential, discourse-level pragmatics (Questions Under Discussion; see Roberts 1996 and related QUD work such as Ginzburg 2012) and consider multi-step question sequences.

Overall assessment
A creative, well-executed cognitive model with a compelling technical framing. I view this as a solid contribution with clear strengths in originality and clarity, and moderate concerns about scalability and breadth for the NeurIPS audience. Strengthening the statistical comparisons, robustness analyses, and outlining a clearer path to larger domains or NL integration would substantially increase its impact.
