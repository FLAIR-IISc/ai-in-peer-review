

This paper proposes a formal model of human question asking by treating questions as executable programs that output answers when run on a world state. The authors apply this approach to a constrained Battleship game where human players ask questions to identify ship configurations. The core contribution is a probabilistic model that combines expected information gain (EIG), question complexity, answer type, and relevance features to predict human question choices and generate novel questions.

The paper's main strength lies in its clear formalization of question asking as program generation, which provides a computationally grounded framework for modeling a cognitive process. The authors demonstrate that their model successfully predicts human question frequencies in the Battleship game (Spearman correlation 0.64) and can generate novel, human-like questions not present in the training data. The model variants provide useful insights into the importance of different features, particularly showing that question complexity is as important as informativeness for human question selection.

The work relates well to previous research in active learning [21], cognitive science [8, 14], and program synthesis [17, 24]. It extends beyond simple active learning label requests by modeling open-ended natural language questions, addressing a gap in current AI systems that typically use canned questions [25, 1]. The compositional semantics (drawing on Montagovian frameworks [10]) effectively captures the structure of human questions, and the grammar-based approach enables novel question generation.

However, several limitations significantly impact the paper's contribution. The task domain is highly constrained: the Battleship game has fixed rules, limited ship configurations, and minimal ambiguity. This severely limits the generalizability of the approach. The core ideas are relatively straightforward applications of well-established techniques (log-linear models, Bayesian information gain) to a narrow problem space. The paper does not address how this approach would scale to more complex, real-world question-asking scenarios with significant ambiguity and diverse contexts.

The novelty is limited. While the paper's specific application to question asking is novel, the underlying technical approach (using compositional semantics and log-linear models) is not innovative for NIPS. The paper doesn't introduce new theoretical insights or algorithms but rather applies existing methods to a specific problem. The connection to broader machine learning challenges is weak, as the work doesn't address fundamental ML challenges like scalability, generalization, or computational efficiency.

The significance for the NIPS community is questionable. The paper focuses on a very narrow cognitive phenomenon in an artificial setting rather than advancing the state of the art in machine learning or AI. While the cognitive science angle is interesting, NIPS emphasizes machine learning contributions, and this work doesn't provide new learning algorithms or significant improvements to existing methods. The generated questions are clever within the Battleship context but don't demonstrate the kind of human-like question asking that would be valuable for practical AI systems.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with proper evaluation methods (leave-one-out cross-validation, bootstrapped log-likelihood comparisons). The grammar design and feature engineering are thoughtful, but the simplicity of the task undermines the significance of these contributions. The authors acknowledge limitations regarding domain specificity, but the paper doesn't convincingly argue for the broader applicability of their approach.

In terms of the four criteria:
- Quality: The technical execution is solid but the constrained domain limits the overall quality of the contribution.
- Clarity: Excellent presentation of the model and results.
- Originality: Limited novelty; the paper applies known techniques to a narrow problem.
- Significance: Minimal impact on the field; the work doesn't advance machine learning practice or theory.

The paper's core contribution is a specific application of compositional semantics and probabilistic modeling to a constrained cognitive task. While technically sound and interesting from a cognitive science perspective, it doesn't provide a significant advance for the NIPS community. The narrow scope and lack of broader implications make it unlikely to interest most NIPS readers, who would expect more substantial contributions to machine learning theory or practice. The work would be better suited for a cognitive science venue rather than NIPS.

The model's ability to generate novel questions within the Battleship context is impressive for that domain, but it doesn't translate to the kind of general question-asking capability that would be valuable for AI systems. Without addressing how this approach would scale to more complex, real-world scenarios, the paper's contribution remains limited. The authors' claim that this represents "a step toward more capable active learning machines" is overstated given the artificial constraints of their task.
