- Novel framing: models human question asking as program generation in a “Battleship” grid domain using a hand-designed PCFG and lambda-calculus.
- Core factors modeled: informativeness (information gain), complexity (grammar probability), and answer type are used to predict question choice.
- Modeling strengths: integrates linguistic theory and cognitive principles of compositionality and computability.
- Quantitative evaluation: uses lesioned models and leave-one-out cross-validation to test factor contributions.
- Key empirical result: all three factors matter, with complexity surprisingly the strongest predictor of human questions.
- Qualitative result: model can generate novel, plausible questions beyond the training set.
- Related work positioning: builds on active learning and dialog systems literature and contrasts with data-hungry visual question generation methods.
- Major limitation: scalability concerns due to reliance on a hand-designed grammar and computational intensity, demonstrated only in a small, low-dimensional domain.
- Requested improvements: authors should present scaling strategies (e.g., grammar learning or approximation techniques) or specify scenarios where limited scalability is acceptable.
- Minor suggestions: perform nested model comparisons for statistical strength and discuss sequential question asking / "Questions under Discussion" literature.
- Acceptance arguments: innovative, strong integration of cognitive science and computation, clear writing, insightful about human question-asking.
- Rejection arguments: limited scalability and dependence on a hand-designed grammar.
- Overall verdict: strong and thought-provoking paper deserving consideration if it better addresses potential for broader impact and scalability.
