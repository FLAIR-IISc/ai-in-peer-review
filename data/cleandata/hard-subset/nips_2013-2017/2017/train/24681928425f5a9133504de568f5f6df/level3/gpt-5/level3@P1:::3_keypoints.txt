- Proposes a probabilistic model treating questions as executable compositional programs with a log-linear energy (features: EIG, complexity, answer type, relevance).
- Implemented in a Battleship-like domain using a Bayesian ideal observer for EIG and trained with importance-sampled maximum likelihood.
- Situates work at intersection of program induction/semantic parsing, active learning, and linguistic/cognitive theories of questions.
- Strength: clear formalization that supports compositionality, executability, and informativeness evaluation over worlds.
- Strength: principled integration of Bayesian EIG with a grammar-based prior capturing complexity and answer-type preferences.
- Strength: generates novel, interpretable, context-sensitive questions beyond training examples.
- Strength: thorough lesioned-baseline comparisons showing complexity preference is necessary; relevance and type priors help.
- Strength: reproducibility — grammar, datasets, and inference are described in sufficient detail.
- Weakness: ambiguous contribution of informativeness — information-agnostic model correlates nearly as well with human frequencies and bootstrap advantage is modest.
- Suggestion: report additional metrics (top-k recall/precision, calibration) and per-context statistical tests to isolate EIG’s contribution.
- Weakness: complexity measure equals −log q(x) under a uniform PCFG, tying “complexity” to specific grammar design choices.
- Suggestion: evaluate alternative complexity measures (program length, AST node count, MDL under different priors) and run sensitivity analyses.
- Weakness: limited generality — demonstrated in a single domain and two contexts were dropped for computational reasons.
- Suggestion: add a second (even toy) domain or a transfer experiment and include approximate EIG for the dropped contexts.
- Weakness: not end-to-end with natural language — models semantic forms rather than language output.
- Suggestion: add a proof-of-concept semantic parser/generator or human judgments on fluency/appropriateness of sampled questions.
- Weakness: related-work positioning could more explicitly contrast novelty and predictive goals relative to prior goal-based question models and semantic-program QA/VQA work.
- Suggestion: include a structured comparison clarifying novelty of program synthesis vs. template selection, sparse-data learning vs. large supervised datasets, and predictive vs. normative evaluation.
- Technical quality: formalization, likelihood, gradient, and importance-sampling are sound; results sensitive to approximations and sparse counts but mitigated via bootstrapping.
- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized; recommend an appendix on complexity sensitivity and extra evaluation metrics.
- Originality: novel and compelling combination of question-as-programs with a learned log-linear model over informativeness and concision.
- Significance: valuable for AI and cognitive science; demonstrates creative, human-like question generation, but claims about human informativeness-complexity tradeoff remain suggestive.
- Questions for authors include sensitivity to grammar/prior, per-context EIG vs. complexity effects, feasibility of approximate EIG, and handling of semantic equivalences.
- Arguments for acceptance: principled framework, creative predictive generation, meaningful baselines/ablations, and cross-disciplinary interest.
- Arguments against acceptance: mixed evidence for informativeness, grammar-entwined complexity measure, single-domain evaluation, and missing end-to-end language interface.
- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on strengthening empirical evidence for informativeness and robustness of the complexity prior.
