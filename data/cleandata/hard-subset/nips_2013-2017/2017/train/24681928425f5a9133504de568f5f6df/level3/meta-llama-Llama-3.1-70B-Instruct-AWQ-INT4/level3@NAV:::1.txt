This paper presents a novel approach to modeling human question asking abilities in computational terms. The authors propose a probabilistic model that treats questions as formal programs that, when executed on the state of the world, output an answer. The model is evaluated using a data set of natural language questions asked by humans in an information-search game similar to Battleship.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of a cognitive model capable of constructing human-like questions. The model specifies a probability distribution over a complex, compositional space of programs, favoring concise programs that help the agent learn in the current context. The authors demonstrate that their approach can predict what questions people will ask and can creatively produce novel questions that were not present in the training set.

The paper's strengths include its novel approach to modeling human question asking, its ability to capture the compositionality and computability of questions, and its evaluation using a large data set of human questions. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the model's performance, including its ability to predict human question frequencies and generate novel questions.

However, the paper's weaknesses include its limited scope, as it only addresses a specific type of question asking in a highly constrained domain. The model's reliance on a grammar-based approach may also limit its ability to generalize to other domains or tasks. Additionally, the paper's focus on a simple, logical task may not be representative of more complex, real-world question asking scenarios.

In terms of the NIPS community's interest, I believe that this paper may not be of significant interest, as it primarily focuses on a specific application of AI to a narrow domain, rather than presenting a more general, theoretical contribution to the field of machine learning or AI. However, the paper's approach to modeling human question asking may be of interest to researchers in cognitive science, linguistics, or human-computer interaction.
