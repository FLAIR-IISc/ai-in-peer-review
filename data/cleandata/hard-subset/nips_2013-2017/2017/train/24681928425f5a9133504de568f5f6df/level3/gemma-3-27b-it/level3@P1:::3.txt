This paper introduces a computational framework for modeling human question asking, proposing to treat questions as programs that, when executed, yield an answer. The authors argue this approach moves beyond simple label-requesting active learning to capture the richness and creativity of human inquiries, specifically focusing on balancing informativeness and complexity. They implement this model within the context of a Battleship-style game, predicting human question choices and generating novel, plausible questions.

The work builds on a strong foundation in active learning (Settles, 2009) and the broader goal of creating AI capable of intelligent information acquisition. Unlike earlier active learning systems limited to label requests, and current dialog systems relying on canned responses (Bordes et al., 2017), this approach aims for more flexible and contextually relevant queries. The framing of questions as programs connects to formal semantic work on question meaning (Groenendijk & Stokhof, 1997) and program induction (Lake et al., 2015).  However, the authors do not extensively discuss related work in goal-directed question asking, such as Hawkins et al. (2015) who proposed a similar model focused on expected value of information, or the Question Under Discussion (QUD) framework by Roberts (1996) which provides a strong account of information seeking in dialogue.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. Technically, the model is sound, leveraging a probabilistic grammar to define a space of possible questions and a log-linear model to score them based on features representing informativeness (measured via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance. The experimental setup, using a human question dataset collected in a previous study (Gureckis et al., 2017), is well-defined, and the authors provide clear details to allow for reproducibility. The writing is clear and well-organized, and the results are presented in a digestible manner. The ability to generate novel questions, even if only qualitatively demonstrated, is a particularly promising aspect of this work, suggesting potential applications in AI systems needing to proactively gather information.

However, the paper also exhibits some weaknesses. While the authors demonstrate that the full model – incorporating all features – outperforms ablated versions in a held-out prediction task (measured by log-likelihood), the practical significance of this improvement is somewhat muted.  The correlation between the model’s energy scores and human question frequencies is moderate, and the bootstrap analysis reveals a considerable uncertainty (19% chance of the reported result being wrong) regarding the importance of the EIG feature. This suggests that the model’s ability to accurately predict *why* humans ask specific questions may be limited. The claim that the work reveals “how people balance informativeness and complexity” feels overstated given the limited statistical support. Furthermore, the reliance on a specific domain (Battleship) raises questions about the generalizability of the findings. While the grammar is designed to be compositional, the specific rules and features might be heavily influenced by the characteristics of the game.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to modeling question asking as program generation.
*   Demonstrates the ability to generate plausible, compositional questions, a significant step towards more intelligent AI systems.
*   Clear presentation and reproducibility.
*   Addresses a challenging problem in AI and cognitive science.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Statistical evidence supporting the importance of informativeness (EIG) is weak.
*   The claim about revealing human cognitive processes is not strongly supported by the results.
*   Missing related work, particularly in goal-directed question asking and information structure.
*   Generalizability to other domains remains an open question.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a compelling idea and solid execution. While the statistical support for certain claims could be stronger, the novelty of the approach and the demonstrated ability to generate creative questions make this a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to address the missing related work and temper the claims about uncovering human cognitive mechanisms. The paper provides a valuable foundation for future research into more human-like question asking agents.
