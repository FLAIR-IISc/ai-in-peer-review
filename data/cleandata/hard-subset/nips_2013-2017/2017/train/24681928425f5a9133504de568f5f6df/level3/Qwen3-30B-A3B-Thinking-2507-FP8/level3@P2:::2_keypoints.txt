- Presents a novel framework modeling human questions as executable programs that map world states to answers.  
- Formalizes questions in a lambda-calculus-like language to capture compositionality and computability.  
- Uses a PCFG with features for expected information gain (EIG), program complexity, answer type, and relevance.  
- Evaluated on a Battleship-style human question dataset (605 questions, 18 contexts); model predicts human frequencies (Spearman ρ=0.64).  
- Model can generate novel, plausible questions not seen in training.  

- Strength: Explains why informativeness alone fails—people trade off informativeness, complexity, and answer-type preferences.  
- Strength: Elegantly integrates linguistic compositionality and cognitive pragmatics.  
- Strength: Thorough evaluation with quantitative log-likelihood comparisons and qualitative novel-question generation.  
- Strength: Clear writing and well-structured model description (grammar rules, feature design).  

- Weakness: Scalability concerns—demonstrated only on a constrained Battleship domain with a small hypothesis space.  
- Weakness: High computational cost (150k samples per gradient step).  
- Weakness: Grammar requires substantial domain-specific engineering (e.g., ship-size predicates).  
- Weakness: No analysis of scalability or transfer to larger, real-world domains.  
- Weakness: Statistical comparisons lack nested model tests (e.g., likelihood ratio tests) despite bootstrap analyses.  

- Originality: Highly original—treats questions as executable programs and uses program synthesis for cognitive modeling.  
- Originality: Distinct from neural or canned-dialog approaches and from prior formal semantics work.  

- Significance: Strong cognitive science contribution explaining question-asking tradeoffs with a precise model.  
- Significance: Limited immediate impact for ML/NIPS due to narrow domain and lack of scalability evidence.  
- Significance: Potential to inform active learning, but no demonstrated advantages over neural baselines in larger settings.  

- Pro: First formal, predictive model of open-ended human question-asking with cognitive validity.  
- Pro: Demonstrates compositionality and complexity–informativeness tradeoff; strong quantitative and qualitative results.  
- Pro: Well-motivated interdisciplinary integration (linguistics, cognitive science, ML).  

- Con: Domain limitation to Battleship, computational burden, and domain-specific grammar design.  
- Con: Missing rigorous statistical tests and limited discussion of ML applicability.  

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: add a discussion of scalability and potential pathways (e.g., neural grammars), include nested model comparisons (likelihood ratio tests), and cite related work on sequential question-asking (e.g., Roberts 1996).
