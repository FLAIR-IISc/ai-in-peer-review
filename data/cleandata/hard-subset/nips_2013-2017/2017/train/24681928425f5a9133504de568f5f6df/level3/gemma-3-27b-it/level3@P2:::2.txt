This paper presents a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking, framing questions as programs that, when executed on a state of the world, yield an answer. The authors evaluate their approach on a dataset of questions asked by humans playing a modified Battleship game, demonstrating that their model can predict human question choices and even generate novel, plausible questions. The core contribution lies in integrating insights from linguistics and cognitive science – specifically, compositionality and computability – into a probabilistic program generation framework.

The work builds upon a rich history of research in active learning, where algorithms query for information to improve performance. However, unlike most existing approaches that focus on simple label requests, this paper tackles the more nuanced problem of modeling the complex, open-ended questions humans pose to learn about ambiguous situations. It also relates to, and differentiates itself from, work in dialog systems and visual question generation, highlighting the unique challenges of goal-directed, context-sensitive questioning in novel scenarios. The authors appropriately cite relevant work, including references to theoretical linguistics ([10, 18, 9]) and cognitive science ([4, 15, 14, 6]).

**Strengths:**

The paper’s primary strength is its innovative approach to modeling question asking. The formalization of questions as programs is a powerful and insightful idea, drawing connections between computational linguistics, cognitive science, and program synthesis. The use of a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) allows for compositional question generation, capturing the fact that complex questions are built from simpler components. The model's ability to generate novel questions, not present in the training data, is particularly impressive and suggests a degree of creativity. The quantitative evaluation, while limited in scope, provides compelling evidence that all three considered factors – informativeness, complexity, and answer type – contribute to human-like question generation. The writing is exceptionally clear and well-organized, making the complex concepts accessible. The connection to cognitive science, specifically the explanation of why informativeness alone is insufficient to predict human behavior, is a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

The most significant weakness is the limited scalability of the approach. The model is demonstrated in a relatively small, low-dimensional domain (a 6x6 grid), and it’s unclear whether the computational cost of program generation and evaluation would remain manageable in more complex environments. The reliance on a hand-designed grammar and the specific rules of the Battleship game raise concerns about generalization to other domains. While the authors acknowledge these limitations, further discussion of potential solutions or scenarios where the method might scale (e.g., by learning the grammar from data or using approximate inference techniques) would strengthen the paper.

The statistical significance of the differences between the models could be further strengthened. While the authors mention bootstrapping, a more formal nested model comparison (e.g., using likelihood ratio tests) would provide more conclusive evidence for the importance of each feature.

**Originality:**

The paper presents a highly original approach to question asking. While elements of the framework – PCFGs, lambda calculus, and information gain – have been used in related fields, the combination of these elements to model *question generation* as program synthesis is novel. The explicit focus on balancing informativeness and complexity, informed by cognitive science principles, also distinguishes this work from existing approaches.

**Significance:**

The results are significant in advancing our understanding of self-directed learning and the cognitive processes underlying question asking. The paper provides a valuable computational model for investigating the interplay between informativeness, complexity, and pragmatic considerations in human inquiry. While the current implementation may not be directly applicable to large-scale machine learning problems, the underlying principles and framework could potentially inspire new approaches to active learning, dialog systems, and other areas where intelligent question asking is crucial. The model's ability to generate novel questions hints at the possibility of creating more creative and adaptable AI agents.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and insightful approach to modeling human question asking.
*   The integration of linguistic and cognitive science principles is a significant strength.
*   The model demonstrates promising results in predicting and generating human-like questions.
*   The writing is exceptionally clear and well-organized.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The limited scalability of the approach raises concerns about its applicability to real-world problems.
*   The reliance on a hand-designed grammar limits the generalizability of the model.
*   The statistical significance of the model comparisons could be strengthened.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field. While the scalability limitations are a concern, the novelty of the approach, the insightful integration of cognitive science principles, and the compelling results warrant acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper, with a minor request for the authors to consider performing nested model comparisons for stronger statistical validation and to address the scalability limitations in the discussion. The work offers a valuable framework for understanding human question asking and has the potential to inspire new approaches to intelligent agents and active learning systems.
