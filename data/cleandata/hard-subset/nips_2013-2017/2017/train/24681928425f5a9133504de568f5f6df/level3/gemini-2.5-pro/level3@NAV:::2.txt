This paper introduces a novel computational model of human question-asking, framing the task as probabilistic program generation. The authors propose a system that models questions as programs in a lambda-calculus formalism, generated by a hand-designed Probabilistic Context-Free Grammar (PCFG) within a "Battleship" grid-world domain. The core of the model is a probability distribution over questions that balances three factors: expected information gain (EIG), complexity (derived from the PCFG), and answer type. This work builds on concepts in active learning by moving beyond simple label requests to compositional queries, and contributes to cognitive science by formalizing a model of self-directed inquiry.

The paper's primary strength is its innovative and technically sound integration of principles from linguistic theory, cognitive science, and probabilistic modeling. The writing is exceptionally clear, making the complex framework easy to follow. The claims are well-supported by a strong evaluation methodology, which appropriately combines quantitative results from leave-one-out cross-validation of lesioned models with qualitative evidence of the model's ability to generate novel, plausible questions. A key original finding is that complexity is a more dominant predictor of human questions than informativeness alone, advancing our understanding of the cognitive constraints on inquiry.

The most significant weakness of the work is its scalability, which limits its potential impact for the broader NIPS community. The reliance on a hand-engineered grammar and a computationally intensive inference procedure is demonstrated only in a small, low-dimensional domain. This raises substantial doubts about the framework's applicability to the larger, more complex problem spaces typical in machine learning research. While the paper is a strong contribution to computational cognitive science, its significance for a general ML audience is not clearly established. The authors should explicitly address how this approach might scale or identify specific ML domains where this knowledge-intensive modeling provides unique advantages over current methods.

For improvement, the authors should conduct nested model comparisons on the log-likelihoods to more rigorously establish the statistical significance of their ablation results. Furthermore, the work could be better contextualized by relating it to the formal semantics and pragmatics literature on sequential dialogue, such as the "Questions under Discussion" framework. In summary, this is a high-quality, original paper that is well-executed, but its limited demonstrated scope is a major concern for acceptance at NIPS.
