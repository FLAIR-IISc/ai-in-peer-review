This paper presents a computational model of human question-asking, framing it as a problem of probabilistic program generation. The authors represent questions as programs in a compositional, artificial language which, when executed on a world state, produce an answer. The model is applied to a dataset of human-generated questions in a constrained, Battleship-like game. A log-linear model is used to predict which questions humans will ask, based on four features: expected information gain (EIG), a penalty for zero-EIG questions, answer type (e.g., Boolean, numerical), and whether the question references game board features. The motivation for this work is situated within active learning, aiming to move beyond simple label requests to richer, more structured queries.

The paper’s primary strengths lie in its clarity and technical execution. The framing of question-asking as program synthesis is an elegant and powerful way to capture compositionality and generativity. The probabilistic model is well-defined, and the ablation studies effectively demonstrate that both informativeness (EIG) and complexity are necessary components for predicting human behavior. The quality of the work is sound, presenting a complete and well-supported analysis within its chosen domain. The manuscript is clearly written and provides sufficient detail to understand the methodology.

However, the paper’s main weaknesses relate to its originality and significance for the NIPS community. While the components are combined effectively, the core ideas are not novel. The use of compositional semantics, expected information gain as a utility function, and log-linear models to combine features are all well-established techniques. The primary concern is the choice of task. The Battleship game is a highly constrained, logically unambiguous toy problem with a small, discrete state space. This environment makes the definition of a formal grammar and the calculation of EIG tractable, but it sidesteps the most difficult challenges in machine-based question generation, such as ambiguity, common sense reasoning, and operating in vast, unstructured environments.

Consequently, the significance of the results for the broader machine learning community is limited. The reliance on a hand-crafted grammar for a simple, fully-observable-in-principle domain makes it unclear how this approach would scale or generalize to more complex, real-world problems that are the focus of NIPS. While the work represents a solid contribution to computational cognitive modeling, its limited scope and reliance on bespoke engineering for a simple task may fail to interest researchers seeking scalable and general-purpose learning algorithms.
