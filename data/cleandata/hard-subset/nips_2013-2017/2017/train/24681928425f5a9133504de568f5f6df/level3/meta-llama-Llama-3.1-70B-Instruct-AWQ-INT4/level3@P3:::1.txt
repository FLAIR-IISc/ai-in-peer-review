The paper "Question Asking as Program Generation" presents a novel approach to modeling human question asking abilities in computational terms. The authors propose a probabilistic model that treats questions as formal programs, which can be executed on the state of the world to output an answer. The model is evaluated using a data set of natural language questions asked by human participants in an information-search game, similar to "Battleship".

The paper's main idea is to recast the problem of question asking as one of program synthesis, where questions are represented as syntactically well-formed programs that can be composed from conceptual building blocks. The authors demonstrate that this approach can capture the productivity and creativity of human question asking, and can be used to generate novel, human-like questions that were not present in the training data.

The model is based on a simple programming language, inspired by lambda calculus and LISP, which allows for the representation of questions as programs. The authors define a grammar that specifies the space of allowable questions/programs, and a probabilistic generative model that aims to predict which questions people will ask. The model uses a log-linear model over four simple question features: expected information gain, answer type, complexity, and relevance.

The paper's strengths include its novel approach to modeling human question asking, its ability to capture the productivity and creativity of human question asking, and its evaluation using a large data set of natural language questions. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the model's performance, including its ability to predict the distribution of human questions and to generate novel, human-like questions.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The task of question asking in the "Battleship" game is highly constrained and logically unambiguous, which may limit the model's applicability to more complex and open-ended question asking tasks. Additionally, the model's reliance on a simple programming language and a predefined grammar may limit its ability to capture the full range of human question asking abilities.

In terms of originality, the paper's approach to modeling human question asking as program generation is novel and innovative. However, the use of probabilistic models and log-linear models is not new, and the paper's contribution is more in the application of these techniques to the specific task of question asking.

In terms of significance, the paper's results are important for understanding human question asking abilities and for developing more advanced active learning algorithms. However, the paper's impact may be limited by its focus on a specific task and its reliance on a simple programming language.

Overall, the paper is well-written and clearly organized, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of their model's performance. However, the paper's limitations and the simplicity of the task may limit its impact and applicability.
