- Presents a computational model framing human question-asking as program generation in a compositional LISP-like language.  
- Evaluated on a Battleship-like dataset where questions resolve ambiguity about hidden grid objects.  
- Uses a probabilistic log-linear model over program space with features: expected information gain (EIG), complexity, answer type, and a relevance filter.  
- Complexity derived from a probabilistic context-free grammar; ablation shows informativeness and complexity together best predict human questions.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and motivates extending active learning to richer, structured queries.  
- Framing questions as programs elegantly captures compositional and generative aspects of human inquiry.  
- Technical approach is sound; experiments (leave-one-out CV, ablation) and qualitative examples are solid.  
- Core ideas (program representations, EIG) are not novel and build on existing cognitive science and active learning work.  
- The Battleship task is a toy, highly simplified domain, limiting generalizability and requiring a hand-crafted grammar.  
- Findings—that humans trade off informativeness and simplicity—are intuitive and unsurprising.  
- The work aligns more with computational cognitive science than with NIPS-style contributions in novel algorithms or large-scale ML advances.  
- Overall: a solid, well-executed modeling paper but limited technical novelty and relevance for a top-tier ML conference.
