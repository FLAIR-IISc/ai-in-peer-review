- Novel computational model framing human question asking as probabilistic program generation.
- Questions represented as compositional programs that return answers when executed on a world state.
- Log-linear probability model combines features: Expected Information Gain (EIG), complexity (program length), answer type, and task relevance.
- Evaluated on a Battleship-like game dataset of human natural language questions.
- Positions work as an extension of active learning and dialogue systems toward more flexible, compositional queries.
- Strength: original and powerful conceptualization with high potential impact for AI and cognitive science.
- Strength: model is elegant and generative, producing novel, human-like questions not seen in training.
- Strength: technically sound, clearly written, well-organized, and appears reproducible (dataset URL and details provided).
- Weakness: experimental support for the claim that people balance informativeness and complexity is mixed and not robust (81% bootstrap consistency raises concern).
- Weakness: correlation results indicate complexity dominates predictions (full model ρ=0.64 vs information-agnostic ρ=0.65), implying marginal contribution from EIG.
- Weakness: sparse data and conflicting log-likelihood vs correlation analyses weaken conclusions about EIG’s role.
- Weakness: omission of key related work (Hawkins et al., 2015 on goal-based Bayesian question asking; Roberts, 1996 QUD framework).
- Arguments for acceptance: highly original framework, demonstrates generation of complex/contextual questions, clear and reproducible presentation.
- Arguments for rejection: insufficient and contradictory experimental evidence for EIG’s role, and missing comparisons to important prior literature.
- Recommendation: accept with revisions to temper claims about EIG’s role and to incorporate/discuss the omitted prior work.
