This paper introduces a cognitive model for generating human-like questions in the context of an information-search game, similar to Battleship. The model represents questions as formal programs that, when executed on the state of the world, output an answer. The authors propose a probabilistic generative model that predicts which questions people will ask, given a context, and can creatively produce novel questions that were not present in the training set.

The paper is well-motivated, as it addresses the problem of active learning, where a learner is able to query an oracle to obtain information that is expected to improve performance. The authors argue that most work on active machine learning has focused on relatively simple types of information requests, whereas humans often learn by asking richer, more complex questions.

The model is based on a simple programming language, inspired by lambda calculus and LISP, which captures the compositionality and computability of questions. The authors define a grammar that defines the space of allowable questions/programs and a probabilistic generative model that samples context-sensitive, relevant programs from this space.

The model is evaluated on a data set of 605 natural language questions asked by human participants in the information-search game. The results show that the model can predict the frequencies of human questions given a game context and can also synthesize novel human-like questions that were not present in the training set.

Strengths:

* The paper introduces a novel approach to modeling question asking as program generation, which is a promising direction for active learning research.
* The model is well-evaluated on a large data set of human questions, demonstrating its ability to predict and generate human-like questions.
* The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of the model and its components, making it easy to follow and understand.

Weaknesses:

* The task is very simple, highly constrained, and logically unambiguous, which may limit the model's applicability to more complex and nuanced domains.
* The core ideas of the paper are not particularly surprising or innovative, as the use of compositional semantics and log-linear models is well-established in the field.
* The reviewer is not convinced that the paper will interest the NIPS community, as the ideas and techniques presented are not particularly novel or groundbreaking.
