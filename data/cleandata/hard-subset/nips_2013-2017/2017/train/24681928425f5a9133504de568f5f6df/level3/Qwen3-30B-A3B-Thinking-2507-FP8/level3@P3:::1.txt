

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper proposes a computational model of human question asking by representing questions as programs that, when executed on a world state, output answers. The authors model question generation as a probabilistic program synthesis task, using a log-linear model over four features: expected information gain (EIG), answer type (Boolean, numerical, color, location), complexity (log probability under the grammar), and relevance (whether the question references game features). The model is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans playing a Battleship-like game to identify ship configurations.

The work builds on several strands of prior research. The compositional semantics approach connects to Montagovian grammar and formal semantics in linguistics [10, 18], while the program representation of questions relates to computational question answering [24, 12] and probabilistic program induction [13]. The active learning motivation aligns with previous work on active learning [21], though the authors correctly note that existing active learning systems typically query for supervised labels rather than open-ended questions.

The paper's strengths include a clear theoretical framework for modeling question asking, a well-defined probabilistic model that successfully predicts human question frequencies (with a Spearman correlation of 0.64), and the ability to generate novel, human-like questions not present in the training data. The evaluation of different model variants provides useful insights about the importance of question complexity and answer type. The grammar-based approach also captures the productivity of human question asking, as 15% of human questions were novel across contexts.

However, the paper's core contributions face significant limitations. The task is highly constrained (a simple Battleship game with only 3 ships on a 6x6 grid), with logically unambiguous questions that require minimal linguistic understanding. The artificial language with compositional semantics is a significant simplification of natural language question asking. The model's features (EIG, complexity, answer type) are relatively straightforward and not particularly novel for active learning research. The paper's claim that "the model specifies a probability distribution over a complex, compositional space of programs" is misleading - the space of programs is actually quite simple and constrained by the specific grammar designed for this domain.

The paper's significance for the NIPS community is questionable. While the authors frame this as a step toward "more human-like question asking in machines," the domain is too narrow and artificial to be broadly applicable. The model does not address the challenges of natural language understanding, dialogue context, or generalization to new domains - all critical for practical applications. The paper's most novel aspect (the grammar-based program representation) is not sufficiently differentiated from prior work in program synthesis for question answering [12, 24].

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound but the claims are overstated. The experimental results are well-presented, but the evaluation is limited to a single constrained game. The authors do acknowledge limitations (e.g., "the grammar is specific to the Battleship domain"), but the paper overstates the generalizability of their approach.

The clarity is good, with clear explanations of the program representation and model components. The writing is well-organized, though the technical depth is limited by the simplicity of the task.

Originality is low - the core idea of modeling questions as programs with a log-linear model over features is not particularly novel. The grammar approach is similar to work in probabilistic programming for cognitive science [5, 13], and the features used are standard in active learning. The paper fails to adequately distinguish itself from related work in program synthesis for question answering [24].

Significance is limited. The model predicts human question frequencies in a very constrained game, but it's unclear how this advances active learning or machine question asking in more realistic settings. The "novel" questions generated (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") are simple extensions of the grammar, not truly creative. The paper does not demonstrate how this approach would scale to more complex domains or how it would improve actual machine learning performance.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a technically sound model for a specific constrained task, its contribution is marginal for the NIPS community. The problem is too narrow, the approach too simple, and the implications for broader AI are overstated. The paper would be better suited for a cognitive science or computational linguistics venue rather than NIPS. The model's ability to predict human question frequencies is interesting but does not represent a significant advance in machine learning or AI. I recommend rejection as the work does not meet the threshold for contribution to the NIPS community.
