This paper proposes a model of human question asking, framing questions as programs generated from a compositional grammar and evaluating them based on expected information gain and other simple features. While the motivation – modeling human question asking to improve active learning – is reasonable, the core ideas feel relatively straightforward and the task itself is highly constrained, leading to a limited scope for significant contribution.

The paper builds upon a well-established line of work in active learning (Settles, 2008), which typically focuses on selecting which labels to request. This work expands that to consider richer, more natural language-like queries, aligning with broader efforts to make active learning more human-centered. Related work in dialog systems (Serban et al., 2017) and visual question generation (Jain et al., 2017; Mostafazadeh et al., 2016) are also acknowledged. However, the authors position their work as distinct, emphasizing the goal-directed and context-sensitive nature of their approach, and the ability to *generate* questions rather than simply selecting from a pre-defined set. The connection to formal linguistics and the “language of thought” hypothesis (Fodor, 1975; Piantadosi et al., 2016) is also clearly articulated, providing a theoretical grounding for the compositional approach.

The strengths of the paper lie in its clear articulation of the problem and its attempt to formalize question asking as program generation. The use of a probabilistic grammar provides a principled way to generate a space of possible questions, and the inclusion of features like expected information gain and complexity attempts to capture key aspects of human question selection. The empirical evaluation, although limited to a single game domain, demonstrates that the model can predict human question frequencies and generate novel, plausible questions. The ablation study, comparing different feature sets, provides some insight into the relative importance of informativeness, complexity, and answer type.

However, the weaknesses are substantial. The task – Battleship – is extremely constrained. The space of possible questions, even with the grammar, is relatively small, and the underlying logic is unambiguous. This limits the generalizability of the findings. While the model can generate *novel* questions, the novelty appears to be largely within the confines of the task. The questions generated, as shown in Figure 3, are reasonably human-like but hardly exhibit the creativity or open-endedness of real-world questions. 

Furthermore, the core ideas – modeling questions as programs and using expected information gain – are not particularly novel. The notion of representing questions formally dates back decades (Groenendijk & Stokhof, 1997), and the application of information gain to question selection is a standard technique in active learning. The primary contribution seems to be the combination of these ideas within a specific, compositional framework and the demonstration that it works reasonably well in a limited domain. However, this combination doesn’t represent a significant leap forward.

The evaluation is also limited. The log-likelihood improvements reported in Table 1, while statistically significant, appear modest. The correlation between model predictions and human question frequencies (0.64) is respectable but not overwhelming. The qualitative evaluation of generated questions relies on subjective judgment.  A more rigorous evaluation would involve comparing the model to simpler baselines or exploring its performance in more complex domains.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Clear articulation of the problem and a principled approach to modeling question asking.
*   Demonstration that the model can predict human question frequencies and generate plausible questions.
*   Ablation study provides some insight into feature importance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The task is highly constrained and lacks real-world complexity.
*   The core ideas are not particularly novel.
*   The evaluation is limited and does not demonstrate a substantial advance over existing work.
*   The paper’s scope is narrow, and the findings are unlikely to have broad impact within the NIPS community.

**Overall:**

I believe this paper is unlikely to be of significant interest to the NIPS community. While technically sound and clearly written, it addresses a relatively simple problem with ideas that are not particularly groundbreaking. The limited scope and modest results do not justify acceptance to a conference focused on cutting-edge research in machine learning and artificial intelligence. The paper might be more suitable for a conference focused on cognitive modeling or computational linguistics.
