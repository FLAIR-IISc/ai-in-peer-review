- Paper frames human question-asking as probabilistic program generation, using a hand-crafted compositional grammar to generate candidate question-programs.
- Core model is a log-linear distribution over programs scored by features including Expected Information Gain (EIG), program complexity, answer type, and relevance.
- Evaluated on a "Battleship"-like toy domain with human-generated natural language questions; parameters fit to data and compared to ablated models.
- Main empirical claim: both informativeness (EIG) and program complexity are necessary to explain human question-asking behavior.

- Strength: elegant, compositional formulation modeling questions as executable programs that supports generative and interpretable question generation.
- Strength: clear motivation to move active learning/dialogue systems beyond simple label queries toward goal-directed, structured questioning.
- Strength: systematic methodology with interpretable features, ablation studies, and qualitative results showing novel, sensible question generation.

- Weakness: technical components (EIG, compositional grammar, log-linear model) are well-established, so novelty is in the combination/application rather than new methods.
- Weakness: experimental domain is highly constrained and simplistic, relying on hand-crafted grammar and domain engineering that limit scalability and realism.
- Weakness: limited relevance to the NIPS/ML community due to toy domain, lack of novel algorithms, and manual NL-to-program translation.

- Paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized.

- Argument for acceptance: presents an elegant computational framework and a strong proof-of-concept linking active learning and compositional language-of-thought ideas.
- Argument for acceptance: convincing results and ablations demonstrating the roles of informativeness and complexity.
- Argument for rejection: primarily reuses known techniques with limited technical novelty.
- Argument for rejection: evaluated on a toy domain not representative of challenging ML problems.
- Argument for rejection: reliance on hand-crafted grammar/manual translation reduces practical significance and generalizability.

- Conclusion: methodologically sound and insightful for cognitive science, but limited ML impact and borderline for acceptance at NIPS due to simplicity and lack of novelty.
