This paper introduces a novel approach to modeling human question asking as program generation, treating questions as formal programs that, when executed on the state of the world, output an answer. The authors propose a probabilistic model that specifies a probability distribution over a complex, compositional space of programs, favoring concise programs that help the agent learn in the current context.

The paper is related to previous work on active learning and question generation, but it differs in its focus on modeling human question asking in a more general and flexible way. The authors use a data set of natural language questions asked by human participants in an information-search game to evaluate their model. The model is able to predict the frequencies of human questions given a game context and can also synthesize novel human-like questions that were not present in the training set.

The authors' approach is innovative in its integration of linguistic and cognitive theory, and the paper is well-written and clear. The evaluation is appropriate, with both quantitative and qualitative results that demonstrate the model's capabilities. The main quantitative finding is that all factors (informativeness, complexity, answer type) contribute non-negligibly, with complexity being the dominant predictor. The qualitative result is that the model generates a series of novel, plausible questions.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of cognitive science, advancing our understanding of self-directed learning and explaining why informativeness alone poorly predicts human question-asking. However, the approach has limitations in terms of scalability, as it is computationally strained and demonstrated only in a small, low-dimensional domain. This raises doubts about its generalization to broader ML/NIPS-relevant problems.

To address this limitation, the authors should demonstrate or argue for scenarios where the method scales or yields novel insights for a broader audience. Additionally, performing nested model comparisons on lesioned-model log-likelihoods to test statistical significance would strengthen the results. The authors may also consider related formal semantics/pragmatics work on sequential question-asking (Questions under Discussion), as mentioned in the minor comments.
