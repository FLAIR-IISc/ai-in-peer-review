- Proposes a probabilistic model that generates questions as formal programs evaluated over possible worlds; trained on human questions in a Battleship grid and can predict and generate plausible novel questions.
- Paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a thorough introduction and background on question-asking and active learning limits.
- Approach is grounded in linguistic and cognitive theories and integrates insights from linguistics, cognitive science, and computer science.
- Evaluation uses quantitative and qualitative methods (leave-one-out cross-validation) and shows the full model outperforms alternatives.
- Model generalizes by producing novel questions not in the training data.
- Strength: innovative interdisciplinary contribution advancing understanding of self-directed learning and question-asking in cognition.
- Major limitation: computationally intensive and demonstrated only in a small, low-dimensional domain.
- Major limitation: relies on a hand-designed grammar that may not generalize to other domains.
- Unclear how the approach scales to more complex domains or larger datasets.
- Suggested improvements: demonstrate scalability or motivate scenarios where the method provides broader insights; explore automating grammar design.
- Minor suggestions: run nested model comparisons on lesioned-model log-likelihoods for significance and consider related formal semantics/pragmatics work (e.g., Questions under Discussion).
- Reviewer scores: Quality 8/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 9/10, Significance 8/10.
- Acceptance arguments: novel approach, strong theoretical grounding, comprehensive evaluation, and thorough motivation.
- Rejection concerns: computational cost, limited domain demonstration, hand-designed grammar, and unclear scalability.
