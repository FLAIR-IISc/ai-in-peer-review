- Models human question-asking as program generation in a typed lambda/LISP-like language with a probabilistic grammar.  
- Uses a log-linear model trading off informativeness (EIG) and complexity (negative log grammar probability), plus answer-type and relevance features.  
- Learns parameters by maximizing likelihood with importance sampling (PCFG proposal, 150k samples, 100k iterations).  
- Evaluated on a Battleship question dataset (leave-one-context-out); full model outperforms lesioned variants and correlates moderately with human frequencies (Spearman ~0.64).  
- Model can generate novel, contextually useful questions not seen in training (productivity).  
- Connects to prior work on questions-as-partitions, Language of Thought/program induction, and semantic parsing.  
- Broadens active learning by allowing compositional, typed queries beyond simple labels; focuses on goal-directed informativeness rather than surface fluency.  
- Technical formulation is coherent: typed compositional programs, clear EIG definition, and standard log-linear parametrization enabling ablations.  
- Optimization approximations are reasonable, but details on estimator variance, sensitivity to proposal distribution, and convergence diagnostics are missing.  
- Evaluation supports main claims but is confined to a highly constrained, hand-engineered domain with the natural-language side bypassed.  
- Comparisons are only to lesioned versions of the same model; no strong external baselines (e.g., retrieval or neural program generators).  
- Clarity is good overall, with helpful formalization and examples; some implementation details placed in the supplement.  
- Suggested clarifications include proposal design and rejection rates, CI/variance for log-likelihoods, mapping from text to programs and inter-annotator agreement, and per-context computational cost.  
- Originality lies in combining EIG–complexity tradeoff with program synthesis to model question generation, but many components are familiar.  
- Significance is conceptually interesting but limited by narrow domain, hand-engineering, and lack of end-to-end natural language or strong ML baselines.  
- Arguments for acceptance: principled unified framework, empirical evidence for complexity+informativeness, demonstrated productivity, and solid cognitive modeling.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited generality, no NL component, weak external baselines, and incremental methodological novelty.  
- Suggested improvements: add stronger baselines, test transfer to other domains, integrate a learned semantic parser, report probability calibration and human preference judgments, and analyze sensitivity to grammar/proposal choices.  
- Overall assessment: well-executed and conceptually clear cognitive model, but narrow scope and limited ML advancement lead to a lean toward rejection for a NIPS audience.
