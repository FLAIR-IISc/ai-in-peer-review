This paper introduces a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking, framing questions as programs that, when executed, yield an answer. The authors tackle the challenge of moving beyond simple label-requesting active learning to a richer space of queries, motivated by the observation that humans often ask questions targeting critical parameters in a learning task. They achieve this by representing questions using a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) grounded in lambda calculus, enabling compositionality and computability. The model is evaluated on a dataset of human questions collected during a “Battleship”-like game, demonstrating its ability to predict human question choices and generate novel, plausible questions.

The work builds on a growing body of research in active learning (Settles, 2009) and dialog systems (Serban et al., 2017), but distinguishes itself by focusing on the *generation* of informative questions rather than selection from a predefined set. While prior work has explored question generation (Mostafazadeh et al., 2016; Jain et al., 2016) often relying on large datasets and deep learning techniques, this approach emphasizes a more cognitive plausibility and ability to generate questions in novel scenarios with limited data. The framing of questions as programs aligns with formal semantic approaches (Groenendijk & Stokhof, 1997; Roberts, 1996) and earlier work on concepts as programs (Lake et al., 2015).

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The authors present a technically sound and well-motivated model that elegantly integrates principles from linguistics and cognitive science. The writing is exceptionally clear and the organization logical, making the complex ideas accessible. The evaluation is comprehensive, combining quantitative results using leave-one-out cross-validation with qualitative assessment of generated questions. Crucially, the paper demonstrates that human question asking isn’t solely driven by maximizing information gain, highlighting the importance of question complexity – a key insight with implications for understanding self-directed learning.  

However, the work also has weaknesses. The most significant limitation is the scalability of the approach. The reliance on a hand-designed grammar and the computationally intensive program execution make it challenging to apply to more complex, high-dimensional domains typical of many NIPS problems. The current implementation is strongly tied to the “Battleship” game, and it's unclear how readily the framework could be adapted to other tasks without substantial engineering effort.  While the authors acknowledge this limitation, the paper would be strengthened by a discussion of potential avenues for scaling or demonstrating the value of this approach in a more broadly relevant context. 

Arguments *for* acceptance:
* Novel and theoretically grounded approach to question generation.
* Clear writing and comprehensive evaluation.
* Significant cognitive science contribution – the demonstration of the importance of complexity in human question asking.
* Successful generation of novel, plausible questions.

Arguments *against* acceptance:
* Limited scalability and domain specificity.
* Computational expense.
* The empirical impact might be limited to the specifics of the task.

Minor suggestions for improvement include performing nested model comparisons to rigorously assess the statistical significance of differences in log-likelihoods between the lesioned models. Additionally, the authors might consider referencing work on “Questions Under Discussion” (QUD) in formal semantics and pragmatics, which provides a framework for understanding how question-asking is shaped by the conversational context and shared knowledge.  

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. While the scalability concerns are valid, the originality, clarity, and theoretical insights presented justify acceptance.  The work provides a compelling cognitive model of question asking and opens up new avenues for research in active learning and human-computer interaction.
