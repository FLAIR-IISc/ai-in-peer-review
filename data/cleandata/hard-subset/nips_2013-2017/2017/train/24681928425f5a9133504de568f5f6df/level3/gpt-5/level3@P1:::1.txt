This paper models human question-asking as program generation. Questions are represented as executable, compositional programs in a small, typed lambda/LISP-like language. A probabilistic grammar defines the space of well-typed programs; a log-linear model over program features assigns probabilities to candidate questions, trading off informativeness and complexity as a function of context. Informativeness is computed as expected information gain (EIG) over a Bayesian hypothesis space of Battleship boards; complexity is the negative log probability under the grammar; additional features capture answer type (Boolean/Number/Color/Location) and a relevance filter. Parameters are learned by maximizing the likelihood of human questions using importance sampling to approximate the partition function and feature expectations. Evaluation on a Battleship question-asking dataset (leave-one-context-out) shows the full model outperforms variants that remove informativeness, complexity, or answer-type features, achieves moderate correlation with human frequencies (Spearman ~0.64 on average), and can sample novel, contextually useful questions not in the training set.

Relation to prior work: The approach connects classic accounts of question semantics as partitions over possible worlds (Groenendijk & Stokhof; Roberts), the Language of Thought/program-induction perspective (Goodman, Tenenbaum; Piantadosi), and semantic parsing/program-execution work (e.g., Wang et al.; Johnson et al.). Compared to active learning, it broadens the query space beyond labels to compositional, typed questions; compared to visual question generation or dialogue systems, it targets goal-directed informativeness rather than surface fluency.

Quality: The technical formulation is coherent: programs are compositional and typed; EIG is defined over a clear hypothesis space with deterministic program execution; the log-linear parametrization with feature-based energies is standard and permits ablations. The optimization approximations (PCFG-based proposal, 150k sampled programs, importance weighting, 100k iterations) are reasonable, though computational details (variance of estimators, sensitivity to proposal distribution, convergence diagnostics) could be elaborated. The evaluation is internally consistent and supports the main claims: complexity matters greatly (removing it degrades fit), informativeness and type features provide additional gains, and the model exhibits productivity. However, the setting is highly constrained: a single, unambiguous domain with a hand-engineered grammar tailored to the task; the natural language side is bypassed (questions are analyzed into programs); and comparisons are only to lesioned versions of the same model, not to strong alternative families (e.g., retrieval or neural program generators conditioned on context semantics). The MI-based equivalence criterion is sensible but details of its robustness are limited.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, with intuitive examples and a logical progression. The formalization of the grammar and the type system is helpful (though largely in the supplement). The description of the Battleship hypothesis space and priors is mostly clear; noting that two contexts were dropped for computational reasons helps transparency. Suggested clarifications: provide more detail on proposal distribution design and rejection rates; report variance/CI for log-likelihood estimates across bootstrap runs; specify the mapping from human text to programs and inter-annotator agreement; and quantify computational cost per context.

Originality: Modeling question generation (not just interpretation) as program synthesis with an explicit EIG–complexity tradeoff is a neat synthesis of ideas from semantics, cognitive modeling, and active learning. That said, several components are familiar: questions-as-partitions/programs, log-linear modeling with hand-designed features, and PCFG-based generation. The novelty lies in combining these pieces to predict human question distributions in a concrete task. The core ideas may feel obvious to readers familiar with program-induction and semantic approaches, and the hand-crafted domain reduces perceived generality.

Significance: The work offers a conceptual contribution—pushing active learning toward richer, compositional queries—and an empirical cognitive model of human question asking. For the NIPS audience, significance is limited by the narrow domain, lack of an end-to-end natural language component, and absence of strong ML baselines. The insights about the importance of complexity alongside EIG are interesting but may be viewed as expected. The impact would be stronger with demonstrations in additional domains or with interfaces to learned semantic parsers.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, principled framework unifying compositional semantics, program generation, and active learning.
- Solid empirical evidence that complexity and informativeness jointly explain human question choices.
- Demonstrates productivity and generation of novel, contextually appropriate questions.
- Well-executed cognitive modeling that could inspire richer ML query strategies.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Highly constrained, hand-engineered domain; limited generality.
- No natural language generation/understanding; relies on semantic representations.
- Evaluation lacks strong external baselines and broader domains; significance to the NIPS community is modest.
- Core methodological components are incremental relative to prior work in semantics and program induction.

Suggestions to improve the paper:
- Add stronger baselines (e.g., retrieval over previous-context questions, template grammars without EIG, neural generators over the program language conditioned on context state).
- Test transfer to at least one additional domain with a different hypothesis space to assess generality and quantify knowledge engineering required.
- Integrate or interface with a learned semantic parser to map natural-language questions to programs, even if noisy, to increase ML relevance.
- Report calibration of model probabilities and human preference judgments for sampled questions.
- Analyze sensitivity to grammar design and proposal distribution; provide ablations of grammar constructs (e.g., mapping, set ops).

Overall assessment: A well-executed and thoughtful cognitive modeling paper that articulates an appealing view of questions as programs and shows informative–complexity tradeoffs. However, given the narrow scope, heavy hand-engineering, and limited ML advancement, I am unconvinced of its significance for NIPS. I lean toward rejection, while acknowledging the conceptual clarity and potential to influence future work at the intersection of active learning and program synthesis.
