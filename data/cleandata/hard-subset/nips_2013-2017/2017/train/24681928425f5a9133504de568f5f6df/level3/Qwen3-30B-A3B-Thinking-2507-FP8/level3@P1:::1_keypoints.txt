- Proposes modeling human questions as executable programs evaluated on world states, applied to a constrained Battleship game.  
- Core contribution: a probabilistic model combining expected information gain (EIG), question complexity, answer type, and relevance to predict and generate questions.  
- Formalization strength: clear, computationally grounded framing of question asking as program generation.  
- Empirical result: model predicts human question frequencies (Spearman 0.64) and can generate novel, human-like questions.  
- Insight: question complexity is as important as informativeness for human question selection.  
- Related work: builds on active learning, cognitive science, and program synthesis, extending beyond simple label requests to open-ended questions.  
- Method: uses compositional (Montagovian) semantics and a grammar-based approach enabling novel question generation.  
- Limitation — domain: Battleship is highly constrained (fixed rules, limited ambiguity), limiting generalizability.  
- Limitation — novelty: technical approach (log-linear models, Bayesian IG, compositional semantics) is largely an application of existing methods.  
- Limitation — scalability/generalization: paper does not address scaling to complex, real-world, ambiguous scenarios or computational efficiency.  
- Venue fit/significance: limited impact for NIPS; more appropriate for cognitive science venues given lack of new ML algorithms or substantial ML advances.  
- Technical quality and clarity: well-written, technically sound, with appropriate evaluation (leave-one-out CV, bootstrapped comparisons) and thoughtful grammar/feature design.  
- Claim overstated: assertion that this is a step toward more capable active learning machines is not convincingly supported given the artificial task constraints.
