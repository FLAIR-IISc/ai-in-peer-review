This paper proposes a cognitive model of question generation that treats questions as executable programs and assigns them probabilities via an energy model combining informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (PCFG-derived length/description length), answer type, and a relevance filter distinguishing belief-state–dependent from world-independent queries. Implemented in a Battleship-like domain, the model predicts which questions people ask to locate ships and can generate novel, compositional questions. Relative to prior work in active learning (Settles), program induction and semantic parsing (Lake et al.; Johnson et al.; Wang et al.), and vision/dialog QG, the key advance is a compositional, executable representation paired with a probabilistic generator that operates over a rich hypothesis space.

Quality: The work is technically careful and reproducible; the inference and optimization details (importance sampling for Z, gradient ascent) are appropriate. Held-out log-likelihood favors the full model over lesioned variants, especially over removing complexity. However, the evidence that EIG specifically drives human choices is weak: models without EIG correlate with human frequencies about as well as the full model, correlations are noisy due to sparse counts, and bootstrap analyses indicate a nontrivial 19% chance that the full model’s log-likelihood advantage over the information-agnostic variant is illusory. Claims about “revealing how people balance informativeness and complexity” therefore overreach the data.

Clarity: The paper is well-written, organized, and transparent about limitations. Minor suggestions: clarify how answer-type priors and the relevance filter interact with EIG, and report uncertainty on correlation estimates.

Originality: Modeling questions as programs and learning a distribution over them in a nontrivial domain is novel and interesting. That said, the paper should more deeply engage with related cognitive/pragmatic accounts, especially Hawkins et al. (2015) on goal-based question selection and Roberts (1996) on QUDs, to situate its contribution.

Significance: The demonstrated ability to generate compositional, useful questions is valuable for both AI and cognitive modeling, even if the role of EIG remains uncertain. Broader impact would be strengthened by evaluations in additional domains and comparisons to neural QG baselines.

Pros:
- Strong, reproducible modeling and clear exposition
- Novel, compositional question generator with compelling qualitative outputs
- Solid predictive improvements from complexity and type features

Cons:
- Weak, noisy evidence for EIG’s contribution
- Limited domain and sparse data
- Incomplete engagement with QUD and prior goal-based models

Suggestions: Add stronger baselines (e.g., neural QG), quantify uncertainty more thoroughly, test in another domain, and explicitly connect the program-based objective to QUD/goal structures. Overall, a promising contribution with caveats about the EIG claim.
