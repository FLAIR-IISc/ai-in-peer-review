

This paper proposes modeling human question-asking as program generation, where questions are formal programs that output answers when executed on a world state. The authors apply this approach to a Battleship game domain, using a log-linear model with features for expected information gain, answer type (Boolean vs. numerical), question complexity, and relevance to board features. The model successfully predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, contextually appropriate questions not present in the training data.

The paper's strengths lie in its clear presentation and reasonable motivation. The connection between question-asking and active learning is well-justified, and the compositional semantic framework (using a Montagovian approach) provides a theoretically grounded representation of questions. The model's ability to generate novel questions demonstrates some creativity within the constrained domain.

However, the paper's contribution faces significant limitations. The core idea of modeling questions as programs has been explored in prior work (e.g., Lake et al., 2015; Goodman et al., 2013), and the task itself is highly constrained to a simple Battleship game with logically unambiguous questions. The four features used in the log-linear model are basic and not particularly innovative, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how this approach generalizes beyond this narrow domain.

The significance is limited: while the model performs adequately on this specific task, it doesn't advance the state-of-the-art in question-asking for more complex, real-world scenarios. The paper fails to address how this framework would scale to more open-ended, practical applications where question-asking is genuinely challenging.

Pro: Clear technical presentation, reasonable motivation, successful demonstration of question generation within the domain.
Con: Limited scope and novelty, simple task that doesn't reflect real-world question-asking complexity, minimal contribution to active learning literature.

While technically sound, the paper's narrow focus and lack of significant novelty make it unlikely to interest the NIPS community, which prioritizes work with broader impact and more substantial methodological advances. The contribution is incremental rather than transformative for the field.
