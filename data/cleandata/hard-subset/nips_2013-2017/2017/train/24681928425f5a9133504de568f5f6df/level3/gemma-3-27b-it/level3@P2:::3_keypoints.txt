- Proposes a novel framework modeling questions as executable programs and demonstrates it in a modified Battleship game, predicting human choices and generating novel questions.  
- Model learns weights for features: informativeness (EIG), complexity, answer type, and relevance to game state.  
- Positions work within active learning and dialog literature (e.g., Settles 2009; Serban 2017) but misses closely related efforts (Hawkins 2015; Roberts 1996).  
- Technical approach is sound: clearly defined grammar, rigorous probabilistic generative model, and importance sampling for inference.  
- Code and dataset are publicly available, and the paper is clearly written and well-organized.  
- Key strength: innovative, compositional question generation with implications for AI agents and cognitive modeling.  
- Feature-weight analysis shows informativeness and complexity matter, offering insight into human question choices.  
- Evidence for the claimed balance between informativeness and complexity is weak: full model beats lesions in log-likelihood but correlation with human frequency is only moderate and complexity-agnostic model performs similarly.  
- Human data sparsity and bootstrap uncertainty weaken claims about EIG’s significance and the model’s capture of nuanced factor interactions.  
- Reliance on a pre-defined grammar may bias results and limit expressiveness; more flexible or grammar-free approaches are suggested for future work.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded, demonstrated in a non-trivial domain, technically sound, reproducible, and impactful.  
- Arguments against acceptance: weak empirical support for key claims, incomplete related work, and potential limitations from the grammar.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address missing related work, temper claims about informativeness vs. complexity, and discuss grammar limitations.  
- Final rating: Accept (with minor revisions).
