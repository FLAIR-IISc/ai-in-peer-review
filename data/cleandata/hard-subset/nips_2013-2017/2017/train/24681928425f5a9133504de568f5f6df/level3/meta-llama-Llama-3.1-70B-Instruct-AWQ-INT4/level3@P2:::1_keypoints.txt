- Introduces a cognitive model that represents human questions as executable programs and generates human-like queries in an information-search game.  
- Uses a probabilistic generative model over a compositional programming language (lambda/LISP-like grammar) to sample context-sensitive questions.  
- Evaluated on a dataset of 605 human natural-language questions from a Battleship-like information-search game.  
- Shows the model predicts question frequencies in context and can synthesize novel, human-like questions not seen in training.  
- Well-motivated framing around active learning and richer human queries beyond simple information requests.  
- Clear, detailed exposition of the model and its components, making reproduction and understanding straightforward.  
- Technically sound evaluation demonstrating predictive and generative performance on a large human dataset.  
- Main weakness: the task is very simple, highly constrained, and logically unambiguous, limiting broader applicability.  
- Core methods (compositional semantics, log-linear models) are well-established, so the contribution is not highly novel.  
- Reviewer doubts the paper’s appeal to the NIPS community due to limited novelty and impact.  
- Pro-acceptance points: novel framing of question-asking as program generation, strong human-data evaluation, and clarity of presentation.  
- Con-acceptance points: limited task complexity, lack of surprising technical innovation, and uncertain community interest.  
- Scores: Quality 6/10, Clarity 8/10, Originality 4/10, Significance 5/10.
