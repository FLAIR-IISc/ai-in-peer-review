This paper proposes a cognitive model for generating human-like questions in the context of an information-search game, specifically the Battleship game. The model treats questions as formal programs that, when executed on the state of the world, output an answer. The authors evaluate their approach using a data set containing natural language questions asked by human participants in the game. The model successfully predicts the frequencies of human questions given a game context and can also synthesize novel human-like questions that were not present in the training set.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and reproducible. The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their approach, including the grammar for producing questions, the probabilistic generative model, and the optimization procedure. The results are presented in a clear and concise manner, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of the implications of their findings.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to capture the compositionality and computability of human questions. The authors show that their model can generate novel questions that are not present in the training set, demonstrating the productivity of the human behavior. The model also outperforms lesioned versions on held-out human data by log-likelihood, indicating that the full model is better at predicting human questions.

However, the paper also has some limitations. The authors note that the model operates on semantic representations rather than on natural language text directly, which may limit its applicability to real-world scenarios. Additionally, the grammar used in the model is specific to the Battleship domain, and it is unclear how much new knowledge engineering will be needed to extend the approach to new domains.

The paper also claims to reveal how people balance informativeness and complexity when producing semantically coherent questions. However, the evidence for this claim is not strongly supported by the experimental results. The authors show that the full model outperforms lesioned versions on held-out human data by log-likelihood, but the difference in performance is not significant for all models. Additionally, the correlation results are noisy due to sparse data, and the bootstrap estimates give a 19% chance that the log-likelihood result is wrong.

Furthermore, the paper is missing some related prior work, such as Hawkins (2015) and Roberts (1996), which also address the issue of question asking and generation.

Overall, the main contribution of the paper is demonstrating the generation of interesting compositional questions for a non-trivial domain, making the approach useful for AI and cognitive modeling. However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations and potential extensions of the approach.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to generating human-like questions in the context of an information-search game.
* The model is technically sound, well-written, and reproducible.
* The results demonstrate the productivity of the human behavior and the ability of the model to generate novel questions.
* The paper has implications for AI and cognitive modeling.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The model operates on semantic representations rather than on natural language text directly, which may limit its applicability to real-world scenarios.
* The grammar used in the model is specific to the Battleship domain, and it is unclear how much new knowledge engineering will be needed to extend the approach to new domains.
* The evidence for the claim that the paper reveals how people balance informativeness and complexity is not strongly supported by the experimental results.
* The paper is missing some related prior work.

Recommendation:

I recommend accepting the paper, but with some revisions to address the limitations and potential extensions of the approach. The authors should provide a more thorough discussion of the implications of their findings and the potential applications of their approach. Additionally, they should consider including more related prior work to provide a more comprehensive overview of the field.
