

**Review**

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work:**  
This paper proposes a computational model for human question asking by treating questions as programs that, when executed on a world state, produce answers. The authors formalize questions as programs in a simple programming language based on lambda calculus, using a probabilistic context-free grammar to generate syntactically valid questions. The model employs a log-linear framework with four features: Expected Information Gain (EIG), question complexity (log-probability under the grammar), answer type (Boolean/numerical/color/location), and relevance to the game board. Evaluated on a Battleship game dataset where humans asked questions to identify ship configurations, the model predicts human question frequencies (Spearman's correlation of 0.64) and generates novel human-like questions not present in training. The work connects to active learning [21], program synthesis [12, 24], and cognitive science [4, 15], but differs from prior question generation approaches [16, 23] by focusing on goal-directed, context-sensitive questioning rather than open-ended generation.

**Strengths:**  
The paper clearly articulates the connection between question asking and program synthesis, providing a formal framework for modeling human question generation. The evaluation is thorough, with careful ablation of key model components (e.g., complexity-agnostic vs. full model). The model successfully predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, contextually appropriate questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?"), demonstrating the model's creativity within the domain. The use of EIG as a feature is well-motivated by active learning principles, and the grammar-based approach elegantly captures question compositionality.

**Weaknesses:**  
The core ideas lack sufficient novelty for NIPS. The approach is a straightforward application of existing techniques (program synthesis, log-linear modeling) to a highly constrained domain. The task—Battleship game with fixed rules, limited question types, and unambiguous answers—is overly simplistic and fails to address the complexities of natural language question asking (e.g., pragmatics, ambiguity, real-world knowledge). The model's four features are basic and lack depth; for instance, complexity is measured only via grammar log-probability, without considering human cognitive load. The evaluation is limited to a single game domain with no comparison to neural approaches (e.g., VQG [16]), and the paper does not demonstrate generalizability to other tasks or domains. The claim of "human-like" questions is restricted to this narrow context, with no evidence of broader applicability.

**Quality:** The technical execution is sound, with appropriate statistical evaluation (log-likelihood, bootstrapping). However, the model is overly simplistic for its claimed contribution, and the experimental design lacks depth (e.g., no analysis of why certain features dominate). The paper is technically complete but does not advance the state of the art significantly.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to evaluation. The grammar and model descriptions are accessible, though the focus on a narrow domain may limit reader interest.

**Originality:** The core insight—that questions can be modeled as programs—is not novel [5, 12, 17]. The specific application to Battleship is a constrained use case that does not represent a significant departure from prior work. The paper fails to distinguish its approach from existing active learning or program synthesis methods in meaningful ways.

**Significance:** The work addresses a narrow, highly controlled problem with limited real-world relevance. The model's inability to generalize beyond the Battleship domain (as acknowledged in the limitations) undermines its significance. The paper does not advance the broader field of question asking or active learning in a demonstrable way; it merely applies existing techniques to a toy domain.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**  
*Pros:* Clear formal framework, thorough evaluation within the domain, successful generation of novel questions.  
*Cons:* Minimal novelty, overly constrained task, lack of broader significance, no evidence of generalizability.

**Overall Assessment:**  
This paper presents a technically sound but limited application of program synthesis to question asking in a highly constrained game setting. While the model accurately predicts human questions in this specific context, the work lacks the originality and significance expected at NIPS. The task is too simple to reflect the complexity of real-world question asking, and the model's narrow scope limits its broader impact. The paper does not advance the field beyond existing active learning or program synthesis work, and its contribution is incremental at best.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's narrow focus, lack of novelty, and limited significance make it unsuitable for NIPS. The authors would benefit from extending the model to more complex, real-world question-asking scenarios or demonstrating a substantial theoretical advance before resubmission.
