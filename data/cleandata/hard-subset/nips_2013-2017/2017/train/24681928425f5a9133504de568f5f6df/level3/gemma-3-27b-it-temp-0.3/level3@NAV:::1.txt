## Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper presents a framework for modeling human question asking by treating questions as formal programs with compositional semantics, evaluated within the context of the game Battleship. The authors leverage a log-linear model with features capturing expected information gain, answer type, and board relevance to predict human question choices. While the motivation – framing question asking as active learning – is reasonable, the core ideas feel incremental and the task itself is heavily constrained. 

The work builds upon the established field of active learning (Settles, 2009) by moving beyond simple label requests to more complex, natural language-like queries. It also connects to research in compositional semantics (Fodor, 1987; Jacobson, 1996) and program induction (Lake et al., 2015), framing question generation as a program synthesis problem. However, the approach doesn’t significantly advance these areas. Existing work in dialog systems (Serban et al., 2017) and visual question generation (Mostafazadeh et al., 2016) already tackles question generation, albeit with different focuses and often relying on larger datasets. This work’s contribution lies in the specific application of a formal program-based approach to a well-defined, albeit limited, game context.

The strengths of the paper include a clear presentation of the model and a thorough empirical evaluation. The authors demonstrate that their model can predict human question choices and even generate novel, plausible questions. The ablation study, comparing different feature sets, provides valuable insight into the importance of complexity alongside informativeness. However, the task's simplicity and the highly constrained nature of the Battleship domain are significant weaknesses. The grammar, while sufficient for this task, feels specifically tailored and its generalizability is questionable. The reliance on a log-linear model, while interpretable, feels somewhat basic given the advancements in neural sequence modeling. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a formal, compositional framework for modeling question asking.
*   Demonstrates the importance of both informativeness and complexity in human question generation.
*   Presents a clear and well-executed empirical evaluation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core ideas are relatively obvious and the task is overly simplified.
*   The approach doesn't offer a substantial advancement over existing work in dialog systems or program induction.
*   Limited generalizability due to the highly constrained domain.

**Overall:** I do not believe this paper is a strong fit for NIPS. While technically sound and clearly written, the limited scope and incremental nature of the contribution do not meet the conference’s standards for originality and significance. The work feels more appropriate for a cognitive science or specialized AI conference focused on active learning or game-playing.
