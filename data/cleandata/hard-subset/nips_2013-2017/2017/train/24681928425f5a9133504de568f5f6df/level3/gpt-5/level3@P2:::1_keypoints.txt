- Proposes modeling questions as executable, compositional programs (PCFG over a lambda/LISP-like language) with a log-linear utility model using EIG, complexity, answer type, and relevance.
- Evaluated on a Battleship-like task mapping human free-form questions to programs, showing full model best predicts human question frequencies and can generate novel context-sensitive questions.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of questions as compositional, executable programs grounded in semantic tradition.
- Interpretable probabilistic model with clean ablation of informativeness, complexity, and type preferences.
- Empirical cross-context evaluation against human data with supportive correlations and bootstrap analyses.
- Demonstrates generative capacity to sample novel, human-like questions.
- Relevant to cognitive modeling of trade-offs between informativeness and complexity.

Weaknesses
- Very constrained domain with handcrafted grammar and hand-designed features; no learned text-to-program mapping.
- Modeling choices (log-linear over EIG and complexity) are straightforward; limited ML technical novelty.
- Informativeness computed with idealized exact semantics over a tiny hypothesis space; scalability is unclear.
- Complexity feature tied to proposal distribution (−log q), potentially conflating modeling and inference.
- Small dataset (605 questions, 16 contexts), with excluded contexts and question types for computational reasons.
- Novel-question evaluation is qualitative; no human comparison to ML baselines.

Quality
- Technically sound within scope; inference and ablations appropriate and claims supported on task.
- Limitations include domain-specific relevance/type encodings, excluded contexts, and coupling of complexity with proposal.

Clarity
- Well-written and organized; formal language and features clearly described.
- Supplementary details (core rewrite rules) are important and could be summarized more in the main text.

Originality
- Moderate: combines existing ideas (questions-as-programs, EIG) into a cognitive predictive model; incremental for ML audiences.

Significance
- Limited for NIPS: narrow, domain-specific task and hand-engineered setup more impactful for cognitive science than mainstream ML.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, interpretable formalization with solid ablations.
- Rare predictive model of open-ended human question asking with compositional structure.
- Potential to inspire ML work on structured, goal-directed question policies.

Arguments against acceptance
- Hand-engineered grammar/features, no natural-language interface.
- Limited novelty and small, curated dataset; unclear scalability and ML relevance.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the proposal distribution q and to using −log q as the complexity feature?
- Can you report performance including the previously excluded contexts and draw-based questions using approximate execution?
- How robust are predictions to small grammar perturbations (adding/removing operators)?
- Can you provide human evaluations of sampled novel questions versus neural baselines on informativeness and answerability?
- What is required to learn the text-to-program mapping and port to a second domain without heavy engineering?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add an end-to-end semantic parser from text to programs (e.g., weak supervision) and report pipeline results.
- Evaluate on a second domain with different predicates to test portability.
- Compare to strong ML question-generation or dialog baselines, including utility-aware policies.
- Separate proposal distribution from the complexity prior (e.g., learnable priors or neural PCFGs).
- Obtain human ratings for sampled questions’ usefulness and clarity.

Recommendation
- Lean reject for NIPS: elegant and valuable for cognitive modeling but limited ML novelty, scope, and scalability.
