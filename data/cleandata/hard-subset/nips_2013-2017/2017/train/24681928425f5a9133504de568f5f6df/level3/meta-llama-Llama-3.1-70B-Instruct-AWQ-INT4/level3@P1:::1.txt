The paper "Question Asking as Program Generation" proposes a novel approach to modeling human question asking abilities in a computational framework. The authors treat questions as formal programs that, when executed on the state of the world, output an answer. They develop a probabilistic model of question generation that captures the compositionality and computability of human questions. The model is evaluated on a data set of natural language questions asked by human participants in an information-search game, and the results show that the model can predict the frequencies of human questions and generate novel, human-like questions.

The paper's main contributions are the development of a formal language for representing questions and a probabilistic model for generating questions. The language is based on lambda calculus and LISP, and it allows for the representation of questions as programs that can be executed on the state of the world. The probabilistic model uses a log-linear model over four simple question features: expected information gain, answer type, and whether the question references board features.

The paper's strengths include its novel approach to modeling human question asking abilities, its ability to capture the compositionality and computability of human questions, and its evaluation on a large data set of natural language questions. The paper's weaknesses include its simplicity and lack of generality, as the task is highly constrained and logically unambiguous. Additionally, the reviewer notes that the core ideas are obvious and may not be of interest to the NIPS community.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their approach. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of their model, including a comparison with alternative models and an analysis of the results.

However, the paper's significance and impact are limited by its simplicity and lack of generality. The task of generating questions in a highly constrained and logically unambiguous domain may not be of interest to the broader NIPS community. Additionally, the paper's contributions, while novel, may not be significant enough to warrant publication in a top-tier conference like NIPS.
