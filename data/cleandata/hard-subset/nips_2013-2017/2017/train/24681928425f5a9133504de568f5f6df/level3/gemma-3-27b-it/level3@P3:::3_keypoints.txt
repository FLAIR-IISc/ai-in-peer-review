- Proposes a cognitive model framing questions as programs evaluated by informativeness (EIG), complexity, answer type, and relevance.  
- Implemented in the Battleship domain, predicting human question choices and generating novel, plausible questions.  
- Uses a probabilistic grammar and importance sampling to handle computation; technically sound and clearly written.  
- Code is available and experiments are reproducible.  
- The full model outperforms ablated versions in log-likelihood, suggesting features jointly improve prediction.  
- Claims that the model reveals how humans balance informativeness and complexity are weakly supported.  
- A model without EIG still correlates with human judgments, implying complexity or other factors may drive human choices.  
- Data sparsity and bootstrap analysis reveal substantial uncertainty (≈19% chance results are wrong) about EIG’s role.  
- Omits relevant prior work, notably Hawkins (2015) and Roberts (1996)’s QUD framework.  
- Distinguishes itself from active learning and visual question generation by targeting richer, creative question generation with limited examples.  
- Novelty: compositional question-as-program generation is a meaningful contribution to AI and cognitive modeling.  
- Main weaknesses: uncertain central claim support, missing related work, and limited demonstrated generalizability beyond Battleship.  
- Reviewer recommendation: weak accept, contingent on tempering claims about human cognition and addressing related-work gaps.
