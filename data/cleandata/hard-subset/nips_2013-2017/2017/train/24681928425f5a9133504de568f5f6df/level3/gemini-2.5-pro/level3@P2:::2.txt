This paper presents a novel computational model of human question asking, framing the problem as one of probabilistic program generation. The core idea is to represent questions as programs in a compositional language (based on lambda calculus) that, when executed on a world state, return an answer. This approach allows for the generation of a rich, open-ended space of possible questions. The model defines a probability distribution over this space using a log-linear model, where the "goodness" of a question is a weighted combination of several features: its expected information gain (EIG) in a given context, its complexity (measured as the negative log-probability under a probabilistic context-free grammar), and its answer type (e.g., boolean, number).

This work connects to several research areas. It significantly extends traditional active learning, which typically considers only simple label queries, by enabling complex, structured information requests. It also provides a goal-directed, cognitive alternative to recent large-scale neural models for question generation (e.g., in VQG), which are less flexible, require massive datasets, and do not explicitly model the learner's intent. The approach is deeply rooted in cognitive science, drawing on the "language of thought" hypothesis and building on prior work that models concepts as programs.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central contribution—modeling question asking as probabilistic program generation—is highly original and conceptually powerful. It elegantly integrates principles from active learning (information gain), linguistics (compositional semantics), and cognitive science (probabilistic models of cognition). For the cognitive science community, this paper presents a significant advance: it is the first formal, predictive model of open-ended human question asking. The key finding that question complexity is a dominant factor, and that EIG alone is a poor predictor of human behavior, provides a valuable insight into the cognitive constraints on self-directed learning.

2.  **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, explaining their formal framework, and detailing their experimental setup. The technical quality is high; the probabilistic model is sound, and the use of importance sampling for approximate inference is appropriate. The work is a complete and carefully executed piece of research.

3.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The model is evaluated rigorously through a combination of quantitative and qualitative methods. The leave-one-out cross-validation setup is appropriate for the dataset size. The lesion study, comparing the full model against variants lacking key components (informativeness, complexity, answer type), convincingly demonstrates that all factors contribute to predicting human questions. The qualitative results, showcasing the model's ability to generate novel and contextually appropriate questions not seen in the training data, are compelling and effectively illustrate the model's generative power.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Scalability and Generalizability:** The primary weakness of the paper is the uncertain scalability of the approach. The model is demonstrated in a small, well-defined grid-world ("Battleship") where the space of world states and relevant concepts is limited. The grammar that defines the question space is hand-designed specifically for this domain. This raises significant questions about how the method would generalize to more complex, higher-dimensional problems of interest to the broader machine learning community. The computational cost of inference (approximating the partition function and expectations over a vast program space) also appears to be a major hurdle. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but the paper would be stronger if it more directly addressed potential paths to scalability.

2.  **Domain-Specificity and Engineering Effort:** Related to the scalability issue, the reliance on a hand-crafted grammar implies substantial domain-specific knowledge engineering would be required to apply this framework to new problems. While the authors rightly point out that some grammatical components (e.g., logical operators, arithmetic) are domain-general, many others appear tailored to the Battleship game. It is unclear how much effort would be needed to port this system to a new domain, which may limit its practical impact as a general-purpose AI technique.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

*   To strengthen the quantitative claims, the authors should consider performing formal statistical tests on the log-likelihood differences between the full model and the lesioned variants. Since the lesioned models are nested within the full model, likelihood-ratio tests could be used to establish the statistical significance of each feature set's contribution.

*   The discussion of related work could be enriched by connecting to the formal semantics and pragmatics literature on discourse structure, particularly the "Questions under Discussion" (QUD) framework (e.g., Roberts, 2012; Ginzburg, 2012). This literature also deals with goal-directed inquiry and how questions are structured to coherently achieve communicative goals, which seems highly relevant to the paper's aims.

*   To address the scalability concerns and increase the paper's impact for the NIPS audience, the authors could discuss potential avenues for future work. For example, could parts of the grammar be learned from data? Could more efficient inference techniques be brought to bear? Even a brief discussion of what class of real-world problems (beyond toy domains) might be amenable to this approach would be very helpful.

**Summary of pros and cons for acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** Highly original and elegant formulation of a challenging problem.
*   **Pro:** Significant contribution to the cognitive science of learning and inquiry.
*   **Pro:** Strong and clear technical presentation, with a thorough quantitative and qualitative evaluation.
*   **Con:** Major questions about the scalability and generalizability of the approach to larger, real-world problems.
*   **Con:** Relies on a hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar, potentially limiting its practical applicability in ML.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that introduces a creative and thought-provoking new direction for modeling active learning and inquiry. While the practical scalability is a significant concern, the novelty of the framework and the importance of its findings for cognitive science make it a strong contribution. It presents a foundational idea that could inspire future work on creating more human-like learning agents.
