

**Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"**

This paper models human question-asking as program generation, treating questions as formal programs that, when executed on a world state, output an answer. The authors introduce a probabilistic model over a context-free grammar (PCFG) of lambda-calculus-like programs, where question goodness is a linear combination of expected information gain (informativeness), program complexity (length/negative log PCFG probability), and answer type. Evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions from a Battleship game domain, the model successfully predicts human question frequencies (Spearman's correlation 0.64) and generates novel, plausible questions.

The work builds on formal semantics of questions as partitions over possible worlds [6, 18] and cognitive models of question asking [8, 19], but differs from prior active learning work [21] by modeling open-ended natural language questions rather than simple label requests. It also contrasts with neural question generation approaches [16, 23] by capturing intentional, goal-directed question asking without requiring large datasets.

**Strengths:**
- *Quality*: The model is technically sound, with systematic evaluation through lesioned models (ignoring informativeness, complexity, or answer type) using leave-one-out cross-validation. The main finding that complexity dominates as a predictor (full model log-likelihood advantage over complexity-agnostic model in 100% of bootstrap samples) is robust.
- *Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear explanations of the program representation, grammar, and probabilistic model. Supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility.
- *Originality*: The integration of linguistic compositionality (lambda calculus, PCFG) with cognitive science and active learning represents a significant theoretical advance. The paper's key insight—treating questions as programs that can be synthesized and evaluated for informativeness and complexity—is novel.
- *Significance*: The work provides the first predictive model of open-ended human question asking, advancing cognitive science by explaining why informativeness alone poorly predicts human behavior (people balance informativeness with complexity).

**Weaknesses:**
- *Quality*: The model is computationally intensive (150,000 samples per optimization step) and demonstrated only on a small, low-dimensional Battleship domain. The paper does not convincingly argue for scalability to broader ML/NIPS-relevant problems.
- *Significance*: The cognitive contribution is strong, but the ML relevance is limited. The authors should clarify the scope and demonstrate or argue for scenarios where the method yields novel insights for a broader audience.

**Minor comments:**
1. Perform nested model comparisons on lesioned-model log-likelihoods to test statistical significance.
2. Consider related work on "Questions under Discussion" [18, 26] in formal semantics/pragmatics.

**Recommendation:** This paper makes a significant contribution to cognitive science with a theoretically elegant model of human question asking. The cognitive insights are valuable and the formal approach is sophisticated. However, its ML/NIPS relevance is limited by the narrow domain and computational constraints. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the primary contribution is to cognitive science rather than machine learning. The authors should clarify the scope in the final version and address the scalability concerns to strengthen the ML relevance. With these clarifications, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
