This paper introduces a novel framework for modeling human question asking, treating questions as programs generated from a compositional grammar. The probability of a given question (program) is defined by a log-linear model, where the energy is a weighted sum of several features: informativeness (Expected Information Gain, EIG), complexity (negative log probability under a PCFG), answer type (e.g., Boolean, Number), and relevance. The model is evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions in a "Battleship"-like game, where participants ask questions to resolve ambiguity about hidden ship configurations. The authors test a full model against several lesioned variants (e.g., information-agnostic, complexity-agnostic) and show that their full model provides the best fit to the human data in terms of log-likelihood. The paper also demonstrates the model's ability to generate novel, human-like questions not seen in the training set.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed probabilistic framework is well-formulated, and the use of importance sampling to approximate the intractable partition function and gradients is an appropriate choice. The experimental design, using leave-one-out cross-validation on different game contexts, is solid. The authors are also commendably transparent about some of the weaknesses in their results, for instance, by reporting the bootstrap analysis which indicates that the log-likelihood advantage of the full model over the information-agnostic model is not overwhelmingly certain (significant in 81% of samples).

However, a significant weakness is that the paper's central claim—that the model reveals how people "balance informativeness and complexity"—is not strongly supported by the presented results. While the full model achieves the best log-likelihood, the reported correlation results are much more ambiguous. The information-agnostic model achieves a Spearman's rho of 0.65, slightly *better* than the full model's 0.64, while the complexity-agnostic model performs disastrously (-0.36). This suggests that complexity is the dominant factor explaining human question frequency in this analysis, and the role of EIG is much less clear. The authors themselves note that EIG alone has no correlation with question frequency. Given the high noise in the data (many questions asked only once) and the mixed signals from the different evaluation metrics, the claims about the role of informativeness should be tempered.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the model is described in a step-by-step, intuitive manner, and the experimental setup and results are easy to follow. The inclusion of examples of programs and generated questions (Figure 3) is very effective in illustrating the model's capabilities. The work is presented with sufficient detail to be reproducible.

**Originality:** The core idea of framing open-ended question generation as probabilistic program synthesis is highly original and powerful. It represents a significant step beyond standard active learning paradigms that query for labels, or dialog systems that rely on canned responses.

However, the paper misses some important related work. The work of Hawkins et al. (2015, CogSci) is highly relevant, as it also presents a goal-based computational model of question asking, combining utility (information gain) and cost (related to semantic complexity). While the formalism is different (logic programs vs. LISP-like programs), the conceptual goal is very similar. Furthermore, the idea that questions partition a space of possible worlds has a long history in formal semantics, notably in the "Question Under Discussion" (QUD) framework (Roberts, 1996). Citing this foundational work would better situate the paper's contributions within the broader literature on the semantics of questions.

**Significance:** Despite the concerns about the empirical support for the EIG feature, the paper's overall contribution is significant. The main achievement is demonstrating a generative system that can produce a rich, compositional, and creative set of questions that are contextually relevant to a non-trivial task. This is a valuable contribution to both AI and computational cognitive science. For AI, it points toward a new generation of active learning agents that can inquire about the world in a more flexible and human-like way. For cognitive science, it provides a concrete, implemented model of a core human cognitive capacity. The qualitative results showing the generation of novel and clever questions are particularly compelling and highlight the promise of this approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Presents a novel and elegant framework for question asking as program generation, a significant conceptual advance over prior work in active learning.
*   **Pro:** The model is demonstrably generative and creative, capable of producing complex, compositional questions that are contextually appropriate and not seen in the training data.
*   **Pro:** The paper is technically solid, very clearly written, and the work is of high interest to both the machine learning and cognitive science communities.
*   **Pro:** The complexity feature is shown to be critically important, which is an interesting finding in itself.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The primary claim that the model explains how humans balance informativeness and complexity is not strongly supported by the evidence. The correlation results suggest complexity is the dominant factor, and the log-likelihood advantage for the EIG feature is of borderline statistical significance.
*   **Con:** The paper fails to cite and discuss highly relevant prior work in computational cognitive science (Hawkins et al., 2015) and formal semantics (Roberts, 1996).

In summary, this is a strong, original, and thought-provoking paper with a powerful central idea. Its main weakness is that the empirical results do not fully support one of its main interpretive claims. However, the value of the framework and the demonstration of generating creative, compositional questions are significant enough to warrant acceptance. The authors should be encouraged to revise the paper to tone down the claims about EIG and to properly situate their work with respect to the missing citations.
