- Introduces a novel model of human question-asking that represents questions as executable programs (lambda calculus + PCFG) in a Battleship grid domain.  
- Predicts human questions by balancing expected information gain (EIG), complexity (length/log-PCFG probability), and answer type.  
- Finds complexity, not pure informativeness, is the dominant predictor of human question choices.  
- Lesioned models show all three factors (EIG, complexity, answer type) contribute non-negligibly.  
- Model generates novel, plausible questions not seen in training, advancing explanations for human behavior beyond informativeness alone.  
- Innovative integration of linguistic compositionality (program synthesis) with cognitive theory, addressing gaps in active learning research.  
- Rigorous evaluation: quantitative (leave-one-out cross-validation, log-likelihood comparisons) and qualitative (novel question generation).  
- Writing is clear and the paper is well situated within prior work (e.g., program induction, self-directed learning).  
- Main weakness: scalability—experiments confined to a small, low-dimensional (6×6) domain, limiting broader ML applicability.  
- Authors do not sufficiently argue how the method would scale or provide novel ML/NIPS-relevant insights beyond cognitive science.  
- Minor issues: lack of nested model comparisons for statistical significance and limited engagement with formal semantics literature on sequential question-asking.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, theoretically insightful, and recommended for acceptance if scalability concerns are addressed in revisions.
