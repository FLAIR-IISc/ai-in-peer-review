This paper models human question-asking as program generation in a Battleship-like grid domain. Questions are represented as lambda-calculus programs sampled from a hand-designed PCFG; a log-linear model scores questions by a weighted combination of expected information gain (EIG), complexity (length/−log PCFG probability), and answer type, with a PCFG-based prior providing inductive bias. Human questions from prior work are hand-coded into the grammar; model variants are compared via leave-one-out cross-validation over contexts. Quantitatively, all factors contribute non-negligibly, with complexity the dominant predictor of human question frequency; qualitatively, the model samples novel, plausible questions. This work connects to NeurIPS threads on active learning, probabilistic program induction, semantic parsing, and formal semantics/pragmatics of questions.

Quality: Technically sound and carefully executed. The formalization of questions-as-programs is coherent, and the inference/learning pipeline (importance sampling for partition function/feature expectations; lesion analyses) is appropriate for the domain scale. The empirical claim that informativeness alone is insufficient is convincingly supported; complexity plays a central role. A concern is scalability: importance sampling over a large program space with context-dependent EIG quickly becomes intractable beyond low-dimensional domains.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The mapping from natural language to programs, feature definitions, and evaluation protocol are described with sufficient detail to support reproduction.

Originality: Novel combination of PCFG priors, information-theoretic utility, and program representations to model question-asking. The integration of answer-type preferences with EIG and complexity is distinctive relative to prior active learning and question generation work.

Significance: Strong cognitive-science contribution to understanding self-directed learning and why EIG-only accounts underperform; potential to inspire ML systems that ask richer queries. However, impact for a broader NeurIPS audience hinges on demonstrating scalability or transferable insights beyond the small Battleship domain.

Pros for acceptance:
- Innovative, principled integration of linguistic/cognitive theory with program-based representations
- Clear writing; thorough quantitative and qualitative evaluation
- Compelling evidence that complexity dominates informativeness in human question choice

Cons against acceptance:
- Scalability and generalization beyond a hand-engineered, low-dimensional domain are unclear
- Reliance on hand-coding questions into a domain-specific grammar may limit applicability

Suggestions:
- Provide nested model comparisons (e.g., paired tests on leave-one-out log-likelihoods) to assess significance of lesioned-model differences.
- Argue for or demonstrate scaling (e.g., amortized EIG, neuralized PCFG priors, pruning/caching) or applications where the framework yields new insights in larger ML settings.
- Connect to formal semantics/pragmatics on sequential question-asking, especially Questions under Discussion (Roberts, 1996) and classic work on question semantics (Groenendijk & Stokhof, 1984).
