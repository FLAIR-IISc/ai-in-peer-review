- Introduces a novel computational model of human question-asking as probabilistic program generation using a lambda-calculus formalism.  
- Questions are generated by a hand-designed PCFG within a "Battleship" grid-world domain.  
- Core model scores questions by expected information gain (EIG), grammar-derived complexity, and answer type.  
- Extends active learning to compositional queries and formalizes self-directed inquiry in cognitive science.  
- Strength: innovative, technically sound integration of linguistic theory, cognitive science, and probabilistic modeling.  
- Strength: exceptionally clear writing that makes the complex framework easy to follow.  
- Strength: strong evaluation combining leave-one-out cross-validation of lesioned models and qualitative examples, showing generation of novel plausible questions.  
- Key empirical finding: complexity is a stronger predictor of human questions than informativeness alone.  
- Major weakness: scalability concerns—hand-engineered grammar and computationally intensive inference tested only in a small, low-dimensional domain.  
- Implication: unclear applicability and significance for broader ML/NIPS audiences without demonstrated scaling or domain transfer.  
- Suggested improvement: explicitly address scalability or identify ML domains where knowledge-intensive modeling has unique advantages.  
- Suggested improvement: perform nested model comparisons on log-likelihoods to establish statistical significance of ablation results.  
- Suggested improvement: better contextualize results with formal semantics/pragmatics literature (e.g., Questions under Discussion).  
- Overall verdict: high-quality, original, well-executed work, but limited demonstrated scope raises major concerns for acceptance at NIPS.
