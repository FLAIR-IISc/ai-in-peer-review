- Introduces a novel framework modeling human question-asking as program generation, treating questions as executable programs.
- Uses a PCFG-based probabilistic generative model with features for informativeness (EIG), complexity (PCFG probability), and answer type.
- Evaluated on 605 natural language questions from a Battleship game; model predicts human question frequencies and generates novel plausible questions.
- Advances cognitive science understanding of self-directed learning by modeling productive, context-sensitive questioning beyond simple label requests.
- Bridges gap with dialog/active learning work by providing the first predictive model that accounts for human question productivity and context sensitivity.
- Shows humans balance informativeness, complexity, and answer-type preferences; pure informativeness metrics fail alone.
- Technically sound: compositional, computable, probabilistic framework grounded in cognitive science and linguistics.
- Thorough evaluation with leave-one-out cross-validation; all features contribute, with complexity emerging as the dominant predictor.
- Qualitative results show creative capacity (e.g., generating human-like novel questions).
- Exceptionally clear, well-organized exposition; grammar design, features, and evaluation are well explained.
- Original formalization distinguishing question-asking from prior program-based question-answering and neural generation approaches.
- Significant implications for cognitive science and ML (more human-like active learning), but limited real-world scope.
- Main limitation: domain-specific grammar and unclear scalability to more complex domains; need argument/evidence for generalization and computational cost.
- Suggested minor improvements: perform nested model comparisons for statistical significance and consider related work on sequential question-asking (e.g., "Questions under Discussion").
- Overall assessment: high-quality, original, methodologically robust work that deserves acceptance despite domain-specific limitations.
