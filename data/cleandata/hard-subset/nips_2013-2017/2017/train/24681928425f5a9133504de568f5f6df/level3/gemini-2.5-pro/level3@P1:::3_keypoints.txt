- Introduces a novel framework treating questions as programs generated from a compositional grammar.  
- Uses a log-linear model with features: informativeness (EIG), complexity (PCFG probability), answer type, and relevance.  
- Evaluated on a human question dataset in a "Battleship"-like game using leave-one-out cross-validation.  
- Uses importance sampling to approximate the intractable partition function and gradients.  
- Full model achieves best log-likelihood and can generate novel, human-like questions unseen in training.  
- Authors report bootstrap analysis showing the log-likelihood advantage of the full model is significant in 81% of samples.  
- Central claim that the model reveals how people balance informativeness and complexity is not strongly supported.  
- Correlation results are ambiguous: information-agnostic model rho=0.65 slightly outperforms full model rho=0.64, while complexity-agnostic model performs very poorly (−0.36).  
- EIG alone has no correlation with question frequency, suggesting complexity may be the dominant factor.  
- High noise in the data (many questions asked only once) weakens empirical conclusions about informativeness.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and reproducible, with effective examples of programs and generated questions.  
- Framing open-ended question generation as probabilistic program synthesis is highly original and powerful.  
- Misses important related work: Hawkins et al. (2015, CogSci) and the Question Under Discussion framework (Roberts, 1996).  
- Significant contribution: a generative system that produces rich, compositional, contextually relevant questions with implications for AI and cognitive science.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel framework, generative creativity, technical soundness, clarity, and the importance of complexity as a feature.  
- Cons for acceptance: weak empirical support for EIG-related claims and missing citations to relevant prior work.  
- Recommendation: accept with revisions to tone down claims about EIG’s role and to include the missing related work.
