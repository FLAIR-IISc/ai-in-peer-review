- Models human question-asking as lambda-calculus program generation in a Battleship-like grid using a hand-designed PCFG and a log-linear score combining EIG, complexity, and answer type with a PCFG prior.
- Human questions are hand-coded into the grammar; models compared via leave-one-out CV; all factors matter but complexity is the dominant predictor; model generates novel plausible questions.
- Technically sound: coherent formalization and appropriate inference/learning (importance sampling, lesion analyses) for the domain scale.
- Empirical claim that informativeness alone is insufficient is convincingly supported; complexity plays a central role.
- Major scalability concern: importance sampling over large program spaces with context-dependent EIG becomes intractable beyond low-dimensional domains.
- Clearly written and well-organized; mapping from natural language to programs, features, and evaluation are reproducible.
- Originality: novel integration of PCFG priors, information-theoretic utility, and program representations; distinctive inclusion of answer-type preferences.
- Significance: strong cognitive-science contribution with potential ML impact, but broader NeurIPS relevance depends on demonstrating scalability or transferable insights.
- Pros: principled linguistic/cognitive integration, clear writing, thorough quantitative and qualitative evaluation, compelling evidence favoring complexity.
- Cons: unclear generalization/scalability and reliance on hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar.
- Suggestions: report nested/paired tests on leave-one-out log-likelihoods; argue for or demonstrate scaling approaches (amortized EIG, neuralized PCFGs, pruning/caching); connect to formal semantics/pragmatics on sequential question-asking (e.g., Roberts 1996; Groenendijk & Stokhof 1984).
