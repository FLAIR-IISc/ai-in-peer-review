- Proposes modeling questions as executable programs using a lambda-calculus-like language and a PCFG.  
- Employs a log-linear model with four features: Expected Information Gain, grammar-based complexity, answer type, and relevance.  
- Evaluated on a Battleship dataset; predicts human question frequencies (Spearman 0.64) and generates novel human-like questions.  
- Formalizes the connection between question asking and program synthesis with a compositional grammar.  
- Thorough evaluation with ablation studies and well-motivated use of EIG from active learning.  
- Core idea lacks novelty—largely an application of existing program synthesis and log-linear techniques.  
- Task domain (Battleship) is overly simplistic and fails to capture natural language complexities like pragmatics and ambiguity.  
- Feature set is shallow (complexity only via grammar log-probability) and ignores cognitive load and deeper influences.  
- Evaluation is limited to one toy domain with no comparison to neural VQG approaches or cross-domain tests.  
- Technical execution and statistical analysis are sound, but experimental analysis lacks depth (e.g., why features dominate).  
- Paper is clearly written and organized, but the narrow focus reduces broader interest.  
- The core insight (questions as programs) is not novel and the specific application offers limited advancement over prior work.  
- Significance is limited by lack of generalizability and real-world relevance.  
- Pros: clear formal framework, solid in-domain evaluation, novel question generation; Cons: minimal novelty, constrained task, limited impact.  
- Overall recommendation: reject—incremental contribution unsuitable for NIPS; needs extension to more complex, real-world scenarios or stronger theoretical advance.
