This paper presents a novel computational model of human question asking, framing it as a problem of probabilistic program generation. The core idea is that questions can be represented as programs in a compositional language which, when executed on a world state, return an answer. The model defines a probability distribution over the space of possible questions using a log-linear model. The probability of a given question is a weighted combination of several features: its informativeness (modeled as Expected Information Gain, EIG), its complexity (related to program length), its answer type (e.g., Boolean, Number), and its relevance to the task. The authors evaluate this framework by modeling a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like game, where the goal is to resolve ambiguity about hidden objects. This work relates to and extends prior research in active learning, which typically considers simpler queries, and dialogue systems, which often rely on less flexible, pre-specified questions.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper is its novel and powerful conceptualization of goal-directed question asking as program synthesis. This is a significant step forward from standard active learning paradigms and provides a formal, generative account of the creativity and compositionality inherent in human inquiry. The framework is elegant and has the potential for high impact in both AI and computational cognitive science. For AI, it suggests a path toward more sophisticated active learning and dialogue agents that can ask rich, human-like questions. For cognitive science, it offers a concrete, predictive model of a fundamental aspect of human learning and reasoning.

The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and well-organized. The authors do a good job of explaining the probabilistic model, the grammar, the feature set, and the optimization procedure. The inclusion of the dataset's URL and sufficient detail in the paper suggests the work is reproducible. The qualitative results, particularly the demonstration that the model can generate novel and clever questions not seen in the training data (Figure 3), are very compelling and effectively showcase the model's generative power.

**Weaknesses:**

The main weakness of the paper lies in the experimental support for its central claim that the model reveals how people "balance informativeness and complexity." The evidence for the role of informativeness (EIG) is mixed and somewhat inconclusive. While the leave-one-out cross-validation shows the full model achieving a better log-likelihood than an information-agnostic version, the authors report that this advantage only holds in 81% of bootstrap samples. A 19% chance that this result is due to noise in the approximation of the partition function is not insignificant and raises questions about the robustness of this finding.

Furthermore, the correlation analysis complicates the picture. The full model's correlation with human question frequencies (Spearman's ρ = 0.64) is almost identical to that of the information-agnostic model (ρ = 0.65), while being substantially better than the complexity-agnostic model (ρ = -0.36). This suggests that complexity is the dominant factor driving the model's predictions, and the contribution of EIG is marginal at best in this analysis. While the authors correctly note that the data is sparse, the conflicting results between the log-likelihood and correlation analyses weaken the paper's conclusion about the interplay between EIG and complexity in human question asking.

Another point of concern is the omission of some highly relevant prior work. The paper would be strengthened by discussing its relationship to:
- Hawkins et al. (2015), "Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers," which also presents a goal-based Bayesian model of question asking in a game-like setting, using EIG to predict human queries.
- The linguistic framework of Questions Under Discussion (QUD), originating from Roberts (1996), "Information structure in discourse," which is a foundational theory for modeling goal-directed inquiry and relevance in discourse.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The core framework of modeling question asking as probabilistic program generation is highly original, powerful, and likely to be influential.
2.  The paper demonstrates the ability to generate complex, compositional, and contextually relevant questions in a non-trivial domain, a significant advance for AI.
3.  The work is presented clearly, is technically solid, and appears reproducible.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The experimental evidence for the central claim about balancing informativeness and complexity is weak and contradictory. The role of EIG in the model is not convincingly demonstrated.
2.  The paper fails to cite and compare against key related work in both cognitive science (Hawkins et al., 2015) and linguistics (Roberts, 1996), which limits its contribution.

In summary, this paper introduces an exciting and important research direction. The core idea is a significant contribution. However, the experimental results do not fully support the specific claims made about the factors driving human question generation, and the paper is missing connections to key literature. While the weaknesses are notable, the strength and originality of the core framework likely warrant acceptance, with the recommendation that the authors revise the paper to temper their claims about EIG and to properly situate their work with respect to the missing prior literature.
