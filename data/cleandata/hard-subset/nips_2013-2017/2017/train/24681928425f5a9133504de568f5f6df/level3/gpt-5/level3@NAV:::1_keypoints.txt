- Method: Models questions as executable lambda-calculus/LISP programs over Battleship boards using a PCFG and a log-linear model with features (EIG, length/complexity, answer type, board-feature indicator).  
- Training/evaluation: Parameters fit by maximizing likelihood on human questions; evaluated with leave-one-context-out prediction and qualitative sampling of novel questions.  
- Inference: Importance sampling for partition function and feature expectations; inference approach is reasonable for the toy domain.  
- Empirical finding: Ablations show both EIG and complexity penalties materially affect question prediction.  
- Engineering concern: Hypothesis space, grammar, and likelihood are heavily hand-engineered for the Battleship task.  
- EIG limitation: EIG computation depends on exact world models and one-step utility, avoiding key challenges in realistic active learning/interactive settings.  
- Clarity: Clear, well-organized paper with reproducible descriptions; some implementation details (proposal calibration, convergence diagnostics, sensitivity to grammar priors) need expansion.  
- Originality: Core components (compositional semantics, EIG utility, log-linear selection) are known; the contribution is a modest integration in a constrained setting.  
- Significance: Limited; predicting questions in a toy game and sampling engineered clever questions is unlikely to interest NeurIPS without scaling, NL interface, or downstream gains.  
- Pros: Clear, interpretable formalization with careful ablations; bridges active learning and compositional semantics; offers an interesting cognitive modeling perspective.  
- Cons: Narrow, hand-crafted domain with limited generality; minimal novelty relative to prior program-induction and pragmatic/QUD work; no natural-language interface or scalable evaluation and unclear ML impact.  
- Recommendation: Reject; to improve for ML venues, demonstrate scalability beyond Battleship, add an NL front-end, compare to neural/question-generation baselines, and show benefits in an active learning loop.
