Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a computational model of question asking that treats questions as executable programs over possible world states. A probabilistic context-free grammar defines a compositional space of well-typed programs (lambda calculus/LISP-like), enabling questions such as “How long is the blue ship?” (size Blue) or more complex compositional queries (e.g., equality over mapped size predicates). A log-linear distribution over programs selects questions using features capturing informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (−log probability under the grammar), answer type (Boolean/Number/Color/Location), and a relevance filter to exclude non-board-referential expressions. The model is evaluated on a Battleship-like task where humans typed free-form natural-language questions to resolve an ambiguous board; the authors map the questions to the program language, fit the model with leave-one-context-out cross-validation, and show that the full model (informativeness + complexity + type + relevance) best predicts human question frequencies, ablates cleanly, and can sample novel, human-like questions.

The work sits at the intersection of active learning [21], formal semantics of questions and partition semantics [6,18], and cognitive “language of thought” modeling [3,5,14,17]. It also connects to program induction and neural program execution for reasoning [12,13], to dialog/question generation (goal-directed and open-domain) [1,20,22], and visual question generation [16,23]. Relative to these, the contribution is not end-to-end language generation but a cognitively motivated, compositional, executable representation of question meaning and a simple utility-based sampling model that accounts for human data in a tightly constrained domain.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of questions as programs over world states, leveraging compositionality and computability in a principled way (aligned with Montague-style semantics).
- Careful probabilistic modeling with interpretable features; clean ablations isolating the role of informativeness, complexity, and type preferences.
- Empirical evaluation against human data with cross-context prediction; correlations and bootstrap analyses support the main claims.
- Demonstrates generative capacity by sampling novel, context-sensitive questions beyond the training set.
- Relevance to cognitive modeling of how people balance informativeness and complexity when asking questions.

Weaknesses
- Very constrained domain with handcrafted grammar and features; no end-to-end link to natural language (the mapping from text to programs is assumed, not learned).
- Core modeling ingredients (log-linear over hand-designed features such as EIG and length/complexity) are straightforward; overall technical novelty for the NIPS ML community is limited.
- Informativeness is computed with an idealized, exact semantics over a small hypothesis space; scalability to realistic tasks is unclear.
- The importance sampler and grammar also define the complexity feature (−log q), blurring modeling and inference choices; potential coupling may bias results.
- Dataset is small (605 Qs, 16 contexts used for training/testing) with domain-specific rules; several contexts and question types had to be dropped for computational reasons.
- Evaluation of “novel” questions is qualitative; no human judgments of quality or utility relative to baselines (e.g., neural VQG, goal-directed dialog policies).

Quality
Technically sound within scope. The probabilistic model, inference (importance sampling), and ablations are appropriate. Claims are supported on the given task. However, there are limitations: (i) the relevance filter and answer-type features encode domain-specific structure; (ii) two contexts and stochastic draw-based questions were excluded; (iii) the complexity feature depends on the same PCFG used for proposal sampling; and (iv) generalization beyond Battleship is not demonstrated.

Clarity
Well-written and organized. The formal language, grammar, and features are clearly described, with intuitive examples. The supplementary materials house critical details (core rewrite rules), which could be summarized more in the main text. A brief discussion of computational complexity and sensitivity to proposal distributions would improve clarity.

Originality
Moderate. Representing questions as executable, compositional programs is well grounded in prior semantics/program-induction literature; using EIG as utility is standard in active learning. The novel aspect is bringing these together to model human question generation and show predictive power in a controlled task. The approach feels more incremental than novel to the NIPS audience.

Significance
Limited for NIPS. The task is simple and domain-specific; the model is not competitive with or directly comparable to mainstream ML question-generation/dialog systems, nor does it advance scalable program synthesis or active learning algorithms. The contribution is more impactful for cognitive science than for machine learning.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, principled formalization with interpretable components and solid ablations.
- Provides a rare predictive model of open-ended human question asking with compositional structure.
- Could inspire ML work on structured, goal-directed question policies beyond label queries.

Arguments against acceptance
- Restricted domain with hand-engineered grammar/features; no natural-language interface.
- Limited technical novelty; straightforward log-linear model over simple features.
- Small dataset, removal of challenging cases, and qualitative novelty evaluation.
- Unclear path to scale or applicability to broader NIPS problems (vision, language, dialog).

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to the proposal distribution q and to using −log q as the complexity feature? Have you tried alternative complexity priors (e.g., program length, MDL, description length of AST)?
2. Can you report correlations/log-likelihoods when all contexts and draw-based questions are included using approximate execution?
3. How robust are predictions to small perturbations of the grammar (e.g., adding/removing operators)?
4. Can you provide human evaluations of sampled novel questions vs. neural baselines (e.g., VQG models) on informativeness and answerability?
5. What would be required to learn the text-to-program mapping and to deploy in a second domain without heavy knowledge engineering?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add an end-to-end variant that learns a semantic parser from text to programs (e.g., weak supervision via denotations) and report full-pipeline results.
- Evaluate on a second domain with different predicates to assess portability.
- Compare against strong ML baselines for question generation/dialog, including utility-aware policies.
- Explore richer, learnable priors over programs (e.g., neurally parameterized PCFGs) and separate the proposal from the complexity penalty.
- Obtain human ratings on sampled questions’ usefulness and clarity.

Recommendation
Lean reject for NIPS: the work is elegant and valuable for cognitive modeling, but the technical novelty and ML significance are limited by the narrow domain, hand-engineered semantics, and lack of end-to-end language handling.
