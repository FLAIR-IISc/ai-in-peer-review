- Proposes a cognitive model that treats questions as executable programs to generate human-like questions in a Battleship information-search game.  
- Evaluated on human question data and successfully predicts question frequencies and synthesizes novel, human-like questions.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-written, reproducible, and provides clear explanations of grammar, generative model, and optimization.  
- Demonstrates the model captures compositionality and computability of human questions, showing productivity beyond the training set.  
- Full model outperforms lesioned versions on held-out data by log-likelihood, indicating better predictive performance.  
- Model operates on semantic representations rather than raw natural language, limiting direct real-world applicability.  
- The grammar is domain-specific (Battleship), raising questions about required knowledge engineering for new domains.  
- Claim that the work reveals how people balance informativeness and complexity is weakly supported by the experiments.  
- Performance differences are not significant across all comparisons, correlation results are noisy, and bootstrap estimates show a 19% chance the log-likelihood result is incorrect.  
- Missing discussion of related prior work such as Hawkins (2015) and Roberts (1996).  
- Main contribution is demonstrating generation of interesting compositional questions in a non-trivial domain, relevant to AI and cognitive modeling.  
- Recommendation: accept with revisions to address limitations, expand discussion of implications/extensions, and include more related prior work.
