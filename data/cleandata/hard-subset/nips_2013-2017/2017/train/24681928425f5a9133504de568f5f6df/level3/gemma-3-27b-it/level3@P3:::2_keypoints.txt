- Presents a novel computational model framing questions as programs from a hand-designed PCFG, scored by information gain, complexity, and answer type, evaluated on a Battleship-like human question dataset.  
- Demonstrates the model predicts human question choices and can generate plausible novel questions.  
- Builds on active learning, dialog systems, and visual question generation but emphasizes compositional, goal-directed question generation from first principles.  
- Aligns with cognitive-science program-synthesis approaches to modeling concepts and reasoning.  
- Strength: innovative integration of linguistic and cognitive theory by representing questions as programs for formal analysis of informativeness and complexity.  
- Strength: clear, well-written exposition with appropriate quantitative evaluation (lesioned models, leave-one-out CV) and compelling qualitative examples.  
- Strength: significant cognitive-science contribution showing informativeness alone is insufficient to predict human question asking.  
- Weakness: limited scalability — computationally strained and demonstrated only in a small, low-dimensional domain.  
- Weakness: reliance on a hand-designed grammar and extensive program-space search limits generalizability to larger, real-world domains.  
- Weakness: dependence on a specific game domain restricts broader impact.  
- Quality: technically sound with clear theoretical basis and well-presented experiments; authors acknowledge limitations.  
- Clarity: exceptionally well-written, organized, and supported by helpful supplementary material.  
- Originality: highly original combination of program synthesis, probabilistic modeling, and cognitive-science principles.  
- Significance: important for understanding cognitive mechanisms of question asking; complexity’s dominant role is noteworthy.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theory-grounded approach, clear writing, appropriate evaluation, cognitive-science significance, and ability to generate novel questions.  
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability and generalizability concerns, hand-designed grammar, and computational cost.  
- Minor suggestion 1: perform more rigorous statistical tests (e.g., likelihood ratio tests) on lesioned-model comparisons.  
- Minor suggestion 2: consider related work in formal semantics/pragmatics on sequential question-asking (QUD framework) for future extensions.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with significant contributions; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address stated comments.
