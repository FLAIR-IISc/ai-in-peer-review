- Introduces a computational framework that models human question asking by representing questions as executable programs over a world state.
- Evaluates the approach on a simplified Battleship-like game, showing the model predicts human question choices and generates novel human-like questions.
- Uses a probabilistic log-linear model with features for informativeness (Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and board relevance.
- Situates the work within active learning, compositional semantics, program induction, and formal question semantics literatures.
- Strength: Clearly written with a rigorous formalism using a programming-language representation of questions.
- Strength: Empirical validation convincingly shows predictive power and realistic question generation (quantitative and qualitative results).
- Strength: Ablation/feature analysis reveals the importance of informativeness, complexity, and answer type, notably complexity.
- Strength: Theoretical grounding in information theory and compositional linguistics is solid.
- Weakness: Task is highly constrained and simple, limiting generalizability to complex real-world scenarios.
- Weakness: Core ideas (questions as programs, compositional semantics) are not fundamentally novel in the field.
- Weakness: Relies on hand-crafted grammar and domain knowledge, which hampers scalability and transfer to new domains.
- Weakness: Comparisons to existing active learning and question-generation baselines are limited.
- Argues for acceptance: Well-executed study, empirical support for program-based question modeling, and public code/data for reproducibility.
- Argues against acceptance: Limited novelty, task simplicity, grammar dependence, and narrow appeal for the broad conference audience.
- Overall assessment: Solid contribution to cognitive science/active learning but not a significant enough advance for NIPS; recommend rejection and submission to a more specialized venue.
