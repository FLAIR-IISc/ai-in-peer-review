- Proposes a novel model treating questions as executable programs and models question generation as a probabilistic process over a compositional program space.  
- Energy of questions defined by weighted features: informativeness (EIG), complexity (program length), answer type, and relevance to the board.  
- Evaluated on human question data from a Battleship-like game; full model outperforms lesioned variants at predicting human question frequencies.  
- Grammar-based program representation effectively captures compositionality and yields novel human-like questions not seen in training.  
- Strong technical quality: careful experimental design, thorough evaluation, clear writing, and reproducibility.  
- Shows that complexity (simplicity bias) matters alongside informativeness when modeling human question selection.  
- Main contribution: demonstrating generation of meaningful, compositional questions in a non-trivial domain with implications for AI and cognitive science.  
- Builds on active learning, program synthesis, and cognitive-linguistic literature but lacks adequate engagement with Hawkins et al. (2015) and Roberts (1996).  
- Insufficient contrast with related semantic-parsing-style approaches (e.g., Wang et al. 2015).  
- Claims about how people balance informativeness and complexity are not strongly supported by results (EIG correlation zero; bootstrap test ambiguous).  
- Evidence for the importance of EIG is ambiguous — advantage of full model over information-agnostic model may be spurious.  
- Limited generalizability: narrow Battleship domain and reliance on substantial domain-specific knowledge; authors don’t quantify required domain knowledge for transfer.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, meaningful contribution, but overstates EIG claims and misses key related work.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to temper EIG claims and properly engage Hawkins (2015) and Roberts (1996).
