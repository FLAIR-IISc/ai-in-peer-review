- Introduces a computational model of human question asking as probabilistic program generation.
- Represents question utility with a log-linear combination of features: Expected Information Gain (EIG), complexity (program length), answer type, and task relevance.
- Implemented and evaluated in a "Battleship" game domain to predict human open-ended questions.
- Frames work as connecting active learning with the "language of thought," proposing a formal, compositional approach to inquiry.
- Major strength: novel, powerful framework for generating rich, compositional questions beyond simple label requests.
- Paper is technically sound, clearly written, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.
- Model can generate creative, contextually useful questions not seen in training, supporting utility for AI and cognitive modeling.
- Main weakness: weak empirical support for the central cognitive claim about how people balance informativeness and complexity.
- Contribution of the EIG feature is tenuous: a model without EIG correlates with human question frequency almost as well.
- Bootstrap analysis shows a 19% chance the log-likelihood advantage of the full model is spurious, undermining claims about EIG.
- Overlooks important related work, notably Hawkins et al. (2015) on goal-directed questioning and the QUD framework (Roberts, 1996).
- Recommendation: accept conditional on revising to moderate claims about EIG’s role and situating the work within the missing prior literature.
