This paper presents a novel computational model of human question asking, framing the problem as one of probabilistic program generation. The central idea is that questions can be represented as programs in a compositional "language of thought." The model defines a log-linear probability distribution over the space of possible programs, where the probability of a given question is determined by a weighted combination of its features: informativeness (Expected Information Gain, EIG), complexity (based on program length), answer type (e.g., Boolean, number), and relevance to the task. This framework is applied to a Battleship-like game, where the goal is to predict the open-ended, natural language questions humans ask to resolve ambiguity about hidden ships on a grid. The approach connects ideas from active learning, program synthesis, and cognitive science, moving beyond the simple label queries of typical active learning systems to model the generation of rich, compositional questions.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of question asking as probabilistic inference over a grammar-defined program space is elegant and well-executed. The use of a log-linear model with importance sampling for optimization is an appropriate methodology for this problem. The authors are also commendable for their transparency regarding the statistical uncertainty of their results, reporting bootstrap estimates for their main log-likelihood comparison.

However, the main weakness of the paper lies in the support for its central claims. The authors conclude that their results "show how people balance informativeness and complexity," but the evidence for the role of informativeness (EIG) is surprisingly weak. While the full model outperforms a lesioned "information-agnostic" model on the log-likelihood of held-out data, the authors report that this advantage only holds in 81% of bootstrap samples, indicating a non-trivial (19%) chance that this result is due to noise. More problematically, the Spearman correlation analysis shows that the information-agnostic model actually correlates slightly *better* with human question frequencies than the full model (0.65 vs. 0.64). This contradictory evidence significantly undermines the claim that the model has uncovered the specific trade-off between EIG and other factors.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the model is described logically, and the experimental evaluation is well-structured. The use of concrete examples and the qualitative results in Figure 3, which showcase novel questions generated by the model, are particularly effective at illustrating the power of the approach. The work appears reproducible, with the authors providing a link to their dataset.

**Originality:** The primary contribution of this work is its novel framing of goal-directed question asking as program generation. This is a significant conceptual advance that synthesizes ideas from multiple fields. While program synthesis has been used for question *answering*, its application to question *generation* in a goal-directed context is new and powerful.

The paper could, however, be strengthened by better situating itself within existing literature. It overlooks key related work, such as Hawkins et al. (2015), which presents a conceptually similar goal-based, Bayesian model of informative question asking. Additionally, the broader linguistic context is not fully addressed; referencing the highly influential Question Under Discussion (QUD) framework (Roberts, 1996) would provide a deeper connection to formal pragmatics and theories of how questions structure discourse.

**Significance:** Despite the ambiguity in the experimental results, the paper's primary significance is strong. The main contribution is the demonstration of a generative system that can produce a rich, compositional, and creative set of questions within a non-trivial domain. This is a valuable proof-of-concept for both AI and cognitive modeling. For AI, it points the way toward active learning agents that can ask more human-like, intelligent questions. For cognitive science, it provides a powerful, formal framework for thinking about the computations underlying human inquiry. The ability of the model to generate sensible and useful questions that were not present in the training data is a compelling demonstration of its productivity and a significant step forward for the field.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and powerful framework for modeling question asking as probabilistic program generation.
*   Demonstrates the ability to generate a rich, compositional, and creative set of questions for a complex task, including novel questions not seen in the training data.
*   The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and appears reproducible.
*   Represents a significant conceptual advance for building more sophisticated active learning agents and for the cognitive modeling of human inquiry.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The central experimental claim—that the model reveals how people balance informativeness and complexity—is not strongly supported by the results. The evidence for the role of EIG is statistically uncertain and contradicted by the correlation analysis.
*   Missing citations to relevant and closely related prior work in cognitive science (Hawkins et al., 2015) and linguistics (Roberts, 1996).
