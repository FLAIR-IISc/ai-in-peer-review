

This paper proposes a novel computational model for human question asking by treating questions as formal programs that, when executed on a world state, output an answer. The authors model question generation as a probabilistic process over a compositional program space, with energy defined as a weighted sum of question features: informativeness (Expected Information Gain), complexity (program length), answer type, and relevance (whether the question refers to the game board). They evaluate the model on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans playing a Battleship-like game to identify ship configurations, demonstrating that their full model outperforms variants without key features in predicting human question frequencies.

The work builds on and extends several strands of prior research. It relates to active learning (e.g., Settles 2010) but addresses a significant gap by modeling richer, open-ended questions rather than simple label requests. It connects to program synthesis (Lake et al. 2015) and cognitive science approaches to language (Fodor 1975; Marcus 1998), though the authors overlook important related work including Hawkins et al. (2015), which develops a goal-directed question model, and Roberts (1996), which establishes the Question Under Discussion (QUD) framework for understanding question meaning. The paper's computational approach to question asking as program generation is conceptually similar to recent work on semantic parsing (Wang et al. 2015), but the authors do not adequately contrast their approach.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a well-constructed model, careful experimental design, and thorough evaluation. The grammar-based program representation effectively captures the compositional nature of human questions, and the probabilistic model successfully generates novel human-like questions not present in the training data. The model variants provide a clear analysis of the relative importance of different features, showing that complexity (not just informativeness) matters for human question selection. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and reproducible, with detailed descriptions of the grammar, features, and optimization procedures.

The main contribution is demonstrating how to generate meaningful, compositional questions in a non-trivial domain, making the approach valuable for both AI (enabling more human-like question asking in machines) and cognitive science (providing a predictive model of human question asking). The authors convincingly show that their compositional program representation enables the generation of novel questions that humans would ask, which is a significant advance over previous approaches that produce stereotyped questions.

However, the paper's claims about how people balance informativeness and complexity are not strongly supported by the results. While the full model outperforms lesioned variants on log-likelihood, the correlation between human question frequencies and EIG alone is zero (as noted in the paper), and the advantage of the full model over the information-agnostic model is only 81% in bootstrap tests (with a 19% chance the log-likelihood difference is spurious). The paper overstates the evidence for the importance of EIG, suggesting that people optimize for information gain when the results are actually ambiguous. The claim that the model reveals "how people balance informativeness and complexity" is not well-supported by the experimental evidence.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the narrow domain (Battleship game) and the fact that the model requires substantial domain-specific knowledge. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they do not sufficiently address how much domain knowledge would be required for generalization to other domains. The approach represents a step toward more capable active learning machines, but the paper does not convincingly demonstrate how this work advances the state of the art beyond the specific domain.

Pro arguments for acceptance: Strong technical contribution to modeling question asking as program generation; effective demonstration of novel question generation; well-executed experiments with clear analysis of model components; significant potential for both AI and cognitive science applications.

Con arguments against acceptance: Overstated claims about the role of informativeness (EIG) in human question selection; missing key related work (Hawkins et al. 2015, Roberts 1996); limited generalizability due to domain-specific knowledge requirements; ambiguous evidence for the core theoretical claim about balancing informativeness and complexity.

Overall, this is a technically sound and well-written paper that makes a meaningful contribution to modeling human question asking. While the claims about the balance between informativeness and complexity are not strongly supported, the work is an important step toward more human-like question asking in machines and provides a valuable computational framework for cognitive science. The paper would be strengthened by a more measured discussion of the EIG results and proper engagement with Hawkins et al. (2015) and Roberts (1996), but the core contribution remains significant. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing these issues.
