Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper models question asking as program generation in a simple, domain-specific, lambda/LISP-like language with compositional (Montagovian) semantics. Questions are executable programs that return Booleans, numbers, colors, or locations when run on a hypothesized Battleship board. A probabilistic context-free grammar defines the question space; a log-linear model over question features induces a distribution over questions conditioned on context. The features include expected information gain (EIG) with a zero-EIG indicator, a complexity/length prior (−log PCFG probability), answer-type indicators (Boolean, Number, Color, Location), and a relevance filter (does the program reference board features). Parameters are learned by maximizing the likelihood of human questions via importance sampling over programs. Evaluation uses a dataset of 605 human questions across 18 Battleship contexts (two contexts omitted for computation), leave-one-out prediction of question distributions, ablations (information-, complexity-, and type-agnostic), and qualitative generation of novel questions.

The work connects to active learning (Settles), semantic/pragmatic theory of questions as partitions over worlds (Groenendijk & Stokhof; Roberts), program-induction approaches to cognition (Goodman et al.; Lake et al.), and executable program representations for reasoning (Johnson et al.). It also relates to dialog and question generation work, but differs by using a highly constrained symbolic language and an ideal-observer EIG objective rather than end-to-end learning from natural language.

Strengths:
- Clear, interpretable formalization of questions as compositional programs; the grammar can represent all questions in the dataset.
- Reasonable active-learning motivation and an explicit trade-off between informativeness and complexity that echoes human preferences.
- Quantitative evaluation with ablations: complexity/length is shown to be critical; informativeness and answer-type features also help.
- Qualitative samples demonstrate the system can synthesize syntactically novel and contextually relevant questions.

Weaknesses:
- Core ideas (questions as programs; utility–complexity trade-off; EIG as utility) are straightforward and have been widely discussed in linguistics, pragmatics, and cognitive modeling; the ML novelty is limited.
- The task is very simple, highly constrained, and logically unambiguous. The bespoke grammar and hand-engineered domain predicates make success unsurprising and reduce external validity.
- The approach sidesteps natural language entirely; no parser or surface realization is learned, and no end-to-end mapping from text to meaning is attempted.
- The feature set is minimal and largely obvious (EIG, length, coarse answer type, relevance), leaving little room for methodological contribution to NIPS/NeurIPS beyond a small cognitive modeling case study.
- Evaluation is limited: small dataset; two contexts dropped; heavy reliance on importance sampling with a uniform PCFG proposal; modest predictive correlations; no comparison to stronger baselines (e.g., k-NN over logical forms from other contexts, neural sequence models with copy, Bayesian RSA-style models).
- Generalization beyond Battleship is unclear; the amount of knowledge engineering needed for new domains is acknowledged but not addressed.

Quality:
- Technically sound within scope; the log-linear framework and EIG computation under an ideal observer are reasonable. However, the inference pipeline leans on a large, fixed proposal (150k programs), and sensitivity analyses (e.g., proposal quality, sample size, grammar limits) are not reported.
- Claims are commensurate with evidence: the model correlates with human question frequencies (avg Spearman ~0.64 for the full model; complexity ablation fails badly), and it can generate plausible novel questions. At the same time, the paper itself notes EIG alone does not predict human frequencies, which weakens the central active-learning rationale unless combined with the length prior.
- The piece is complete as a cognitive modeling paper, but as an ML contribution it reads as a focused case study.

Clarity:
- Well written and organized. The formalism, features, and optimization are clearly specified, with helpful examples. The role of each feature is explicit. Some implementation details (e.g., proposal distribution choice, equivalence testing cost, effect of dropping contexts) could be expanded.

Originality:
- The combination of program synthesis, EIG-based utility, and a length prior for human-like question asking in this toy domain is mildly novel. However, representing queries as programs, using information gain, and balancing informativeness with complexity are well-trodden ideas in adjacent literatures. Relative to prior NIPS work on program induction and visual reasoning, the contribution is incremental.

Significance:
- Likely limited impact for the NIPS community. The approach does not advance state of the art in active learning, dialog, or program induction; it does not provide new scalable algorithms, datasets, or theoretical insights likely to be adopted by practitioners. The main value is as a cognitive model in a tightly controlled setting.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, interpretable model linking compositional semantics and active learning.
- Ablations and qualitative samples support the main claims.
- Could interest a niche audience at the intersection of cognitive science and ML.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited ML novelty; simple features and hand-crafted grammar in a toy domain.
- Narrow scope and limited generality; no natural-language interface or broader domains.
- Modest empirical gains with limited baselines; computational choices lack robustness analysis.
- Unclear relevance to central NIPS topics beyond a cognitive modeling case study.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Demonstrate generalization to at least one additional domain with minimal re-engineering, or learn parts of the grammar/predicate inventory from data.
- Integrate a learned NL→LF parser and evaluate end-to-end from human text to executable programs, or provide a surface realization module and human evaluation of generated texts.
- Provide stronger baselines (e.g., retrieval over logical forms, neural sequence generators with constraints, RSA-based pragmatic models).
- Analyze sensitivity to proposal distributions, sample budgets, grammar depth limits, and feature choices; report uncertainty on correlations.
- Close the loop: show active querying reduces board identification steps in simulation or with human oracles.

Overall recommendation:
While the paper is careful and clear, the core ideas are obvious and the task is too simple and constrained to make a compelling NIPS contribution. I do not believe this work will interest the broader NIPS community in its present form. I recommend rejection, with the suggestion to target a cognitive science venue or substantially broaden the technical contribution and empirical scope for ML audiences.
