## Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper presents a cognitive model of question asking, framing questions as programs generated by a grammar and evaluated based on informativeness, complexity, answer type, and relevance. The model is implemented and tested within the Battleship domain, aiming to predict human question choices and even generate novel, human-like questions. The work is technically sound, well-written, and the code is made available for reproducibility – a significant strength. 

The core idea of representing questions as programs is a compelling one, building on ideas of compositionality and computability in cognition. It moves beyond simple label-requesting active learning towards a more nuanced understanding of information seeking. The evaluation, comparing the full model against lesioned versions, demonstrates the importance of the proposed features, particularly complexity, in predicting human behavior. The ability to generate novel questions is also a noteworthy achievement, showcasing the generative capacity of the model.

However, the evidence supporting the claim that the model *reveals* how humans balance informativeness and complexity is somewhat weak. While the full model outperforms lesioned versions in log-likelihood, the correlation with human judgments is comparable for models *without* the EIG feature. The authors acknowledge this, and the bootstrap estimates highlight the uncertainty surrounding the role of EIG. This suggests that complexity, or other unmodeled factors, may be driving the observed correlations. 

Furthermore, the paper lacks sufficient engagement with related work. The authors should discuss Hawkins (2015), which also proposes a goal-based question model, and Roberts (1996) which lays out the foundational QUD (Question Under Discussion) framework relevant to understanding question generation. These omissions weaken the positioning of the work within the broader field.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-executed.
*   Novel approach to modeling question asking as program generation.
*   Demonstrates the generation of interesting compositional questions in a non-trivial domain.
*   Reproducibility is a strong positive.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Evidence for the importance of informativeness (EIG) is uncertain.
*   Claims about revealing human cognitive processes are not strongly supported.
*   Missing related work (Hawkins 2015, Roberts 1996).



**Overall:** Despite the limitations, this paper presents a valuable contribution. The approach is innovative and the results are promising. While the claims about cognitive mechanisms are overstated, the demonstration of generating human-like questions is a significant step forward and has potential for both AI and cognitive modeling applications. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the missing related work and temper the claims regarding cognitive insight.
