This paper introduces a computational framework for modeling human question asking, specifically within the context of an information-seeking game (Battleship). The central idea is to represent questions as formal programs—executable expressions that yield an answer when applied to a world state. The authors propose a probabilistic model, trained on a dataset of human questions, to predict which questions people will ask and to generate novel, human-like questions. They demonstrate that their model, incorporating features related to informativeness, complexity, and answer type, outperforms variants lacking these components.

The work builds on a long line of research in active learning, where systems learn by strategically querying an oracle. While prior work in this area has largely focused on simple label requests, this paper seeks to address more complex, human-like queries. It also connects to research in dialog systems and visual question generation, but distinguishes itself by focusing on goal-directed, context-sensitive questioning in a novel scenario, rather than relying on large datasets of image-question pairs or canned responses. The framing of questions as programs draws on ideas from linguistics (compositional semantics) and cognitive science (language of thought hypothesis). 

The strengths of the paper lie in its clear articulation of the problem—modeling the richness of human questions—and its principled approach using a formal, compositional framework. The experimental evaluation provides a solid empirical grounding, demonstrating that the proposed model can predict human question choices and generate plausible new questions. The ablation study, comparing different model variants, effectively highlights the importance of both informativeness and complexity in generating human-like questions.  The inclusion of a novel dataset created for this task is a valuable contribution.

However, the paper also has significant weaknesses. The primary concern is the limited scope and simplicity of the task. The Battleship game, while providing a controlled environment, is a highly constrained and logically unambiguous domain. The success of the model in this setting doesn't necessarily generalize to more open-ended, real-world question-asking scenarios. The core ideas – that questions can be formalized, that informativeness and complexity matter – are not particularly novel or surprising, and the specific grammar developed feels somewhat ad-hoc to the task.  While the authors acknowledge some limitations, they underestimate the degree to which the simplicity of the task drives the results. The reliance on a log-linear model, while practical, feels somewhat limiting given the potentially complex interplay between the features.  Furthermore, the quantitative evaluation relies on Spearman’s rank correlation, which, given the sparsity of the data (many questions asked only once), has limited interpretative power.  The qualitative evaluation of generated questions, while interesting, is subjective.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel, albeit limited, computational model of human question asking.
*   Demonstrates empirically that the model can predict and generate plausible questions in a controlled setting.
*   Provides a valuable dataset for future research in this area.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The task is overly simplified and lacks real-world relevance.
*   The core ideas are not particularly novel.
*   The model's success is likely due to the constrained nature of the domain.
*   The paper is unlikely to have a broad impact on the NIPS community, as it sits at the intersection of several fields without making a substantial contribution to any single one.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a weak accept. While technically sound and clearly written, its limited scope and lack of significant novelty make it a questionable fit for NIPS. The paper represents an incremental contribution to a niche area and is unlikely to be widely cited or built upon by the NIPS community. The reviewers should carefully consider whether the methodological rigor and empirical results justify inclusion in the conference, especially given the potential for more impactful work.
