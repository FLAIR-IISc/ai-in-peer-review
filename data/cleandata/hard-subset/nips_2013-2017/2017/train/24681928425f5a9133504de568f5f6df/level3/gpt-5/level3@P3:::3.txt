Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a cognitive and computational framework in which questions are formal programs executed on possible world states, with a probabilistic model selecting among a rich, compositional space of programs. The unnormalized log-probability of a question is a weighted sum of features capturing informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (length via a PCFG prior), answer type (Boolean/number/color/location), and a relevance indicator distinguishing belief-state-dependent questions from world-independent expressions. The approach is evaluated in a Battleship-style domain (6×6 grid, three colored ships), using leave-one-context-out fits to predict which natural-language questions humans ask in ambiguous board states. The full model yields better held-out log-likelihoods than lesioned variants; however, correlations with human question frequencies are essentially unchanged when EIG is removed. The model also generates plausible compositional questions not seen in training.

This work connects to several literatures: active learning beyond label queries, program induction and the “language of thought” view, and program-based semantic parsing. It is also closely related to goal-based cognitive models of question-asking, including Hawkins et al. (2015), who formalize question utility in terms of informativeness and costs, and to Roberts’ (1996) Questions Under Discussion (QUD) framework, which views questions as partitions over worlds. While both Hawkins (2015) and Roberts (1996) are cited, the paper would benefit from a deeper engagement clarifying how the present program-generation account differs from and extends these frameworks (e.g., the role of compositionality and program synthesis vs. utility over fixed question spaces).

Strengths
- Technically sound and carefully executed modeling: clear probabilistic formulation, coherent grammar, and tractable approximate inference via importance sampling.
- Reproducibility: dataset and code are available; the grammar spans all human questions; optimization details are thorough.
- Compositional generalization: convincingly demonstrates generation of novel, human-like questions in a non-trivial domain.
- Empirical evaluation: held-out likelihoods and rank correlations provide complementary views; ablations are informative (complexity feature clearly important).

Weaknesses
- Evidence for informativeness (EIG) is equivocal. Models without EIG correlate with human frequencies about as well as the full model (e.g., Spearman ~0.65 vs. 0.64). Bootstrap estimates suggest a 19% chance the full model’s log-likelihood advantage over the information-agnostic variant does not hold, so the role of EIG remains uncertain given current data and approximations.
- Claims about revealing how people balance informativeness and complexity are stronger than what the results support. Complexity (length prior) and answer type capture most of the predictable structure; the incremental contribution of EIG is small and unstable.
- Data sparsity yields noisy correlations (many questions asked once), limiting power to separate features and increasing sensitivity to approximation error in Z and EIG.
- The “relevance” feature is an ad hoc filter rather than a principled prior over world-referential programs; this risks double-counting with the grammar prior.
- Limited engagement with closely related cognitive/pragmatic accounts (Hawkins 2015; QUD) beyond citation; a more explicit contrast would situate the contribution better.

Quality
Overall technically solid. The modeling choices are appropriate and transparently reported. However, the causal interpretation that humans explicitly trade EIG against complexity is not strongly supported: likelihood and correlational evidence are consistent with simpler accounts where complexity and answer-type preferences dominate. The bootstrap analysis is a welcome addition and appropriately tempers claims. Additional uncertainty quantification for Z and EIG (e.g., PSIS-LOO, AIS/SMC for normalizers) would strengthen conclusions.

Clarity
Well-written and easy to follow. The grammar and features are clearly specified, and the training/inference procedures are reproducible. Two suggestions:
- Clarify the conceptual status of the relevance feature and whether it can be absorbed into a prior over programs.
- More explicitly present per-context differences where EIG helps vs. hurts, to avoid averaging away informative heterogeneity.

Originality
High in the way it treats questions as programs and performs question generation via program synthesis in a non-trivial domain. This goes beyond prior goal-based models that typically assume a fixed question space. The combination of compositional generation with utility-guided selection is novel and impactful.

Significance
Moderate to high. The main contribution is a practical, general recipe for generating interesting, compositional questions, useful for both AI systems that learn by asking and for cognitive modeling. The empirical claims about the specific role of EIG in human question choice are less conclusive, but the framework is promising and likely to be built upon.

Suggestions for improvement
- Deepen the contrast with Hawkins et al. (2015) and the QUD literature (Roberts, 1996). For example, map your program-induced partitions to QUDs and discuss how program composition corresponds to subquestion structure and strategy.
- Strengthen the EIG analysis:
  - Control for complexity and answer type via matched comparisons: do human-chosen questions have higher EIG than length/type-matched alternatives?
  - Report per-context deltas where EIG improves predictions; analyze systematic properties of such contexts.
  - Provide uncertainty on Z and EIG (e.g., PSIS-LOO for predictive fit; AIS/SMC for normalizers; Bayesian bootstrap for correlations).
- Reduce dependence on the relevance filter by (i) folding it into a structured prior over world-referential programs or (ii) restricting the grammar to world-referential constructs.
- Explore participant-level variability (hierarchical model) and richer cost functions (e.g., answerer load, time-to-answer), possibly measured in a follow-up experiment.
- Evaluate generalization beyond Battleship to test portability of the grammar and priors.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, compositional framework that can synthesize novel, useful questions.
- Solid engineering and reproducibility.
- Clear advance over fixed-question-space models; relevant to active learning and cognitive science communities.

Cons for acceptance
- Central claim about informativeness-complexity trade-off in human behavior is under-supported by noisy, mixed evidence.
- Heavy reliance on a single domain and a relevance filter; limited theoretical integration with existing pragmatic accounts.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper is technically strong and showcases a compelling approach to compositional question generation with promising implications for AI and cognitive modeling. Temper claims about EIG’s role and expand the theoretical connections, and this will be a very solid contribution.
