## Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper presents a novel approach to modeling human question asking, framing it as program generation within a “Battleship” grid domain. The authors leverage a hand-designed Probabilistic Context-Free Grammar (PCFG) and lambda calculus to represent questions, and evaluate a model that combines expected information gain, complexity (based on PCFG probability), and answer type to predict question selection. The work is a compelling attempt to bridge cognitive science and computational modeling of active learning, moving beyond simple label-requesting strategies. 

The paper builds upon prior work in active learning (Settles, 2009) and dialog systems (Serban et al., 2016), highlighting the limitations of existing approaches in capturing the richness and intentionality of human queries. It differentiates itself from recent deep learning approaches to question generation (Jain et al., 2017; Mostafazadeh et al., 2016) by focusing on goal-directed questioning in a novel scenario, rather than relying on large datasets of pre-existing question-answer pairs. The use of a formal program representation connects to work in formal semantics and pragmatics (Groenendijk & Stokhof, thesis; Roberts, 1996), though the authors could benefit from explicitly discussing the “Questions under Discussion” framework.

The strengths of this work are considerable. The integration of linguistic and cognitive theory is innovative, and the clear writing facilitates understanding of a complex approach. The quantitative evaluation, using lesioned models and leave-one-out cross-validation, is appropriate, and the qualitative demonstration of novel question generation is convincing. The finding that complexity is a dominant predictor of question selection is particularly insightful, challenging the assumption that informativeness alone drives human inquiry. 

However, the scalability of the approach is a significant limitation. The reliance on a hand-designed grammar and the computational strain of the model restrict its applicability to larger, more complex domains. While the authors acknowledge this, demonstrating or arguing for scenarios where the method *could* scale, or yield novel insights applicable to broader ML problems, is crucial. The nested model comparison suggested by the reviewers would strengthen the statistical rigor of the quantitative findings. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to a challenging problem, with clear writing, appropriate evaluation, and potentially significant cognitive science contributions. 
**Arguments against Acceptance:** The limited scalability and domain specificity raise concerns about the broader impact of the work.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution. Addressing the scalability concerns and performing the suggested statistical analysis would further enhance its impact.
