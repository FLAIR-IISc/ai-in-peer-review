This paper presents a computational model of human question asking, framing the problem as one of probabilistic program generation. The authors propose that humans generate questions by composing smaller conceptual building blocks into programs that, when executed on a world state, produce an answer. The work is evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions in a "Battleship"-like game, where players ask questions to resolve ambiguity about the game board. The model defines a probability distribution over a space of programs (questions) using a log-linear model. The features of this model include the Expected Information Gain (EIG) of the question, its complexity (derived from a probabilistic context-free grammar), and its answer type (e.g., Boolean, Number). By fitting this model to the human data, the authors show it can predict the frequency of human questions in held-out contexts and generate novel, contextually relevant questions.

This work connects to the field of active learning by moving beyond simple label queries to a much richer, compositional space of possible queries. While program induction has been explored at NIPS and in cognitive science (e.g., Lake et al., 2015), its application to *generating* questions as a form of active inquiry is a novel perspective. It also contrasts with recent deep learning approaches to question generation, which are typically data-hungry and less explicitly goal-directed.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Formulation:** The core idea of modeling goal-directed question asking as probabilistic program generation is elegant and compelling. It provides a formal framework for understanding the compositionality and creativity inherent in human inquiry, offering a fresh perspective for the active learning community.
2.  **Sound Methodology:** The paper presents a clear and technically sound probabilistic model. The use of a log-linear model with features for informativeness, complexity, and answer type is well-motivated. The experimental validation, including leave-one-out cross-validation and ablation studies that demonstrate the importance of both informativeness and complexity, is thorough and supports the central claims.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the model is explained in sufficient detail, and the results are presented effectively. The qualitative examples of newly generated questions are particularly insightful and demonstrate the generative power of the approach.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope and Scalability:** The primary weakness of this work lies in its evaluation domain. The "Battleship" game is a highly constrained, logically-defined toy problem. The entire system relies on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar that defines the space of possible questions. While the authors note that some components are domain-general (e.g., logical connectives, set operations), the core semantic primitives are tied to the game's rules (e.g., ship size, color, location). It is unclear how this approach would scale to more complex, ambiguous, or open-ended domains where such a clean formal semantics is not available. This reliance on significant domain engineering may limit the work's impact and interest for the general NIPS audience, which often values scalable, broadly applicable methods.
2.  **Novelty of Technical Components:** While the overall framing is novel, the core technical components of the model are fairly standard. The use of Expected Information Gain is a cornerstone of Bayesian active learning, and penalizing complexity is a classic application of Occam's razor, often implemented via grammar-based priors in program induction. The combination of these well-known ideas in a log-linear model, while effective for this task, may not be seen as a significant technical leap.
3.  **Audience Fit:** The paper feels closer in spirit to a computational cognitive science paper than a mainstream NIPS paper. The focus is on modeling human behavior in a specific task to understand the cognitive principles at play (balancing informativeness and complexity). While this is a valuable scientific contribution, its direct relevance and utility for machine learning practitioners and researchers might be limited due to the scalability concerns mentioned above.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel and thought-provoking perspective on active learning and query generation. The work is a complete, well-executed piece of research that cleanly demonstrates the value of modeling questions as compositional programs. It could inspire new research directions in creating more intelligent and inquisitive learning agents.
*   **Con:** The contribution is demonstrated in a toy domain with a hand-engineered grammar, raising serious doubts about its applicability to real-world machine learning problems. The core technical ideas are not fundamentally new, and the paper's focus on modeling human data may make it a better fit for a different venue. The significance for the broader NIPS community is therefore questionable.

In summary, this is a well-written and interesting paper presenting a clean, elegant idea. However, its significance and potential impact on the wider machine learning community are constrained by the simplicity of the task domain and the heavy reliance on domain-specific engineering.
