

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper proposes modeling human question asking as program generation, where questions are formal programs that, when executed on a world state, output an answer. The authors develop a probabilistic model that predicts human question frequencies in a Battleship-like game by combining features of question informativeness (measured via Expected Information Gain), complexity (program length), answer type, and relevance. The model successfully predicts human question frequencies in held-out contexts and generates novel, human-like questions not present in the training data.

The work builds on several important NIPS contributions. It extends program synthesis approaches like Lake et al. (2015) and Wang et al. (2015) to question generation, framing questions as executable programs rather than simple label requests. It also relates to active learning (Settles 2010) by modeling more complex information requests. However, the paper misses important connections to Hawkins et al. (2015), which develops a similar goal-directed question model, and Roberts (1996), which provides a theoretical framework for questions as part of discourse structure. These omissions weaken the paper's positioning within the broader literature.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a clear probabilistic model, well-defined features, and thorough evaluation methodology. The implementation is reproducible, with code available online. The ablation studies comparing model variants provide valuable insights into which features matter for human-like question generation. However, the evidence for the importance of informativeness (EIG) is surprisingly weak - the bootstrap analysis shows a 19% chance the log-likelihood advantage over the information-agnostic model is not real. The claim that people balance informativeness and complexity is not strongly supported by the results, as the information-agnostic model correlates with human question frequencies as well as the full model.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and provides clear examples of the program representation. The grammar for question generation is well-explained, and the evaluation methodology is transparent. The supplementary materials provide additional detail on the grammar and features.

**Originality:** The main contribution is demonstrating how to generate compositional questions in a non-trivial domain, which is novel in the context of active learning. The program-based representation of questions offers a fresh perspective that extends beyond previous work. However, the approach builds directly on existing program synthesis work without fully differentiating itself from these related approaches, and the missing citations to Hawkins 2015 and Roberts 1996 undermine the novelty claim.

**Significance:** The work has significant potential for both cognitive science (providing a computational model of question asking) and AI (enabling more human-like question asking in machines). The ability to generate novel, meaningful questions is a promising step toward more capable active learning systems. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the narrow domain (Battleship game) and the lack of evidence that the model's features capture how humans actually balance informativeness and complexity.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking, demonstrates its ability to predict human question frequencies, and successfully generates novel human-like questions. The approach is technically sound, well-executed, and has clear potential for applications in cognitive science and AI.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The evidence for the importance of the key feature (informativeness) is weak, and the paper's claims about human cognitive processes are not strongly supported by the results. The missing citations to important related work (Hawkins 2015, Roberts 1996) weaken the novelty claim and theoretical grounding.

Overall, this is a well-written, technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of active learning and cognitive modeling. While the evidence for the importance of informativeness is weaker than claimed, the ability to generate novel, human-like questions in a non-trivial domain represents a significant step forward. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the missing related work and providing clearer evidence for the claimed cognitive mechanisms.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the missing citations and clarify the evidence for the role of informativeness in human question asking. The paper's core contribution of modeling questions as programs that generate novel human-like questions is compelling and deserves publication at NIPS.
