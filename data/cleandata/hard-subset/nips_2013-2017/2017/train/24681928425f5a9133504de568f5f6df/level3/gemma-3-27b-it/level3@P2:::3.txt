This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking, framing questions as programs that, when executed, yield an answer. The authors demonstrate this approach in the context of a modified Battleship game, showing that their model can predict human question choices and generate novel, plausible questions. The model learns weights for features representing question informativeness (measured via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance to the game state.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in active learning, which traditionally focuses on simple label requests. It distinguishes itself by tackling the richer, more nuanced question-asking behavior observed in humans. The authors appropriately position their work within this landscape, referencing key papers in active learning (Settles, 2009) and dialog systems (Serban et al., 2017). However, the related work section could benefit from acknowledging more closely related efforts. Specifically, Hawkins et al. (2015) present a goal-based model of question asking, and Roberts (1996) introduces the Question Under Discussion (QUD) framework, a formal theory of pragmatics that offers a different perspective on information seeking through questions.

The technical soundness of the paper is high. The authors clearly define their grammar for representing questions as programs and provide a rigorous description of the probabilistic generative model. The optimization procedure, employing importance sampling to address the intractability of exact inference, is well-explained. Furthermore, the code and dataset are publicly available, greatly enhancing reproducibility. The writing is clear and well-organized, facilitating understanding of the complex concepts presented.

The strengths of this work lie in its innovative approach to modeling question asking and its successful application to a non-trivial domain. The ability to generate novel, compositional questions is a significant achievement, offering potential benefits for both AI (e.g., building more interactive agents) and cognitive modeling (e.g., understanding the cognitive processes underlying human inquiry). The analysis of feature weights, demonstrating the importance of both informativeness and complexity, provides valuable insights into the factors governing human question choices.

However, the paper's claims regarding the balance between informativeness and complexity are not fully supported by the experimental results. While the full model outperforms its lesioned counterparts in terms of log-likelihood, the correlation between model energy and human question frequency is only moderate, and the complexity-agnostic model performs comparably well in this regard. The authors acknowledge the limitations imposed by the sparsity of the human data, and the bootstrap analysis reveals some uncertainty in the significance of the EIG feature. This raises questions about the extent to which the model truly captures the nuanced interplay between these factors in human question asking.

Furthermore, the reliance on a pre-defined grammar, while necessary for computational tractability, introduces a potential bias. While the grammar is sufficient to represent the observed human questions, it may not be expressive enough to capture the full range of human creativity. Future work could explore more flexible, potentially grammar-free, approaches to question generation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically grounded approach to modeling question asking.
*   Successful demonstration of question generation in a challenging domain.
*   Technical soundness, clarity, and reproducibility.
*   Potential for significant impact in AI and cognitive science.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Claims regarding the balance between informativeness and complexity are not strongly supported by experimental results.
*   Related work section is incomplete, missing relevant contributions (Hawkins, 2015; Roberts, 1996).
*   Reliance on a pre-defined grammar may limit the model’s expressiveness.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite these minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The innovative approach, solid technical foundation, and promising results outweigh the limitations. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the missing related work and to temper the claims regarding the precise balance between informativeness and complexity. A brief discussion of the potential limitations imposed by the grammar would also be beneficial.

**Rating:** Accept (with minor revisions)
