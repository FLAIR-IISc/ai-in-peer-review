- Paper models human question-asking as program generation: questions are lambda-calculus-like programs evaluated on world states.  
- Introduces a PCFG-based probabilistic model combining expected information gain, program complexity, and answer type.  
- Evaluated on Battleship question dataset; model predicts human question frequencies (Spearman ρ = 0.64) and generates novel plausible questions.  
- Systematic evaluation with lesioned models and leave-one-out cross-validation; complexity found to be the dominant predictor.  
- Technical approach is sound and robust (full model outperforms complexity-agnostic model in 100% of bootstrap samples).  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and reproducible, with detailed supplementary materials.  
- Original contribution: integrates linguistic compositionality (lambda calculus, PCFG) with cognitive models and active learning.  
- Significant cognitive-science insight: explains why humans balance informativeness with complexity rather than maximizing informativeness alone.  
- Major weakness: computationally intensive (≈150k samples per optimization step) and demonstrated only on a small, low-dimensional Battleship domain.  
- ML/NIPS relevance is limited; scope and scalability to broader ML problems are not convincingly argued or demonstrated.  
- Minor suggestions: perform nested model comparisons for statistical significance and consider related "Questions under Discussion" literature.  
- Recommendation: accept for its cognitive-science contribution, but clarify scope and address scalability to strengthen ML relevance.
