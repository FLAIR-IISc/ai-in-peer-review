- Proposes modeling human question asking as program generation: questions are programs executed on a world state to produce answers.  
- Uses a log-linear model over four features: expected information gain (EIG), answer type, complexity (grammar probability), and relevance.  
- Evaluated on human questions from a Battleship-like game to infer ship configurations.  
- Builds on compositional semantics, program-based question answering, and probabilistic program induction literature.  
- Strength: clear theoretical framework and probabilistic model that predicts human question frequencies (Spearman 0.64).  
- Strength: model can generate novel, human-like questions and captures some productivity (15% novel human questions).  
- Strength: ablation of model variants shows importance of complexity and answer type features.  
- Limitation: task domain is highly constrained (3 ships on 6x6 grid) with logically unambiguous questions.  
- Limitation: artificial compositional language is a major simplification of natural language question asking.  
- Limitation: features (EIG, complexity, answer type) are straightforward and not novel relative to active learning work.  
- Critique: claim of modeling a complex compositional program space is misleading — the program space is simple and grammar-constrained.  
- Significance for NIPS questioned: limited applicability to natural language understanding, dialogue, or cross-domain generalization.  
- Originality low: approach resembles prior probabilistic programming and program synthesis work and isn’t well differentiated.  
- Quality: technically sound and clearly written, but claims are overstated and evaluation limited to a single constrained game.  
- Recommendation: reject for NIPS; more suitable for cognitive science or computational linguistics venues.
