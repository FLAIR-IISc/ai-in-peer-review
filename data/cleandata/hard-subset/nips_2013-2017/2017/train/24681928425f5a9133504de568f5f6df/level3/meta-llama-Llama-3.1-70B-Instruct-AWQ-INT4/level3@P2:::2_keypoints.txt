- Proposes modeling human question asking as program generation, treating questions as executable programs on world states.  
- Introduces a probabilistic model over a compositional program space that favors concise, context-useful programs.  
- Evaluated on a dataset of human questions from an information-search game.  
- Model predicts human question frequencies and synthesizes novel, human-like questions.  
- Integrates linguistic and cognitive theory in an innovative way.  
- Quantitative results show informativeness, complexity, and answer type all matter, with complexity dominant.  
- Qualitative results demonstrate generation of plausible novel questions.  
- Paper is well-written, clear, and well-organized with reproducible methodological detail.  
- Technically sound with well-supported claims and appropriate evaluation.  
- Significant contribution to cognitive science and implications for education and HCI.  
- Main limitation: scalability — computationally strained and tested only in a small, low-dimensional domain.  
- Lacks clear argument or demonstration of how the method generalizes to broader ML/NIPS-relevant problems.  
- Needs nested model comparisons on lesioned-model log-likelihoods to test statistical significance.  
- Suggests citing related formal semantics/pragmatics work on sequential question-asking (Questions under Discussion).  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions addressing scalability/generalization and additional statistical model comparisons.
