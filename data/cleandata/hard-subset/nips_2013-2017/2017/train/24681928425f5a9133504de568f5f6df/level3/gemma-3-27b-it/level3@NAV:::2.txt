## Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper presents a novel and compelling approach to modeling human question asking, framing it as program generation within a “Battleship” grid domain. The core idea – representing questions as executable programs defined by a hand-designed Probabilistic Context-Free Grammar (PCFG) and lambda-calculus – is both innovative and grounded in cognitive science principles of compositionality and computability. The authors effectively leverage information gain, complexity (measured by grammar probability), and answer type to predict human question behavior.

The work builds on the active learning literature (Settles, 2009) and surpasses it by addressing the limitations of simple label-requesting approaches, aiming for more human-like, richer queries. It’s also connected to work on dialog systems (Serban et al., 2017) but distinguishes itself by focusing on goal-directed question asking rather than canned responses. The authors appropriately position their work relative to recent advancements in visual question generation (Jain et al., 2017) highlighting the advantage of their approach in novel scenarios lacking extensive training data. 

The strength of this paper lies in its integration of linguistic theory with computational modeling. The quantitative evaluation using lesioned models and leave-one-out cross-validation, though limited in scale, convincingly demonstrates that all three modeled factors – informativeness, complexity, and answer type – contribute to predicting human question choice, with complexity surprisingly being the dominant predictor. The qualitative demonstration of novel, plausible question generation further strengthens the claims.

However, the paper’s scalability is a significant limitation. The reliance on a hand-designed grammar and the computational strain of the approach, demonstrated only in a small, low-dimensional domain, casts doubt on its applicability to more complex, real-world problems. While acknowledged by the authors, this remains a major concern for NIPS, where scaling to large datasets and complex scenarios is often expected. The authors should address this directly – either by demonstrating potential scaling strategies (e.g., grammar learning, approximation techniques) or by articulating specific scenarios where their approach offers unique advantages even with limited scalability. 

Minor points: Performing nested model comparisons on the lesioned model log-likelihoods would strengthen the statistical significance of the findings. Additionally, exploring related work on sequential question asking and "Questions under Discussion" (Groenendijk & Stokhof, 1997; Roberts, 1996) would enrich the discussion of related approaches. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:** 
* Innovative approach to modeling human question asking.
* Strong integration of cognitive science and computational modeling.
* Clear writing and appropriate evaluation.
* Demonstrates a nuanced understanding of human question-asking behavior.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
* Limited scalability and applicability to larger, more complex problems.
* Reliance on a hand-designed grammar.



Overall, this is a strong and thought-provoking paper. While the scalability concerns are real, the originality and insights provided justify consideration for acceptance, contingent on a stronger discussion of the method's potential for broader impact.
