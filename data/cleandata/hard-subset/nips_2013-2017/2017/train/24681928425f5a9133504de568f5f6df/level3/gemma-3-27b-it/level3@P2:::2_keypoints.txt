- Presents a novel framework modeling human question asking by treating questions as executable programs.  
- Evaluated on human questions from a modified Battleship game, showing prediction of human choices and generation of novel plausible questions.  
- Core contribution: integrates compositionality and computability from linguistics/cognitive science into probabilistic program generation.  
- Builds on active learning but addresses more complex, open-ended human questions rather than simple label queries.  
- Differentiates from dialog systems and visual question generation by focusing on goal-directed, context-sensitive questioning.  
- Uses a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) to enable compositional question generation.  
- Model balances informativeness, complexity, and answer type to predict human question behavior.  
- Demonstrates creativity by generating novel questions not seen in training data.  
- Quantitative evaluation provides evidence that informativeness alone is insufficient to explain human questioning.  
- Writing is clear and well-organized, making complex concepts accessible.  
- Main weakness: limited scalability—demonstrated only in a small 6x6 grid domain.  
- Reliance on a hand-designed grammar and domain-specific rules raises generalization concerns.  
- Computational cost of program generation/evaluation may not be manageable in more complex environments.  
- Statistical comparisons could be stronger; nested model comparisons or formal tests recommended.  
- Originality: novel combination of PCFGs, lambda calculus, and information gain for question-as-program synthesis.  
- Significance: advances understanding of self-directed learning and cognitive processes in question asking; could inspire active learning and dialog systems.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, insightful approach; strong integration of cognitive science; promising empirical results; excellent writing.  
- Rejection arguments: scalability and generalizability concerns; reliance on hand-crafted grammar; statistical validation needs strengthening.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to improve statistical validation and discuss scalability/possible solutions.
