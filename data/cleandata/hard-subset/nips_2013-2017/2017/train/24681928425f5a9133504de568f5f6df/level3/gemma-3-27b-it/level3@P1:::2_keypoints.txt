- Introduces a novel computational framework that models human question asking by treating questions as executable programs.  
- Represents questions with a probabilistic context-free grammar grounded in lambda calculus to enable compositionality and computability.  
- Evaluated on a human-generated “Battleship”-style dataset, showing the model predicts human question choices and can generate plausible novel questions.  
- Advances beyond label-requesting active learning by generating informative, structured queries rather than selecting from predefined sets.  
- Emphasizes cognitive plausibility and the ability to generalize with limited data compared to large-data deep-learning approaches.  
- Connects to formal semantic and concept-as-programs literature, aligning with theoretical work on questions and meaning.  
- Strengths: technically sound, well-motivated, clear writing, logical organization, and comprehensive (quantitative + qualitative) evaluation.  
- Key scientific insight: human question asking balances information gain with question complexity, not purely maximizing information.  
- Major weakness: scalability—reliance on a hand-designed grammar and expensive program execution limits applicability to higher-dimensional domains.  
- Implementation is tightly tied to the Battleship task, raising concerns about adaptation effort and generality.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel theory-driven approach, clear presentation, cognitive contribution about complexity, and successful novel question generation.  
- Rejection arguments: limited scalability, computational cost, and potentially narrow empirical impact.  
- Minor suggestions: run nested model comparisons for statistical rigor and cite Questions Under Discussion (QUD) literature to situate conversational context.  
- Overall verdict: strong, original contribution that merits acceptance despite valid scalability concerns.
