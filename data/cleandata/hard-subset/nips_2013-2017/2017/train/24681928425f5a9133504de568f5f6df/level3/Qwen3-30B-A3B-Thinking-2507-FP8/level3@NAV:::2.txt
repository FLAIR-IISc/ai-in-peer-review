

This paper introduces a novel computational model of human question-asking by formalizing questions as executable programs (using lambda calculus and a PCFG grammar) within a Battleship grid domain. The model predicts human questions by balancing three key factors: expected information gain (EIG), complexity (length/log-PCFG probability), and answer type. The authors demonstrate that complexity—not pure informativeness—is the dominant predictor of human question choices, with lesioned models confirming all factors contribute non-negligibly. The model also generates novel, plausible questions not present in training data, advancing cognitive science by explaining why informativeness alone fails to predict human behavior.  

**Strengths**: The integration of linguistic compositionality (via program synthesis) and cognitive theory is highly innovative, addressing a gap in active learning literature that typically focuses on simple label queries. The evaluation is rigorous: quantitative (leave-one-out cross-validation, log-likelihood comparisons) and qualitative (novel question generation). The writing is exceptionally clear, and the paper situates itself well within prior work (e.g., Lake et al. on program induction, Gureckis on self-directed learning).  

**Weaknesses**: The primary limitation is scalability—demonstrated only in a tiny, low-dimensional domain (6×6 grid), raising doubts about applicability to broader ML/NIPS-relevant problems. The authors do not sufficiently argue for scenarios where this method scales or yields novel insights beyond cognitive science. Minor issues include the absence of nested model comparisons for statistical significance of lesioned models and insufficient engagement with formal semantics work on sequential question-asking (e.g., Roberts’ "Questions under Discussion," cited in key points).  

**Overall**: This is a high-quality, technically sound contribution with strong cognitive-science significance. While the scalability concern is valid, the model’s theoretical insights into human question-asking (balancing complexity/informativeness) are compelling and well-supported. For NIPS, the work’s novelty and clarity outweigh the domain limitation, provided the authors address scalability in revisions. **Recommendation: Accept**.
