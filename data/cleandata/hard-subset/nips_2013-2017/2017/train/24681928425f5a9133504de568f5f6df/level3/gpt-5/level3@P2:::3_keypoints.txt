- Models question asking as program generation: questions are executable lambda/LISP-like programs scored by a log-linear model using EIG, complexity, answer type, and relevance.
- Uses a probabilistic grammar for a large compositional question space and fits parameters by likelihood with importance sampling (150k proposals per step).
- Evaluation on a Battleship-like task (605 human questions, 18 contexts; 2 contexts omitted for compute) with leave-one-context-out fits.
- Full model outperforms lesioned variants on held-out log-likelihood; complexity ablation shows a particularly strong effect.
- Correlation between model energy and human question frequencies is moderate (Spearman ~0.64); model also generates plausible novel questions.
- Connects to formal semantics (questions as partitions), QUD/pragmatics, compositional language-of-thought, probabilistic program induction, and program-based QA; novelty lies in generating questions as programs guided by EIG and complexity.
- Log-linear formulation, grammar prior, and Bayesian EIG are sound; importance sampling is appropriate but Z and expected-feature estimates may have high variance.
- Empirical support is mixed: full model beats lesions, but evidence that EIG uniquely explains human choices is weak and limited by data sparsity (many singletons).
- Dataset, grammar, and training details are released and sufficiently described for reproducibility.
- Paper is clearly written and well-organized; examples and task are helpful, but comparisons to QUD/RSA and Hawkins et al. could be more explicit.
- Originality: framing question generation as probabilistic program synthesis is novel relative to active learning, VQG, and prior program-based QA work.
- Significance: provides a predictive account for compositional question generation relevant to cognitive science and suggests paths for AI question-generation, but evaluation is domain-limited and grammar-engineered.
- Strengths: elegant executable-program representation, principled EIG+complexity objective, interpretable features, strong complexity ablation, novel question generation, clear exposition, and released data.
- Weaknesses: weak unique evidence for EIG, noisy/noisy LL estimates, domain-specific grammar and omitted contexts, lack of simpler or neural baselines, no human evaluation of outputs, no parameter regularization or uncertainty estimates.
- Suggested improvements: temper EIG claims, add per-context significance tests and CIs, use PSIS/WAIC, report partial correlations controlling for complexity, add PCFG/template/neural baselines, run human usefulness ratings, report sensitivity to proposal size and Z variance, add regularization and test portability to another domain.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, principled approach with clear cognitive and AI relevance, compositional modeling, meaningful ablations, and data release.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited evidence for EIG’s impact, single engineered domain with exclusions and limited baselines, and potential instability from approximate inference.
- Overall: creative and well-executed contribution that advances question generation as program synthesis, but acceptance contingent on tempering claims about informativeness and adding stronger analyses/baselines.
