This paper proposes a cognitive model of question asking, framing questions as programs and evaluating them based on informativeness, complexity, answer type, and relevance. The model is implemented in the Battleship domain and demonstrates an ability to predict human question choices and generate novel, plausible questions. While technically sound and well-written, the claims regarding revealing human balancing of informativeness and complexity are weakly supported, and the paper overlooks relevant prior work. Despite these limitations, the demonstration of compositional question generation in a non-trivial domain represents a valuable contribution to both AI and cognitive modeling.

The paper builds upon a growing body of work in active learning (Settles, 2009) and dialog systems (Serban et al., 2017), but distinguishes itself by moving beyond simple label requests or canned responses to explore the generation of richer, more creative questions.  Unlike visual question generation approaches (Jain et al., 2017; Mostafazadeh et al., 2016) which rely on large paired image-question datasets, this work tackles question asking in a novel scenario with limited prior examples. The framing of questions as programs aligns with earlier work formalizing question meaning as partitions over possible worlds (Groenendijk & Stokhof, 1997) and concepts as programs (Lake et al., 2015; Piantadosi et al., 2017), but uniquely focuses on *generating* these programs. A significant omission is related work by Hawkins (2015) which also proposes a goal-based question model, and Roberts (1996) presenting the QUD (Question Under Discussion) framework, a foundational work in understanding information seeking.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The technical approach is solid, leveraging a probabilistic grammar and importance sampling to handle the computational challenges.  The writing is clear and well-organized, making the complex ideas accessible. The code is reproducible, a major plus. The experimental results demonstrate that the full model outperforms ablated versions in terms of log-likelihood, indicating that the included features collectively contribute to predicting human behavior.  The ability to generate novel questions, exemplified in Figure 3, is particularly compelling and suggests the potential for practical applications.

However, the paper’s claims about uncovering how humans balance informativeness and complexity are not convincingly supported. While the full model performs best overall, the authors acknowledge that the model *without* EIG correlates with human judgments as well. This suggests complexity and other factors might be more influential than informativeness in human question selection, challenging the central hypothesis. The authors correctly point out the noise in the data due to sparsity, and the bootstrap analysis reveals a significant uncertainty (19% chance the result is wrong) regarding the role of EIG.  This weakens the primary conclusion.

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work. The omission of Hawkins (2015) is particularly glaring, given its similar focus on goal-directed question models. The QUD framework (Roberts, 1996) provides a valuable theoretical lens for understanding information seeking and question formulation, and its absence limits the contextualization of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of modeling questions as programs and generating them compositionally is a novel contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The probabilistic model and optimization techniques are well-described and justified.
*   **Reproducibility:**  Code availability promotes verification and future research.
*   **Potential Impact:** The ability to generate meaningful questions has implications for both AI (active learning, dialog systems) and cognitive science.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Weak Support for Key Claim:** Evidence for the role of informativeness (EIG) is uncertain and not strongly supported by the results.
*   **Missing Related Work:** Significant omissions in the related work section diminish the paper’s impact and contextualization.
*   **Limited Generalizability:** The model is currently specific to the Battleship domain, and the transferability to other tasks remains unclear.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak accept**. While the technical quality and originality are commendable, the weak support for the central claims and the omission of relevant work prevent a stronger recommendation. Addressing the related work gaps and tempering the claims about revealing human cognitive processes would significantly strengthen the paper.  The demonstrated capability for compositional question generation remains a valuable contribution, justifying acceptance despite these weaknesses.
