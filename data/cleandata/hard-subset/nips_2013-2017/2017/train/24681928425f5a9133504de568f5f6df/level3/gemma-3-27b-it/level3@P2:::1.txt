This paper introduces a computational framework for modeling human question asking, framing questions as programs that, when executed on a world state, yield an answer. The authors evaluate their approach by modeling questions asked by humans playing a simplified Battleship-like game, demonstrating the ability to predict human question choices and generate novel, human-like questions. The model utilizes a probabilistic log-linear framework incorporating features related to question informativeness (Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance to the game board.

The work builds on a rich history of research in active learning (Settles, 2008), where systems actively query for information to improve learning. While existing active learning primarily focuses on label requests, this paper aims to move towards richer, more human-like queries. It also connects to work in computational linguistics and cognitive science exploring the compositional structure of language (Fodor, 1975; Jacobson, 1996) and the relationship between language and thought (Piantadosi et al., 2016). Recent advancements in visual question generation (Mostafazadeh et al., 2016) are acknowledged, but the authors argue their approach uniquely focuses on goal-directed question asking in a novel scenario, unlike data-hungry deep learning methods. The connection to formalizing question meaning as world partitions (Groenendijk & Stokhof, 1997) and program induction (Lake et al., 2015) is also well-placed.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation, approach, and results. The use of a formal programming language to represent questions is a valuable step towards a more rigorous understanding of question asking.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The evaluation on the human question dataset provides strong evidence that the model can capture aspects of human question generation, both in terms of predicting observed questions and generating novel ones. The quantitative results (Table 1) and the qualitative examples (Figure 3) are compelling.
*   **Feature Analysis:** The ablation study, comparing the full model to variants with missing features, provides insight into the relative importance of informativeness, complexity, and answer type. The finding that complexity plays a crucial role is particularly interesting.
*   **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The grounding of the approach in concepts from information theory (Expected Information Gain) and formal linguistics (compositionality) provides a solid theoretical base.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope & Simplicity of Task:** The Battleship-like game, while serving as a useful testbed, is a highly constrained and logically unambiguous environment. The core ideas, while presented effectively, feel somewhat obvious within this setting. The task's simplicity limits the generalizability of the findings to more complex, real-world scenarios.
*   **Lack of Novelty in Core Ideas:** The central concept of representing questions as programs, and utilizing compositional semantics, is not fundamentally novel. Related work acknowledges these ideas in the context of question answering and cognitive modeling. The primary contribution seems to be the specific application and successful implementation within this particular game.
*   **Domain Knowledge & Grammar Engineering:** The grammar, while providing a formal representation of questions, requires significant domain knowledge and engineering effort to design. The reliance on a hand-crafted grammar limits the scalability and adaptability of the approach to new domains.
*   **Limited Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison to alternative models (ablation study) is useful, but a more thorough comparison to existing active learning techniques or alternative question generation approaches would strengthen the evaluation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a well-executed study that provides empirical support for the idea that human question asking can be modeled as program generation.
*   The feature analysis provides valuable insights into the cognitive factors that influence question generation.
*   The code and dataset are publicly available, facilitating reproducibility and future research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The simplicity of the task and the lack of fundamentally novel ideas limit the broader impact of the work.
*   The reliance on a hand-crafted grammar and the limited comparison to alternative methods raise concerns about the generalizability of the approach.
*   The paper’s focus may be too narrow for the broad NIPS audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper represents a solid contribution to the field of computational cognitive science and active learning. However, its limited scope and lack of substantial novelty raise concerns about its suitability for NIPS. While the technical execution is sound and the results are convincing within the chosen setting, the paper does not present a significant enough advance to warrant acceptance at this highly competitive conference. I recommend **reject** with the suggestion that the authors consider submitting to a more specialized venue in cognitive science or computational linguistics.
