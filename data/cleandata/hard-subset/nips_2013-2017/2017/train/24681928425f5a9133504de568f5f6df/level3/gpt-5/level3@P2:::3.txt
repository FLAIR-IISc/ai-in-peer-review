Summary and relation to prior work
This paper models question asking as program generation: questions are formal programs (lambda/LISP-like) that, when executed on a world state, return an answer. A probabilistic grammar defines a large, compositional hypothesis space of question-programs. A log-linear model assigns unnormalized energy to each program using features for informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (−log prior under the grammar), answer type (Boolean/Number/Color/Location), and a relevance filter. Parameters are fit by maximizing the likelihood of human questions via importance sampling over the grammar; inference approximates the partition function and expected feature values with 150k proposals per step.

Evaluation uses a Battleship-like information search task with 605 human-authored questions across 18 contexts (two contexts are omitted for computational reasons). In leave-one-context-out fits, the full model outperforms lesioned variants (without EIG, without complexity, without type features) on held-out log-likelihood. Correlations between model energy and human question frequencies are moderate on average (Spearman ~0.64). The model also samples plausible, novel questions not present in the training set.

The approach connects to traditions in formal semantics (questions as partitions; e.g., Groenendijk & Stokhof) and the QUD/pragmatics literature (e.g., Roberts, 1996), and to cognitive science on compositional “language of thought” models and probabilistic program induction. It also relates to work in program-based question answering/semantic parsing (e.g., Wang et al.; Johnson et al.) and prior cognitive models of question asking (e.g., Hawkins et al., 2015; RSA-inspired accounts). The main novelty is generating and selecting questions (not answers) as compositional programs guided by EIG and complexity.

Quality
- Soundness: The log-linear formulation, grammar-based complexity prior, and Bayesian EIG are standard and reasonable. Optimization via importance sampling is appropriate for this space, though high-variance estimates of Z and expected features are a concern.
- Empirical support: The full model has better held-out log-likelihood than lesions, especially vs removing complexity. However, evidence that EIG specifically explains human choices is mixed: the info-agnostic variant attains similar rank correlations to the full model, and bootstrap comparisons suggest the full model only beats the info-agnostic version in ~81% of samples. Data sparsity (many singleton questions) limits the strength of correlation-based conclusions.
- Reproducibility: The dataset is released; the grammar and training details are described clearly enough to reproduce.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and explicit about its assumptions, grammar, features, and optimization. The Battleship task and examples help. Some claims could be better calibrated to match the strength of the evidence (see below). A short, explicit comparison to QUD/RSA-style models and to Hawkins et al. would improve situating.

Originality
Treating question generation as probabilistic program synthesis and learning a distribution over a broad, typed, compositional question space is novel relative to most active learning and VQG/dialog work. Compared to prior program-based QA/semantic parsing, shifting focus to generating useful questions under uncertainty is original in both AI and cognitive modeling.

Significance
- For cognitive science: Provides a concrete, predictive account of open-ended question generation that captures compositionality and some balance between informativeness and cognitive cost.
- For AI: Demonstrates a path toward creative, goal-directed question generation beyond label queries or fixed templates; could inform active learning and dialog agents.
- Caveats: The current evaluation is confined to a single, engineered domain and relies on an engineered grammar; the magnitude of EIG’s contribution is uncertain, and the model does not directly handle natural language.

Strengths
- Elegant formulation of questions as executable programs with a compositional grammar.
- Principled objective combining information gain and complexity; clear, interpretable features.
- Competitive predictive performance on held-out human data; strong ablation showing the critical role of complexity.
- Ability to generate novel, human-like questions beyond the training set.
- Clear exposition, dataset availability, and reasonable optimization strategy.

Weaknesses and questions
- Evidence for EIG’s unique contribution is weak: similar correlations for full vs info-agnostic models; only 81% bootstrap advantage; claims about “how people balance informativeness and complexity” feel stronger than the data justify.
- Evaluation metrics are limited: correlations are noisy due to sparse counts; LL estimates depend on approximations to Z with potential high variance.
- Domain specificity: grammar includes Battleship-specific predicates; 14 questions and 2 contexts removed due to computational issues; unclear how much knowledge engineering transfers to new domains.
- Baselines: No comparison to simpler compositional reweighting baselines (e.g., grammar-only PCFG prior), template-based models, or neural baselines adapted to this constrained domain.
- No human evaluation of generated questions’ usefulness/fluency; qualitative examples are compelling but anecdotal.
- Regularization/uncertainty: No explicit regularization or uncertainty estimates on θ; results may be sensitive to sampling noise.

Suggestions for improvement
- Calibrate claims about EIG; add per-context significance tests; report confidence intervals for LL and correlations via bootstrap; consider PSIS/WAIC alternatives for model comparison.
- Partial correlation/ablation within contexts to disentangle EIG from answer type and complexity; report whether EIG predicts residual variance after controlling for complexity.
- Add baselines: (i) PCFG-only prior, (ii) nearest-neighbor/template reuse, (iii) neural sequence models constrained by answer type.
- Human study rating generated questions for usefulness and answerability; A/B tests holding complexity constant to test EIG’s effect on perceived quality.
- Report sensitivity to proposal distribution size; add L2 regularization on θ; provide variance of Z estimates.
- Strengthen discussion contrasting with QUD/RSA models and Hawkins et al. (beyond citations): what is gained/lost by a program-synthesis framing?
- Explore a second domain (even small-scale) to demonstrate portability and reduce dependence on Battleship-specific constructs; or show how much of the grammar is reused.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, principled approach to question generation with clear cognitive and AI relevance.
- Strong compositional modeling and meaningful ablations; complexity clearly important.
- Demonstrated ability to generate diverse, human-like questions.
- Clear writing and available data; likely to inspire follow-on work at NIPS.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited evidence that informativeness (EIG) materially improves predictions beyond complexity/type biases.
- Evaluation confined to one engineered domain with exclusions; limited baselines and no human evaluation of generated outputs.
- Approximate inference may induce instability in LL comparisons; claims could be more cautiously stated.

Overall
This is a creative, well-executed paper that meaningfully advances modeling of question asking as program generation. While I have reservations about the strength of the evidence for EIG’s role and the narrow evaluation domain, the core idea and framework are compelling. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on tempering claims about informativeness, adding stronger baselines/analyses, and clarifying connections to QUD/RSA and prior cognitive models.
