- Proposes modeling human question asking as executable programs with compositional semantics, evaluated in the Battleship game.  
- Uses a log-linear model with features for expected information gain, answer type (Boolean vs numerical), and board relevance to predict human question choices.  
- Motivated as extending active learning beyond simple label requests; reasonable framing.  
- Builds on prior active learning, computational linguistics, and cognitive science work; contrasts with end-to-end deep learning by focusing on goal-directed, executable questions.  
- Leverages a novel dataset of human questions collected from Battleship.  
- Strengths: clear problem statement and formalization of questions as programs.  
- Strengths: thorough exploration of feature space and quantitative results showing informativeness and complexity matter.  
- Strengths: qualitative ability to generate novel questions is promising.  
- Weaknesses: core ideas feel incremental and the task/domain are overly simplified and constrained.  
- Weaknesses: grammar is basic and the unambiguous game limits question complexity.  
- Weaknesses: reliance on manually engineered features and a log-linear model feels dated compared to end-to-end deep learning.  
- Conclusion: technically sound and clearly written but limited scalability, generalizability, and impact make it unlikely to interest the NIPS community.
