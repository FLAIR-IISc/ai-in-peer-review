- Proposes a cognitively motivated model treating questions as executable programs in a hand-crafted lambda-calculus/LISP-like language with a PCFG prior.
- Samples candidate program-questions and scores them by expected information gain (EIG) over a Bayesian hypothesis space plus complexity and answer-type preferences in a log-linear model.
- Fitted to 605 human questions (40 participants, 18 contexts) with hand-mapped natural language to programs; lesioned variants evaluated via leave-one-out cross-validation.
- Full model achieves best held-out log-likelihood; complexity (PCFG-based) is a particularly important predictor; model generates novel human-like questions.
- Technical approach is sound: compositional executable questions enable principled EIG computation and a natural complexity prior via the grammar.
- Learning setup (log-linear energy, importance sampling, ablations) and evaluation protocol are appropriate, though inference relies on large-scale sampling and approximate normalization.
- Main methodological concerns: manual NL→program mapping, computationally heavy inference, domain-specific hypothesis space, and two contexts dropped for computational reasons.
- Metric divergence: model log-likelihoods and human frequency rank correlations sometimes disagree (information-agnostic model has slightly higher average rank correlation); this needs discussion.
- Paper is clearly written with illustrative examples; would benefit from summarizing core grammar rules and answer-type signatures in the main text and reporting uncertainty on correlations.
- Original framing (questions as program generation) is novel and synthesizes cognitive science, formal semantics, and ML; contrasts with prior program-based answering work by focusing on question generation.
- Significance: notable for cognitive science and human-centered AI, but mixed for NeurIPS due to small, hand-engineered domain and lack of end-to-end NL interface.
- Strength: elegant integration of compositional semantics, executable programs, Bayesian EIG, and a learned log-linear model over a PCFG.
- Strength: empirically grounded with ablations showing informativeness, complexity, and answer-type all contribute to predicting human questions.
- Strength: demonstrates generative creativity—novel plausible questions align with human intuitions.
- Weakness: scalability and generalization limited by small Battleship domain, dropped contexts, and heavy computational cost.
- Weakness: heavy dependence on hand-designed grammar and manual NL-to-program mapping limits transferability to new domains.
- Weakness: evaluation inconsistencies (likelihood vs. rank correlation) and limited statistical testing of ablations beyond bootstrap Z comparisons.
- Weakness: utility model limited to entropy reduction; complexity penalty tied to grammar design and may conflate multiple factors.
- Suggestions: provide nested model comparisons, paired statistical tests, and confidence intervals for correlations.
- Suggestions: analyze sensitivity to grammar prior and alternative complexity measures (token length, AST depth, cognitive cost proxies).
- Suggestions: report computational costs and discuss scaling strategies (amortized inference, variational approximations, pruning).
- Suggestions: clarify discrepancy between correlation and likelihood, add per-context analyses, and connect to sequential multi-turn question frameworks.
- Suggestions: outline a concrete path to a learned NL interface (compositional semantic parsing) and demonstrate transfer to a second domain.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, well-motivated framework with solid empirical evidence that balancing informativeness and complexity predicts human behavior.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited scope, engineered representations, approximate evaluation with metric inconsistencies, and no end-to-end NL component.
