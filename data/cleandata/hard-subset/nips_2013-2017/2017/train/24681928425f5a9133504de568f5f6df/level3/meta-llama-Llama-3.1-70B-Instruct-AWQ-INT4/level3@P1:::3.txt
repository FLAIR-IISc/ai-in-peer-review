This paper presents a novel approach to modeling human question asking abilities in a computational framework. The authors propose a cognitive model that treats questions as formal programs that, when executed on the state of the world, output an answer. The model specifies a probability distribution over a complex, compositional space of programs, favoring concise programs that help the agent learn in the current context.

The authors evaluate their approach by modeling the types of open-ended questions generated by humans who were attempting to learn about an ambiguous situation in a game. The results show that the model predicts what questions people will ask and can creatively produce novel questions that were not present in the training set. The authors also compare a number of model variants, finding that both question informativeness and complexity are important for producing human-like questions.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and reproducible. The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their approach, including the probabilistic generative model, the grammar for producing questions, and the optimization procedure. The results are presented in a clear and concise manner, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of the implications of their findings.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its ability to demonstrate the generation of interesting compositional questions for a non-trivial domain. This makes the approach useful for AI and cognitive modeling, as it provides a new way to think about question asking as a cognitive tool. The paper also highlights the importance of considering both informativeness and complexity when modeling human question asking abilities.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The authors claim that their results reveal how people balance informativeness and complexity when producing semantically coherent questions, but this claim is not strongly supported by the experimental results.
