- Proposes modeling human question asking as programs generated from a compositional probabilistic grammar, evaluated by expected information gain and simple features.  
- Motivation—to improve active learning by modeling human-like question asking—is reasonable but not ambitious.  
- Builds on established active learning work and relates to dialog systems, visual question generation, and formal linguistics (language of thought).  
- Theoretical grounding is clear, leveraging compositionality and probabilistic grammars.  
- Strength: clear problem formulation and principled program-based approach to generate a space of questions.  
- Strength: incorporates features (expected information gain, complexity, answer type) and uses ablation to assess their contributions.  
- Empirical result: model predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, plausible questions in a single game domain.  
- Major weakness: experimental task (Battleship) is highly constrained with a small, unambiguous question space that limits generalizability.  
- Generated questions show limited creativity and novelty mostly confined to the task setting.  
- Core ideas (questions as formal programs; information gain for selection) are not novel and have decades of precedent.  
- Evaluation is limited: modest log-likelihood gains, moderate correlation with humans (0.64), and qualitative assessments are subjective.  
- Paper lacks stronger baselines, broader domains, and more rigorous or diverse evaluations.  
- Arguments for acceptance: principled approach, empirical fit to human data, and informative ablation analysis.  
- Arguments against acceptance: narrow task, limited novelty, constrained evaluation, and unlikely broad impact for a major ML venue.  
- Overall verdict: technically sound and well-written but probably better suited to cognitive modeling or computational linguistics than a top-tier ML conference.
