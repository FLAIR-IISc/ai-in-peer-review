This paper presents a novel computational model of human question asking, framing questions as programs generated from a hand-designed probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) and evaluated using a combination of information gain, complexity, and answer type. The model is evaluated on a dataset of human questions from a “Battleship”-like game, demonstrating its ability to both predict human question choices and generate plausible novel questions.

This work builds upon a growing body of research at NIPS and elsewhere exploring active learning and human-computer interaction. Unlike traditional active learning methods focused on simple label requests, this work tackles the challenge of richer, more human-like queries expressed in a compositional and interpretable form. It connects to work in dialog systems (e.g., Bordes et al., 2016; Serban et al., 2017) but surpasses their reliance on canned responses or large-scale data by focusing on goal-directed question generation from first principles. It also resonates with recent efforts in visual question generation (VQG) (e.g., Jain et al., 2016) but distinguishes itself by operating in a context requiring more nuanced reasoning and without the benefit of large paired image-question datasets. The framing of questions as programs aligns with broader efforts in cognitive science to understand concepts and reasoning through computational models (e.g., Lake et al., 2015; Piantadosi et al., 2016).

**Strengths:** The paper’s key strength lies in its innovative integration of linguistic and cognitive theory. The decision to represent questions as programs is a powerful one, allowing for a formal analysis of informativeness and complexity. The authors provide a clear and well-written exposition of their model, making it accessible to a broad audience. The quantitative evaluation, using lesioned models and leave-one-out cross-validation, is appropriate for the task. Furthermore, the qualitative demonstration of the model’s ability to generate novel, plausible questions is compelling. This work offers a significant cognitive-science contribution by advancing our understanding of self-directed learning and explaining why informativeness alone is insufficient to predict human question-asking behavior.

**Weaknesses:** The primary limitation of this work is its scalability. The approach is computationally strained and demonstrated only in a small, low-dimensional domain. This raises serious questions about its generalizability to broader, more complex real-world problems typically addressed in the NIPS community. The hand-designed grammar and the need for extensive search over the program space make it difficult to envision how this method could be applied to domains with larger state spaces or more complex question types. The reliance on a specific game domain, while allowing for controlled evaluation, limits the broader impact of the findings.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical basis for the model is clearly articulated, and the experimental results are well-presented and analyzed. The authors are careful to discuss the limitations of their work and acknowledge the challenges associated with scalability.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The notation is clear, and the explanations are concise and easy to follow. The supplementary material provides helpful details about the grammar and experimental setup.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The combination of program synthesis, probabilistic modeling, and cognitive science principles is novel. While related work exists in areas such as active learning and dialog systems, this paper presents a unique approach to modeling human question asking.

**Significance:** The results are important for understanding the cognitive mechanisms underlying question asking. The finding that complexity plays a dominant role in predicting human questions is particularly noteworthy. While the current implementation is limited in its scope, the underlying principles could potentially be applied to other domains.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically grounded approach to modeling human question asking.
*   Clear writing and appropriate evaluation methodology.
*   Significant cognitive-science contribution.
*   Demonstrated ability to generate plausible novel questions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scalability and generalizability.
*   Reliance on a hand-designed grammar and a specific game domain.
*   Computational cost.



**Minor Comments:**

1.  While the lesioned model comparisons are informative, a more rigorous statistical test of significance would be beneficial. Performing nested model comparisons on the lesioned-model log-likelihoods (e.g., using a likelihood ratio test) would provide a more robust assessment of the contribution of each feature.
2.  The authors should consider related work in formal semantics and pragmatics on sequential question-asking, specifically the “Questions under Discussion” (QUD) framework (e.g., Groenendijk & Stokhof, 1997; Roberts, 1996). This work provides a formal account of how questions build upon each other in a discourse context, and could potentially inform future extensions of the model.

**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the understanding of human question asking. While the scalability limitations are a concern, the novelty, clarity, and theoretical grounding of the work make it a worthwhile contribution to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the comments above.
