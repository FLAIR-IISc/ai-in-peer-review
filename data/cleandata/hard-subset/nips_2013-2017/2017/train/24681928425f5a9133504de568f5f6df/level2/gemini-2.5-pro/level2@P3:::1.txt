This paper presents a novel computational framework for modeling goal-directed, human-like question asking. The core contribution is to formalize questions as programs that are executed on a world state to produce an answer. This recasts the problem of question generation as one of program synthesis. The work proposes a probabilistic generative model over a compositional space of programs defined by a grammar. This model favors programs (questions) that are both informative, as measured by Expected Information Gain (EIG), and concise, penalizing complexity. The model is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like game. The results demonstrate that the proposed approach can predict the frequency of human questions in unseen contexts and can also generate novel, syntactically and semantically coherent questions that were not observed in the training data.

The paper is well-situated within the fields of active learning and cognitive science. It significantly extends typical active learning paradigms, which are often limited to simple label queries, by introducing a framework for generating rich, structured queries. This connects to long-standing goals in AI for creating more flexible and human-like learning agents. The approach also builds on influential ideas in cognitive science, such as the "language of thought" and probabilistic models of concept learning, applying them to the novel problem of question generation.

**Quality and Clarity**

The paper is technically sound and clearly written. The motivation for treating questions as programs is well-argued, highlighting the principles of compositionality and computability. The description of the grammar, the probabilistic log-linear model, and the feature set (informativeness, complexity, answer type) is detailed and easy to follow. The experimental methodology is appropriate, using leave-one-out cross-validation and performing ablation studies to demonstrate the importance of different model components. The inclusion of qualitative examples of novel generated questions provides compelling evidence for the model's generative capabilities. The work is a complete piece of research, presenting a clear hypothesis, a formal model, and a thorough empirical evaluation.

**Originality**

The central idea of modeling question *generation* as program synthesis is highly original. While program synthesis has been used successfully in question *answering* to parse natural language queries into executable forms, its application to generating questions from scratch in a goal-directed manner is a novel contribution. The work successfully synthesizes concepts from formal semantics, Bayesian inference, and program induction into a coherent model of a complex cognitive ability. It clearly distinguishes itself from contemporary deep learning approaches to question generation, which are typically less data-efficient, less interpretable, and less adept at generating context-sensitive, goal-directed queries.

**Significance**

The work is of high significance to both the machine learning and computational cognitive science communities.
For machine learning, it presents a concrete step toward more sophisticated active learning agents that can probe their environment with complex, structured questions. This could have a substantial impact on areas where intelligent information gathering is critical, such as robotics, scientific discovery, and interactive AI systems.
For cognitive science, it provides the first predictive computational model of open-ended human question asking, offering a compelling account of how people balance the trade-off between informativeness and complexity when formulating inquiries. The finding that complexity is a critical constraint, and that EIG alone is insufficient to explain human behavior, is an important insight.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

While the contributions are strong, the paper could be improved by addressing the following points:

1.  **Generalization and Domain Specificity:** The grammar is hand-engineered for the Battleship domain. The paper acknowledges this limitation but could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the path toward generalization. While some components are domain-general (e.g., logical operators, set theory), many core functions are specific to the game's objects and properties. A discussion on how this framework might be extended to new domains, and whether parts of the grammar could be learned from data rather than specified a priori, would strengthen the paper's outlook.

2.  **Interface with Natural Language:** The model operates on the semantic level of programs, abstracting away from the surface form of natural language. This is a reasonable simplification, but the connection back to language is a critical challenge for building a complete question-asking system. Including a brief discussion of the potential challenges and approaches for both parsing natural language questions into this programmatic form and for generating natural language text from a synthesized program would make the work more complete.

3.  **Computational Complexity:** The model relies on importance sampling to approximate the intractable normalization constant and expectations, and the paper notes that two game contexts were excluded for computational reasons. This suggests that the approach may face scalability challenges as the hypothesis space or the complexity of the grammar grows. A brief analysis or discussion of the computational complexity and potential avenues for making the inference more efficient would be a valuable addition.

4.  **Evaluation of Novel Questions:** The qualitative examples of novel generated questions are impressive. To further substantiate the claim that these questions are "human-like," a small-scale human evaluation could be beneficial. For example, human participants could be asked to rate the quality, relevance, or naturalness of the model-generated questions compared to human-generated ones. This is a non-essential suggestion but would provide stronger evidence for the model's creative capacity.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Pros:**
*   Highly original and significant framing of question asking as probabilistic program generation.
*   Technically sound model that effectively combines a formal grammar with Bayesian inference.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating predictive accuracy and generative capabilities on a challenging dataset.
*   Provides important insights into the cognitive constraints (informativeness vs. complexity) that shape human inquiry.

**Cons:**
*   The hand-crafted, domain-specific nature of the grammar raises questions about scalability and generalization.
*   The model abstracts away from the natural language interface, which is a key challenge for real-world application.
*   Potential computational bottlenecks may limit applicability to more complex domains.

In summary, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel and powerful framework for understanding and generating complex, goal-directed questions. It makes significant contributions to both machine learning and cognitive science. The limitations, primarily concerning generalization, are openly acknowledged and represent exciting directions for future research.
