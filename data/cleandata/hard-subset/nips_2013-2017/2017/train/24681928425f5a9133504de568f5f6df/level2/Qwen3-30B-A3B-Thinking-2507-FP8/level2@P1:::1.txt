

This paper presents a novel computational framework modeling human question asking as program generation, where questions are formal programs that execute on a world state to produce answers. The authors develop a probabilistic model that combines compositional grammar (with lambda expressions and set operators) and features capturing question informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (via grammar probability), answer type, and relevance. Evaluated on a dataset of 605 human questions from a Battleship-style game, the model successfully predicts human question frequencies (Spearman correlation 0.64) and generates novel, contextually relevant questions not present in training. The model outperforms variants that omit key features, demonstrating the importance of balancing informativeness and complexity in human-like question generation.

Strengths: The paper makes a significant contribution by formalizing question asking through program synthesis—a departure from prior work on canned dialog systems or neural question generation that requires massive datasets. The compositional grammar elegantly captures the structure of human questions (e.g., "Are all ships the same size?" as `(= (map (λ x (size x)) (set Blue Red Purple)))`), and the probabilistic model’s feature engineering is well-motivated. The evaluation is thorough: cross-validation shows the full model outperforms lesioned variants (e.g., complexity-agnostic model correlates -0.36 vs. full model’s 0.64), and the generated questions (e.g., "What is the column of the top left of the tiles that have the color of the bottom right corner of the board?") demonstrate genuine creativity. The work bridges cognitive science (explaining how humans balance informativeness and complexity) and AI (advancing active learning beyond label queries).

Weaknesses: The grammar is tightly coupled to the Battleship domain, raising questions about scalability. While the authors note this limitation, they could better address how to adapt the framework to new domains (e.g., would the same functions suffice for a medical diagnosis task?). The paper also lacks comparison to recent neural program synthesis approaches (e.g., Lake et al.’s *Human-level concept learning*), though their focus on human-like *question generation* rather than program induction justifies the choice. The model’s reliance on a uniform prior over ship configurations (Section 4.5) could be more explicitly justified.

Originality: The core idea of modeling questions as executable programs is fresh, distinguishing it from both traditional active learning (which queries labels) and neural question generation (which requires paired data). It builds on but extends prior work in compositional semantics (Fodor, Jacobson) and program induction (Lake et al.), making a clear contribution to NIPS’ interest in cognitive modeling and program synthesis.

Significance: This work provides the first predictive model of open-ended human question asking, with potential to improve AI systems’ ability to learn through dialogue. The framework could inspire more human-like interactive learning in robotics or education. While domain-specific now, the compositional approach suggests a path toward generalizable question-asking agents.

Pro: Strong theoretical grounding, rigorous evaluation, novel framework, and compelling novel question generation.  
Con: Domain specificity requires clearer path to generalization; limited discussion of neural alternatives.

Overall, this is a high-quality, original contribution that advances both cognitive science and AI. The model’s ability to generate novel, contextually relevant questions—without relying on large datasets—makes it particularly significant for active learning. The authors effectively address the limitations (e.g., grammar domain specificity) and provide a clear roadmap for future work. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and aligns with NIPS’ focus on foundational AI research. I recommend acceptance.
