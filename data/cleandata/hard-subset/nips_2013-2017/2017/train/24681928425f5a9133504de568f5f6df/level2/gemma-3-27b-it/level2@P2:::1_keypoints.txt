- Novel "question as a program" framework offering a theoretically grounded, compositional approach to modeling question asking.
- Strong empirical results: model outperforms ablations and shows correlation between predicted energies and human question frequencies.
- Generative capability: can produce novel, plausible questions not seen in training.
- Clear writing and motivation, with links to cognitive science themes like compositionality and language of thought.
- Well-defined experimental setup using an existing game dataset and appropriate leave-one-out cross-validation.
- Concern about grammar complexity and unclear scalability to more complex domains (100-function limit noted).
- Domain specificity: current grammar tailored to Battleship; unclear how much knowledge engineering other domains would require.
- High computational cost due to importance sampling for gradient approximation; needs more discussion and possible optimizations.
- EIG calculation depends on a Bayesian ideal observer; results may be sensitive to prior choices and robustness should be evaluated.
- Limited comparison to state-of-the-art neural question generation (e.g., Transformers); deeper engagement would strengthen the paper.
- Qualitatively: technically sound, clearly presented, highly original, and potentially significant if generalized.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically solid approach with compelling experimental support and high potential impact.
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability, computational cost, and limited generalizability across domains.
- Suggested improvements: detail computational costs and optimizations, discuss scaling the grammar, compare to neural QG models, analyze EIG prior sensitivity, and include grammar-component ablations.
- Overall recommendation: accept pending revisions addressing scalability, computational cost, and comparative evaluations.
