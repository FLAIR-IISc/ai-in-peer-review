- Novel framework modeling human questions as executable programs generated by a probabilistic compositional grammar.  
- Probabilistic model combines grammar-derived complexity, Expected Information Gain (informativeness), answer type, and relevance features.  
- Grammar includes lambda expressions and set operators to capture compositional structure of human questions.  
- Evaluated on 605 human questions from a Battleship-style game; model predicts question frequencies with Spearman ρ = 0.64.  
- Generates novel, contextually relevant questions not seen in training, demonstrating creativity beyond template methods.  
- Outperforms lesioned variants, showing importance of balancing informativeness and complexity (complexity-agnostic model correlates −0.36).  
- Strength: formalizes question asking via program synthesis, avoiding massive paired-data requirements of neural QG.  
- Strength: well-motivated feature engineering and thorough cross-validation evaluation.  
- Strength: bridges cognitive science and AI by explaining human trade-offs between informativeness and complexity.  
- Weakness: grammar is tightly coupled to the Battleship domain, raising scalability and generalization concerns.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of adapting the grammar/functions to other domains (e.g., medical diagnosis).  
- Weakness: lacks comparison to recent neural program synthesis approaches despite partial justification.  
- Weakness: reliance on a uniform prior over ship configurations needs clearer justification.  
- Originality: fresh idea framing questions as executable programs, extending compositional semantics and program induction.  
- Significance: first predictive model of open-ended human question asking with potential application to interactive learning systems.  
- Overall assessment: high-quality, original contribution with clear roadmap for future generalization; recommendation to accept.
