## Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper presents a novel framework for modeling human question asking, framing questions as formal programs that, when executed, yield answers. The authors argue this approach captures the compositional nature of questions and allows for the generation of human-like queries in an information-seeking game (Battleship). They introduce a probabilistic model, evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions, demonstrating its ability to predict and even creatively produce novel questions.

The core strength of this work lies in its insightful conceptualization of questions as programs. This is a compelling idea, moving beyond simple label requests prevalent in active learning and towards richer, more human-aligned inquiry. The experimental validation is also strong. The model demonstrably predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, plausible questions—a significant achievement. The ablation study, comparing the full model to variants lacking specific features (informativeness, complexity, answer type), effectively highlights the importance of all components, with complexity being particularly crucial.

However, the paper suffers from some limitations. While the grammar is presented, it’s somewhat difficult to grasp its full expressiveness without a deep dive into the supplementary material. A more intuitive explanation of the grammar and its relation to natural language would be beneficial. Additionally, the evaluation relies heavily on a single, specific game. While well-defined, it raises questions about the generalizability of the findings to other domains. Further, the comparison to existing dialog systems feels somewhat limited, focusing primarily on systems with restricted question sets. A more nuanced discussion of how this work relates to recent advances in large language models capable of open-ended question generation would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and compelling framework for modeling question asking.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating predictive power and generative capability.
*   Insightful ablation study validating the importance of multiple features.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited domain of evaluation (Battleship game).
*   Grammar explanation could be more accessible.
*   Discussion of related work could be more comprehensive, particularly regarding LLMs.

**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a novel contribution. While the domain specificity and presentation of the grammar are areas for improvement, the core idea and experimental validation are impressive. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity of the grammar and broaden the discussion of related work.
