- Presents a novel framework treating questions as executable programs, a compelling idea for analysis and generation.  
- Evaluates the model on a game-based question dataset and shows it can predict human questions and generate novel, human-like queries.  
- Paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and situates itself effectively within active learning and dialog literature.  
- Related work coverage is comprehensive and the task/domain choice is clearly justified.  
- Formal grammar for questions enables systematic exploration of question space and is a theoretical strength.  
- Probabilistic model is well-defined and learns from data, despite being complex.  
- Experimental results are promising, demonstrating predictive and generative success.  
- Ablation studies convincingly show the importance of informativeness and complexity features.  
- Model complexity and detailed implementation may hinder reproducibility.  
- Grammar appears domain-specific; discussion of generalization to other contexts is limited.  
- Dependence on a specific Bayesian ideal observer implementation for informativeness is a potential limitation.  
- Feature set could be expanded (e.g., question similarity, pragmatic factors) to improve performance.  
- Description of cross-validation is misleading—training used 15 contexts, not strict leave-one-out.  
- Qualitative evaluation of generated questions is limited; more systematic/user studies are needed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, well-written, promising results, thorough ablations, tackles an important problem.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reproducibility concerns, limited generalizability, reliance on specific informativeness implementation, limited qualitative evaluation.  
- Overall: strong paper recommended for acceptance, contingent on addressing reproducibility, generalizability, and expanding qualitative evaluation (e.g., release code, more discussion, user study).
