- Model: represents questions as executable programs in a typed LISP-like grammar with a log-linear distribution over program space.  
- Features: uses informativeness (EIG under a Bayesian observer), complexity (−log q(x) under a PCFG), answer type, and relevance.  
- Learning/inference: matches human question frequencies via feature expectation matching using importance sampling (150k samples).  
- Dataset/performance: trained on 605 natural-language questions from Battleship across 16 contexts; predicts held-out human distributions with avg Spearman ≈ 0.64.  
- Generativity: produces novel, context-sensitive questions not in training, defining equivalence classes by answer distributions over hypotheses.  
- Ablation result: both informativeness and complexity are necessary to reproduce human behavior; complexity ablation degrades performance substantially.  
- Novelty/equivalence: uses a principled mutual-information-based equivalence criterion, though thresholds and counts per context are not reported.  
- Originality: high — novel combination of program synthesis, Bayesian utility, and learned costs for modeling question asking.  
- Theoretical relevance: connects active learning, program induction/semantic parsing, and pragmatic/formal models of questions.  
- Practical relevance: suggests a path to more human-like, goal-directed question generation beyond surface-form VQG.  
- Evaluation limitations: modest dataset size and dropped data (2 contexts, 14 questions) raise questions about robustness and sensitivity to exclusions.  
- Inference concerns: reliance on importance sampling for normalizers and expectations lacks diagnostics (ESS, variance, convergence) and sensitivity analyses.  
- Complexity prior issue: complexity modeled via hand-designed PCFG (−log q(x)), conflating grammar design with cognitive cost; alternatives (MDL, learned costs, RT proxies) are not explored.  
- Utility assumptions: EIG assumes a single goal (identify exact board) and a literal answerer; people may pursue subgoals, check constraints, or consider answerer costs — pragmatic models (e.g., RSA) are missing.  
- Generalization concern: many grammar symbols are Battleship-specific and porting to new domains may require substantial engineering; no cross-domain experiment shown.  
- Natural language interface gap: mapping between NL and program semantics (and back) is left for future work, limiting immediate dialog-system applicability.  
- Human evaluation missing: novelty and quality of generated questions are shown qualitatively but lack human ratings of usefulness/naturalness.  
- Methodological suggestions: add baselines (EIG-only, PCFG-only, retrieval, bag-of-constructs), report sampling diagnostics, sensitivity to sample size, and runtime.  
- Suggested extensions: explore learned/alternative cost functions, pragmatic answerer models, human evaluations, closed-loop gameplay tests, and a small second-domain instantiation.  
- Acceptance arguments: elegant, compositional, principled model with empirical evidence that combining informativeness and complexity predicts human choices and generates creative questions.  
- Rejection arguments: limited-scale/domain-specific evaluation, dependence on hand-crafted grammar and complexity prior, and lack of human/NL evaluation reduce immediate impact.
