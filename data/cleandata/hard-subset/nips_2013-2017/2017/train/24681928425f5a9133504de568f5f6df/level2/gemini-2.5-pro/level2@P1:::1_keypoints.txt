- Introduces a novel framework that models human question asking by representing questions as compositional programs and treating asking as probabilistic program generation.  
- Defines a log-linear model over program-questions using features: expected information gain (EIG), program complexity, and answer type.  
- Evaluated on a Battleship-like dataset, trained to predict human question distributions in game contexts.  
- Full model (informativeness + complexity + answer type) best fits human data and outperforms ablated variants.  
- Demonstrates generative capability: model can produce novel, contextually appropriate questions not seen in training.  
- Strong originality: moves beyond label-query active learning toward rich, compositional query generation, bridging ML and cognitive science.  
- Technical quality: well-defined model, well-motivated features, and effective ablation showing complexity is crucial.  
- Clarity: paper is exceptionally well-written with clear motivation and concrete program–question examples.  
- Limitation: relies on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar; would benefit from explicit decomposition of domain-general vs domain-specific rules.  
- Limitation: abstracts away natural language (semantic-to-text / text-to-program gap); needs discussion of interfacing with modern NLP methods.  
- Limitation: uses importance sampling with a uniform PCFG proposal which may be high-variance post-training; consider stability, adaptive importance sampling, or variational approaches.  
- Limitation: average correlation (0.64) is good but performance varies by context; paper should analyze when and why performance degrades.  
- Conclusion: excellent, significant contribution and strong foundation for future work despite grammar-specific limitations.
