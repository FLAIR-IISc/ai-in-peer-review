- Proposes novel framework treating questions as executable programs that run on a world state to yield answers.  
- Core idea is compelling and shifts focus from simple label requests to goal-directed, executable queries.  
- Evaluated on a game dataset, showing the model can predict human question choices and generate novel, human-like questions.  
- Builds on active learning and dialog systems literature, extending them to richer, compositional queries.  
- Connects to visual question generation but claims to capture human-like intelligence without massive training data.  
- Formalization as programs enables compositional representation and reasoning about informativeness and complexity.  
- Uses a principled probabilistic model to learn question features and predict human behavior.  
- Experimental results are promising, with both quantitative prediction and qualitative generated-question examples.  
- Ablation study highlights the importance of both informativeness and complexity for human-like question generation.  
- Related work section is thorough and the paper is clearly written.  
- Grammar description is in the supplement and should be integrated into the main text with more examples.  
- The method for determining the “relevance” feature needs a more detailed explanation.  
- Operates on semantic program representations, leaving unclear how readily it extends to natural language.  
- Reliance on a specific game context raises concerns about generalizability to other tasks or datasets.  
- Ablation log-likelihood differences are relatively small; more rigorous statistical analysis is needed.  
- Bootstrapping comparisons are appropriate but should be elaborated further.  
- Computational complexity and potential scalability issues are noted and warrant discussion.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations and propose future work; overall strengths justify a recommendation for acceptance.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel framework, strong experiments and generative ability, thorough related work, clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited generalizability, complexity/scalability concerns, limited statistical significance, and incomplete link to natural language.
