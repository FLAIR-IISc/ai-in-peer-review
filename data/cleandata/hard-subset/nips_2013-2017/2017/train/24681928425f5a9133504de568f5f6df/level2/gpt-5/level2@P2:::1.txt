Summary
This paper models question asking as program generation. Questions are formalized as executable programs in a simple, typed, lambda-calculus/LISP-like language; when run on a hypothesized world state, a program returns an answer (Boolean/Number/Color/Location). A probabilistic context-free grammar defines the compositional hypothesis space of programs. A log-linear model over features selects among programs, balancing context-sensitive informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (−log probability under the PCFG), answer type priors, and a relevance filter. Parameters are fit via approximate maximum likelihood with importance sampling. The model is evaluated on a Battleship-style information search dataset comprising 605 natural-language questions across 18 contexts (16 used in modeling) and tested via leave-one-context-out. Results show improved held-out log-likelihood over ablations that remove informativeness, complexity, or type features, reasonable rank correlations between predicted energies and human frequencies, and the ability to sample novel, plausible questions.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects formal semantics (questions as partitions over worlds), program induction in cognitive science, and active learning (EIG), and contrasts with dialog systems and neural question generation approaches that lack goal-directed informativity and compositionality. Prior QA-as-program work and pragmatic models of question asking are appropriately cited. The contribution is to generate (not merely evaluate/select) questions via a compositional grammar and to show that a cost–benefit trade-off (informativeness vs complexity) predicts human choices.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formalization: representing questions as programs affords compositionality, executability, and explicit answer types.
- Cogent cognitive hypothesis: people trade off informativeness and complexity; ablations substantiate the necessity of both factors.
- Generativity: the grammar covers all observed questions and produces novel ones judged qualitatively sensible for held-out contexts.
- Interpretable model with explicit features, approximate but transparent learning and inference.
- Relevant to NeurIPS communities in active learning, program induction, and cognitively-inspired AI.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Domain specificity and potential circularity: the grammar is engineered to express all questions in this Battleship domain and was designed with access to the full dataset; generalization to new domains is untested. The utility of the approach for broader question asking hinges on portability of primitives.
- Evaluation scope: the dataset is small (605 questions) and two most complex contexts were dropped; 14 questions relying on a probabilistic “draw” operator were removed, potentially biasing the sample. The impact of these omissions is not quantified.
- Approximation and stability: log-likelihood and gradients rely on importance sampling with a uniform PCFG proposal and 150k samples; variances/SEs of Z and gradient estimates are not reported. It is hard to assess robustness of model comparisons.
- Complexity feature: using −log q(x) under the (uniform-choice) PCFG conflates “human comprehensibility” with a modeling artifact (the grammar’s rule branching structure). A simpler length/depth penalty or token count might be more interpretable; comparisons are not reported.
- Metrics: correlations between model energies and human frequencies are plausible, but the information-agnostic variant reportedly attains a slightly higher mean Spearman correlation (0.65) than the full model (0.64), which warrants clarification. If LL prefers the full model but correlation does not, the paper should reconcile these metrics.
- Mapping between natural language and programs: the study relies on translating human questions into programs. Procedures, inter-annotator agreement, and ambiguity resolution are not reported, yet affect ground-truth frequencies and equivalence classes.
- Human evaluation of generated questions: qualitative examples are compelling, but there is no blinded human rating of usefulness/naturalness, nor a comparison to human questions in the same context (e.g., Turing-style indistinguishability).

Quality
The technical formulation is sound and well-motivated. The learning objective and inference are standard but adapted thoughtfully to the compositional, infinite-like program space. Major claims (importance of both EIG and complexity; capacity to generate novel, context-sensitive questions) are supported, though the empirical basis is limited by dataset size, domain specificity, and approximations. Additional diagnostics and baselines would strengthen the conclusions.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and generally well-organized. Some details (full grammar, proposal distribution, EIG computation for large answer spaces, handling of vectors/sets, and the NL-to-program mapping) appear in the supplement or are only briefly described; summarizing key points in the main text would aid reproducibility. The statement that “two features for informativeness, one for length” later described as “complexity = −log q” could be made consistent.

Originality
Framing question asking as program synthesis and learning a distribution over questions conditioned on context is original relative to prior active learning and dialog work. While related to program-based QA and cognitive models of concept learning, applying this framework to question generation with empirical validation is a novel and interesting contribution.

Significance
Conceptually significant for both cognitive science and AI: it advances a compositional, goal-directed account of question asking. Practical impact for large-scale dialog systems is limited in the current domain-specific form, but the approach could inspire richer query policies in active learning and interactive agents.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report variance/SEs for Z estimates, gradient Monte Carlo noise, and LLs; add CIs for correlations; clarify why the info-agnostic model slightly out-correlates the full model.
- Analyze the effect of dropping contexts 1–2 and “draw” questions; re-run analyses including them if feasible, or quantify the bias.
- Compare complexity penalties: program length, node count, maximum depth, and −log q; report fitted weights and predictive differences.
- Provide details and reliability for NL-to-program annotation; release annotations and code (dataset link is provided).
- Include an EIG-only baseline and a length-only baseline explicitly; add a retrieval baseline that reuses questions from training contexts via semantic templates.
- Add human evaluation of generated questions (usefulness, naturalness) and a forced-choice comparison against human questions in the same context.
- Demonstrate portability by applying the same grammar core to a second domain with minimal additions, or articulate a path to learning domain primitives.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, principled formulation; interpretable and generative.
- Empirical evidence that informativeness and complexity both matter.
- Bridges cognitive theory and ML; likely to stimulate follow-up work.

Cons for acceptance
- Narrow domain, engineered grammar; limited external validity.
- Heavy reliance on approximations without thorough uncertainty analysis.
- Limited human evaluation of generation quality.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. Confidence: Medium.
