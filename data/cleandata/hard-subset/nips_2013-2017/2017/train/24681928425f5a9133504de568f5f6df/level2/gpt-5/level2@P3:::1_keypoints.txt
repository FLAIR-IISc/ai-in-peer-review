- Proposes treating questions as executable programs from a typed, lambda-calculus–inspired grammar with a log-linear model trading off expected information gain (EIG), complexity (PCFG prior), and answer type plus relevance filtering.
- Computes informativeness via a Bayesian ideal observer in a Battleship-like game and learns parameters by maximizing human question likelihood using importance sampling.
- Evaluation on 605 human questions (18 contexts, 2 dropped) shows the full model outperforms ablations, can generate novel human-like questions, and that complexity is critical.
- Formalization is coherent: clear grammar, features, learning objective, and principled EIG and complexity terms; approximations via importance sampling are reasonable.
- Empirical protocol (leave-one-context-out, ablations) supports claims with log-likelihoods and Spearman correlations (avg 0.64 for full model).
- Limitations: two contexts and 14 questions dropped, hand-designed grammar, and unquantified sensitivity to grammar and proposal distribution q(x).
- Manuscript is clearly written and organized; some supplementary details (full grammar, types, examples) could be moved or summarized in main text.
- Modeling question equivalence via mutual information over answer partitions is a useful procedure that could be emphasized more.
- Novelty: framing question asking as program generation with learned EIG/complexity tradeoff is a novel, generative predictive contribution beyond prior partition semantics.
- Significance: advances computational models of question asking and has cognitive and active-learning implications, though broader ML impact needs cross-domain and natural-language demonstrations.
- Strength: principled compositional program formulation with demonstrated predictive power and insightful ablations highlighting complexity’s role.
- Strength: qualitative evidence of creative, novel question generation and synthesis of cognitive and ML perspectives.
- Weakness: domain-specific, relies on hand-engineered grammar with unclear generalization to other domains.
- Weakness: computational scalability issues evidenced by dropped contexts/operators and heavy reliance on hypothesis enumeration.
- Weakness: limited baselines—no RSA-style or neural baselines, no human evaluation of generated questions, and no simple length-penalized EIG comparison.
- Weakness: no sensitivity analysis to proposal distribution, sample size, or alternative complexity measures.
- Essential recommendations: add sensitivity analyses for complexity and proposal distribution, include an EIG−λ·length baseline, and report computational costs and scalability details.
- Optional recommendations: test an RSA-style objective baseline, run a small human evaluation of generated questions, and discuss paths to natural-language realization.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel compositional formulation, empirical evidence balancing informativeness and complexity, clear writing, and likely to spur follow-up work.
- Arguments against acceptance: narrow domain, engineered grammar, scalability concerns, and limited comparison to alternative baselines.
- Overall recommendation: lean toward acceptance given conceptual advance and careful modeling despite domain and scalability limitations.
