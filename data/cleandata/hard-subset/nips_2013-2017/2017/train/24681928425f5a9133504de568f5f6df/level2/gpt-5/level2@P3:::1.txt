Summary
The paper proposes a computational framework for question generation that treats questions as executable programs defined by a typed, lambda-calculus–inspired grammar. A log-linear model over program features assigns probabilities to questions, trading off informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (via the PCFG prior −log q(x)), and answer type (Boolean, Number, Color, Location), plus a relevance filter. Informativeness is computed with a Bayesian ideal observer over hypotheses in a Battleship-like game; complexity discourages long/unnatural programs. Parameters are learned by maximizing the likelihood of human questions using importance sampling to approximate gradients and the partition function. Evaluation on a dataset of 605 human questions across 18 contexts (two dropped for computational reasons) shows: (a) the full model predicts human question distributions better than ablated variants (lesioned EIG, complexity, or type features), (b) the model can sample novel questions that appear human-like, and (c) complexity is critical—removing it degrades predictions substantially. The work relates to active learning [21], formal semantics of questions as partitions [6, 18], cognitive models of compositionality/program induction [4, 5, 13], and semantic parsing/program-executing QA [24, 12], while focusing uniquely on question asking rather than answering.

Quality
- Soundness: The formalization is coherent, with clear definitions of the grammar, features, and learning objective. Informativeness is grounded in Bayesian belief updating with EIG; complexity is principled via the PCFG prior. Approximations (importance sampling for expectations and Z) are reasonable and bootstrapped comparisons mitigate variance.
- Empirics: Leave-one-context-out evaluation with ablations supports the claims. Reporting includes log-likelihood comparisons and rank correlations (average Spearman 0.64 for the full model, strongly negative for the complexity-agnostic variant). Novel question generation is demonstrated qualitatively.
- Limitations: Two contexts were dropped due to hypothesis-space size and 14 questions removed due to a probabilistic draw operator, which narrows empirical scope. The approach depends on a hand-designed grammar tailored to the domain; sensitivity to grammar design and to the proposal distribution q(x) is not quantified. Comparisons to alternative cognitive models (e.g., RSA-style questioners that weigh informativeness against cost) and to data-driven neural baselines trained on the same dataset are limited.

Clarity
The manuscript is clearly written and well organized. The motivation, formal definitions, and optimization details are accessible. Some critical details (full grammar, type system, additional examples) are in the supplement; including a minimal, self-contained grammar subset in the main text would aid readability. The procedure for determining program equivalence via mutual information over answer partitions is a nice touch and could be described more prominently.

Originality
Modeling question asking as program generation with a compositional grammar and learned utility over EIG and complexity is novel relative to prior work in active learning, question answering, and neural question generation. While the formal link to partition semantics is known, the contribution is a generative, predictive model of which questions are asked, not merely their meanings.

Significance
The approach advances computational models of question asking, demonstrating that human question distributions can be predicted by balancing informativeness and complexity. This has implications for cognitive science and for building more flexible active learners. Broader ML impact would be strengthened by demonstrations beyond the Battleship domain and by interfacing with natural language.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation of questions as programs with compositional generalization.
- Demonstrated predictive value on human data, with careful ablations highlighting the essential role of complexity.
- Plausible synthesis of cognitive and ML perspectives; informative connection to EIG and program priors.
- Qualitative evidence of creative, novel question generation.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Domain specificity: reliance on a hand-engineered grammar; unclear how much generalization carries to new domains without significant engineering.
- Computational constraints: dropping contexts and operators suggests scalability limits for EIG over large hypothesis spaces.
- Evaluation breadth: limited baselines beyond feature ablations; no human evaluation of the naturalness/utility of generated novel questions; no comparison to RSA-style questioner utilities or to simple length-regularized EIG.
- Sensitivity: no analysis of robustness to the proposal distribution q(x), sample size, or alternative complexity measures (e.g., token length).

Recommendations for improvement
Essential (feasible for camera-ready):
- Add a sensitivity analysis to (a) the complexity feature (compare −log q(x) vs. program length), and (b) the proposal distribution (e.g., resampling with different seeds/mixtures of grammar probabilities) to show robustness of likelihood and correlations.
- Include a baseline that ranks questions purely by EIG with a simple length penalty (e.g., EIG − λ·length) to isolate the benefit of the PCFG-based complexity prior.
- Report computational cost (sampling size, runtime per context, hypothesis set sizes) and discuss scalability.

Optional but valuable:
- Incorporate an RSA-style objective (informativeness minus cost) as a baseline, given prior cognitive work on question informativeness and cost [9, 18].
- Provide a small human evaluation of a few generated questions (e.g., pairwise preference vs. human questions) to complement qualitative examples.
- Discuss a path to natural language realization (e.g., mapping programs to surface forms via a semantic parser/generator [24]) and whether the grammar supports reversible mapping.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and timely formulation of question asking with a strong compositional foundation.
- Empirical evidence that balances of informativeness and complexity predict human behavior.
- Clear writing and thoughtful analysis; likely to spur follow-up in both ML and cognitive modeling.

Arguments against acceptance
- Narrow domain and engineered grammar limit immediate generality; absence of broader-domain experiments.
- Scalability concerns due to hypothesis enumeration and importance sampling.
- Limited comparison to alternative cognitive/neural baselines beyond ablations.

Overall recommendation
Lean toward acceptance. The conceptual advance and careful modeling outweigh domain limitations, and the paper should be of interest to the NIPS community working at the intersection of ML, program induction, and human-centered AI.
