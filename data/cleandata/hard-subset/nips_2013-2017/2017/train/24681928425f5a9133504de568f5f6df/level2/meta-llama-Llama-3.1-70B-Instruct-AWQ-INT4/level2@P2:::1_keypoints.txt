- Proposes a computational framework that models human question asking by treating questions as executable programs.  
- Develops a probabilistic generative model that favors concise, contextually informative programs to guide learning.  
- Represents questions with a compositional grammar based on lambda calculus/LISP.  
- Evaluated on a dataset of human natural-language questions from an information-search (Battleship) game.  
- Model predicts which questions humans will ask and synthesizes novel, human-like questions not in training data.  
- Compares against information-agnostic, complexity-agnostic, and type-agnostic baselines; full model best fits the data.  
- Paper is well-written with clear explanation of methods and results.  
- Strengths: novel probabilistic approach, formal grammar for questions, empirical evaluation on human data.  
- Limitation: operates on semantic representations rather than directly on natural language text.  
- Limitation: grammar and model are specific to the Battleship domain, limiting generalizability.  
- Significance: advances modeling of human question asking and has implications for AI systems that learn by asking.  
- Recommendation: integrate with computational-linguistics tools to map between semantics and natural language.  
- Recommendation: develop a more general grammar/model and evaluate across diverse domains.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel approach, promising results, ability to predict and generate human-like questions.  
- Rejection arguments: reliance on semantic inputs and domain-specific grammar limit practical applicability and generality.
