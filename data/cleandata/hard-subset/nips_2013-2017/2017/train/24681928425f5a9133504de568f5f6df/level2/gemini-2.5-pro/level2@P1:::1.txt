This paper presents a novel computational framework for modeling goal-directed, open-ended human question asking. The core idea is to represent questions as programs in a compositional language. This recasts the problem of question asking as one of probabilistic program generation. The authors define a log-linear model over the space of possible programs (questions), where the probability of a question is determined by a weighted combination of features, including its expected information gain (EIG), its complexity (related to program length), and its answer type (e.g., Boolean, Number). This approach is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans playing a "Battleship"-like game. The model is trained to predict the distribution of human questions in given game contexts. The results show that the full model, incorporating informativeness, complexity, and answer type, provides the best fit to the human data. Furthermore, the model is shown to be capable of generating novel, contextually appropriate questions that were not observed in the training set. This work connects ideas from active learning, program synthesis, and computational cognitive science to provide one of the first predictive models of open-ended human question generation.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central contribution of framing question asking as probabilistic program generation is highly original and significant. It moves beyond the typical active learning paradigm of querying for labels, proposing a formal mechanism for generating a much richer, compositional space of queries. This provides a powerful new direction for research in active learning and a significant step toward creating more human-like intelligent agents. The work successfully bridges machine learning and cognitive science, providing both a potential path forward for AI and a compelling computational model of a fundamental aspect of human intelligence.

2.  **Technical Quality and Soundness:** The paper is technically solid. The proposed probabilistic model is well-defined, and the features chosen (informativeness, complexity, answer type) are well-motivated. The ablation study, which compares the full model to variants lacking key components, is particularly effective. It provides strong evidence for the central claim that human question asking is not just about maximizing information gain, but involves a crucial trade-off with complexity. The finding that the complexity-agnostic model performs very poorly is a key result.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, explaining their formal approach, and presenting the results. The use of concrete examples of questions and their corresponding program representations (e.g., "Are all ships the same size?") is very effective in making the core ideas accessible.

4.  **Generative Capability:** A major strength is the demonstration that the model can generate novel, creative, and sensible questions (Figure 3). This goes beyond simply predicting frequencies of existing questions and shows that the model has captured some of the underlying generative principles of human inquiry in this domain.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Domain Specificity of the Grammar:** The primary limitation of the current work is its reliance on a hand-crafted grammar specific to the Battleship domain. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but the paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the path towards greater generality. For instance, it would be useful to explicitly categorize the grammar rules into domain-general components (e.g., logical operators, set functions, lambda calculus constructs) and domain-specific ones (e.g., primitives like `size(Ship)`). This would give readers a clearer sense of the engineering effort required to apply this framework to a new domain and highlight which parts might be learnable.

2.  **The Semantic-to-Text Gap:** The model operates entirely on the level of semantic programs, abstracting away from natural language. While this is a reasonable simplification for a foundational paper, it sidesteps the very challenging problems of semantic parsing (text-to-program) and natural language generation (program-to-text). A brief discussion of how the proposed system might interface with modern NLP models to bridge this gap would be valuable for situating the work within the broader AI landscape.

3.  **Inference and Optimization:** The model relies on importance sampling with a uniform probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) as the proposal distribution to approximate the partition function and gradients. This can be high-variance if the proposal distribution is a poor match for the true model distribution, especially after the feature weights (`theta`) have been learned. Could the authors comment on the stability of the training process? It would be interesting to know if they considered more advanced techniques, such as adaptive importance sampling or variational methods, which might offer more robust approximations for this class of models.

4.  **Analysis of Model Performance:** The average correlation of 0.64 between model predictions and human frequencies is solid, but Figure 2 shows considerable variability across contexts. The paper would benefit from a qualitative analysis of why the model performs better in some contexts than others. Does performance degrade when the space of plausible hypotheses is very large, or when the number of potentially useful questions is high? Such an analysis could provide deeper insights into the model's limitations and suggest avenues for future improvement.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that introduces a highly novel and compelling computational account of human question asking. The core idea is elegant, the experiments are well-designed, and the results are strong. The work makes a significant contribution to both machine learning and cognitive science. While the reliance on a hand-crafted grammar limits its immediate applicability, it lays a strong foundation for future research. The strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses, and it represents a clear and exciting advance in our understanding of intelligent inquiry.
