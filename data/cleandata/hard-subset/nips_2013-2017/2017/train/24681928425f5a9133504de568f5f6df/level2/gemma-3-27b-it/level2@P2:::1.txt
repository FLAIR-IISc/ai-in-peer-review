This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question asking by treating questions as programs. The core idea is compelling – representing questions as executable programs that, when run on a world state, yield an answer. The authors evaluate this model on a dataset of questions asked by humans playing a game, demonstrating the model can predict human questions and even generate novel, plausible questions. The work builds upon active learning, but moves beyond simple label requests to richer, more human-like inquiries.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The "question as a program" perspective is a significant departure from traditional approaches to question generation and active learning. It offers a theoretically grounded and potentially powerful way to model the cognitive process of question asking.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The model demonstrably outperforms several ablations, demonstrating the importance of both informativeness *and* complexity in generating human-like questions. The correlation between model-predicted energies and human question frequencies is encouraging.
* **Generative Capability:** The ability to generate novel questions that are not present in the training data is a significant achievement. The provided examples (Fig. 3) are quite convincing.
* **Clear Writing & Motivation:** The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the motivation for the work and the key ideas behind the approach. The connection to cognitive science (compositionality, language of thought) is well-established.
* **Well-defined Experimental Setup:** The use of the existing game dataset ([19]) provides a solid foundation for evaluation, and the leave-one-out cross-validation scheme is appropriate.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Grammar Complexity & Scalability:** While the grammar is sufficient for the current dataset, its scalability to more complex domains is unclear.  The restriction to questions with 100 or fewer functions (footnote 3) suggests potential limitations.  More discussion on the practical considerations of scaling the grammar would be valuable.
* **Domain Specificity:** The grammar, despite some general components, is still tailored to the Battleship domain. While acknowledged by the authors, a deeper discussion of how the approach might be adapted to other, very different domains is needed.  How much "knowledge engineering" would be required?
* **Computational Cost:** The importance sampling used for approximating the gradient is computationally expensive. This is mentioned briefly, but a more detailed discussion of the computational resources required and potential optimizations would be helpful.
* **EIG Calculation:** The reliance on a Bayesian ideal observer for calculating EIG is a strong assumption. While reasonable, it’s worth discussing potential sensitivities to the priors used in the Bayesian calculation.  How robust are the results to different prior specifications?
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The related work section mentions VQG and dialog systems, but doesn’t deeply engage with recent advances in neural question generation (e.g., Transformers). A more direct comparison to these models, even if the comparison is limited by the differences in task setup, would strengthen the paper.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** Technically sound, with a well-defined probabilistic model and a clear experimental methodology. Claims are generally well-supported by experimental results. It feels like a complete piece of work given its scope.
* **Clarity:**  Excellent. The paper is very well-written and easy to follow.
* **Originality:** High. The “question as a program” framework is novel and represents a significant advance in modeling question asking.
* **Significance:** Potentially high.  If this approach can be scaled and generalized, it could have a substantial impact on active learning, human-computer interaction, and cognitive science.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to modeling human question asking.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model.
* The work addresses a challenging and important problem, with potential for significant impact.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The scalability of the grammar and the computational cost of the inference procedure are potential concerns.
* The domain specificity of the current implementation limits its generalizability.



**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational cost and potential optimizations for the inference procedure.
* Elaborate on the challenges of scaling the grammar to more complex domains and discuss potential solutions.
* Include a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art neural question generation models.
* Discuss the sensitivity of the EIG calculation to the choice of priors.
* Consider presenting ablation studies on the different components of the grammar to better understand their individual contributions.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with a novel idea and compelling results. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors address the suggestions for improvement in a revised version.
