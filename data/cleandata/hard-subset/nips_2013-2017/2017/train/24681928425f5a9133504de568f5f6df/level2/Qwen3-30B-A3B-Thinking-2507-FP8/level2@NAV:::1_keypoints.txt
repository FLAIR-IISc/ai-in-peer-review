- Proposes a novel framework treating questions as executable programs in a lambda-calculus-based language capturing compositionality and computability.
- Uses a probabilistic generative model that balances Expected Information Gain, program complexity, answer type preferences, and game relevance.
- Evaluated on 605 natural-language questions from a Battleship-style game, demonstrating empirical validity.
- Full model outperforms ablated variants missing informativeness, complexity, or answer-type components.
- Achieves strong correlation with human question frequencies (Spearman’s ρ = 0.64) and generates novel, context-appropriate questions.
- Advances prior work by modeling rich, compositional, and computable questions rather than stereotyped canned questions.
- Strong theoretical grounding in cognitive science (language of thought), linguistics (compositionality), and active learning.
- Thorough evaluation including careful ablation studies and cross-validation.
- Limitation: operates on semantic representations rather than directly on natural language, requiring an encoding step.
- Limitation: grammar includes domain-specific elements, raising questions about scalability and adaptation to new domains.
- Limitation: does not sufficiently address handling of ambiguous or incomplete contextual information.
- Overall recommendation: significant contribution with minor refinements to domain generalization and language processing; recommend acceptance.
