- Novel framework: treats questions as executable programs with a probabilistic generative model balancing informativeness and complexity.  
- Evaluation: tested on 605-question Battleship-like dataset, predicting human frequencies and synthesizing novel contextually relevant questions.  
- Novelty/contribution: reframes question generation as program synthesis leveraging compositionality and computability.  
- Strong evaluation/results: quantitative (log-likelihood) and qualitative metrics show alignment with human question-asking and importance of informativeness-complexity tradeoff.  
- Generative capability: can produce novel, human-like questions not in the training set.  
- Clarity and reproducibility: well-written, logically structured, with supplementary materials.  
- Domain-specific grammar: grammar is partially tailored to Battleship, limiting generalizability and requiring manual engineering for other domains.  
- Natural language gap: operates on semantic representations rather than raw natural language, reducing real-world applicability without NLP integration.  
- Sparse human data: small dataset with many singleton questions risks limited robustness and potential overfitting.  
- Complexity dominance: complexity appears to play an outsized role in predictions, possibly overshadowing creativity and contextual nuance.  
- For acceptance: addresses an underexplored problem with a novel approach, strong results, and useful applications in active learning, dialogue, and cognitive modeling.  
- Against acceptance: domain-specific grammar and reliance on semantic representations plus small domain-specific dataset limit scalability and generalizability.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions; address natural language integration and demonstrate cross-domain applicability.
