- Proposes a novel probabilistic model that treats questions as formal programs executed on world states.
- Model biases toward concise, context-helpful programs to support learning.
- Claims the model can predict which questions people ask and generate novel, human-like questions.
- Claims increased flexibility and generalizability over prior question-asking approaches and captures goal-directed questioning.
- Evaluated on a human question dataset from an information-search game; model predicts question frequencies given context.
- Demonstrates the model can generate novel questions not present in training data.
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and uses helpful figures and tables.
- Ideas are useful for understanding human question asking and for improving active learning, with relevance to NLP, HCI, and cognitive science.
- Authors provide a thorough related-work review and use accurate technical terminology.
- Originality is high: treating questions as probabilistic programs is an innovative contribution.
- Completeness is high: detailed model description, reproducibility details, and discussion of limitations/extensions.
- Limitation: model operates on semantic representations rather than raw natural language, limiting applicability.
- Limitation: some grammar components are specific to the Battleship domain, which may reduce generalizability.
- Paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of limitations and potential extensions.
