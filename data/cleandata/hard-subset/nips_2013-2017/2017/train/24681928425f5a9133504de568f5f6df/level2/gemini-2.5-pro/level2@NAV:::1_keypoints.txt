- Introduces a novel model framing human question asking as probabilistic program generation (questions = compositional programs that return answers).  
- Defines a probabilistic model over program space via a grammar, favoring informative (high EIG) and simple (low complexity) questions.  
- Evaluated on a dataset of natural human questions in an ambiguous "Battleship"-like game.  
- Model predicts the distribution of human questions and can generate novel, contextually relevant questions unseen in training.  
- Strength: original and significant formalism that moves beyond label-request active learning toward goal-directed inquiry.  
- Strength: sound technical execution with a clear probabilistic model and rigorous evaluation.  
- Strength: ablation studies demonstrate both informativeness and complexity are necessary; complexity-agnostic model performs poorly.  
- Strength: qualitative examples of generated questions are compelling; paper is well-written and well-situated in prior work.  
- Main weakness: reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar requiring substantial engineering to port to new domains.  
- Main weakness: empirical validation limited to a single, constrained game environment, raising generalizability concerns.  
- Suggested future direction: learn grammar components from data to bridge symbolic programs and neural language models.  
- Overall verdict: significant, psychologically plausible contribution likely to inspire follow-up work; strong candidate for acceptance.
