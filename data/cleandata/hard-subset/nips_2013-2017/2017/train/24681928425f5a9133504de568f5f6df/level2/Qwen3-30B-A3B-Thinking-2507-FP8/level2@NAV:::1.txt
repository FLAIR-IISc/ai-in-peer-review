

This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by treating questions as formal programs that execute on the state of the world to produce answers. The authors model questions as programs in a simple programming language based on lambda calculus, capturing both compositionality (building questions from conceptual primitives) and computability (questions as executable programs). The model uses a probabilistic generative approach that weighs question informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (favoring concise programs), answer type preferences, and relevance to the game context.

The paper's main strength lies in its compelling evaluation using a dataset of 605 natural language questions from a Battleship-style game. The authors demonstrate that their full model, incorporating all key features, significantly outperforms variants that omit any single feature (informativeness, complexity, or answer type). The model achieves strong correlation (Spearman's ρ=0.64) with human question frequencies and successfully generates novel, contextually appropriate questions not present in the training data. This represents a meaningful advance over previous work on question asking, which has largely focused on stereotyped or canned questions in constrained domains.

The paper excels in its theoretical grounding, connecting to cognitive science (language of thought), linguistics (compositionality), and active learning. The probabilistic program framework provides a principled way to model the human trade-off between question informativeness and complexity. The evaluation is thorough, with careful ablation studies and cross-validation.

However, the work has limitations. The model operates on semantic representations rather than natural language directly, and the grammar contains domain-specific elements that would require significant adaptation for new domains. While the authors acknowledge these limitations, they could better discuss how their approach might generalize to other domains without requiring extensive knowledge engineering. Additionally, the paper doesn't sufficiently address how the model would handle questions with ambiguous or incomplete context.

Overall, this is a significant contribution that advances our understanding of human question asking and provides a promising framework for developing more human-like question-asking systems. The model's ability to generate novel, contextually relevant questions represents a meaningful step toward more natural human-machine interaction. With minor refinements to address domain generalization and language processing, this work could have substantial impact in cognitive science and AI. I recommend acceptance.
