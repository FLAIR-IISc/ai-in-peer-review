This paper presents a novel approach to modeling human question asking abilities in a computational framework. The authors propose a probabilistic model that treats questions as formal programs that, when executed on the state of the world, output an answer. The model specifies a probability distribution over a complex, compositional space of programs, favoring concise programs that help the agent learn in the current context.

The main claims of the paper are that the proposed model can predict what questions people will ask, and can creatively produce novel questions that were not present in the training set. The authors also claim that their approach is more flexible and generalizable than previous approaches to question asking, and that it can capture intentional, goal-directed forms of human question asking.

The support for these claims is provided through a series of experiments and evaluations. The authors use a data set of natural language questions asked by human participants in an information-search game, and show that their model can predict the frequencies of human questions given a game context. They also demonstrate that the model can generate novel, human-like questions that were not present in the training set.
