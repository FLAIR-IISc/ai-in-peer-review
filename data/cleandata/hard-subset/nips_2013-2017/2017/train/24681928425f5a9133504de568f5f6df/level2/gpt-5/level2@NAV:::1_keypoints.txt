- Proposes a cognitive model treating questions as executable programs, sampling from a compositional PCFG-defined program space.  
- Uses a log-linear utility combining expected information gain (EIG), program complexity (−log PCFG prob), answer type, and a relevance filter.  
- Evaluated on a Battleship-style dataset (605 human questions, 18 contexts); model predicts held-out human distributions (average Spearman 0.64) and generates novel human-like questions.  
- Ablation studies show both informativeness and complexity terms are necessary; full model outperforms variants missing EIG, type, or complexity.  
- Technical methods (probabilistic formulation, EIG computation, importance sampling, optimization) are clearly described and sound.  
- Main methodological weaknesses: manual NL→program translation with no inter-annotator agreement or error analysis, domain-specific grammar, dropped two hardest contexts plus 14 “draw” questions.  
- Dependence on approximate normalization and importance sampling introduces possible proposal bias that is not quantified.  
- Clarity is good; paper would benefit from including full grammar, releasing code, and detailing the NL→program mapping protocol for reproducibility.  
- Originality: novel framing of question generation as program synthesis with a utility-regularized distribution, distinct from standard active learning and neural VQG.  
- Significance for cognitive science: first predictive account of open-ended human question asking and explanation for complexity beyond EIG.  
- Significance for AI: suggests richer goal-directed query generation but practical impact limited by single-domain focus and lack of end-to-end NL.  
- Suggested experiments: test on an additional domain with minimal engineering and compare to neural baselines trained on same semantic targets.  
- Suggested implementation: integrate a semantic parser for NL→program mapping and report mapping accuracy/IAA.  
- Modeling extensions: incorporate richer answer costs (cognitive load, answerability under noise) and reintroduce probabilistic draws with tractable approximations.  
- Robustness checks requested: analyze sensitivity to priors, proposal distribution, and normalization constant (Z) estimation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: principled formulation, strong ablation evidence, novel cross-disciplinary contribution.  
- Arguments against acceptance: small single-domain dataset, manual semantics, dropped data, and limited external validation.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept.
