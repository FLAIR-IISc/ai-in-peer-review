This paper proposes a cognitive model of question asking that treats questions as executable programs and learns a distribution over a compositional program space to generate context-sensitive, informative queries. A PCFG-based grammar (lambda/LISP style) defines the space; a log-linear model trades off expected information gain (EIG), program complexity (via −log PCFG probability), answer type, and a relevance filter. Evaluated on a Battleship-style dataset of 605 human questions across 18 contexts, the model predicts held-out human question distributions (average Spearman 0.64) and samples novel, “human-like” questions. Ablations show both informativeness and complexity are necessary; the full model outperforms information-, type-, and especially complexity-agnostic variants, with bootstrapped robustness analyses. This builds on prior work in active learning, semantic/pragmatic accounts of questions as partitions, semantic parsing as program execution, and language-of-thought/program-induction models popular at NIPS.

Quality: Technically sound overall. The probabilistic formulation, EIG computation over a context-specific hypothesis space, and importance sampling approximations are clearly described; optimization details are given. However, the study relies on hand-translating natural language into programs (no inter-annotator agreement or error analysis reported), uses a domain-specific grammar, drops two hardest contexts and 14 “draw” questions, and depends on approximate normalization; potential proposal bias is not quantified.

Clarity: Well written and organized; core ideas and assumptions are explicit and related work is fairly comprehensive. Reproducibility would benefit from including the full grammar in the main text, code release, and details on the NL→program mapping protocol.

Originality: Modeling question generation as program synthesis with a learned utility-regularized distribution is novel and differentiates from label-query active learning and neural VQG/goal-oriented dialog.

Significance: For cognitive modeling, the paper offers the first predictive account of open-ended human question asking and a principled explanation for the role of complexity beyond EIG. For AI, it suggests a path toward richer, goal-directed queries, though practical impact is limited by domain specificity and lack of end-to-end NL.

Constructive suggestions:
- Evaluate on an additional domain with minimal engineering; include comparisons to neural baselines trained on the same semantic targets.
- Integrate a semantic parser to map NL to programs; report mapping accuracy/IAA.
- Model answer costs beyond length (e.g., cognitive load or answerability under noise).
- Analyze sensitivity to priors, proposal distribution, and Z estimation; reintroduce probabilistic draw with tractable approximations.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, principled formulation; strong ablation evidence; creative, novel question generation.
- Bridges cognitive science and AI with tangible, testable predictions.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Small, single-domain dataset; manual semantics; dropped contexts/questions; limited external validation.

Recommendation: Weak accept.
