This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question asking by treating questions as formal programs. The core idea is compelling: questions are not merely requests for information but executable programs that, when run on a world state, yield an answer. The authors evaluate this framework by modeling questions asked by humans in a game setting, demonstrating the ability to predict human question choices and even generate novel, human-like questions. 

The work builds upon existing research in active learning, which often focuses on simple label requests, and extends it to richer, more human-like queries. It connects to work in dialog systems, but emphasizes the importance of intentional, goal-directed questioning beyond canned responses. The paper also acknowledges connections to visual question generation but argues that their approach captures human-level intelligence in novel scenarios without requiring extensive training data. The related work section is thorough and appropriately contextualizes the project within the broader literature.

A key strength of the paper lies in its formalization of questions as programs. This allows for a compositional representation of questions and enables reasoning about their informativeness and complexity. The probabilistic model, while complex, provides a principled way to learn question features and predict human behavior. The experimental results are promising, demonstrating that the model can accurately predict human question choices and generate plausible novel questions. The ablation study, comparing different model variants, highlights the importance of both informativeness and complexity in generating human-like questions. The inclusion of generated questions as examples provides a useful qualitative assessment of the model’s capabilities.

However, several areas could be improved. The description of the grammar, while present in the supplementary material, could benefit from being more integrated into the main text, perhaps with more illustrative examples. A more detailed explanation of how the “relevance” feature is determined would also be beneficial.  While the authors acknowledge the limitations of operating on semantic representations rather than natural language directly, it is unclear how readily the approach could be extended to handle more naturalistic language. The reliance on a specific game context raises questions about the generalizability of the findings. Further evaluation on different tasks or datasets would strengthen the claims. While the ablation studies are insightful, the differences in log-likelihood values are relatively small. More rigorous statistical analysis would be welcome to confirm the significance of these differences. The bootstrapping used to compare the models is a good approach but could be elaborated. 

The authors do a commendable job of acknowledging limitations and suggesting future work. The complexity of the approach is significant, and the computational burden is noted, although a discussion of scalability would be helpful. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and compelling framework for modeling human question asking.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating predictive power and generative capabilities.
*   Thorough related work and careful ablation studies.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Reliance on a specific game context limits generalizability.
*   Complexity of the approach and potential scalability issues.
*   Limited statistical significance in ablation study results.
*   The link between semantic program representation and natural language is not fully explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of AI and cognitive science. The proposed framework for modeling question asking is innovative and well-motivated, and the experimental results are promising. While there are areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. This work has the potential to advance our understanding of human cognition and to inspire the development of more intelligent and interactive AI systems. A recommendation for acceptance is warranted, with a request for the authors to address the noted limitations in future work.
