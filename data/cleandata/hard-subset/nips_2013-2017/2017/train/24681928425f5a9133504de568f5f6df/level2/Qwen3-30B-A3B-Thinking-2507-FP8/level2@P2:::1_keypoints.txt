- Proposes a novel computational model treating questions as executable programs mapping world states to answers.  
- Presents a probabilistic generative model combining informativeness (EIG), complexity (concise programs), answer type, and relevance.  
- Evaluated on 605 human questions from a Battleship-like game, predicting human question frequencies and generating novel, human-like questions.  
- Modeling questions as programs offers a new formalism grounded in compositionality and computability.  
- Quantitative log-likelihood comparisons show the full model outperforms variants missing informativeness, complexity, or answer-type features.  
- Qualitative examples demonstrate generation of plausible novel questions not in training data.  
- Distinct from prior active learning and neural question-generation work; authors acknowledge these differences.  
- Writing, organization, and grammar design for question generation are clear and technically sound.  
- Supplementary materials are mentioned and would aid reproducibility.  
- Major limitation: model is tightly coupled to the Battleship domain with limited discussion of generalization.  
- Evaluation limited to a single dataset; applicability to diverse or real-world scenarios is untested.  
- Computational complexity of EIG calculations is not adequately addressed and may not scale to harder domains.  
- Significance: promising step toward human-like question asking and interactive AI, but immediate impact constrained by domain specificity.  
- Overall judgment: strengths outweigh scope limitations; recommend acceptance with suggestions to address generalization and scalability in future work.
