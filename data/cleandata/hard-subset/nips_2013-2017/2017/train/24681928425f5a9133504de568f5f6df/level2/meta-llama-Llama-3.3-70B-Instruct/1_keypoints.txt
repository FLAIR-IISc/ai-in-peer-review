- Introduces a probabilistic model that represents questions as executable programs mapping world states to answers.  
- Evaluated on human natural-language questions collected in an information-search (Battleship) game.  
- Model predicts which questions human learners will ask and can generate novel human-like questions absent from training data.  
- Claims the model captures the compositionality and computability of human questions and serves as a predictive model of open-ended question asking.  
- Supports claims with theoretical analysis, experimental results, and comparisons to alternative models.  
- Strength: novel, innovative formalization of question asking combining compositionality and computability.  
- Strength: strong predictive and generative performance, including synthesis of plausible novel questions.  
- Strength: thorough evaluation, including baseline comparisons and limitations analysis.  
- Weakness: operates on semantic representations rather than raw natural language, limiting direct real-world applicability.  
- Weakness: some grammar components are Battleship-specific, implying substantial knowledge engineering needed to generalize.  
- Recommendation summary: strengths outweigh weaknesses; contributes to AI and cognitive science with implications for active learning systems.  
- Additional critique: paper could expand discussion of broader applications, limitations, and potential biases.
