- Introduces a probabilistic program-generation model of human question asking that represents questions as compositional programs via a generative grammar.  
- Uses a log-linear model that trades off informativeness (Expected Information Gain) against complexity (grammar-based simplicity prior).  
- Evaluated on a "Battleship"-like dataset and shown to predict human question distributions and generate novel, contextually relevant questions.  
- High technical quality: well-founded probabilistic framework, appropriate use of EIG, and an elegant complexity prior from the grammar.  
- Strong empirical validation: leave-one-out cross-validation and ablation study support the informativeness–complexity trade-off.  
- Notable result: complexity-agnostic model performs very poorly, indicating maximizing EIG alone is insufficient.  
- Exceptionally clear writing and presentation, with helpful examples and an effective illustrative figure (Fig. 3).  
- Highly original: novel application of probabilistic program generation to model the act of asking questions, combining Bayesian utility with symbolic generative structure.  
- Significant impact potential for active learning, human-in-the-loop AI, and computational cognitive science as a predictive, generative "language of thought" model.  
- Key weakness: reliance on a hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar limits generalization and requires a pathway for learning domain primitives.  
- Lacks a natural language interface—does not address parsing NL to programs or generating fluent NL from programs, limiting practical dialogue applications.  
- Computational cost concern: inference uses heavy importance sampling (150,000 proposals), raising questions about scalability and resource requirements.  
- Overall judgment: excellent, influential paper; strengths substantially outweigh limitations and it is a strong fit for NIPS.
