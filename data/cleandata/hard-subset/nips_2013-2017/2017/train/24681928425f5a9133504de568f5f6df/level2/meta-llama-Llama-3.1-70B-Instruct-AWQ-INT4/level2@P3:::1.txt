**Summary of the Main Ideas**

This paper introduces a cognitive model that treats questions as formal programs that, when executed on the state of the world, output an answer. The model specifies a probability distribution over a complex, compositional space of programs, favoring concise programs that help the agent learn in the current context. The authors evaluate their approach by modeling the types of open-ended questions generated by humans who were attempting to learn about an ambiguous situation in a game. The model predicts what questions people will ask and can creatively produce novel questions that were not present in the training set.

**Related Work**

The authors discuss related work in active learning, AI dialog systems, and question generation. They highlight the limitations of current approaches, such as the lack of representational capacity to consider a richer range of queries, and the inability to capture intentional, goal-directed forms of human question asking.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach**: The paper introduces a new approach to modeling question asking as program generation, which offers a fresh perspective on the problem.
2. **Formalism**: The authors provide a formalism for representing questions as programs, which allows for a more precise and computational understanding of question asking.
3. **Evaluation**: The paper includes a thorough evaluation of the model, using a data set of human-generated questions and comparing the model's performance to alternative models.
4. **Insights**: The authors provide insights into the importance of compositionality, computability, and informativeness in human question asking.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Domain specificity**: The model is specific to the Battleship domain, and it is unclear how much new knowledge engineering will be needed to extend the approach to new domains.
2. **Limited generalizability**: The model's performance may not generalize to other domains or tasks, and further evaluation is needed to assess its robustness.
3. **Complexity**: The model's complexity may make it difficult to interpret and understand, particularly for non-experts.

**Originality**

The paper's approach to modeling question asking as program generation is novel and original. The authors' use of a formalism to represent questions as programs is also innovative.
