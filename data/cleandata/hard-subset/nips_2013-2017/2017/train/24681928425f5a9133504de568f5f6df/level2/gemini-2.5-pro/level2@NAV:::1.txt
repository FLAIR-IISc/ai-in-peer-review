This paper presents a novel computational model of human question asking, framing the problem as one of probabilistic program generation. The core idea is that questions can be represented as compositional programs that, when executed, return an answer. The authors develop a probabilistic model over a space of programs defined by a grammar, which favors questions that are both informative (high Expected Information Gain) and simple (low complexity). This approach is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in an ambiguous "Battleship"-like game. The model successfully predicts the distribution of human questions and can generate novel, contextually relevant questions not seen during training.

The paper’s primary strengths are its originality and significance. By treating question asking as program synthesis, the work provides a compelling new direction for active learning, moving far beyond the typical label-request paradigm. The technical execution is sound, with a clearly defined probabilistic model and a rigorous evaluation that uses ablations to demonstrate the necessity of both informativeness and complexity features for explaining human behavior. The finding that a complexity-agnostic model performs very poorly is a particularly strong result. The qualitative examples of novel, model-generated questions are impressive and effectively support the claim of generative capacity. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly positions its contributions with respect to prior work in active learning and dialogue systems.

The main weakness of the approach is its reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar. While the authors note that many components are domain-general, significant engineering effort would be required to apply this framework to a new domain. This raises questions about the scalability and generalizability of the method. The experimental validation is also confined to a single, relatively constrained game environment. Future work could explore methods for learning the grammar's components from data, potentially bridging the gap between this symbolic approach and modern neural language models.

Despite this limitation, the paper makes a significant and original contribution. It introduces a powerful new formalism for goal-directed inquiry and provides strong evidence that this "questions-as-programs" framework is a psychologically plausible model of human cognition. The work is likely to inspire new research in both cognitive science and AI. The paper is a clear and strong candidate for acceptance.
