## Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper presents a novel framework for modeling human question asking, framing questions as formal programs that, when executed, yield an answer. The core idea – treating questions as executable code – is a compelling approach to capturing the semantic richness and goal-directedness often missing in traditional active learning. The authors demonstrate this by modeling a game-based information seeking scenario, showing their model can predict human question choices and even generate novel, plausible questions.

The strength of this work lies in its theoretical grounding and the attempt to bridge cognitive science and machine learning. The authors convincingly argue for the importance of compositionality and computability in human question asking, and the grammar they develop effectively captures this. The experimental evaluation, using a dataset of human-generated questions in a “Battleship”-like game, provides a solid foundation for their claims. The ablation study, comparing different model variants, clearly demonstrates the importance of both question informativeness (EIG) and complexity, a nuanced finding often overlooked. 

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge limitations, the scope is relatively narrow. The model is heavily tied to the specific game domain, and the extent to which the approach generalizes to other contexts remains unclear. The reliance on a hand-crafted grammar, while necessary for controlling the program space, introduces a potential bottleneck for scalability and adaptability. Furthermore, the evaluation, while statistically significant, relies on a relatively small dataset, and the generated questions are evaluated qualitatively. A more rigorous, automated evaluation of question quality would be beneficial. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to modeling question asking.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating predictive power and generative capabilities.
*   Clear articulation of the importance of both informativeness and complexity.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited generalizability due to domain specificity.
*   Reliance on a hand-crafted grammar.
*   Qualitative evaluation of generated questions.
*   Relatively small dataset size.

**Overall:**

Despite these limitations, this is a valuable contribution to the field. The paper presents a significant step towards more human-like question asking in machines and offers a fresh perspective on active learning. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically encouraging the authors to discuss the limitations regarding generalizability more thoroughly and to explore potential avenues for automating the evaluation of generated questions. A brief discussion of how the grammar could be learned or adapted to new domains would also strengthen the paper.
