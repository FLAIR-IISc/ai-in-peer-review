- Introduces a novel computational framework treating questions as executable programs that output answers when run on a world state.  
- Proposes a probabilistic model that samples context-sensitive, compositional questions from a grammar and scores them by informativeness, complexity, answer type, and domain relevance.  
- Addresses an important gap in active learning by modeling rich, open-ended human questions rather than simple label queries.  
- Evaluates on a Battleship-like dataset of human questions and achieves strong predictive performance (Spearman ρ = 0.64) while generating novel questions.  
- Demonstrates strong technical quality with thorough ablation studies showing informativeness, complexity, and answer type are all necessary; complexity-agnostic model performs poorly (ρ = -0.36).  
- Grammar design enables compositional question generation and accounts for productivity (15% of human questions were context-unique).  
- Uses rigorous evaluation methodology (leave-one-out cross-validation and bootstrapping) and well-justified metrics.  
- Highly original synthesis drawing from linguistics, cognitive science, and machine learning; differs from prior visual QG and canned dialog approaches.  
- Provides a clear theoretical foundation linking formal semantics of questions and Bayesian inference.  
- Significant implications for building human-like active learners and for computational models of human cognition; approach is extensible to other domains.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations (need for domain knowledge, gap to natural language) but note many grammar components are domain-general.  
- Well-written, clearly organized paper with appropriate supplementary technical details and careful comparison to related work; meets NIPS criteria for quality, originality, significance, and clarity.
