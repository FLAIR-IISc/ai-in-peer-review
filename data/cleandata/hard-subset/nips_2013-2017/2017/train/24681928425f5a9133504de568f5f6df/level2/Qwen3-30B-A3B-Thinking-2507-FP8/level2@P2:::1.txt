

This paper proposes an innovative computational model for human-like question asking by treating questions as formal programs that, when executed on a world state, output an answer. The authors present a probabilistic generative model that incorporates features for question informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (favoring concise programs), answer type (Boolean, Number, Color, Location), and relevance. The model is evaluated on a dataset of 605 natural language questions asked by humans in a Battleship-like game, demonstrating its ability to predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions not present in the training data.

The paper makes a significant contribution by modeling questions as programs, which offers a novel formalism for understanding question asking. The authors successfully demonstrate that their model, which combines compositionality and computability, outperforms variants that omit key features (informativeness, complexity, or answer type) in predicting human question frequencies. The quantitative evaluation (log-likelihood comparisons) and qualitative generation of novel questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") are compelling evidence for the model's validity. The paper's theoretical grounding in compositionality and computability aligns with cognitive science and linguistics literature, providing a strong foundation for the approach.

The work stands out from prior active learning research (which typically focuses on simple label requests) and natural language question generation (which often relies on large datasets of paired questions and images). The authors properly acknowledge these distinctions in their related work section, though they could have more thoroughly connected to recent advances in program synthesis and probabilistic programming for language.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical progression from problem statement to model to evaluation. The grammar for question generation is carefully designed to capture compositionality, and the probabilistic model is technically sound. The supplementary materials (mentioned but not directly referenced) would be valuable for reproducibility.

However, several limitations warrant consideration. First, the model is tightly coupled to the Battleship domain, with limited discussion of how to generalize to other domains. While the authors acknowledge that some grammar elements are domain-specific, they don't provide a clear path forward for extending the approach to more complex or varied contexts. The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of what aspects of the grammar could be preserved across domains versus what would need to be engineered anew.

Second, the evaluation is limited to a single dataset. While the Battleship game provides a controlled environment for studying question asking, the model's performance on more diverse or real-world scenarios remains untested. The authors should acknowledge this limitation and suggest how future work could address it.

Third, the paper doesn't adequately address the computational complexity of the EIG calculation, which could become prohibitive in more complex domains. The authors mention that the grammar excludes certain rules due to computational complexity, but they don't discuss how this might scale to more challenging scenarios.

The significance of this work is promising for AI, particularly for developing more human-like question-asking capabilities in machines. The ability to generate novel, contextually appropriate questions represents an important step toward more sophisticated interactive learning systems. However, the immediate impact is somewhat limited by the domain specificity, and the paper would be strengthened by a more concrete discussion of how this approach might integrate with broader AI systems.

The paper's strengths—novel formalism, clear theoretical grounding, and strong evaluation—outweigh its weaknesses, which are largely limitations of scope rather than flaws in the core approach. The model represents a significant step toward understanding human question asking computationally and could inspire more human-like question-asking in AI systems.

I recommend acceptance, as the paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding human question asking through a computational lens. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates a novel approach that could influence future research in active learning and human-AI interaction. The authors should address the domain generalization challenges in future work, but these do not diminish the current contribution.
