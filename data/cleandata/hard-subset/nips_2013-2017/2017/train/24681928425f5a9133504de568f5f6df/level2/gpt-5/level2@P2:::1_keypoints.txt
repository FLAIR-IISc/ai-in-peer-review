- Summary: Models question asking as program generation using a typed lambda-calculus-like language, a PCFG over programs, and a log-linear model trading off EIG, complexity, answer-type priors, and relevance; trained with importance sampling and evaluated on a 605-question Battleship-style dataset with leave-one-context-out.
- Main empirical findings: Full model improves held-out log-likelihood over ablations, shows reasonable rank correlations with human frequencies, and can generate novel plausible questions.
- Relation to prior work: Connects formal semantics, program induction, and active learning; contrasts with neural question generation and prior QA-as-program/pragmatic models by generating compositional, goal-directed questions.
- Contribution novelty: Generates questions via a compositional grammar and demonstrates that an informativeness-vs-complexity trade-off predicts human choices.
- Strength: Clear, principled formalization enabling compositionality, executability, and explicit answer types.
- Strength: Cognitive plausibility — ablations support the hypothesis that humans trade off informativeness and complexity.
- Strength: Generativity — grammar covers observed questions and produces sensible novel questions for held-out contexts.
- Strength: Interpretable model with explicit features and transparent (if approximate) learning and inference.
- Strength: Relevant to active learning, program induction, and cognitive AI communities.
- Weakness: Domain specificity and potential circularity — grammar was engineered with access to the dataset, raising questions about portability to new domains.
- Weakness: Limited dataset and exclusions — small dataset (605), two complex contexts dropped, and 14 “draw” questions removed without quantifying impact.
- Weakness: Approximation and stability concerns — importance sampling (150k samples) used for Z and gradients but variances/SEs not reported, making robustness unclear.
- Weakness: Complexity feature conflation — −log q(x) under the PCFG may reflect grammar artifacts rather than human comprehensibility; alternative penalties not compared.
- Weakness: Metric inconsistency — info-agnostic variant slightly out-correlates humans in Spearman correlation (0.65 vs 0.64), requiring reconciliation with LL-based model selection.
- Weakness: NL-to-program mapping under-specified — annotation procedures, inter-annotator agreement, and ambiguity resolution are not reported.
- Weakness: Lack of human evaluation of generated questions — no blinded ratings or direct human comparisons to assess naturalness/usefulness.
- Quality assessment: Technical formulation is sound and well-motivated, but empirical claims are limited by dataset size, domain specificity, and approximation choices.
- Clarity: Paper is generally clear and organized; however, key details (full grammar, proposal, EIG computation, NL-to-program mapping) are deferred to the supplement and should be summarized in main text.
- Originality: Framing question asking as program synthesis and learning a context-conditional question distribution is novel and interesting.
- Significance: Conceptually important for cognitive science and AI, though practical impact is limited by current domain specificity.
- Suggested improvements: Report uncertainty estimates (SEs/CIs), analyze dropped contexts and removed questions, compare alternative complexity penalties, provide NL-to-program annotation details and release code, add stronger baselines, and include human evaluations.
- Pros for acceptance: Elegant, interpretable formulation; empirical support for informativeness and complexity trade-off; bridges cognitive theory and ML.
- Cons for acceptance: Narrow domain and engineered grammar, heavy reliance on approximations without uncertainty analysis, and limited human evaluation.
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept; Confidence: Medium.
