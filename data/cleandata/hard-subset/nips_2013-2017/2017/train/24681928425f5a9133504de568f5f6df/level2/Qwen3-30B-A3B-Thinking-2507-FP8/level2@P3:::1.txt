

The paper introduces a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by treating questions as formal programs that, when executed on a world state, output an answer. The authors propose a probabilistic model that generates context-sensitive questions by sampling from a compositional program space defined by a grammar, incorporating features for question informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (via grammar probability), answer type, and relevance to the domain.

This work addresses a significant gap in active learning literature, as most systems focus on simple label requests while humans ask rich, open-ended questions. The authors evaluate their approach on a dataset of human questions asked in a Battleship-like game, where players sought to identify ship configurations. The model successfully predicts human question frequencies (Spearman correlation of 0.64) and generates novel questions not present in the training data.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a well-constructed probabilistic model. The authors conduct thorough ablation studies, showing that all components (informativeness, complexity, answer type) contribute to performance, with the complexity-agnostic model performing particularly poorly (Spearman correlation of -0.36). The grammar design enables compositional question generation, accounting for the productivity of human question asking (15% of human questions were unique to a context). The evaluation methodology is rigorous, using leave-one-out cross-validation and bootstrapping to assess model performance.

The work is highly original, drawing on concepts from linguistics (compositionality), cognitive science (language of thought), and machine learning (program synthesis). It differs significantly from prior work in visual question generation (which requires large datasets of paired images and questions) and goal-directed dialog systems (which use canned questions with limited flexibility). The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation connecting to formal semantics of questions and Bayesian inference.

The significance of the work is substantial. It advances active learning by enabling richer, more human-like question asking and provides a computational explanation for how humans balance informativeness and complexity when formulating questions. This has implications for building more capable AI systems that can learn through human-like questioning and for understanding human cognition. The approach could be extended to other domains, though the current grammar has some domain-specific elements.

The paper acknowledges limitations thoughtfully, including the need for domain knowledge and the gap between semantic representations and natural language. However, the authors note that many aspects of the grammar are domain-general (e.g., logical connectives, set operations), and the model's ability to generate novel questions demonstrates its potential for broader application.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with technical details appropriately placed in supplementary materials. The evaluation is comprehensive, addressing both quantitative prediction of human question frequencies and qualitative generation of novel questions. The authors carefully compare their approach to related work, demonstrating how it advances beyond previous methods.

The work makes a strong contribution to NIPS by bridging cognitive science and machine learning with a novel computational approach to question asking. It represents a significant step toward more human-like active learning systems and provides a theoretically grounded model of human question generation. The model's ability to both predict human question patterns and generate novel, contextually appropriate questions demonstrates its value as a computational model of human cognition.

This paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is technically sound with thorough evaluation, clearly written with appropriate organization, highly original in its approach to question asking, and significant in its potential to advance both active learning and computational cognitive science. The work is well-positioned to influence future research in these areas and represents a clear contribution to the field.
