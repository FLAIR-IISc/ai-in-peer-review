Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a computational model of human question asking that treats questions as executable programs defined by a typed, LISP-like grammar. A log-linear distribution over this compositional space is learned from data, with features capturing informativeness (expected information gain, EIG, in a Bayesian ideal observer over Battleship boards), complexity (negative log-probability under a probabilistic grammar), answer type (Boolean/Number/Color/Location), and relevance. The model is trained to match human question frequencies via feature expectation matching using importance sampling. On a dataset of 605 natural-language questions from an information-search game (Battleship), the model predicts held-out human question distributions and generates novel, human-like questions not seen in training. Ablations indicate that both informativeness and complexity are necessary to reproduce human behavior.

This work sits at the intersection of active learning (beyond label queries), program induction and semantic parsing (questions as programs), and formal/pragmatic models of questions in linguistics and cognitive science (e.g., questions as partitions, Bayesian ideal observer). It complements dialog system work (goal-directed and neural) and VQG, arguing for goal-directed, compositional representations rather than surface-form generation alone. Compared to prior cognitive work (e.g., Hawkins et al.), the contribution here is a generative model over question programs that predicts human question distributions and synthesizes new ones.

Strengths
- Clear, original formulation: Representing questions as programs and learning a distribution over a rich, typed grammar is elegant and aligns with compositionality in language/cognition.
- Principled utility: Informativeness via EIG over a context-specific hypothesis space provides a normative grounding; the complexity feature captures cognitive cost/conciseness.
- Empirical validation: Leave-one-context-out predictions correlate with human frequencies (avg Spearman ~0.64); the complexity ablation degrades substantially, supporting the informativeness–complexity trade-off claim.
- Generativity: The model produces novel, context-sensitive questions not present in training; equivalence classes are defined by answer distributions over hypotheses.
- Relevance to NIPS: Bridges active learning, program induction, and pragmatic reasoning; proposes a path to more human-like querying in AI.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Evaluation scope and robustness: The dataset is modest (605 questions over 16 contexts used for training/testing), two contexts and 14 questions were dropped for computational reasons. It is unclear how sensitive results are to these exclusions or to the importance sampling budget (150k samples) and initialization.
- Approximate inference concerns: Log-likelihoods rely on importance sampling for Z and model expectations; diagnostics of variance/ESS or convergence are not reported. The bootstrap over Z is helpful but limited.
- Complexity prior dependence: Using −log q(x) under the hand-designed PCFG conflates complexity with the grammar design; alternative priors (MDL/description length, learned production costs, or human response-time proxies) could provide a more interpretable complexity/cost model.
- Utility model assumptions: EIG is computed for a single goal (identify the exact board) with a literal answerer; people may ask subgoal or constraint-checking questions, or trade off answerer costs. Pragmatic models (e.g., RSA with answering costs or ambiguity) could be informative comparisons.
- Generalization beyond the domain: Many symbols are Battleship-specific. While constructs like sets, map, and arithmetic are general, the amount of knowledge engineering required to port to new domains is unclear. No experiment demonstrates cross-domain transfer.
- Natural language interface: The model operates over program semantics. The mapping from NL to programs (and back) is left for future work, limiting immediate applicability to dialog systems.
- Human judgments of novelty/quality: The novelty examples are compelling but evaluated qualitatively. Human ratings of usefulness/naturalness would strengthen the claim of human-likeness.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a coherent probabilistic formulation and reasonable features. The empirical methodology is thoughtful but could benefit from stronger robustness checks (sampling diagnostics, sensitivity to grammar variations, and additional baselines such as EIG-only, PCFG-only, or context-similarity retrieval). The equivalence criterion for novelty (mutual information over answer distributions) is principled; specifying thresholds and reporting how many unique equivalence classes exist per context would improve transparency.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized. The main model components are clearly introduced. Some essential details (full grammar, thresholds for equivalence, hypothesis-space sizes per context, computational costs) are in the supplement; elevating a subset to the main text would help. A brief worked example of computing EIG for one question/context would aid reproducibility.

Originality
High. Generative modeling of question programs for human-like question asking is novel and distinct from prior work in QA/VQG and pragmatic modeling. The blend of program synthesis, Bayesian utility, and learned costs is creative.

Significance
Moderate to high. Conceptually, this is an important step toward richer active learning and human-like querying; practically, scaling to broader domains and integrating NL interfaces remain open. The approach could catalyze further work in compositional, goal-directed question generation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report importance sampling diagnostics (effective sample sizes, variance of Z estimates), sensitivity to sample size, and runtime.
- Include additional baselines: (a) EIG-only, (b) PCFG-only (complexity prior without EIG), (c) nearest-neighbor retrieval by context similarity, (d) bag-of-constructs log-linear without EIG.
- Explore alternative/learned cost functions (production-specific penalties, MDL) and/or pragmatic models that incorporate answerer effort and ambiguity.
- Provide human evaluations of generated questions (usefulness, clarity) and, if feasible, a closed-loop study where the model’s questions are answered to measure information gain in gameplay.
- Discuss portability by sketching how to instantiate the grammar in a second domain, or include a small-scale second-domain experiment.
- Clarify the novelty-equivalence threshold and report counts of equivalence classes per context.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, compositional, and principled model of question asking with clear ties to cognitive theory and AI.
- Solid empirical evidence that combining informativeness and complexity predicts human choices and enables creative generation.
- Opens a promising direction for active learning and dialog: questions as programs.

Arguments against acceptance
- Evaluation is limited in scale and domain; robustness and scalability are not yet demonstrated.
- Heavy reliance on a hand-crafted grammar and a complexity prior tied to that grammar.
- Lack of human evaluation for generated questions and of an NL interface limits immediate impact.

Overall, this is a thoughtful, well-executed paper with novel ideas and promising results; addressing evaluation robustness and generalization would further strengthen it.
