- Core contribution: formalizes questions as executable programs and reframes question generation as probabilistic program synthesis favoring informative (EIG) and concise queries.  
- Model: probabilistic generative model over a grammar-defined compositional program space with features for informativeness, complexity, and answer type.  
- Evaluation: tested on a Battleship-like human question dataset; predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, coherent questions.  
- Positioning: connects active learning and cognitive science, extending active learning from simple label queries to rich, structured queries.  
- Theoretical grounding: builds on "language of thought," formal semantics, Bayesian inference, and program induction.  
- Quality and clarity: technically sound, clearly written, detailed model/grammar description, appropriate experiments, and informative ablation studies.  
- Originality: novel application of program synthesis to goal-directed question generation, distinct from typical deep-learning QG approaches.  
- Significance: important for ML (advanced active learning agents) and cognitive science (first predictive model of open-ended human question asking).  
- Key cognitive finding: human question asking balances informativeness and complexity—EIG alone is insufficient.  
- Limitation — domain specificity: grammar is hand-engineered for Battleship; needs discussion on generalization and learnable grammar components.  
- Limitation — language interface: abstracts away natural language; should discuss parsing NL to programs and generating NL from programs.  
- Limitation — computational scalability: relies on importance sampling, excluded contexts indicate potential scalability/inference bottlenecks.  
- Suggestion — human evaluation: add small-scale human ratings to validate that generated novel questions are perceived as human-like.  
- Overall verdict: strong, original, and significant work with acknowledged limitations that point to clear future directions.
