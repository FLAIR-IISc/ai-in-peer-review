This paper introduces a novel computational model of human question asking, framing it as a problem of probabilistic program generation. The core idea is to represent questions not as natural language strings but as formal, compositional programs. The authors define a grammar that generates a space of possible questions (programs) and propose a log-linear model to define a probability distribution over this space. This model favors questions that balance high informativeness, measured by Expected Information Gain (EIG) over a set of possible world states, with low complexity, measured as a penalty for longer or more complex programs. The model is evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions in a "Battleship"-like game. The results show that the model can successfully predict the distribution of questions asked by humans in new game contexts and can also generate novel, contextually relevant questions that were not observed in the training data. This work provides a significant conceptual bridge between the fields of active learning, by extending simple queries to a rich, structured space, and cognitive science, by offering a formal "language of thought" model for a complex cognitive ability.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The probabilistic framework is well-founded, and the model components are clearly motivated. The use of EIG to formalize informativeness is standard and appropriate, while the use of the generative grammar's probability to formalize complexity is an elegant way to enforce a simplicity prior. The experimental validation is thorough and compelling. The leave-one-out cross-validation design is sound, and the ablation study provides strong evidence for the paper's central claim: that human question asking involves a trade-off between informativeness and complexity. The fact that the complexity-agnostic model performs extremely poorly (negative correlation with human data) is a particularly strong result, highlighting that simply maximizing EIG is insufficient. The qualitative results, where the model generates creative and useful questions, are impressive and effectively demonstrate the generative power of the approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The motivation for moving beyond existing active learning and dialogue systems is persuasively argued in the introduction. The authors do an excellent job of breaking down their model into understandable components: the program representation, the grammar, the probabilistic model, and the inference procedure. The examples provided throughout the text are helpful for building intuition. Figure 3, in particular, is very effective at showcasing the model's capabilities in a concrete way. The manuscript is well-organized and provides enough detail to understand the proposed method.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While program induction has been used for concept learning and semantic parsing for question answering, the application of a probabilistic program generation framework to model the *asking* of goal-directed questions is a novel and insightful contribution. It represents a significant leap from the simple label-request queries in most active learning literature and the less-interpretable, data-hungry models in VQG and dialogue systems. The synthesis of Bayesian reasoning for utility (EIG) with a symbolic, generative framework for the query space is a powerful and new combination for this problem.

**Significance:** This paper has the potential for high impact across multiple fields. For machine learning, it presents a new and promising paradigm for active learning and human-in-the-loop AI. The idea of an agent that can form and ask complex, compositional questions to resolve its uncertainty is a major step towards more intelligent and efficient interactive systems. For computational cognitive science, this work provides the first predictive, generative model of open-ended human question asking. It offers a computationally precise account of how people balance the drive for information with cognitive constraints, grounding abstract ideas about the "language of thought" in a concrete, testable model. The ideas presented are likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up research.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**
Despite the paper's many strengths, there are a few limitations that represent opportunities for future work.

1.  **Domain-Specificity of the Grammar:** The most significant limitation is the reliance on a hand-engineered grammar tailored to the "Battleship" domain. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the path towards generalization. Which components of the grammar are domain-general (e.g., logic, arithmetic) and which are specific? How might the domain-specific primitives be learned or acquired in a new environment? Addressing these questions would bolster the paper's claims about being a general framework.

2.  **Lack of Natural Language Interface:** The model operates entirely at the level of semantic representations ("language of thought"), abstracting away from the challenges of parsing natural language questions into programs or generating fluent text from them. While this is a reasonable simplification to focus on the core cognitive problem, it limits the immediate applicability for building practical AI dialogue systems. A brief discussion on how the proposed model could be integrated with existing work on semantic parsing would be a valuable addition.

3.  **Computational Cost:** The inference method relies on importance sampling with a large number of proposals (150,000) to approximate the intractable partition function. This suggests that training and inference may be computationally intensive. It would be helpful if the authors could provide some details on the computational resources required and comment on the scalability of this approach to domains with larger state spaces or more expressive grammars.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel, elegant, and powerful framework for understanding and modeling a fundamental aspect of intelligence. The strengths in originality, technical quality, and potential impact significantly outweigh the limitations, which are primarily avenues for future research. This work is a perfect fit for NIPS and is likely to be highly influential.
