This paper presents a novel framework for modeling human question asking by treating questions as formal programs. The core idea—that questions can be understood as procedures that, when executed, yield an answer—is compelling and offers a potentially powerful way to analyze and generate questions. The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of questions asked during a game, demonstrating its ability to predict human questions and even generate novel, human-like queries.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles an important problem. The connection to active learning is well-established, and the authors effectively highlight the limitations of existing approaches that struggle with the richness and creativity of human questioning. The introduction does a good job of setting the stage and explaining the motivation. The related work section is comprehensive and appropriately positions the current work within the broader literature on active learning, dialog systems, and question generation. The description of the game and dataset is clear, and the justification for focusing on this specific domain is reasonable.

The strengths of this work lie in its theoretical grounding and the elegance of the program-as-question formulation. The introduction of a formal grammar for generating questions allows for systematic exploration of the question space. The probabilistic model, while complex, is well-defined and allows for learning from data. The experimental results are promising; the model’s ability to predict human questions and generate novel ones is a significant achievement. The ablation studies demonstrating the importance of both informativeness and complexity are particularly valuable. 

However, there are also some weaknesses. The complexity of the model and the associated implementation details might be a barrier to reproducibility. While the authors acknowledge limitations regarding the domain-specificity of the grammar, a more thorough discussion of how the approach might be generalized to other contexts would be beneficial. The reliance on a specific implementation of the Bayesian ideal observer for calculating informativeness could also be seen as a limitation. The features used in the model, while well-justified, could potentially be expanded upon. For instance, incorporating features related to question similarity or pragmatic factors might further improve performance. The statement that the evaluation is done with leave-one-out cross validation is a bit misleading, as 15 contexts are used for training and the rest for testing.

Furthermore, the qualitative evaluation of generated questions is somewhat limited. While the examples provided are encouraging, a more systematic assessment of the quality and relevance of generated questions would strengthen the claims. It would be interesting to see a user study where participants are asked to evaluate the generated questions in the context of the game.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to modeling question asking.
*   Clear and well-written paper.
*   Promising experimental results demonstrating the model's ability to predict and generate human-like questions.
*   Thorough ablation studies highlighting the importance of key model components.
*   Addresses a challenging and important problem in AI and cognitive science.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Model complexity and potential reproducibility issues.
*   Limited discussion of generalizability to other domains.
*   Reliance on a specific implementation of informativeness calculation.
*   Somewhat limited qualitative evaluation of generated questions.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The idea of modeling questions as programs is innovative and provides a promising new direction for research in active learning and question generation. The experimental results are compelling, and the ablation studies provide valuable insights into the factors that influence human question asking. I recommend acceptance, with a request that the authors address the reproducibility concerns by providing more details about the implementation and potentially releasing the code. Addressing the concerns about generalizability and including a more thorough qualitative evaluation of the generated questions would also further strengthen the paper.
