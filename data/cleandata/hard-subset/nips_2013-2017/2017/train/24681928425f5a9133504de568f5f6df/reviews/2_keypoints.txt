- Task and domain: models human question-asking with K-ary answers in a "battleship" grid domain using a hand-designed PCFG and lambda-calculus question representations.  
- Model: PCFG provides a prior; question goodness is a linear combo of expected information gain, complexity (length / negative log PCFG probability), and answer type.  
- Data and evaluation: human questions from prior work were hand-coded into the grammar; lesioned models were compared via leave-one-out cross-validation.  
- Main quantitative finding: all factors (informativeness, complexity, answer type) contribute non-negligibly, with complexity being the dominant predictor.  
- Qualitative result: the model generates a series of novel, plausible questions.  
- Strengths: innovative integration of linguistic and cognitive theory, clear writing, and appropriate quantitative and qualitative evaluation.  
- Cognitive-science contribution: advances understanding of self-directed learning and explains why informativeness alone poorly predicts human question-asking.  
- Limitation — scalability: approach is computationally strained and demonstrated only in a small, low-dimensional domain, raising doubts about generalization to broader ML/NIPS-relevant problems.  
- Suggested next step: authors should demonstrate or argue for scenarios where the method scales or yields novel insights for a broader audience.  
- Minor comment 1: perform nested model comparisons on lesioned-model log-likelihoods to test statistical significance.  
- Minor comment 2: consider related formal semantics/pragmatics work on sequential question-asking (Questions under Discussion); two references provided.
