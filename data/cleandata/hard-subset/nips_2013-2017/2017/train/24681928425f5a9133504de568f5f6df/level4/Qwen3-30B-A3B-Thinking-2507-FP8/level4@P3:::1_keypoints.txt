- Presents a computational model of human question asking as program generation: questions are formal programs executed on possible worlds, modeled by a probabilistic grammar with features for EIG, complexity, answer type, and relevance, evaluated on a Battleship-like dataset.

- Strong technical soundness: principled probabilistic framework with cognitively grounded features and appropriate cross-validation experimental design.

- Clear contribution and exposition: grammar-based question representation is well explained and the controlled game environment is an effective testbed.

- Empirical validation: model predicts human question frequencies (Spearman ρ = 0.64), generates novel meaningful questions, and ablations show informativeness and complexity matter.

- Novelty: framing question asking as compositional program generation in a goal-directed, low-prior-knowledge domain is a notable contribution.

- Limited scope/significance: evaluation on a highly constrained Battleship game limits broader impact and claims about advancing active learning machines.

- Lack of comparative analysis: no comparisons to alternative methods (e.g., neural seq2seq or other program-synthesis approaches) to contextualize performance.

- Simplistic informativeness model: EIG uses a uniform prior over boards, which may not reflect human belief structures or biases.

- Computational/implementation concerns: heavy reliance on importance sampling (150k samples) and long gradient ascent (100k iterations) with unclear scalability and runtime discussion.

- Argument for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded approach with compositional representation and convincing in-domain empirical results.

- Argument against acceptance: constrained domain, domain-specific grammar limits generalizability, and lack of scaling evidence weakens community significance.

- Recommendation: borderline (weak accept); paper is technically sound but needs either broader evaluation or a clearer roadmap for scaling to realistic, complex question-asking domains.
