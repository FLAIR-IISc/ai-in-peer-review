- Models human question asking as formal programs that, when executed on a world state, produce multi-valued answers.  
- Applied to a partially observable “battleship” domain: identify hidden colored shapes by asking single-word answer questions to maximize information gain.  
- Uses a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) as a prior over lambda-calculus questions; complexity = negative log probability under the PCFG.  
- Question goodness is a linear combination of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity, and answer type.  
- Human data from a prior study were translated into the model’s grammar for evaluation.  
- Model variants compared with leave-one-out cross-validation; all factors matter, with complexity being particularly important.  
- Presents novel questions generated by the model and offers rigorous quantitative and qualitative analyses.  
- Advances cognitive and linguistic theory on question asking, contributing to self-directed learning and richer active learning paradigms.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written and clear.  
- Major limitation: unclear scalability and generalization beyond the small, computationally demanding battleship domain.  
- Authors should better articulate scenarios or applications of interest to the broader NIPS community.  
- Minor suggestion: use nested model comparisons (likelihood ratio tests) for statistical support of model differences.  
- Minor suggestion: consider related formal semantics/pragmatics work (e.g., Questions under Discussion); cite Ginzburg (1995) and Rojas-Esponda (2013).
