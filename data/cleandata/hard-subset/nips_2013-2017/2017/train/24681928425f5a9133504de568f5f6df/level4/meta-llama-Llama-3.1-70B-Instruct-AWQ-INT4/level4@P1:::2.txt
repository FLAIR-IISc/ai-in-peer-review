The authors examine human question asking where answers are K-ary, providing an innovative perspective that unifies modern linguistic and cognitive theory. They define a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) for a "battleship" domain, where there are hidden colored shapes in a partially observable grid. The task of the agent is to ask a question with a single word answer that provides as much information about the state of the board. The PCFG served as a prior over questions, which were defined as statements in lambda calculus. Question goodness was defined as a linear function of its informativeness (expected information gain), its complexity (in terms of its length or negative log probability under the PCFG with a uniform distribution over rewrite rules), and "answer type" (e.g., whether it provides a true/false or color as an answer).

The authors provide a clear and well-written paper, with a model that is appropriately evaluated from both quantitative and qualitative perspectives. The human data came from a previously published paper and was hand-coded into their grammar. They compared lesioned versions of the model via leave one out cross-validation and found that all factors provided a non-negligible contribution (although complexity was clearly the most important). Finally, they presented a series of novel questions.

From the perspective of a computational cognitive scientist, this paper advances the field of self-directed learning. Previous work found that question informativeness was a poor predictor of human question asking. This follow-up paper resolves part of why this is the case (it is secondary to complexity and may in part be due to the size of possible questions available to the model). Thus, the paper contributes to the cognitive science literature, which may be sufficient to justify its publication in NIPS.

However, there are serious limitations to their approach, which may limit its interest to the more general NIPS audience. Although it may be possible to automate the human question text to statements in some semantic logic, it is unclear how the approach could scale or extend to domains of interest to the broader community. The battleship domain is small with only a few different features, and their method is already computationally strained. That being said, the ideas are interesting enough (active learning with more rich questions) to be of interest, though it may be that the authors will need to provide a clear scenario of interest to the broader community where their technique is likely to be applicable and provide novel insights.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy for the reader to follow. The authors provide enough information for an expert reader to reproduce their results.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to modeling question asking in humans, treating questions as formal programs that, when executed on the state of the world, output an answer. This approach is closely related to formalizing question meaning as a partition over possible worlds, a notion used in previous studies in linguistics and psychology. However, the authors' contribution is unique in tackling question asking and providing a method for generating meaningful questions/programs from scratch.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by providing a predictive model of open-ended human question asking. The results have implications for active learning and self-directed learning, and the approach has the potential to foster novel research in these areas.

Minor comments:

1. It may not be revealing, but the authors can do a nested model comparison with the log-likelihoods of the lesioned models and get tests of statistical significance.
2. Moving forward, for sequential question asking, there are other models within the linguistics literature of question-asking in formal semantics and pragmatics. A lot of the work comes out of the Questions under Discussion literature. The authors may want to consider exploring these models in future work.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a novel approach to modeling question asking in humans.
* The paper advances the field of self-directed learning.
* The paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research.
* The approach has the potential to foster novel research in active learning and self-directed learning.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The approach may not scale or extend to domains of interest to the broader community.
* The method is already computationally strained in the battleship domain.
* The authors may need to provide a clear scenario of interest to the broader community where their technique is likely to be applicable and provide novel insights.
