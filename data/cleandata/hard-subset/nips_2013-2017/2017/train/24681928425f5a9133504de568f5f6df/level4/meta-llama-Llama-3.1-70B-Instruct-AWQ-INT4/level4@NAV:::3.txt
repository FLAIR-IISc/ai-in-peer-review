This paper proposes a cognitive model designed to predict the occurrence of specific questions within particular contexts, or belief states. The model defines the unnormalized log probability of a question as a weighted combination of factors including informativeness, complexity, answer type, and relevance to the belief state or world-independent logical facts. The authors apply this model to the Battleship domain, where the objective is to locate clusters of grid elements representing ships. The full model, incorporating all these features, outperformed its lesioned counterparts in predicting human data, as measured by log-likelihood. However, when considering correlation with human judgments, the model excluding expected information gain achieved comparable results.

From a technical standpoint, the paper appears sound. However, its impact is more pronounced in the realm of AI than cognitive science. While the authors claim that their model predicts human question-asking behavior and sheds light on the balance between informativeness and complexity, I remain unconvinced. The results from the Battleship domain suggest that humans are either slightly influenced by expected information gain or not at all, according to the log-likelihood and correlation results, respectively. These findings do not provide conclusive insights into how humans balance these factors in general.

The paper is well-written and clear, allowing for potential reproduction of the results. Nevertheless, it lacks references to prior work. Notably, the concept of modeling questions as programs that output answers when executed on a possible world's state is reminiscent of Hawkins' (2015) notion of a "goal" and Roberts' (1996) question-under-discussion framework. Hawkins' questioner model also employs expected information gain and trades it off with question complexity, albeit in a simpler domain.

I believe that this type of model, featuring a compositional prior over questions and selection based on expected information gain and other factors, holds promise for future cognitive and AI models. The primary contribution of this paper lies in demonstrating the feasibility of generating interesting, compositional questions for a non-trivial domain using this approach.
