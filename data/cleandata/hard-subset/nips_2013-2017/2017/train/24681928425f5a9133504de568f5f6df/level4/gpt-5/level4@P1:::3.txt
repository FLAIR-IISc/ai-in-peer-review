This paper proposes a computational model of open-ended question generation. Questions are represented as programs in a simple, compositional language (lambda calculus/LISP style) that, when executed on a hypothesized world state, return an answer. A probabilistic grammar defines the space of well-formed question-programs. A log-linear model assigns each question an energy based on features: informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (captured via −log probability under the grammar, roughly favoring shorter programs), answer type (Boolean/Number/Color/Location), and a relevance indicator (whether the program refers to the game state versus being a world-independent expression). The model is trained with importance sampling and optimized to match human question distributions.

The model is evaluated in the Battleship domain, where players see partially revealed 6×6 boards with three colored ships and ask one natural-language question to reduce uncertainty. Using leave-one-context-out evaluation, the full model outperforms lesioned variants that remove informativeness, complexity, or answer type features in terms of held-out log-likelihood. However, the EIG-agnostic model correlates about as well with human frequencies as the full model, with correlations overall limited by sparse data. The model can also sample novel, compositional questions that were not in the training set.

Relation to prior work: Representing questions as goals/programs over possible worlds connects closely to the Question-Under-Discussion (QUD) framework (Roberts, 1996) and to the questioner model of Hawkins et al. (2015), which also trades off EIG with question complexity (albeit in a simpler domain). The use of programs to represent meaning also relates to work in semantic parsing and program induction. The paper would benefit from explicitly situating its contribution with respect to these strands.

Quality: The approach is technically sound, with a clear specification of the grammar, features, training objective, and approximate inference. The leave-one-out setup and lesion analyses are appropriate. Given the level of detail, I could likely reproduce the results.

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized. The modeling assumptions and the Battleship task are clearly explained, and the figures/examples are helpful.

Originality: The central ideas—questions as programs, a compositional prior over questions, and selecting questions via EIG and simplicity—are not conceptually new, given QUD-based approaches and Hawkins (2015). The main novelty lies in scaling to a richer domain with a more expressive grammar and demonstrating question generation rather than only evaluation.

Significance: As an AI contribution, the paper is compelling: it shows that a compositional program-based prior, combined with simple features, can generate and rank useful, human-like questions in a nontrivial domain. As a cognitive science contribution, the evidence is less decisive. The paper claims to show how people balance informativeness and complexity, but the empirical picture is mixed: in this dataset, people appear to be influenced by EIG only weakly (if at all) when considering correlation with human frequencies, while the log-likelihood advantage of including EIG exists but, by the authors’ own bootstrap, has a nontrivial chance (19%) of not holding. Given the sparsity of human data, these results do not yet strongly constrain how people trade off these factors more generally.

Strengths
- Clear, compositional representation of questions as executable programs.
- Principled, probabilistic treatment that integrates informativeness, complexity, answer type, and relevance.
- Demonstrates generation of novel, human-like questions in a challenging domain.
- Lesion analyses show that complexity and other features matter for predicting human behavior.

Weaknesses
- Limited cognitive claim: the evidence that people optimize EIG beyond preferring simpler questions is weak or ambiguous in this dataset.
- Missing citations to closely related work (e.g., QUD; Hawkins 2015) that developed similar ideas about questions as goals and EIG–complexity trade-offs.
- Correlation analyses are noisy due to sparse data, making it hard to draw strong conclusions about the relative importance of features.

Suggestions for improvement
- Situate the contribution more explicitly with respect to QUD (Roberts, 1996) and Hawkins et al. (2015), clarifying what is new here (e.g., expressive grammar, program generation at scale, richer domain).
- Temper cognitive claims about how people balance informativeness and complexity, or provide additional analyses that more robustly isolate EIG’s contribution beyond complexity and type (e.g., by examining contexts where complexity is matched).
- Discuss limitations due to data sparsity and outline plans for collecting denser human data or additional domains to strengthen cognitive conclusions.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong AI contribution demonstrating compositional question generation in a nontrivial setting.
- Technically sound model with clear methodology and insightful lesion studies.
- Potentially useful component for broader AI systems that need to ask informative, structured questions.

Cons for acceptance
- Conceptual novelty is limited relative to prior QUD-based and EIG/complexity models.
- Cognitive claims are stronger than the evidence supports in this dataset.
- Missing key citations to prior work that framed questions as goal/program-like objects.

Overall, I view the main contribution as showing that a compositional, program-based model with simple features can generate interesting questions in a realistic domain. This is valuable for AI and may serve as a building block for future cognitive and interactive systems, even if the cognitive implications remain tentative.
