The authors investigate human question asking in scenarios with K-ary answers, focusing on a “battleship” domain where agents must identify hidden colored shapes on a partially observable grid. They define a hand-crafted Probabilistic Context-Free Grammar (PCFG) to model question structure, representing questions as statements in lambda calculus. Question quality is evaluated using a linear combination of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (measured by question length or negative log probability under the PCFG, assuming a uniform distribution over rewrite rules), and answer type (e.g., true/false vs. color identification). Human question data, originally from a prior publication, was manually encoded into their grammar. Through leave-one-out cross-validation of lesioned model versions, the authors demonstrate that each factor contributes significantly to question goodness, with complexity being the most influential. They also present a novel set of questions generated by their model.

Question asking represents a crucial area within linguistics and hypothesis testing, and this work offers an innovative perspective by integrating modern linguistic and cognitive theory to analyze human questioning behavior. This approach has the potential to stimulate new research in active learning, encouraging the development of models capable of formulating more sophisticated queries. The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, and the model is thoroughly evaluated through both quantitative and qualitative analyses.

From a computational cognitive science perspective, this paper represents a significant advance in self-directed learning. Previous research indicated that question informativeness alone is a weak predictor of human question selection. This work partially explains this finding by demonstrating that complexity plays a more dominant role and may account for discrepancies due to the limited scope of questions accessible to existing models. This contribution to the cognitive science literature arguably justifies publication in NIPS.

However, the approach’s limitations raise concerns about its broader applicability within the NIPS community. While automating the translation of human questions into semantic logic may be feasible, it remains unclear how the method could scale to domains of greater complexity and relevance to the wider machine learning audience. The battleship domain is inherently small, with a limited feature space, and the current method is already computationally demanding. Despite these limitations, the core ideas—specifically, active learning with richer question structures—are intriguing and warrant consideration. The authors might strengthen the paper by providing a concrete scenario of broader interest where their technique could yield novel insights.
