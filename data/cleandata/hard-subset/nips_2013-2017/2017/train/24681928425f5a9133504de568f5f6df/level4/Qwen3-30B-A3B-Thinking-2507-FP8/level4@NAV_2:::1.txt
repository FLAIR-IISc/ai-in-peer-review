

This paper proposes a computational model of human question asking by treating questions as executable programs that output answers when run on possible world states. The authors formalize questions as programs in a lambda calculus-based language, using a probabilistic generative model with features capturing informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (program length), answer type, and relevance to the game board. Evaluated on a Battleship game dataset of 605 human questions, the model successfully predicts human question frequencies (Spearman correlation 0.64) and generates novel human-like questions not present in training.

**Quality**: The technical approach is sound, with a well-structured probabilistic model (log-linear with four features) and rigorous evaluation via cross-validation. The model variants (lesioning informativeness/complexity/answer types) provide clear evidence for the necessity of each component. However, the task is highly constrained to a single game domain with unambiguous rules, limiting the scope of the claims about "human-like" questioning. The model's reliance on predefined domain knowledge (e.g., ship properties) is acknowledged but not sufficiently addressed for broader applicability.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally clear, with well-motivated formalism (Section 4), detailed grammar specifications (Table SI-1), and effective visualization of results (Figures 2-3). The connection between program execution and question meaning is elegantly explained, and the supplementary materials provide full technical details. Minor improvements could be made to clarify the EIG computation (Section 4.5) and the "filter" feature (f∅).

**Originality**: The core idea of modeling questions as executable programs is novel within the context of human question asking, extending Lake et al.'s (NIPS 2015) work on probabilistic programs. While active learning frameworks (e.g., [21]) and VQG systems (e.g., [16]) have been explored, this paper uniquely formalizes *goal-directed, context-sensitive question generation* using compositional program synthesis. It distinguishes itself from prior work by generating novel questions without large training datasets and capturing subtle context-dependent utility.

**Significance**: The work advances computational cognitive science by providing the first predictive model of open-ended human question asking, demonstrating how people balance informativeness and complexity. However, the narrow domain (Battleship) and lack of real-world applicability limit its significance for the broader NIPS community. While the formalism is elegant, the results do not clearly demonstrate a *demonstrable advance* over existing active learning methods for practical tasks (e.g., the model doesn't improve learning speed in actual machine learning pipelines). The generated questions (Figure 3) are clever but remain within the confines of the game's logical structure.

**Pro/Con Summary**:  
*Pro*: Novel formalism of questions as programs; strong quantitative prediction of human questions (0.64 correlation); rigorous model ablation; clear technical execution.  
*Con*: Limited to a single constrained game domain; domain-specific knowledge required; no evidence of generalization to real-world questioning or improved machine learning performance; minimal discussion of broader implications beyond cognitive science.  

The paper makes a technically rigorous contribution to cognitive modeling but falls short of the significance expected for NIPS, as it does not advance machine learning practice or address open problems beyond a narrow experimental setting. The model's utility is primarily theoretical, with limited potential for application in AI systems.
