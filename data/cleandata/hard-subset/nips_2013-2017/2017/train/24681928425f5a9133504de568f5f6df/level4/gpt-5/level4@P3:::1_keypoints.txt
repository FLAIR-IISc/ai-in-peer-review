- Proposal: Model questions as executable programs using a PCFG and a log-linear probability model with features for EIG, complexity, answer type, and relevance.
- Informativeness: Computes expected information gain via Bayesian updating over a Battleship-like hypothesis space, averaging entropy reduction across answers.
- Training/Evaluation: Trained on 605 questions in 18 contexts; evaluated with leave-one-context-out CV; full model outperforms ablations and can sample novel questions.
- Positioning: Bridges active learning, program-based semantics, and neural question generation, emphasizing goal-driven utility and compositionality in small-data settings.
- Strength — framing: Clear, principled representation linking compositional semantics, active learning, and cognitive modeling.
- Strength — interpretability: Interpretable features (EIG + complexity + answer-type) explain preference tradeoffs like avoiding high-EIG but complex questions.
- Strength — empirical: Ablations and cross-validation show both informativeness and complexity matter, with complexity particularly critical.
- Strength — generativity: Grammar enables sensible, sometimes clever novel question generation.
- Strength — theory: Operationalizes classic question theories (partitions, informativeness) in a concrete generative model.
- Weakness — domain specificity: Grammar includes many Battleship-specific primitives, limiting generality and risking overfitting.
- Weakness — inference: Likelihoods/gradients rely on importance sampling from a huge program space with unstated stability/variance; some rules dropped for computation.
- Weakness — evaluation metrics: Uses approximate log-likelihoods and rank correlations without human judgments for sampled question quality; creativity evaluation is qualitative.
- Weakness — baselines: Lacks comparisons to retrieval/template baselines, simple neural generators, or utility-only policies.
- Clarity issue: Technical details (full grammar, hypothesis sizes, costs) deferred to supplements; needs clearer discussion of computational cost and sensitivity.
- Originality: Representation not entirely new, but novel in applying executable-program questions with learned utility- and complexity-aware distribution; modeling choices are standard.
- Significance: Provides cognitive insight (tradeoff between informativeness and complexity) but limited ML impact due to narrow domain, manual grammar, and lack of broader demos.
- Suggestions — evaluation: Test additional domains to assess portability of grammar and features.
- Suggestions — baselines: Add retrieval/template baselines, conditional neural generators, and utility-only comparisons.
- Suggestions — human eval: Collect human ratings for informativeness and clarity of sampled questions.
- Suggestions — robustness: Report variance of importance sampling, learning curves, sensitivity to grammar choices and computational costs.
- Suggestions — language interface: Demonstrate mapping between natural language and programs with a basic semantic parser.
- Suggestions — utility variants: Explore alternative objectives (cost-sensitive utilities, answerer workload) beyond EIG.
- Pros for acceptance: Elegant interpretable formulation, empirical evidence for joint role of complexity and informativeness, and demonstration of compositional generalization.
- Cons for acceptance: Domain-specific grammar, weak baselines and no end-to-end language handling, reliance on approximate likelihoods and qualitative samples, and computational pruning.
- Recommendation: Borderline reject — thoughtful cognitive model with limited methodological novelty and empirical scope; needs broader evaluation, stronger baselines, and human validation.
