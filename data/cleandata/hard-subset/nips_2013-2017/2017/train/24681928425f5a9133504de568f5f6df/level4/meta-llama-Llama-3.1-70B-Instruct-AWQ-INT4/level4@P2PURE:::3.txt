The paper presents a cognitive model designed to predict when specific questions are asked in certain contexts, referred to as belief states. The unnormalized log probability of a question is calculated as the weighted sum of four key factors: informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (approximately, question length), answer type, and "relevance" (whether the question pertains to the belief state or world-independent logical facts). This model is applied to the Battleship domain, where the objective is to locate a number of ships (clusters of grid elements) within a grid. The full model, incorporating all the mentioned features, outperformed lesioned versions with fewer features in predicting held-out human data in terms of log-likelihood. However, in terms of correlation with human judgments, the model that did not account for expected information gain (EIG) performed similarly to the full model.

From a technical standpoint, the paper appears to be sound. Nevertheless, I believe the paper's contribution is more significant in the context of AI research rather than cognitive science. While the authors claim that "We find that our model predicts what question people will ask" and "from a CogSci standpoint, our results show how people balance informativeness and complexity," I am not convinced that these findings provide substantial insights into how humans balance these factors. In the Battleship domain, the results suggest that people are either marginally influenced by EIG (based on log-likelihood results, which have a 19% chance of being incorrect according to bootstrap estimates) or not at all (based on correlation results, which are also noisy due to sparse human data). I do not think these results allow for general conclusions about how humans balance these factors.

The paper is well-written and clear, making it possible for me to reproduce the results. However, the authors have omitted relevant prior work. One of the apparent innovations of the paper is the notion of modeling questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer. This concept is similar to the idea of a "goal" used in Hawkins 2015 ("Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers.") and is based on the notion of a question-under-discussion (QUD) introduced by Roberts 1996 ("Information structure in discourse: Towards an integrated formal theory of pragmatics."). The questioner model by Hawkins also employs expected information gain and trades it off with question complexity, albeit in a simpler domain.

I believe that this type of model, which features a compositional prior over questions and chooses based on EIG and other factors, holds significant potential as a component for cognitive and AI models, and I anticipate its increased use in the future. In my opinion, the primary contribution of this paper lies in demonstrating the feasibility of generating interesting, compositional questions for a non-trivial domain using this approach.
