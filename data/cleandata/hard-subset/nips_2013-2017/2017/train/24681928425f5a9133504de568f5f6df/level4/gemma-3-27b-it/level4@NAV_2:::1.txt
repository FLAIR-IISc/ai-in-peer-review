This paper introduces a computational framework for modeling human question asking, framing questions as formal programs that, when executed, yield answers. The authors evaluate their approach by modeling questions posed by humans playing a game of information seeking, demonstrating the ability to predict and even generate novel, human-like questions. A probabilistic model, employing a log-linear combination of features – including question informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance – is used to assess question utility.

The motivation, connecting active learning with richer, more human-like queries, is well-founded and aligns with broader goals in AI. The core idea of representing questions as programs, enabling a compositional approach, is also a reasonable one, drawing on concepts from linguistics and cognitive science. The paper situates itself within related work in active learning, dialog systems, and visual question generation, correctly pointing out the limitations of current approaches in capturing the nuances of human questioning. The experimental setup, utilizing a dataset of human questions from a game context, provides a solid foundation for evaluation.

A key strength of the work lies in demonstrating that both question informativeness and complexity are important factors in generating human-like questions. The ablation studies, comparing the full model to variants lacking specific features, provide valuable insight into the relative contributions of each component. Furthermore, the ability to generate novel questions that are not present in the training data demonstrates the model’s capacity for generalization, exceeding simple memorization of observed questions. 

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The task itself, while well-defined, is relatively constrained compared to open-ended real-world question asking. The reliance on a specifically designed grammar, while enabling a formal treatment of questions, may limit the model’s scalability and adaptability to more complex domains. While the paper relates to existing work, it doesn't delve deeply into the connection between its probabilistic framework and theories of Bayesian program learning, which could offer additional theoretical grounding. The discussion of the limitations is somewhat brief and could be expanded to acknowledge potential challenges in scaling this approach to less structured environments.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of program synthesis to model human question asking.
*   Demonstrated ability to predict and generate human-like questions in a game context.
*   Insightful ablation studies highlighting the importance of informativeness and complexity.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation of the model and experimental results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The task is relatively constrained and may not generalize well to open-ended scenarios.
*   The reliance on a pre-defined grammar could limit scalability.
*   Limited discussion of connections to broader theoretical frameworks in Bayesian program learning.
*   The core ideas, while solid, are relatively straightforward and may not represent a significant leap forward for the NIPS community.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the understanding of human question asking. While the task is constrained and the ideas are not groundbreaking, the approach is sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the analysis is insightful. However, the limited scope and the relatively incremental nature of the work suggest that it may be more appropriate for a conference with a more specialized focus on cognitive modeling or computational linguistics.
