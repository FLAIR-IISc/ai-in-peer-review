- Proposes modeling human question-asking as program generation, representing questions as compositional LISP-like programs executed on possible world states.  
- Defines a probabilistic log-linear model over programs (generated by a CFG) that balances informativeness (EIG) and structural simplicity.  
- Evaluated on human questions from a "Battleship"-style information-seeking game, showing qualitative and quantitative matches.  
- Strength: novel and principled formulation that moves beyond simple active-learning queries and could inspire HCI and dialogue work.  
- Strength: technically sound with thorough evaluation and ablations showing both EIG and complexity are important; generates sensible novel questions.  
- Strength: clear, well-written presentation explaining core concepts effectively.  
- Weakness: relies on a highly constrained, unambiguous game domain, raising scalability and generalizability concerns for real-world settings.  
- Weakness: core technical components (PCFGs, log-linear models, EIG) are established methods; novelty is in their combination/application rather than new methodology.  
- Weakness: heavy reliance on a hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar limits applicability and autonomy for new domains.  
- Overall: elegant, thought-provoking framework but its impact is limited by domain simplicity and required hand-engineering, leaving generalization to complex real-world domains unproven.
