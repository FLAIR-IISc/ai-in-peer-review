

The paper introduces a cognitive model designed to predict question selection across different contexts (belief states). Within this framework, the unnormalized log probability of a question is computed as a weighted sum of four components: informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (approximate question length), answer type, and "relevance" (whether the question pertains to the belief state rather than world-independent facts). This model is evaluated in the Battleship domain, where the objective is to infer ship locations within a grid. The full model incorporating all features demonstrates superior predictive performance on held-out human data (measured by log-likelihood) compared to ablated variants omitting specific features. However, in terms of correlation with human judgments, the model excluding EIG performs comparably to the full model.  

The work is technically robust. It is more strongly positioned as an AI contribution than a cognitive science advancement. While the paper claims "our model predicts what questions people ask" and "results show how people balance informativeness and complexity," the evidence does not convincingly support these cognitive conclusions. In Battleship, human question selection appears either minimally influenced by EIG (per log-likelihood results, with bootstrap estimates suggesting a 19% chance of error) or unaffected (per correlation results, which are noisy due to sparse human data). These findings do not substantiate generalizable insights into how people balance these factors.  

The paper is well-written and reproducible. However, it omits critical prior work. The core innovation—"modeling questions as programs that execute on possible worlds to produce answers"—directly corresponds to Hawkins (2015)'s "goal" concept and Roberts (1996)'s "question-under-discussion" (QUD). Hawkins' questioner model similarly employs EIG and question complexity but in a simpler domain.  

This compositional question-generation approach—using a prior over questions to select based on EIG and other factors—is a meaningful contribution for both cognitive and AI modeling. The paper's primary value lies in demonstrating its viability for generating non-trivial compositional questions within a complex domain.
