- Proposes a cognitive model that scores questions by a weighted sum of four factors: informativeness (EIG), complexity (approx. length), answer type, and relevance (belief-state vs. world-independent facts).
- Models questions as programs that execute on possible worlds to produce answers.
- Applies the model to the Battleship domain (finding ship clusters on a grid).
- Full model outperforms lesioned variants on held-out human data by log-likelihood.
- However, a model without EIG performs similarly to the full model on correlation with human judgments.
- Log-likelihood advantage for EIG is uncertain (bootstrap suggests a ~19% chance the result is incorrect); human data are sparse/noisy.
- Paper is technically sound, well-written, and reproducible.
- Claims about cognitive insights (how humans balance informativeness and complexity) are not strongly supported by the noisy/sparse results.
- Authors omitted relevant prior work (e.g., Hawkins 2015 and Roberts 1996 / QUD), which use similar ideas of questions-as-goals and trading EIG vs. complexity.
- Main contribution is demonstrating feasibility of generating compositional, interesting questions in a non-trivial domain, with strong potential for AI applications.
