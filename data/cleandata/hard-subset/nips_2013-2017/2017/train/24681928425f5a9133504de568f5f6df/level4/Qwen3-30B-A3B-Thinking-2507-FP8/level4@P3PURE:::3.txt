

The paper introduces a cognitive model for predicting question selection in specific contexts (belief states), where the unnormalized log probability of a question is defined as a weighted sum of informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (question length), answer type, and "relevance" (whether the question refers to the belief state rather than world-independent facts). This model is evaluated in the Battleship domain, where the task is inferring ship locations within a grid. The full model outperforms lesioned variants in predicting held-out human data (log-likelihood), but models excluding EIG correlate nearly as well with human judgments. 

The paper is technically sound and well-written, with clear exposition enabling reproducibility. However, its contribution leans more toward AI than cognitive science. While the authors claim the model "predicts what questions people will ask" and demonstrates "how people balance informativeness and complexity," the evidence does not support this cognitive interpretation. The Battleship domain yields conflicting conclusions: log-likelihood results suggest EIG has a small influence (with bootstrap estimates indicating a 19% probability that the observed difference is due to chance), while correlation metrics (compromised by sparse human data) imply no EIG influence. These results do not establish how people balance these factors in general. 

The paper omits critical prior work. Its claimed innovation—modeling questions as programs that execute on possible worlds to generate answers—aligns with Hawkins (2015) ("Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers?"), which uses the concept of a "goal" and incorporates EIG with question complexity. This prior work also employs a similar trade-off but in a simpler domain. The notion of question-under-discussion (QUD) from Roberts (1996) further contextualizes the model. 

Despite these gaps, the approach—using a compositional prior over questions with EIG and complexity trade-offs—is a promising framework for cognitive and AI systems. The paper’s primary contribution lies in demonstrating that such a model can generate meaningful compositional questions for a non-trivial domain. I expect this methodology to see broader adoption in future work.
