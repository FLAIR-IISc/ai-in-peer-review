The authors investigate human question asking in scenarios with K-ary answers, focusing on a “battleship” domain featuring hidden, colored shapes within a partially observable grid. They introduce a manually defined Probabilistic Context-Free Grammar (PCFG) to model questions, represented as statements in lambda calculus. Question quality is assessed using a combined metric incorporating information gain, complexity (based on length and PCFG generation probability), and answer type. The model leverages a previously published human dataset, hand-coded to align with their grammar. Through leave-one-out cross-validation of lesioned model variants, the authors demonstrate that each factor – informativeness, complexity, and answer type – contributes meaningfully to question selection, with complexity proving most influential. The work concludes with the presentation of novel questions generated by the model.

This research offers a valuable and innovative perspective, bridging linguistic and cognitive theory to examine human question asking. It has the potential to stimulate new research directions in active learning, particularly regarding the development of models capable of formulating more sophisticated queries. The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and presents a comprehensive evaluation encompassing both quantitative and qualitative analyses. 

From a computational cognitive science standpoint, this work represents a significant advancement in self-directed learning. Prior research indicated a weak correlation between question informativeness and human question-asking behavior; this paper sheds light on this discrepancy, suggesting that complexity plays a dominant role and may be linked to the size of the question space. This contribution to the cognitive science literature is substantial and, in my assessment, sufficient to warrant publication at NIPS.

However, the approach’s scalability and generalizability raise concerns that may limit its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. While translating human questions into a formal semantic logic may be feasible, extending this methodology to domains of wider interest remains unclear. The current “battleship” domain is relatively small and computationally demanding, suggesting potential limitations in scaling the method. Nevertheless, the core ideas – particularly the exploration of active learning with richer question representations – are intriguing. To broaden its impact, the authors could benefit from outlining a compelling, real-world application scenario where their technique could yield novel insights. 

Minor comments:

1) A nested model comparison using the log-likelihoods (LLs) of the lesioned models could provide statistical significance tests for the observed differences.
2) For future work involving sequential question asking, the authors might consider relevant literature from linguistics, specifically formal semantics and pragmatics, such as the “Questions under Discussion” framework. Relevant references include:

Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. Linguistics and Philosophy, 18(5), 459-527.
Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17, 131-139.
