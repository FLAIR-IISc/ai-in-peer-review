- Paper models human question-asking with K-ary answers in a “battleship” domain using a hand-specified PCFG over lambda-calculus questions and a linear utility combining informativeness, complexity, and answer type.  
- Questions’ informativeness is measured by expected information gain; complexity is length or negative log PCFG probability; answer type distinguishes e.g. yes/no vs. color.  
- Human question data were hand-coded into the grammar and evaluated with leave-one-out cross-validation and lesioned-model comparisons.  
- Results: all factors contribute non-negligibly, with complexity being the strongest predictor of human question choices.  
- The paper is clear, well-written, and offers an innovative unification of linguistic and cognitive theories with potential to inspire richer active learning models.  
- Advances cognitive science of self-directed learning by explaining why informativeness alone poorly predicts human questions—complexity and question-space size matter.  
- Major limitation: scalability and generality are unclear—the small battleship domain and computational cost may not extend to broader, real-world domains.  
- To increase impact, authors should articulate a concrete, larger-scale scenario where the method applies and yields novel insights.  
- Minor suggestion: perform nested model comparisons using lesioned-model log-likelihoods to test statistical significance.  
- Minor pointer: for sequential question-asking, consider literature in formal semantics/pragmatics (e.g., Ginzburg 1995; Rojas-Esponda 2013).
