- Task: model human question asking in a “battleship” domain where agents ask single-word, K-ary answers about hidden colored shapes on a partially observable grid.  
- Representation: authors hand-define a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) over questions, encoded as lambda-calculus statements; human questions were hand-coded into this grammar.  
- Scoring: question goodness is a linear combination of expected information gain, question complexity (length or negative log prob under PCFG with uniform rewrite rules), and answer type (e.g., boolean vs. color).  
- Evaluation: lesioned-model comparisons via leave-one-out cross-validation show all factors matter, with complexity being the dominant predictor.  
- Results: model fits both quantitatively and qualitatively and generates a set of novel, interesting questions.  
- Contribution: unifies linguistic and cognitive theories to study question asking and advances self-directed learning / active learning literature.  
- Insight: explains prior findings that informativeness alone poorly predicts human questions—complexity can dominate and dataset/question-space size matters.  
- Clarity: paper is very clear and well-written, with appropriate evaluations.  
- Limitation: domain is small and hand-crafted; scalability and applicability to larger, real-world domains are unclear and computationally strained.  
- Recommendation: authors should better motivate a broader NIPS-relevant scenario where the technique provides novel insights.  
- Minor suggestion: perform nested model comparisons using log-likelihoods to test statistical significance of lesions.  
- Literature note: for sequential question-asking, cite Questions Under Discussion / formal semantics/pragmatics work (e.g., Ginzburg 1995; Rojas-Esponda 2013).
