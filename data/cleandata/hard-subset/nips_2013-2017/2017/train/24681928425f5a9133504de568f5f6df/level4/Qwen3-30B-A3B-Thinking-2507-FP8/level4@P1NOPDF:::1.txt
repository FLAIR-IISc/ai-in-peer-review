

This paper models user questions in a Battleship-like game, replacing direct queries about individual squares with questions over an artificial language featuring compositional logical semantics (Montagovian). The authors employ a log-linear model trained on four simple features: expected information gain (informativeness), answer type (Boolean or numerical), and whether the question references board features.  

While the motivation for active learning is reasonable, the core contributions are unremarkable. The task is trivially simple, highly constrained, and unambiguous—offering no meaningful challenge to the NIPS community. The proposed approach relies on basic statistical modeling of features that lack novelty or depth. The artificial language framework, though technically precise, does not advance beyond straightforward applications of existing active learning principles. Crucially, the paper fails to demonstrate why this specific setup—where questions are logically well-defined and the environment is fully observable—provides insights relevant to broader machine learning challenges. The work lacks the theoretical or empirical substance expected at NIPS, and its simplicity suggests minimal potential for generalization to real-world problems. I find this work uninteresting for the NIPS community.
