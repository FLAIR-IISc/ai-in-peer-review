- Presents a computational model that frames human questions as compositional programs executing on possible worlds within a probabilistic framework.
- Formalizes question generation as program synthesis, drawing on linguistic compositionality and cognitive science principles.
- Evaluated on a human question dataset from a Battleship-style game where players infer hidden ship configurations via single-word-answer questions.
- Demonstrates that human question selection trades off informativeness (Expected Information Gain) and complexity (program length/grammar probability).
- Quantitative leave-one-out cross-validation shows all model features matter, with complexity especially important for predicting human behavior.
- Qualitatively generates novel, human-like questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") beyond the training data.
- Clear theoretical foundation and exceptionally well-written, with accessible presentation of complex concepts.
- Uses a lambda-calculus formalization and grammar-based approach to capture question compositionality and open-endedness.
- Strong evaluation design combining quantitative prediction of question frequencies and qualitative generation.
- Main limitation: narrow, domain-specific implementation requiring substantial domain knowledge engineering.
- Scalability concerns due to high computational cost (≈150,000 samples per iteration) for larger domains.
- Significant contribution to computational cognitive science as the first predictive model of open-ended human question asking.
- Broader ML community may question generalizability to complex active learning scenarios; authors should articulate concrete future applications.
- Minor suggestions: use nested model comparisons on log-likelihood differences rather than only bootstrapping, and engage more with Questions under Discussion literature (e.g., Ginzburg; Rojas-Esponda).
