This paper investigates reproducing user questions in a Battleship game, framing the questioning process not as individual square queries, but as inquiries expressed through an artificial language with compositional, Montagovian semantics. The authors employ a log-linear model utilizing four features: expected information gain (representing informativeness), answer type (Boolean or numerical), and indicators of board-related content within the question.

While the motivation, viewed through the lens of active learning, is sensible, the presented ideas largely feel intuitive and lack substantial novelty. The task itself is highly constrained, logically unambiguous, and relatively simple. Consequently, the contributions do not appear significant enough to warrant interest from the NeurIPS community. 

Specifically, the core concept of leveraging information gain and question type in an active learning scenario, while valid, doesn’t present a surprising or particularly sophisticated approach. The artificial language, although utilizing a formal semantic framework, doesn't appear to introduce complexities that necessitate the proposed modeling choices. The simplicity of the Battleship domain, with its clear and defined rules, diminishes the generalizability of the findings to more complex real-world active learning problems. 

The experimental evaluation does not sufficiently demonstrate a compelling advantage over simpler baselines or existing active learning strategies. Further, the paper lacks a thorough discussion of related work, failing to adequately position the contribution within the broader active learning landscape. The limited scope and lack of substantial novelty, coupled with the relatively simple experimental setup, lead me to believe this work is unlikely to be of broad interest to the NeurIPS audience.
