- Novel framing: Questions are modeled as executable programs, connecting to formal linguistics and the “language of thought” hypothesis.  
- Task/domain: Evaluated in a Battleship-style game where agents infer hidden colored shapes on a grid.  
- Model: Probabilistic model uses a custom PCFG to generate and score questions by informativeness (EIG), complexity (grammar probability), and answer type.  
- Evaluation: Predicts human question choices and generates novel, human-like questions; combines quantitative and qualitative analyses.  
- Cognitive insight: Shows that incorporating complexity improves prediction of human question behavior, addressing prior mixed findings about informativeness alone.  
- Technical quality: Model is well-defined, mathematically grounded, and clearly presented.  
- Strengths summary: Original approach, solid evaluation, and contribution to active learning and computational cognitive science.  
- Main limitation: Domain specificity—the implementation is tied to a small Battleship domain, raising scalability concerns.  
- Practical bottleneck: Reliance on a hand-crafted grammar limits applicability to new or larger domains.  
- Open question: Unclear how well program-based question generation and informativeness/complexity weighting transfer to broader, real-world settings.  
- Recommendation: Accept, contingent on authors addressing scalability and outlining paths to generalization.  
- Minor suggestions: Perform nested model comparisons (likelihood ratio tests) and cite/address formal semantics/pragmatics literature (e.g., Questions under Discussion).
