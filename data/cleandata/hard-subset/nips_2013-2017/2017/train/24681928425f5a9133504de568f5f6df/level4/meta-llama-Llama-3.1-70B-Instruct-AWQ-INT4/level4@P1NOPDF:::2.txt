The authors investigate human question asking in a scenario where answers are K-ary, focusing on a "battleship" domain with hidden colored shapes in a partially observable grid. They define a PCFG by hand for this domain, where some tiles are revealed to contain part of a ship of a specific color or are empty. The agent's task is to ask a question with a single word answer that provides the most information about the state of the board. The PCFG serves as a prior over questions, which are defined as statements in lambda calculus. The authors evaluate question goodness using a linear function of its informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (in terms of its length or negative log probability under the PCFG with a uniform distribution over rewrite rules), and "answer type" (e.g., whether it provides a true/false or color as an answer).

The human data, obtained from a previously published paper, was hand-coded into their grammar. The authors compare lesioned versions of the model using leave-one-out cross-validation and find that all factors contribute non-negligibly to the model's performance, although complexity is clearly the most important factor. They also present a series of novel questions.

Question-asking is a crucial domain within linguistics and hypothesis testing, and the authors provide an innovative perspective that unifies modern linguistic and cognitive theory to examine how people ask questions. This has the potential to foster novel research in the active learning literature, enabling models to ask more sophisticated questions. The paper is extremely clear and well-written, and the model is appropriately evaluated from both quantitative and qualitative perspectives.

From the perspective of a computational cognitive scientist, this paper advances the field of self-directed learning. Previous work found that question informativeness was a poor predictor of human question asking, and this follow-up paper resolves part of why this is the case (it is secondary to complexity and may be due in part to the size of possible questions available to the model). Thus, the paper contributes to the cognitive science literature, which may be sufficient to justify its publication in NIPS.

However, there are serious limitations to their approach, which may limit its interest to the more general NIPS audience. Although it may be possible to automate human question text into statements in some semantic logic, it is unclear how the approach could scale or extend to domains of interest to the broader community. The battleship domain is small, with only a few different features, and their method is already computationally strained. Nevertheless, the ideas are interesting enough (active learning with more rich questions) to be of interest, and the authors may need to provide a clear scenario of interest to the broader community where their technique is likely to be applicable and provide novel insights.

Minor comments:

1. A nested model comparison with the log-likelihoods of the lesioned models could potentially provide tests of statistical significance, which may be worth exploring.
2. For sequential question asking, there are other models within the linguistics literature of question-asking in formal semantics and pragmatics, such as the Questions under Discussion literature. Some relevant references include:
Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. Linguistics and Philosophy, 18(5), 459-527.
Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17, 131-139.
