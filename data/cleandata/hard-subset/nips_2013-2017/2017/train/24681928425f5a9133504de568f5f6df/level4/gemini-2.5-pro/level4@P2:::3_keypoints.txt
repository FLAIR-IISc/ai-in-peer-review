- Models human question asking by representing questions as executable programs that partition possible worlds; uses features: EIG (informativeness), complexity, answer type, and relevance.
- Evaluated on a Battleship-style human-question dataset; full model better fits held-out data than ablations and can generate novel, human-like questions.
- Strength — Significance/Originality: Shows viability of program generation for open-ended, compositional question asking, beyond simple active-learning or template-based systems.
- Strength — Technical Quality: Probabilistic grammar over a lambda-calculus language elegantly captures compositionality and the productivity of human questions.
- Strength — Clarity: Well-written, clearly organized, and likely reproducible by an expert reader.
- Weakness — Prior Work: Insufficiently situates the formalism relative to QUD literature (e.g., Roberts 1996) and prior computational models (e.g., Hawkins et al. 2015) that trade off EIG and complexity.
- Weakness — Cognitive Claims: Evidence for the role of informativeness (EIG) is ambiguous — complexity seems to be a stronger predictor and bootstrap estimates weaken the EIG advantage.
- Summary: A novel, promising proof-of-concept for question-as-program modeling that advances AI/cognitive modeling of inquiry, though cognitive claims should be more cautiously stated.
