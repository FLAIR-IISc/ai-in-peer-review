- Paper studies generating user questions in Battleship using an artificial compositional (Montagovian) language instead of natural queries.  
- Uses a log-linear model with four simple features: expected information gain, answer type (Boolean/numerical), and board relevance.  
- Motivation framed as active learning is reasonable.  
- Task and artificial language are very simple, constrained, and logically unambiguous.  
- Ideas feel obvious and lack substantial novelty.  
- Core contribution is mapping game state to compositional-language questions, but this is narrowly scoped.  
- Simplicity of game and language limits generalizability and impact on real-world NLP/active learning.  
- Artificial language avoids ambiguity and does not address natural language complexities.  
- Log-linear model learning to ask reasonable questions is unsurprising and not a major advancement.  
- Features used are intuitive and commonly employed; their combination offers no new insights.  
- Experimental evaluation lacks comparisons to stronger or recent baselines beyond uniform random and greedy IG.  
- Single-game evaluation limits the strength of conclusions; variations or other tasks are needed.  
- Overall judged technically sound but lacking novelty and broad NIPS relevance; recommendation: reject.
