- Introduces a cognitive model predicting likelihood of specific questions from belief states using a linear combination of features (EIG, complexity, answer type, relevance).  
- Features: informativeness via Expected Information Gain (EIG), complexity approximated by question length, answer type, and relevance (belief-state vs. world-independent).  
- Evaluated in the Battleship game domain for locating ships on a grid.  
- Full model outperforms ablated versions on held-out human data by log-likelihood.  
- For correlation with human judgments, a model excluding EIG performs comparably to the full model.  
- Methodology is technically sound and described with sufficient detail for reproducibility.  
- Paper reads more as an AI contribution than a cognitive-science one; the cognitive claim about balancing informativeness and complexity is not compelling.  
- Influence of EIG on human question-asking appears tenuous: log-likelihood effect is minor and bootstrap analysis gives a 19% chance the finding is spurious.  
- Correlation-based results are noisy due to limited human data, making firm conclusions about human trade-offs difficult.  
- Manuscript is clearly written and well-organized.  
- Overlooks prior literature: the "questions as programs" framing parallels Hawkins (2015) and Roberts (1996) QUD; Hawkins also trades off EIG and complexity.  
- Despite critiques, the combined compositional + EIG-based approach is promising and successfully scaled to generate meaningful, compositionally structured questions in a non-trivial domain.
