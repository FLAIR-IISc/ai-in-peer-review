This paper presents a computational framework for modeling human question asking, with the goal of predicting which questions people will ask in a given context. The central idea is to represent questions as programs in a compositional language. When executed on a possible world state, a program returns an answer, thereby partitioning the space of possibilities. The authors develop a probabilistic model over this space of programs, where the unnormalized log probability of a question is a weighted sum of several features: informativeness (Expected Information Gain, EIG), complexity (related to program length), answer type (e.g., boolean, number), and relevance.

The model is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-style game, where the goal is to identify the location of hidden ships on a grid. The primary findings are that a model incorporating all features provides a better fit to held-out human data (in terms of log-likelihood) than ablated versions that lack key features. The model is also capable of generating novel, human-like questions not observed in the training set.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The main contribution of this work is demonstrating the viability of the program generation approach for modeling open-ended question asking in a non-trivial, compositional domain. This is a significant step beyond active learning systems that are limited to simple label requests or dialogue systems that rely on canned templates. The framework is powerful and likely to be influential for future AI and cognitive science research on inquiry and information search.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed model is technically sound. The use of a probabilistic grammar over a lambda calculus-based language is an elegant way to define a rich, compositional space of questions. This generative capacity allows the model to account for the productivity of human question asking, where novel questions are frequently generated for new contexts—a key finding that models based on reusing past questions cannot easily explain.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors effectively explain the different components of their model, from the grammar and program features to the optimization procedure. The examples provided are helpful for understanding how questions are translated into programs. The work should be reproducible by an expert reader.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Understated Connection to Prior Work:** The presentation of the core idea—modeling questions as programs that partition a set of possible worlds—could be better situated within existing literature. This formalism is closely related to the semantic definition of a question-under-discussion (QUD) in linguistics (e.g., Roberts, 1996, "Information structure in discourse") and has been applied in prior computational cognitive science work. For instance, Hawkins et al. (2015, "Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers") also proposed a model of question asking that trades off EIG with question complexity, albeit in a simpler domain. A discussion of how the current work builds upon or differs from these precedents would strengthen the paper's claims of originality and provide valuable context for the reader.

2.  **Ambiguous Support for Cognitive Claims:** The claims regarding the model's contributions to cognitive science seem somewhat overstated given the results. The paper suggests it shows "how people balance informativeness and complexity," but the evidence for the role of informativeness (EIG) is ambiguous. While the log-likelihood metric favors the full model, the authors report that the Spearman correlation with human question frequencies is nearly identical for the full model (0.64) and an information-agnostic model (0.65), and far worse for the complexity-agnostic model (-0.36). This suggests that complexity is a much stronger predictor of human behavior than EIG in this analysis. Furthermore, the authors note that the log-likelihood advantage over the information-agnostic model has a 19% chance of not holding, based on bootstrap estimates. This mixed evidence makes it difficult to draw strong conclusions about the specific trade-offs human question-askers are making in this task. The paper would be stronger if it acknowledged this ambiguity and discussed its implications more thoroughly.

**Summary and Recommendation**

This paper presents a novel and promising approach for modeling and generating complex, open-ended questions. The core idea of treating question asking as program synthesis is powerful, and the demonstration of this approach in a non-trivial domain is a valuable contribution to the field. While the cognitive science claims about how humans trade off informativeness and complexity are not fully supported by the quantitative results, the paper succeeds as a proof-of-concept for a new class of active learning and AI systems. I expect this work to be of significant interest to the NIPS community and to inspire future research in this direction.
