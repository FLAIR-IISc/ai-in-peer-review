

This paper introduces a novel computational framework modeling human question-asking as program generation, treating questions as executable programs that output answers when evaluated on possible world states. The authors evaluate their approach on a human question-asking dataset from a Battleship-like game, where players infer hidden ship configurations through single-word-answer questions. Key innovations include: (1) modeling questions as lambda-calculus programs to capture compositionality (e.g., combining predicates like "size" and "color"), (2) defining question quality via a log-linear model balancing informativeness (Expected Information Gain), complexity (grammar-based length), answer type, and relevance, and (3) demonstrating that human-like questions emerge from optimizing this trade-off. The model outperforms variants that omit any key feature (e.g., complexity-agnostic models fail significantly), and generates novel, contextually relevant questions not in the training data.

**Strengths**  
*Originality & Significance*: The work bridges cognitive science and machine learning by formalizing human question-asking as program synthesis—a significant departure from prior active learning (e.g., Settles, 2012) or neural question-answering (e.g., Mostafazadeh et al., 2016). It resolves a key cognitive paradox: previous work (Hawkins et al., 2015) found question informativeness poorly predicted human behavior, but this paper shows complexity (not just informativeness) is critical. This advances self-directed learning theory (Gureckis & Markant, 2014) and provides the first predictive model for open-ended human question-asking, addressing a gap in NIPS’ cognitive science literature. The approach could inspire more human-like active learning in AI, aligning with NIPS’ focus on foundational ML advances.  
*Quality & Clarity*: The model is technically sound, with rigorous evaluation via leave-one-out cross-validation (log-likelihood comparisons), qualitative novelty generation (Fig. 3), and clear justification of features (e.g., EIG computation via Bayesian updating). The paper is exceptionally well-written, with precise definitions, intuitive examples (e.g., "(size Blue)" for "How long is the blue ship?"), and thorough ablation studies. The grammar design (SI-1) and feature engineering (e.g., "filter" feature for game relevance) demonstrate careful attention to cognitive plausibility.

**Weaknesses**  
*Generalizability*: The model’s reliance on manual grammar engineering for a narrow Battleship domain (with fixed ship types, colors, and grid size) limits its scalability. While the authors note domain-general constructs (e.g., logical operators), extending to richer domains (e.g., natural language, real-world tasks) would require significant knowledge engineering, as highlighted by the computational strain in the current setup. This restricts broader NIPS relevance—most audience members would seek approaches applicable to vision, NLP, or robotics, not a constrained game.  
*Methodological Gap*: The authors acknowledge that translating natural language to lambda-calculus programs is "hand-coded" (Sec. 3), but do not address how this step would scale. A clearer path to end-to-end learning (e.g., integrating with semantic parsers like Wang et al., 2015) would strengthen the case for wider adoption. The paper also omits discussion of how the model handles sequential question-asking (e.g., building on prior answers), a common active learning scenario.

**Recommendation**  
The paper makes a meaningful contribution to cognitive science and active learning, with strong technical execution. Its core insight—that complexity balances informativeness in human question-asking—deserves publication in NIPS for its theoretical advancement. However, the narrow domain and manual grammar engineering temper its immediate impact for the broader ML community. To maximize relevance, the authors should:  
1) Explicitly discuss extension pathways to larger domains (e.g., "For real-world applications, the grammar could be bootstrapped from task-specific ontologies, as in Lake et al., 2015"),  
2) Address the natural language-to-program translation bottleneck in the limitations section.  

*Minor suggestions*:  
- Include statistical tests for log-likelihood differences (e.g., bootstrap comparisons) to strengthen model comparisons.  
- Reference formal semantics work on question-asking (e.g., Ginzburg, 1995; Rojas-Esponda, 2013) to position the linguistic contribution more fully.

**Summary**  
This is a high-quality, innovative paper that advances cognitive science with a mathematically grounded model of human question-asking. While the Battleship domain limits immediate scalability, the framework’s core principles (compositionality, complexity-informativeness trade-off) offer a promising foundation for future work. The paper’s clarity, rigorous evaluation, and theoretical significance align well with NIPS’ mission, making it suitable for acceptance pending minor revisions addressing generalizability.
