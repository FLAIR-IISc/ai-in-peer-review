This paper presents a cognitive model intended to predict which questions people ask in specific contexts (i.e., given their belief states). In this model, the unnormalized log probability of a question is a weighted sum of four factors: informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (approximated by question length), answer type, and relevance (whether the question pertains to the current belief state or to world-independent logical facts). The model is evaluated in the Battleship domain, where the goal is to identify the locations of ships on a grid.

The authors demonstrate that their full model achieves a better log-likelihood on held-out human data than lesioned versions that omit one or more features. However, when evaluated on its correlation with human judgments, a model that excludes expected information gain (EIG) performs comparably to the full model.

The paper is generally technically sound, well-written, and clear, and the results appear to be reproducible. The core idea of using a compositional prior over questions and selecting them based on EIG and other factors is a promising direction for both AI and cognitive models. The main contribution of this work is demonstrating that this approach can successfully generate interesting, compositional questions in a non-trivial domain.

However, the paper is more compelling as a contribution to AI than to cognitive science. The authors claim, "our results show how people balance informativeness and complexity," but the evidence for this is not convincing. In the Battleship domain, the influence of EIG on human question-asking appears to be either small (based on the log-likelihood results, which bootstrap estimates suggest have a 19% chance of being spurious) or non-existent (based on the correlation results). Given the noisy data and conflicting outcomes across different metrics, these results are insufficient to draw strong general conclusions about how people balance these competing factors.

Furthermore, the paper omits some important prior work. The central idea to "model questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer" is equivalent to the notion of a "goal" in Hawkins et al. (2015, "Why do you ask?") and is founded on the concept of a Question-Under-Discussion (QUD) introduced by Roberts (1996). The questioner model in Hawkins et al. also uses expected information gain and trades it off against question complexity, although it is applied to a much simpler domain.
