- Proposes a cognitive model predicting questions as a weighted sum of four features (EIG, complexity, answer type, relevance) evaluated in a Battleship domain.
- Full model attains higher held-out log-likelihood than lesioned variants, but omitting EIG yields similar correlation with human judgments.
- Methodology appears technically sound and clearly described.
- Cognitive-science claims are weak: evidence for a trade-off between informativeness and complexity is limited or inconsistent depending on the evaluation metric.
- Bootstrap uncertainty (≈19% chance of favoring the wrong model) and sparse human data undermine strong conclusions about EIG’s role.
- Paper is well written and likely reproducible from the provided information.
- Omits relevant prior work (e.g., Hawkins 2015 and Roberts 1996 / QUD framework) that also models questions and EIG-complexity trade-offs.
- Overall, the compositional, EIG-based approach is promising for AI and cognitive modeling, but stronger empirical evidence is needed to support broad cognitive claims.
