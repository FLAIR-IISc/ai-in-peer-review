

This paper proposes a computational model where human question-asking is formalized as program generation, with questions represented as executable programs over possible world states. The model predicts human question frequencies in a Battleship game context by combining features of informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (program length), answer type, and relevance (game context dependency). Evaluated on 605 human-generated questions, the full model (incorporating all features) significantly outperforms variants omitting key components in log-likelihood (bootstrap confidence: 81% vs. information-agnostic, 100% vs. complexity-agnostic). The model also generates novel, contextually appropriate questions not present in training.  

**Quality**: The technical approach is sound, with rigorous evaluation via cross-validation, bootstrapped log-likelihood comparisons, and qualitative generation of novel questions. The probabilistic framework (log-linear model over program features) is well-motivated and properly implemented. A limitation is the sparse human data (many questions asked only once), which constrains cognitive inferences about human question-choosing behavior.  

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally clear, with precise definitions of program grammar, features, and evaluation metrics. The supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility (e.g., grammar rules, feature specifications). The writing is concise and well-structured, making complex concepts accessible.  

**Originality**: The core innovation—modeling questions as executable programs for open-ended question generation—advances beyond prior NIPS work on active learning (e.g., Settles 2010, which focuses on label queries). However, the program-as-question framing overlaps with Hawkins et al. (CogSci 2015, cited as [19] in the paper) and Roberts (1996) on question-under-discussion. The paper should more explicitly position its contribution against these works, as the novelty lies in applying this formalism to compositional, open-ended question generation in a non-trivial domain.  

**Significance**: The work makes a strong contribution to AI, demonstrating a scalable method for generating human-like questions in structured domains—a significant step toward more capable active learning systems. While the cognitive science claims ("how people balance informativeness and complexity") are under-supported by the data (sparse human frequencies limit causal inference), the model provides a valuable, testable framework for future cognitive and AI research.  

**Pro for Acceptance**:  
- Novel AI approach to compositional question generation with rigorous evaluation.  
- Demonstrates generation of genuinely novel, contextually relevant questions.  
- Clear technical execution and reproducibility.  

**Con for Acceptance**:  
- Overstates cognitive significance given sparse human data; claims about human balancing of factors lack strong evidence.  
- Insufficient engagement with prior work on program-based question modeling (Hawkins 2015, Roberts 1996).  

Overall, the paper makes a significant technical contribution to AI and active learning, though its cognitive science claims require qualification. It is well-suited for NIPS as a methodological advance, with clear potential for future applications.
