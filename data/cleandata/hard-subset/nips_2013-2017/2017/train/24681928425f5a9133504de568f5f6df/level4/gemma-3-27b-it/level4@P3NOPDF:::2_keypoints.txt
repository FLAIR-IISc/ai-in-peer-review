- Investigates human question asking with K-ary answers, modeling tradeoffs among informativeness, complexity, and answer type.  
- Uses a “battleship” partially observable grid domain and a PCFG to generate questions; questions formalized in lambda calculus.  
- Evaluates question “goodness” as a linear combination of expected information gain, grammatical complexity (length & PCFG probability), and answer-type features.  
- PCFG was hand-coded from prior human question data rather than learned automatically.  
- Model evaluated with leave-one-out cross-validation on lesioned variants, showing all three factors matter and complexity is the dominant predictor.  
- Presents novel questions generated by the model.  
- Paper is clear, well-written, and combines quantitative and qualitative analyses; makes a substantive contribution to cognitive science and self-directed learning.  
- Explains why informativeness alone poorly predicts human choices—complexity constraints and limited question space matter.  
- Major limitation: scalability and generalizability are unclear given the small battleship domain and existing computational strain.  
- Authors should motivate broader NIPS-relevant applications and provide a roadmap for adapting the method to larger, more complex domains.  
- Minor recommendation: perform nested model comparisons using log-likelihoods for formal statistical tests of model differences.  
- For sequential question-asking work, consider literature in formal semantics/pragmatics (e.g., Questions under Discussion) and the suggested references.
