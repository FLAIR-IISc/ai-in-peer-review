- Proposal: Model questions as programs executable on possible world states to predict and generate human-like questions in an information-search game.
- Formalization: Uses a simple programming language and a probabilistic model balancing expected information gain (EIG), question complexity, answer type, and contextual relevance.
- Evaluation: Tested on 605 human questions from a Battleship-like game with cross-validation and bootstrapping to compare model variants.
- Main contribution: Formalizes question asking as program generation, enabling compositional, context-sensitive question modeling.
- Missing literature: Fails to engage key prior work on question semantics and QUD (Roberts 1996) and Hawkins' EIG-based question selection (2015).
- Technical quality: Model is well-constructed with clear features; ablation shows the full model (especially complexity) is important.
- Generative ability: Model can generate novel, contextually relevant human-like questions beyond the training set.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Formalism, grammar, experiments, and supplementary materials are clearly presented and reproducible.
- Predictive performance: Achieves a 0.64 Spearman correlation between predicted and actual question frequencies across contexts.
- Originality caveat: Novel programmatic approach within this domain, but core idea of balancing informativeness and complexity echoes earlier work.
- Overstated claim: The paper’s claim to be the “first predictive model” of open-ended human question asking is exaggerated given prior studies.
- Significance: Methodological advance with potential for AI dialogue systems; cognitive science implications are promising but not fully demonstrated.
- Strengths: Clear formalization, rigorous statistical evaluation, successful novel question generation, and strong writing.
- Weaknesses: Insufficient engagement with relevant prior literature, overstated novelty, limited insight into human cognitive trade-offs, and domain-specific focus.
- Recommendation summary: Technically strong and worth accepting with minor revisions to better contextualize related linguistic and cognitive work.
