- Models human question asking as program generation using a typed, lambda-calculus/LISP-like language and a hand-crafted PCFG over a Battleship-style 6×6 grid.  
- Executable programs produce answers of specific types (Boolean, number, color, location) when run on hypothesized world states.  
- A log-linear model scores questions with features for expected information gain (EIG), complexity (−log PCFG prob), answer type, and relevance; parameters fit by likelihood maximization with importance sampling and leave-one-context-out CV.  
- Bridges active learning, semantic/pragmatic question theories, and program-based cognitive models, explaining prior failures of EIG-only accounts via complexity and answer-type costs.  
- Clear, well-organized exposition and principled formalization; grammar captures compositionality and supports productivity beyond templates.  
- Probabilistic framework is sound and interpretable, aligning with resource-rational cognitive theories.  
- Empirical evaluation uses a challenging open-ended human question dataset; lesioning and LOCO CV show all feature families matter, especially complexity.  
- Model can generate novel, context-sensitive, human-like questions, demonstrating compositional generalization.  
- Analysis links model energies to human frequencies (mean Spearman 0.64) and includes bootstrap comparisons across variants.  
- Heavy reliance on hand-engineered grammar and semantics tailored to a small domain, raising scalability concerns to richer environments (vision, language, science).  
- Inference is computationally expensive (large importance samples) and required dropping some rules/contexts for tractability, creating potential bias.  
- No learned language interface: mapping from natural language to programs is assumed, limiting applicability to dialog or real-world use.  
- Evaluation lacks breadth of baselines (retrieval/template, seq2seq conditioned on context, neural semantic parsers) to benchmark benefits of the program-based approach.  
- Approximation and stability issues: normalizing constants estimated by importance sampling with limited analysis of variance, sensitivity, or effective sample sizes.  
- Dataset mismatches introduced by removing rules/contexts (e.g., draw) are not fully accounted for.  
- Fit metrics are moderate and sparse (many once-asked questions); additional distributional metrics (KL, calibration) and significance tests would strengthen claims.  
- Technically sound overall, but practical limitations and hand-engineering make this a domain-bound demonstration.  
- Writing and methodological detail are clear enough to enable reproduction given the grammar and code.  
- Original contribution: framing question generation as utility-sensitive program synthesis with an explicit complexity-informativeness trade-off.  
- Significance: compelling for computational cognitive science; broader ML/NIPS relevance depends on a clear path to scalability and integration with language/perception.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel principled formulation, empirical evidence for complexity and answer-type importance, compositional generalization, clear experiments and writing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: hand-crafted domain-specific grammar, limited alternative-model comparisons, computational approximations that compromise robustness.  
- Suggestions: add statistical tests for model comparisons, stronger baselines (retrieval, seq2seq, neural semantic parser), and report IS variance/ESS and sensitivity analyses.  
- Suggestions (cont.): discuss scaling routes (amortized inference, neurally guided PCFGs, learned complexity priors, integration with semantic parsers) and connections to sequential/question-under-discussion dialog literature.  
- Suggestion to clarify and report the impact of removing the draw rule and dropping contexts, e.g., results with/without those components.
