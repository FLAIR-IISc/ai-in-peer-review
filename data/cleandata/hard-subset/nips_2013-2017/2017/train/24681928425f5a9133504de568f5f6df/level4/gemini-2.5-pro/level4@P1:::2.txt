This paper presents a computational model of human question asking in a "Battleship"-like game, where a player must identify hidden colored ships on a partially observable grid by asking questions that can be answered with a single word. The core of the model is a hand-crafted probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) that generates a compositional space of possible questions, represented as programs in lambda calculus. The probability of a particular question being asked is determined by a log-linear model whose features include the question's informativeness (Expected Information Gain), its complexity (negative log probability under a uniform PCFG), and its answer type (e.g., Boolean, color). The model is trained on a dataset of human questions (from a previous study) that were manually translated into the programming language. Through leave-one-out cross-validation and model lesion studies, the authors demonstrate that informativeness, complexity, and answer type all contribute to predicting human questions, with complexity being the most critical factor. The paper concludes by qualitatively demonstrating the model's ability to generate novel, human-like questions.

**Strengths**

The paper is extremely clear, well-written, and addresses the important topic of goal-directed question asking, connecting active learning with cognitive science and linguistics.

Its primary strength is the innovative approach of modeling questions as programs generated from a compositional grammar. This provides a powerful framework for studying how humans construct and select informative queries. This perspective has the potential to inspire new lines of research in active learning, moving beyond simple label requests to more sophisticated, structured queries.

From a computational cognitive science perspective, the paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on self-directed learning. It offers a compelling explanation for a puzzle from previous work, which found that informativeness alone is a poor predictor of human questions. By showing that question complexity is a more dominant constraint, the model provides a more nuanced and accurate account of human behavior. This contribution to cognitive science may be sufficient on its own to warrant publication at NIPS.

Finally, the evaluation is thorough, including both quantitative model comparisons (using cross-validation and lesion studies) and a qualitative analysis of the model's generative capabilities.

**Weaknesses**

The primary weakness of the paper concerns its scalability and generalizability, which may limit its immediate impact on the broader NIPS community.

The model relies on a hand-crafted grammar specific to the "Battleship" domain. While the authors note that some components are domain-general, it is unclear how much effort would be required to adapt this framework to new, more complex domains. The process of translating natural language questions into the formal language was also done by hand, and automating this step is a significant challenge.

The "Battleship" world is relatively simple, yet the method is described as computationally intensive. This raises concerns about whether the approach can scale to problems with larger state spaces or more complex compositional structures, which are common in machine learning applications.

While the core ideas are interesting, the paper could be strengthened by discussing a clearer path toward applying these concepts in a domain of broader interest to the NIPS audience. Outlining how this form of programmatic active learning could provide novel insights or advantages in areas like reinforcement learning, robotics, or scientific discovery would significantly increase the paper's significance.

**Minor Comments**

1) For the model comparison in Table 1, the authors could consider performing a nested model comparison using the log-likelihoods to formally test the statistical significance of each feature set's contribution.

2) For future work on sequential question asking, the authors may find the "Questions under Discussion" (QUD) framework from formal semantics and pragmatics to be relevant. This literature provides formal models of how dialogue context shapes question-asking strategies. Some pointers include:
*   Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. *Linguistics and Philosophy, 18*(5), 459-527.
*   Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. *Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17*, 131-139.
