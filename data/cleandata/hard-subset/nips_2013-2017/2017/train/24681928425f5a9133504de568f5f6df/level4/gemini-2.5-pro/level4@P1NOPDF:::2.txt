This paper examines human question-asking in a "Battleship"-like domain with K-ary answers. The authors hand-craft a Probabilistic Context-Free Grammar (PCFG) to model a space of possible questions. In their task, an agent must ask a single-word-answer question to gain maximal information about the state of a partially observable grid containing hidden, colored ships. The PCFG serves as a prior over questions, which are formally represented as lambda calculus expressions. The quality of a question is modeled as a linear combination of three factors: its informativeness (expected information gain), its complexity (its length or negative log-probability under the PCFG), and its "answer type" (e.g., whether it elicits a true/false or a color response). Using a human dataset from a previous study, which was hand-coded into their formalism, the authors perform a leave-one-out cross-validation analysis on lesioned versions of their model. The results demonstrate that all three factors make a non-negligible contribution to predicting human questions, with complexity being the most significant predictor. Finally, they present a qualitative analysis of novel questions generated by the model.

Question-asking is a fundamental topic in linguistics and cognitive science, particularly in the study of hypothesis testing and information search. The authors present an innovative framework that unifies modern linguistic and cognitive theories to model how humans ask questions. This work has the potential to inspire novel research in the active learning community, particularly in developing agents that can ask more sophisticated and human-like questions. The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The model is evaluated thoroughly, with both quantitative and qualitative analyses providing strong support for the authors' claims.

From a computational cognitive science perspective, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of self-directed learning. Previous work has often found that simple informativeness metrics are poor predictors of human question-asking behavior. This paper helps resolve this discrepancy by demonstrating that other factors, particularly question complexity, play a more dominant role. The authors convincingly argue that the preference for simpler questions can outweigh the drive for maximal information gain, a finding that also sheds light on the importance of the size of the hypothesis space of possible questions. Thus, the paper offers a valuable contribution to the cognitive science literature, which in itself could be sufficient to warrant publication at NIPS, given the conference's interest in computational models of cognition.

However, the approach has serious limitations that may restrict its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. First, while the authors manually mapped human-generated text to a logical representation, it remains unclear how this process could be automated. This reliance on manual encoding poses a significant challenge for scaling the approach or extending it to new, more complex domains relevant to the broader machine learning community. Second, the "Battleship" domain is relatively small and simple, with only a few features. The authors note that their method is already computationally strained even in this constrained environment, raising questions about its feasibility for larger, real-world problems. Despite these limitations, the core ideas—particularly the integration of rich, structured questions into active learning—are compelling. To strengthen the paper for the NIPS audience, the authors could benefit from outlining a clearer roadmap or a compelling use case where their technique could be applied to provide novel insights in a domain of broader interest.

Minor comments:
1) While it may not change the overall conclusions, a nested model comparison using the log-likelihoods from the lesioned models could provide formal statistical significance tests for the contribution of each component.
2) For future work on sequential question-asking, the authors might find it fruitful to connect their approach with existing models in formal semantics and pragmatics. The "Questions under Discussion" (QUD) framework, for instance, offers a rich theoretical basis for modeling dialogue and inquiry.
