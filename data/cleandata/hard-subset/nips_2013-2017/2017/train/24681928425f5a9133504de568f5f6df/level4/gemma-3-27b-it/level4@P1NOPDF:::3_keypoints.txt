- Paper introduces a cognitive model predicting question selection as a weighted sum of informativeness (EIG), complexity (length), answer type, and relevance.
- Model operationalizes question probability via an unnormalized log probability combining those factors.
- Applied the model to the Battleship domain to predict how people ask questions to locate hidden ships.
- Full model outperformed ablated versions on held-out human data by log-likelihood.
- When evaluating correlation with human judgments, the model without EIG performed comparably to the full model.
- Evidence for people’s sensitivity to EIG is weak: log-likelihood gains could be spurious (bootstrap ~19%) and correlation results are noisy due to limited data.
- Paper is technically sound, clearly written, and results appear reproducible.
- Reviewer argues the contribution is stronger for AI than for cognitive science and does not convincingly advance understanding of how people balance informativeness and complexity.
- Significant omission: limited discussion of relevant prior work (notably Hawkins 2015 and Roberts 1996) that use related question-as-program/QUD ideas and tradeoffs between EIG and complexity.
- Reviewer finds the compositional prior + EIG approach intuitively appealing and sees the main contribution as demonstrating feasibility for generating compositional questions in a non-trivial domain.
