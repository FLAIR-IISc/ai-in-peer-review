- Investigates human question asking with K-ary answers in a “battleship” grid, using a hand-crafted PCFG and lambda-calculus to represent questions.
- Evaluates question quality via a linear combination of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (length or negative log PCFG probability), and answer type.
- Human question data from prior work were manually encoded into the grammar.
- Leave-one-out cross-validation of lesioned models shows each factor significantly contributes to question goodness.
- Complexity is the most influential factor in predicting human question choice.
- Model produces a novel set of generated questions.
- Paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and evaluated both quantitatively and qualitatively.
- Integrates linguistic and cognitive theory to advance active learning and richer question-asking models.
- From a cognitive science view, explains why informativeness alone is a weak predictor—complexity better accounts for human behavior.
- Main limitation: scalability and broader ML applicability are unclear due to the small battleship domain and computational demands.
- Recommendation: strengthen paper by demonstrating a concrete, broader-interest scenario where the technique yields novel insights.
- Minor comments: perform nested model comparisons using log-likelihoods for statistical significance; consider literature on sequential question asking (e.g., Questions under Discussion; Ginzburg 1995; Rojas-Esponda 2013).
