This paper proposes a computational cognitive model designed to predict which questions people ask in a given context, represented as a belief state. The model defines the unnormalized log probability of a question as a weighted linear combination of four features: informativeness (measured by expected information gain, or EIG), complexity (approximated by question length), answer type, and relevance (distinguishing between questions about the belief state versus world-independent logical facts).

The model is evaluated in the Battleship domain, where the goal is to locate hidden ships on a grid. The authors demonstrate that the full model, incorporating all four features, achieves a higher log-likelihood on held-out human data compared to ablated versions with fewer features. However, the results are less conclusive regarding the role of informativeness; a model without the EIG feature performs comparably to the full model when measured by correlation with human judgments.

**Strengths**

The paper's primary contribution is demonstrating the viability of a compositional, program-synthesis approach to question generation in a non-trivial domain. The approach of modeling questions as programs evaluated by EIG and other pragmatic factors is compelling and likely to be influential for future AI and cognitive models. The work is technically sound, and the paper is well-written and clear, to the extent that the results appear to be reproducible.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper is a strong contribution from an AI perspective, its claims as a cognitive science project are less convincing. The paper asserts that "our results show how people balance informativeness and complexity," but the evidence presented is insufficient to support a strong conclusion about human cognition.

Specifically, the role of EIG in human question-asking remains ambiguous based on these results. The improvement in log-likelihood from including the EIG feature is marginal (with bootstrap estimates indicating a 19% chance of the result being due to noise), while the correlation analysis shows no significant benefit from its inclusion. Given these mixed and noisy results, it is difficult to conclude much about the general principles people use to balance these factors, especially when generalizing beyond the specific context of the Battleship game.

**Relation to Prior Work**

The paper could be strengthened by acknowledging and discussing relevant prior work. The core idea of "model[ing] questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer" is closely related to the concept of a "goal" in Hawkins (2015, "Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers.") and draws from the well-established notion of a Question-Under-Discussion (QUD) from Roberts (1996, "Information structure in discourse: Towards an integrated formal theory of pragmatics."). Furthermore, the Hawkins (2015) model also formalizes a trade-off between EIG and question complexity, albeit in a simpler domain. Situating the current work within this context would better highlight its specific contributions, namely the application and scaling of these ideas to a more complex, compositional environment.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this paper presents a valuable AI contribution by successfully applying a program-synthesis framework for question generation to a complex task. The model itself is interesting and the general approach is promising. However, the cognitive science claims about how people balance informativeness and complexity are not strongly supported by the mixed empirical results.
