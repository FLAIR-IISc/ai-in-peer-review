

This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question-asking behavior by treating questions as executable programs within a compositional grammar. The authors address a significant gap in active learning literature—where most systems rely on simple label requests—by modeling how humans generate rich, context-sensitive questions in a Battleship-style game. Their key insight is that questions can be formalized as programs that, when executed on possible world states, yield answers. The model uses a probabilistic generative approach with four features: expected information gain (EIG) for informativeness, question complexity (via grammar log-probability), answer type (Boolean/numerical/color/location), and relevance (whether the question references the game board). Evaluated on 605 human-generated questions across 18 contexts, the model successfully predicts question frequencies (Spearman’s ρ = 0.64) and generates novel, human-like questions not seen in training.

**Strengths:**  
The paper makes a clear, technically sound contribution to computational cognitive science. Its core innovation—formalizing questions as executable programs—provides a principled way to model compositionality and context sensitivity, moving beyond prior work that relies on canned questions (e.g., goal-directed dialog systems [25, 1]) or large-scale VQG datasets [16, 23]. The grammar-based approach elegantly captures human question productivity (e.g., generating "Are all ships the same size?" from atomic predicates), and the log-linear model with EIG features demonstrates how humans balance informativeness and complexity—a finding absent in existing active learning frameworks. The empirical evaluation is rigorous: cross-validated predictions, ablation studies showing complexity’s critical role (lesioning it drops correlation to -0.36), and qualitative generation of novel questions (e.g., "What is the column of the top left of the tiles that have the color of the bottom right corner of the board?") convincingly validate the model. The work also thoughtfully acknowledges limitations (e.g., domain-specific grammar), setting a clear path for future generalization.

**Weaknesses:**  
The paper’s scope is narrow, focusing solely on a constrained Battleship game with predefined answer types. While the authors justify this as a "testbed" for question generation, the domain lacks the ambiguity and open-endedness typical of real-world learning scenarios (e.g., medical diagnosis or scientific inquiry). The grammar’s heavy reliance on domain knowledge (e.g., ship sizes/positions) limits generalizability, and the model’s reliance on a pre-specified semantic space—rather than learning from raw language—reduces its applicability to natural language question-asking. Though the authors note this as a limitation, the paper does not sufficiently address how to bridge this gap (e.g., via recent neural program synthesis tools [24]). Additionally, the claim that "humans can ask intelligent questions in novel scenarios with minimal practice" is under-supported; the data set involves 18 contexts with identical rules, not truly novel tasks.

**Originality and Significance:**  
The work is original in its *computational formalism*—treating question asking as program synthesis rather than language generation—but builds directly on established concepts (e.g., Bayesian active learning [21], compositional semantics [10]). It does not replicate prior NIPS work (e.g., Lake et al.’s program induction [13]), but the specific application to open-ended human question generation represents a meaningful extension. Significance is moderate: while the model advances *how* we model human question-asking, its direct impact on machine learning practice is limited by the narrow domain. The approach could inform future dialog systems or active learning algorithms, but the paper does not clearly articulate how. Crucially, it provides the first predictive model for *context-sensitive, open-ended question generation*—a gap unaddressed by prior NIPS work on VQG [16] or goal-directed dialogue [25].

**Quality and Clarity:**  
The technical execution is strong: the grammar design, EIG computation, and ablation studies are well-motivated and correctly implemented. The writing is clear and well-organized, with precise descriptions of the model’s components (e.g., how λ-expressions enable set operations). The supplementary materials adequately support reproducibility. However, the paper overstates the novelty of its "human-like" generation (e.g., questions like "Are all ships horizontal?" are stereotypical of the game), and the lack of comparison to neural question generators (e.g., Jain et al.’s VAE [11]) weakens the significance argument.

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
*Pro:* First formal model of context-sensitive, compositional question generation; rigorous evaluation showing human-like question prediction and novel generation; clear demonstration of how humans trade off informativeness vs. complexity.  
*Con:* Narrow domain (Battleship) limits generalizability; domain-specific grammar requires substantial engineering; minimal connection to broader NIPS themes (e.g., neural architectures for language).  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper makes a technically solid contribution to cognitive modeling but offers only incremental progress for NIPS, where broader impact and generalizability are prioritized. While the framework is elegant and the evaluation thorough, the constrained task and lack of clear pathways to real-world application reduce its significance for the conference. I recommend *weak rejection*—the work is sound but does not advance the field in a way that merits NIPS publication. Future versions should address generalizability (e.g., testing on a new domain with minimal grammar modification) and better contextualize the contribution against neural question generation methods.
