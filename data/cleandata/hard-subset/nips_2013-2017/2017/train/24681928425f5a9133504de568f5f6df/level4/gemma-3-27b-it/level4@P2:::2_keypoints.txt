- Models human question asking as lambda-calculus programs within a “battleship” domain to predict K-ary questions.
- Defines a PCFG over program-questions as a prior and scores questions by a linear function of informativeness, complexity, and answer type.
- Evaluates on an existing dataset with questions hand-coded into the grammar and shows generated novel questions.
- Uses ablation and leave-one-out cross-validation to assess component contributions; complexity is the most important factor.
- Paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and technically sound.
- Combines quantitative and qualitative evaluation for a robust assessment.
- Advances cognitive science by showing informativeness alone cannot explain human questioning and highlighting complexity’s role.
- Promising implications for active learning by enabling more sophisticated question generation.
- Major scalability concerns: approach strains resources even in the small battleship domain and may not scale to real-world complexity.
- Domain dependence: relies on a manually defined grammar tied to the battleship setting, limiting generalizability.
- Practicality issue: unclear feasibility and automation of translating natural language questions into the formal semantic logic.
- Needs demonstration of broader-domain applicability and unique insights achievable only via this method to appeal to NIPS.
- Statistical weaknesses: comparisons lack reported significance tests between model variants.
- Minor suggestion: perform nested model comparisons (likelihood ratio tests) to strengthen quantitative claims.
- Minor suggestion: engage more with formal semantics/pragmatics literature (e.g., Questions under Discussion) in future work.
