- Task: model human single-word question asking in a finite-answer “battleship” domain to maximize information about hidden grid states.  
- Method: hand-crafted Probabilistic Context-Free Grammar (PCFG) used as a prior over questions, with questions represented in lambda calculus.  
- Question scoring: linear combination of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (length or negative log-probability under PCFG), and answer type.  
- Data: previously collected human questions were manually coded into the proposed grammar.  
- Evaluation: lesioned models assessed with leave-one-out cross-validation, showing each factor contributes to performance.  
- Key result: complexity is the most influential factor, helping explain why informativeness alone poorly predicts human question choices.  
- Outputs: the model generates novel, plausible questions for the task.  
- Contribution: unifies linguistic and cognitive theory to advance understanding of human question formulation and self-directed learning.  
- Presentation: paper is exceptionally clear and rigorously evaluated both quantitatively and qualitatively.  
- Limitation: scalability concerns—small, feature-poor battleship domain and computational demands may limit broader ML relevance.  
- Recommendation: authors should present a concrete, larger-scale scenario where the approach would apply and yield insights.  
- Minor suggestion 1: perform nested model comparisons using log-likelihoods to test statistical significance.  
- Minor suggestion 2: consider sequential question-asking models and relevant linguistics literature (e.g., Questions under Discussion; Ginzburg 1995; Rojas-Esponda 2013).
