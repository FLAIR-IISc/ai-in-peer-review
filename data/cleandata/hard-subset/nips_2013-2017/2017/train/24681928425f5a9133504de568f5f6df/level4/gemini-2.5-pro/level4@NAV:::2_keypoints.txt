- Paper models human question-asking in a K-ary "battleship" domain using a hand-constructed PCFG over lambda-calculus questions.  
- Objective: pick single-word-answer questions that maximally reduce uncertainty about the hidden board.  
- Questions scored as a linear combination of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (length or neg. log-prob under uniform-rule PCFG), and answer type (e.g., boolean vs. color).  
- Human data from a prior study were hand-encoded into the grammar and used for evaluation.  
- Leave-one-out cross-validation with ablations shows all three factors matter, with complexity the strongest predictor.  
- Model generates novel questions and the paper presents qualitative examples.  
- Strength: integrates linguistic and cognitive theories, advancing understanding of question-asking and active learning.  
- Manuscript is very well written; evaluations are thorough both quantitatively and qualitatively.  
- Contribution to cognitive science: explains why informativeness alone fails to predict human asking—complexity and question space constraints dominate.  
- Major limitation: manual semantic encoding and limited "battleship" domain raise questions about scalability and generalization to richer, real-world tasks.  
- Concern about computational cost even in the simple domain; scalability not addressed.  
- Recommendation to improve impact: articulate clear, broader application scenarios where the method gives new insights.  
- Minor suggestion 1: perform nested model comparisons (log-likelihood tests) to assess statistical significance of component contributions.  
- Minor suggestion 2: for sequential question-asking, engage with formal semantics/pragmatics literature (e.g., Questions under Discussion) and see suggested references.
