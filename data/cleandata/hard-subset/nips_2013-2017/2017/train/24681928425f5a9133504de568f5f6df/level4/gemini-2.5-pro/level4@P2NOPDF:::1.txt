This paper presents a model to predict user-generated questions in a modified game of Battleship. In this version of the game, players do not query individual squares directly but instead formulate questions using an artificial language with compositional logical semantics. The authors use a log-linear model to predict question choice based on four simple features: the expected information gain (informativeness) of a question, its answer type (Boolean vs. numerical), and whether the question pertains to the board's configuration.

The high-level motivation for this work, which frames human question-asking as a form of active learning, is sound. Understanding the principles that guide human information-gathering strategies is a valuable goal for the community.

However, the paper's contributions are limited by the straightforwardness of its ideas and the highly constrained nature of the experimental task. The features selected for the model—informativeness and question complexity—are standard and arguably the most obvious choices for this type of problem. The primary weakness, however, lies in the task itself. The modified Battleship game is a "toy" problem that is fully specified, highly constrained, and logically unambiguous. This artificial environment strips away the complexity and ambiguity inherent in real-world reasoning and natural language, making the modeling challenge significantly less difficult.

While the approach may be effective within this narrow domain, the simplicity of the task means the findings lack novelty and are unlikely to generalize to more complex, real-world scenarios. In conclusion, due to the limited technical novelty and the constrained experimental setting, I do not believe this paper will be of broad interest to the NIPS community.
