- Studies question asking as program generation in a simplified “Battleship” game using a compositional logic-based language.  
- Proposes a probabilistic log-linear model with features for expected information gain, answer type, and board relevance to predict/generate questions.  
- Motivation: connects active learning to richer, human-inspired querying strategies.  
- Contribution feels incremental and builds heavily on established linguistics and cognitive science work.  
- Task is highly constrained and lacks real-world ambiguity, limiting ecological validity.  
- Model successfully predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, human-like questions in the game.  
- Empirical finding: complexity, informativeness, and answer type jointly drive human-like question generation.  
- Generalization beyond the simplified domain is unclear.  
- Heavy reliance on a carefully designed grammar and pre-defined features raises scalability concerns.  
- Lacks comparisons to a broader set of strong machine-learning baselines.  
- Strength: clear formal framing of question asking as program generation.  
- Strength: empirical evaluation on human question data.  
- Strength: ablation/feature analysis highlights importance of complexity.  
- Weakness: limited novelty and conceptual breakthrough.  
- Weakness: constrained task and potential overstatement of generalizability.  
- Overall recommendation: solid but incremental work; reviewer recommends rejection for NIPS due to limited originality, scope, and relevance.
