This paper investigates human question asking in scenarios with K-ary answers, focusing on a “battleship” domain involving hidden, colored shapes on a partially observable grid. The authors construct a hand-defined Probabilistic Context-Free Grammar (PCFG) to model questions, representing them as statements within lambda calculus. A question’s quality is assessed using a combined score considering informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (measured by length and negative log probability under the PCFG – assuming uniform rewrite rule probabilities), and answer type (e.g., true/false vs. color identification). Human question data, originally from a prior publication, was manually encoded into their defined grammar. Through leave-one-out cross-validation, the authors demonstrate that each factor—informativeness, complexity, and answer type—contributes meaningfully to the model, with complexity being the most influential. The paper concludes with a presentation of novel questions generated by their approach.

Question asking is a central topic in linguistics and hypothesis testing, and this work offers a novel and valuable integration of modern linguistic and cognitive theory to study human questioning behavior. This approach holds significant potential to stimulate new research in active learning, potentially enabling models to formulate more sophisticated and effective queries. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented. The evaluation is comprehensive, incorporating both quantitative analysis and qualitative insights.

From the perspective of computational cognitive science, this work represents an important advance in the area of self-directed learning. Prior research indicated that question informativeness alone is a weak predictor of human question selection. This paper partially resolves this discrepancy by demonstrating that complexity plays a crucial, and potentially dominant, role, a finding likely influenced by the limited space of possible questions available to the model. This contribution is strong enough to warrant consideration for publication in NIPS, particularly within the cognitive science community. 

However, the approach has substantial limitations that may restrict its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. While a process for automatically translating human question text into semantic logic *might* be feasible, it’s currently unclear how this method could scale to domains of wider interest within the machine learning community. The battleship domain is relatively small, featuring a limited number of features, and the computational demands of the current methodology are already significant. Nevertheless, the core ideas – leveraging richer questions in active learning – are intriguing and potentially impactful. To broaden its relevance, the authors should explicitly outline a compelling real-world scenario where their technique could be applied and offer insights into how it might outperform existing methods.

Minor Comments:

1) A nested model comparison using the log-likelihoods (LLs) of the lesioned models could provide statistical significance testing for the observed differences in performance.

2) For future work involving sequential question asking, the authors should consider related models from the linguistics literature concerning question-asking within formal semantics and pragmatics, particularly the “Questions under Discussion” framework. Relevant references include:

Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. *Linguistics and Philosophy*, *18*(5), 459-527.
Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. *Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam)*, *17*, 131-139.
