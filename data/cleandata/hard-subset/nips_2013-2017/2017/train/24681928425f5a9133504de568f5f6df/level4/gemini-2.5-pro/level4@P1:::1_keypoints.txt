- Represents questions as compositional LISP-like programs executed over possible world states to yield answers.  
- Uses a log-linear probabilistic model defined over a context-free grammar to predict human question distributions in a Battleship-style game.  
- Model features include expected information gain (EIG), program complexity (length), answer type, and relevance to game state.  
- Evaluated by predicting human question frequencies on held-out contexts and generating novel, plausible questions.  
- Strength: elegant formalization connecting program synthesis with active learning, cognitive science, and formal semantics.  
- Strength: clear motivation to study richer forms of inquiry beyond simple label requests.  
- Strength: experimental results and ablations show balancing informativeness and complexity is crucial and improves fit to human behavior.  
- Weakness: limited originality—core techniques (probabilistic grammars, log-linear models, EIG) are standard.  
- Weakness: highly constrained artificial domain sidesteps key challenges like natural language parsing, ambiguity resolution, and perceptual grounding.  
- Weakness: limited scalability and generalizability due to reliance on hand-crafted grammar and fully specified world model.  
- Conclusion: technically sound and well-executed within the narrow task, but too specialized and artificial to claim broad human-like question-asking impact; better suited to specialized venues.
