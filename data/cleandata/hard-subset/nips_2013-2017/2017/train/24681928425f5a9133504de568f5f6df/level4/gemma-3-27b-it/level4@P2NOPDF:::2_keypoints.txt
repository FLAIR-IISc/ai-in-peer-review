- Paper models human question asking in a K-ary “battleship” grid domain using a hand-defined PCFG and lambda-calculus semantic representations.  
- Questions are scored by a combination of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (length and negative log probability under PCFG), and answer type.  
- Human question data (from prior work) were manually encoded into the grammar for evaluation.  
- Leave-one-out cross-validation shows informativeness, complexity, and answer type each contribute to model performance.  
- Complexity is the single most influential factor in predicting human question choice.  
- The model generates novel, linguistically-plausible questions as a demonstration.  
- Strong contribution: integrates linguistic and cognitive theory, with implications for active learning and self-directed learning research.  
- Paper is well written with comprehensive quantitative and qualitative evaluation.  
- Important advance: explains why informativeness alone is a weak predictor by showing complexity’s dominant role (possibly aided by the small question space).  
- Major limitation: unclear scalability to broader ML domains—small, feature-limited battleship domain and substantial computational cost.  
- Recommendation: authors should present a compelling real-world application and clarify how the approach outperforms existing methods.  
- Minor suggestions: perform nested model comparisons using log-likelihoods for statistical testing, and consider literature on sequential question asking (e.g., Questions Under Discussion; Ginzburg 1995; Rojas-Esponda 2013).
