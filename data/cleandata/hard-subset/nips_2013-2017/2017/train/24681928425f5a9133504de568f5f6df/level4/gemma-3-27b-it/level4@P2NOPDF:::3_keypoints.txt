- Proposes a cognitive model that scores questions as a weighted sum of four factors: informativeness (EIG), complexity (length), answer type, and relevance to belief state.
- Evaluated in a Battleship domain; model predicts human question choices by locating hidden ships on a grid.
- Full model (all four features) outperforms ablated variants on held-out human data by log-likelihood.
- Inclusion of EIG shows modest log-likelihood benefit but large bootstrap uncertainty (~19% chance the effect is incorrect).
- Correlation with human judgments is similar with or without EIG, possibly due to sparse human data.
- Paper is technically sound, well-written, and results appear reproducible.
- Claims about explaining “how people balance informativeness and complexity” are weakly supported by the evidence.
- Missing discussion of relevant prior work, notably Hawkins (2015) and the QUD literature (Roberts 1996), which use similar EIG/complexity tradeoffs.
- Main contribution: demonstrates feasibility of generating compositional, meaningful questions in a relatively complex domain, with potential value for AI and cognitive modeling.
