The paper introduces a cognitive model designed to predict question selection based on underlying belief states. The model defines the unnormalized log probability of a question as a weighted combination of informativeness (measured by expected information gain – EIG), complexity (approximated by question length), answer type, and relevance – distinguishing between references to the belief state and world-independent facts. This model was tested within the Battleship domain, a task involving locating hidden ships on a grid. The complete model, incorporating all features, demonstrated superior performance in predicting held-out human question data (as measured by log-likelihood) compared to ablated versions. However, the model’s correlation with human judgments was comparable with and without consideration of EIG.

While technically sound, the work appears to contribute more strongly to the field of Artificial Intelligence than to cognitive science. The authors claim the model “predicts what question people will ask” and offers insights into the human balance between informativeness and complexity. However, the findings provide limited evidence for *how* people achieve this balance. The Battleship results present an ambiguous picture: humans are either slightly influenced by EIG (suggested by log-likelihood, though with a 19% chance of error based on bootstrap estimates), or not at all (as indicated by the noisy correlation results, likely due to limited human data). These results do not strongly support generalizable conclusions about human decision-making in question selection.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized; replication of the results seems feasible. 

A significant omission is relevant prior work. The approach of “modeling questions as programs that, when executed on a possible world state, output an answer” aligns with Hawkins’ (2015) concept of “goals” – as used to model question asking based on informative answers – and builds upon the question-under-discussion (QUD) framework introduced by Roberts (1996). Hawkins (2015) also utilizes EIG and complexity trade-offs, though within a simpler domain.

Despite this, the presented modeling approach – incorporating a compositional prior over questions and utilizing EIG alongside other factors – holds considerable promise for both cognitive and AI modeling, and is likely to see further development. The primary contribution of this work lies in demonstrating the successful application of this approach to generate meaningful compositional questions within a non-trivial domain.
