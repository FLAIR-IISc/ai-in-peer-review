- Proposes modeling human questions as executable programs within a compositional grammar to fill a gap in active learning beyond simple label requests.  
- Key insight: questions can be formalized as programs that, when executed on possible worlds, yield answers.  
- Model: probabilistic generative log-linear model with four features—expected information gain (EIG), question complexity (grammar log-probability), answer type, and relevance.  
- Evaluation: tested on 605 human questions across 18 contexts; predicts question frequencies (Spearman’s ρ = 0.64) and generates novel, human-like questions.  
- Strength: novel formalism captures compositionality and context-sensitivity, moving beyond canned-question and VQG approaches.  
- Strength: grammar-based approach models human productivity (compositional generation) and shows humans trade off informativeness vs. complexity.  
- Strength: rigorous empirical evaluation including cross-validation, ablation studies (complexity lesion drops correlation to -0.36), and qualitative novel question generation.  
- Weakness: narrow, domain-specific focus on a constrained Battleship game that lacks real-world ambiguity and open-endedness.  
- Weakness: heavy reliance on domain knowledge and a pre-specified semantic space; does not learn from raw natural language, limiting applicability.  
- Weakness: paper does not sufficiently address how to generalize the grammar or integrate neural program synthesis tools.  
- Weakness: claim that humans can ask intelligent questions in novel scenarios is under-supported by data from repeated-rule contexts.  
- Originality: formally treating question-asking as program synthesis is original and extends Bayesian active learning and compositional semantics.  
- Significance: moderate—advances cognitive modeling of question-asking but limited direct impact on ML practice due to narrow domain and unclear application paths.  
- Quality and clarity: technically well-executed, clearly written, and reproducible with adequate supplementary material.  
- Critique: overstates novelty of some generated questions and lacks comparison to neural question-generation baselines, weakening broader significance.  
- Pro/con summary: Pro—first formal model of context-sensitive compositional question generation with rigorous evaluation; Con—limited generalizability and weak connection to neural language methods.  
- Recommendation: weak rejection—solid cognitive-model contribution but insufficient generalizability and broader impact for NIPS; suggest testing new domains and comparing to neural approaches.
