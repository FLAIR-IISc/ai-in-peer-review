This paper proposes a computational model for goal-directed question asking, framing the problem as probabilistic program generation. The central idea is to represent questions as programs in a compositional, LISP-like language, which are executed over possible world states to yield an answer. A probabilistic model, specifically a log-linear model defined over a context-free grammar, is used to predict the distribution of human-generated questions in a constrained 'Battleship'-style game. The model's features capture key properties of questions, including their expected information gain (EIG), complexity (program length), answer type, and relevance to the game state. The authors evaluate their model by its ability to predict human question frequencies in held-out game contexts and to generate novel, plausible questions.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper's core strength lies in its elegant formalization of question asking as program synthesis. This is a principled approach that connects active learning with ideas from cognitive science and formal semantics.
*   The motivation to move beyond simple label requests in active learning is well-founded and important for the field. The paper makes a clear case for studying richer forms of inquiry.
*   The experimental results, within the defined task, are positive. The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate that balancing informativeness (EIG) with complexity is crucial for modeling human behavior, outperforming models that rely on either property alone.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Originality:** While the synthesis of these ideas is new in this specific context, the core technical components are well-established. The use of probabilistic grammars for generation, log-linear models for combining features, and Expected Information Gain for selecting queries are all standard techniques in their respective fields. The contribution is thus more of an application of existing methods to a novel dataset rather than a fundamental methodological advance.
*   **Highly Constrained Domain:** The primary weakness is the highly constrained and artificial nature of the experimental domain. The 'Battleship' game provides a clean, logically unambiguous world where the semantics of the programming language can be perfectly grounded. This setup sidesteps the most challenging aspects of building a system for human-like question asking, namely:
    1.  Parsing natural language into a formal representation.
    2.  Resolving semantic and syntactic ambiguity.
    3.  Grounding concepts in noisy, high-dimensional perceptual input.
    By operating directly on pre-defined semantic representations, the model avoids the very problems that much of the NIPS community is actively working on in areas like NLP, reinforcement learning, and computer vision.
*   **Limited Significance and Scalability:** Consequently, it is difficult to see a clear path for scaling this approach to more realistic, open-ended domains. The reliance on a hand-crafted grammar and a fully specified world model makes the framework difficult to generalize. This limits the potential significance and impact of the work for the broader machine learning community, which typically values methods that can handle greater complexity and ambiguity. The claim of modeling "human-like" question asking is strong, but the evidence is drawn from a narrow, artificial task that may not capture the full richness of human inquiry.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro:** The paper presents a clean, technically sound, and well-executed study that formalizes question asking as program induction. The motivation is strong, and the results clearly support the authors' claims within the chosen domain.
*   **Con:** The work's novelty is limited, as it combines standard techniques. More importantly, the highly simplified and constrained experimental task avoids the core challenges of natural language understanding and grounding, which severely limits the work's potential impact and generalizability.

**Conclusion:**

In summary, while the paper presents an interesting and well-realized piece of work on a novel dataset, its contribution is ultimately limited by the simplicity of the task and the reliance on established techniques. The central concern is that the artificial environment removes the most difficult problems associated with building a general-purpose, human-like question-asking system. For this reason, the paper may be a better fit for a more specialized cognitive science or computational linguistics venue. While the high-level motivation is relevant to NIPS, the specific implementation and evaluation are likely too narrow to be of broad interest to the community.
