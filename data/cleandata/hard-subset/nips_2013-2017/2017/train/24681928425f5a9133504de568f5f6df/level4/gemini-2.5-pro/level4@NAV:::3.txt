This work introduces a cognitive model designed to predict the likelihood of specific questions arising from particular belief states. The model computes the unnormalized log probability of a question as a linear combination of several features: informativeness (measured by Expected Information Gain), complexity (approximated by question length), the type of answer it elicits, and its relevance (distinguishing between queries about the belief state and those concerning logical, world-independent facts). The authors evaluate this framework in the Battleship game domain, where the objective is to locate ships on a grid. When evaluated against held-out human data, the complete model, incorporating all features, outperforms ablated versions in terms of log-likelihood. However, regarding the correlation with human judgments, a model excluding EIG performs comparably to the full model.

The methodology presented is technically sound.

This paper reads more successfully as a contribution to AI rather than to cognitive science. While the authors claim their model reveals how people balance informativeness against complexity, the evidence for this cognitive claim is not compelling. Specifically, the influence of EIG on human question-asking appears tenuous; the log-likelihood results suggest a minor effect, but bootstrap analysis indicates this finding has a 19% probability of being spurious. Furthermore, the correlation-based results, which are themselves noisy due to limited human data, show no significant effect of EIG. Given these findings, it is difficult to draw firm conclusions about the general principles governing how humans trade off these factors.

The manuscript is clearly written and well-organized, and the methodology is described with sufficient detail to suggest the results are reproducible.

The submission overlooks some key prior literature. The central idea of framing questions as "programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer" is presented as a novel contribution. However, this formalism is equivalent to the concept of a "goal" in Hawkins (2015, "Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers.") and builds upon the idea of a Question-Under-Discussion (QUD) from Roberts (1996, "Information structure in discourse..."). Notably, the Hawkins model also optimizes a trade-off between expected information gain and question complexity, albeit in a considerably simpler setting.

Despite these critiques, the proposed modeling approach—which combines a compositional prior over questions with decision-making based on EIG and other utilities—is a promising direction for both AI and cognitive modeling and is likely to see wider adoption. The primary contribution of this work, therefore, is its successful demonstration that this framework can be scaled to generate meaningful, compositionally structured questions within a complex, non-trivial domain.
