

The authors investigate human question-asking behavior in K-ary answer domains, introducing a computational model grounded in linguistic theory. They define a hand-crafted PCFG for a "Battleship" domain—where agents infer hidden colored shapes in a partially observable grid—using questions structured as lambda calculus statements. The model evaluates question quality via a linear combination of three factors: (1) informativeness (expected information gain), (2) complexity (measured by statement length or negative log probability under the PCFG with uniform rule weights), and (3) answer type (e.g., true/false vs. color). Human question data, drawn from prior work and hand-coded into the grammar, was used for model training. Model variants were compared via leave-one-out cross-validation, revealing that all factors contributed non-negligibly to human-like question selection, with complexity being the strongest predictor. The model also generated novel, domain-appropriate questions.

The work offers a significant theoretical advance by unifying linguistic and cognitive frameworks to explain human question-asking, addressing a key gap where prior models failed to predict human behavior using informativeness alone. The authors demonstrate that complexity and question space size—not just informativeness—explain human choices, advancing self-directed learning research and offering a novel lens for active learning. The paper is exceptionally clear, well-structured, and rigorously evaluated through both quantitative metrics and qualitative analysis of generated questions.

From a computational cognitive science perspective, this contribution substantially advances the field. It resolves a persistent puzzle: why informativeness alone poorly predicts human questions. The model shows complexity and the size of the question space are critical factors, providing a mechanistic explanation for prior discrepancies. This cognitive insight alone justifies publication in NIPS.

However, the approach faces serious scalability limitations that may restrict its broader appeal. The Battleship domain is highly constrained (few features, small state space), and the method is already computationally intensive. Crucially, the authors rely on manual grammar engineering and hand-coding of human questions into lambda calculus statements. While the linguistic approach is innovative, it is unclear how this could generalize to real-world domains (e.g., medical diagnosis, robotics) where question spaces are vast and semantics are ambiguous. The paper lacks a compelling case for where this technique would yield novel insights beyond the Battleship setting. To justify wider NIPS interest, the authors must explicitly identify a high-impact application domain where their method—handling rich question structures via PCFGs—provides advantages over existing active learning approaches.

Minor suggestions:  
1. A nested model comparison using log-likelihoods of lesioned models would strengthen statistical claims about factor contributions.  
2. For future work on sequential questioning, the authors should engage with formal semantics literature on "Questions under Discussion" (e.g., Ginzburg, 1995; Rojas-Esponda, 2013), which offers established frameworks for modeling pragmatic question-asking dynamics.  

In summary, this is a theoretically rich and well-executed study that resolves a key cognitive puzzle. While its immediate domain is narrow, the core idea—integrating linguistic structure and cognitive constraints into active learning—holds significant potential. The authors should clearly articulate a broader application scenario to maximize impact. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and makes a valuable contribution to cognitive science and active learning.
