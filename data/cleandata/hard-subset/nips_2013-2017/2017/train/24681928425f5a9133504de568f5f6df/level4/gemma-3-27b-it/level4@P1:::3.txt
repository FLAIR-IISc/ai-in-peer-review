The paper presents a cognitive model intended to predict question selection in specific contexts (belief states). The model defines the unnormalized log probability of a question as a weighted sum of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (roughly, question length), answer type, and “relevance” (whether the question pertains to the belief state or world-independent facts). This model is applied to a Battleship-style game where the task is to infer the locations of ships within a grid. The full model, incorporating all features, outperformed ablated versions with fewer features in predicting held-out human data (measured by log-likelihood). However, correlation with human judgments revealed that a model excluding expected information gain performed comparably to the full model. 

The paper appears technically sound overall. 

While the work succeeds as an AI project, its contributions to cognitive science are less convincing. The paper claims to “predict what questions people will ask” and to offer insights into “how people balance informativeness and complexity.” However, the evidence for these claims is weak. In the Battleship domain, human question selection appears to be only marginally influenced by expected information gain (according to the log-likelihood results, which have a non-negligible chance of being spurious based on bootstrap estimates) or not at all (as suggested by the correlation results, which are noisy due to sparse human data). These results provide limited basis for strong conclusions about how people balance these factors generally.

The paper is well-written and clear, and the results appear reproducible.

The related work section is incomplete. The notion of “modeling questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer” is similar to the concept of a “goal” in Hawkins (2015, "Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers"), and builds on the notion of a question-under-discussion (QUD) introduced by Roberts (1996, "Information structure in discourse: Towards an integrated formal theory of pragmatics”). Hawkins' questioner model also employs expected information gain and balances it with question complexity, albeit within a simpler domain.

This type of model – combining a compositional prior over questions with selection based on EIG and other factors – is a promising approach for both cognitive and AI modeling, and is likely to see further development. The main contribution of this paper is demonstrating the feasibility of generating interesting, compositional questions for a non-trivial domain using this approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to modeling question asking as program generation.
*   The model successfully generates novel, human-like questions within a defined domain.
*   The empirical results demonstrate that both informativeness and complexity are important factors in human question selection, even if their relative weighting isn't definitively established.
*   The work is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The cognitive science claims are not strongly supported by the experimental results. The evidence for the role of expected information gain is ambiguous.
*   The related work section omits relevant prior research on question meaning and goal-directed question asking (e.g., Hawkins, Roberts).
*   The domain is somewhat limited, and the generalizability of the findings to more complex scenarios is unclear.
