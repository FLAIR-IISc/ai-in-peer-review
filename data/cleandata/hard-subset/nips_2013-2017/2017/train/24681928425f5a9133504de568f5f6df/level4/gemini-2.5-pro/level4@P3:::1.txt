This paper presents a computational model of human question asking, framing the problem as one of program generation. The central idea is to represent questions as formal programs in a compositional language. When executed on a given world state, these programs return an answer. The authors propose a probabilistic log-linear model that defines a distribution over the space of possible programs, which are generated by a context-free grammar. This model favors questions that are both informative (high Expected Information Gain) and simple (low complexity, as measured by program length). The framework is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like game, where players ask questions to resolve ambiguity about the game board. The model is shown to predict the frequency of human questions in held-out contexts and can also generate novel, human-like questions not seen in the training data.

The work connects to several areas. It extends the traditional active learning paradigm, which typically involves simple label queries, to a much richer, compositional space of possible inquiries. It also contrasts with data-driven dialogue and question-generation systems, which are often less goal-directed or require massive datasets, by proposing a structured, knowledge-rich model that can operate with limited data in a new domain. The idea of representing concepts as programs builds upon related work in cognitive science, particularly the "language of thought" hypothesis.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The core contribution—framing goal-directed question asking as probabilistic program generation—is highly original and compelling. It provides a powerful formalism for moving beyond the simple queries common in active learning and towards the creative, compositional questions that characterize human intelligence. This conceptual shift is a significant strength of the paper.

2.  **Sound Methodology and Analysis:** The technical approach is solid. The use of a grammar to define the hypothesis space of questions and a log-linear model to score them based on features is a well-founded methodology. The lesion studies are particularly effective, clearly demonstrating that both informativeness (EIG) and complexity are critical for modeling human behavior. The finding that a model without a complexity penalty performs extremely poorly is an important result, highlighting that people do not simply optimize for raw information gain.

3.  **Strong Generative Capability:** A key success of the model is its ability to generate novel, contextually relevant, and clever questions that were not in the human dataset. This demonstrates that the system has learned a productive and compositional system for question asking, rather than simply memorizing or re-weighting examples from the training set.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, explaining their formal approach, and presenting their results in an accessible manner.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Generalizability and Scalability:** The primary weakness is the reliance on a highly constrained, "toy world" domain. The Battleship game is logically clean and unambiguous, and the grammar defining the space of questions is hand-crafted specifically for this domain. It is unclear how this approach would scale to more complex, noisy, and open-ended real-world scenarios. A significant amount of domain-specific knowledge engineering appears to be required to define the grammar and semantic primitives for any new task. A discussion of the challenges and potential paths toward applying this framework in less structured domains would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Disconnection from Natural Language:** The model operates on semantic representations (programs) rather than natural language text itself. The process of translating the original human questions into the formal language is a crucial step that is handled manually. This sidesteps the difficult problem of semantic parsing. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it is a significant one, as a complete question-asking system would need to bridge this gap.

3.  **Simplicity of Features:** While the included features (EIG, complexity, answer type) are shown to be effective, they are relatively simple. It is possible that more nuanced features related to cognitive effort, pragmatic considerations, or discourse structure could further improve the model's predictive accuracy.

### Summary of Arguments

*   **Pro Acceptance:**
    *   Proposes a novel and significant conceptual framework for question asking as program synthesis.
    *   The methodology is technically sound, and the experiments convincingly show that both informativeness and complexity are crucial components.
    *   Demonstrates impressive qualitative results by generating creative and meaningful new questions.
    *   The paper is clear, well-written, and addresses an important problem at the intersection of machine learning and cognitive science.

*   **Con Acceptance:**
    *   The evaluation is confined to a simple, highly structured domain, raising concerns about the scalability and generalizability of the approach.
    *   The model relies on hand-crafted grammars and sidesteps the challenge of parsing natural language into its formal program representation.
    *   The contribution is primarily in cognitive modeling, and its immediate impact on large-scale machine learning applications may be limited, potentially making it a better fit for a more specialized conference.

Overall, despite the limitations regarding scalability, I believe the novelty and elegance of the core idea make this a strong contribution. It presents a new and promising direction for thinking about active learning and building more intelligent, inquisitive agents. The paper is of high quality and is likely to be of significant interest to a substantial portion of the NIPS community.
