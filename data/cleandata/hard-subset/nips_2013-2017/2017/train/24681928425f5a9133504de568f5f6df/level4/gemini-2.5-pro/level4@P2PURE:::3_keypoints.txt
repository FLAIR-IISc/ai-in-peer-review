- Proposes a cognitive model predicting which questions people ask, scoring questions by a weighted sum of informativeness (EIG), complexity (question length), answer type, and relevance (belief-state vs world-independent facts).
- Models questions as programs that, when executed on a world state, yield answers.
- Evaluated in the Battleship domain to locate ships on a grid.
- Ablation studies: the full model outperforms reduced-feature models on held-out human log-likelihood.
- On correlation with human judgments, a model without the EIG feature performs comparably to the full model.
- Reviewer judges the paper technically sound.
- Reviewer doubts cognitive-science claims that results show how people balance informativeness and complexity; EIG’s influence appears minimal or non-robust (bootstrap suggests ~19% chance of spurious effect; correlation data sparse/noisy).
- Paper is well-written, clear, and methodology appears reproducible.
- Suggests acknowledging prior related work, notably Hawkins (2015) and QUD from Roberts (1996), which similarly trade off EIG and complexity.
- Views the contribution as stronger for AI than cognitive science: demonstrating feasibility of compositional question generation combined with decision-theoretic principles is a significant, promising step.
