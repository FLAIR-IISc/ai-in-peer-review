This paper introduces a computational model of human question asking, framing questions as formal programs that, when executed, yield an answer. The model learns a probability distribution over a compositional space of programs, favoring those that are both informative and concise. Evaluated on a dataset of questions asked by humans playing a spatial reasoning game (Battleship), the model successfully predicts human question frequencies and can generate novel, human-like questions. The authors demonstrate that both question informativeness (measured via expected information gain) and complexity are important factors in generating questions resembling those asked by humans.

The paper is technically sound and well-motivated, building upon the established field of active learning and aiming to bridge the gap between simplistic label-requesting algorithms and the rich, nuanced questions posed by humans. The authors clearly articulate their approach, positioning it within related work in cognitive science and artificial intelligence. They present a compelling argument for modeling questions as programs and provide a robust empirical evaluation using a carefully designed dataset. The inclusion of model variants (lesioning different features) allows for a nuanced understanding of the relative contributions of informativeness, complexity, and answer type to human question generation.

While the work demonstrates a successful application of the proposed model, its contribution to cognitive science is less clear-cut. The authors claim to provide insight into *how* people balance informativeness and complexity, but the results are somewhat ambiguous. The log-likelihood results suggest some influence of EIG, although with a degree of uncertainty (approximately 19% chance of being incorrect based on bootstrapping). Conversely, the correlation analysis shows little improvement by including EIG.  This leaves the core cognitive claim—that the model reveals the trade-offs people make—somewhat under-supported. Further investigation, potentially with larger datasets or more ecologically valid tasks, would be needed to strengthen this claim. Despite this limitation, the work offers a valuable computational framework for studying question asking. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The methodology is described in sufficient detail to allow for reproduction of the results. The code is available, which is commendable.  

A significant omission in the related work section is the connection to prior work explicitly framing questions as goals or leveraging similar concepts of question meaning as computations. The paper's core idea of modeling questions as programs that output answers echoes Hawkins (2015) (“Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers.”), which also utilizes expected information gain and balances it with question complexity, albeit in a simpler domain. Furthermore, the notion of modeling questions as computations relates to the question-under-discussion (QUD) framework introduced by Roberts (1996) (“Information structure in discourse: Towards an integrated formal theory of pragmatics.”).  Acknowledging these works would provide valuable context and demonstrate a broader awareness of the existing literature.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of modeling questions as programs within a probabilistic framework is a novel contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The model is well-defined, implemented, and evaluated.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The model demonstrates predictive accuracy on a human dataset and generates plausible, novel questions.
*   **Potential Impact:** The framework has the potential to influence future research in active learning, cognitive science, and dialog systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Cognitive Interpretability:** The cognitive claims regarding human trade-offs between informativeness and complexity are not strongly supported by the results.
*   **Related Work:**  Key prior work on question meaning and goal-directed questioning is not adequately addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field. While the cognitive science claims could be strengthened, the technical innovation and empirical results are compelling. I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors address the omission in related work and qualify their claims regarding the cognitive implications of the results.
