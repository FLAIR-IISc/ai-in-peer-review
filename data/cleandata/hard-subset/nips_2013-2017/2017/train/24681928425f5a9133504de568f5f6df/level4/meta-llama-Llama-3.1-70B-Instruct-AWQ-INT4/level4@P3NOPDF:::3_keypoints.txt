- Presents a cognitive model that predicts question types (given belief states) using a weighted sum of factors: informativeness (EIG), complexity, answer type, and relevance.  
- Models questions as programs that output answers when executed on a possible-world state.  
- Applied to the Battleship domain (locating ship clusters on a grid) to generate and evaluate questions.  
- Full model (all features) achieves higher held-out human-data log-likelihood than lesioned versions.  
- Omitting expected information gain (EIG) yields similar correlation with human judgments as the full model.  
- Influence of EIG on human question-asking is unclear: log-likelihood advantage is noisy (19% bootstrap error) and correlation results are noisy due to sparse data.  
- Domain simplicity (Battleship) limits the paper’s ability to generalize insights about how people balance informativeness and complexity.  
- Technically sound and clearly written, with thorough model explanation.  
- Fails to adequately discuss prior related work (e.g., Hawkins 2015, Roberts 1996); the program-as-question idea and EIG/complexity tradeoff are not entirely novel.  
- Contribution: demonstrates feasibility of generating compositional, interesting questions in a non-trivial domain and offers a potentially useful component for cognitive/AI models.
