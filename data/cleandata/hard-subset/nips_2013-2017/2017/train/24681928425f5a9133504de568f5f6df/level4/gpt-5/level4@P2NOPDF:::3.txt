The paper proposes a cognitive model that predicts which questions people ask in particular contexts (belief states). In this framework, the unnormalized log probability of a question is a weighted linear combination of four features: informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (roughly, question length), answer type, and relevance (whether the question targets the current belief state versus world-independent logical facts). The model is evaluated in the Battleship domain, where the goal is to locate several ships (clusters of grid cells) on a grid.

Empirically, the full model with all features outperforms ablated variants in predicting held-out human data, measured by log-likelihood. However, when evaluated by correlation with human judgments, a model omitting expected information gain performs comparably to the full model.

The paper appears technically sound. It is clearly written, and the methodology seems replicable.

Substantively, the work reads as a stronger AI contribution than a cognitive science one. The paper claims “We find that our model predicts what question people will ask” and “from a CogSci standpoint, our results show how people balance informativeness and complexity.” I remain unconvinced that the results reveal much about how people trade off these factors. In the Battleship setting, the evidence suggests either a modest influence of expected information gain (if we trust the log-likelihood comparison, which by the authors’ bootstrap has a 19% chance of being overturned) or no influence (if we focus on the correlation results, which are themselves noisy due to limited human data). Given these ambiguities, the paper does not convincingly establish how people balance informativeness and complexity in general.

The paper omits relevant prior work. A key claimed innovation is to model questions as programs that, when executed on a possible world state, return an answer. This is closely aligned with the notion of a “goal” in Hawkins (2015, “Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers”) and ultimately traces back to the question-under-discussion framework introduced by Roberts (1996, “Information structure in discourse: Towards an integrated formal theory of pragmatics”). Hawkins also models a questioner that trades off expected information gain against question complexity, albeit in a simpler domain.

Overall, I find the modeling approach compelling. A compositional prior over questions combined with selection based on expected information gain and other features is a sensible component for both cognitive and AI systems, and I expect it to see broader use. In my view, the paper’s main contribution is to demonstrate that this approach can generate interesting, compositional questions in a nontrivial domain.
