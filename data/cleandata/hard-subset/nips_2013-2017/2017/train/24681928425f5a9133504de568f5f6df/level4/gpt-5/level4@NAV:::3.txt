The paper introduces a cognitive model aimed at predicting which questions are asked in particular contexts (belief states). In this framework, the unnormalized log probability of a question is given by a weighted sum of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (approximately question length), answer type, and a “relevance” term (whether the question targets the belief state or only world-independent logical facts). The model is evaluated in the Battleship domain, where the task is to infer the locations of ships (clusters on a grid). The full model including all of these features outperformed ablated/lesioned variants with fewer features in predicting held-out human data (by log-likelihood). In terms of correlation with human judgments, the model that omitted EIG performed about as well as the full model.

The paper appears generally technically sound.

I see it as more successful as an AI contribution than as a cognitive science one. The paper claims that “We find that our model predicts what question people will ask” and that “from a CogSci standpoint, our results show how people balance informativeness and complexity.” However, I am not convinced we learn much about how people balance these factors. In the Battleship setting, the evidence suggests that people are either only slightly influenced by EIG (if we trust the log-likelihood analyses, which bootstrap estimates indicate have a 19% chance of being wrong) or not influenced by EIG at all (if we rely on the correlation analyses, which are quite noisy due to sparse human data). I don’t think these results justify strong conclusions about the general balance between these factors.

The paper is clearly written, and I could likely reproduce the results.

Some relevant prior work is missing. One apparent innovation is to “model questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer.” This corresponds to the notion of a “goal” in Hawkins 2015 (“Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers.”) and builds on the question-under-discussion (QUD) framework introduced by Roberts 1996 (“Information structure in discourse: Towards an integrated formal theory of pragmatics.”). The Hawkins questioner model likewise uses expected information gain and trades it off against question complexity, though in a much simpler domain.

I think this style of model (with a compositional prior over questions and selection based on EIG and other factors) is a sensible component for both cognitive and AI systems, and I expect it to see broader use. I view the primary contribution of this paper as demonstrating that one can generate interesting compositional questions for a non-trivial domain using this approach.
