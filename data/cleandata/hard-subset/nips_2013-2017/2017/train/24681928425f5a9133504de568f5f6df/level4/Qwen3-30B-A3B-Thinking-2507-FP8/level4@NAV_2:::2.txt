

The authors examine human question asking in a structured domain where answers are constrained to single-word responses (e.g., true/false, numbers, colors). They propose a computational framework modeling questions as programs in a lambda calculus-based language, where each question corresponds to a program that, when executed on a possible world state, yields an answer. The model employs a probabilistic generative approach with features capturing question informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (negative log probability under a probabilistic context-free grammar), answer type (Boolean/Number/Color/Location), and relevance to the domain. Evaluated on human-generated questions from a Battleship-style information-search game, the model quantitatively predicts question frequencies (with a Spearman correlation of 0.64) and qualitatively generates novel, human-like questions not present in the training data. Ablation studies demonstrate that all features contribute meaningfully to human-like question generation, with complexity being particularly critical.

The paper excels in clarity and technical quality. It is exceptionally well-written, with precise definitions of the programmatic question space, clear model specification (Sections 4.1–4.6), and thorough empirical validation (Table 1, Figure 2). The model directly addresses a key gap in active learning literature by formalizing how humans balance informativeness and complexity—resolving prior observations that question informativeness alone poorly predicts human behavior. This advances cognitive science by providing the first predictive model of open-ended human question asking, aligning with NIPS' emphasis on foundational contributions (Significance). The work is original in its fusion of compositional linguistics, probabilistic programming, and cognitive theory (Originality), and its rigorous evaluation across multiple model variants (Quality).

However, the narrow domain (Battleship) limits broader applicability to NIPS' general audience. While the approach is elegant, the grammar and features are highly domain-specific, and scaling to more complex domains (e.g., natural language interactions) remains unclear. The computational burden of the model (e.g., importance sampling over 150,000 questions) further constrains practical deployment. Though the cognitive science contribution is strong, the paper does not sufficiently articulate a compelling scenario where this method would provide novel insights beyond the current domain (Significance). Future work should demonstrate extension to a more generalizable task or discuss domain-agnostic components (e.g., logical connectives, set operations).

Pro: Novel program-based framework for question generation; rigorous quantitative/qualitative evaluation; resolves key cognitive science puzzle (informativeness vs. complexity); excellent writing and clarity.  
Con: Limited to a constrained domain; unclear path to broader applicability; computational complexity may hinder adoption.
