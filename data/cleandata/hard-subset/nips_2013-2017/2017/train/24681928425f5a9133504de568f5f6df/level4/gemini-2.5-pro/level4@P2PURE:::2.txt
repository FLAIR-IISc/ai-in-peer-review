This paper examines human question-asking in settings with K-ary answers. The authors manually define a Probabilistic Context-Free Grammar (PCFG) for a "battleship" domain where hidden colored shapes occupy a partially observable grid. The agent's task is to formulate a question, answerable with a single word, that maximally reduces uncertainty about the board's state. The PCFG serves as a prior over the space of possible questions, which are represented as statements in lambda calculus. The quality of a question is modeled as a linear combination of three factors: its informativeness (measured by expected information gain), its complexity (defined by its length or negative log probability under the PCFG), and its "answer type" (e.g., whether it elicits a true/false response or a color). Human data, sourced from a previously published study, was manually coded into the authors' grammatical formalism. Through a leave-one-out cross-validation analysis of ablated models, the authors demonstrate that all three factors make non-negligible contributions to predicting human questions, with complexity being the most significant predictor. The paper also presents a qualitative analysis of novel questions generated by the model.

The study addresses question-asking, a fundamentally important topic at the intersection of linguistics and hypothesis testing. The authors present an innovative framework that unifies modern linguistic and cognitive theory to model how people ask questions. This approach has the potential to inspire new research in active learning, particularly in developing agents that can ask more sophisticated and human-like questions. The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, and the model is evaluated appropriately using both quantitative and qualitative methods.

From a computational cognitive science perspective, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of self-directed learning. It offers a compelling explanation for why previous work found informativeness to be a poor predictor of human questions, demonstrating that its influence is secondary to complexity and moderated by the vast space of possible questions. This contribution to the cognitive science literature is substantial and could, on its own, merit publication at NIPS.

Despite these strengths, the proposed approach has serious limitations that may restrict its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. First, the reliance on manually translating natural language questions into a logical representation raises significant concerns about scalability. It is unclear how this process could be automated or how the framework could be extended to more complex domains relevant to the wider machine learning community. The battleship domain is relatively simple, with a small feature space, yet the proposed method is already described as computationally strained. While the core ideas—particularly enabling active learning with a richer set of questions—are compelling, the paper's impact would be strengthened if the authors could outline a clearer path for applying their technique to a domain of broader interest, highlighting where it could provide novel insights.

Minor comments:
1) It might be worth performing a nested model comparison using the log-likelihoods from the ablated models. This would allow for formal statistical significance tests of each component's contribution.
2) For future work on sequential question-asking, the authors may find the linguistics literature on formal semantics and pragmatics to be relevant. In particular, the "Questions under Discussion" (QuD) framework offers models for dialogue structure. The references below provide a starting point for this literature.

Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. *Linguistics and Philosophy*, 18(5), 459-527.
Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. *Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam)*, 17, 131-139.
