Summary
This paper proposes a probabilistic, compositional model of question generation that treats questions as executable programs over possible world states. A context-free grammar (augmented with lambda abstraction, map, and set operators) defines a rich, typed program space that captures the compositional structure of questions. A log-linear model assigns probabilities to programs using features for informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (via the grammar’s prior, favoring shorter programs), answer type (Boolean/Number/Color/Location), and a relevance filter to exclude programs not referencing the game state. Parameters are fit by maximizing the likelihood of human-produced questions using importance sampling to approximate the partition function and feature expectations. The model is evaluated on a Battleship-style dataset of 605 natural-language questions (mapped to programs) elicited from 40 participants across partially revealed 6×6 boards. Using leave-one-context-out evaluation (16 contexts; two dropped for computational reasons), the full model predicts human question distributions better (in approximate log-likelihood) than ablations that remove informativeness, complexity, or answer-type features. The model also generates novel, human-like questions not seen in the data.

Relation to prior work
The formulation builds on longstanding ideas in semantics and pragmatics—questions as partitions over possible worlds and the question-under-discussion (QUD) framework (Groenendijk & Stokhof; Roberts 1996)—and on cognitive/AI accounts that select utterances or queries to maximize information subject to costs. In particular, Hawkins et al. (2015) modeled question asking as a trade-off between EIG and question cost in simpler settings. The present work goes beyond these by (i) providing a generative grammar over executable, typed programs enabling combinatorial productivity and (ii) demonstrating quantitative prediction and creative generation in a nontrivial, structured domain. It also connects to program induction and semantic parsing traditions (e.g., “language of thought” approaches; Wang et al. 2015; Lake et al.), and to active learning beyond label queries.

Strengths
- Clear formulation: Representing questions as executable programs provides a precise semantics, a principled measure of informativeness via EIG over hypotheses, and a natural path to generation.
- Compositionality: The grammar covers the entire dataset and supports productivity; about 15% of human questions are context-unique, which a retrieval-only model would miss.
- Quantitative evaluation with ablations: The full model outperforms lesioned variants in held-out log-likelihood; complexity is especially critical (complexity-agnostic performs poorly).
- Qualitative creativity: The model samples novel, contextually relevant questions that appear human-like and vary in structure and type.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The paper is well-organized, the modeling choices are explicit, and optimization/inference details are given.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Cognitive claims may be stronger than the evidence. While the log-likelihood comparisons favor including informativeness, correlations between model energies and human frequencies (average Spearman ≈ 0.64) do not show a clear advantage for EIG: the information-agnostic model correlates ≈ 0.65, and bootstrap comparisons suggest a nontrivial chance (≈19%) that the informativeness advantage reverses. This tempers claims about how people balance informativeness and complexity. Suggestions:
  - Include targeted behavioral analyses that manipulate complexity costs or prior uncertainty to more directly test the EIG–complexity trade-off.
  - Report additional fit metrics less sensitive to sparsity (e.g., top-k accuracy, calibration, or hierarchical modeling of question frequency).
- Domain specificity and engineering. The grammar mixes domain-general operators with Battleship-specific predicates, contexts 1–2 are dropped, and a probabilistic “draw” rule is removed due to computational cost. This raises questions about scalability and generalization. Suggestions:
  - Clarify what portion of the grammar is reusable across domains, and provide a small-scale demonstration (or error analysis) in a second domain.
  - Analyze sensitivity to the grammar prior and proposal distribution; compare alternative complexity measures (e.g., description length vs. -log grammar prior).
- Approximate inference and evaluation. The reliance on importance sampling for both training and normalizer estimation may affect stability. Suggestions:
  - Report variance of the log-likelihood estimates, effective sample sizes, and convergence checks.
  - Where possible, validate with an alternative sampler or control experiments (e.g., reduced question space) to bound approximation error.
- Positioning with respect to prior theory. The paper cites relevant work but underplays its conceptual connections to QUD/partition semantics and to Hawkins et al. (2015), which also optimize EIG under a cost. Strengthen the related work section to clarify what is genuinely new (e.g., program-synthesis-based generation, typed functional language, quantitative prediction in a richer domain) versus what is an instantiation of prior frameworks.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with appropriate modeling choices and ablation analyses. Some computational compromises (dropped contexts/rules) and reliance on approximate normalization merit additional robustness checks.

Clarity
Well-written, well-structured, and sufficiently detailed to reproduce. Including the full grammar in the supplement and releasing code/data (the dataset link is provided) are strong points.

Originality
Moderately high. While the objective—choose questions by informativeness under cost—has precedents, the combination of (i) explicit program-based semantics, (ii) a generative grammar enabling creative synthesis, and (iii) quantitative modeling of open-ended question distributions in a nontrivial domain is novel.

Significance
Promising for both AI (goal-directed question generation) and cognitive modeling (a formal account of compositional question production). The empirical evidence supports the importance of complexity and provides mixed but suggestive support for informativeness; broader impact will depend on generalization beyond Battleship and tighter cognitive tests.

Pros
- Elegant program-based formalization with a compositional grammar.
- Solid ablations; strong evidence for complexity’s role.
- Demonstrates creative generation of novel, context-sensitive questions.
- Clear writing and transparent methodology.

Cons
- Limited and somewhat mixed evidence for the informativeness component driving human behavior.
- Domain-specific engineering and computational exclusions.
- Underdeveloped connection to QUD and prior question-asking models.
- Evaluation relies on approximate normalizers and sparse human counts.

Overall assessment
A thoughtful and technically competent paper that advances question generation by marrying program synthesis with active learning objectives. I recommend acceptance if the authors strengthen the discussion of prior theory (especially QUD and Hawkins et al.), temper cognitive claims about the EIG–complexity trade-off, and add robustness/sensitivity analyses to bolster the empirical claims.
