- Proposes a cognitive model that predicts when humans ask specific questions by combining informativeness (EIG), complexity, answer type, and relevance.  
- Evaluated in the Battleship domain; the full model outperforms lesioned variants on held-out human question log-likelihood.  
- Models excluding EIG correlate equally well with human judgments, making EIG’s role ambiguous.  
- Statistical evidence for EIG’s effect is weak (bootstrap indicates only ~19% probability the effect is non-zero).  
- Correlation results are noisy due to sparse human data, preventing strong cognitive conclusions.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-written, and reproducible.  
- Primary contribution is more to AI (question-generation systems) than to cognitive science.  
- Claims that the model reveals how people balance informativeness and complexity are overstated given ambiguous empirical support.  
- Overlooks prior work, notably Hawkins (2015) and Roberts (1996), which have similar ideas about questions as goals/QUD and trade-offs with EIG.  
- The compositional question-generation approach is compelling and feasible in a non-trivial domain, showing promise for dialog systems.  
- Recommendations: explicitly contextualize against Hawkins and Roberts, clarify/temper claims about EIG’s cognitive role, and moderate claims about understanding human balancing mechanisms.
