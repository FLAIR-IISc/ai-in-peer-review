- Introduces a cognitive model that predicts which questions people ask by assigning an energy (unnormalized log-probability) as a linear function of features: expected information gain (EIG), complexity, answer type, and a relevance indicator.  
- Instantiates the model in a Battleship-like domain to infer ship positions and sizes.  
- Full model including all features achieves higher held-out log-likelihood on human data than lesioned variants.  
- However, in correlation with human question frequencies, the model omitting EIG performs about as well as the full model.  
- Evidence for EIG’s influence is weak or inconsistent (bootstrap suggests a 19% chance the log-likelihood advantage reverses; correlation analyses are noisy due to sparse human data).  
- Reviewer finds the paper technically sound, well written, and likely reproducible.  
- Views the work as more compelling for AI (generating compositional questions) than as a strong cognitive-science claim about how people trade off informativeness and complexity.  
- Notes missing prior work: relates closely to Hawkins (2015) and the QUD framework (Roberts 1996), which also model tradeoffs between informativeness and complexity.  
- Main contribution: demonstrates that a compositional question-as-programs prior combined with EIG-related factors can generate interesting, compositional questions in a nontrivial domain.
