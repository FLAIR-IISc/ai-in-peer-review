- Proposes a cognitive model that predicts which questions people ask by scoring questions as a weighted sum of four features: informativeness (expected information gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance.  
- Models questions as programs that return answers when executed on possible world states and evaluates in the Battleship domain.  
- Full model outperforms ablated variants on held-out human data measured by log-likelihood.  
- When evaluated by correlation with human judgments, a model without expected information gain performs comparably to the full model.  
- Evidence for the role of expected information gain is ambiguous: log-likelihood advantage could be overturned (19% bootstrap) and correlation results are noisy due to limited data.  
- Paper is technically sound, clearly written, and methodologically replicable.  
- Claims about revealing how people trade off informativeness and complexity are unconvincing given the ambiguous empirical evidence.  
- Omits relevant prior work, notably Hawkins (2015) and the question-under-discussion framework from Roberts (1996), which cover similar ideas about goals and tradeoffs.  
- Overall contribution: a compelling compositional question-generation model that can produce interesting questions in nontrivial domains, more clearly an AI than a CogSci advance.
