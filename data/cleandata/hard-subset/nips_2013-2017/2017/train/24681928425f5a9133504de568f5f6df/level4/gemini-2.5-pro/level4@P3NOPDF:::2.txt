This paper examines human question-asking in a setting where questions elicit one of K possible answers. The authors develop their model in a "battleship"-style domain, where hidden, colored shapes are located on a partially observable grid. The goal is to ask a single-word-answer question that provides maximal information about the hidden state of the board.

The core of the authors' contribution is a cognitive model that formalizes the space of possible questions using a manually constructed probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG). This grammar generates questions represented as statements in lambda calculus, serving as a structured prior over the hypothesis space of questions. The model posits that the utility of a question is a linear combination of three factors: its informativeness (measured as expected information gain), its complexity (defined as its negative log-probability under the PCFG), and its answer type (e.g., whether it is a true/false question or one that asks for a color). The model was evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions from a previously published study, which were hand-coded into the proposed grammar. Through a leave-one-out cross-validation analysis of lesioned models, the authors demonstrate that all three factors make a non-negligible contribution to predicting human questions, with complexity being the most important component. The paper concludes with a qualitative analysis of novel questions generated by the model.

**Strengths:**

The paper presents a novel and well-executed study on the important topic of question-asking, which lies at the intersection of linguistics, cognitive science, and machine learning. Its primary strength is its innovative unification of modern linguistic theory (compositional semantics via a PCFG) with cognitive science principles (Bayesian models of cognition) to model a complex human behavior. This approach has the potential to inspire new lines of research in active learning, enabling agents to ask more sophisticated and human-like questions beyond simple feature queries. The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, and the model is appropriately evaluated from both quantitative and qualitative perspectives.

From a computational cognitive science perspective, this work makes a significant contribution to the literature on self-directed learning. It offers a compelling explanation for why previous models based solely on information gain have struggled to fully capture human question-asking behavior. By demonstrating the dominant role of representational complexity, the paper provides a more nuanced and accurate account of the cognitive trade-offs involved. This advance in modeling human intelligence is a valuable contribution in its own right and is relevant to the NIPS community.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite its strengths, the paper has significant limitations regarding its scalability and potential impact on the broader machine learning community, which tempers my enthusiasm.

1.  **Generalizability and Scalability:** The reliance on a hand-crafted grammar for a relatively simple "battleship" domain is a major bottleneck. It is unclear how this approach could be extended to more complex, open-ended domains of interest to the broader NIPS audience (e.g., visual question answering, interactive learning agents) without prohibitive manual effort in grammar engineering.

2.  **Natural Language Understanding:** The experiments rely on human questions that were manually translated into their corresponding logical forms. A practical system would require automatically parsing natural language questions into the semantic representations defined by the grammar—a notoriously challenging open problem.

3.  **Computational Cost:** The authors note that their method is already computationally strained in this small-scale setting. This raises further concerns about its applicability to problems with the larger state and action spaces commonly found in active learning and reinforcement learning tasks.

To broaden its appeal and impact, the paper would be substantially strengthened by a more detailed discussion of a potential path toward scalability or by outlining a clear application scenario where this rich, structured approach to question-asking would provide a distinct advantage over existing methods in the machine learning literature.

**Minor Comments:**

1.  It may be possible to strengthen the quantitative analysis by performing nested model comparisons using the log-likelihoods from the lesioned models. This would provide formal statistical tests for the significance of each model component.

2.  For future work on sequential question-asking, the authors might find the "Questions under Discussion" (QUD) framework from formal semantics and pragmatics to be relevant. This literature provides sophisticated models of how discourse context constrains the pragmatics of question-asking. The following are good pointers to this work:
    *   Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. *Linguistics and Philosophy*, 18(5), 459-527.
    *   Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. *Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam)*, 17, 131-139.
