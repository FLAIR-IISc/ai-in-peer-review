The paper presents a cognitive model that is intended to predict when certain questions are asked in certain contexts (belief states). Under this model, the unnormalized log probability of a question is defined as the weighted sum of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (roughly, question length), answer type, and "relevance" (whether the question refers to the belief state or just world-independent logical facts). This model is applied to the Battleship domain where the task is to figure out where within a grid a number of ships (clusters of grid elements) are located.

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere, particularly in the areas of active learning and cognitive science. The authors' approach to modeling questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer, is an innovative extension of previous work on goal-directed dialog systems and compositional semantics. The use of expected information gain and complexity as factors in determining question probability is also consistent with previous research in cognitive science.

The paper is technically sound, and the authors' evaluation of the model using human data from the Battleship domain is thorough and well-designed. The results show that the full model with all of the features mentioned above did better at predicting held-out human data (in terms of log-likelihood) than lesioned versions with fewer features. However, the correlation with human judgments is less clear, with the model that didn't take into account EIG doing about as well as the full model.

From a cognitive science perspective, the paper's contribution is somewhat limited. While the authors claim that their results show how people balance informativeness and complexity, the evidence for this is not strong. The results could be interpreted as showing that people are either influenced by EIG a little or not at all, but this does not provide a clear understanding of how people balance these factors in general.

The paper is well-written and clear, and the authors provide sufficient detail for the results to be reproduced. However, the paper is missing some prior work, particularly the notion of a "goal" used in Hawkins 2015 and the concept of a question-under-discussion (QUD) introduced by Roberts 1996. The authors should acknowledge and build on this previous work to strengthen their contribution.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and well-designed cognitive model that is applied to a non-trivial domain.
* The authors provide a thorough evaluation of the model using human data.
* The paper is well-written and clear, and the results are easy to follow.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper's contribution to cognitive science is limited, and the evidence for the authors' claims about how people balance informativeness and complexity is not strong.
* The paper is missing some prior work, which should be acknowledged and built on to strengthen the contribution.
* The results could be interpreted as showing that people are either influenced by EIG a little or not at all, but this does not provide a clear understanding of how people balance these factors in general.

Overall, I think the paper makes a contribution to AI research, particularly in the area of active learning and compositional semantics. However, the contribution to cognitive science is more limited, and the authors should be encouraged to strengthen their claims and build on previous work in this area.
