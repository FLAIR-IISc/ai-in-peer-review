- Task/setup: Models human single-word question-asking in a K-ary "battleship" grid using a PCFG over semantic forms.  
- Question representation: Questions formalized as lambda-calculus statements derived from the PCFG.  
- Objective: Questions scored by a linear combination of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity, and answer type.  
- Complexity measure: Complexity operationalized as question length or negative log probability under a uniform PCFG.  
- Data: Human question data from prior work was hand-coded into the grammar for evaluation.  
- Evaluation: Ablation via leave-one-out cross-validation shows all three factors contribute non-negligibly.  
- Key finding: Complexity is the most significant factor explaining human question choices.  
- Contribution: Introduces novel, richer question examples and integrates linguistic/cognitive theories with hypothesis testing.  
- Impact: Advances understanding of self-directed learning and resolves prior puzzles where informativeness poorly predicted human behavior.  
- Clarity and rigor: Paper is exceptionally clear, well-structured, and supported by quantitative and qualitative analyses.  
- Major limitation: Scalability and computational complexity even in the small domain limit broader ML/real-world applicability.  
- Missing extension: No clear pathway shown for applying the method to real-world domains; authors should identify compelling scenarios (e.g., medical diagnosis, robotics).  
- Minor suggestion 1: Use nested model comparisons with log-likelihoods for statistical tests of factor contributions.  
- Minor suggestion 2: For sequential question-asking, engage with "Questions under Discussion" formal semantics/pragmatics literature (e.g., Ginzburg 1995; Rojas-Esponda 2013).
