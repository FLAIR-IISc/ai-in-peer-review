- Introduces a cognitive model that predicts question choice from belief states using an unnormalized log-probability as a weighted sum of four features: informativeness (EIG), complexity, answer type, and relevance.

- Evaluated in the Battleship domain; the full four-feature model achieves higher held-out human log-likelihood than ablated variants.

- However, in correlation with human judgments the model without EIG performs comparably to the full model.

- Claims about revealing how people balance informativeness and complexity are not convincingly supported; the paper reads stronger as an AI/system contribution than a cognitive-science one.

- Results are ambiguous about EIG’s role: log-likelihood improvements are modest with a 19% bootstrap chance of being spurious, and correlation analyses are weakened by sparse human data.

- Paper is well-written, clear, and reproducible but omits key prior work (Hawkins 2015; Roberts 1996) that closely relates to modeling questions as programs/QUD and using EIG and complexity.

- Despite cognitive limitations, the compositional prior plus EIG-based selection is a coherent, promising framework and demonstrates feasibility of generating meaningful compositional questions in a non-trivial domain.
