1. Model represents questions as compositional programs (lambda-calculus) scored for informativeness (EIG) and concision (grammar length).  
2. Defines a probability distribution favoring informative, concise questions.  
3. Evaluated on a Battleship-like game; model predicts human questions and generates novel, contextually relevant questions.  
4. A log-linear model combining EIG, complexity, answer type, and relevance outperforms lesioned variants on log-likelihood.  
5. Technical approach is solid: clear probabilistic formulation, appropriate use of importance sampling, and sound leave-one-out evaluation.  
6. Empirical support for cognitive claims is mixed: correlation results show full and information-agnostic models have similar Spearman rho (0.64 vs 0.65).  
7. Authors should directly address the apparent contradiction that EIG may not be critical for predicting human question frequencies.  
8. Paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and appears reproducible; figures (e.g., generated questions) are effective.  
9. Important prior work is not discussed (e.g., Roberts 1996 on QUD; Hawkins et al. 2015 on EIG/complexity) and should be acknowledged.  
10. Primary novelty is scaling the EIG/complexity program-based approach to a complex, compositional domain — an important engineering advance.  
11. The work is significant for active learning and cognitive modeling, enabling more creative, open-ended question generation than template-based systems.  
12. Arguments for acceptance: novel application, technical soundness, generativity, and broader significance.  
13. Arguments for rejection: missing key citations and weak/conflicting evidence for the claimed cognitive trade-off.  
14. Recommendation: accept, contingent on revising to thoroughly discuss related prior work and temper cognitive claims.
