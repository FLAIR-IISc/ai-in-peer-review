- Proposes a novel model treating questions as executable programs in a PCFG, using lambda calculus to yield single-word answers.  
- Frames question generation as program synthesis over a compositional space.  
- Defines question quality with a log-linear model combining informativeness (EIG), complexity (grammar log-probability), answer type, and relevance.  
- Key insight: human question preferences balance informativeness with simplicity, explaining prior failures of informativeness-only models.  
- Evaluated on 605 human questions in a Battleship-like domain; full model outperforms ablated variants and predicts human questions better.  
- Model generates novel, human-like questions not present in training data.  
- Strength: technically rigorous with lesion studies, cross-validation, and clear statistical validation.  
- Strength: exceptionally clear presentation and well-designed grammar/examples (e.g., lambda set operations).  
- Strength: original integration of linguistic compositionality and probabilistic program synthesis for open-ended question asking.  
- Significance: addresses a gap in active learning and advances cognitive theories of self-directed learning.  
- Weakness: limited scope—Battleship domain is constrained, limiting generalizability to real-world settings.  
- Weakness: scalability concerns—PCFG approach may be computationally intensive for complex domains like visual scenes.  
- Weakness: related work discussion could better cite recent program induction and formal semantics literature (e.g., Lake, Ginzburg, Rojas-Esponda).  
- Pro/Con summary: first model to predict compositionality and novelty in human questions but hampered by domain specificity and grammar engineering requirements.  
- Recommendation: Accept, with minor revisions to clarify scalability and expand related-work citations.
