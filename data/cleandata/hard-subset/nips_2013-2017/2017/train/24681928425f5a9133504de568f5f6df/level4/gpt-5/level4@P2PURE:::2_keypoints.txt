- Models human question-asking in a Battleship-like grid using a hand-specified PCFG and lambda-calculus question representations.
- Scores questions by a linear combination of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (length or negative log PCFG probability), and answer type.
- Human data were encoded into the grammar; leave-one-out cross-validation with lesioned models shows all factors matter, with complexity clearly dominant.
- Presents novel questions and provides clear, well-written quantitative and qualitative evaluation; has potential to inspire richer active-learning models.
- Cognitive-science contribution: explains why informativeness alone poorly predicts human question choice—complexity and question-space size are important—advances self-directed learning theory.
- Major limitation: scalability and generality are unclear—the small Battleship domain and computational strain raise questions about applicability to broader NIPS-relevant domains.
- Minor suggestion: perform nested model comparisons using lesioned-model log-likelihoods to test statistical significance.
- Minor suggestion: connect to sequential question-asking literature (Questions Under Discussion), e.g., Ginzburg (1995) and Rojas-Esponda (2013).
