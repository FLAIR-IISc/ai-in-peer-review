The authors examine human question asking where answers can take on multiple values. They define a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) for a "Battleship" domain, where hidden colored shapes exist within a partially observable grid – some tiles are revealed, indicating either a ship of a specific color or an empty space. The task for an agent is to ask a question with a single-word answer that maximizes information gain about the board’s state. This PCFG serves as a prior over questions, which are formally represented as statements in lambda calculus. Question quality is evaluated using a linear function incorporating informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (measured by question length or negative log probability under the PCFG), and answer type (e.g., true/false or color). Human question data, sourced from a prior publication, was translated into their grammar. The authors compared variants of the model using leave-one-out cross-validation, finding that all factors contribute to performance, with complexity being particularly important. Finally, they present examples of novel questions generated by the model.

Question-asking is a crucial area within linguistics and hypothesis testing. The authors offer an innovative perspective, unifying modern linguistic and cognitive theory to investigate how humans formulate questions, potentially fostering novel research in active learning by enabling models to ask more sophisticated queries. The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and thoroughly evaluated both quantitatively and qualitatively. 

From a computational cognitive science perspective, this work advances the field of self-directed learning. Previous research indicated that question informativeness alone is a poor predictor of human question asking. This follow-up study helps explain this phenomenon by demonstrating the importance of complexity, and potentially the limitations of considering only a limited space of possible questions. Thus, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the cognitive science literature, potentially justifying its publication in NIPS.

However, there are significant limitations to their approach that may restrict its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. While automating the translation of human question text into semantic logic may be feasible, it’s unclear how the approach would scale or generalize to domains of interest to the wider community. The Battleship domain is relatively small, with only a few key features, and their method is already computationally demanding. Nevertheless, the core ideas—active learning with richer questions—are interesting enough to warrant consideration, although the authors may need to clearly articulate a compelling scenario where their technique could be applicable and provide novel insights.

Minor comments:

1) A more rigorous comparison of the models could be achieved through nested model comparison using likelihood ratio tests, assessing the statistical significance of the differences in log-likelihoods between the lesioned models.
2) For future work addressing sequential question asking, consider relevant literature in formal semantics and pragmatics, particularly research on “Questions under Discussion.” This work could provide a useful theoretical foundation. Some pointers to this literature include:
Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. *Linguistics and Philosophy*, *18*(5), 459-527.
Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. *Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam)*, *17*, 131-139.



Arguments for acceptance:

*   Novel and insightful approach to modeling human question asking as program generation.
*   Clear writing and thorough evaluation.
*   Advances the field of self-directed learning in cognitive science.
*   Demonstrates the importance of complexity, alongside informativeness, in human question choice.

Arguments against acceptance:

*   Limited scalability and generalizability to domains beyond the Battleship game.
*   Computational demands may be high for larger, more complex problems.
*   Requires substantial domain knowledge and manual grammar construction.
