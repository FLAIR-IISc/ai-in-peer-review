- Models human question asking as program generation/selection in a Battleship-like grid world using lambda-calculus-style programs that return single-word answers.  
- Uses a hand-crafted PCFG as a prior over compositional question space and a log-linear energy combining informativeness (EIG), complexity, and answer type.  
- Fits the model to 605 human questions (hand-coded into the grammar) and evaluates with leave-one-out cross-validation and lesioned variants.  
- Main empirical finding: all features matter, with complexity penalties being the most critical to match human behavior.  
- Demonstrates the ability to synthesize plausible novel questions beyond the training set.  
- Relates to active learning, question semantics (questions as partitions), and program-based semantic parsing approaches.  
- Contrasts with template-based dialog and VQG systems by emphasizing goal-directed, context-sensitive, compositional question formation.  
- Technically sound, well-presented, and appropriately evaluated with quantitative and qualitative analyses.  
- Clear writing, explicit modeling assumptions, and illustrative examples linking formalism to human data.  
- Original integration of executable program representations and PCFG-based priors to predict human question distributions.  
- Significant for cognitive science (predictive account of open-ended question asking) and suggests directions for ML agents that ask structured questions.  
- Main limitation: domain specificity and unclear scalability beyond the small Battleship domain due to computational demands.  
- Reliance on hand-crafted semantic representations and manual mapping from natural language limits practical applicability and generalization.  
- Broader impact is speculative without concrete demonstrations in larger, real-world domains.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel, coherent framework with careful human-data evaluation and insights about complexity vs. informativeness.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited generality, scalability concerns, and need for hand-engineered grammars with no end-to-end NL mapping.  
- Suggestions: report formal nested model comparisons/statistical tests, articulate concrete application scenarios, and discuss scaling strategies (pruning, amortized inference, learned priors).  
- Suggests connecting sequential question asking to formal semantics/pragmatics (QUD literature) and cites Ginzburg (1995) and Rojas-Esponda (2013).
