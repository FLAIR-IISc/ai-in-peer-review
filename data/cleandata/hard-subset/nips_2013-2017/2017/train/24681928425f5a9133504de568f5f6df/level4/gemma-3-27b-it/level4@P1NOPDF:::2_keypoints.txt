- Proposes a PCFG over questions in a Battleship-like grid domain, representing questions in lambda calculus and optimizing a linear score combining expected information gain, complexity, and answer type.
- Questions have single-word answers (e.g., color or true/false) and the PCFG acts as a prior with complexity measured by question length or negative log probability under the grammar.
- Human question data from prior work was manually encoded into the grammar to fit the model.
- Evaluated lesioned models via leave-one-out cross-validation; all factors matter, with complexity being the most influential predictor of human question choice.
- The model generates novel questions, and the paper presents both quantitative and qualitative evaluations.
- Paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and offers an innovative unification of linguistic and cognitive theory for question-asking behavior.
- Important cognitive-science contribution: explains why informativeness alone poorly predicts human questions—complexity and question space constraints play key roles.
- Major limitation: small, low-dimensional Battleship domain and computational demands raise questions about scalability to broader, more interesting domains.
- Concern about broader NIPS appeal unless authors present realistic application scenarios demonstrating where this technique yields novel insights.
- Minor suggestion: perform nested model comparisons using log-likelihoods of lesioned models to test statistical significance.
- Minor suggestion: consider sequential question-asking frameworks from linguistics (e.g., Questions Under Discussion) and relevant references (Ginzburg 1995; Rojas-Esponda 2013).
