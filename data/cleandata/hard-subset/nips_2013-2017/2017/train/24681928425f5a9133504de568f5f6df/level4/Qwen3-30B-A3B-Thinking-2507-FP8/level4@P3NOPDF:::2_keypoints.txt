- Task: models human question-asking in a K-ary "battleship" grid with hidden colored shapes.  
- Model: hand-constructed PCFG generating lambda-calculus questions.  
- Scoring: linear combination of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (length / negative log PCFG prob), and answer type.  
- Data & eval: human questions from prior work hand-coded into grammar; evaluated with leave-one-out cross-validation.  
- Results: all three factors matter; complexity is the strongest predictor; answer type also important; informativeness is secondary.  
- Explanation: complexity dominates likely because of the combinatorial explosion of possible questions in the domain.  
- Demonstration: authors generate novel questions to showcase model capabilities.  
- Contribution: unifies formal semantics, cognitive theory, and active learning to explain human hypothesis testing.  
- Strengths: clear presentation, rigorous quantitative and qualitative evaluation, strong theoretical grounding.  
- Main weakness: very narrow, toy domain (small grid, few features) limiting generality.  
- Scalability concerns: PCFG is hand-crafted and approach is computationally intensive; extension to richer/natural domains is unaddressed.  
- Missing ML impact: paper lacks a compelling, concrete application to broader ML areas (robotics, healthcare, large-scale NLP).  
- Suggested analyses/future work: perform nested model comparisons (log-likelihoods) and engage sequential-question semantics literature (e.g., Ginzburg, Rojas-Esponda).  
- Recommendation: valuable cognitive-science contribution but currently not sufficiently scalable or broadly applicable for NeurIPS; consider specialized venues or add scalability/application work.
