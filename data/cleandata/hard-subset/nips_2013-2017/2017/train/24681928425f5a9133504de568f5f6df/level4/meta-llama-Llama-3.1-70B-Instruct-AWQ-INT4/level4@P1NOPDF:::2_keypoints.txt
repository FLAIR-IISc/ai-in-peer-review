- Studies human question asking in a K-ary-answer "battleship" domain with hidden colored shapes on a partially observable grid.  
- Defines a hand-crafted PCFG prior over questions, where questions are lambda-calculus statements and some tiles reveal colored ship parts or empty cells.  
- Agent's task is to ask a single-word-answer question that maximally informs about the board state.  
- Question goodness is a linear combination of expected information gain, complexity (length or -log probability under the PCFG with uniform rewrite rules), and answer type (e.g., yes/no vs. color).  
- Human question data from a prior paper was hand-coded into the grammar for evaluation.  
- Lesioned-model comparisons using leave-one-out cross-validation show all factors matter, with complexity being the dominant predictor.  
- The model produces novel questions and is evaluated both quantitatively and qualitatively; the paper is clear and well-written.  
- From a computational cognitive science perspective, the work advances self-directed learning and helps explain why informativeness alone poorly predicted human question asking previously.  
- The approach has potential to enrich active learning by enabling richer, more sophisticated questions.  
- Major limitation: the small, low-feature battleship domain and computational strain raise doubts about scalability and relevance to broader NIPS audiences.  
- Authors should clarify scenarios or domains where the technique can scale and yield novel insights for a wider community.  
- Minor suggestion: perform nested model comparisons with lesioned-model log-likelihoods to test statistical significance.  
- Minor suggestion: consider related linguistics/pragmatics literature on sequential question-asking (e.g., Questions under Discussion; Ginzburg 1995; Rojas-Esponda 2013).
