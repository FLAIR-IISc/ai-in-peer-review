- Proposes a cognitive model that predicts question selection by assigning unnormalized log-probabilities as a weighted sum of four features: informativeness (EIG), complexity (question length), answer type, and relevance.  
- Applies the model to the Battleship domain to locate hidden ships on a grid.  
- Full model including all four features outperforms ablated versions on held-out human question-choice log-likelihood.  
- Correlation between model predictions and human judgments is similar for the full model and a version without EIG.  
- Evidence for people balancing informativeness (EIG) and complexity is weak or ambiguous in this dataset.  
- Bootstrap estimates indicate a 19% chance the log-likelihood improvement could be spurious.  
- Noisy correlation results and sparse human data limit strong cognitive conclusions about EIG effects.  
- Paper is technically sound, clearly written, and reproducible.  
- Related-work section omits prior goal-directed question-generation research, notably Hawkins (2015) and Roberts (1996) on QUD and EIG-based questioners.  
- The compositional prior plus selection by EIG/other factors is a valuable modelling approach for cognitive and AI systems.  
- Main contribution is demonstrating feasibility of generating meaningful compositional questions in a non-trivial domain, with potential for broader application.
