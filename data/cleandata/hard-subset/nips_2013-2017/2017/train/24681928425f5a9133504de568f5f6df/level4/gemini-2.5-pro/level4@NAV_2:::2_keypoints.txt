- Presents a computational model framing human question asking as probabilistic program generation in a compositional language.  
- Defines a probabilistic context-free grammar for a "Battleship"-like domain as a prior over question programs.  
- Models question probability with a log-linear function of features: expected information gain (EIG), complexity, and answer type.  
- Fits model parameters to human-generated questions translated into formal programs from prior data.  
- Uses leave-one-out cross-validation and lesion studies to evaluate feature contributions.  
- Finds that informativeness, complexity, and answer type all help predict human questions, with complexity especially critical.  
- Demonstrates the model can generate novel, contextually relevant questions not in the training set.  
- Strength: original and significant integration of cognitive science, formal linguistics, and machine learning ideas.  
- Strength: thorough quantitative and qualitative evaluation; highlights complexity outperforming raw informativeness.  
- Strength: paper is very clear and well organized despite technical content.  
- Weakness: scalability concerns to larger, more complex domains; grammar and inference appear strained.  
- Weakness: heavy dependence on manual translation of natural language to program representations limits practical applicability.  
- Minor: suggest formal nested model tests for statistical significance of feature contributions.  
- Minor: recommend connecting to "Questions under Discussion" (QUD) literature for sequential question-asking models (Ginzburg 1995; Rojas-Esponda 2013).
