This paper presents a computational model of human question-asking, framing it as a problem of probabilistic program generation. The core idea is that questions can be represented as programs in a compositional language (akin to LISP) which, when executed on a world state, return an answer. The authors develop a probabilistic model over this space of programs, defined by a hand-crafted grammar. This model, a log-linear formulation, favors questions that are both informative (high Expected Information Gain) and simple (low complexity, derived from the grammar). The model is evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions from a "Battleship"-like game, where participants ask open-ended questions to resolve ambiguity about the game state. The authors demonstrate that their model can predict the frequency of human questions in held-out contexts and can also generate novel, plausible questions not seen in the training data. This work connects ideas from active learning, which typically involves simple queries, with program induction models from cognitive science, applying them to the generative task of question-asking.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The central contribution—modeling goal-directed, open-ended question-asking as probabilistic program synthesis—is novel and conceptually elegant. It provides a significant extension to the standard active learning paradigm, moving beyond simple label requests to a much richer, compositional space of queries. This formal approach to a complex cognitive ability is a valuable contribution and could inspire new directions in creating more inquisitive and human-like AI agents.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed model is technically sound. The use of a log-linear model to balance informativeness (EIG) and complexity is well-motivated and implemented effectively. The ablation studies (lesioning model components) provide clear evidence for the importance of both features. In particular, the finding that the "complexity-agnostic" model performs very poorly provides strong support for the hypothesis that humans do not merely optimize for information gain but are also constrained by a simplicity bias.

3.  **Compelling Results:** The model demonstrates a solid ability to predict human behavior, achieving a good correlation with human question frequencies in a challenging leave-one-out cross-validation setup. The qualitative results, where the model generates novel and contextually relevant questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?"), are particularly compelling and effectively showcase the generative power of the framework. This ability to account for the productivity of human questioning is a key strength over models that might simply learn to re-rank existing questions.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection):**

1.  **Limited Scope and Generalizability:** The primary weakness is the reliance on a highly constrained and logically unambiguous "toy" domain (the Battleship game). While this serves as a clean testbed, it raises serious questions about the scalability and applicability of the approach to more complex, real-world problems. The semantics of the world are perfectly defined, which sidesteps many of the hard challenges in question-asking, such as ambiguity, unstated assumptions, and common-sense reasoning.

2.  **Dependence on Hand-Crafted Grammar:** The approach is fundamentally dependent on a domain-specific, hand-engineered grammar to define the space of possible questions. This represents a significant knowledge engineering bottleneck. While the authors note that some components are domain-general (e.g., logical connectives, set operators), many core functions are tailored to the game. It is unclear how this framework would be extended to a new domain without considerable manual effort, which limits its practical significance as a general-purpose machine learning method.

3.  **Bypassing Natural Language Processing:** The model operates on semantic program representations, entirely sidestepping the difficult problem of parsing natural language questions into these forms. While the paper's focus is on the cognitive modeling of question generation, the lack of an interface with natural language text makes it an incomplete system from an AI or ML perspective. The authors suggest this could be bridged by other tools, but this is a non-trivial challenge that is central to building a truly usable question-asking system.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper introduces a thought-provoking and original framework for modeling human question-asking as program generation. The approach is technically sound, and the results provide strong evidence that people balance informativeness and complexity when formulating questions. The ability to generate novel, creative questions is a key strength.

However, the work is constrained by its reliance on a simple, unambiguous domain and a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar. This raises concerns about the generalizability and scalability of the method, which may limit its immediate impact on the broader NIPS community. The paper feels more aligned with computational cognitive science than mainstream machine learning, though its connections to active learning are clear.

Overall, despite the limitations, the novelty of the core idea and the quality of the execution make it a worthwhile contribution. It presents a promising new direction for research into more sophisticated active learning and interpretable generative models. I believe the strengths outweigh the weaknesses and recommend acceptance.
