

This paper proposes a computational model of human question asking by treating questions as executable programs that operate on a world state to produce answers. The authors model questions as programs in a simple programming language based on lambda calculus and LISP, where the program's structure reflects the compositional nature of human questions. They introduce a probabilistic generative model that predicts human question choices based on four key features: expected information gain (EIG), question complexity, answer type (Boolean, numerical, color, location), and relevance to the game board. The model is evaluated on a dataset of 605 natural language questions asked by humans in a Battleship-style game, where players must identify hidden ship configurations through strategic questioning.

The work relates to several strands of prior research. It extends active learning by modeling richer question types beyond simple label requests [21], aligns with program synthesis approaches for language understanding [12, 24], and builds on cognitive science work on compositional semantics [4, 10, 15]. The paper's approach to modeling questions as programs that output answers when executed on a world state connects to formal semantics work [6, 9, 18] and recent advances in program induction for visual reasoning [13]. However, the authors could have more thoroughly engaged with related work in visual question generation [16, 23] and goal-directed dialog systems [1, 25], which also address question generation but with different constraints.

The paper's strengths include its clear formalization of questions as programs, which provides a principled way to model compositional question structure. The probabilistic model effectively incorporates multiple relevant features (informativeness, complexity, answer type, relevance) and demonstrates that all are necessary for accurately predicting human question patterns. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with ablation studies showing that removing any key feature (informativeness, complexity, or answer type) significantly degrades performance. The model also successfully generates novel, human-like questions not present in the training data, demonstrating its creative potential.

However, several weaknesses limit the paper's contribution to the NIPS community. First, the problem domain (Battleship game with constrained question types) is highly specific and narrow, which raises questions about the broader applicability of the approach. The model's performance is impressive within this narrow context, but it's unclear how the method would scale to more open-ended question-asking scenarios or real-world applications. Second, while the authors claim their work represents a "step toward more capable active learning machines," they provide little evidence that their approach advances active learning beyond the specific Battleship context. The paper doesn't address how this framework would integrate with more complex machine learning systems or real-world domains.

Regarding originality, the core idea of modeling questions as programs has been explored in related work [12, 13, 24], and the specific application to question asking in a constrained game setting doesn't represent a significant departure from prior NIPS contributions. The paper's novelty lies more in the specific implementation and evaluation on this dataset than in a fundamentally new theoretical insight. The authors would have strengthened their contribution by more explicitly distinguishing their approach from related work on program induction and question generation.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with appropriate methodological details and a clear presentation of results. The figures effectively illustrate the model's capabilities, and the supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. However, the significance of the work for the broader NIPS community is questionable. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how this model advances the state of the art in active learning, program synthesis, or question generation beyond the specific Battleship context. The results don't provide new insights about human cognition that would interest cognitive scientists, nor do they offer practical tools for machine learning practitioners.

The paper's limitations include its domain-specific grammar, which would require significant re-engineering to apply to other domains. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't provide a compelling argument for why their approach would generalize to more complex question-asking scenarios. The evaluation is limited to a single game context, and the authors don't demonstrate the model's performance on more diverse or challenging question-asking tasks.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a technically sound model that successfully predicts human question patterns in a constrained game, the work lacks sufficient significance and broader applicability to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The ideas are largely incremental within the context of program synthesis and active learning, and the specific application to Battleship questions doesn't demonstrate meaningful advancement beyond existing approaches. The model's narrow scope and limited generalizability to real-world question-asking scenarios make it of limited interest to the NIPS community, which prioritizes work with broader impact and significance.

Arguments for rejection: The problem is too narrow and constrained; the contribution is incremental rather than novel; the significance for the broader NIPS community is limited; the model lacks generalizability beyond the specific game domain.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper provides a clear formalization of questions as programs; the probabilistic model incorporates multiple relevant features; the evaluation demonstrates the importance of question complexity and informativeness; the model successfully generates novel, human-like questions.
