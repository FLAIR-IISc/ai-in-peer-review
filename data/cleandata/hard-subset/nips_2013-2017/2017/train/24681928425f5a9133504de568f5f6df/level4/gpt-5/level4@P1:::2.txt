This paper models human question asking as program generation and selection in a constrained domain (a Battleship-like grid world). Questions are represented as lambda-calculus-style programs that, when executed on a hypothesized world state, return a single-word answer (Boolean, number, color, or location). A hand-crafted probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) over this domain serves as a prior over the compositional space of questions. The model assigns probabilities to questions via a log-linear energy function combining features for informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (negative log probability under the PCFG with uniform rule choices), and answer type. Using a previously collected dataset of 605 human questions (hand-coded into the grammar), the authors fit the model and compare lesioned variants in a leave-one-out cross-validation setup. They find all factors contribute, with complexity being most critical, and demonstrate the ability to synthesize plausible novel questions.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects active learning (typically limited to label queries) with richer, compositional question asking, drawing on linguistic theories of question semantics (e.g., questions as partitions over worlds) and program-based representations used in semantic parsing and probabilistic program induction. It contrasts with template-based dialog and VQG systems that lack goal-directed, context-sensitive question formation. This synthesis brings cognitive and linguistic insights to machine learning settings.

Quality: The approach is technically sound and carefully presented. The model operationalizes compositional question generation with a clear grammar and a reasonable feature set capturing informativeness, complexity, and type preferences. The evaluation is appropriate: quantitative prediction of human question distributions via held-out contexts, comparisons to lesioned models, and qualitative generation of novel questions. The conclusion that complexity is a key determinant of human question choice is well supported, and the paper appropriately contextualizes why EIG alone underpredicts human behavior.

Clarity: The paper is very well written and easy to follow. Assumptions, modeling choices, and evaluation procedures are clearly described. The connection between the formalism (programs) and human data is explicit, and the examples are illustrative.

Originality: Treating questions as executable programs within a PCFG-defined compositional space, and learning a probabilistic model over this space to predict human question distributions, is an original and compelling integration of ideas from active learning, cognitive science, and formal semantics.

Significance: For computational cognitive science, the paper provides a concrete, predictive account of open-ended human question asking and clarifies the role of complexity vs. informativeness. For ML, it suggests a path toward agents that ask more structured, goal-directed questions. However, the practical impact for the broader NIPS audience may be limited by scalability and domain specificity (see weaknesses).

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation of question asking as program generation and selection over a compositional hypothesis space.
- Strong empirical grounding in human data; both quantitative prediction and qualitative generation are demonstrated.
- Insightful result that complexity penalties are crucial to match human question behavior, helping reconcile prior findings that informativeness alone is insufficient.
- Well executed comparisons with lesioned models showing distinct contributions of informativeness, complexity, and answer type.

Weaknesses
- Domain specificity and scalability: The Battleship domain is small with limited feature types; the approach is already computationally demanding. It is unclear how the method would scale to richer domains or more complex naturalistic settings without substantial engineering.
- Dependence on hand-crafted semantic representations: Human questions are hand-coded into the grammar, and mapping from natural language to formal programs is not addressed. This limits practical applicability and generalization to broader ML tasks.
- Broader impact remains speculative: While the ideas are promising, the paper does not provide a concrete scenario outside the toy domain where the approach would offer clear benefits over existing ML question-asking frameworks.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, coherent framework bridging cognitive theory and ML.
- Careful quantitative and qualitative evaluation with human data.
- Advances understanding of human question asking (complexity vs. informativeness).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited generality and unclear scalability to domains of interest to the NIPS community.
- Requires hand-engineered grammars and semantic annotations; no end-to-end pipeline from natural language.

Suggestions and minor comments
- Consider formal nested model comparisons using the log-likelihoods of lesioned models to provide statistical tests of the contribution of each feature set.
- To broaden relevance, articulate a clear application scenario (e.g., scientific discovery assistants, interactive vision) where compositional question generation would be feasible and beneficial, and discuss computational strategies for scaling (e.g., pruning, amortized inference, or learned priors over programs).
- For sequential question asking, connect more explicitly to formal semantics and pragmatics work on the dynamics of discourse and Questions under Discussion (QUD). Pointers:
  - Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. Linguistics and Philosophy, 18(5), 459–527.
  - Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. SemDial (DialDam), 131–139.

Overall, this is a clear, thoughtful, and well-executed paper with strong cognitive-science contributions and intriguing implications for active learning. Its main limitations concern scalability and generality beyond the hand-engineered Battleship domain.
