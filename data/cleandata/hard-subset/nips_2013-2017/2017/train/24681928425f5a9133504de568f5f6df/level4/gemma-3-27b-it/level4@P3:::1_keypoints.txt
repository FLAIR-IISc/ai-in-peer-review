- Introduces a computational framework modeling human questions as executable programs and evaluates it in a “Battleship”-like game.  
- Represents questions with a compositional program-like language and uses a log-linear model with features for informativeness, complexity, answer type, and relevance.  
- Builds on prior work in linguistics, program synthesis, and active learning to offer a more expressive approach to information-seeking queries.  
- Successfully integrates program synthesis, Bayesian inference, and cognitive science into a generative model of question asking.  
- Empirically predicts human question frequencies and generates plausible, novel questions in the defined task.  
- Ablation studies show feature importance, particularly highlighting question complexity as critical for human-like behavior.  
- Task is well-defined but relatively constrained, raising concerns about generalization to real-world, complex domains.  
- Reliance on a predefined grammar enables representation but introduces artificiality and unclear transferability to new domains.  
- Lacks comparisons to simpler baseline models (e.g., random sampling weighted by length) beyond feature ablation.  
- Descriptions of some features, notably “relevance,” need more elaboration and clarity.  
- Scalability and effort required to adapt the grammar to other domains are potential practical concerns.  
- Suggests future work to ground the program representations in natural language and explore NLP integration.  
- Overall assessment: novel, promising, well-written contribution with limitations on generalizability and baseline evaluation.  
- Recommendation: weak acceptance, urging authors to address generalizability and baseline comparisons in future work.
