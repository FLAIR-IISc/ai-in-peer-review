The authors investigate human question-asking behavior in a "battleship" domain, where the task is to ask a single-word question that maximizes information gain about the state of a partially observable grid. They define a hand-crafted probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) for this domain, where questions are represented as statements in lambda calculus. The PCFG serves as a prior over questions, and question goodness is evaluated as a linear function of its informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (length or negative log probability under the PCFG), and answer type.

The authors leverage human data from a previously published paper, which was manually encoded into their grammar. Through leave-one-out cross-validation, they compare lesioned versions of the model and find that all factors contribute significantly to question goodness, with complexity being the most important. The paper also presents a set of novel questions generated by the model.

This work offers a valuable perspective on question-asking, a crucial domain in linguistics and hypothesis testing. By integrating modern linguistic and cognitive theories, the authors provide a novel framework for understanding how people ask questions, which has the potential to inspire new research in active learning with more sophisticated question-asking models. The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, and the model is thoroughly evaluated from both quantitative and qualitative perspectives.

From a computational cognitive science perspective, this paper advances the field of self-directed learning. Previous research has shown that question informativeness is a poor predictor of human question-asking behavior, and this paper helps to resolve this discrepancy by demonstrating that complexity and answer type are more important factors. This contribution to the cognitive science literature may be sufficient to justify publication in NIPS.

However, there are significant limitations to this approach that may limit its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. While it may be possible to automate the conversion of human questions to semantic logic, it is unclear how the approach can be scaled or extended to more complex domains. The battleship domain is relatively small, with only a few features, and the method is already computationally strained. Nevertheless, the ideas presented in the paper are interesting enough to warrant consideration, particularly in the context of active learning with rich questions. To increase the paper's appeal to the broader community, the authors may need to provide a clear scenario where their technique can be applied and provide novel insights.

Minor comments:

1. A nested model comparison using the log-likelihoods of the lesioned models may provide a more revealing analysis and allow for tests of statistical significance.
2. For sequential question-asking, there are other models in the linguistics literature, particularly in formal semantics and pragmatics, that may be relevant. The Questions under Discussion literature, for example, offers a range of approaches to question-asking. Some relevant references include:
Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. Linguistics and Philosophy, 18(5), 459-527.
Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17, 131-139.
