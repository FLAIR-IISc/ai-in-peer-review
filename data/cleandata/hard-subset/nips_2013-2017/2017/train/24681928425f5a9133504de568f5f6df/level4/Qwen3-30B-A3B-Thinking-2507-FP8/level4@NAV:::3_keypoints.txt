- Introduces a cognitive model that predicts question selection across belief states.  
- Unnormalized log probability of a question is a weighted sum of four components: informativeness (EIG), complexity, answer type, and relevance.  
- Evaluated in the Battleship domain (inferring ship locations on a grid).  
- Full model (all features) yields superior predictive performance on held-out human data by log-likelihood versus ablated variants.  
- However, the model omitting EIG achieves comparable correlation with human judgments, suggesting EIG may not drive correlation-based fits.  
- Evidence for claims that the model reveals how people balance informativeness and complexity is unconvincing.  
- Human question selection appears minimally influenced by EIG (bootstrap suggests ~19% chance results are due to error) and correlation results are noisy due to sparse data.  
- Work is technically robust and reproducible but is positioned more as an AI contribution than a cognitive-science advance.  
- Paper omits critical prior work—particularly Hawkins (2015) (“goal” concept) and Roberts (1996) (question-under-discussion, QUD).  
- The core innovation (modeling questions as programs executed on possible worlds) directly corresponds to these prior concepts.  
- Main contribution: demonstrates viability of a compositional question-generation approach for producing non-trivial questions in a complex domain.
