This paper attempts to reproduce user questions in a game of Battleship where asking about individual squares is replaced by asking questions essentially over an artificial language with compositional logical semantics. The authors propose a novel approach to modeling question asking as program generation, where questions are treated as formal programs that output an answer when executed on the state of the world. A log-linear model is used over a set of features of the questions, including expected information gain (informativeness), answer type (Boolean, Number, Color, and Location), complexity, and relevance to the game board.

At a high level, the motivation in terms of active learning is reasonable, as it aims to develop a model of context-sensitive, goal-directed question asking in humans. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed model, including a comparison with alternative models that lesion one key property, such as informativeness, complexity, or answer type. The results show that the full model, which uses all features, provides the best account of the data, and that lesioning its key components results in lower quality predictions.

However, the task is very simple, highly constrained, and logically unambiguous, which may limit the generalizability of the findings to more complex and open-ended question-asking scenarios. While the authors acknowledge some limitations of their current approach, such as operating on semantic representations rather than natural language text directly, and the need for domain-specific knowledge, it is unclear how much new knowledge engineering will be needed to extend this approach to different domains.

Overall, the paper provides a solid contribution to the field of active learning and question asking, but its simplicity and limited scope may not be of interest to the broader NIPS community.

Strengths:

* Novel approach to modeling question asking as program generation
* Thorough evaluation of the proposed model
* Comparison with alternative models to identify key features

Weaknesses:

* Simple and highly constrained task
* Limited generalizability to more complex and open-ended question-asking scenarios
* Domain-specific knowledge required

Acceptance arguments:

* The paper provides a solid contribution to the field of active learning and question asking.
* The proposed model is novel and well-evaluated.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

Rejection arguments:

* The task is too simple and highly constrained.
* The findings may not generalize to more complex and open-ended question-asking scenarios.
* The paper may not be of interest to the broader NIPS community.
