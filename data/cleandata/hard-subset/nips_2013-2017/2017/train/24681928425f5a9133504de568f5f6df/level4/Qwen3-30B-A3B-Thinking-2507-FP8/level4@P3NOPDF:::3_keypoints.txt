- Proposes a cognitive model predicting human question-asking in Battleship using a weighted sum of EIG, question complexity, answer type, and relevance.
- Full model outperforms ablated versions on held-out human data by log-likelihood, but a model without EIG correlates nearly as well with human judgments.
- Paper is technically sound, well-written, and reproducible.
- Primary contribution is a compositional question-generation framework treating questions as executable programs that can produce non-trivial, human-like questions.
- Approach aligns with prior work on goal-oriented question models (Hawkins, 2015) and QUD theory (Roberts, 1996), but the paper underemphasizes these connections.
- Cognitive claims are overstated: the evidence for how people balance informativeness and complexity is ambiguous.
- The log-likelihood advantage for the full model is marginal (bootstrap p=0.19), so the EIG effect may not be robust.
- Correlation analyses are noisy due to sparse human data, preventing clear conclusions about whether humans use or ignore EIG.
- AI contribution is stronger: EIG-based generation with compositional priors and complexity penalties yields plausible questions and scales to a non-trivial domain.
- Novelty claim conflates compositional program representation (present in Hawkins) with the integration of EIG and complexity; the real advance is scalability to Battleship.
- Key cognitive-science literature on question-asking and pragmatic reasoning is omitted, weakening contextual framing.
- Reliance on a single, simple domain limits generalizability and prevents broader cognitive claims.
- Paper prioritizes predictive accuracy over mechanistic explanations, missing opportunities to probe why humans prefer certain questions (e.g., relevance vs. answer type).
- Recommendations: temper cognitive assertions, clarify relations to Hawkins (2015) and Roberts (1996), cite missing literature, and test the model in richer domains to strengthen cognitive claims.
