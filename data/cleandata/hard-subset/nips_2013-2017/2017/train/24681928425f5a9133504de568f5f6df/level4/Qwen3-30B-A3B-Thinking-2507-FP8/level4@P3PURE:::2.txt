

The authors investigate human question asking with K-ary answers, defining a hand-crafted PCFG for a "battleship" domain. In this domain, hidden colored shapes occupy a partially observable grid (with some tiles revealing ship segments or emptiness), and the agent's task is to pose a single-word-answer question maximizing information about the board state. The PCFG serves as a prior over questions, defined as statements in lambda calculus. Question goodness is modeled as a linear combination of three factors: informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (length or negative log probability under the PCFG with uniform rule distribution), and "answer type" (e.g., true/false vs. color). Human data, sourced from a prior publication, was hand-coded into the grammar. The authors compare lesioned model variants via leave-one-out cross-validation, demonstrating that all factors contribute non-negligibly (with complexity being the most significant). They further present a series of novel questions.

Question asking is a pivotal domain in linguistics and hypothesis testing. The authors offer an innovative synthesis of modern linguistic and cognitive theory to examine human question-asking patterns, with potential to advance active learning through more sophisticated question-asking models. The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, with a model appropriately evaluated through quantitative and qualitative analyses.

From a computational cognitive science perspective, this work significantly advances self-directed learning. Prior research identified question informativeness as a poor predictor of human behavior; this paper resolves part of this discrepancy (complexity is secondary to informativeness and may stem from the limited question space available to the model). This contribution to cognitive science literature alone justifies publication in NIPS.

However, the approach has serious limitations that may restrict its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. While automating human question text into semantic logic statements may be feasible in constrained domains, scalability to more complex, community-relevant applications remains unclear. The battleship domain is small (with few features), and the method already exhibits computational strain. To broaden impact, the authors should clearly articulate a domain of interest to the wider community where their technique would provide novel insights. 

Minor comments:  
1) Nested model comparisons using log-likelihoods of lesioned models could yield statistical significance tests.  
2) For sequential question asking, future work should consider formal semantics and pragmatics frameworks from linguistics, particularly the "Questions under Discussion" literature (e.g., Ginzburg, 1995; Rojas-Esponda, 2013).
