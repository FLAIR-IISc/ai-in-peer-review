- Proposes a cognitive model predicting which questions people ask, with log-probability a weighted sum of four factors: informativeness (EIG), complexity (approx. length), answer type, and relevance.
- Models questions as programs that execute on world states to produce answers.
- Evaluated in a Battleship domain (inferring ship locations on a grid).
- Full model outperforms lesioned variants on held-out human data by log-likelihood.
- Omitting EIG yields similar performance to full model in correlational analyses with human judgments.
- Evidence for human sensitivity to EIG is weak: log-likelihood gains may be spurious (19% bootstrap) and correlations are noisy due to sparse data.
- Paper is technically sound, clearly written, and reproducible.
- More of a strong AI/engineering contribution than a definitive cognitive-science account of how people trade off informativeness and complexity.
- Missing prior work citations, notably Hawkins 2015 and Roberts 1996 (QUD framework), which use related EIG-vs-complexity ideas.
- Main contribution: demonstrating generation of interesting, compositional questions in a nontrivial domain; such models are plausible and likely broadly useful.
